**Acetone** (**2-propanone** or **dimethyl ketone**) is an [organic compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound") with the [formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")(CH 3)2 CO.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-22) It is the simplest and smallest [ketone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketone "Ketone") (R−C(=O)−R'). It is a colorless, highly [volatile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound "Volatile organic compound"), and [flammable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flammable "Flammable") liquid with a characteristic pungent odor.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-Myers-23)

Acetone is [miscible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miscibility "Miscibility") with [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Properties_of_water "Properties of water") and serves as an important [organic solvent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_solvent "Organic solvent") in industry, home, and laboratory. About 6.7 million [tonnes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tonne "Tonne") were produced worldwide in 2010, mainly for use as a solvent and for production of [methyl methacrylate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_methacrylate "Methyl methacrylate") and [bisphenol A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bisphenol_A "Bisphenol A"), which are precursors to widely used [plastics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plastic "Plastic").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-r1-24)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-Ullmann-25) It is a common building block in [organic chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_chemistry "Organic chemistry"). It serves as a solvent in household products such as [nail polish remover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nail_polish#Nail_polish_remover "Nail polish") and [paint thinner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paint_thinner "Paint thinner"). It has [volatile organic compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound "Volatile organic compound") (VOC)-exempt status in the United States.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-26)

Acetone is produced and disposed of in the human body through normal metabolic processes. Small quantities of it are present naturally in blood and urine. People with [diabetic ketoacidosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diabetic_ketoacidosis "Diabetic ketoacidosis") produce it in larger amounts. Medical [ketogenic diets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketogenic_diet "Ketogenic diet") that increase [ketone bodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketone_bodies "Ketone bodies") (acetone, [β-hydroxybutyric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%92-hydroxybutyric_acid "Β-hydroxybutyric acid") and [acetoacetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetoacetic_acid "Acetoacetic acid")) in the blood are used to suppress [epileptic attacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epilepsy "Epilepsy") in children with treatment-resistant [epilepsy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epilepsy "Epilepsy").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-Freeman2007-27)

From the 17th century, and before modern developments in [organic chemistry nomenclature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_chemistry_nomenclature "Organic chemistry nomenclature"), acetone was given many different names. They included "spirit of Saturn", which was given when it was thought to be a compound of [lead](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lead "Lead") and, later, "pyro-acetic spirit" and "pyro-acetic ester".[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-gorman1962-6)

Prior to the name "acetone" being coined by [French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France "France") chemists, it was named "mesit" (from the Greek μεσίτης, meaning mediator) by [Carl Reichenbach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Reichenbach "Carl Reichenbach"), who also claimed that [methyl alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_alcohol "Methyl alcohol") consisted of mesit and [ethyl alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethyl_alcohol "Ethyl alcohol").[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-28)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-gorman1962-6) Names derived from mesit include [mesitylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesitylene "Mesitylene") and [mesityl oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesityl_oxide "Mesityl oxide") which were first synthesised from acetone.

In 1839, the name "acetone" began to be used and the term was composed of “daughter of” and acetum (acetic acid) because it was obtained from acetic acid.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-Myers-23) Unlike many compounds with the _acet-_ prefix which have a 2-carbon chain, acetone has a 3-carbon chain. That has caused confusion because there cannot be a [ketone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketone "Ketone") with 2 carbons. The prefix refers to acetone's relation to [vinegar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinegar "Vinegar") (_acetum_ in [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin"), also the source of the words "acid" and "acetic"), rather than its chemical structure.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-chemtymology-29)

Acetone was first produced by [Andreas Libavius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andreas_Libavius "Andreas Libavius") in 1606 by distillation of [lead(II) acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lead(II)_acetate "Lead(II) acetate").[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-30)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-31)

In 1832, French chemist [Jean-Baptiste Dumas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Dumas "Jean-Baptiste Dumas") and German chemist [Justus von Liebig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Justus_von_Liebig "Justus von Liebig") determined the [empirical formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empirical_formula "Empirical formula") for acetone.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-32)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-33) In 1833, French chemists [Antoine Bussy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antoine_Bussy "Antoine Bussy") and [Michel Chevreul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michel_Chevreul "Michel Chevreul") decided to name acetone by adding the suffix _-one_ to the stem of the corresponding acid (viz, [acetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid "Acetic acid")) just as a similarly prepared product of what was then confused with [margaric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Margaric_acid "Margaric acid") was named margarone.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-34)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-chemtymology-29) By 1852, English chemist [Alexander William Williamson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_William_Williamson "Alexander William Williamson") realized that acetone was methyl [acetyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetyl "Acetyl");[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-35) the following year, the French chemist [Charles Frédéric Gerhardt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Fr%C3%A9d%C3%A9ric_Gerhardt "Charles Frédéric Gerhardt") concurred.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-36) In 1865, the German chemist [August Kekulé](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/August_Kekul%C3%A9 "August Kekulé") published the modern structural formula for acetone.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-37)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-38)[Johann Josef Loschmidt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Josef_Loschmidt "Johann Josef Loschmidt") had presented the structure of acetone in 1861,[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-39) but his privately published booklet received little attention. During [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I"), [Chaim Weizmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaim_Weizmann "Chaim Weizmann") developed the process for industrial production of acetone (Weizmann Process).[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-40)

Acetone is produced directly or indirectly from [propene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene "Propene"). Approximately 83% of acetone is produced via the [cumene process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cumene_process "Cumene process");[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-Ullmann-25) as a result, acetone production is tied to phenol production. In the cumene process, [benzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene "Benzene") is [alkylated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkylated "Alkylated") with propylene to produce [cumene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cumene "Cumene"), which is [oxidized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidation "Oxidation") by air to produce [phenol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenol "Phenol") and acetone:

[![Image 22: Overview of the cumene process](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Cumene-process-overview-2D-skeletal.png/330px-Cumene-process-overview-2D-skeletal.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cumene-process-overview-2D-skeletal.png "Overview of the cumene process")
Other processes involve the direct oxidation of propylene ([Wacker-Hoechst process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wacker-Hoechst_process "Wacker-Hoechst process")), or the [hydration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydration_reaction "Hydration reaction") of propylene to give [2-propanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2-propanol "2-propanol"), which is oxidized (dehydrogenated) to acetone.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-Ullmann-25)

Previously, acetone was produced by the [dry distillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dry_distillation "Dry distillation") of [acetates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetate "Acetate"), for example [calcium acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_acetate "Calcium acetate") in [ketonic decarboxylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketonic_decarboxylation "Ketonic decarboxylation").

Ca(CH 3 COO)2 → CaO(s) + CO 2(g) + (CH 3)2 CO
After that time, during [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I"), acetone was produced using [acetone-butanol-ethanol fermentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone-butanol-ethanol_fermentation "Acetone-butanol-ethanol fermentation") with _[Clostridium acetobutylicum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clostridium\_acetobutylicum "Clostridium acetobutylicum")_[bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria "Bacteria"), which was developed by [Chaim Weizmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaim_Weizmann "Chaim Weizmann") (later the first president of [Israel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel "Israel")) in order to help the British war effort,[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-Ullmann-25)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-41) in the preparation of [Cordite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cordite "Cordite").[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-42) This acetone-butanol-ethanol fermentation was eventually abandoned when newer methods with better yields were found.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-Ullmann-25)

In 2010, the worldwide production capacity for acetone was estimated at 6.7 million tonnes per year.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-CEH-43) With 1.56 million tonnes per year, the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") had the highest production capacity,[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-44) followed by [Taiwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiwan "Taiwan") and [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PRC "PRC"). The largest producer of acetone is [INEOS Phenol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/INEOS_Phenol "INEOS Phenol"), owning 17% of the world's capacity, with also significant capacity (7–8%) by [Mitsui](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mitsui "Mitsui"), [Sunoco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunoco "Sunoco") and [Shell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Dutch_Shell "Royal Dutch Shell") in 2010.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-CEH-43) INEOS Phenol also owns the world's largest production site (420,000 tonnes/annum) in [Beveren](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beveren "Beveren") (Belgium). [Spot price](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spot_price "Spot price") of acetone in summer 2011 was 1100–1250 USD/tonne in the United States.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-icispricing_132-45)

Chemical properties
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetone&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Chemical properties")]

Acetone is reluctant to form a hydrate:[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-Lemal2004-46)

(CH 3)2 C=O + H 2 O ⇌ (CH 3)2 C(OH)2 K = 10−3 M−1
Like most ketones, acetone exhibits the [keto–enol tautomerism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keto%E2%80%93enol_tautomerism "Keto–enol tautomerism") in which the nominal [keto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketone "Ketone") structure (CH 3)2 C=O of acetone itself is in equilibrium with the [enol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enol "Enol") isomer (CH 3)C(OH)=(CH 2) (**prop-1-en-2-ol**). In acetone vapor at ambient temperature, only 2.4×10−7% of the molecules are in the enol form.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-hine1976-47)

[![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Acetone_KetoEnol.svg/300px-Acetone_KetoEnol.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Acetone_KetoEnol.svg)
In the presence of suitable [catalysts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalyst "Catalyst"), two acetone molecules also combine to form the compound [diacetone alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diacetone_alcohol "Diacetone alcohol")(CH 3)C=O(CH 2)C(OH)(CH 3)2, which on [dehydration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dehydration_reaction "Dehydration reaction") gives [mesityl oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesityl_oxide "Mesityl oxide")(CH 3)C=O(CH)=C(CH 3)2. This product can further combine with another acetone molecule, with loss of another molecule of water, yielding [phorone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phorone "Phorone") and other compounds.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-48)

Acetone is a weak Lewis base that forms adducts with soft acids like [I 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iodine "Iodine") and hard acids like [phenol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenol "Phenol"). Acetone also forms complexes with divalent metals.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-49)[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-50)

Under ultraviolet light, acetone fluoresces.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-51)

The [flame temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flame_temperature "Flame temperature") of pure acetone is 1980°C.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-52)

At its melting point (−96°C) is claimed to polymerize to give a white elastic solid, soluble in acetone, stable for several hours at room temperature. To do so, a vapor of acetone is co-condensed with [magnesium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnesium "Magnesium") as a catalyst onto a very cold surface.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-karg1960-53)[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-kawa1962-54)[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-cata1996-55)

Humans exhale several milligrams of acetone per day. It arises from decarboxylation of [acetoacetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetoacetate "Acetoacetate").[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-drug-56)[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-57) Small amounts of acetone are produced in the body by the [decarboxylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decarboxylation "Decarboxylation") of [ketone bodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketone_bodies "Ketone bodies"). Certain dietary patterns, including prolonged fasting and high-fat low-carbohydrate dieting, can produce [ketosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketosis "Ketosis"), in which acetone is formed in body tissue. Certain health conditions, such as alcoholism and diabetes, can produce [ketoacidosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketoacidosis "Ketoacidosis"), uncontrollable ketosis that leads to a sharp, and potentially fatal, increase in the acidity of the blood. Since it is a byproduct of fermentation, acetone is a byproduct of the distillery industry.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-drug-56)

Acetone can then be metabolized either by [CYP2E1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CYP2E1 "CYP2E1") via [methylglyoxal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylglyoxal "Methylglyoxal") to [D-lactate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lactic_acid "Lactic acid") and [pyruvate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyruvic_acid "Pyruvic acid"), and ultimately [glucose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glucose "Glucose")/energy, or by a different pathway via [propylene glycol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propylene_glycol "Propylene glycol") to [pyruvate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyruvate "Pyruvate"), [lactate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lactic_acid "Lactic acid"), [acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetate "Acetate") (usable for energy) and [propionaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propionaldehyde "Propionaldehyde").[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-Glew2010-58)[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-59)[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-60)

About a third of the world's acetone is used as a solvent, and a quarter is consumed as [acetone cyanohydrin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone_cyanohydrin "Acetone cyanohydrin"), a precursor to [methyl methacrylate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_methacrylate "Methyl methacrylate").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-r1-24)

### Chemical intermediate

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetone&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Chemical intermediate")]

Acetone is used to [synthesize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_synthesis "Organic synthesis")[methyl methacrylate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_methacrylate "Methyl methacrylate"). It begins with the initial conversion of acetone to [acetone cyanohydrin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone_cyanohydrin "Acetone cyanohydrin") via reaction with [hydrogen cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide "Hydrogen cyanide") (HCN):

(CH 3)2 CO + HCN → (CH 3)2 C(OH)CN
In a subsequent step, the [nitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrile "Nitrile") is [hydrolyzed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrolysis "Hydrolysis") to the unsaturated [amide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amide "Amide"), which is [esterified](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ester "Ester"):

(CH 3)2 C(OH)CN + CH 3 OH → CH 2 C(CH 3)CO 2 CH 3 + NH 3
The third major use of acetone (about 20%)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-r1-24) is synthesizing [bisphenol A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bisphenol_A "Bisphenol A"). Bisphenol A is a component of many polymers such as [polycarbonates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycarbonate "Polycarbonate"), [polyurethanes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyurethane "Polyurethane"), and [epoxy resins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epoxy_resin "Epoxy resin"). The synthesis involves the [condensation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensation_reaction "Condensation reaction") of acetone with [phenol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenol "Phenol"):

(CH 3)2 CO + 2 C 6 H 5 OH → (CH 3)2 C(C 6 H 4 OH)2 + H 2 O
Many millions of kilograms of acetone are consumed in the production of the solvents [methyl isobutyl alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methyl_isobutyl_alcohol&action=edit&redlink=1 "Methyl isobutyl alcohol (page does not exist)") and [methyl isobutyl ketone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_isobutyl_ketone "Methyl isobutyl ketone"). These products arise via an initial [aldol condensation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aldol_condensation "Aldol condensation") to give [diacetone alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diacetone_alcohol "Diacetone alcohol").[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-Ullmann-25)

2 (CH 3)2 CO → (CH 3)2 C(OH)CH 2 C(O)CH 3
Condensation with acetylene gives [2-methylbut-3-yn-2-ol](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=2-methylbut-3-yn-2-ol&action=edit&redlink=1 "2-methylbut-3-yn-2-ol (page does not exist)"), precursor to synthetic [terpenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terpene "Terpene") and [terpenoids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terpenoid "Terpenoid").[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-61)

Acetone is a good solvent for many plastics and some synthetic fibers. It is used for thinning [polyester resin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyester_resin "Polyester resin"), cleaning tools used with it, and dissolving two-part [epoxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epoxy "Epoxy") and [superglue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superglue "Superglue") before they harden. It is used as one of the volatile components of some [paints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paint "Paint") and [varnishes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Varnish "Varnish").[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-Myers-23) As a heavy-duty degreaser, it is useful in the preparation of metal prior to painting or [soldering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soldering "Soldering"), and to remove [rosin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flux_(metallurgy)#Rosin_fluxes "Flux (metallurgy)") flux after soldering (to prevent adhesion of dirt and electrical leakage and perhaps corrosion or for cosmetic reasons), although it may attack some electronic components, such as polystyrene capacitors.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-attack-62)

Although itself [flammable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flammable "Flammable"), acetone is used extensively as a solvent for the safe transportation and storage of [acetylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene "Acetylene"), which cannot be safely [pressurized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressurization "Pressurization") as a pure compound. Vessels containing a porous material are first filled with acetone followed by acetylene, which dissolves into the acetone. One litre of acetone can dissolve around 250 [litres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Litre "Litre") of acetylene at a pressure of 10 bars (1.0 MPa).[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-63)[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-64)

Acetone is used as a solvent by the [pharmaceutical industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharmaceutical_industry "Pharmaceutical industry") and as a [denaturant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denaturation_(food) "Denaturation (food)") in [denatured alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denatured_alcohol "Denatured alcohol").[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-65) Acetone is also present as an [excipient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excipient "Excipient") in some [pharmaceutical drugs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharmaceutical_drug "Pharmaceutical drug").[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-66)[_[needs update](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual\_of\_Style/Dates\_and\_numbers#Chronological\_items "Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers")_]

#### Lab and domestic solvent

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetone&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Lab and domestic solvent")]

A variety of [organic reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_reaction "Organic reaction") employ acetone as a [polar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_polarity "Chemical polarity"), [aprotic solvent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aprotic_solvent "Aprotic solvent"), e.g. the [Jones oxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jones_oxidation "Jones oxidation").

Because acetone is cheap, volatile, and dissolves or decomposes with most laboratory chemicals, an acetone rinse is the standard technique to remove solid residues from [laboratory glassware](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laboratory_glassware "Laboratory glassware") before a final wash.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-67) Despite common [desiccatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desiccant "Desiccant") use, acetone dries only via bulk displacement and dilution. It forms no [azeotropes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azeotrope "Azeotrope") with water (see [azeotrope tables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azeotrope_tables "Azeotrope tables")).[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-68) Acetone also removes certain [stains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Staining "Staining") from [microscope slides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microscope_slide "Microscope slide").[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-69)

Acetone freezes well below −78°C. An acetone/dry ice mixture [cools](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cooling_bath "Cooling bath") many low-temperature reactions.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-AA-70)[Make-up artists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Make-up_artist "Make-up artist") use acetone to remove skin adhesive from the netting of wigs and mustaches by immersing the item in an acetone bath, then removing the softened glue residue with a stiff brush.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-71) Acetone is a main ingredient in many nail polish removers because it breaks down nail polish.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-72) It is used for all types of nail polish removal, like gel nail polish, dip powder and acrylic nails.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-73)

[Proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proteins "Proteins") precipitate in acetone.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-Simpson2009-74) The chemical modifies peptides, both at α- or ε-amino groups, and in a poorly understood but rapid modification of certain glycine residues.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-Simpson2009-74)

In [pathology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathology "Pathology"), acetone helps find [lymph nodes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lymph_nodes "Lymph nodes") in fatty tissues (such as the [mesentery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesentery "Mesentery")) for [tumor staging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tumor_staging "Tumor staging").[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-acetone-patho-springer-75) The liquid dissolves the fat and hardens the nodes, making them easier to find.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-acetone-patho-wiley-76)

Dermatologists use acetone with alcohol for [acne treatments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acne_treatment "Acne treatment") to [chemically peel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_peel "Chemical peel") dry skin. Common agents used today for chemical peeling are [salicylic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salicylic_acid "Salicylic acid"), [glycolic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycolic_acid "Glycolic acid"), [azelaic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azelaic_acid "Azelaic acid"), 30% [salicylic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salicylic_acid "Salicylic acid") in [ethanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol "Ethanol"), and [trichloroacetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trichloroacetic_acid "Trichloroacetic acid") (TCA). Prior to chemexfoliation, the skin is cleaned and excess fat removed in a process called defatting. Acetone, [hexachlorophene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexachlorophene "Hexachlorophene"), or a combination of these agents was used in this process.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-77)

Acetone has been shown to have [anticonvulsant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticonvulsant "Anticonvulsant") effects in animal models of [epilepsy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epilepsy "Epilepsy"), in the absence of toxicity, when administered in millimolar concentrations.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-Likhodii-78) It has been hypothesized that the high-fat low-carbohydrate [ketogenic diet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketogenic_diet "Ketogenic diet") used clinically to control drug-resistant epilepsy in children works by elevating acetone in the brain.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-Likhodii-78) Because of their higher energy requirements, children have higher acetone production than most adults – and the younger the child, the higher the expected production. This indicates that children are not uniquely susceptible to acetone exposure. External exposures are small compared to the exposures associated with the ketogenic diet.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-acc-79)

Acetone's most hazardous property is its extreme flammability. In small amounts, acetone burns with a [dull blue flame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidizing_flame "Oxidizing flame"); in larger amounts, fuel evaporation causes incomplete combustion and a [bright yellow flame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reducing_flame "Reducing flame"). When hotter than acetone's [flash point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flash_point "Flash point") of −20°C (−4°F), air mixtures of 2.5‑12.8% acetone (by volume) may explode or cause a [flash fire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flashover "Flashover"). Vapors can flow along surfaces to distant ignition sources and flash back.

[Static](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Static_electricity "Static electricity") discharge may also ignite acetone vapors, though acetone has a very high ignition initiation energy and accidental ignition is rare.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-msds-80) Acetone's [auto-ignition temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auto-ignition_temperature "Auto-ignition temperature") is the relatively high 465°C (869°F);[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-ig-19) moreover, auto-ignition temperature depends upon experimental conditions, such as exposure time, and has been quoted as high as 535°C.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-81) Even pouring or spraying acetone over red-glowing coal will not ignite it, due to the high vapour concentration and the cooling effect of evaporation.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-msds-80)

Acetone should be stored away from strong oxidizers, such as concentrated [nitric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitric_acid "Nitric acid") and [sulfuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid") mixtures.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-82) It may also explode when mixed with [chloroform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloroform "Chloroform") in the presence of a base.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-83)[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] When oxidized without combustion, for example with [hydrogen peroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide "Hydrogen peroxide"), acetone may form [acetone peroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone_peroxide "Acetone peroxide"), a highly [unstable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_stability "Chemical stability")[primary explosive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_explosive "Primary explosive"). Acetone peroxide may be formed accidentally, e.g. when waste [peroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide "Hydrogen peroxide") is poured into waste solvents.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-84)

Acetone occurs naturally as part of certain metabolic processes in the human body, and has been studied extensively and is believed to exhibit only slight toxicity in normal use. There is no strong evidence of chronic health effects if basic precautions are followed.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-85) It is generally recognized to have low acute and chronic toxicity if ingested and/or inhaled.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-sids-86) Acetone is not currently regarded as a [carcinogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carcinogen "Carcinogen"), a [mutagen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutagen "Mutagen"), or a concern for chronic [neurotoxicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neurotoxicity "Neurotoxicity") effects.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-msds-80)

Acetone can be found as an ingredient in a variety of consumer products ranging from cosmetics to processed and unprocessed foods. Acetone has been rated as a [generally recognized as safe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generally_recognized_as_safe "Generally recognized as safe") (GRAS) substance when present in drinks, baked foods, desserts, and preserves at concentrations ranging from 5 to 8 mg/L.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-sids-86)

Acetone is however an irritant, causing mild skin and moderate-to-severe eye irritation. At high vapor concentrations, it may depress the [central nervous system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_nervous_system "Central nervous system") like many other solvents.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-87) Acute toxicity for mice by ingestion (LD 50) is 3 g/kg, and by inhalation (LC 50) is 44 g/m 3 over 4 hours.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-88)

### Environmental effects

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetone&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Environmental effects")]

Although acetone occurs naturally in the environment in plants, trees, volcanic gases, forest fires, and as a product of the breakdown of body fat,[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-atsdr-89) the majority of the acetone released into the environment is of industrial origin.[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] Acetone evaporates rapidly, even from water and soil. Once in the atmosphere, it has a 22-day half-life and is degraded by UV light via [photolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photolysis "Photolysis") (primarily into [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane") and [ethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethane "Ethane").[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-90)) Consumption by microorganisms contributes to the dissipation of acetone in soil, animals, or waterways.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-atsdr-89)

In 1995, the [United States Environmental Protection Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency "United States Environmental Protection Agency") (EPA) removed acetone from the list of [volatile organic compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound "Volatile organic compound"). The companies requesting the removal argued that it would "contribute to the achievement of several important environmental goals and would support EPA's pollution prevention efforts", and that acetone could be used as a substitute for several compounds that are listed as hazardous air pollutants (HAP) under section 112 of the [Clean Air Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clean_Air_Act_(United_States) "Clean Air Act (United States)").[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-91) In making its decision EPA conducted an extensive review of the available toxicity data on acetone, which was continued through the 2000s. It found that the evaluable "data are inadequate for an assessment of the human carcinogenic potential of acetone".[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-smell-9)

On 30 July 2015, scientists reported that upon the first touchdown of the _[Philae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philae\_(spacecraft) "Philae (spacecraft)")_ lander on [comet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet "Comet")[67P](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/67P/Churyumov%E2%80%93Gerasimenko "67P/Churyumov–Gerasimenko")'s surface, measurements by the COSAC and Ptolemy instruments revealed sixteen [organic compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound"), four of which were seen for the first time on a comet, including [acetamide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetamide "Acetamide"), acetone, [methyl isocyanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_isocyanate "Methyl isocyanate"), and [propionaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propionaldehyde "Propionaldehyde").[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-wapo20150730-92)[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-esa20150730-93)[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone#cite_note-SCI-20150731-94)
