A **cylinder** (from [Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language")[κύλινδρος](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BA%CF%8D%CE%BB%CE%B9%CE%BD%CE%B4%CF%81%CE%BF%CF%82#Ancient_Greek "wikt:κύλινδρος")_(\_kúlindros\_)_'roller, tumbler')[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)#cite_note-1) has traditionally been a [three-dimensional solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry "Solid geometry"), one of the most basic of [curvilinear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear "Curvilinear") geometric [shapes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape "Shape"). In [elementary geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_geometry "Elementary geometry"), it is considered a [prism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_(geometry) "Prism (geometry)") with a [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle") as its base.

A cylinder may also be defined as an [infinite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_set "Infinite set") curvilinear [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(mathematics) "Surface (mathematics)") in various modern branches of geometry and [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology"). The shift in the basic meaning—solid versus surface (as in a solid [ball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ball_(mathematics) "Ball (mathematics)") versus [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere") surface)—has created some ambiguity with terminology. The two concepts may be distinguished by referring to **solid cylinders** and **cylindrical surfaces**. In the literature the unadorned term "cylinder" could refer to either of these or to an even more specialized object, the _[right circular cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right\_circular\_cylinder "Right circular cylinder")_.

The definitions and results in this section are taken from the 1913 text _Plane and Solid Geometry_ by [George A. Wentworth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_A._Wentworth "George A. Wentworth") and David Eugene Smith ([Wentworth & Smith 1913](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)#CITEREFWentworthSmith1913)).

A _cylindrical surface_ is a [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(mathematics) "Surface (mathematics)") consisting of all the points on all the lines which are [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_lines "Parallel lines") to a given line and which pass through a fixed [plane curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_curve "Plane curve") in a plane not parallel to the given line. Any line in this family of parallel lines is called an _element_ of the cylindrical surface. From a [kinematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics "Kinematics") point of view, given a plane curve, called the _directrix_, a cylindrical surface is that surface traced out by a line, called the _generatrix_, not in the plane of the directrix, moving parallel to itself and always passing through the directrix. Any particular position of the generatrix is an element of the cylindrical surface.

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Cylinders.svg/250px-Cylinders.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cylinders.svg)

A right and an oblique circular cylinder

A [solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry "Solid geometry") bounded by a cylindrical surface and two [parallel planes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_planes "Parallel planes") is called a (solid) _cylinder_. The line segments determined by an element of the cylindrical surface between the two parallel planes is called an _element of the cylinder_. All the elements of a cylinder have equal lengths. The region bounded by the cylindrical surface in either of the parallel planes is called a _base_ of the cylinder. The two bases of a cylinder are [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_(geometry) "Congruence (geometry)") figures. If the elements of the cylinder are perpendicular to the planes containing the bases, the cylinder is a _right cylinder_, otherwise it is called an _oblique cylinder_. If the bases are [disks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disk_(mathematics) "Disk (mathematics)") (regions whose boundary is a [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle_(mathematics) "Circle (mathematics)")) the cylinder is called a _circular cylinder_. In some elementary treatments, a cylinder always means a circular cylinder.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)#cite_note-2) An _open cylinder_ is a cylindrical surface without the bases.

The _height_ (or altitude) of a cylinder is the [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") distance between its bases.

The cylinder obtained by rotating a [line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment") about a fixed line that it is parallel to is a _cylinder of revolution_. A cylinder of revolution is a right circular cylinder. The height of a cylinder of revolution is the length of the generating line segment. The line that the segment is revolved about is called the _axis_ of the cylinder and it passes through the centers of the two bases.

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Circular_cylinder_rh.svg/250px-Circular_cylinder_rh.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Circular_cylinder_rh.svg)

A right circular cylinder with radius _r_ and height _h_

### Right circular cylinders

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cylinder&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Right circular cylinders")]

The bare term _cylinder_ often refers to a solid cylinder with circular ends perpendicular to the axis, that is, a right circular cylinder, as shown in the figure. The cylindrical surface without the ends is called an _open cylinder_. The formulae for the [surface area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_area "Surface area") and the [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume") of a right circular cylinder have been known from early antiquity.

A right circular cylinder can also be thought of as the [solid of revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_of_revolution "Solid of revolution") generated by rotating a rectangle about one of its sides. These cylinders are used in an integration technique (the "disk method") for obtaining volumes of solids of revolution.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)#cite_note-FOOTNOTESwokowski1983283-3)

A tall and thin _needle cylinder_ has a height much greater than its diameter, whereas a short and wide _disk cylinder_ has a diameter much greater than its height.

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Cylindric_section.svg/250px-Cylindric_section.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cylindric_section.svg)

Cylindric section

A cylindric section is the intersection of a cylinder's surface with a [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(geometry) "Plane (geometry)"). They are, in general, curves and are special types of [_plane sections_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_section_(geometry) "Cross section (geometry)"). The cylindric section by a plane that contains two elements of a cylinder is a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWentworthSmith1913354-4) Such a cylindric section of a right cylinder is a [rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle "Rectangle").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWentworthSmith1913354-4)

A cylindric section in which the intersecting plane intersects and is perpendicular to all the elements of the cylinder is called a _right section_.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWentworthSmith1913357-5) If a right section of a cylinder is a circle then the cylinder is a circular cylinder. In more generality, if a right section of a cylinder is a [conic section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conic_section "Conic section") (parabola, ellipse, hyperbola) then the solid cylinder is said to be parabolic, elliptic and hyperbolic, respectively.

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Blue_cut-cylinder.gif/250px-Blue_cut-cylinder.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Blue_cut-cylinder.gif)

Cylindric sections of a right circular cylinder

For a right circular cylinder, there are several ways in which planes can meet a cylinder. First, planes that intersect a base in at most one point. A plane is tangent to the cylinder if it meets the cylinder in a single element. The right sections are circles and all other planes intersect the cylindrical surface in an [ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)#cite_note-6) If a plane intersects a base of the cylinder in exactly two points then the line segment joining these points is part of the cylindric section. If such a plane contains two elements, it has a rectangle as a cylindric section, otherwise the sides of the cylindric section are portions of an ellipse. Finally, if a plane contains more than two points of a base, it contains the entire base and the cylindric section is a circle.

In the case of a right circular cylinder with a cylindric section that is an ellipse, the [eccentricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eccentricity_(mathematics) "Eccentricity (mathematics)")_e_ of the cylindric section and [semi-major axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-major_axis "Semi-major axis")_a_ of the cylindric section depend on the radius of the cylinder _r_ and the angle _α_ between the secant plane and cylinder axis, in the following way: ![Image 6: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}e&=\cos \alpha ,\\[1ex]a&={\frac {r}{\sin \alpha }}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7bb6d1ccc236e72f66ab323c7983ff18cffe3dbc)

If the base of a circular cylinder has a [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius")_r_ and the cylinder has height h, then its [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume") is given by ![Image 7: {\displaystyle V=\pi r^{2}h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d4495afedaf8e7b093bfcaf0b5969678df6a89e)

This formula holds whether or not the cylinder is a right cylinder.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWentworthSmith1913359-7)

This formula may be established by using [Cavalieri's principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cavalieri%27s_principle "Cavalieri's principle").

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Elliptic_cylinder_abh.svg/250px-Elliptic_cylinder_abh.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Elliptic_cylinder_abh.svg)

A solid elliptic right cylinder with the semi-axes _a_ and _b_ for the base ellipse and height _h_

In more generality, by the same principle, the volume of any cylinder is the product of the area of a base and the height. For example, an elliptic cylinder with a base having [semi-major axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-major_and_semi-minor_axes "Semi-major and semi-minor axes")a, semi-minor axis b and height h has a volume _V_ = _Ah_, where A is the area of the base ellipse (= π _ab_). This result for right elliptic cylinders can also be obtained by integration, where the axis of the cylinder is taken as the positive x-axis and _A_(_x_) = _A_ the area of each elliptic cross-section, thus: ![Image 9: {\displaystyle V=\int _{0}^{h}A(x)dx=\int _{0}^{h}\pi abdx=\pi ab\int _{0}^{h}dx=\pi abh.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0285fb685d7f296b43726f3952153873341244f)

Using [cylindrical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylindrical_coordinates "Cylindrical coordinates"), the volume of a right circular cylinder can be calculated by integration ![Image 10: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}V&=\int _{0}^{h}\int _{0}^{2\pi }\int _{0}^{r}s\,\,ds\,d\phi \,dz\\[5mu]&=\pi \,r^{2}\,h.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d02accdfaa805404f4ed1af1bd3fffd776def96)

Having radius _r_ and altitude (height) h, the [surface area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_area "Surface area") of a right circular cylinder, oriented so that its axis is vertical, consists of three parts:

*   the area of the top base: π _r_ 2
*   the area of the bottom base: π _r_ 2
*   the area of the side: 2π _rh_

The area of the top and bottom bases is the same, and is called the _base area_, _B_. The area of the side is known as the _lateral area_, _L_.

An _open cylinder_ does not include either top or bottom elements, and therefore has surface area (lateral area) ![Image 11: {\displaystyle L=2\pi rh}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91add351a1bbbcbe2b0b4a0d5761a92e40dda37f)

The surface area of the solid right circular cylinder is made up the sum of all three components: top, bottom and side. Its surface area is therefore ![Image 12: {\displaystyle A=L+2B=2\pi rh+2\pi r^{2}=2\pi r(h+r)=\pi d(r+h)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b07476c33b3b25743c0b7ae4ec513e2e204fbda0) where _d_ = 2 _r_ is the [diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diameter "Diameter") of the circular top or bottom.

For a given volume, the right circular cylinder with the smallest surface area has _h_ = 2 _r_. Equivalently, for a given surface area, the right circular cylinder with the largest volume has _h_ = 2 _r_, that is, the cylinder fits snugly in a cube of side length = altitude ( = diameter of base circle).[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)#cite_note-8)

The lateral area, L, of a circular cylinder, which need not be a right cylinder, is more generally given by ![Image 13: {\displaystyle L=e\times p,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7a3e97e43aff769c3454dd8e2d6b68a5ae89d9ff) where e is the length of an element and p is the perimeter of a right section of the cylinder.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWentworthSmith1913358-9) This produces the previous formula for lateral area when the cylinder is a right circular cylinder.

### Right circular hollow cylinder (cylindrical shell)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cylinder&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Right circular hollow cylinder (cylindrical shell)")]

[![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Zylinder-rohr-s.svg/250px-Zylinder-rohr-s.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Zylinder-rohr-s.svg)

Hollow cylinder

A _right circular hollow cylinder_ (or _cylindrical shell_) is a three-dimensional region bounded by two right circular cylinders having the same axis and two parallel [annular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annulus_(mathematics) "Annulus (mathematics)") bases perpendicular to the cylinders' common axis, as in the diagram.

Let the height be _h_, internal radius _r_, and external radius _R_. The volume is given by subtracting the volume of the inner imaginary cylinder (i.e. hollow space) from the volume of the outer cylinder: ![Image 15: {\displaystyle V=\pi \left(R^{2}-r^{2}\right)h=2\pi \left({\frac {R+r}{2}}\right)h(R-r).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/749e2dc1594c50abd2f3d26917f2f754dbd77e6d) Thus, the volume of a cylindrical shell equals 2 π ×average radius ×height × thickness.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)#cite_note-FOOTNOTESwokowski1983292-10)

The surface area, including the top and bottom, is given by ![Image 16: {\displaystyle A=2\pi \left(R+r\right)h+2\pi \left(R^{2}-r^{2}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c7163aaee9f291631e00588c2ec0c86a35bc88b) Cylindrical shells are used in a common integration technique for finding volumes of solids of revolution.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)#cite_note-FOOTNOTESwokowski1983291-11)

### _On the Sphere and Cylinder_

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cylinder&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: On the Sphere and Cylinder")]

[![Image 17](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Esfera_Arqu%C3%ADmedes.svg/250px-Esfera_Arqu%C3%ADmedes.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Esfera_Arqu%C3%ADmedes.svg)

A sphere has 2/3 the volume and surface area of its circumscribing cylinder including its bases

In the treatise by this name, written c. 225 BCE, [Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes") obtained the result of which he was most proud, namely obtaining the formulas for the volume and surface area of a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere") by exploiting the relationship between a sphere and its [circumscribed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumscribe "Circumscribe")[right circular cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_circular_cylinder "Right circular cylinder") of the same height and [diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diameter "Diameter"). The sphere has a volume two-thirds that of the circumscribed cylinder and a surface area two-thirds that of the cylinder (including the bases). Since the values for the cylinder were already known, he obtained, for the first time, the corresponding values for the sphere. The volume of a sphere of radius r is ⁠4/3⁠π _r_ 3 = ⁠2/3⁠ (2 π _r_ 3). The surface area of this sphere is 4 π _r_ 2 = ⁠2/3⁠ (6 π _r_ 2). A sculpted sphere and cylinder were placed on the tomb of Archimedes at his request.

Cylindrical surfaces
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cylinder&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Cylindrical surfaces")]

In some areas of geometry and topology the term _cylinder_ refers to what has been called a **cylindrical surface**. A cylinder is defined as a surface consisting of all the points on all the lines which are parallel to a given line and which pass through a fixed plane curve in a plane not parallel to the given line.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlbert201643-12) Such cylinders have, at times, been referred to as _generalized cylinders_. Through each point of a generalized cylinder there passes a unique line that is contained in the cylinder.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlbert201649-13) Thus, this definition may be rephrased to say that a cylinder is any [ruled surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruled_surface "Ruled surface") spanned by a one-parameter family of parallel lines.

A cylinder having a right section that is an [ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse"), [parabola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabola "Parabola"), or [hyperbola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbola "Hyperbola") is called an **elliptic cylinder**, **parabolic cylinder** and **hyperbolic cylinder**, respectively. These are degenerate [quadric surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadric_surface "Quadric surface").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)#cite_note-14)

[![Image 18](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Zylinder-parabol-s.svg/250px-Zylinder-parabol-s.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Zylinder-parabol-s.svg)

Parabolic cylinder

When the principal axes of a quadric are aligned with the reference frame (always possible for a quadric), a general equation of the quadric in three dimensions is given by ![Image 19: {\displaystyle f(x,y,z)=Ax^{2}+By^{2}+Cz^{2}+Dx+Ey+Gz+H=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91cc61b650c3331e8c29e399a90fccba2ffa9365) with the coefficients being [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") and not all of A, B and C being 0. If at least one variable does not appear in the equation, then the quadric is degenerate. If one variable is missing, we may assume by an appropriate [rotation of axes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_of_axes "Rotation of axes") that the variable z does not appear and the general equation of this type of degenerate quadric can be written as[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlbert201674-15)![Image 20: {\displaystyle A\left(x+{\frac {D}{2A}}\right)^{2}+B\left(y+{\frac {E}{2B}}\right)^{2}=\rho ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9557f7c49e2518e5bf3dc1b333751625aee2cd20) where ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \rho =-H+{\frac {D^{2}}{4A}}+{\frac {E^{2}}{4B}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2a283171f06d68e406f140ca93424f61927eee6)

If _AB_> 0 this is the equation of an _elliptic cylinder_.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlbert201674-15) Further simplification can be obtained by [translation of axes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_of_axes "Translation of axes") and scalar multiplication. If ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \rho }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f7d439671d1289b6a816e6af7a304be40608d64) has the same sign as the coefficients A and B, then the equation of an elliptic cylinder may be rewritten in [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates") as: ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \left({\frac {x}{a}}\right)^{2}+\left({\frac {y}{b}}\right)^{2}=1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7a0898f719e99960159cb7bcb0f33035f68b18b8) This equation of an elliptic cylinder is a generalization of the equation of the ordinary, _circular cylinder_ (_a_ = _b_). Elliptic cylinders are also known as _cylindroids_, but that name is ambiguous, as it can also refer to the [Plücker conoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pl%C3%BCcker_conoid "Plücker conoid").

If ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \rho }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f7d439671d1289b6a816e6af7a304be40608d64) has a different sign than the coefficients, we obtain the _imaginary elliptic cylinders_: ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \left({\frac {x}{a}}\right)^{2}+\left({\frac {y}{b}}\right)^{2}=-1,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a4b8facd63726299aeda23a5a7d731a94bfb6f3d) which have no real points on them. (![Image 26: {\displaystyle \rho =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ba6310b27df5f9c9b0b1732e08cce27b99d68cf) gives a single real point.)

### Hyperbolic cylinder

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cylinder&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Hyperbolic cylinder")]

If A and B have different signs and ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \rho \neq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2980df10662caf87367d399d4fc85cb41185639), we obtain the _hyperbolic cylinders_, whose equations may be rewritten as: ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \left({\frac {x}{a}}\right)^{2}-\left({\frac {y}{b}}\right)^{2}=1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/508902209a2bc56a789ed3f70120ba17ca7e2a95)

Finally, if _AB_ = 0 assume, [without loss of generality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Without_loss_of_generality "Without loss of generality"), that _B_ = 0 and _A_ = 1 to obtain the _parabolic cylinders_ with equations that can be written as:[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlbert201675-16)![Image 29: {\displaystyle x^{2}+2ay=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fad338d06f86932125d1f93d7ae3a66dcce79a82)

[![Image 30](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/Texas_Gulf_Sulphur_Company_%2810428629273%29.jpg/250px-Texas_Gulf_Sulphur_Company_%2810428629273%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Texas_Gulf_Sulphur_Company_(10428629273).jpg)

In [projective geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_geometry "Projective geometry"), a cylinder is simply a cone whose [apex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_(geometry) "Apex (geometry)") is at infinity, which corresponds visually to a cylinder in perspective appearing to be a cone towards the sky.

Projective geometry
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cylinder&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Projective geometry")]

In [projective geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_geometry "Projective geometry"), a cylinder is simply a [cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone_(geometry) "Cone (geometry)") whose [apex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apex_(geometry) "Apex (geometry)") (vertex) lies on the [plane at infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_at_infinity "Plane at infinity"). If the cone is a quadratic cone, the plane at infinity (which passes through the vertex) can intersect the cone at two real lines, a single real line (actually a coincident pair of lines), or only at the vertex. These cases give rise to the hyperbolic, parabolic or elliptic cylinders respectively.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)#cite_note-17)

This concept is useful when considering [degenerate conics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degenerate_conic "Degenerate conic"), which may include the cylindrical conics.

[![Image 31](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/TychoBrahePlanetarium-Copenhagen.jpg/250px-TychoBrahePlanetarium-Copenhagen.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:TychoBrahePlanetarium-Copenhagen.jpg)

[Tycho Brahe Planetarium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tycho_Brahe_Planetarium "Tycho Brahe Planetarium") building, Copenhagen, is an example of a truncated cylinder

A _solid circular cylinder_ can be seen as the limiting case of a [n-gonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon") prism where _n_ approaches [infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinity "Infinity"). The connection is very strong and many older texts treat [prisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_(geometry) "Prism (geometry)") and cylinders simultaneously. Formulas for surface area and volume are derived from the corresponding formulas for prisms by using inscribed and circumscribed prisms and then letting the number of sides of the prism increase without bound.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry)#cite_note-18) One reason for the early emphasis (and sometimes exclusive treatment) on circular cylinders is that a circular base is the only type of geometric figure for which this technique works with the use of only elementary considerations (no appeal to calculus or more advanced mathematics). Terminology about prisms and cylinders is identical. Thus, for example, since a _truncated prism_ is a prism whose bases do not lie in parallel planes, a solid cylinder whose bases do not lie in parallel planes would be called a _truncated cylinder_.

From a polyhedral viewpoint, a cylinder can also be seen as a [dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polyhedron "Dual polyhedron") of a [bicone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bicone "Bicone") as an infinite-sided [bipyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipyramid "Bipyramid").
