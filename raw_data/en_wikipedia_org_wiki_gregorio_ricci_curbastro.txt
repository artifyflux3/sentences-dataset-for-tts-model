**Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro** (Italian:[[ɡreˈɡɔːrjo ˈrittʃi kurˈbastro]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Italian "Help:IPA/Italian"); 12 January 1853– 6 August 1925) was an Italian [mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorio_Ricci-Curbastro#cite_note-1) He is most famous as the discoverer of [tensor calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_calculus "Tensor calculus").

With his former student [Tullio Levi-Civita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita "Tullio Levi-Civita"), he wrote his most famous single publication,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorio_Ricci-Curbastro#cite_note-2) a pioneering work on the calculus of [tensors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor"), signing it as Gregorio Ricci. This appears to be the only time that Ricci-Curbastro used the shortened form of his name in a publication, and continues to cause confusion.

Ricci-Curbastro also published important works in other fields, including a book on higher [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra "Algebra") and infinitesimal analysis,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorio_Ricci-Curbastro#cite_note-3) and papers on the theory of [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number"), an area in which he extended the research begun by [Richard Dedekind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Dedekind "Richard Dedekind").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorio_Ricci-Curbastro#cite_note-4)

Early life and education
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Gregorio_Ricci-Curbastro&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Early life and education")]

Completing privately his high school studies at only 16 years of age, he enrolled on the course of philosophy-mathematics at [Rome University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sapienza_University_of_Rome "Sapienza University of Rome") (1869). The following year the [Papal State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vatican_State "Vatican State") fell and so Gregorio was called by his father to the city of his birth, [Lugo di Romagna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lugo,_Emilia-Romagna "Lugo, Emilia-Romagna"). Subsequently he attended courses at [University of Bologna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Bologna "University of Bologna") during the year 1872 - 1873, then transferred to the [Scuola Normale Superiore di Pisa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scuola_Normale_Superiore_di_Pisa "Scuola Normale Superiore di Pisa").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorio_Ricci-Curbastro#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorio_Ricci-Curbastro#cite_note-6)

In 1875 he graduated in [Pisa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pisa "Pisa") in physical sciences and mathematics with a thesis on [differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equation "Differential equation"), entitled "On Fuchs's Research Concerning Linear Differential Equations". During his various travels he was a student of the mathematicians [Enrico Betti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enrico_Betti "Enrico Betti"), [Eugenio Beltrami](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eugenio_Beltrami "Eugenio Beltrami"), [Ulisse Dini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ulisse_Dini "Ulisse Dini") and [Felix Klein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein "Felix Klein").

Studies on absolute differential calculus
-----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Gregorio_Ricci-Curbastro&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Studies on absolute differential calculus")]

In 1877 Ricci-Curbastro obtained a scholarship at the [Technical University of Munich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technical_University_of_Munich "Technical University of Munich"), Bavaria, and he later worked as an assistant of Ulisse Dini, his teacher.

In 1880 he became a lecturer of mathematics at the University of Padua where he dealt with [Riemannian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_geometry "Riemannian geometry") and differential quadratic forms.

He formed a research group in which Tullio Levi-Civita worked, with whom he wrote the fundamental treatise on absolute differential calculus (also known as Ricci calculus) with coordinates or tensor calculus on [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold"), which then became the [lingua franca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lingua_franca "Lingua franca") of the subsequent theory of [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein")'s general relativity. In fact absolute differential calculus had a crucial role in developing the theory, as is shown in a letter written by Albert Einstein to Ricci-Curbastro's nephew. In this context Ricci-Curbastro identified the so-called [Ricci tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_tensor "Ricci tensor") which would have a crucial role within that theory.

The advent of tensor calculus in dynamics goes back to [Lagrange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange "Lagrange"), who originated the general treatment of a [dynamical system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamical_system "Dynamical system"), and to [Riemann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann "Riemann"), who was the first to think about geometry in an arbitrary number of dimensions. He was also influenced by the works of [Christoffel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elwin_Bruno_Christoffel "Elwin Bruno Christoffel") and of [Lipschitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolf_Lipschitz "Rudolf Lipschitz") on the quadratic forms. In fact, it was essentially Christoffel's idea of [covariant differentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_derivative "Covariant derivative")[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorio_Ricci-Curbastro#cite_note-7) that allowed Ricci-Curbastro to make the greatest progress.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorio_Ricci-Curbastro#cite_note-8)

Ricci-Curbastro received many honours for his contributions.

He is honoured by mentions in various Academies amongst which are:

*   The Veneto Institute of Science - Istituto veneto di scienze - letters and articles (from 1892), of which he was then president from 1916 to 1919.
*   The Lincei Academy - [Accademia dei Lincei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accademia_dei_Lincei "Accademia dei Lincei") - of which he was a member from 1899.
*   The Academy of Padua - Accademia di Padova - from 1905.
*   The Science Academy of Turin - Accademia delle Scienze di Torino - from 1918.
*   The Galileian Academy of Science - Accademia Galileiana di Scienze, Lettere ed Arti - letters and articles, of which he was then president from 1920 to 1922.
*   The [Academy of Sciences of the Institute of Bologna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academy_of_Sciences_of_the_Institute_of_Bologna "Academy of Sciences of the Institute of Bologna") - Reale Accademia di Bologna - from 1922.
*   The [Pontifical Academy of Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pontifical_Academy_of_Sciences "Pontifical Academy of Sciences") - Accademia Pontificia delle Scienze - from 1925.

He participated actively in political life, both in his native town and in Padua, and contributed with his projects to the Ravenna-area land drainage and the Lugo aqueduct.

An [asteroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid "Asteroid"), [13642 Ricci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_minor_planets:_13001%E2%80%9314000 "List of minor planets: 13001–14000"), is named after him.
