In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), **Ricci calculus** constitutes the rules of index notation and manipulation for [tensors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensors "Tensors") and [tensor fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_fields "Tensor fields") on a [differentiable manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold"), with or without a [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor") or [connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(mathematics) "Connection (mathematics)").[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#cite_note-1)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#cite_note-2)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#cite_note-3)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#cite_note-4) It is also the modern name for what used to be called the **absolute differential calculus** (the foundation of tensor calculus), **tensor calculus** or **tensor analysis** developed by [Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorio_Ricci-Curbastro "Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro") in 1887–1896, and subsequently popularized in a paper written with his pupil [Tullio Levi-Civita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita "Tullio Levi-Civita") in 1900.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#cite_note-5)[Jan Arnoldus Schouten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_Arnoldus_Schouten "Jan Arnoldus Schouten") developed the modern notation and formalism for this mathematical framework, and made contributions to the theory, during its applications to [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") and [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry") in the early twentieth century.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#cite_note-6) The basis of modern tensor analysis was developed by [Bernhard Riemann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernhard_Riemann "Bernhard Riemann") in a paper from 1861.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#cite_note-7)

A component of a tensor is a [real number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") that is used as a coefficient of a basis element for the tensor space. The tensor is the sum of its components multiplied by their corresponding basis elements. Tensors and tensor fields can be expressed in terms of their components, and operations on tensors and tensor fields can be expressed in terms of operations on their components. The description of tensor fields and operations on them in terms of their components is the focus of the Ricci calculus. This notation allows an efficient expression of such tensor fields and operations. While much of the notation may be applied with any tensors, operations relating to a [differential structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_structure "Differential structure") are only applicable to tensor fields. Where needed, the notation extends to components of non-tensors, particularly [multidimensional arrays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multidimensional_array "Multidimensional array").

A tensor may be expressed as a linear sum of the [tensor product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product "Tensor product") of [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(mathematics_and_physics) "Vector (mathematics and physics)") and [covector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covector "Covector") basis elements. The resulting tensor components are labelled by indices of the basis. Each index has one possible value per [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension") of the underlying [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space"). The number of indices equals the degree (or order) of the tensor.

For compactness and convenience, the Ricci calculus incorporates [Einstein notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_notation "Einstein notation"), which implies summation over indices repeated within a term and [universal quantification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_quantification "Universal quantification") over free indices. Expressions in the notation of the Ricci calculus may generally be interpreted as a set of simultaneous equations relating the components as functions over a manifold, usually more specifically as functions of the coordinates on the manifold. This allows intuitive manipulation of expressions with familiarity of only a limited set of rules.

Tensor calculus has many applications in [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering") and [computer science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science "Computer science") including [elasticity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elasticity_(physics) "Elasticity (physics)"), [continuum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics "Continuum mechanics"), [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism") (see [mathematical descriptions of the electromagnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_descriptions_of_the_electromagnetic_field "Mathematical descriptions of the electromagnetic field")), [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") (see [mathematics of general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics_of_general_relativity "Mathematics of general relativity")), [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory"), and [machine learning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine_learning "Machine learning").

Working with a main proponent of the [exterior calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_calculus "Exterior calculus")[Élie Cartan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89lie_Cartan "Élie Cartan"), the influential geometer [Shiing-Shen Chern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shiing-Shen_Chern "Shiing-Shen Chern") summarizes the role of tensor calculus:[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#cite_note-8)

> In our subject of differential geometry, where you talk about manifolds, one difficulty is that the geometry is described by coordinates, but the coordinates do not have meaning. They are allowed to undergo transformation. And in order to handle this kind of situation, an important tool is the so-called tensor analysis, or Ricci calculus, which was new to mathematicians. In mathematics you have a function, you write down the function, you calculate, or you add, or you multiply, or you can differentiate. You have something very concrete. In geometry the geometric situation is described by numbers, but you can change your numbers arbitrarily. So to handle this, you need the Ricci calculus.

Notation for indices
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ricci_calculus&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Notation for indices")]

#### Space and time coordinates

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ricci_calculus&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Space and time coordinates")]

Where a distinction is to be made between the space-like basis elements and a time-like element in the four-dimensional spacetime of classical physics, this is conventionally done through indices as follows:[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#cite_note-9)

*   The lowercase [Latin alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_alphabet "Latin alphabet")_a_, _b_, _c_, ... is used to indicate restriction to 3-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), which take values 1, 2, 3 for the spatial components; and the time-like element, indicated by 0, is shown separately.
*   The lowercase [Greek alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_alphabet "Greek alphabet")_α_, _β_, _γ_, ... is used for 4-dimensional [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime"), which typically take values 0 for time components and 1, 2, 3 for the spatial components.

Some sources use 4 instead of 0 as the index value corresponding to time; in this article, 0 is used. Otherwise, in general mathematical contexts, any symbols can be used for the indices, generally running over all dimensions of the vector space.

#### Coordinate and index notation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ricci_calculus&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Coordinate and index notation")]

The author(s) will usually make it clear whether a subscript is intended as an index or as a label.

For example, in 3-D Euclidean space and using [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates"); the [coordinate vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_vector "Coordinate vector")**A** = (_A_ 1, _A_ 2, _A_ 3) = (_A_ x, _A_ y, _A_ z) shows a direct correspondence between the subscripts 1, 2, 3 and the labels x, y, z. In the expression _A i_, _i_ is interpreted as an index ranging over the values 1, 2, 3, while the x, y, z subscripts are only labels, not variables. In the context of spacetime, the index value 0 conventionally corresponds to the label t.

Indices themselves may be _labelled_ using [diacritic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diacritic "Diacritic")-like symbols, such as a [hat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumflex "Circumflex") (ˆ), [bar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macron_(diacritic) "Macron (diacritic)") (¯), [tilde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tilde "Tilde") (˜), or prime (′) as in:

![Image 1: {\displaystyle X_{\hat {\phi }}\,,Y_{\bar {\lambda }}\,,Z_{\tilde {\eta }}\,,T_{\mu '}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d7b71e8e0a2f211ca902dbfd24225b85dd3e9af)
to denote a possibly different [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)") for that index. An example is in [Lorentz transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_transformation "Lorentz transformation") from one [frame of reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_of_reference "Frame of reference") to another, where one frame could be unprimed and the other primed, as in:

![Image 2: {\displaystyle v^{\mu '}=v^{\nu }L_{\nu }{}^{\mu '}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9531592a4c1f8c9f82f918f1fd62482f87969084)
This is not to be confused with [van der Waerden notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_der_Waerden_notation "Van der Waerden notation") for [spinors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinor "Spinor"), which uses hats and overdots on indices to reflect the chirality of a spinor.

### Upper and lower indices

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ricci_calculus&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Upper and lower indices")]

Ricci calculus, and [index notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_index_notation "Abstract index notation") more generally, distinguishes between lower indices (subscripts) and upper indices (superscripts); the latter are _not_ exponents, even though they may look as such to the reader only familiar with other parts of mathematics.

In the special case that the metric tensor is everywhere equal to the identity matrix, it is possible to drop the distinction between upper and lower indices, and then all indices could be written in the lower position. Coordinate formulae in linear algebra such as ![Image 3: {\displaystyle a_{ij}b_{jk}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e83a68ed8b62f71b1b0d4823b7c0861de5e401b3) for the product of matrices may be examples of this. But in general, the distinction between upper and lower indices should be maintained.

A _lower index_ (subscript) indicates covariance of the components with respect to that index:

![Image 4: {\displaystyle A_{\alpha \beta \gamma \cdots }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1babea3ee94799d2f8126184c41788d43430d6e6)
An _upper index_ (superscript) indicates contravariance of the components with respect to that index:

![Image 5: {\displaystyle A^{\alpha \beta \gamma \cdots }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3477a1ad78c8035b297b30f236be657f43968b4)
A tensor may have both upper and lower indices:

![Image 6: {\displaystyle A_{\alpha }{}^{\beta }{}_{\gamma }{}^{\delta \cdots }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d4063d286b1c0bea0b5473f291abbb6b73192f72)
Ordering of indices is significant, even when of differing variance. However, when it is understood that no indices will be raised or lowered while retaining the base symbol, covariant indices are sometimes placed below contravariant indices for notational convenience (e.g. with the [generalized Kronecker delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_Kronecker_delta "Generalized Kronecker delta")).

#### Tensor type and degree

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ricci_calculus&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Tensor type and degree")]

The number of each upper and lower indices of a tensor gives its _type_: a tensor with _p_ upper and _q_ lower indices is said to be of type (_p_, _q_), or to be a type-(_p_, _q_) tensor.

The number of indices of a tensor, regardless of variance, is called the _degree_ of the tensor (alternatively, its _valence_, _order_ or _rank_, although _rank_ is ambiguous). Thus, a tensor of type (_p_, _q_) has degree _p_ + _q_.

The same symbol occurring twice (one upper and one lower) within a term indicates a pair of indices that are summed over:

![Image 7: {\displaystyle A_{\alpha }B^{\alpha }\equiv \sum _{\alpha }A_{\alpha }B^{\alpha }\quad {\text{or}}\quad A^{\alpha }B_{\alpha }\equiv \sum _{\alpha }A^{\alpha }B_{\alpha }\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/70aa42f4d4ae0c60b985316d7315018a0402935a)
The operation implied by such a summation is called [tensor contraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_contraction "Tensor contraction"):

![Image 8: {\displaystyle A_{\alpha }B^{\beta }\rightarrow A_{\alpha }B^{\alpha }\equiv \sum _{\alpha }A_{\alpha }B^{\alpha }\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a3e108fe153f5989462eb205ed8fd235c27856f)
This summation may occur more than once within a term with a distinct symbol per pair of indices, for example:

![Image 9: {\displaystyle A_{\alpha }{}^{\gamma }B^{\alpha }C_{\gamma }{}^{\beta }\equiv \sum _{\alpha }\sum _{\gamma }A_{\alpha }{}^{\gamma }B^{\alpha }C_{\gamma }{}^{\beta }\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5e36b553f8fcbcb811afad6d65d34908fbda320)
Other combinations of repeated indices within a term are considered to be ill-formed, such as

The reason for excluding such formulae is that although these quantities could be computed as arrays of numbers, they would not in general transform as tensors under a change of basis.

If a tensor has a list of all upper or lower indices, one shorthand is to use a capital letter for the list:[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#cite_note-10)

![Image 10: {\displaystyle A_{i_{1}\cdots i_{n}}B^{i_{1}\cdots i_{n}j_{1}\cdots j_{m}}C_{j_{1}\cdots j_{m}}\equiv A_{I}B^{IJ}C_{J},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bc601536b8b79be6a4cad82fa131390978cec09c)
where _I_ = _i_ 1 _i_ 2 ⋅⋅⋅ _i n_ and _J_ = _j_ 1 _j_ 2 ⋅⋅⋅ _j m_.

#### Sequential summation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ricci_calculus&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Sequential summation")]

A pair of vertical bars | ⋅ | around a set of all-upper indices or all-lower indices (but not both), associated with contraction with another set of indices when the expression is [completely antisymmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antisymmetric_tensor "Antisymmetric tensor") in each of the two sets of indices:[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#cite_note-11)

![Image 11: {\displaystyle A_{|\alpha \beta \gamma |\cdots }B^{\alpha \beta \gamma \cdots }=A_{\alpha \beta \gamma \cdots }B^{|\alpha \beta \gamma |\cdots }=\sum _{\alpha <\beta <\gamma }A_{\alpha \beta \gamma \cdots }B^{\alpha \beta \gamma \cdots }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/400c1172bfeba2575be237bb6fbb2836fe483c43)
means a restricted sum over index values, where each index is constrained to being strictly less than the next. More than one group can be summed in this way, for example:

![Image 12: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}&A_{|\alpha \beta \gamma |}{}^{|\delta \epsilon \cdots \lambda |}B^{\alpha \beta \gamma }{}_{\delta \epsilon \cdots \lambda |\mu \nu \cdots \zeta |}C^{\mu \nu \cdots \zeta }\\[3pt]={}&\sum _{\alpha <\beta <\gamma }~\sum _{\delta <\epsilon <\cdots <\lambda }~\sum _{\mu <\nu <\cdots <\zeta }A_{\alpha \beta \gamma }{}^{\delta \epsilon \cdots \lambda }B^{\alpha \beta \gamma }{}_{\delta \epsilon \cdots \lambda \mu \nu \cdots \zeta }C^{\mu \nu \cdots \zeta }\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8147a102c1e44f90d4fc7d00507d039ec893e687)
When using multi-index notation, an underarrow is placed underneath the block of indices:[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#cite_note-12)

![Image 13: {\displaystyle A_{\underset {\rightharpoondown }{P}}{}^{\underset {\rightharpoondown }{Q}}B^{P}{}_{Q{\underset {\rightharpoondown }{R}}}C^{R}=\sum _{\underset {\rightharpoondown }{P}}\sum _{\underset {\rightharpoondown }{Q}}\sum _{\underset {\rightharpoondown }{R}}A_{P}{}^{Q}B^{P}{}_{QR}C^{R}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb1fc534ca3c2589ea80ce6b8dfa7e5a3d0c19aa)
where

![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\underset {\rightharpoondown }{P}}=|\alpha \beta \gamma |\,,\quad {\underset {\rightharpoondown }{Q}}=|\delta \epsilon \cdots \lambda |\,,\quad {\underset {\rightharpoondown }{R}}=|\mu \nu \cdots \zeta |}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8108aa73020edce1c9f915cb74516fa7a088fd8e)
By contracting an index with a non-singular [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor"), the [type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed_tensor "Mixed tensor") of a tensor can be changed, converting a lower index to an upper index or vice versa:

![Image 15: {\displaystyle B^{\gamma }{}_{\beta \cdots }=g^{\gamma \alpha }A_{\alpha \beta \cdots }\quad {\text{and}}\quad A_{\alpha \beta \cdots }=g_{\alpha \gamma }B^{\gamma }{}_{\beta \cdots }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db9ddafe12943f4d1b6947a74d80c1da87cca61e)
The base symbol in many cases is retained (e.g. using _A_ where _B_ appears here), and when there is no ambiguity, repositioning an index may be taken to imply this operation.

General outlines for index notation and operations
--------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ricci_calculus&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: General outlines for index notation and operations")]

Tensors are equal [if and only if](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If_and_only_if "If and only if") every corresponding component is equal; e.g., tensor _A_ equals tensor _B_ if and only if

![Image 22: {\displaystyle A^{\alpha }{}_{\beta \gamma }=B^{\alpha }{}_{\beta \gamma }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1347f27ad3f717d458dbf326822ba0ea17af2d9a)
for all _α_, _β_, _γ_. Consequently, there are facets of the notation that are useful in checking that an equation makes sense (an analogous procedure to [dimensional analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensional_analysis "Dimensional analysis")).

Indices not involved in contractions are called _free indices_. Indices used in contractions are termed _dummy indices_, or _summation indices_.

### A tensor equation represents many ordinary (real-valued) equations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ricci_calculus&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: A tensor equation represents many ordinary (real-valued) equations")]

The components of tensors (like _A α_, _B β γ_ etc.) are just real numbers. Since the indices take various integer values to select specific components of the tensors, a single tensor equation represents many ordinary equations. If a tensor equality has _n_ free indices, and if the dimensionality of the underlying vector space is _m_, the equality represents _m n_ equations: each index takes on every value of a specific set of values.

For instance, if

![Image 23: {\displaystyle A^{\alpha }B_{\beta }{}^{\gamma }C_{\gamma \delta }+D^{\alpha }{}_{\beta }{}E_{\delta }=T^{\alpha }{}_{\beta }{}_{\delta }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/67679cf50bf310b7f95ba86f39046cbacd7fe10b)
is in [four dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-dimensional_space "Four-dimensional space") (that is, each index runs from 0 to 3 or from 1 to 4), then because there are three free indices (_α_, _β_, _δ_), there are 4 3 = 64 equations. Three of these are:

![Image 24: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}A^{0}B_{1}{}^{0}C_{00}+A^{0}B_{1}{}^{1}C_{10}+A^{0}B_{1}{}^{2}C_{20}+A^{0}B_{1}{}^{3}C_{30}+D^{0}{}_{1}{}E_{0}&=T^{0}{}_{1}{}_{0}\\A^{1}B_{0}{}^{0}C_{00}+A^{1}B_{0}{}^{1}C_{10}+A^{1}B_{0}{}^{2}C_{20}+A^{1}B_{0}{}^{3}C_{30}+D^{1}{}_{0}{}E_{0}&=T^{1}{}_{0}{}_{0}\\A^{1}B_{2}{}^{0}C_{02}+A^{1}B_{2}{}^{1}C_{12}+A^{1}B_{2}{}^{2}C_{22}+A^{1}B_{2}{}^{3}C_{32}+D^{1}{}_{2}{}E_{2}&=T^{1}{}_{2}{}_{2}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abcec97cacb5a4f893b81e9ae6818834c9a22252)
This illustrates the compactness and efficiency of using index notation: many equations which all share a similar structure can be collected into one simple tensor equation.

### Indices are replaceable labels

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ricci_calculus&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Indices are replaceable labels")]

Replacing any index symbol throughout by another leaves the tensor equation unchanged (provided there is no conflict with other symbols already used). This can be useful when manipulating indices, such as using index notation to verify [vector calculus identities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus_identities "Vector calculus identities") or identities of the [Kronecker delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta "Kronecker delta") and [Levi-Civita symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#Properties "Levi-Civita symbol") (see also below). An example of a correct change is:

![Image 25: {\displaystyle A^{\alpha }B_{\beta }{}^{\gamma }C_{\gamma \delta }+D^{\alpha }{}_{\beta }{}E_{\delta }\rightarrow A^{\lambda }B_{\beta }{}^{\mu }C_{\mu \delta }+D^{\lambda }{}_{\beta }{}E_{\delta }\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6695460fbd579d937bf76b8d32e8fbf7ebf2d630)
whereas an erroneous change is:

![Image 26: {\displaystyle A^{\alpha }B_{\beta }{}^{\gamma }C_{\gamma \delta }+D^{\alpha }{}_{\beta }{}E_{\delta }\nrightarrow A^{\lambda }B_{\beta }{}^{\gamma }C_{\mu \delta }+D^{\alpha }{}_{\beta }{}E_{\delta }\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/102a4bae4102a8ed0d61eaced13e0146465f103c)
In the first replacement, _λ_ replaced _α_ and _μ_ replaced _γ_ _everywhere_, so the expression still has the same meaning. In the second, _λ_ did not fully replace _α_, and _μ_ did not fully replace _γ_ (incidentally, the contraction on the _γ_ index became a tensor product), which is entirely inconsistent for reasons shown next.

### Indices are the same in every term

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ricci_calculus&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Indices are the same in every term")]

The free indices in a tensor expression always appear in the same (upper or lower) position throughout every term, and in a tensor equation the free indices are the same on each side. Dummy indices (which implies a summation over that index) need not be the same, for example:

![Image 27: {\displaystyle A^{\alpha }B_{\beta }{}^{\gamma }C_{\gamma \delta }+D^{\alpha }{}_{\delta }E_{\beta }=T^{\alpha }{}_{\beta }{}_{\delta }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3bf681a1b966d47e87b5996d98812c1a90f10197)
as for an erroneous expression:

![Image 28: {\displaystyle A^{\alpha }B_{\beta }{}^{\gamma }C_{\gamma \delta }+D_{\alpha }{}_{\beta }{}^{\gamma }E^{\delta }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f83dc742b7ad539824026d36300e51d2f75eb658)
In other words, non-repeated indices must be of the same type in every term of the equation. In the above identity, _α_, _β_, _δ_ line up throughout and _γ_ occurs twice in one term due to a contraction (once as an upper index and once as a lower index), and thus it is a valid expression. In the invalid expression, while _β_ lines up, _α_ and _δ_ do not, and _γ_ appears twice in one term (contraction) _and_ once in another term, which is inconsistent.

### Brackets and punctuation used once where implied

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ricci_calculus&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Brackets and punctuation used once where implied")]

When applying a rule to a number of indices (differentiation, symmetrization etc., shown next), the bracket or punctuation symbols denoting the rules are only shown on one group of the indices to which they apply.

If the brackets enclose _covariant indices_ – the rule applies only to _all covariant indices enclosed in the brackets_, not to any contravariant indices which happen to be placed intermediately between the brackets.

Similarly if brackets enclose _contravariant indices_ – the rule applies only to _all enclosed contravariant indices_, not to intermediately placed covariant indices.

Symmetric and antisymmetric parts
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ricci_calculus&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Symmetric and antisymmetric parts")]

[Parentheses, ()](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#Parentheses "Bracket"), around multiple indices denotes the symmetrized part of the tensor. When symmetrizing _p_ indices using _σ_ to range over permutations of the numbers 1 to _p_, one takes a sum over the [permutations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permutation "Permutation") of those indices _α_ _σ_(_i_) for _i_ = 1, 2, 3, ..., _p_, and then divides by the number of permutations:

![Image 29: {\displaystyle A_{(\alpha _{1}\alpha _{2}\cdots \alpha _{p})\alpha _{p+1}\cdots \alpha _{q}}={\dfrac {1}{p!}}\sum _{\sigma }A_{\alpha _{\sigma (1)}\cdots \alpha _{\sigma (p)}\alpha _{p+1}\cdots \alpha _{q}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1921f3053c5b9dfc3a4094cd441de6f996a51d67)
For example, two symmetrizing indices mean there are two indices to permute and sum over:

![Image 30: {\displaystyle A_{(\alpha \beta )\gamma \cdots }={\dfrac {1}{2!}}\left(A_{\alpha \beta \gamma \cdots }+A_{\beta \alpha \gamma \cdots }\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4def7d4f14408ffde9b0a964dc3e9a52f2eb638a)
while for three symmetrizing indices, there are three indices to sum over and permute:

![Image 31: {\displaystyle A_{(\alpha \beta \gamma )\delta \cdots }={\dfrac {1}{3!}}\left(A_{\alpha \beta \gamma \delta \cdots }+A_{\gamma \alpha \beta \delta \cdots }+A_{\beta \gamma \alpha \delta \cdots }+A_{\alpha \gamma \beta \delta \cdots }+A_{\gamma \beta \alpha \delta \cdots }+A_{\beta \alpha \gamma \delta \cdots }\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6fbd68033f6e8a2295a841344c8b1dbda54331f)
The symmetrization is [distributive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributive_property "Distributive property") over addition;

![Image 32: {\displaystyle A_{(\alpha }\left(B_{\beta )\gamma \cdots }+C_{\beta )\gamma \cdots }\right)=A_{(\alpha }B_{\beta )\gamma \cdots }+A_{(\alpha }C_{\beta )\gamma \cdots }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3a9517b75ed561d6c6f635b9fab0096732e783b)
Indices are not part of the symmetrization when they are:

*   not on the same level, for example; ![Image 33: {\displaystyle A_{(\alpha }B^{\beta }{}_{\gamma )}={\dfrac {1}{2!}}\left(A_{\alpha }B^{\beta }{}_{\gamma }+A_{\gamma }B^{\beta }{}_{\alpha }\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de2acbe01e3c86afed50b5174a05b17998dc16f9)
*   within the parentheses and between vertical bars (i.e. |⋅⋅⋅|), modifying the previous example; ![Image 34: {\displaystyle A_{(\alpha }B_{|\beta |}{}_{\gamma )}={\dfrac {1}{2!}}\left(A_{\alpha }B_{\beta \gamma }+A_{\gamma }B_{\beta \alpha }\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3a1b1d8bf35c92ec5a2bbb40b7e1e9e77f919bc)

Here the _α_ and _γ_ indices are symmetrized, _β_ is not.

[Square brackets, []](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#Square_brackets "Bracket"), around multiple indices denotes the _anti_ symmetrized part of the tensor. For _p_ antisymmetrizing indices – the sum over the permutations of those indices _α_ _σ_(_i_) multiplied by the [signature of the permutation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signature_(permutation) "Signature (permutation)")sgn(_σ_) is taken, then divided by the number of permutations:

![Image 35: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}&A_{[\alpha _{1}\cdots \alpha _{p}]\alpha _{p+1}\cdots \alpha _{q}}\\[3pt]={}&{\dfrac {1}{p!}}\sum _{\sigma }\operatorname {sgn}(\sigma )A_{\alpha _{\sigma (1)}\cdots \alpha _{\sigma (p)}\alpha _{p+1}\cdots \alpha _{q}}\\={}&\delta _{\alpha _{1}\cdots \alpha _{p}}^{\beta _{1}\dots \beta _{p}}A_{\beta _{1}\cdots \beta _{p}\alpha _{p+1}\cdots \alpha _{q}}\\\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d1ed3a3d65899f83cceb9f6cc97981f79e26ee8)
where _δ_ _β_ 1⋅⋅⋅_β p_

_α_ 1⋅⋅⋅_α p_ is the [generalized Kronecker delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_Kronecker_delta "Generalized Kronecker delta") of degree 2 _p_, with scaling as defined below.

For example, two antisymmetrizing indices imply:

![Image 36: {\displaystyle A_{[\alpha \beta ]\gamma \cdots }={\dfrac {1}{2!}}\left(A_{\alpha \beta \gamma \cdots }-A_{\beta \alpha \gamma \cdots }\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fef2be00b1aa41e860f5f8702aca3b7d5367c2ac)
while three antisymmetrizing indices imply:

![Image 37: {\displaystyle A_{[\alpha \beta \gamma ]\delta \cdots }={\dfrac {1}{3!}}\left(A_{\alpha \beta \gamma \delta \cdots }+A_{\gamma \alpha \beta \delta \cdots }+A_{\beta \gamma \alpha \delta \cdots }-A_{\alpha \gamma \beta \delta \cdots }-A_{\gamma \beta \alpha \delta \cdots }-A_{\beta \alpha \gamma \delta \cdots }\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bed48763fa258288b5223bfca013c8077b73c1cc)
as for a more specific example, if _F_ represents the [electromagnetic tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_tensor "Electromagnetic tensor"), then the equation

![Image 38: {\displaystyle 0=F_{[\alpha \beta ,\gamma ]}={\dfrac {1}{3!}}\left(F_{\alpha \beta ,\gamma }+F_{\gamma \alpha ,\beta }+F_{\beta \gamma ,\alpha }-F_{\beta \alpha ,\gamma }-F_{\alpha \gamma ,\beta }-F_{\gamma \beta ,\alpha }\right)\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7b0ed84d31ca18fe3439603344b790da0d240dd4)
represents [Gauss's law for magnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss%27s_law_for_magnetism "Gauss's law for magnetism") and [Faraday's law of induction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faraday%27s_law_of_induction "Faraday's law of induction").

As before, the antisymmetrization is distributive over addition;

![Image 39: {\displaystyle A_{[\alpha }\left(B_{\beta ]\gamma \cdots }+C_{\beta ]\gamma \cdots }\right)=A_{[\alpha }B_{\beta ]\gamma \cdots }+A_{[\alpha }C_{\beta ]\gamma \cdots }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/36bebf226cf448c16e018ba4c4be7d3c23e8c1d0)
As with symmetrization, indices are not antisymmetrized when they are:

*   not on the same level, for example; ![Image 40: {\displaystyle A_{[\alpha }B^{\beta }{}_{\gamma ]}={\dfrac {1}{2!}}\left(A_{\alpha }B^{\beta }{}_{\gamma }-A_{\gamma }B^{\beta }{}_{\alpha }\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4836ea3af90f4cfd3a577ba3aee7758824dc096)
*   within the square brackets and between vertical bars (i.e. |⋅⋅⋅|), modifying the previous example; ![Image 41: {\displaystyle A_{[\alpha }B_{|\beta |}{}_{\gamma ]}={\dfrac {1}{2!}}\left(A_{\alpha }B_{\beta \gamma }-A_{\gamma }B_{\beta \alpha }\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca6e27819f5c787c31c829d21cefff2a45f1e3ee)

Here the _α_ and _γ_ indices are antisymmetrized, _β_ is not.

### Sum of symmetric and antisymmetric parts

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ricci_calculus&action=edit&section=26 "Edit section: Sum of symmetric and antisymmetric parts")]

Any tensor can be written as the sum of its symmetric and antisymmetric parts on two indices:

![Image 42: {\displaystyle A_{\alpha \beta \gamma \cdots }=A_{(\alpha \beta )\gamma \cdots }+A_{[\alpha \beta ]\gamma \cdots }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c77f98f8ba96ab296be8245ee3b8655edb17f9e)
as can be seen by adding the above expressions for _A_(_αβ_)_γ_⋅⋅⋅ and _A_[_αβ_]_γ_⋅⋅⋅. This does not hold for other than two indices.

For compactness, derivatives may be indicated by adding indices after a comma or semicolon.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#cite_note-14)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#cite_note-15)

While most of the expressions of the Ricci calculus are valid for arbitrary bases, the expressions involving partial derivatives of tensor components with respect to coordinates apply only with a [coordinate basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_basis "Coordinate basis"): a basis that is defined through differentiation with respect to the coordinates. Coordinates are typically denoted by _x_ _μ_, but do not in general form the components of a vector. In flat spacetime with linear coordinatization, a tuple of _differences_ in coordinates, Δ _x_ _μ_, can be treated as a contravariant vector. With the same constraints on the space and on the choice of coordinate system, the partial derivatives with respect to the coordinates yield a result that is effectively covariant. Aside from use in this special case, the partial derivatives of components of tensors do not in general transform covariantly, but are useful in building expressions that are covariant, albeit still with a coordinate basis if the partial derivatives are explicitly used, as with the covariant, exterior and Lie derivatives below.

To indicate partial differentiation of the components of a tensor field with respect to a coordinate variable _x_ _γ_, a _[comma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comma "Comma")_ is placed before an appended lower index of the coordinate variable.

![Image 43: {\displaystyle A_{\alpha \beta \cdots ,\gamma }={\dfrac {\partial }{\partial x^{\gamma }}}A_{\alpha \beta \cdots }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b63efa701ee57d909eca3aa31d3830f2a5ad2f55)
This may be repeated (without adding further commas):

![Image 44: {\displaystyle A_{\alpha _{1}\alpha _{2}\cdots \alpha _{p}\,,\,\alpha _{p+1}\cdots \alpha _{q}}={\dfrac {\partial }{\partial x^{\alpha _{q}}}}\cdots {\dfrac {\partial }{\partial x^{\alpha _{p+2}}}}{\dfrac {\partial }{\partial x^{\alpha _{p+1}}}}A_{\alpha _{1}\alpha _{2}\cdots \alpha _{p}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/17f53a721623a4a3ddbf0d164ce2b94cb666f9a5)
These components do _not_ transform covariantly, unless the expression being differentiated is a scalar. This derivative is characterized by the [product rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_rule "Product rule") and the derivatives of the coordinates

![Image 45: {\displaystyle x^{\alpha }{}_{,\gamma }=\delta _{\gamma }^{\alpha },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ebc66507054568a40df772a5802fb11052335369)
where _δ_ is the [Kronecker delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta "Kronecker delta").

The covariant derivative is only defined if a [connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(mathematics) "Connection (mathematics)") is defined. For any tensor field, a _[semicolon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semicolon "Semicolon")_ (; ) placed before an appended lower (covariant) index indicates covariant differentiation. Less common alternatives to the semicolon include a _[forward slash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slash\_(punctuation) "Slash (punctuation)")_ ( / )[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#cite_note-16) or in three-dimensional curved space a single vertical bar (|).[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#cite_note-17)

The covariant derivative of a scalar function, a contravariant vector and a covariant vector are:

![Image 46: {\displaystyle f_{;\beta }=f_{,\beta }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/451b7dd246ff7d71812c5982aeada009d110b57f)![Image 47: {\displaystyle A^{\alpha }{}_{;\beta }=A^{\alpha }{}_{,\beta }+\Gamma ^{\alpha }{}_{\gamma \beta }A^{\gamma }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e991bea1ccd05dc2c1608f58deb3e73a8bc8fe6b)![Image 48: {\displaystyle A_{\alpha ;\beta }=A_{\alpha ,\beta }-\Gamma ^{\gamma }{}_{\alpha \beta }A_{\gamma }\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3cbd0594dd68956d26487450bc9bbe6a77d36a8a)
where Γ _α γβ_ are the connection coefficients.

For an arbitrary tensor:[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#cite_note-18)

![Image 49: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}T^{\alpha _{1}\cdots \alpha _{r}}{}_{\beta _{1}\cdots \beta _{s};\gamma }&\\=T^{\alpha _{1}\cdots \alpha _{r}}{}_{\beta _{1}\cdots \beta _{s},\gamma }&+\,\Gamma ^{\alpha _{1}}{}_{\delta \gamma }T^{\delta \alpha _{2}\cdots \alpha _{r}}{}_{\beta _{1}\cdots \beta _{s}}+\cdots +\Gamma ^{\alpha _{r}}{}_{\delta \gamma }T^{\alpha _{1}\cdots \alpha _{r-1}\delta }{}_{\beta _{1}\cdots \beta _{s}}\\&-\,\Gamma ^{\delta }{}_{\beta _{1}\gamma }T^{\alpha _{1}\cdots \alpha _{r}}{}_{\delta \beta _{2}\cdots \beta _{s}}-\cdots -\Gamma ^{\delta }{}_{\beta _{s}\gamma }T^{\alpha _{1}\cdots \alpha _{r}}{}_{\beta _{1}\cdots \beta _{s-1}\delta }\,.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed190b0581f2ee5d90837d8cf6bfd954327ecc41)
An alternative notation for the covariant derivative of any tensor is the subscripted nabla symbol ∇_β_. For the case of a vector field _A α_:[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#cite_note-19)

![Image 50: {\displaystyle \nabla _{\beta }A^{\alpha }=A^{\alpha }{}_{;\beta }\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e83fe0b5ce22298ae3cf6ca15d3cf6f54b9fad6)
The covariant formulation of the [directional derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directional_derivative "Directional derivative") of any tensor field along a vector _v γ_ may be expressed as its contraction with the covariant derivative, e.g.:

![Image 51: {\displaystyle v^{\gamma }A_{\alpha ;\gamma }\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89ad8a802177e47998545e7394cac007149a4857)
The components of this derivative of a tensor field transform covariantly, and hence form another tensor field, despite subexpressions (the partial derivative and the connection coefficients) separately not transforming covariantly.

This derivative is characterized by the product rule:

![Image 52: {\displaystyle (A^{\alpha }{}_{\beta \cdots }B^{\gamma }{}_{\delta \cdots })_{;\epsilon }=A^{\alpha }{}_{\beta \cdots ;\epsilon }B^{\gamma }{}_{\delta \cdots }+A^{\alpha }{}_{\beta \cdots }B^{\gamma }{}_{\delta \cdots ;\epsilon }\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ece0f3a884124b77e562288c47edc804e891ffa0)
A [Koszul connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koszul_connection "Koszul connection") on the [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle") of a [differentiable manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold") is called an [affine connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_connection "Affine connection").

A connection is a [metric connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_connection "Metric connection") when the covariant derivative of the metric tensor vanishes:

![Image 53: {\displaystyle g_{\mu \nu ;\xi }=0\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50e12fa29f0b0034072cde3086999e5f3757d42e)
An [affine connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_connection "Affine connection") that is also a metric connection is called a [Riemannian connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_connection "Riemannian connection"). A Riemannian connection that is torsion-free (i.e., for which the [torsion tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_tensor "Torsion tensor") vanishes: _T_ _α_ _βγ_ = 0) is a [Levi-Civita connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection "Levi-Civita connection").

The Γ _α_ _βγ_ for a Levi-Civita connection in a coordinate basis are called [Christoffel symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christoffel_symbols "Christoffel symbols") of the second kind.

The exterior derivative of a totally antisymmetric type (0, _s_) tensor field with components _A_ _α_ 1⋅⋅⋅_α_ _s_ (also called a [differential form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form")) is a derivative that is covariant under basis transformations. It does not depend on either a metric tensor or a connection: it requires only the structure of a differentiable manifold. In a coordinate basis, it may be expressed as the antisymmetrization of the partial derivatives of the tensor components:[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#cite_note-20): 232–233

![Image 54: {\displaystyle (\mathrm {d} A)_{\gamma \alpha _{1}\cdots \alpha _{s}}={\frac {\partial }{\partial x^{[\gamma }}}A_{\alpha _{1}\cdots \alpha _{s}]}=A_{[\alpha _{1}\cdots \alpha _{s},\gamma ]}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71004231b37834cf42c9a1f6327be680986e7502)
This derivative is not defined on any tensor field with contravariant indices or that is not totally antisymmetric. It is characterized by a graded product rule.

The Lie derivative is another derivative that is covariant under basis transformations. Like the exterior derivative, it does not depend on either a metric tensor or a connection. The Lie derivative of a type (_r_, _s_) tensor field _T_ along (the flow of) a contravariant vector field _X_ _ρ_[may be expressed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_derivative#Coordinate_expressions "Lie derivative") using a coordinate basis as[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#cite_note-21)

![Image 55: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}({\mathcal {L}}_{X}T)^{\alpha _{1}\cdots \alpha _{r}}{}_{\beta _{1}\cdots \beta _{s}}&\\=X^{\gamma }T^{\alpha _{1}\cdots \alpha _{r}}{}_{\beta _{1}\cdots \beta _{s},\gamma }&-\,X^{\alpha _{1}}{}_{,\gamma }T^{\gamma \alpha _{2}\cdots \alpha _{r}}{}_{\beta _{1}\cdots \beta _{s}}-\cdots -X^{\alpha _{r}}{}_{,\gamma }T^{\alpha _{1}\cdots \alpha _{r-1}\gamma }{}_{\beta _{1}\cdots \beta _{s}}\\&+\,X^{\gamma }{}_{,\beta _{1}}T^{\alpha _{1}\cdots \alpha _{r}}{}_{\gamma \beta _{2}\cdots \beta _{s}}+\cdots +X^{\gamma }{}_{,\beta _{s}}T^{\alpha _{1}\cdots \alpha _{r}}{}_{\beta _{1}\cdots \beta _{s-1}\gamma }\,.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b515590c0963e87352663fa6974e83f921e45535)
This derivative is characterized by the product rule and the fact that the Lie derivative of a contravariant vector field along itself is zero:

![Image 56: {\displaystyle ({\mathcal {L}}_{X}X)^{\alpha }=X^{\gamma }X^{\alpha }{}_{,\gamma }-X^{\alpha }{}_{,\gamma }X^{\gamma }=0\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50860fb9d8caa4ede85e8df454ae884e06272977)
The Kronecker delta is like the [identity matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_matrix "Identity matrix") when multiplied and contracted:

![Image 57: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\delta _{\beta }^{\alpha }\,A^{\beta }&=A^{\alpha }\\\delta _{\nu }^{\mu }\,B_{\mu }&=B_{\nu }.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7bb8b9a602a84392bd4c5f8d06254f241363b351)
The components _δ_ _α_

_β_ are the same in any basis and form an invariant tensor of type (1, 1), i.e. the identity of the [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle") over the [identity mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_mapping "Identity mapping") of the [base manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_manifold "Base manifold"), and so its trace is an invariant.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#cite_note-22) Its [trace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trace_(linear_algebra) "Trace (linear algebra)") is the dimensionality of the space; for example, in four-dimensional [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime"),

![Image 58: {\displaystyle \delta _{\rho }^{\rho }=\delta _{0}^{0}+\delta _{1}^{1}+\delta _{2}^{2}+\delta _{3}^{3}=4.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f5473576b6ecf375e4f09bdb32d4a89025758b5)
The Kronecker delta is one of the family of generalized Kronecker deltas. The generalized Kronecker delta of degree 2 _p_ may be defined in terms of the Kronecker delta by (a common definition includes an additional multiplier of _p_! on the right):

![Image 59: {\displaystyle \delta _{\beta _{1}\cdots \beta _{p}}^{\alpha _{1}\cdots \alpha _{p}}=\delta _{\beta _{1}}^{[\alpha _{1}}\cdots \delta _{\beta _{p}}^{\alpha _{p}]},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/836fd04ca45b520cd9af075de8d7dbd7f0752750)
and acts as an antisymmetrizer on _p_ indices:

![Image 60: {\displaystyle \delta _{\beta _{1}\cdots \beta _{p}}^{\alpha _{1}\cdots \alpha _{p}}\,A^{\beta _{1}\cdots \beta _{p}}=A^{[\alpha _{1}\cdots \alpha _{p}]}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee3b68e70b8aa0aa35e6023ddfacf8f8b0d51c44)
An affine connection has a torsion tensor _T_ _α_ _βγ_:

![Image 61: {\displaystyle T^{\alpha }{}_{\beta \gamma }=\Gamma ^{\alpha }{}_{\beta \gamma }-\Gamma ^{\alpha }{}_{\gamma \beta }-\gamma ^{\alpha }{}_{\beta \gamma },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d0567772a5f0de399e311ef01fbf40018f7e498)
where _γ_ _α_ _βγ_ are given by the components of the Lie bracket of the local basis, which vanish when it is a coordinate basis.

For a Levi-Civita connection this tensor is defined to be zero, which for a coordinate basis gives the equations

![Image 62: {\displaystyle \Gamma ^{\alpha }{}_{\beta \gamma }=\Gamma ^{\alpha }{}_{\gamma \beta }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/44704229543d421eec76d614dbfd6d664c4dcfb9)
If this tensor is defined as

![Image 63: {\displaystyle R^{\rho }{}_{\sigma \mu \nu }=\Gamma ^{\rho }{}_{\nu \sigma ,\mu }-\Gamma ^{\rho }{}_{\mu \sigma ,\nu }+\Gamma ^{\rho }{}_{\mu \lambda }\Gamma ^{\lambda }{}_{\nu \sigma }-\Gamma ^{\rho }{}_{\nu \lambda }\Gamma ^{\lambda }{}_{\mu \sigma }\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02edb442f0445eccc3d62812562ba73456f21f56)
then it is the [commutator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutator "Commutator") of the covariant derivative with itself:[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#cite_note-23)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#cite_note-24)

![Image 64: {\displaystyle A_{\nu ;\rho \sigma }-A_{\nu ;\sigma \rho }=A_{\beta }R^{\beta }{}_{\nu \rho \sigma }\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9003a6ab21e1c907d39fd2925d98758febea2097)
since the connection is torsionless, which means that the torsion tensor vanishes.

This can be generalized to get the commutator for two covariant derivatives of an arbitrary tensor as follows:

![Image 65: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}T^{\alpha _{1}\cdots \alpha _{r}}{}_{\beta _{1}\cdots \beta _{s};\gamma \delta }&-T^{\alpha _{1}\cdots \alpha _{r}}{}_{\beta _{1}\cdots \beta _{s};\delta \gamma }\\&\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!=-R^{\alpha _{1}}{}_{\rho \gamma \delta }T^{\rho \alpha _{2}\cdots \alpha _{r}}{}_{\beta _{1}\cdots \beta _{s}}-\cdots -R^{\alpha _{r}}{}_{\rho \gamma \delta }T^{\alpha _{1}\cdots \alpha _{r-1}\rho }{}_{\beta _{1}\cdots \beta _{s}}\\&+R^{\sigma }{}_{\beta _{1}\gamma \delta }T^{\alpha _{1}\cdots \alpha _{r}}{}_{\sigma \beta _{2}\cdots \beta _{s}}+\cdots +R^{\sigma }{}_{\beta _{s}\gamma \delta }T^{\alpha _{1}\cdots \alpha _{r}}{}_{\beta _{1}\cdots \beta _{s-1}\sigma }\,\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca0d1fb611338c09353b29a8b4deed5d0975724f)
which are often referred to as the _Ricci identities_.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus#cite_note-25)

The metric tensor _g_ _αβ_ is used for lowering indices and gives the length of any [space-like](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-like "Space-like") curve

![Image 66: {\displaystyle {\text{length}}=\int _{y_{1}}^{y_{2}}{\sqrt {g_{\alpha \beta }{\frac {dx^{\alpha }}{d\gamma }}{\frac {dx^{\beta }}{d\gamma }}}}\,d\gamma \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/98cd6b25c5cb15c72d973b146153c17fc3a4aab3)
where _γ_ is any [smooth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_function "Smooth function")[strictly monotone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monotonic_function "Monotonic function")[parameterization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametrization_(geometry) "Parametrization (geometry)") of the path. It also gives the duration of any [time-like](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time-like "Time-like") curve

![Image 67: {\displaystyle {\text{duration}}=\int _{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}{\sqrt {{\frac {-1}{c^{2}}}g_{\alpha \beta }{\frac {dx^{\alpha }}{d\gamma }}{\frac {dx^{\beta }}{d\gamma }}}}\,d\gamma \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e836952c41e93626144409fe1059c160c5bd6a29)
where _γ_ is any smooth strictly monotone parameterization of the trajectory. See also _[Line element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line\_element "Line element")_.
