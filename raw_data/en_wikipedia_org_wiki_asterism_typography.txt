In [typography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Typography "Typography"), an **asterism**, ⁂, is a typographic symbol consisting of three [asterisks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asterisks "Asterisks") placed in a [triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle "Triangle"), which is used for a variety of purposes. The name originates from the [astronomical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy") term for a [group of stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asterism_(astronomy) "Asterism (astronomy)").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asterism_(typography)#cite_note-Humez-1)

The asterism was originally used as a type of [dinkus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dinkus "Dinkus") in typography, though increasingly rarely.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asterism_(typography)#cite_note-D13-2) It can also be used to mean "untitled" or author or title withheld– as seen, for example, in some editions of _[Album for the Young](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Album\_for\_the\_Young "Album for the Young")_ by composer [Robert Schumann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Schumann "Robert Schumann") ([№](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E2%84%96 "№") 21, 26, and 30).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asterism_(typography)#cite_note-3)

In [meteorology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorology "Meteorology"), an asterism in a [station model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Station_model "Station model") plot indicates moderate snowfall.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asterism_(typography)#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asterism_(typography)#cite_note-5)

Dinkus
------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Asterism_(typography)&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Dinkus")]

Main article: [Dinkus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dinkus "Dinkus")

[![Image 5: Asterisms in use](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/James_Joyce%2C_Ulysses%2C_1ed_2pr%2C_p240.jpg/250px-James_Joyce%2C_Ulysses%2C_1ed_2pr%2C_p240.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:James_Joyce,_Ulysses,_1ed_2pr,_p240.jpg)

Asterisms used as dinkuses in the [James Joyce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Joyce "James Joyce") novel _[Ulysses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ulysses\_(novel) "Ulysses (novel)")_, the "Wandering Rocks" chapter, from the 1922 edition.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asterism_(typography)#cite_note-6) The 1961 edition used a hollow [white star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_(glyph)#Five_points "Star (glyph)") (☆), and the 1984 edition used a row of three asterisks.

A dinkus is a typographical device to divide text, such as at [section breaks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Section_(typography)#Flourished_section_breaks "Section (typography)"). Its purpose is to "indicate minor breaks in text",[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asterism_(typography)#cite_note-7) to call attention to a passage, or to separate sub-chapters in a book. An asterism used this way is thus a type of dinkus: nowadays this usage of the symbol is nearly obsolete.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asterism_(typography)#cite_note-D13-2) More commonly used dinkuses are three [dots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interpunct "Interpunct") or three asterisks in a horizontal row.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asterism_(typography)#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asterism_(typography)#cite_note-Crystal-9)
