The **radian per second** (symbol: **rad⋅s−1** or **rad/s**) is the unit of [angular velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity") in the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") (SI). The [radian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian") per [second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second "Second") is also the SI unit of [angular frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_frequency "Angular frequency") (symbol _ω_, omega). The radian per second is defined as the angular frequency that results in the [angular displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_displacement "Angular displacement") increasing by one radian every second.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian_per_second#cite_note-1)

Relation to other units
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Radian_per_second&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Relation to other units")]

A [frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency") of one [hertz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hertz "Hertz") (1 Hz), or one [cycle per second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cycle_per_second "Cycle per second") (1 cps), corresponds to an angular frequency of 2 π radians per second. This is because one [cycle of rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cycle_(rotational_unit) "Cycle (rotational unit)") corresponds to an [angular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle") rotation of 2 π radians.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian_per_second#cite_note-2)

Since the radian is a [dimensionless unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensionless_unit "Dimensionless unit") in the [SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI "SI"), the radian per second is dimensionally equivalent to the hertz—both can be expressed as [reciprocal seconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reciprocal_seconds "Reciprocal seconds"), s−1. So, context is necessary to specify which [kind of quantity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kind_of_quantity "Kind of quantity") is being expressed, angular frequency or ordinary frequency.

One radian per second also corresponds to about 9.55 [revolutions per minute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolutions_per_minute "Revolutions per minute") (rpm).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian_per_second#cite_note-3)_Degrees per second_ may also be defined, based on [degree of arc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_(angle) "Degrees (angle)"), where 1 degree per second (°/s) is equivalent to ⁠π/180⁠ rad⋅s−1.

Coherent units
--------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Radian_per_second&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Coherent units")]

A use of the unit radian per second is in calculation of the power transmitted by a shaft. In the [International System of Quantities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Quantities "International System of Quantities") (SI) and the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units"), widely used in [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") and [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering"), the power _p_ is equal to the angular speed _ω_ multiplied by the [torque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque")_τ_ applied to the shaft: _p_ = _ω_ ⋅ _τ_. When [coherent units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coherence_(units_of_measurement) "Coherence (units of measurement)") are used for these quantities, which are respectively the [watt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Watt "Watt"), the radian per second, and the [newton-metre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton-metre "Newton-metre"), and thus [W](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Watt "Watt") = rad/s × [N·m](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton-metre "Newton-metre"), no numerical factor needed when performing the numerical calculation. When the units are not coherent (e.g. [horsepower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horsepower "Horsepower"), [turn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turn_(angle) "Turn (angle)")/min, and [pound-foot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pound-foot_(torque) "Pound-foot (torque)")), an additional factor will generally be necessary.
