Chemical compound

**Methylidyne**, or (**unsubstituted**) **carbyne**, is an [organic compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound") whose molecule consists of a single [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") atom [bonded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covalent_bond "Covalent bond") to a [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") atom. It is the parent compound of the [carbynes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbyne "Carbyne"), which can be seen as obtained from it by substitution of other [functional groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_group "Functional group") for the hydrogen.

The carbon atom is left with either one or three unpaired [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") (unsatisfied [valence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valence_(chemistry) "Valence (chemistry)") bonds), depending on the molecule's [excitation state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excited_state "Excited state"); making it a [radical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_(chemistry) "Radical (chemistry)"). Accordingly, the [chemical formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula") can be CH• or CH 3• (also written as **⫶**CH); each dot representing an unpaired electron. The corresponding systematic names are **methylidyne** or **hydridocarbon(•)**, and **methanetriyl** or **hydridocarbon(3•)**. However, the formula is often written simply as CH.

Methylidyne is a highly [reactive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_reaction "Chemical reaction") gas that is quickly destroyed in ordinary conditions but is abundant in the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium") (and was one of the first [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule") to be detected there).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylidyne_radical#cite_note-4)

Nomenclature
------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methylidyne_radical&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Nomenclature")]

The [trivial name](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trivial_name "Trivial name")_carbyne_ is the [preferred IUPAC name](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preferred_IUPAC_name "Preferred IUPAC name").

Following the substitutive nomenclature, the molecule is viewed as methane with three hydrogen atoms removed, yielding the systematic name "methylidyne".

Following the additive nomenclature, the molecule is viewed as a hydrogen atom bonded to a carbon atom, yielding the name "hydridocarbon".

By default, these names pay no regard to the excitation state of the molecule. When that attribute is considered, the states with one unpaired electron are named "methylylidene" or "hydridocarbon(•)", whereas the excited states with three unpaired electrons are named "methanetriyl" or "hydridocarbon(3•)".

Occurrence and reactivity
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methylidyne_radical&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Occurrence and reactivity")]

### Fischer–Tropsch intermediate

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methylidyne_radical&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Fischer–Tropsch intermediate")]

Methylidyne-like species are implied intermediates in the [Fischer–Tropsch process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fischer%E2%80%93Tropsch_process "Fischer–Tropsch process"), the [hydrogenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogenation "Hydrogenation") of CO to produce [hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon"). Methylidyne entities are assumed to bond to the [catalyst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalyst "Catalyst")'s surface. A hypothetical sequence is:[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylidyne_radical#cite_note-Nestle1980-6)

M _n_ CO + ⁠1/2⁠ H 2 → M n COH M _n_ COH + H 2 → M n CH + H 2 O M _n_ CH + ⁠1/2⁠ H 2 → M _n_ CH 2
The M _n_ CH intermediate has a tridentate methylidine ligand. The [methylene ligand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transition_metal_carbene_complex "Transition metal carbene complex") (H 2 C) is then poised couple to CO or to another methylene, thereby growing the C–C chain.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylidyne_radical#cite_note-7)

### Amphotericity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methylidyne_radical&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Amphotericity")]

The methylylidyne group can exhibit both [Lewis acidic and Lewis basic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lewis_acids_and_bases "Lewis acids and bases") character.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylidyne_radical#cite_note-8) Such behavior is only of theoretical interest since it is not possible to produce methylidyne.

### In interstellar space

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methylidyne_radical&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: In interstellar space")]

In October 2016, astronomers reported that the methylidyne radical **⫶**CH, the carbon-hydrogen positive ion **:**CH+, and the carbon ion **⫶**C+ are the result of [ultraviolet light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet_light "Ultraviolet light") from [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star"), rather than in other ways, such as the result of turbulent events related to [supernovas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova "Supernova") and [young stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Young_star "Young star"), as thought earlier.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylidyne_radical#cite_note-9)

Preparation
-----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methylidyne_radical&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Preparation")]

Methylidyne can be prepared from [bromoform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bromoform "Bromoform").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylidyne_radical#cite_note-Nestle1980-6)
