In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), **kinematics** studies the geometrical aspects of motion of physical objects independent of forces that set them in motion. Constrained motion such as linked machine parts are also described as kinematics.

Kinematics is concerned with systems of specification of objects' positions and velocities and mathematical transformations between such systems. These systems may be rectangular like [Cartesian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system"), [Curvilinear coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates "Curvilinear coordinates") like [polar coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates "Polar coordinates") or other systems. The object trajectories may be specified with respect to other objects which may themselves be in motion relative to a standard reference. Rotating systems may also be used.

Numerous practical problems in kinematics involve constraints, such as mechanical linkages, ropes, or rolling disks.

**Kinematics** is a subfield of [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") and [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), developed in [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics"), that describes the [motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion "Motion") of points, [bodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_object "Physical object") (objects), and systems of bodies (groups of objects) without considering the [forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force") that cause them to move.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-Whittaker-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-Beggs-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-Wright-3) Kinematics differs from [_dynamics_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamics_(mechanics) "Dynamics (mechanics)") (also known as [_kinetics_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetics_(physics) "Kinetics (physics)")) which studies the effect of forces on bodies.

Kinematics, as a field of study, is often referred to as the "geometry of motion" and is occasionally seen as a branch of both applied and pure [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") since it can be studied without considering the mass of a body or the forces acting upon it.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-6) A kinematics problem begins by describing the geometry of the system and declaring the [initial conditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Initial_conditions "Initial conditions") of any known values of position, velocity and/or acceleration of points within the system. Then, using arguments from geometry, the position, velocity and acceleration of any unknown parts of the system can be determined. In his work _Space and its Nature_, the scholar [Ibn al-Haytham](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibn_al-Haytham "Ibn al-Haytham") is credited with being the first to treat geometry and kinematics as a unified concept. To quantify the properties of space, he compared the dimensions of a body when it was in motion versus when it was at rest.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-7)

Another way to describe kinematics is as the specification of the possible states of a physical system. Dynamics then describes the evolution of a system through such states. [Robert Spekkens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Spekkens "Robert Spekkens") argues that this division cannot be empirically tests and thus has no physical basis.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-8)

Kinematics is used in [astrophysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysics "Astrophysics") to describe the motion of [celestial bodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_bodies "Celestial bodies") and collections of such bodies. In [mechanical engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_engineering "Mechanical engineering"), [robotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robotics "Robotics"), and [biomechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomechanics "Biomechanics"),[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-Biewener-9) kinematics is used to describe the motion of systems composed of joined parts (multi-link systems) such as an [engine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engine "Engine"), a [robotic arm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robot_kinematics "Robot kinematics") or the [human skeleton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_skeleton "Human skeleton").

[Geometric transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_transformation "Geometric transformation"), including so-called [rigid transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_transformation "Rigid transformation"), are used to describe the movement of components in a [mechanical system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_system "Mechanical system"), simplifying the derivation of the equations of motion. They are also central to [dynamic analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics").

[Kinematic analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robot_kinematics "Robot kinematics") is the process of measuring the kinematic [quantities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_quantity "Physical quantity") used to describe motion. In engineering, for instance, kinematic analysis may be used to find the range of movement for a given [mechanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanism_(engineering) "Mechanism (engineering)") and, working in reverse, using [kinematic synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burmester_theory "Burmester theory") to design a mechanism for a desired range of motion.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-McCarthy2010-10) In addition, kinematics applies [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry") to the study of the [mechanical advantage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_advantage "Mechanical advantage") of a [mechanical system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_system "Mechanical system") or mechanism.

[Relativistic kinematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_mechanics#Relativistic_kinematics "Relativistic mechanics") applies the [special theory of relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_theory_of_relativity "Special theory of relativity") to the geometry of object motion. It encompasses [time dilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_dilation "Time dilation"), [length contraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length_contraction "Length contraction") and the [Lorentz transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_transformation "Lorentz transformation").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-11): 12.8  The kinematics of relativity operates in a [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") geometry where spatial points are augmented with a time coordinate to form [4-vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-vectors "Four-vectors").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-12): 221

[Werner Heisenberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg "Werner Heisenberg") reinterpreted classical kinetics for quantum systems in his 1925 paper ["On the quantum-theoretical reinterpretation of kinematical and mechanical relationships"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umdeutung_paper "Umdeutung paper").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-13) Dirac noted the similarity in structure between Heisenberg's formulations and classical [Poisson brackets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poisson_brackets "Poisson brackets").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-14): 143  In a follow up paper in 1927 Heisenberg showed that classical kinematic notions like velocity and energy are valid in quantum mechanics, but pairs of conjugate kinematic and dynamic quantities cannot be simultaneously measure, a result he called indeterminacy, but which became known as the [uncertainty principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncertainty_principle "Uncertainty principle").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-15)

The term kinematic is the English version of [A.M. Ampère](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andr%C3%A9-Marie_Amp%C3%A8re "André-Marie Ampère")'s _cinématique_,[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-16) which he constructed from the [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language")κίνημα _kinema_ ("movement, motion"), itself derived from κινεῖν _kinein_ ("to move").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-Bottema-18)

Kinematic and cinématique are related to the French word cinéma, but neither are directly derived from it. However, they do share a root word in common, as cinéma came from the shortened form of cinématographe, "motion picture projector and camera", once again from the Greek word for movement and from the Greek γρᾰ́φω _grapho_ ("to write").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-19)

Particle kinematics is the study of the trajectory of particles. The position of a particle is defined as the coordinate vector from the origin of a coordinate frame to the particle. For example, consider a tower 50 m south from your home, where the coordinate frame is centered at your home, such that east is in the direction of the _x_-axis and north is in the direction of the _y_-axis, then the coordinate vector to the base of the tower is **r** = (0 m, −50 m, 0 m). If the tower is 50 m high, and this height is measured along the _z_-axis, then the coordinate vector to the top of the tower is **r** = (0 m, −50 m, 50 m).

In the most general case, a three-dimensional coordinate system is used to define the position of a particle. However, if the particle is constrained to move within a plane, a two-dimensional coordinate system is sufficient. All observations in physics are incomplete without being described with respect to a reference frame.

The position vector of a particle is a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") drawn from the origin of the [reference frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reference_frame "Reference frame") to the particle. It expresses both the distance of the point from the origin and its direction from the origin. In three dimensions, the position vector ![Image 5: {\displaystyle {\bf {r}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee502367ef30806b379d23d38cb6cb250b712f30) can be expressed as ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} =(x,y,z)=x{\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+y{\hat {\mathbf {y} }}+z{\hat {\mathbf {z} }},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3c615944b2d729bf4b1795ca6c1321feefb8dc21) where ![Image 7: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4), ![Image 8: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d), and ![Image 9: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98) are the [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates") and ![Image 10: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {x} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a5451354f837d5d89774e1de386b44a903d929d), ![Image 11: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {y} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3826fc591cfe42348f58b15e4c23152f30931ef) and ![Image 12: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {z} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d4717aa1fda3698bf5489269af7a5904c39c07d) are the [unit vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vectors "Unit vectors") along the ![Image 13: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4), ![Image 14: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d), and ![Image 15: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98) coordinate axes, respectively. The magnitude of the position vector ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \left|\mathbf {r} \right|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e8cceeadf87ea574efdd71a4deeffa1191f0a06) gives the distance between the point ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1) and the origin. ![Image 18: {\displaystyle |\mathbf {r} |={\sqrt {x^{2}+y^{2}+z^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d680889aafe7cafbd733da0ac82dc54af7db1d7a) The [direction cosines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direction_cosine "Direction cosine") of the position vector provide a quantitative measure of direction. In general, an object's position vector will depend on the frame of reference; different frames will lead to different values for the position vector.

The _trajectory_ of a particle is a vector function of time, ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} (t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7216e69d28fdb11c29c859f015a284c1ec6f06ac), which defines the curve traced by the moving particle, given by ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} (t)=x(t){\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+y(t){\hat {\mathbf {y} }}+z(t){\hat {\mathbf {z} }},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e40842c80bdf481e42e3be033d6ddfda90fc13f) where ![Image 21: {\displaystyle x(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d54c275db3a1e620737b58e143b0818107fa5f5c), ![Image 22: {\displaystyle y(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/397de1edef5bf2ee15c020f325d7d781a3aa7f50), and ![Image 23: {\displaystyle z(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/14f9d8936c28d327f65c390886501cde7ff012a4) describe each coordinate of the particle's position as a function of time.

[![Image 24](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Distancedisplacement.svg/300px-Distancedisplacement.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Distancedisplacement.svg)

The distance travelled is always greater than or equal to the displacement.

The [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") of a particle is a vector quantity that describes the _direction_ as well as the magnitude of motion of the particle. More mathematically, the rate of change of the position vector of a point with respect to time is the velocity of the point. Consider the ratio formed by dividing the difference of two positions of a particle ([displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Displacement_(geometry) "Displacement (geometry)")) by the time interval. This ratio is called the [average velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Average_velocity "Average velocity") over that time interval and is defined as![Image 25: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\bar {v}} ={\frac {\Delta \mathbf {r} }{\Delta t}}={\frac {\Delta x}{\Delta t}}{\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+{\frac {\Delta y}{\Delta t}}{\hat {\mathbf {y} }}+{\frac {\Delta z}{\Delta t}}{\hat {\mathbf {z} }}={\bar {v}}_{x}{\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+{\bar {v}}_{y}{\hat {\mathbf {y} }}+{\bar {v}}_{z}{\hat {\mathbf {z} }}\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27df6f6d8199a3e34ee56bf3f544226c8da57217)where ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \Delta \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2589325234008fec973d471bfcaace3af062fdb6) is the displacement vector during the time interval ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \Delta t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c28867ecd34e2caed12cf38feadf6a81a7ee542). In the limit that the time interval ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \Delta t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c28867ecd34e2caed12cf38feadf6a81a7ee542) approaches zero, the average velocity approaches the instantaneous velocity, defined as the [time derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_derivative "Time derivative") of the position vector, ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} =\lim _{\Delta t\to 0}{\frac {\Delta \mathbf {r} }{\Delta t}}={\frac {{\text{d}}\mathbf {r} }{{\text{d}}t}}=v_{x}{\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+v_{y}{\hat {\mathbf {y} }}+v_{z}{\hat {\mathbf {z} }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e170327d62f0a96330f03db154c23e77bff9f16) Thus, a particle's velocity is the time rate of change of its position. Furthermore, this velocity is [tangent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent "Tangent") to the particle's trajectory at every position along its path. In a non-rotating frame of reference, the derivatives of the coordinate directions are not considered as their directions and magnitudes are constants.

The [speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed "Speed") of an object is the magnitude of its velocity. It is a scalar quantity: ![Image 30: {\displaystyle v=|\mathbf {v} |={\frac {{\text{d}}s}{{\text{d}}t}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cae14c81e56eb1bf7380e1ec7f9de3599766695f) where ![Image 31: {\displaystyle s}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01d131dfd7673938b947072a13a9744fe997e632) is the arc-length measured along the trajectory of the particle. This arc-length must always increase as the particle moves. Hence, ![Image 32: {\displaystyle {\frac {{\text{d}}s}{{\text{d}}t}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/256c01b433eda9690ae2f5a3afe12695bcaf6650) is non-negative, which implies that speed is also non-negative.

The velocity vector can change in magnitude and in direction or both at once. Hence, the acceleration accounts for both the rate of change of the magnitude of the velocity vector and the rate of change of direction of that vector. The same reasoning used with respect to the position of a particle to define velocity, can be applied to the velocity to define acceleration. The [acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration") of a particle is the vector defined by the rate of change of the velocity vector. The [average acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Average_acceleration "Average acceleration") of a particle over a time interval is defined as the ratio. ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\bar {a}} ={\frac {\Delta \mathbf {\bar {v}} }{\Delta t}}={\frac {\Delta {\bar {v}}_{x}}{\Delta t}}{\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+{\frac {\Delta {\bar {v}}_{y}}{\Delta t}}{\hat {\mathbf {y} }}+{\frac {\Delta {\bar {v}}_{z}}{\Delta t}}{\hat {\mathbf {z} }}={\bar {a}}_{x}{\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+{\bar {a}}_{y}{\hat {\mathbf {y} }}+{\bar {a}}_{z}{\hat {\mathbf {z} }}\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca218a219a92654a212ee7dafe8a1ec58c610d9f) where Δ**v** is the average velocity and Δ _t_ is the time interval.

The acceleration of the particle is the limit of the average acceleration as the time interval approaches zero, which is the time derivative, ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} =\lim _{\Delta t\to 0}{\frac {\Delta \mathbf {v} }{\Delta t}}={\frac {{\text{d}}\mathbf {v} }{{\text{d}}t}}=a_{x}{\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+a_{y}{\hat {\mathbf {y} }}+a_{z}{\hat {\mathbf {z} }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/756adf468916f7cfab35d18fba8d2e437f341614)

Alternatively, ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} =\lim _{(\Delta t)^{2}\to 0}{\frac {\Delta \mathbf {r} }{(\Delta t)^{2}}}={\frac {{\text{d}}^{2}\mathbf {r} }{{\text{d}}t^{2}}}=a_{x}{\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+a_{y}{\hat {\mathbf {y} }}+a_{z}{\hat {\mathbf {z} }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a2ce36949523601bf48456efa5e234e45c393f8)

Thus, acceleration is the first derivative of the velocity vector and the second derivative of the position vector of that particle. In a non-rotating frame of reference, the derivatives of the coordinate directions are not considered as their directions and magnitudes are constants.

The magnitude of the [acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration") of an object is the magnitude |**a**| of its acceleration vector. It is a scalar quantity: ![Image 36: {\displaystyle |\mathbf {a} |=|{\dot {\mathbf {v} }}|={\frac {{\text{d}}v}{{\text{d}}t}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e9fbab815857ee37662834de1758e0be02b9a0dd)

### Relative position vector

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kinematics&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Relative position vector")]

[A relative position vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Displacement_(vector) "Displacement (vector)") is a vector that defines the position of one point relative to another. It is the difference in position of the two points. The position of one point _A_ relative to another point _B_ is simply the difference between their positions

![Image 37: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} _{A/B}=\mathbf {r} _{A}-\mathbf {r} _{B}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75de85031b07732d2e0eb274d64706231ef4d1a3)
which is the difference between the components of their position vectors.

If point _A_ has position components ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} _{A}=\left(x_{A},y_{A},z_{A}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df99584c323a039b9ebc59c38c1ab4e66a06631f)

and point _B_ has position components ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} _{B}=\left(x_{B},y_{B},z_{B}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4d5e5c721f8d3b4387abe4dae81626fe129bd6b4)

then the position of point _A_ relative to point _B_ is the difference between their components: ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} _{A/B}=\mathbf {r} _{A}-\mathbf {r} _{B}=\left(x_{A}-x_{B},y_{A}-y_{B},z_{A}-z_{B}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3cbef37c36e39b5b140e3423463f36084e9af2a)

[![Image 41](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Relative_velocity.svg/330px-Relative_velocity.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Relative_velocity.svg)

Relative velocities between two particles in classical mechanics.

The velocity of one point relative to another is simply the difference between their velocities ![Image 42: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{A/B}=\mathbf {v} _{A}-\mathbf {v} _{B}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea7c89938d96ec912e05c17a2a5e303f3c80334b) which is the difference between the components of their velocities.

If point _A_ has velocity components ![Image 43: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{A}=\left(v_{A_{x}},v_{A_{y}},v_{A_{z}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23b36dbe216a212287711a4cddd5738201da1496) and point _B_ has velocity components ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{B}=\left(v_{B_{x}},v_{B_{y}},v_{B_{z}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c8b0e5cd776d26d81235607d8a91ec8e8c1b6513) then the velocity of point _A_ relative to point _B_ is the difference between their components: ![Image 45: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{A/B}=\mathbf {v} _{A}-\mathbf {v} _{B}=\left(v_{A_{x}}-v_{B_{x}},v_{A_{y}}-v_{B_{y}},v_{A_{z}}-v_{B_{z}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/800b9a2677c88daea5fffa235d96b81ac2525f46)

Alternatively, this same result could be obtained by computing the time derivative of the relative position vector **r**B/A.

### Relative acceleration

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kinematics&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Relative acceleration")]

The acceleration of one point _C_ relative to another point _B_ is simply the difference between their accelerations. ![Image 46: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} _{C/B}=\mathbf {a} _{C}-\mathbf {a} _{B}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/266fedd01c95aac0a08211413e177d9b992376f9) which is the difference between the components of their accelerations.

Particle trajectories in cylindrical-polar coordinates
------------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kinematics&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Particle trajectories in cylindrical-polar coordinates")]

It is often convenient to formulate the trajectory of a particle **r**(_t_) = (_x_(_t_), _y_(_t_), _z_(_t_)) using polar coordinates in the _X_–_Y_ plane. In this case, its velocity and acceleration take a convenient form.

Recall that the trajectory of a particle _P_ is defined by its coordinate vector **r** measured in a fixed reference frame _F_. As the particle moves, its coordinate vector **r**(_t_) traces its trajectory, which is a curve in space, given by: ![Image 89: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} (t)=x(t){\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+y(t){\hat {\mathbf {y} }}+z(t){\hat {\mathbf {z} }},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e40842c80bdf481e42e3be033d6ddfda90fc13f) where **x̂**, **ŷ**, and **ẑ** are the [unit vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vectors "Unit vectors") along the _x_, _y_ and _z_ axes of the [reference frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system")_F_, respectively.

Consider a particle _P_ that moves only on the surface of a circular cylinder _r_(_t_) = constant, it is possible to align the _z_ axis of the fixed frame _F_ with the axis of the cylinder. Then, the angle _θ_ around this axis in the _x_–_y_ plane can be used to define the trajectory as, ![Image 90: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} (t)=r\cos(\theta (t)){\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+r\sin(\theta (t)){\hat {\mathbf {y} }}+z(t){\hat {\mathbf {z} }},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c366d8bd359c64827e5dc8c63bedb06facf180ea) where the constant distance from the center is denoted as _r_, and _θ_(_t_) is a function of time.

The cylindrical coordinates for **r**(_t_) can be simplified by introducing the radial and tangential unit vectors, ![Image 91: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {r} }}=\cos(\theta (t)){\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+\sin(\theta (t)){\hat {\mathbf {y} }},\quad {\hat {\mathbf {\theta } }}=-\sin(\theta (t)){\hat {\mathbf {x} }}+\cos(\theta (t)){\hat {\mathbf {y} }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d79d684da2d6c2fe12516ba8fc0924dd4fb895b6) and their time derivatives from elementary calculus: ![Image 92: {\displaystyle {\frac {{\text{d}}{\hat {\mathbf {r} }}}{{\text{d}}t}}=\omega {\hat {\mathbf {\theta } }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9212377291608e1141f45f7467f0e46e9f939620)![Image 93: {\displaystyle {\frac {{\text{d}}^{2}{\hat {\mathbf {r} }}}{{\text{d}}t^{2}}}={\frac {{\text{d}}(\omega {\hat {\mathbf {\theta } }})}{{\text{d}}t}}=\alpha {\hat {\mathbf {\theta } }}-\omega ^{2}{\hat {\mathbf {r} }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1cb2e8a0beed8112400a185bf6c08b5fcd9486f3)

![Image 94: {\displaystyle {\frac {{\text{d}}{\hat {\mathbf {\theta } }}}{{\text{d}}t}}=-\omega {\hat {\mathbf {r} }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a469c1a3b5d210665f5dedeeb08d368bfaffcf9b)![Image 95: {\displaystyle {\frac {{\text{d}}^{2}{\hat {\mathbf {\theta } }}}{{\text{d}}t^{2}}}={\frac {{\text{d}}(-\omega {\hat {\mathbf {r} }})}{{\text{d}}t}}=-\alpha {\hat {\mathbf {r} }}-\omega ^{2}{\hat {\mathbf {\theta } }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0898a802ad6f5b89b154f9bb01b11925a68b8ce3)

Using this notation, **r**(_t_) takes the form, ![Image 96: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} (t)=r{\hat {\mathbf {r} }}+z(t){\hat {\mathbf {z} }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0de7c8717561514e8291df82a8a2130078c5473) In general, the trajectory **r**(_t_) is not constrained to lie on a circular cylinder, so the radius _R_ varies with time and the trajectory of the particle in cylindrical-polar coordinates becomes: ![Image 97: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} (t)=r(t){\hat {\mathbf {r} }}+z(t){\hat {\mathbf {z} }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/578cb66a405cec146abaf5bfecb4011e72085100) Where _r_, _θ_, and _z_ might be continuously differentiable functions of time and the function notation is dropped for simplicity. The velocity vector **v**_P_ is the time derivative of the trajectory **r**(_t_), which yields: ![Image 98: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{P}={\frac {\text{d}}{{\text{d}}t}}\left(r{\hat {\mathbf {r} }}+z{\hat {\mathbf {z} }}\right)=v{\hat {\mathbf {r} }}+r\mathbf {\omega } {\hat {\mathbf {\theta } }}+v_{z}{\hat {\mathbf {z} }}=v({\hat {\mathbf {r} }}+{\hat {\mathbf {\theta } }})+v_{z}{\hat {\mathbf {z} }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dc5ac67abaabbf323d19da340aa2386aaf68e70d)

Similarly, the acceleration **a**_P_, which is the time derivative of the velocity **v**_P_, is given by: ![Image 99: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} _{P}={\frac {\text{d}}{{\text{d}}t}}\left(v{\hat {\mathbf {r} }}+v{\hat {\mathbf {\theta } }}+v_{z}{\hat {\mathbf {z} }}\right)=(a-v\omega ){\hat {\mathbf {r} }}+(a+v\omega ){\hat {\mathbf {\theta } }}+a_{z}{\hat {\mathbf {z} }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81bcf18ed55816b96d063951477e252828a099e6)

The term ![Image 100: {\displaystyle -v\omega {\hat {\mathbf {r} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e437366cbde37f32bdf01995c8ea65a2bea9ef69) acts toward the center of curvature of the path at that point on the path, is commonly called the [centripetal acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_acceleration "Centripetal acceleration"). The term ![Image 101: {\displaystyle v\omega {\hat {\mathbf {\theta } }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c23077b7c95720508880351fa377ecf394381abf) is called the [Coriolis acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coriolis_acceleration "Coriolis acceleration").

If the trajectory of the particle is constrained to lie on a cylinder, then the radius _r_ is constant and the velocity and acceleration vectors simplify. The velocity of **v**P is the time derivative of the trajectory **r**(_t_), ![Image 102: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{P}={\frac {\text{d}}{{\text{d}}t}}\left(r{\hat {\mathbf {r} }}+z{\hat {\mathbf {z} }}\right)=r\omega {\hat {\mathbf {\theta } }}+v_{z}{\hat {\mathbf {z} }}=v{\hat {\mathbf {\theta } }}+v_{z}{\hat {\mathbf {z} }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5cd93b2a8039193a61fba02d75616d57031ebc9)

Point trajectories in a body moving in the plane
------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kinematics&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Point trajectories in a body moving in the plane")]

The movement of components of a [mechanical system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_system "Mechanical system") are analyzed by attaching a [reference frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") to each part and determining how the various reference frames move relative to each other. If the structural stiffness of the parts are sufficient, then their deformation can be neglected and rigid transformations can be used to define this relative movement. This reduces the description of the motion of the various parts of a complicated mechanical system to a problem of describing the geometry of each part and geometric association of each part relative to other parts.

[Geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") is the study of the properties of figures that remain the same while the space is transformed in various ways—more technically, it is the study of invariants under a set of transformations.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-25) These transformations can cause the displacement of the triangle in the plane, while leaving the vertex angle and the distances between vertices unchanged. Kinematics is often described as applied geometry, where the movement of a mechanical system is described using the rigid transformations of Euclidean geometry.

The coordinates of points in a plane are two-dimensional vectors in **R**2 (two dimensional space). Rigid transformations are those that preserve the [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_formula "Distance formula") between any two points. The set of rigid transformations in an _n_-dimensional space is called the special [Euclidean group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_group "Euclidean group") on **R**_n_, and denoted [SE(_n_)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SE(n) "SE(n)").

### Displacements and motion

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kinematics&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Displacements and motion")]

[![Image 111: Boulton & Watt Steam Engine](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/SteamEngine_Boulton%26Watt_1784.png/330px-SteamEngine_Boulton%26Watt_1784.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:SteamEngine_Boulton%26Watt_1784.png)

The movement of each of the components of the Boulton & Watt Steam Engine (1784) is modeled by a continuous set of rigid displacements.

The position of one component of a mechanical system relative to another is defined by introducing a reference frame, say _M_, on one that moves relative to a fixed frame, _F,_ on the other. The rigid transformation, or displacement, of _M_ relative to _F_ defines the relative position of the two components. A displacement consists of the combination of a [rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation") and a [translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry) "Translation (geometry)").

The set of all displacements of _M_ relative to _F_ is called the [configuration space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(physics) "Configuration space (physics)") of _M._ A smooth curve from one position to another in this configuration space is a continuous set of displacements, called the [motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion_(physics) "Motion (physics)") of _M_ relative to _F._ The motion of a body consists of a continuous set of rotations and translations.

### Matrix representation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kinematics&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Matrix representation")]

The combination of a rotation and translation in the plane **R**2 can be represented by a certain type of 3×3 matrix known as a homogeneous transform. The 3×3 homogeneous transform is constructed from a 2×2 [rotation matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_matrix "Rotation matrix")_A_(_φ_) and the 2×1 translation vector **d** = (_d x_, _d y_), as: ![Image 112: {\displaystyle [T(\phi ,\mathbf {d} )]={\begin{bmatrix}A(\phi )&\mathbf {d} \\\mathbf {0} &1\end{bmatrix}}={\begin{bmatrix}\cos \phi &-\sin \phi &d_{x}\\\sin \phi &\cos \phi &d_{y}\\0&0&1\end{bmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50078656eb1b2395a2ad84383cd16868c54649b1) These homogeneous transforms perform rigid transformations on the points in the plane _z_ = 1, that is, on points with coordinates **r** = (_x_, _y_, 1).

In particular, let **r** define the coordinates of points in a reference frame _M_ coincident with a fixed frame _F_. Then, when the origin of _M_ is displaced by the translation vector **d** relative to the origin of _F_ and rotated by the angle φ relative to the x-axis of _F_, the new coordinates in _F_ of points in _M_ are given by: ![Image 113: {\displaystyle \mathbf {P} =[T(\phi ,\mathbf {d} )]\mathbf {r} ={\begin{bmatrix}\cos \phi &-\sin \phi &d_{x}\\\sin \phi &\cos \phi &d_{y}\\0&0&1\end{bmatrix}}{\begin{bmatrix}x\\y\\1\end{bmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9654bfae7e5d0f825ec57848bd5ab493f408804f)

Homogeneous transforms represent [affine transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_transformation "Affine transformation"). This formulation is necessary because a [translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry) "Translation (geometry)") is not a [linear transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_transformation "Linear transformation") of **R**2. However, using [projective geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_geometry "Projective geometry"), so that **R**2 is considered a subset of **R**3, translations become affine linear transformations.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-26)

If a rigid body moves so that its [reference frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system")_M_ does not rotate (_θ_ = 0) relative to the fixed frame _F_, the motion is called pure translation. In this case, the trajectory of every point in the body is an offset of the trajectory **d**(_t_) of the origin of _M,_ that is: ![Image 114: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} (t)=[T(0,\mathbf {d} (t))]\mathbf {p} =\mathbf {d} (t)+\mathbf {p} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32f22d9b4fcc015f80b746db422f93b8d8d145dc)

Thus, for bodies in pure translation, the velocity and acceleration of every point _P_ in the body are given by: ![Image 115: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{P}={\dot {\mathbf {r} }}(t)={\dot {\mathbf {d} }}(t)=\mathbf {v} _{O},\quad \mathbf {a} _{P}={\ddot {\mathbf {r} }}(t)={\ddot {\mathbf {d} }}(t)=\mathbf {a} _{O},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40e57e65faf6489d374924170c50c0201683b77a) where the dot denotes the derivative with respect to time and **v**_O_ and **a**_O_ are the velocity and acceleration, respectively, of the origin of the moving frame _M_. Recall the coordinate vector **p** in _M_ is constant, so its derivative is zero.

Rotation of a body around a fixed axis
--------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kinematics&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Rotation of a body around a fixed axis")]

[![Image 116](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Rotating_body.PNG/250px-Rotating_body.PNG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Rotating_body.PNG)

Figure 1: The angular velocity vector **Ω** points up for counterclockwise rotation and down for clockwise rotation, as specified by the [right-hand rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-hand_rule "Right-hand rule"). Angular position _θ_(_t_) changes with time at a rate _ω_(_t_) = d _θ_/d _t_.

Objects like a playground [merry-go-round](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Merry-go-round "Merry-go-round"), ventilation fans, or hinged doors can be modeled as rigid bodies rotating about a single fixed axis.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-Gregory-2006-27): 37  The _z_-axis has been chosen by convention.

This allows the description of a rotation as the angular position of a planar reference frame _M_ relative to a fixed _F_ about this shared _z_-axis. Coordinates **p** = (_x_, _y_) in _M_ are related to coordinates **P** = (X, Y) in _F_ by the matrix equation: ![Image 117: {\displaystyle \mathbf {P} (t)=[A(t)]\mathbf {p} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a4e865fc0f6be2e59e479f4b0a03f8114971382)

where ![Image 118: {\displaystyle [A(t)]={\begin{bmatrix}\cos(\theta (t))&-\sin(\theta (t))\\\sin(\theta (t))&\cos(\theta (t))\end{bmatrix}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53916525d50fa175a581d579e61b62d7d8a4610d) is the rotation matrix that defines the angular position of _M_ relative to _F_ as a function of time.

If the point **p** does not move in _M_, its velocity in _F_ is given by ![Image 119: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{P}={\dot {\mathbf {P} }}=[{\dot {A}}(t)]\mathbf {p} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/701dae17b9e581be69746145fd6133e7f07f2f16) It is convenient to eliminate the coordinates **p** and write this as an operation on the trajectory **P**(_t_), ![Image 120: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{P}=[{\dot {A}}(t)][A(t)^{-1}]\mathbf {P} =[\Omega ]\mathbf {P} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8fac9117de99ee6837c171f5f46eede17890bcba) where the matrix ![Image 121: {\displaystyle [\Omega ]={\begin{bmatrix}0&-\omega \\\omega &0\end{bmatrix}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eed637d1916abe2a271e19c744c416d1cf20e4f0) is known as the angular velocity matrix of _M_ relative to _F_. The parameter _ω_ is the time derivative of the angle _θ_, that is: ![Image 122: {\displaystyle \omega ={\frac {{\text{d}}\theta }{{\text{d}}t}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/646ca425f22d9d411f929dce67a8368f8a20ab65)

The acceleration of **P**(_t_) in _F_ is obtained as the time derivative of the velocity, ![Image 123: {\displaystyle \mathbf {A} _{P}={\ddot {P}}(t)=[{\dot {\Omega }}]\mathbf {P} +[\Omega ]{\dot {\mathbf {P} }},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/62a4ca56d89ccc6d2ec6d0daffd6907b9c47ccd5) which becomes ![Image 124: {\displaystyle \mathbf {A} _{P}=[{\dot {\Omega }}]\mathbf {P} +[\Omega ][\Omega ]\mathbf {P} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50d611f89cc9b7358cec772370a6ee8d79a792a9) where ![Image 125: {\displaystyle [{\dot {\Omega }}]={\begin{bmatrix}0&-\alpha \\\alpha &0\end{bmatrix}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d6ebb7e70e2d2370e781ee86ea55cd0fb7d469d7) is the angular acceleration matrix of _M_ on _F_, and ![Image 126: {\displaystyle \alpha ={\frac {{\text{d}}^{2}\theta }{{\text{d}}t^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6f91449e65ed9eb2b531028533de94350f3e7328)

The description of rotation then involves these three quantities:

The equations of translational kinematics can easily be extended to planar rotational kinematics for constant angular acceleration with simple variable exchanges: ![Image 127: {\displaystyle \omega _{\mathrm {f} }=\omega _{\mathrm {i} }+\alpha t\!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1919b69e08c82ced02615ad3c2566e7ee613b026)![Image 128: {\displaystyle \theta _{\mathrm {f} }-\theta _{\mathrm {i} }=\omega _{\mathrm {i} }t+{\tfrac {1}{2}}\alpha t^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7c58e4707320316fad78c4006e6e39253b446093)![Image 129: {\displaystyle \theta _{\mathrm {f} }-\theta _{\mathrm {i} }={\tfrac {1}{2}}(\omega _{\mathrm {f} }+\omega _{\mathrm {i} })t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ea152f27ea7253e05cc417b2c762a8db62604d1)![Image 130: {\displaystyle \omega _{\mathrm {f} }^{2}=\omega _{\mathrm {i} }^{2}+2\alpha (\theta _{\mathrm {f} }-\theta _{\mathrm {i} }).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/467af92c83b05383187599a5d3ad7548344dc60d)

Here _θ_ i and _θ_ f are, respectively, the initial and final angular positions, _ω_ i and _ω_ f are, respectively, the initial and final angular velocities, and _α_ is the constant angular acceleration. Although position in space and velocity in space are both true vectors (in terms of their properties under rotation), as is angular velocity, angle itself is not a true vector.

Point trajectories in body moving in three dimensions
-----------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kinematics&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Point trajectories in body moving in three dimensions")]

Important formulas in kinematics define the [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") and acceleration of points in a moving body as they trace trajectories in three-dimensional space. This is particularly important for the center of mass of a body, which is used to derive equations of motion using either [Newton's second law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_second_law "Newton's second law") or [Lagrange's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics").

In order to define these formulas, the movement of a component _B_ of a mechanical system is defined by the set of rotations [A(_t_)] and translations **d**(_t_) assembled into the homogeneous transformation [T(_t_)]=[A(_t_), **d**(_t_)]. If **p** is the coordinates of a point _P_ in _B_ measured in the moving [reference frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system")_M_, then the trajectory of this point traced in _F_ is given by: ![Image 131: {\displaystyle \mathbf {P} (t)=[T(t)]\mathbf {p} ={\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf {P} \\1\end{bmatrix}}={\begin{bmatrix}A(t)&\mathbf {d} (t)\\0&1\end{bmatrix}}{\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf {p} \\1\end{bmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e928b50529956cc7ca762cee6969f7321c5cf9d) This notation does not distinguish between **P** = (X, Y, Z, 1), and **P** = (X, Y, Z), which is hopefully clear in context.

This equation for the trajectory of _P_ can be inverted to compute the coordinate vector **p** in _M_ as: ![Image 132: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} =[T(t)]^{-1}\mathbf {P} (t)={\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf {p} \\1\end{bmatrix}}={\begin{bmatrix}A(t)^{\text{T}}&-A(t)^{\text{T}}\mathbf {d} (t)\\0&1\end{bmatrix}}{\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf {P} (t)\\1\end{bmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e1aa622ad90b69b42880d95b90afb0b27407a60) This expression uses the fact that the transpose of a rotation matrix is also its inverse, that is: ![Image 133: {\displaystyle [A(t)]^{\text{T}}[A(t)]=I.\!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e132dec7e35a3f7b5135d601bb20851ce2abe646)

The velocity of the point _P_ along its trajectory **P**(_t_) is obtained as the time derivative of this position vector, ![Image 134: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{P}=[{\dot {T}}(t)]\mathbf {p} ={\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf {v} _{P}\\0\end{bmatrix}}=\left({\frac {d}{dt}}{\begin{bmatrix}A(t)&\mathbf {d} (t)\\0&1\end{bmatrix}}\right){\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf {p} \\1\end{bmatrix}}={\begin{bmatrix}{\dot {A}}(t)&{\dot {\mathbf {d} }}(t)\\0&0\end{bmatrix}}{\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf {p} \\1\end{bmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4d216e15ab36a895fa2fa109fbdf77b9590d5ccb) The dot denotes the derivative with respect to time; because **p** is constant, its derivative is zero.

This formula can be modified to obtain the velocity of _P_ by operating on its trajectory **P**(_t_) measured in the fixed frame _F_. Substituting the [inverse transform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_Laplace_transform "Inverse Laplace transform") for **p** into the velocity equation yields: ![Image 135: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {v} _{P}&=[{\dot {T}}(t)][T(t)]^{-1}\mathbf {P} (t)\\[4pt]&={\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf {v} _{P}\\0\end{bmatrix}}={\begin{bmatrix}{\dot {A}}&{\dot {\mathbf {d} }}\\0&0\end{bmatrix}}{\begin{bmatrix}A&\mathbf {d} \\0&1\end{bmatrix}}^{-1}{\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf {P} (t)\\1\end{bmatrix}}\\[4pt]&={\begin{bmatrix}{\dot {A}}&{\dot {\mathbf {d} }}\\0&0\end{bmatrix}}A^{-1}{\begin{bmatrix}1&-\mathbf {d} \\0&A\end{bmatrix}}{\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf {P} (t)\\1\end{bmatrix}}\\[4pt]&={\begin{bmatrix}{\dot {A}}A^{-1}&-{\dot {A}}A^{-1}\mathbf {d} +{\dot {\mathbf {d} }}\\0&0\end{bmatrix}}{\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf {P} (t)\\1\end{bmatrix}}\\[4pt]&={\begin{bmatrix}{\dot {A}}A^{\text{T}}&-{\dot {A}}A^{\text{T}}\mathbf {d} +{\dot {\mathbf {d} }}\\0&0\end{bmatrix}}{\begin{bmatrix}\mathbf {P} (t)\\1\end{bmatrix}}\\[6pt]\mathbf {v} _{P}&=[S]\mathbf {P} .\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6aa70149e117b2b14b1f6d473dd453dc5530c7e6) The matrix [_S_] is given by: ![Image 136: {\displaystyle [S]={\begin{bmatrix}\Omega &-\Omega \mathbf {d} +{\dot {\mathbf {d} }}\\0&0\end{bmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90d988bfd6ff36b285d6294bf167b63d5f91bdef) where ![Image 137: {\displaystyle [\Omega ]={\dot {A}}A^{\text{T}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21d0117f60aeca689c59abbfe4b88b1a287a1b63) is the angular velocity matrix.

Multiplying by the operator [_S_], the formula for the velocity **v**P takes the form: ![Image 138: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{P}=[\Omega ](\mathbf {P} -\mathbf {d} )+{\dot {\mathbf {d} }}=\omega \times \mathbf {R} _{P/O}+\mathbf {v} _{O},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/127655f82eb90e56b116afbcf8aa567b908e516b) where the vector _ω_ is the angular velocity vector obtained from the components of the matrix [Ω]; the vector ![Image 139: {\displaystyle \mathbf {R} _{P/O}=\mathbf {P} -\mathbf {d} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21424ef3de081f617ff951e0e52e3950e75830d6) is the position of _P_ relative to the origin _O_ of the moving frame _M_; and ![Image 140: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{O}={\dot {\mathbf {d} }},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4c110d0c67ace33c3154077f6567839129bed7d5) is the velocity of the origin _O_.

The acceleration of a point _P_ in a moving body _B_ is obtained as the time derivative of its velocity vector: ![Image 141: {\displaystyle \mathbf {A} _{P}={\frac {d}{dt}}\mathbf {v} _{P}={\frac {d}{dt}}\left([S]\mathbf {P} \right)=[{\dot {S}}]\mathbf {P} +[S]{\dot {\mathbf {P} }}=[{\dot {S}}]\mathbf {P} +[S][S]\mathbf {P} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/80998957f2829ada7a5e77e2fb68a31285e843cb)

This equation can be expanded firstly by computing ![Image 142: {\displaystyle [{\dot {S}}]={\begin{bmatrix}{\dot {\Omega }}&-{\dot {\Omega }}\mathbf {d} -\Omega {\dot {\mathbf {d} }}+{\ddot {\mathbf {d} }}\\0&0\end{bmatrix}}={\begin{bmatrix}{\dot {\Omega }}&-{\dot {\Omega }}\mathbf {d} -\Omega \mathbf {v} _{O}+\mathbf {A} _{O}\\0&0\end{bmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a39702184d0f72216205c32ebcfa8e0e9c3b478) and ![Image 143: {\displaystyle [S]^{2}={\begin{bmatrix}\Omega &-\Omega \mathbf {d} +\mathbf {v} _{O}\\0&0\end{bmatrix}}^{2}={\begin{bmatrix}\Omega ^{2}&-\Omega ^{2}\mathbf {d} +\Omega \mathbf {v} _{O}\\0&0\end{bmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/557b35e24d57f15d0c9d2998053809be7105a2f9)

The formula for the acceleration **A**_P_ can now be obtained as: ![Image 144: {\displaystyle \mathbf {A} _{P}={\dot {\Omega }}(\mathbf {P} -\mathbf {d} )+\mathbf {A} _{O}+\Omega ^{2}(\mathbf {P} -\mathbf {d} ),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e9915d3aeacc270c09d59972a06b7207f07fc1ed) or ![Image 145: {\displaystyle \mathbf {A} _{P}=\alpha \times \mathbf {R} _{P/O}+\omega \times \omega \times \mathbf {R} _{P/O}+\mathbf {A} _{O},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c5c9e0a90584346d36469ebed0b20b823bd82d6) where _α_ is the angular acceleration vector obtained from the derivative of the angular velocity vector; ![Image 146: {\displaystyle \mathbf {R} _{P/O}=\mathbf {P} -\mathbf {d} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21424ef3de081f617ff951e0e52e3950e75830d6) is the relative position vector (the position of _P_ relative to the origin _O_ of the moving frame _M_); and ![Image 147: {\displaystyle \mathbf {A} _{O}={\ddot {\mathbf {d} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7b96a847c563138f1547a153c7210830f4b73581) is the acceleration of the origin of the moving frame _M_.

Kinematic constraints
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kinematics&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Kinematic constraints")]

Kinematic constraints are constraints on the movement of components of a mechanical system. Kinematic constraints can be considered to have two basic forms, (i) constraints that arise from hinges, sliders and cam joints that define the construction of the system, called [holonomic constraints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holonomic_system "Holonomic system"), and (ii) constraints imposed on the velocity of the system such as the knife-edge constraint of ice-skates on a flat plane, or rolling without slipping of a disc or sphere in contact with a plane, which are called [non-holonomic constraints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-holonomic_system "Non-holonomic system"). The following are some common examples.

A [kinematic coupling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_coupling "Kinematic coupling") exactly constrains all 6 degrees of freedom.

### Rolling without slipping

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kinematics&action=edit&section=26 "Edit section: Rolling without slipping")]

An object that rolls against a [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(topology) "Surface (topology)") without slipping obeys the condition that the [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") of its [center of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_mass "Center of mass") is equal to the [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product") of its angular velocity with a vector from the point of contact to the center of mass: ![Image 148: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {v}}_{G}(t)={\boldsymbol {\Omega }}\times {\boldsymbol {r}}_{G/O}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86018aacd5410a1df289681d3ae74582ff96b82a)

For the case of an object that does not tip or turn, this reduces to ![Image 149: {\displaystyle v=r\omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23b5b5c066f5be8dee84c4cb33dea2383de8b012).

This is the case where bodies are connected by an idealized cord that remains in tension and cannot change length. The constraint is that the sum of lengths of all segments of the cord is the total length, and accordingly the time derivative of this sum is zero.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-Kelvin-28)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-Thompson2-29)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-Fogiel-30) A dynamic problem of this type is the [pendulum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum "Pendulum"). Another example is a drum turned by the pull of gravity upon a falling weight attached to the rim by the inextensible cord.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-Church-31) An _equilibrium_ problem (i.e. not kinematic) of this type is the [catenary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catenary "Catenary").[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-Kline-32)

[Reuleaux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franz_Reuleaux "Franz Reuleaux") called the ideal connections between components that form a machine [kinematic pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_pair "Kinematic pair"). He distinguished between higher pairs which were said to have line contact between the two links and lower pairs that have area contact between the links. J. Phillips shows that there are many ways to construct pairs that do not fit this simple classification.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-33)

A lower pair is an ideal joint, or holonomic constraint, that maintains contact between a point, line or plane in a moving solid (three-dimensional) body to a corresponding point line or plane in the fixed solid body. There are the following cases:

*   A revolute pair, or hinged joint, requires a line, or axis, in the moving body to remain co-linear with a line in the fixed body, and a plane perpendicular to this line in the moving body maintain contact with a similar perpendicular plane in the fixed body. This imposes five constraints on the relative movement of the links, which therefore has one degree of freedom, which is pure rotation about the axis of the hinge.
*   A [prismatic joint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prismatic_joint "Prismatic joint"), or slider, requires that a line, or axis, in the moving body remain co-linear with a line in the fixed body, and a plane parallel to this line in the moving body maintain contact with a similar parallel plane in the fixed body. This imposes five constraints on the relative movement of the links, which therefore has one degree of freedom. This degree of freedom is the distance of the slide along the line.
*   A cylindrical joint requires that a line, or axis, in the moving body remain co-linear with a line in the fixed body. It is a combination of a revolute joint and a sliding joint. This joint has two degrees of freedom. The position of the moving body is defined by both the rotation about and slide along the axis.
*   A spherical joint, or ball joint, requires that a point in the moving body maintain contact with a point in the fixed body. This joint has three degrees of freedom.
*   A planar joint requires that a plane in the moving body maintain contact with a plane in fixed body. This joint has three degrees of freedom.

Generally speaking, a higher pair is a constraint that requires a curve or surface in the moving body to maintain contact with a curve or surface in the fixed body. For example, the contact between a cam and its follower is a higher pair called a _cam joint_. Similarly, the contact between the involute curves that form the meshing teeth of two gears are cam joints.

[![Image 150: Illustration of a Four-bar linkage from Kinematics of Machinery, 1876](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Kinematics_of_Machinery_-_Figure_21.jpg/330px-Kinematics_of_Machinery_-_Figure_21.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Kinematics_of_Machinery_-_Figure_21.jpg)

Illustration of a four-bar linkage from [Kinematics of Machinery, 1876](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/The_Kinematics_of_Machinery "s:The Kinematics of Machinery")

Rigid bodies ("links") connected by [kinematic pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic_pair "Kinematic pair") ("joints") are known as _[kinematic chains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematic\_chain "Kinematic chain")_. [Mechanisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanism_(engineering) "Mechanism (engineering)") and robots are examples of kinematic chains. The [degree of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(mechanics) "Degrees of freedom (mechanics)") of a kinematic chain is computed from the number of links and the number and type of joints using the [mobility formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chebychev%E2%80%93Gr%C3%BCbler%E2%80%93Kutzbach_criterion "Chebychev–Grübler–Kutzbach criterion"). This formula can also be used to enumerate the [topologies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") of kinematic chains that have a given degree of freedom, which is known as _type synthesis_ in machine design.

The planar one degree-of-freedom [linkages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linkage_(mechanical) "Linkage (mechanical)") assembled from _N_ links and _j_ hinges or sliding joints are:

*   _N_ = 2, _j_ = 1: a two-bar linkage that is the lever;
*   _N_ = 4, _j_ = 4: the [four-bar linkage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-bar_linkage "Four-bar linkage");
*   _N_ = 6, _j_ = 7: a [six-bar linkage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Six-bar_linkage "Six-bar linkage"). This must have two links ("ternary links") that support three joints. There are two distinct topologies that depend on how the two ternary linkages are connected. In the [Watt topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Six-bar_linkage#Watt_six-bar_linkage "Six-bar linkage"), the two ternary links have a common joint; in the [Stephenson topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Six-bar_linkage#Stephenson_six-bar_linkage "Six-bar linkage"), the two ternary links do not have a common joint and are connected by binary links.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics#cite_note-34)
*   _N_ = 8, _j_ = 10: eight-bar linkage with 16 different topologies;
*   _N_ = 10, _j_ = 13: ten-bar linkage with 230 different topologies;
*   _N_ = 12, _j_ = 16: twelve-bar linkage with 6,856 topologies.

For larger chains and their linkage topologies, see R. P. Sunkari and [L. C. Schmidt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linda_Schmidt "Linda Schmidt"), "Structural synthesis of planar kinematic chains by adapting a Mckay-type algorithm", _Mechanism and Machine Theory_ #41, pp.1021–1030 (2006).
