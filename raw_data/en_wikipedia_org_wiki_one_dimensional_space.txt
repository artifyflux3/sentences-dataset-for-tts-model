A **one-dimensional space** (**1D space**) is a [mathematical space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_space "Mathematical space") in which location can be specified with a single [coordinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate "Coordinate"). An example is the [number line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_line "Number line"), each [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)") of which is described by a single [real number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-dimensional_space#cite_note-1) Any [straight line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Straight_line "Straight line") or smooth [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve") is a one-dimensional space, regardless of the dimension of the [ambient space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ambient_space "Ambient space") in which the line or curve is embedded. Examples include the [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle") on a plane, or a [parametric space curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametric_space_curve "Parametric space curve"). In [physical space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_space "Physical space"), a 1D [subspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subspace_(mathematics) "Subspace (mathematics)") is called a "linear [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(physics) "Dimension (physics)")" ([rectilinear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectilinear_motion "Rectilinear motion") or [curvilinear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_motion "Curvilinear motion")), with [units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_(physics) "Unit (physics)") of [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length "Length") (e.g., [metre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre "Metre")).

In [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry") there are several structures that are one-dimensional spaces but are usually referred to by more specific terms. Any [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)")K{\displaystyle K}![Image 14: {\displaystyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76fce82a62ed5461908f0dc8f037de4e3686b0) is a one-dimensional [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") over itself. The [projective line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_line "Projective line") over K,{\displaystyle K,}![Image 15: {\displaystyle K,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fea4b5006afa0eb81c21b577efe304ab545d571) denoted P 1(K),{\displaystyle \mathbf {P} ^{1}(K),}![Image 16: {\displaystyle \mathbf {P} ^{1}(K),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/784fbf827b1aefdd0050dc63eee195fdcab58537) is a one-dimensional space. In particular, if the field is the [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_numbers "Complex numbers")C,{\displaystyle \mathbb {C} ,}![Image 17: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6ff6a3dc2982018ff20f1d2c927afc74a217be6) then the [complex projective line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_projective_line "Complex projective line")P 1(C){\displaystyle \mathbf {P} ^{1}(\mathbb {C} )}![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mathbf {P} ^{1}(\mathbb {C} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c870777c79bac7a8ffb75f7edaa7c9de48acc1e2) is one-dimensional with respect to C{\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }![Image 19: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7) (but is sometimes called the [Riemann sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_sphere "Riemann sphere"), as it is a model of the [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere"), [two-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-dimensional_space "Two-dimensional space") with respect to real-number coordinates).

For every [eigenvector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvector "Eigenvector") of a [linear transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_transformation "Linear transformation")_T_ on a vector space _V_, there is a one-dimensional space _A_ ⊂ _V_ generated by the eigenvector such that _T_(_A_) = _A_, that is, _A_ is an [invariant set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_set "Invariant set") under the action of _T_.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-dimensional_space#cite_note-2)

In [Lie theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_theory "Lie theory"), a one-dimensional subspace of a [Lie algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra "Lie algebra") is mapped to a [one-parameter group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-parameter_group "One-parameter group") under the [Lie group–Lie algebra correspondence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group%E2%80%93Lie_algebra_correspondence "Lie group–Lie algebra correspondence").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-dimensional_space#cite_note-3)

More generally, a [ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)") is a [length-one](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length_of_a_module "Length of a module")[module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_(mathematics) "Module (mathematics)") over itself. Similarly, the [projective line over a ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_line_over_a_ring "Projective line over a ring") is a one-dimensional space over the ring. In case the ring is an [algebra over a field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra_over_a_field "Algebra over a field"), these spaces are one-dimensional with respect to the algebra, even if the algebra is of higher dimensionality.
