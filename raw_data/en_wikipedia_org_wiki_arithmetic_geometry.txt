The [hyperelliptic curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperelliptic_curve "Hyperelliptic curve") defined by ![Image 2: {\displaystyle y^{2}=x(x+1)(x-3)(x+2)(x-2)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/96acd0c52a495e3ae3ec9d8150884f52754bc377) has only finitely many [rational points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_point "Rational point") (such as the points ![Image 3: {\displaystyle (-2,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/25e098d5557c0d6c91800a08fbb61d93a613ce93) and ![Image 4: {\displaystyle (-1,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a8f5f5f03e9f4380e41314d8c5d0129861c8ecf6)) by [Faltings's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faltings%27s_theorem "Faltings's theorem").

In mathematics, **arithmetic geometry** is roughly the application of techniques from [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry") to problems in [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-1) Arithmetic geometry is centered around [Diophantine geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diophantine_geometry "Diophantine geometry"), the study of [rational points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_point "Rational point") of [algebraic varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety "Algebraic variety").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-Quanta-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-poonen-notes-3)

In more abstract terms, arithmetic geometry can be defined as the study of [schemes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scheme_(mathematics) "Scheme (mathematics)") of [finite type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_morphism#Morphisms_of_finite_type "Finite morphism") over the [spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectrum_of_a_ring "Spectrum of a ring") of the [ring of integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_of_integers "Ring of integers").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-4)

The classical objects of interest in arithmetic geometry are rational points: [sets of solutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solution_set "Solution set") of a [system of polynomial equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/System_of_polynomial_equations "System of polynomial equations") over [number fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_field "Number field"), [finite fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field "Finite field"), [p-adic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P-adic_field "P-adic field"), or [function fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_function_field "Algebraic function field"), i.e. [fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)") that are not [algebraically closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraically_closed "Algebraically closed") excluding the [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number"). Rational points can be directly characterized by [height functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Height_function "Height function") which measure their arithmetic complexity.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-5)

The structure of algebraic varieties defined over non-algebraically closed fields has become a central area of interest that arose with the modern abstract development of algebraic geometry. Over finite fields, [étale cohomology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89tale_cohomology "Étale cohomology") provides [topological invariants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_property "Topological property") associated to algebraic varieties.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-grothendieck-cohomology-6)[p-adic Hodge theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P-adic_Hodge_theory "P-adic Hodge theory") gives tools to examine when cohomological properties of varieties over the [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") extend to those over [p-adic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P-adic_field "P-adic field").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-7)

### 19th century: early arithmetic geometry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Arithmetic_geometry&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: 19th century: early arithmetic geometry")]

In the early 19th century, [Carl Friedrich Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss "Carl Friedrich Gauss") observed that non-zero [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer") solutions to [homogeneous polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_polynomial "Homogeneous polynomial") equations with [rational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number "Rational number") coefficients exist if non-zero rational solutions exist.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-8)

In the 1850s, [Leopold Kronecker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leopold_Kronecker "Leopold Kronecker") formulated the [Kronecker–Weber theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker%E2%80%93Weber_theorem "Kronecker–Weber theorem"), introduced the theory of [divisors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divisor_(algebraic_geometry) "Divisor (algebraic geometry)"), and made numerous other connections between number theory and [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra "Algebra"). He then conjectured his "[liebster Jugendtraum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker%27s_Jugendtraum "Kronecker's Jugendtraum")" ("dearest dream of youth"), a generalization that was later put forward by Hilbert in a modified form as his [twelfth problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert%27s_problems "Hilbert's problems"), which outlines a goal to have number theory operate only with rings that are quotients of [polynomial rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_ring "Polynomial ring") over the integers.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-Princeton-9)

### Early-to-mid 20th century: algebraic developments and the Weil conjectures

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Arithmetic_geometry&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Early-to-mid 20th century: algebraic developments and the Weil conjectures")]

In the late 1920s, [André Weil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Weil "André Weil") demonstrated profound connections between algebraic geometry and number theory with his doctoral work leading to the [Mordell–Weil theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mordell%E2%80%93Weil_theorem "Mordell–Weil theorem") which demonstrates that the set of rational points of an [abelian variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_variety "Abelian variety") is a [finitely generated abelian group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finitely_generated_abelian_group "Finitely generated abelian group").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-10)

Modern foundations of algebraic geometry were developed based on contemporary [commutative algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_algebra "Commutative algebra"), including [valuation theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valuation_theory "Valuation theory") and the theory of [ideals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_(ring_theory) "Ideal (ring theory)") by [Oscar Zariski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oscar_Zariski "Oscar Zariski") and others in the 1930s and 1940s.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-11)

In 1949, [André Weil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Weil "André Weil") posed the landmark [Weil conjectures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weil_conjectures "Weil conjectures") about the [local zeta-functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_zeta-function "Local zeta-function") of algebraic varieties over finite fields.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-12) These conjectures offered a framework between algebraic geometry and number theory that propelled [Alexander Grothendieck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Grothendieck "Alexander Grothendieck") to recast the foundations making use of [sheaf theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheaf_theory "Sheaf theory") (together with [Jean-Pierre Serre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Serre "Jean-Pierre Serre")), and later scheme theory, in the 1950s and 1960s.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-13)[Bernard Dwork](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernard_Dwork "Bernard Dwork") proved one of the four Weil conjectures (rationality of the local zeta function) in 1960.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-14) Grothendieck developed étale cohomology theory to prove two of the Weil conjectures (together with [Michael Artin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Artin "Michael Artin") and [Jean-Louis Verdier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Louis_Verdier "Jean-Louis Verdier")) by 1965.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-grothendieck-cohomology-6)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-15) The last of the Weil conjectures (an analogue of the [Riemann hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_hypothesis "Riemann hypothesis")) would be finally proven in 1974 by [Pierre Deligne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Deligne "Pierre Deligne").[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-16)

### Mid-to-late 20th century: developments in modularity, p-adic methods, and beyond

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Arithmetic_geometry&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Mid-to-late 20th century: developments in modularity, p-adic methods, and beyond")]

Between 1956 and 1957, [Yutaka Taniyama](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yutaka_Taniyama "Yutaka Taniyama") and [Goro Shimura](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goro_Shimura "Goro Shimura") posed the [Taniyama–Shimura conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modularity_theorem "Modularity theorem") (now known as the modularity theorem) relating [elliptic curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_curves "Elliptic curves") to [modular forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modular_forms "Modular forms").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-18) This connection would ultimately lead to [the first proof](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wiles%27s_proof_of_Fermat%27s_Last_Theorem "Wiles's proof of Fermat's Last Theorem") of [Fermat's Last Theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermat%27s_Last_Theorem "Fermat's Last Theorem") in number theory through algebraic geometry techniques of [modularity lifting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lift_(mathematics) "Lift (mathematics)") developed by [Andrew Wiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrew_Wiles "Andrew Wiles") in 1995.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-wiles1995-19)

In the 1960s, Goro Shimura introduced [Shimura varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shimura_variety "Shimura variety") as generalizations of [modular curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modular_curve "Modular curve").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-20) Since the 1979, Shimura varieties have played a crucial role in the [Langlands program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Langlands_program "Langlands program") as a natural realm of examples for testing conjectures.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-21)

In papers in 1977 and 1978, [Barry Mazur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barry_Mazur "Barry Mazur") proved the [torsion conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_conjecture "Torsion conjecture") giving a complete list of the possible torsion subgroups of elliptic curves over the rational numbers. Mazur's first proof of this theorem depended upon a complete analysis of the rational points on certain [modular curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modular_curve "Modular curve").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-22)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-23) In 1996, the proof of the torsion conjecture was extended to all number fields by [Loïc Merel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lo%C3%AFc_Merel "Loïc Merel").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-24)

In 1983, [Gerd Faltings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gerd_Faltings "Gerd Faltings") proved the [Mordell conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faltings%27s_theorem "Faltings's theorem"), demonstrating that a curve of genus greater than 1 has only finitely many rational points (where the Mordell–Weil theorem only demonstrates [finite generation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finitely_generated_abelian_group "Finitely generated abelian group") of the set of rational points as opposed to finiteness).[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-25)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-26)

In 2001, the proof of the [local Langlands conjectures for GL n](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_Langlands_conjectures#Local_Langlands_conjectures_for_GLn "Local Langlands conjectures") was based on the geometry of certain Shimura varieties.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-27)

In the 2010s, [Peter Scholze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Scholze "Peter Scholze") developed [perfectoid spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perfectoid_space "Perfectoid space") and new cohomology theories in arithmetic geometry over p-adic fields with application to [Galois representations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galois_representations "Galois representations") and certain cases of the [weight-monodromy conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weight-monodromy_conjecture "Weight-monodromy conjecture").[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-28)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_geometry#cite_note-29)
