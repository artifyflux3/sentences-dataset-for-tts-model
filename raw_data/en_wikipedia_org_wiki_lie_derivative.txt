In [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), the **Lie derivative** ( [_LEE_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key")), named after [Sophus Lie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sophus_Lie "Sophus Lie") by [Władysław Ślebodziński](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W%C5%82adys%C5%82aw_%C5%9Alebodzi%C5%84ski "Władysław Ślebodziński"),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_derivative#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_derivative#cite_note-2) evaluates the change of a [tensor field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field "Tensor field") (including scalar functions, [vector fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field") and [one-forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-form "One-form")), along the [flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flow_(mathematics) "Flow (mathematics)") defined by another vector field. This change is coordinate invariant and therefore the Lie derivative is defined on any [differentiable manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold").

Functions, tensor fields and forms can be differentiated with respect to a vector field. If _T_ is a tensor field and _X_ is a vector field, then the Lie derivative of _T_ with respect to _X_ is denoted ![Image 1: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}_{X}T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cbac865bc1c7bdc0902303329669c353fb752611). The [differential operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_operator "Differential operator")![Image 2: {\displaystyle T\mapsto {\mathcal {L}}_{X}T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/85f582f11cb716c938d4e2bf7aa87c68373628f1) is a [derivation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivation_(differential_algebra) "Derivation (differential algebra)") of the algebra of [tensor fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_fields "Tensor fields") of the underlying manifold.

The Lie derivative commutes with [contraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_contraction "Tensor contraction") and the [exterior derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative "Exterior derivative") on [differential forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_forms "Differential forms").

Although there are many concepts of taking a derivative in differential geometry, they all agree when the expression being differentiated is a function or [scalar field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field "Scalar field"). Thus in this case the word "Lie" is dropped, and one simply speaks of the derivative of a function.

The Lie derivative of a vector field _Y_ with respect to another vector field _X_ is known as the "[Lie bracket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_bracket_of_vector_fields "Lie bracket of vector fields")" of _X_ and _Y_, and is often denoted [_X_,_Y_] instead of ![Image 3: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}_{X}Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fd1e6c305b263c2c55fdac7e57f69fbc6a505c4d). The space of vector fields forms a [Lie algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra "Lie algebra") with respect to this Lie bracket. The Lie derivative constitutes an infinite-dimensional [Lie algebra representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra_representation "Lie algebra representation") of this Lie algebra, due to the identity

![Image 4: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}_{[X,Y]}T={\mathcal {L}}_{X}{\mathcal {L}}_{Y}T-{\mathcal {L}}_{Y}{\mathcal {L}}_{X}T,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eb7fbc05f582444cbb60942562fc44e80e68bc38)
valid for any vector fields _X_ and _Y_ and any tensor field _T_.

Considering vector fields as [infinitesimal generators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra "Lie algebra") of [flows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flow_(mathematics) "Flow (mathematics)") (i.e. one-dimensional [groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") of [diffeomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffeomorphism "Diffeomorphism")) on _M_, the Lie derivative is the [differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra_representation#Infinitesimal_Lie_group_representations "Lie algebra representation") of the representation of the [diffeomorphism group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffeomorphism#Diffeomorphism_group "Diffeomorphism") on tensor fields, analogous to Lie algebra representations as [infinitesimal representations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra_representation#Infinitesimal_Lie_group_representations "Lie algebra representation") associated to [group representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_representation "Group representation") in [Lie group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group") theory.

Generalisations exist for [spinor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinor "Spinor") fields, [fibre bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fibre_bundle "Fibre bundle") with a [connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(mathematics) "Connection (mathematics)") and [vector-valued differential forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector-valued_differential_forms "Vector-valued differential forms").

A 'naïve' attempt to define the derivative of a [tensor field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field "Tensor field") with respect to a [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field") would be to take the [components](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor#As_multidimensional_arrays "Tensor") of the tensor field and take the [directional derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directional_derivative "Directional derivative") of each component with respect to the vector field. However, this definition is undesirable because it is not invariant under [changes of coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold#Transition_map "Manifold"), e.g. the naive derivative expressed in [polar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinate_system "Polar coordinate system") or [spherical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinate_system "Spherical coordinate system") differs from the naive derivative of the components in [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system"). On an abstract [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold") such a definition is meaningless and ill defined.

In [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), there are three main coordinate independent notions of differentiation of tensor fields:

1.   Lie derivatives,
2.   derivatives with respect to [connections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(differential_geometry) "Connection (differential geometry)"),
3.   the [exterior derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative "Exterior derivative") of totally antisymmetric covariant tensors, i.e. [differential forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_forms "Differential forms").

The main difference between the Lie derivative and a derivative with respect to a connection is that the latter derivative of a tensor field with respect to a [tangent vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space "Tangent space") is well-defined even if it is not specified how to extend that tangent vector to a vector field. However, a connection requires the choice of an additional geometric structure (e.g. a [Riemannian metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold") in the case of [Levi-Civita connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection "Levi-Civita connection"), or just an abstract [connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(differential_geometry) "Connection (differential geometry)")) on the manifold. In contrast, when taking a Lie derivative, no additional structure on the manifold is needed, but it is impossible to talk about the Lie derivative of a tensor field with respect to a single tangent vector, since the value of the Lie derivative of a tensor field with respect to a vector field _X_ at a point _p_ depends on the value of _X_ in a neighborhood of _p_, not just at _p_ itself. Finally, the exterior derivative of differential forms does not require any additional choices, but is only a well defined derivative of differential forms (including functions), thus excluding vectors and other tensors that are not purely differential forms.

Lie transport of a vector ![Image 6: {\displaystyle v_{y}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/922aa64dad09633a401e14be9b4389795835cd8c) from point ![Image 7: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) to point ![Image 8: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) along the vector flow field ![Image 9: {\displaystyle u}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3e6bb763d22c20916ed4f0bb6bd49d7470cffd8).

The idea of Lie derivatives is to use a vector field to define a notion of transport (Lie transport). A smooth vector field defines a smooth flow on the manifold, which allows vectors to be transported between two points on the same line of flow (This contrasts with connections, which allows transport between arbitrary points). Intuitively, a vector ![Image 10: {\displaystyle Y(p)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c40d2314e0aeef14f5a515b6c52967e21c2ccea4) based at point ![Image 11: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) is transported by flowing its base point to ![Image 12: {\displaystyle p'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40e623e3163571a220ed60ecb31aa78c24104b85), while flowing its tip point ![Image 13: {\displaystyle p+Y(p)\delta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d12d8129e9afe458a8d73c3f5d94173b5d94c0e9) to ![Image 14: {\displaystyle p'+\delta p'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fda7feada562f37db37efa71ba9fd363e6a91db).

The Lie derivative may be defined in several equivalent ways. To keep things simple, we begin by defining the Lie derivative acting on scalar functions and vector fields, before moving on to the definition for general tensors.

### The Lie derivative of a tensor field

#### Algebraic definition

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lie_derivative&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Algebraic definition")]

We now give an algebraic definition. The algebraic definition for the Lie derivative of a tensor field follows from the following four axioms:

**Axiom 1.** The Lie derivative of a function is equal to the directional derivative of the function. This fact is often expressed by the formula ![Image 58: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}_{Y}f=Y(f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d18d6c35c449f57742b092388415c5d3a88eda70)**Axiom 2.** The Lie derivative obeys the following version of Leibniz's rule: For any tensor fields _S_ and _T_, we have ![Image 59: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}_{Y}(S\otimes T)=({\mathcal {L}}_{Y}S)\otimes T+S\otimes ({\mathcal {L}}_{Y}T).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4e6a2f674521d5ec25c1a92edbb037e97f85aae)**Axiom 3.** The Lie derivative obeys the Leibniz rule with respect to [contraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_contraction "Tensor contraction"): ![Image 60: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}_{X}(T(Y_{1},\ldots ,Y_{n}))=({\mathcal {L}}_{X}T)(Y_{1},\ldots ,Y_{n})+T(({\mathcal {L}}_{X}Y_{1}),\ldots ,Y_{n})+\cdots +T(Y_{1},\ldots ,({\mathcal {L}}_{X}Y_{n}))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3999d4565a3142e3ab51307c096b357ea8faca61)**Axiom 4.** The Lie derivative commutes with exterior derivative on functions: ![Image 61: {\displaystyle [{\mathcal {L}}_{X},d]=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8f679d41dd5abe3834ff93d87e8dbe5f43baae41)
Using the first and third axioms, applying the Lie derivative ![Image 62: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}_{X}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ce4db2d17b365a7321dbfdb8f8bc512dd911ea54) to ![Image 63: {\displaystyle Y(f)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/546dc0c50d2b216846801e63048b7d65c4b72743) shows that

![Image 64: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}_{X}Y(f)=X(Y(f))-Y(X(f)),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c756d58c4261aeffda22fc61d6534c1baf5578da)
which is one of the standard definitions for the [Lie bracket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_bracket_of_vector_fields "Lie bracket of vector fields").

The Lie derivative acting on a differential form is the [anticommutator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutator#Ring_theory "Commutator") of the [interior product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interior_product "Interior product") with the exterior derivative. So if α is a differential form,

![Image 65: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}_{Y}\alpha =i_{Y}d\alpha +di_{Y}\alpha .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/03bdb96a77ffdce3bc8a382bab4dda6bc57f7f94)
This follows easily by checking that the expression commutes with exterior derivative, is a derivation (being an anticommutator of graded derivations) and does the right thing on functions. This is **Cartan's magic formula**. See [interior product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interior_product "Interior product") for details.

Explicitly, let _T_ be a tensor field of type (_p_, _q_). Consider _T_ to be a differentiable [multilinear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilinear_map "Multilinear map") of [smooth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_function "Smooth function")[sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Section_(fiber_bundle) "Section (fiber bundle)")_α_ 1, _α_ 2, ..., _α_ _p_ of the cotangent bundle _T_∗_M_ and of sections _X_ 1, _X_ 2, ..., _X_ q of the [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle")_TM_, written _T_(_α_ 1, _α_ 2, ..., _X_ 1, _X_ 2, ...) into **R**. Define the Lie derivative of _T_ along _Y_ by the formula

![Image 66: {\displaystyle ({\mathcal {L}}_{Y}T)(\alpha _{1},\alpha _{2},\ldots ,X_{1},X_{2},\ldots )=Y(T(\alpha _{1},\alpha _{2},\ldots ,X_{1},X_{2},\ldots ))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/434146a8e14770567a1cb3d0f54d3029506120ba)![Image 67: {\displaystyle -T({\mathcal {L}}_{Y}\alpha _{1},\alpha _{2},\ldots ,X_{1},X_{2},\ldots )-T(\alpha _{1},{\mathcal {L}}_{Y}\alpha _{2},\ldots ,X_{1},X_{2},\ldots )-\ldots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e698d9675d0d7c36c7c029edb23e11048e14d35)![Image 68: {\displaystyle -T(\alpha _{1},\alpha _{2},\ldots ,{\mathcal {L}}_{Y}X_{1},X_{2},\ldots )-T(\alpha _{1},\alpha _{2},\ldots ,X_{1},{\mathcal {L}}_{Y}X_{2},\ldots )-\ldots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffe3f51b82ccd09f44aaf1e7c330869255632f9c)
The analytic and algebraic definitions can be proven to be equivalent using the properties of the pushforward and the [Leibniz rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Leibniz_rule "General Leibniz rule") for differentiation. The Lie derivative commutes with the contraction.

### The Lie derivative of a differential form

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lie_derivative&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: The Lie derivative of a differential form")]

A particularly important class of tensor fields is the class of [differential forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_forms "Differential forms"). The restriction of the Lie derivative to the space of differential forms is closely related to the [exterior derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative "Exterior derivative"). Both the Lie derivative and the exterior derivative attempt to capture the idea of a derivative in different ways. These differences can be bridged by introducing the idea of an [interior product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interior_product "Interior product"), after which the relationships falls out as an identity known as **Cartan's formula**. Cartan's formula can also be used as a definition of the Lie derivative on the space of differential forms.

Let _M_ be a manifold and _X_ a vector field on _M_. Let ![Image 69: {\displaystyle \omega \in \Lambda ^{k}(M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dfbe34d4b68ff2ad319be0f8344abbb880f25831) be a _k_-[form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form"), i.e., for each ![Image 70: {\displaystyle p\in M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35ad2c18a15749505c928763cd4fdb56f4982816), ![Image 71: {\displaystyle \omega (p)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02c9e903e57a81e8964561485fe7ed2e14be3443) is an [alternating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternating_form "Alternating form")[multilinear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilinear_map "Multilinear map") from ![Image 72: {\displaystyle (T_{p}M)^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45dd64c1029d838f75f5e70e54035f10f214843c) to the real numbers. The [interior product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interior_product "Interior product") of _X_ and _ω_ is the (_k_ − 1)-form ![Image 73: {\displaystyle i_{X}\omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6f22c7619db13f7850c478e3d2b268bd0257863) defined as

![Image 74: {\displaystyle (i_{X}\omega )(X_{1},\ldots ,X_{k-1})=\omega (X,X_{1},\ldots ,X_{k-1})\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/142390ecdb5d65a43a7efbd82fdcd82ac8029bd3)
The differential form ![Image 75: {\displaystyle i_{X}\omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6f22c7619db13f7850c478e3d2b268bd0257863) is also called the **contraction** of _ω_ with _X_, and

![Image 76: {\displaystyle i_{X}:\Lambda ^{k}(M)\rightarrow \Lambda ^{k-1}(M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61a0b8a2e5b27fdc0b498eabd3b343d3fe809d05)
is a [![Image 77: {\displaystyle \wedge }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1caa4004cb216ef2930bb12fe805a76870caed94)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_algebra "Exterior algebra")-[antiderivation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivation_(abstract_algebra) "Derivation (abstract algebra)") where [![Image 78: {\displaystyle \wedge }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1caa4004cb216ef2930bb12fe805a76870caed94)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_algebra "Exterior algebra") is the [wedge product on differential forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_algebra "Exterior algebra"). That is, ![Image 79: {\displaystyle i_{X}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb2f1b6a552361bf8b27aaa44d209c55fa807585) is **R**-linear, and

![Image 80: {\displaystyle i_{X}(\omega \wedge \eta )=(i_{X}\omega )\wedge \eta +(-1)^{k}\omega \wedge (i_{X}\eta )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ce5eeaaaf15cb5a42fb29d8ecc5abbed28cd5a37)
for ![Image 81: {\displaystyle \omega \in \Lambda ^{k}(M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dfbe34d4b68ff2ad319be0f8344abbb880f25831) and η another differential form. Also, for a function ![Image 82: {\displaystyle f\in \Lambda ^{0}(M)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ddaa86bb47a89962f320c194c81df024f236e572), that is, a real- or complex-valued function on _M_, one has

![Image 83: {\displaystyle i_{fX}\omega =f\,i_{X}\omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d866e72b70688b206f624873e10491b261e8e0df)
where ![Image 84: {\displaystyle fX}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de626df67ebb41c1d53204dd862872aafd0aed20) denotes the product of _f_ and _X_. The relationship between [exterior derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative "Exterior derivative") and Lie derivatives can then be summarized as follows. First, since the Lie derivative of a function _f_ with respect to a vector field _X_ is the same as the directional derivative _X_(_f_), it is also the same as the [contraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form#Operations_on_forms "Differential form") of the exterior derivative of _f_ with _X_:

![Image 85: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}_{X}f=i_{X}\,df}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d80effa315e9da32b081a2db7029a16b3c1e2e1)
For a general differential form, the Lie derivative is likewise a contraction, taking into account the variation in _X_:

![Image 86: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}_{X}\omega =i_{X}d\omega +d(i_{X}\omega ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b02d952c6ef85f37f1121ea1463a33282cc7078c)
This identity is known variously as **Cartan formula**, **Cartan homotopy formula** or **Cartan's magic formula**. See [interior product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interior_product "Interior product") for details. The Cartan formula can be used as a definition of the Lie derivative of a differential form. Cartan's formula shows in particular that

The Lie derivative also satisfies the relation

![Image 88: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}_{fX}\omega =f{\mathcal {L}}_{X}\omega +df\wedge i_{X}\omega .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b78c0f84bcc6a3d128e87e8a60d1994ee602455e)

### The Lie derivative of a spinor field

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lie_derivative&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: The Lie derivative of a spinor field")]

A definition for Lie derivatives of [spinors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinors "Spinors") along generic spacetime vector fields, not necessarily [Killing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Killing_vector_field "Killing vector field") ones, on a general (pseudo) [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold") was already proposed in 1971 by [Yvette Kosmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvette_Kosmann-Schwarzbach "Yvette Kosmann-Schwarzbach").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_derivative#cite_note-autogenerated317-5) Later, it was provided a geometric framework which justifies her _ad hoc_ prescription within the general framework of Lie derivatives on [fiber bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundles "Fiber bundles")[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_derivative#cite_note-6) in the explicit context of gauge natural bundles which turn out to be the most appropriate arena for (gauge-covariant) field theories.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_derivative#cite_note-7)

In a given [spin manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_manifold "Spin manifold"), that is in a Riemannian manifold ![Image 130: {\displaystyle (M,g)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68e27d2e539fd0c3a9a7efab6257abd17de7fc57) admitting a [spin structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_structure "Spin structure"), the Lie derivative of a [spinor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinor "Spinor")[field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)")![Image 131: {\displaystyle \psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45e5789e5d9c8f7c79744f43ecaaf8ba42a8553a) can be defined by first defining it with respect to infinitesimal isometries (Killing vector fields) via the [André Lichnerowicz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Lichnerowicz "André Lichnerowicz")'s local expression given in 1963:[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_derivative#cite_note-8)

![Image 132: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}_{X}\psi :=X^{a}\nabla _{a}\psi -{\frac {1}{4}}\nabla _{a}X_{b}\gamma ^{a}\gamma ^{b}\psi \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e171ac624a895bd1218186025b67f297ba043697)
where ![Image 133: {\displaystyle \nabla _{a}X_{b}=\nabla _{[a}X_{b]}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/103db546d1db84978f6cf6e16b155bef765207e9), as ![Image 134: {\displaystyle X=X^{a}\partial _{a}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/980fb9397e084965e46330280d9fbe0f085c365a) is assumed to be a [Killing vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Killing_vector_field "Killing vector field"), and ![Image 135: {\displaystyle \gamma ^{a}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4524b5eb99a73b9ffff968e4a01631affa196021) are [Dirac matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_matrices "Dirac matrices").

It is then possible to extend Lichnerowicz's definition to all vector fields (generic infinitesimal transformations) by retaining Lichnerowicz's local expression for a _generic_ vector field ![Image 136: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab), but explicitly taking the antisymmetric part of ![Image 137: {\displaystyle \nabla _{a}X_{b}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a92add088560a62aed37458f3341c29698f496f2) only.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_derivative#cite_note-autogenerated317-5) More explicitly, Kosmann's local expression given in 1972 is:[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_derivative#cite_note-autogenerated317-5)

![Image 138: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}_{X}\psi :=X^{a}\nabla _{a}\psi -{\frac {1}{8}}\nabla _{[a}X_{b]}[\gamma ^{a},\gamma ^{b}]\psi \,=\nabla _{X}\psi -{\frac {1}{4}}(dX^{\flat })\cdot \psi \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/996010d923d1c8077881028f756c5f90400733bf)
where ![Image 139: {\displaystyle [\gamma ^{a},\gamma ^{b}]=\gamma ^{a}\gamma ^{b}-\gamma ^{b}\gamma ^{a}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d68ce243b0c43c962a40b9fc13aec71bdabb2916) is the commutator, ![Image 140: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab) is [exterior derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative "Exterior derivative"), ![Image 141: {\displaystyle X^{\flat }=g(X,-)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d838f11f7dcd41152db9facc40275b382e4f48b9) is the dual 1 form corresponding to ![Image 142: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) under the metric (i.e. with lowered indices) and ![Image 143: {\displaystyle \cdot }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba2c023bad1bd39ed49080f729cbf26bc448c9ba) is Clifford multiplication.

It is worth noting that the spinor Lie derivative is independent of the metric, and hence also of the [connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(differential_geometry) "Connection (differential geometry)"). This is not obvious from the right-hand side of Kosmann's local expression, as the right-hand side seems to depend on the metric through the spin connection (covariant derivative), the dualisation of vector fields (lowering of the indices) and the Clifford multiplication on the [spinor bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinor_bundle "Spinor bundle"). Such is not the case: the quantities on the right-hand side of Kosmann's local expression combine so as to make all metric and connection dependent terms cancel.

To gain a better understanding of the long-debated concept of Lie derivative of spinor fields one may refer to the original article,[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_derivative#cite_note-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_derivative#cite_note-10) where the definition of a Lie derivative of spinor fields is placed in the more general framework of the theory of Lie derivatives of sections of fiber bundles and the direct approach by Y. Kosmann to the spinor case is generalized to gauge natural bundles in the form of a new geometric concept called the [Kosmann lift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kosmann_lift "Kosmann lift").

As for the tensor counterpart, also for spinors the vanishing of the Lie derivative along a Killing vector implements on the spinor the symmetries encoded by that Killing vector. However, differently from tensors, from spinors it is possible to build bi-linear quantities (such as the velocity vector ![Image 144: {\displaystyle {\overline {\psi }}\gamma ^{a}\psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f16d12e9c609bd8edd2d08959593ae06ff85da91) or the spin axial-vector ![Image 145: {\displaystyle {\overline {\psi }}\gamma ^{a}\gamma ^{5}\psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3408b222a7bacd9ad1e180f10970abf8d17e502c)) which are tensors. A natural question that now arises is whether the vanishing of the Lie derivative along a Killing vector of a spinor is equivalent to the vanishing of the Lie derivative along the same Killing vector of all the spinor bi-linear quantities. While a spinor that is Lie-invariant implies that all its bi-linear quantities are also Lie invariant, the converse is in general not true.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_derivative#cite_note-11)

### Covariant Lie derivative

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lie_derivative&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Covariant Lie derivative")]

If we have a principal bundle over the manifold M with G as the structure group, and we pick X to be a covariant vector field as section of the tangent space of the principal bundle (i.e. it has horizontal and vertical components), then the covariant Lie derivative is just the Lie derivative with respect to X over the principal bundle.

Now, if we're given a vector field _Y_ over _M_ (but not the principal bundle) but we also have a [connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_(mathematics) "Connection (mathematics)") over the principal bundle, we can define a vector field X over the principal bundle such that its horizontal component matches _Y_ and its vertical component agrees with the connection. This is the covariant Lie derivative.

See [connection form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_form "Connection form") for more details.

### Nijenhuis–Lie derivative

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lie_derivative&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Nijenhuis–Lie derivative")]

Another generalization, due to [Albert Nijenhuis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Nijenhuis "Albert Nijenhuis"), allows one to define the Lie derivative of a differential form along any section of the bundle Ω _k_(_M_, T _M_) of differential forms with values in the tangent bundle. If _K_∈Ω _k_(_M_, T _M_) and α is a differential _p_-form, then it is possible to define the interior product _i_ _K_ α of _K_ and α. The Nijenhuis–Lie derivative is then the anticommutator of the interior product and the exterior derivative:

![Image 146: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}_{K}\alpha =[d,i_{K}]\alpha =di_{K}\alpha -(-1)^{k-1}i_{K}\,d\alpha .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6625780b9736c9c558385a08737aac20d7f80bf)
In 1931, [Władysław Ślebodziński](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W%C5%82adys%C5%82aw_%C5%9Alebodzi%C5%84ski "Władysław Ślebodziński") introduced a new differential operator, later called by [David van Dantzig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_van_Dantzig "David van Dantzig") that of Lie derivation, which can be applied to scalars, vectors, tensors and affine connections and which proved to be a powerful instrument in the study of groups of automorphisms.

The Lie derivatives of general geometric objects (i.e., sections of [natural fiber bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_bundle "Natural bundle")) were studied by [A. Nijenhuis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Nijenhuis "Albert Nijenhuis"), Y. Tashiro and [K. Yano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kentaro_Yano_(mathematician) "Kentaro Yano (mathematician)").

For a quite long time, physicists had been using Lie derivatives, without reference to the work of mathematicians. In 1940, [Léon Rosenfeld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Rosenfeld "Léon Rosenfeld")[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_derivative#cite_note-12)—and before him (in 1921) [Wolfgang Pauli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Pauli "Wolfgang Pauli")[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_derivative#cite_note-13)—introduced what he called a ‘local variation’ ![Image 147: {\displaystyle \delta ^{\ast }A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07a7e71a15b138d8f0396910fcf01d9547dadf58) of a geometric object ![Image 148: {\displaystyle A\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6aaf5ce10d6add44b973e28fb3d95f37abf3721) induced by an infinitesimal transformation of coordinates generated by a vector field ![Image 149: {\displaystyle X\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7028e89b7722d12ec0ea8780f26a9912456b63f5). One can easily prove that his ![Image 150: {\displaystyle \delta ^{\ast }A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07a7e71a15b138d8f0396910fcf01d9547dadf58) is ![Image 151: {\displaystyle -{\mathcal {L}}_{X}(A)\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/56323cd08b7f4e212d6959aa9a470a621c3c7e7e).
