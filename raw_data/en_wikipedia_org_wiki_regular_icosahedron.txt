The **regular icosahedron** (or simply icosahedron) is a [convex polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polyhedron "Convex polyhedron") that can be constructed from [pentagonal antiprism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagonal_antiprism "Pentagonal antiprism") by attaching two [pentagonal pyramids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagonal_pyramid "Pentagonal pyramid") with [regular faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon") to each of its pentagonal faces, or by putting points onto the cube. The resulting polyhedron has 20 [equilateral triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilateral_triangle "Equilateral triangle") as its faces, 30 edges, and 12 vertices. It is an example of a [Platonic solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid "Platonic solid") and of a [deltahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deltahedron "Deltahedron"). The icosahedral graph represents the [skeleton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skeleton_(topology) "Skeleton (topology)") of a regular icosahedron.

Many polyhedra and other related figures are constructed from the regular icosahedron, including its 59 [stellations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellation "Stellation"). The [great dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_dodecahedron "Great dodecahedron"), one of the [Kepler–Poinsot polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%E2%80%93Poinsot_polyhedra "Kepler–Poinsot polyhedra"), is constructed by either stellation of the [regular dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron "Regular dodecahedron") or [faceting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faceting "Faceting") of the icosahedron. Some of the [Johnson solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johnson_solid "Johnson solid") can be constructed by removing the pentagonal pyramids. The regular icosahedron's [dual polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polyhedron "Dual polyhedron") is the [regular dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron "Regular dodecahedron"), and their relation has a historical background in the comparison mensuration. It is analogous to a four-dimensional [polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytope "Polytope"), the [600-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/600-cell "600-cell").

Regular icosahedra can be found in nature; a well-known example is the [capsid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capsid "Capsid") in biology. Other applications of the regular icosahedron are the usage of its net in [cartography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartography "Cartography"), and the twenty-sided dice that may have been used in ancient times but are now [commonplace in modern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D20_System "D20 System")[tabletop role-playing games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tabletop_role-playing_games "Tabletop role-playing games").

The regular icosahedron is a twenty-sided polyhedron wherein the faces are [equilateral triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilateral_triangle "Equilateral triangle"). It is one of the eight convex [deltahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deltahedra "Deltahedra"), a polyhedron wherein all of its faces are equilateral triangles.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-1) Variously, it can be constructed as follows:

*   Started by attaching two [pentagonal pyramids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagonal_pyramid "Pentagonal pyramid") with [regular faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon") to the base of a [pentagonal antiprism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagonal_antiprism "Pentagonal antiprism"). This replaces the bases of a pentagonal antiprism with ten triangular pyramids.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-2) The regular icosahedron is classified as a [composite polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composite_polyhedron "Composite polyhedron")—a polyhedron that can be disintegrated into smaller convex polyhedra. It has alternative names called **bicapped pentagonal antiprism**,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKing2020-3) or **gyroelongated pentagonal bipyramid** due to its construction process through [gyroelongation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gyroelongation "Gyroelongation")—polyhedra construction by attaching two pyramids onto the base of an [antiprism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiprism "Antiprism").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGailiunas2023339-4)

Three mutually perpendicular [golden rectangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_rectangle "Golden rectangle"), with edges connecting their corners, form a regular icosahedron.

The regular icosahedron can be unfolded into 44,380 different [nets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_(geometry) "Net (geometry)").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDennisMcNairWoolfKauffman2018[httpsbooksgooglecombooksid4hVeDwAAQBAJpgPA169_169]-8) The earliest net appeared in [Albrecht Durer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albrecht_Durer "Albrecht Durer")'s _Painter's Manual_ in 1525.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHart1998198-9)

### Surface area and volume

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Regular_icosahedron&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Surface area and volume")]

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Regular_icosahedron.stl/250px-Regular_icosahedron.stl.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Regular_icosahedron.stl)

3D model of a regular icosahedron

The [surface area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_area "Surface area") of a polyhedron is the sum of the areas of its faces. In the case of a regular icosahedron, its surface area ![Image 8: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) is twenty times that of each of its equilateral triangle faces. Its volume ![Image 9: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) can be obtained as twenty times that of a pyramid whose base is one of its faces and whose apex is the regular icosahedron's center; or as the sum of the volume of two uniform [pentagonal pyramids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagonal_pyramid "Pentagonal pyramid") and a [pentagonal antiprism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagonal_antiprism "Pentagonal antiprism"). Given that the edge length ![Image 10: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) of a regular icosahedron, both expressions are:[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-10)![Image 11: {\displaystyle A=5{\sqrt {3}}a^{2}\approx 8.660a^{2},\qquad V={\frac {5\varphi ^{2}}{6}}a^{3}\approx 2.182a^{3}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb587f8f0a649f96bc93123ce1f7ed60a3c3e4b8)

### Relation to the spheres

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Regular_icosahedron&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Relation to the spheres")]

The [insphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insphere "Insphere") of a convex polyhedron is a sphere touching every polyhedron's face within. The [circumsphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumsphere "Circumsphere") of a convex polyhedron is a sphere that contains the polyhedron and touches every vertex. The [midsphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midsphere "Midsphere") of a convex polyhedron is a sphere tangent to every edge. Given that the edge length ![Image 12: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) of a regular icosahedron, the radius of insphere (inradius) ![Image 13: {\displaystyle r_{I}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e177223a0b94e134c613f3e13b02a6050229c1f4), the radius of circumsphere (circumradius) ![Image 14: {\displaystyle r_{C}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f4045733e6ea6c11a3061b638c8c046cb43f06a), and the radius of midsphere (midradius) ![Image 15: {\displaystyle r_{M}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e24df07649f274efde2fe766dbfb26e5c4d51409) are, respectively:[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-11)![Image 16: {\displaystyle r_{I}={\frac {\varphi ^{2}a}{2{\sqrt {3}}}}\approx 0.756a,\qquad r_{C}={\frac {\sqrt {\varphi ^{2}+1}}{2}}a\approx 0.951a,\qquad r_{M}={\frac {\varphi }{2}}a\approx 0.809a.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9de886bc95c8580cf6411a31355b333c674ee961)

A problem dating back to the ancient Greeks is determining which of two shapes has a larger volume: a regular icosahedron inscribed in a sphere, or a regular dodecahedron inscribed in the same sphere. The problem was solved by [Hero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hero_of_Alexandria "Hero of Alexandria"), [Pappus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pappus_of_Alexandria "Pappus of Alexandria"), and [Fibonacci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fibonacci "Fibonacci"), among others.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHerz-Fischler2013[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidaYjXZJwLARQCpgPA138_138%E2%80%93140]-12)[Apollonius of Perga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollonius_of_Perga "Apollonius of Perga") discovered the curious result that the ratio of volumes of these two shapes is the same as the ratio of their surface areas.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimmons2007[httpsbooksgooglecombooksid3KOst4Mon90CpgPA50_50]-13) Both volumes have formulas involving the [golden ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_ratio "Golden ratio"), but taken to different powers.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTESutton2002[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidvgo7bTxDmIsCpgPA55_55]-14) As it turns out, the regular icosahedron occupies less of the sphere's volume (60.54%) than the regular dodecahedron (66.49%).[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-15)

The [dihedral angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihedral_angle "Dihedral angle") of a regular icosahedron can be calculated by adding the angle of pentagonal pyramids with regular faces and a pentagonal antiprism. The dihedral angle of a pentagonal antiprism and pentagonal pyramid between two adjacent triangular faces is approximately 38.2°. The dihedral angle of a pentagonal antiprism between pentagon-to-triangle is 100.8°, and the dihedral angle of a pentagonal pyramid between the same faces is 37.4°. Therefore, for the regular icosahedron, the dihedral angle between two adjacent triangles, on the edge where the pentagonal pyramid and pentagonal antiprism are attached, is 37.4° + 100.8° = 138.2°.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-16)

The regular icosahedron has three types of [closed geodesics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_geodesic "Closed geodesic"). These are paths on its surface that are locally straight: they avoid the polyhedron's vertices, follow line segments across the faces that they cross, and form [complementary angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complementary_angle "Complementary angle") on the two incident faces of each edge that they cross. The first geodesic forms a [regular decagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decagon "Decagon") perpendicular to the longest diagonal and has the length ![Image 17: {\displaystyle 5}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29483407999b8763f0ea335cf715a6a5e809f44b). The other two geodesics are non-[planar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_plane "Euclidean plane"), with lengths ![Image 18: {\displaystyle 3{\sqrt {3}}\approx 5.196}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb1d72a8cb0983be822927eea2c79f0ad3930ca5) and ![Image 19: {\displaystyle 2{\sqrt {7}}\approx 5.292}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59d61664f54afcc93c829560b47da5c0bf254d1f).[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-17)

[![Image 20](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/Sphere_symmetry_group_ih.png/250px-Sphere_symmetry_group_ih.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Sphere_symmetry_group_ih.png)

Illustration of a icosahedral symmetry. The five-fold, three-fold, and two-fold are labeled in blue, red, and magenta, respectively. The mirror planes are the cyan [great circles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_circle "Great circle").

The regular icosahedron has the thirty-one axes of [rotational symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_symmetry "Rotational symmetry") (that is, rotating around an axis that results in an identical appearance). There are six axes passing through two opposite vertices, ten axes rotating a triangular face, and fifteen axes passing through any of its edges. Respectively, these axes are five-fold rotational symmetry (0°, 72°, 144°, 216°, and 288°), three-fold rotational symmetry (0°, 120°, and 240°), and two-fold rotational symmetry (0° and 180°).[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-18) The regular icosahedron also has fifteen mirror planes that can be represented as [great circles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_circle "Great circle") on a sphere. It divides the surface of a sphere into 120 triangles [fundamental domains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_domain "Fundamental domain"); these triangles are called [Mobius triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mobius_triangle "Mobius triangle"). Both reflections and rotational symmetries are the [isometries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isometry "Isometry")—transformations in order to maintain the appearance—which forms the [full icosahedral symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Full_icosahedral_symmetry "Full icosahedral symmetry")![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathrm {I} _{\mathrm {h} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a9fdfce71bda597451d015e732f835e6fc14b348) of order 120.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHart1998196-19) This symmetry group is [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_isomorphism "Group isomorphism") to the product of the rotational symmetry group and the [cyclic group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_group "Cyclic group") of size two, generated by the reflection through the center of the regular icosahedron.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTESeidel1991[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidbrziBQAAQBAJpgPA364_364]-20)

The rotational [symmetry group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_group "Symmetry group") of the regular icosahedron is isomorphic to the [alternating group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternating_group "Alternating group") on five letters. This non-[abelian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group")[simple group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_group "Simple group") is the only non-trivial [normal subgroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_subgroup "Normal subgroup") of the [symmetric group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_group "Symmetric group") on five letters.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGray2018[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidgl1oDwAAQBAJpgPA371_371]-21) Since the [Galois group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galois_group "Galois group") of the general [quintic equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quintic_equation "Quintic equation") is isomorphic to the symmetric group on five letters, and this normal subgroup is simple and non-abelian, the general quintic equation does not have a solution in radicals. The proof of the [Abel–Ruffini theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abel%E2%80%93Ruffini_theorem "Abel–Ruffini theorem") uses this simple fact,[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTERotman1998[httpsbooksgooglecombooksid0kHhBwAAQBAJpgPA75_74%E2%80%9375]-22) and [Felix Klein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein "Felix Klein") wrote a book that made use of the theory of icosahedral symmetries to derive an analytical solution to the general quintic equation.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-23)

The regular icosahedron is [isogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isogonal_figure "Isogonal figure"), [isohedral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isohedral "Isohedral"), and [isotoxal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotoxal "Isotoxal"): any two vertices, two faces, and two edges of a regular icosahedron can be transformed by rotations and reflections under its symmetry orbit, which preserves the appearance. Each regular polyhedron has a [convex hull](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_hull "Convex hull") on its edge midpoints; [icosidodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosidodecahedron "Icosidodecahedron") is the convex hull of a regular icosahedron.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTESenechal1989[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidOToVjZW9CKMCpgPA12_12]-24) Each vertex is surrounded by five equilateral triangles, so the regular icosahedron denotes ![Image 22: {\displaystyle 3.3.3.3.3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e1b7b29f098c03ad521844146993623d2b9e3818) in [vertex configuration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_configuration "Vertex configuration") or ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \{3,5\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66b0e609b5b043b56b53807d368a5a85829dc1f0) in [Schläfli symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_symbol "Schläfli symbol").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWalterDeloudi2009[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidnVx-tu596twCpgPA50_50]-25)

[![Image 24](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Twenty-sided_die_%28icosahedron%29_with_faces_inscribed_with_Greek_letters_MET_10.130.1158_001.jpg/250px-Twenty-sided_die_%28icosahedron%29_with_faces_inscribed_with_Greek_letters_MET_10.130.1158_001.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Twenty-sided_die_(icosahedron)_with_faces_inscribed_with_Greek_letters_MET_10.130.1158_001.jpg)

A twenty-sided die from Ptolemaic Egypt, inscribed with Greek letters on the faces

[![Image 25](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b2/ScatDice.JPG/250px-ScatDice.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:ScatDice.JPG)

The _Scattergories_ twenty-sided die, excluding the six letters Q, U, V, X, Y, and Z

Dice are among the most common objects that utilize different polyhedra, one of which is the regular icosahedron. The twenty-sided die was found in ancient times. One example is the die from [Ptolemaic Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemaic_Egypt "Ptolemaic Egypt"), which was later used with Greek letters inscribed on the faces in the period of Greece and Rome.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-26) Another example was found in the treasure of [Tipu Sultan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tipu_Sultan "Tipu Sultan"), which was made out of gold and with numbers written on each face.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTECromwell1997[httpsarchiveorgdetailspolyhedra0000crompage4mode1upviewtheater_4]-27)

In several [roleplaying games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roleplaying_game "Roleplaying game"), such as _[Dungeons & Dragons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dungeons\_%26\_Dragons "Dungeons & Dragons")_, the twenty-sided die (labeled as [d20](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dice#Polyhedral_dice "Dice")) is commonly used in determining success or failure of an action. It may be numbered from "0" to "9" twice, in which form it usually serves as a ten-sided die ([d10](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dice#Non-cubical_dice "Dice")); most modern versions are labeled from "1" to "20".[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-28)_[Scattergories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scattergories "Scattergories")_ is another board game in which the player names the category entries on a card within a given set time. The naming of such categories is initially with the letters contained in every twenty-sided dice.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFlanaganGregory2015[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidhViuEAAAQBAJpgPA85_85]-29)

### Natural forms and sciences

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Regular_icosahedron&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Natural forms and sciences")]

[![Image 26](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Circogonia_icosahedra.jpg/120px-Circogonia_icosahedra.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Circogonia_icosahedra.jpg)

In [virology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virology "Virology"), [herpes virus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herpesviridae "Herpesviridae") have icosahedral [shells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capsid "Capsid"), especially well-known in [adenovirus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adenovirus "Adenovirus").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGallardoP%C3%A9rez-IllanaMart%C3%ADn-Gonz%C3%A1lezSan_Mart%C3%ADn2021-30) The outer protein shell of [HIV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HIV "HIV") is enclosed in a regular icosahedron, as is the head of a typical [myovirus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myovirus "Myovirus").[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStraussStrauss200835%E2%80%9362-31) Several species of [radiolarians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiolarian "Radiolarian") discovered by [Ernst Haeckel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernst_Haeckel "Ernst Haeckel"), described their shells as like-shaped various regular polyhedra; one of which is _Circogonia icosahedra_, whose skeleton is shaped like a regular icosahedron.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-32)

In chemistry, the [closo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closo_cluster "Closo cluster")-[carboranes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboranes "Carboranes") are [compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_compound "Chemical compound") with a shape resembling the regular icosahedron.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpokoyny2013-33) The [crystal twinning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal_twinning "Crystal twinning") with [icosahedral shapes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedral_twins "Icosahedral twins") also occurs in crystals, especially [nanoparticles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanoparticle "Nanoparticle").[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHofmeister2004-34) Many [borides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal_structure_of_boron-rich_metal_borides "Crystal structure of boron-rich metal borides") and [allotropes of boron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allotropes_of_boron "Allotropes of boron") such as [α-](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allotropes_of_boron#%CE%B1-rhombohedral_boron "Allotropes of boron") and [β-rhombohedral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allotropes_of_boron#%CE%B2-rhombohedral_boron "Allotropes of boron") contain boron B 12 icosahedron as a basic structure unit.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDronskowskiKikkawaStein2017[httpsbooksgooglecombookside0VBDwAAQBAJpgPA436_435%E2%80%93436]-35)

In cartography, [R. Buckminster Fuller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R._Buckminster_Fuller "R. Buckminster Fuller") used the net of a regular icosahedron to create a map known as [Dymaxion map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dymaxion_map "Dymaxion map"), by subdividing the net into triangles, followed by calculating the grid on the Earth's surface, and transferring the results from the sphere to the polyhedron. This projection was created during the time that Fuller realized that [Greenland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenland "Greenland") is smaller than [South America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_America "South America").[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTECromwell1997[httpsarchiveorgdetailspolyhedra0000crompage7mode1upviewtheater_7]-36)

In the [Thomson problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomson_problem "Thomson problem"), concerning the minimum-energy configuration of ![Image 27: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) charged particles on a sphere, and for the [Tammes problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tammes_problem "Tammes problem") of constructing a [spherical code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_code "Spherical code") maximizing the smallest distance among the points, the minimum solution known for ![Image 28: {\displaystyle n=12}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38fb7dfd38b5db65d78bb6f2c1bf9e6e6cd07c14) places the points at the vertices of a regular icosahedron, [inscribed in a sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumscribed_sphere "Circumscribed sphere"). This configuration is proven optimal for the Tammes problem, but a rigorous solution to this instance of the Thomson problem is unknown.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWhyte1952-37)

In tensegrity, the regular icosahedron is composed of six struts and twenty-four cables that connect twelve nodes. One self-stress state is present within the combination achieved through the use of cellular [morphogenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphogenesis "Morphogenesis").[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAloui_et_al.2019-38)

[![Image 30](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Mysterium_Cosmographicum_solar_system_model.jpg/250px-Mysterium_Cosmographicum_solar_system_model.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Mysterium_Cosmographicum_solar_system_model.jpg)

The regular icosahedron is one of the five [Platonic solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid "Platonic solid"). The regular polyhedra have been known since antiquity, but are named after [Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato") who, in his [_Timaeus_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timaeus_(dialogue) "Timaeus (dialogue)") dialogue, identified these with the five [elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_elements "Classical elements"), whose elementary units were attributed these shapes: [fire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fire_(classical_element) "Fire (classical element)") (tetrahedron), [air](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_(classical_element) "Air (classical element)") (octahedron), [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_(classical_element) "Water (classical element)") (icosahedron), [earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_(classical_element) "Earth (classical element)") (cube) and the shape of the universe as a whole (dodecahedron). [Euclid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid "Euclid")'s [_Elements_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements "Euclid's Elements") defined the Platonic solids and solved the problem of finding the ratio of the circumscribed sphere's diameter to the edge length.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHeath1908262,_478,_480-39)

Following their identification with the elements by Plato, [Johannes Kepler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Kepler "Johannes Kepler") in his _[Harmonices Mundi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonices\_Mundi "Harmonices Mundi")_ sketched each of them, in particular, the regular icosahedron.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTECromwell1997[httpsarchiveorgdetailspolyhedra0000crompage55_55]-40) In his _[Mysterium Cosmographicum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mysterium\_Cosmographicum "Mysterium Cosmographicum")_, he also proposed a model of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") based on the placement of Platonic solids in a concentric sequence of increasing radius of the inscribed and circumscribed spheres whose radii gave the distance of the six known planets from the common center. The ordering of the solids, from innermost to outermost, consisted of: [regular octahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_octahedron "Regular octahedron"), regular icosahedron, [regular dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron "Regular dodecahedron"), [regular tetrahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_tetrahedron "Regular tetrahedron"), and [cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube "Cube").[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTELivio2003147-41)

[Leonardo da Vinci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci "Leonardo da Vinci")'s _[Divina proportione](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divina\_proportione "Divina proportione")_ drew both regular icosahedron and dodecahedron.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHart1998195-42)

[![Image 31](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Icosahedron_graph.svg/250px-Icosahedron_graph.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Icosahedron_graph.svg)

Icosahedral graph

Every [Platonic graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_graph "Platonic graph"), including the **icosahedral graph**, is a [polyhedral graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedral_graph "Polyhedral graph"): they [can be drawn in the plane without crossing its edges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planar_graph "Planar graph"), and [the removal of any two of its vertices leaves a connected subgraph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K-vertex-connected_graph "K-vertex-connected graph"). According to [Steinitz's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steinitz%27s_theorem "Steinitz's theorem"), the icosahedral graph endowed with these heretofore properties represents the [skeleton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skeleton_(topology) "Skeleton (topology)") of a regular icosahedron.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBickle2020[httpsbooksgooglecombooksid2sbVDwAAQBAJpgPA147_147]-43)

The icosahedral graph has twelve vertices, the same number of vertices as a regular icosahedron. These vertices are connected by five edges from each vertex, making the icosahedral graph [5-regular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_graph "Regular graph").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFallatHogben2014[httpsbooksgooglecombooksid8hnYCwAAQBAJpgSA46-PA29_29]Section_46-44) The icosahedral graph is [Hamiltonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_graph "Hamiltonian graph"), because it has a cycle that can visit each vertex exactly once.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHopkins2004-45) Any subset of four vertices has three connected edges, with one being the central of all of those three, and the icosahedral graph has no [induced subgraph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Induced_subgraph "Induced subgraph"), a [claw-free graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claw-free_graph "Claw-free graph").[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChudnovskySeymour2005-46)

The icosahedral graph is a [graceful graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graceful_graph "Graceful graph"), meaning that each vertex can be labeled with an [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer") between 0 and 30 inclusive, in such a way that the [absolute difference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_difference "Absolute difference") between the labels of an edge's two vertices is different for every edge.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGallian1998-47)

### Inscribed in other Platonic solids

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Regular_icosahedron&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Inscribed in other Platonic solids")]

The regular icosahedron is the [dual polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polyhedron "Dual polyhedron") of the [regular dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron "Regular dodecahedron"). A regular icosahedron can be inscribed in a regular dodecahedron by placing its vertices at the face centers of the regular dodecahedron, and vice versa. The regular dodecahedron has icosahedral symmetry.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-49)

Apart from the construction above, the regular icosahedron can be inscribed in a regular octahedron by placing its twelve vertices on the twelve edges of the octahedron such that they divide each edge in the [golden section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_section "Golden section"). Because the resulting segments are unequal, there are five different ways to do this consistently, so five disjoint icosahedra can be inscribed in each octahedron.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoxeterdu_ValFlatherPetrie19384-50) Another relation between the two is that they are part of the progressive transformation from the [cuboctahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuboctahedron "Cuboctahedron")'s rigid struts and flexible vertices, known as [jitterbug transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jitterbug_transformation "Jitterbug transformation").[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVerheyen1989-51)

A regular icosahedron of edge length ![Image 33: {\displaystyle 1/\varphi \approx 0.618}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee2956736e09abd4c4d71925d61a4979d14025d2) can be inscribed in a unit-edge-length cube by placing six of its edges—three orthogonal opposite pairs—on the square faces of the cube, centered on the face centers and parallel or perpendicular to the square's edges.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBorovik20068%E2%80%939%C2%A75._How_to_draw_an_icosahedron_on_a_blackboard-52) Because there are five times as many icosahedron edges as cube faces, there are five ways to do this consistently, so five disjoint icosahedra can be inscribed in each cube. The edge lengths of the cube and the inscribed icosahedron are in the golden ratio.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The regular icosahedron has a large number of [stellations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellation "Stellation"), constructed by extending the faces of a regular icosahedron. [Coxeter et al. (1938)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#CITEREFCoxeterdu_ValFlatherPetrie1938) in their work, _[The Fifty-Nine Icosahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Fifty-Nine\_Icosahedra "The Fifty-Nine Icosahedra")_, identified fifty-nine stellations for the regular icosahedron. The regular icosahedron itself is the zeroth stellation of an icosahedron, and the first stellation has each original face augmented by a low pyramid. The [final stellation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Final_stellation_of_the_icosahedron "Final stellation of the icosahedron") includes all of the cells in the icosahedron's stellation diagram, meaning every three intersecting face planes of the icosahedral core intersect either on a vertex of this polyhedron or inside it.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-53)

The [triakis icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triakis_icosahedron "Triakis icosahedron") is a [Catalan solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalan_solid "Catalan solid") constructed by attaching the base of triangular pyramids onto each face of a regular icosahedron, the [Kleetope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kleetope "Kleetope") of an icosahedron.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrigagliaPalladinoVaccaro2018-55) The [truncated icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truncated_icosahedron "Truncated icosahedron") is an [Archimedean solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedean_solid "Archimedean solid") constructed by truncating the vertices of a regular icosahedron; the resulting polyhedron may be considered as a [football](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ball_(association_football) "Ball (association football)") because of having a pattern of numerous hexagonal and pentagonal faces,[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-56) or a structural form of [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") known as [buckminsterfullerene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckminsterfullerene "Buckminsterfullerene") that has 60 carbon atoms bonded together.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKatz2006[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidGmQR1tuk5IgCpgPA361_361]-57)

A [Johnson solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johnson_solid "Johnson solid") is a polyhedron whose faces are all regular but which is not [uniform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_polyhedron "Uniform polyhedron"). In other words, they do not include the [Archimedean solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedean_solid "Archimedean solid"), the [Catalan solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalan_solid "Catalan solid"), the [prisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_(geometry) "Prism (geometry)"), or the [antiprisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiprism "Antiprism"). Some Johnson solids can be derived by removing part of a regular icosahedron, a process known as _diminishment_. They are [gyroelongated pentagonal pyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gyroelongated_pentagonal_pyramid "Gyroelongated pentagonal pyramid"), [metabidiminished icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabidiminished_icosahedron "Metabidiminished icosahedron"), and [tridiminished icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tridiminished_icosahedron "Tridiminished icosahedron"), which remove one, two, and three pentagonal pyramids from the icosahedron, respectively.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBerman1971-58)

The [edge-contracted icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge-contracted_icosahedron "Edge-contracted icosahedron") has a surface like a regular icosahedron but with [some faces lie in the same plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coplanar "Coplanar").[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTETsuruta2024[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidkLckEQAAQBAJpgPA112_112]-59)

### Spherical icosahedron

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Regular_icosahedron&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Spherical icosahedron")]

[![Image 44](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Uniform_tiling_532-t2.png/250px-Uniform_tiling_532-t2.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Uniform_tiling_532-t2.png)

[![Image 45](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Impossiball_solved.jpg/250px-Impossiball_solved.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Impossiball_solved.jpg)

The spherical icosahedron represents a regular icosahedron projected to a sphere, a part of [spherical polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_polyhedron "Spherical polyhedron"). It can be modeled by the [arc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_(geometry) "Arc (geometry)") of [great circles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_circle "Great circle"), creating bounds as the edges of [spherical triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_triangle "Spherical triangle").[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYackel2013-60) Identified by [R. Buckminster Fuller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R._Buckminster_Fuller "R. Buckminster Fuller"), there are [31 great circles in a spherical icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/31_great_circles_of_the_spherical_icosahedron "31 great circles of the spherical icosahedron").[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-61) Its dual is the [spherical dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_dodecahedron "Spherical dodecahedron").[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEYackel2013-60) The appearance of this shape may be found in _[Impossiball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impossiball "Impossiball")_, a similar toy to [Rubik's Cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubik%27s_Cube "Rubik's Cube").[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJoyner2008[httpsbooksgooglecombooksid6an_DwAAQBAJpgPA82_82]-62)

[![Image 46](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Jessen_icosahedron_with_snub_icosahedron.png/250px-Jessen_icosahedron_with_snub_icosahedron.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Jessen_icosahedron_with_snub_icosahedron.png)

Regular icosahedron and its non-convex variant, which differs from Jessen's icosahedron in having different vertex positions and non-right-angled dihedrals

Another related shape can be derived by keeping the vertices of a regular icosahedron in their original positions and replacing certain pairs of equilateral triangles with pairs of isosceles triangles. The resulting polyhedron has the non-convex version of the regular icosahedron. Nonetheless, it is occasionally incorrectly known as [Jessen's icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jessen%27s_icosahedron "Jessen's icosahedron") because of the similar visual, of having the same combinatorial structure and symmetry as Jessen's icosahedron;[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-63) the difference is that the non-convex one does not form a tensegrity structure and does not have right-angled dihedrals.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPugh1976b[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidMcEOfJu3NQACpgPA11_11],_[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidMcEOfJu3NQACpgPA26_26]-64)

The regular icosahedron is [analogous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-dimensional_space#Dimensional_analogy "Four-dimensional space") to the [600-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/600-cell "600-cell"), a [regular 4-dimensional polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_4-polytope#Regular_convex_4-polytopes "Regular 4-polytope").[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBarnes2012[httpsbooksgooglecombooksid7YCUBUd-4BQCpgPA79_79]-65) This polytope has six hundred regular tetrahedra as its [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(geometry) "Cell (geometry)").[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStillwell2005[httpbooksgooglecombooksidI1QPQic_PxwCpgPA173_173]-66) The regular icosahedron is a [cell for the honeycomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedral_honeycomb "Icosahedral honeycomb") in a [hyperbolic three-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_space "Hyperbolic space").[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoxeter1999[httpbooksgooglecombooksidp4o-Uf-i-IUCpgPA212_212%E2%80%93213]-67)

*   [Binary icosahedral group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_icosahedral_group "Binary icosahedral group"), a double cover of the group of rotations of the icosahedron 
    *   [Icosian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosian "Icosian"), a set of quaternions with the same symmetry

*   [Disdyakis triacontahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disdyakis_triacontahedron "Disdyakis triacontahedron"), a 120-sided convex polyhedron whose faces are the fundamental domains in icosahedral symmetry. Six such regions can be painted on each face of an icosahedron
*   [Dogic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dogic "Dogic"), an icosahedral version of [Rubik's Cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubik%27s_Cube "Rubik's Cube")
*   [Geodesic grid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic_grid "Geodesic grid")
*   [Geodesic polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic_polyhedron "Geodesic polyhedron")
*   [Goldberg polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goldberg_polyhedron "Goldberg polyhedron"), a convex polyhedron made from hexagons and pentagons with icosahedral symmetry
*   [Hemi-icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemi-icosahedron "Hemi-icosahedron")
*   [Polyhedral map projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedral_map_projection "Polyhedral map projection"), map projections taking the globe to a polyhedron
*   [Rhombic triacontahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombic_triacontahedron "Rhombic triacontahedron"), a 30-sided convex polyhedron with icosahedral symmetry whose faces lie in the 2-fold symmetry planes
*   [Tensegrity icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensegrity_icosahedron "Tensegrity icosahedron")
*   [Tetrahedral-icosahedral honeycomb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedral-icosahedral_honeycomb "Tetrahedral-icosahedral honeycomb"), another hyperbolic honeycomb involving some icosahedral cells
*   [Zometool](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zometool "Zometool"), a construction toy based on the icosahedral symmetry system

The stellation process on the icosahedron creates a number of related [polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron "Polyhedron") and [compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_polyhedron "Compound polyhedron") with [icosahedral symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedral_symmetry "Icosahedral symmetry").
