A **regular dodecahedron** or **pentagonal dodecahedron**[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-sathyanarayana-1) is a [dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron "Dodecahedron") composed of [regular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon")[pentagonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagonal "Pentagonal") faces, three meeting at each [vertex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)"). It is one of the [Platonic solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid "Platonic solid"), described in [Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato")'s [dialogues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_dialog "Platonic dialog") as the shape of the universe itself. [Johannes Kepler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Kepler "Johannes Kepler") used the dodecahedron in his 1596 [model of the Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mysterium_Cosmographicum "Mysterium Cosmographicum"). However, the dodecahedron and other Platonic solids had already been described by other philosophers since antiquity.

The regular dodecahedron is a [truncated trapezohedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truncated_trapezohedron "Truncated trapezohedron") because it is the result of [truncating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truncation_(geometry) "Truncation (geometry)") axial vertices of a [pentagonal trapezohedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagonal_trapezohedron "Pentagonal trapezohedron"). It is also a [Goldberg polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goldberg_polyhedron "Goldberg polyhedron") because it is the initial polyhedron to construct new polyhedra by the process of [chamfering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chamfer "Chamfer"). It has a relation with other Platonic solids, one of them is the [regular icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron "Regular icosahedron") as its [dual polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polyhedron "Dual polyhedron"). Other new polyhedra can be constructed by using a regular dodecahedron.

The regular dodecahedron's metric properties and construction are associated with the [golden ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_ratio "Golden ratio"). The regular dodecahedron is featured in some artistic and narrative works. Some toys and artifacts are also shaped like regular dodecahedra, including the [Roman dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_dodecahedron "Roman dodecahedron"). Regular dodecahedra can also be found in nature and supramolecules, as well as the shape of the universe. The [skeleton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skeleton_(topology) "Skeleton (topology)") of a regular dodecahedron can be represented as the [graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_(discrete_mathematics) "Graph (discrete mathematics)") called the **dodecahedral graph**, a [Platonic graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_graph "Platonic graph"). Its property of the [Hamiltonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_graph "Hamiltonian graph"), a [path](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Path_(graph_theory) "Path (graph theory)") visits all of its vertices exactly once, can be found in a toy called [icosian game](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosian_game "Icosian game").

As a Platonic solid
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Regular_dodecahedron&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: As a Platonic solid")]

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Kepler_Dodecahedron_Universe.jpg/250px-Kepler_Dodecahedron_Universe.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Kepler_Dodecahedron_Universe.jpg)

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Mysterium_Cosmographicum_solar_system_model.jpg/250px-Mysterium_Cosmographicum_solar_system_model.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Mysterium_Cosmographicum_solar_system_model.jpg)

The regular dodecahedron is a polyhedron with twelve pentagonal faces, thirty edges, and twenty vertices.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-sutton-2) It is one of the [Platonic solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid "Platonic solid"), a set of polyhedrons in which the faces are [regular polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygons "Regular polygons") that are [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_(geometry) "Congruence (geometry)") and the same number of faces meet at a vertex.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-hs-3) This set of polyhedrons is named after [Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato"). In _[Theaetetus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theaetetus\_(dialogue) "Theaetetus (dialogue)")_, a dialogue of Plato, Plato hypothesized that the classical elements were made of the five uniform regular solids. Plato described the regular dodecahedron, obscurely remarked, "...the god used [it] for arranging the constellations on the whole heaven". [Timaeus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timaeus_(dialogue) "Timaeus (dialogue)"), as a personage of Plato's dialogue, associates the other four Platonic solids—[regular tetrahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_tetrahedron "Regular tetrahedron"), [cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube_(geometry) "Cube (geometry)"), [regular octahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_octahedron "Regular octahedron"), and [regular icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron "Regular icosahedron")—with the four [classical elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_element "Classical element"), adding that there is a fifth solid pattern which, though commonly associated with the regular dodecahedron, is never directly mentioned as such; "this God used in the delineation of the universe."[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-4)[Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") also postulated that the heavens were made of a fifth element, which he called [aithêr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aether_(classical_element) "Aether (classical element)") (_aether_ in Latin, _ether_ in American English).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-wildberg-5)

Following its attribution with nature by Plato, [Johannes Kepler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Kepler "Johannes Kepler") in his _[Harmonices Mundi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonices\_Mundi "Harmonices Mundi")_ sketched each of the Platonic solids, one of them is a regular dodecahedron.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-cromwell-6) In his _[Mysterium Cosmographicum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mysterium\_Cosmographicum "Mysterium Cosmographicum")_, Kepler also proposed the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") by using the Platonic solids setting into another one and separating them with six spheres resembling the six planets. The ordered solids started from the innermost to the outermost: regular octahedron, regular icosahedron, regular dodecahedron, regular tetrahedron, and cube.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTELivio2003[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidbUARfgWRH14CpgPA147_147]-7)

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Dodecahedron.stl/250px-Dodecahedron.stl.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Dodecahedron.stl)

3D model of a regular dodecahedron

Many antiquity philosophers described the regular dodecahedron, including the rest of the Platonic solids. [Theaetetus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theaetetus_(mathematician) "Theaetetus (mathematician)") gave a mathematical description of all five and may have been responsible for the first known proof that no other convex regular polyhedra exist. [Euclid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid "Euclid") completely mathematically described the Platonic solids in the [_Elements_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements "Euclid's Elements"), the last book (Book XIII) of which is devoted to their properties. Propositions 13–17 in Book XIII describe the construction of the tetrahedron, octahedron, cube, icosahedron, and dodecahedron in that order. For each solid, Euclid finds the ratio of the diameter of the circumscribed sphere to the edge length. In Proposition 18 he argues that there are no further convex regular polyhedra. [Iamblichus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iamblichus "Iamblichus") states that [Hippasus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippasus "Hippasus"), a Pythagorean, perished in the sea, because he boasted that he first divulged "the sphere with the twelve pentagons".[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-8)

The regular dodecahedron, as the family of Platonic solids, is a [regular polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polyhedron "Regular polyhedron"). It is [isogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isogonal_figure "Isogonal figure"), [isohedral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isohedral "Isohedral"), and [isotoxal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotoxal "Isotoxal"): any two vertices, two faces, and two edges of a regular dodecahedron can be transformed by rotations and reflections under its symmetry orbit respectively, which preserves the appearance.

### Relation to the regular icosahedron

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Regular_dodecahedron&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Relation to the regular icosahedron")]

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/01_Dodekaeder_umschreibt_Ikosaeder.svg/300px-01_Dodekaeder_umschreibt_Ikosaeder.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:01_Dodekaeder_umschreibt_Ikosaeder.svg)

The regular icosahedron inside the regular dodecahedron

The [dual polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polyhedron "Dual polyhedron") of a dodecahedron is the [regular icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron "Regular icosahedron"). One property of the dual polyhedron generally is that the original polyhedron and its dual share the same [three-dimensional symmetry group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_groups_in_three_dimensions "Point groups in three dimensions"). In the case of the regular dodecahedron, it has the same symmetry as the regular icosahedron, the [icosahedral symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedral_symmetry "Icosahedral symmetry")![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mathrm {I} _{\mathrm {h} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a9fdfce71bda597451d015e732f835e6fc14b348).[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-erickson-9) The regular dodecahedron has ten three-fold axes passing through pairs of opposite vertices, six five-fold axes passing through the opposite faces centers, and fifteen two-fold axes passing through the opposite sides midpoints.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-weils-10)

When a regular dodecahedron is inscribed in a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere"), it occupies more of the sphere's volume (66.49%) than an icosahedron inscribed in the same sphere (60.55%).[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-be-11) The resulting of both spheres' volumes initially began from the problem by ancient Greeks, determining which of two shapes has a larger volume: an icosahedron inscribed in a sphere, or a dodecahedron inscribed in the same sphere. The problem was solved by [Hero of Alexandria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hero_of_Alexandria "Hero of Alexandria"), [Pappus of Alexandria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pappus_of_Alexandria "Pappus of Alexandria"), and [Fibonacci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fibonacci "Fibonacci"), among others.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-herz-12)[Apollonius of Perga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollonius_of_Perga "Apollonius of Perga") discovered the curious result that the ratio of volumes of these two shapes is the same as the ratio of their surface areas.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-simmons-13) Both volumes have formulas involving the golden ratio but are taken to different powers.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-sutton-2)

[Golden rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_rectangle "Golden rectangle") may also related to both regular icosahedron and regular dodecahedron. The regular icosahedron can be constructed by intersecting three golden rectangles perpendicularly, arranged in two-by-two orthogonal, and connecting each of the golden rectangle's vertices with a segment line. There are 12 regular icosahedron vertices, considered as the center of 12 regular dodecahedron faces.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-marar-14)

### Relation to the regular tetrahedron

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Regular_dodecahedron&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Relation to the regular tetrahedron")]

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Chiroicosahedron-in-dodecahedron.png/250px-Chiroicosahedron-in-dodecahedron.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Chiroicosahedron-in-dodecahedron.png)

Five tetrahedra inscribed in a dodecahedron. Five opposing tetrahedra (not shown) can also be inscribed.

As two opposing tetrahedra can be inscribed in a cube, and five cubes can be inscribed in a dodecahedron, ten tetrahedra in five cubes can be inscribed in a dodecahedron: two opposing sets of five, with each set covering all 20 vertices and each vertex in two tetrahedra (one from each set, but not the opposing pair). As stated by [Coxeter et al. (1938)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#CITEREFCoxeterdu_ValFlatherPetrie1938),[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-coxeter-1938-15)

> "Just as a tetrahedron can be inscribed in a cube, so a cube can be inscribed in a dodecahedron. By reciprocation, this leads to an octahedron circumscribed about an icosahedron. In fact, each of the twelve vertices of the icosahedron divides an edge of the octahedron according to the "[golden section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_section "Golden section")". Given the icosahedron, the circumscribed octahedron can be chosen in five ways, giving a [compound of five octahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_of_five_octahedra "Compound of five octahedra"), which comes under our definition of [stellated icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellated_icosahedron "Stellated icosahedron"). (The reciprocal compound, of five cubes whose vertices belong to a dodecahedron, is a stellated [triacontahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triacontahedron "Triacontahedron").) Another stellated icosahedron can at once be deduced, by stellating each octahedron into a [stella octangula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stella_octangula "Stella octangula"), thus forming a [compound of ten tetrahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_of_ten_tetrahedra "Compound of ten tetrahedra"). Further, we can choose one tetrahedron from each stella octangula, so as to derive a [compound of five tetrahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_of_five_tetrahedra "Compound of five tetrahedra"), which still has all the rotation symmetry of the icosahedron (i.e. the icosahedral group), although it has lost the reflections. By reflecting this figure in any plane of symmetry of the icosahedron, we obtain the complementary set of five tetrahedra. These two sets of five tetrahedra are enantiomorphous, i.e. not directly congruent, but related like a pair of shoes. [Such] a figure which possesses no plane of symmetry (so that it is enantiomorphous to its mirror-image) is said to be _[chiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiral "Chiral")_."

The regular dodecahedron can be interpreted as the [Goldberg polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goldberg_polyhedron "Goldberg polyhedron"). It is a set of polyhedra containing hexagonal and pentagonal faces. Other than two Platonic solids—tetrahedron and cube—the regular dodecahedron is the initial of the Goldberg polyhedron construction, and the next polyhedron is obtained by truncating all of its edges, a process called [chamfer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chamfer_(geometry) "Chamfer (geometry)"). This process can be continuously repeated, resulting in more new Goldberg's polyhedra. These polyhedra are classified as the first class of a Goldberg polyhedron.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-hart-23)

The [stellations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellation "Stellation") of the regular dodecahedron make up three of the four [Kepler–Poinsot polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%E2%80%93Poinsot_polyhedra "Kepler–Poinsot polyhedra"). The first stellation of a regular dodecahedron is constructed by attaching its layer with pentagonal pyramids, forming a [small stellated dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_stellated_dodecahedron "Small stellated dodecahedron"). The second stellation is by attaching the small stellated dodecahedron with [wedges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wedge_(geometry) "Wedge (geometry)"), forming a [great dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_dodecahedron "Great dodecahedron"). The third stellation is by attaching the great dodecahedron with the sharp triangular pyramids, forming a [great stellated dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_stellated_dodecahedron "Great stellated dodecahedron").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTECromwell1997[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidOJowej1QWpoCpgPA265_265]-24)

Other related polyhedra are:

*   [Pentagonal trapezohedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagonal_trapezohedron "Pentagonal trapezohedron"), the commencement of constructing the regular dodecahedron by truncating its two axial vertices.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-montroll-25)
*   Two [Archimedean solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedean_solid "Archimedean solid"): [truncated dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truncated_dodecahedron "Truncated dodecahedron") and [snub dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snub_dodecahedron "Snub dodecahedron"). Respectively, these polyhedra are obtained by truncating all vertices of a regular dodecahedron and separating the pentagonal faces of a regular dodecahedron before filling the gap with equilateral triangles.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-ziya-26)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-kappraff-27)
*   [Pentakis dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentakis_dodecahedron "Pentakis dodecahedron") is the [Kleetope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kleetope "Kleetope") of a regular dodecahedron is obtained by affixing pentagonal pyramids to each face, similar to the small stellated dodecahedron, although the difference is the pyramids' altitude. It is also a [Catalan solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalan_solid "Catalan solid").[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-cg-28)
*   Four [Johnson solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johnson_solid "Johnson solid")—[augmented dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augmented_dodecahedron "Augmented dodecahedron"), [parabiaugmented dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabiaugmented_dodecahedron "Parabiaugmented dodecahedron"), [metabiaugmented dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabiaugmented_dodecahedron "Metabiaugmented dodecahedron"), [triaugmented dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triaugmented_dodecahedron "Triaugmented dodecahedron")—obtained by attaching four pentagonal pyramids onto the faces of a regular dodecahedron.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-berman-29)

### In arts and popular cultures

Roman dodecahedra

[![Image 29](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Megaminx6.jpg/142px-Megaminx6.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Megaminx6.jpg)

A solved six-color sided Megaminx

Regular dodecahedra have been used as dice and may also have been used as divinatory devices. Small hollow bronze [Roman dodecahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_dodecahedra "Roman dodecahedra") have been found in the northern parts of the Roman empire; their purpose is uncertain.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-guggenberger-30)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-hill-31)

In [20th-century art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/20th-century_art "20th-century art"), regular dodecahedra appear in the work of [M. C. Escher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M._C._Escher "M. C. Escher"), such as his lithographs _[Reptiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reptiles\_(M.\_C.\_Escher) "Reptiles (M. C. Escher)")_,[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-dunlap-32) and [Salvador Dalí](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salvador_Dal%C3%AD "Salvador Dalí")'s painting _[The Sacrament of the Last Supper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Sacrament\_of\_the\_Last\_Supper "The Sacrament of the Last Supper")_ in which the room is a hollow regular dodecahedron).[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-FOOTNOTELivio2003[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidbUARfgWRH14CpgPA9_9]-33)[Gerard Caris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gerard_Caris "Gerard Caris") based his entire artistic oeuvre on the regular dodecahedron and the pentagon, presented as a new art movement coined Pentagonism.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-34)

In modern [role-playing games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Role-playing_games "Role-playing games"), the regular dodecahedron is often used as a twelve-sided die, one of the more common [polyhedral dice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dice#Polyhedral_dice "Dice"). The [Megaminx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megaminx "Megaminx") is a twisted puzzle similar to the [Rubik's Cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubik%27s_Cube "Rubik's Cube") but the shape is pentagonal faces dodecahedral.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-joyner-35)

In the children's novel _[The Phantom Tollbooth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Phantom\_Tollbooth "The Phantom Tollbooth")_, the regular dodecahedron appears as a character in the land of Mathematics. Each face of the regular dodecahedron describes the various [facial expressions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Facial_expression "Facial expression"), swiveling to the front as required to match his mood.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-36)

In [Bertrand Russell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bertrand_Russell "Bertrand Russell")'s 1954 short story _The Mathematician's Nightmare: The Vision of Professor Squarepunt_, the number 5 said: "I am the number of fingers on a hand. I make pentagons and pentagrams. And but for me dodecahedra could not exist; and, as everyone knows, the universe is a dodecahedron. So, but for me, there could be no universe."[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-37)

Crystal structure of Co 20 L 12 dodecahedron

The fossil [coccolithophore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coccolithophore "Coccolithophore")_[Braarudosphaera bigelowii](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Braarudosphaera\_bigelowii "Braarudosphaera bigelowii")_, a unicellular coastal [phytoplanktonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phytoplanktonic "Phytoplanktonic")[alga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alga "Alga"), has a calcium carbonate shell with a regular dodecahedral structure about 10 micrometers across.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-hokh-38)

The hydrocarbon [dodecahedrane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedrane "Dodecahedrane") and [quasicrystal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasicrystal "Quasicrystal") such as [holmium–magnesium–zinc quasicrystal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holmium%E2%80%93magnesium%E2%80%93zinc_quasicrystal "Holmium–magnesium–zinc quasicrystal") have a dodecahedral shape (see figure).[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-cf-39) Some regular crystals, such as [garnet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Garnet "Garnet") and [diamond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diamond "Diamond") are also said to exhibit "dodecahedral" [habit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal_habit "Crystal habit"), but this statement actually refers to the [rhombic dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombic_dodecahedron "Rhombic dodecahedron") shape.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-wn-40)

Various models have been proposed for the global geometry of the universe. These proposals include the [Poincaré dodecahedral space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homology_sphere#Cosmology "Homology sphere"), a positively curved space consisting of a regular dodecahedron whose opposite faces correspond (with a small twist). This was proposed by [Jean-Pierre Luminet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Luminet "Jean-Pierre Luminet") and colleagues in 2003,[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-luminet-41) and an optimal orientation on the sky for the model was estimated in 2008.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-rbsg-42)

[![Image 34](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Jogo_icosiano_01.jpg/250px-Jogo_icosiano_01.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Jogo_icosiano_01.jpg)

The dodecahedral graph's Hamiltonian property and the mathematical toy [Icosian game](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosian_game "Icosian game")

According to [Steinitz's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steinitz%27s_theorem "Steinitz's theorem"), the [graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_(discrete_mathematics) "Graph (discrete mathematics)") can be represented as the [skeleton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skeleton_(topology) "Skeleton (topology)") of a polyhedron; roughly speaking, a framework of a polyhedron. Such a graph has two properties. It is [planar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planar_graph "Planar graph"), meaning the edges of a graph are connected to every vertex without crossing other edges. It is also [three-connected graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K-vertex-connected_graph "K-vertex-connected graph"): whenever a graph with more than three vertices and two of its vertices removed, the edges remain connected.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-grunbaum-2003-43)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-ziegler-44) The skeleton of a regular dodecahedron can be represented as a graph, and it is called the **dodecahedral graph**, a [Platonic graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_graph "Platonic graph").[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-rudolph-45)

This graph can also be constructed as the [generalized Petersen graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_Petersen_graph "Generalized Petersen graph")![Image 35: {\displaystyle G(10,2)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/70f978207d9fd0e32515f4136d9c9a4bf70975e9), where the vertices of a decagon are connected to those of two pentagons, one pentagon connected to odd vertices of the decagon and the other pentagon connected to the even vertices.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-ps-46) Geometrically, this can be visualized as the ten-vertex equatorial belt of the dodecahedron connected to the two 5-vertex polar regions, one on each side.

The high degree of symmetry of the polygon is replicated in the properties of this graph, which are [distance-transitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance-transitive_graph "Distance-transitive graph"),[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-gr-47)[distance-regular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance-regular_graph "Distance-regular graph"), and [symmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_graph "Symmetric graph"). The [automorphism group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_automorphism "Graph automorphism") has order a hundred and twenty. The vertices can be [colored](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_coloring "Graph coloring") with 3 colors, as can the edges, and the [diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_diameter "Graph diameter") is five.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-48)

The dodecahedral graph is [Hamiltonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_graph "Hamiltonian graph"), meaning a [path](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Path_(graph_theory) "Path (graph theory)") visits all of its vertices exactly once. The name of this property is named after [William Rowan Hamilton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Rowan_Hamilton "William Rowan Hamilton"), who invented a [mathematical game](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_game "Mathematical game") known as the [icosian game](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosian_game "Icosian game"). The game's object was to find a [Hamiltonian cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_cycle "Hamiltonian cycle") along the edges of a dodecahedron.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron#cite_note-bondy-murty-49)

*   [Icosahedral twins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedral_twins "Icosahedral twins") - Nanoparticles which can have the shape of a regular dodecahedron.
