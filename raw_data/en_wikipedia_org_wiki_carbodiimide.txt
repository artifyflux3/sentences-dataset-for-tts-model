General structure of _trans_-carbodiimides: The core functional group is shown in blue with attached [R groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Substituent "Substituent")

In [organic chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_chemistry "Organic chemistry"), a **carbodiimide** (systematic IUPAC name: **methanediimine**[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimide#cite_note-1)) is a [functional group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_group "Functional group") with the formula RN=C=NR. On [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth") they are exclusively synthetic, but in [interstellar space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_space "Interstellar space") the parent compound HN=C=NH has been detected by its [maser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maser "Maser") emissions.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimide#cite_note-2)

A well known carbodiimide is [dicyclohexylcarbodiimide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dicyclohexylcarbodiimide "Dicyclohexylcarbodiimide"), which is used in [peptide synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peptide_synthesis "Peptide synthesis").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimide#cite_note-Williams-3) Dialkylcarbodiimides are stable. Some diaryl derivatives tend to convert to [dimers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimer_(chemistry) "Dimer (chemistry)") and [polymers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymers "Polymers") upon standing at room temperature, though this mostly occurs with low melting point carbodiimides that are liquids at room temperature.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimide#cite_note-Monagle-4) Solid diaryl carbodiimides are more stable, but can slowly undergo [hydrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrolysis "Hydrolysis") in the presence of water over time.

Structure and bonding
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbodiimide&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Structure and bonding")]

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/CSD_CIF_PMCBIM.jpg/250px-CSD_CIF_PMCBIM.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:CSD_CIF_PMCBIM.jpg)

Structure of C(NCHPh 2)2 as determined by [X-ray crystallography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_crystallography "X-ray crystallography") (color scheme: gray = C, blue = N)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimide#cite_note-Kristall-5)

From the perspective of bonding, carbodiimides are [isoelectronic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isoelectronic "Isoelectronic") with [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide"). Three principal resonance structures describe carbodiimides:

RN=C=NR ↔ RN+≡C-N−R ↔ RN−-C≡N+R
The N=C=N core is relatively linear and the C-N=C angles approach 120°. In the case of C(NCHPh 2)2, the central N=C=N angle is 170° and the C-N=C angles are within 1° of 126°.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimide#cite_note-Kristall-5) The C=N distances are short, nearly 120 pm, as is characteristic of double bonds. Carbodiimides are [chiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiral "Chiral"), possessing C 2-symmetry and therefore [axial chirality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axial_chirality "Axial chirality").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimide#cite_note-6) However, due to the low [energy barrier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_barrier "Energy barrier") to the molecule rotating and thereby converting quickly between its isomers, the actual isolation of one optical isomer of a carbodiimide is extremely difficult. It has been demonstrated at least once, in the case of conformationally restricted [cyclic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_compound "Cyclic compound") carbodiimides; though there are other reports of one-handed axially chiral carbodiimides, their validity has since been called into question on experimental and computational grounds.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimide#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimide#cite_note-8)

The parent compound, methanediimine, (HN=C=NH), is a [tautomer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tautomer "Tautomer") of [cyanamide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanamide "Cyanamide").

### From thioureas and ureas

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbodiimide&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: From thioureas and ureas")]

A classic route to carbodiimides involves dehydrosulfurization of [thioureas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thiourea "Thiourea"). A typical reagent for this process is [mercuric oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercuric_oxide "Mercuric oxide"):[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimide#cite_note-CR-9)

(R(H)N)2 CS + HgO → (RN)2 C + HgS + H 2 O
This reaction can often be conducted as stated, even though carbodiimides react with water. In some cases, a dehydrating agent is added to the reaction mixture.

The dehydration of N,N'-dialkylureas gives carbodiimides:

(R(H)N)2 CO → (RN)2 C + H 2 O
[Phosphorus pentoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_pentoxide "Phosphorus pentoxide")[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimide#cite_note-ulrich-10) and p-[Toluenesulfonyl chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toluenesulfonyl_chloride "Toluenesulfonyl chloride") have been used as a dehydrating agents.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimide#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimide#cite_note-SF-12)

Isocyanates can be converted to carbodiimides with loss of carbon dioxide:[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimide#cite_note-13)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimide#cite_note-Monagle-4)

2 RN=C=O → (RN)2 C + CO 2
The reaction is catalyzed by [phosphine oxides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine_oxide "Phosphine oxide"). This reaction is reversible.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimide#cite_note-CR-9)

Compared to other heteroallenes, carbodiimides are very weak electrophiles and only react with nucleophiles in the presence of catalysts, such as acids.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimide#cite_note-14) In this way, [guanidines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guanidine "Guanidine") can be prepared.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimide#cite_note-Williams-3) As weak bases, carbodiimides bind to Lewis acids to give [adducts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adduct "Adduct").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimide#cite_note-CR-9)

Carbodiimides are reagents for the [Moffatt oxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moffatt_oxidation "Moffatt oxidation"), a protocol for conversion of an alcohol to a carbonyl (ketone or aldehyde) using [dimethyl sulfoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfoxide "Dimethyl sulfoxide") as the oxidizing agent:[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimide#cite_note-15)

(CH 3)2 SO + (CyN)2 C + R 2 CHOH → (CH 3)2 S + (CyNH)2 CO + R 2 C=O
Typically the sulfoxide and diimide are used in excess.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimide#cite_note-16) The reaction generates [dimethyl sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimethyl_sulfide "Dimethyl sulfide") and a [urea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urea "Urea") as byproducts.

In [organic synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_synthesis "Organic synthesis"), compounds containing the carbodiimide functionality are used as dehydration agents. Specifically they are often used to convert [carboxylic acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboxylic_acid "Carboxylic acid") to [amides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amide "Amide") or [esters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ester "Ester"). Additives, such as [N-hydroxybenzotriazole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-hydroxybenzotriazole "N-hydroxybenzotriazole") or [N-hydroxysuccinimide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-hydroxysuccinimide "N-hydroxysuccinimide"), are often added to increase yields and decrease side reactions.

[![Image 3: Amide coupling utilizing a carbodiimide](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Carbodiimide_Amide_Coupling_Scheme.png/500px-Carbodiimide_Amide_Coupling_Scheme.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Carbodiimide_Amide_Coupling_Scheme.png "Amide coupling utilizing a carbodiimide")

Amide coupling utilizing a carbodiimide

Polycarbodiimides can also be used as crosslinkers for aqueous resins, such as [polyurethane dispersions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyurethane_dispersion "Polyurethane dispersion") or acrylic dispersion. Here the polycarbodiimide reacts with carboxylic acids, whose functional groups are often present in such aqueous resins, to form N-acyl urea. The result is the formation of covalent bonds between the polymer chains, making them crosslinked.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimide#cite_note-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimide#cite_note-18)

#### Amide formation pathway

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbodiimide&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Amide formation pathway")]

The formation of an amide using a carbodiimide is a common reaction, but carries the risk of several side reactions. The acid **1** will react with the carbodiimide to produce the key intermediate: the O-acylisourea **2**, which can be viewed as a [carboxylic ester](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ester "Ester") with an activated leaving group. The O-acylisourea will react with amines to give the desired amide **3** and urea **4**.

The possible reactions of the O-acylisourea **2** produce both desired and undesired products. The O-acylisourea **2** can react with an additional carboxylic acid **1** to give an [acid anhydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid_anhydride "Acid anhydride")**5**, which can react further to give the amide **3**. The main undesired reaction pathway involves the rearrangement of the O-acylisourea **2** to the stable _N_-[acylurea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acylurea "Acylurea")**6**. The use of solvents with low dielectric constants such as [dichloromethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dichloromethane "Dichloromethane") or [chloroform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloroform "Chloroform") can minimize this side reaction.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbodiimide#cite_note-19)

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/DCC_Structure.png/250px-DCC_Structure.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:DCC_Structure.png)

Structure of [_N_,_N_'-dicyclohexylcarbodiimide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N,N%27-Dicyclohexylcarbodiimide "N,N'-Dicyclohexylcarbodiimide")

DCC (acronym for [_N_,_N_'-dicyclohexylcarbodiimide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N,N%27-Dicyclohexylcarbodiimide "N,N'-Dicyclohexylcarbodiimide")) was one of the first carbodiimides developed as a reagent. It is widely used for amide and ester formation, especially for [solid-phase synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid-phase_synthesis "Solid-phase synthesis") of [peptides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peptide "Peptide"). DCC has achieved popularity mainly because of its high-yielding amide coupling reactions and the fact that it is quite inexpensive.

However, DCC does have some serious drawbacks, and its use is often avoided for several reasons:

1.   The byproduct _N_,_N_'-[dicyclohexylurea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dicyclohexylurea "Dicyclohexylurea") is mostly removed by filtration, but trace impurities can be difficult to remove. It is incompatible with traditional solid-phase peptide synthesis.
2.   DCC is a potent [allergen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allergen "Allergen"), and repeated contact with skin increases the probability of sensitization to the compound. Clinical reports of individuals who cannot enter rooms where peptide coupling agents are used have been reported.

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/DIC_Structure.png/250px-DIC_Structure.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:DIC_Structure.png)

Structure of [_N_,_N_'-diisopropylcarbodiimide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N,N%27-Diisopropylcarbodiimide "N,N'-Diisopropylcarbodiimide")

In contrast to DCC, DIC ([_N_,_N_'-diisopropylcarbodiimide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N,N%27-Diisopropylcarbodiimide "N,N'-Diisopropylcarbodiimide")) is a liquid. Its hydrolysis product, N,N'-diisopropylurea, is soluble in organic solvents.

EDC is a water-soluble carbodiimide reagent used for a wide range of purposes. Apart from uses similar to those of DCC and DIC, it is also used for various biochemical purposes as a [crosslinker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-link "Cross-link") or [chemical probe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleic_acid_structure_determination#Carbodiimides "Nucleic acid structure determination").

1-cyclohexyl-(2-morpholinoethyl)carbodiimide metho-_p_-toluene sulfonate is a carbodiimide developed for the [chemical probing of RNA structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleic_acid_structure_determination#Carbodiimides "Nucleic acid structure determination") in biochemistry.
