[SIS Building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SIS_Building "SIS Building") (1994) by [Terry Farrell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terry_Farrell_(architect) "Terry Farrell (architect)"): Detail view of the British intelligence service ([MI6](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MI6 "MI6")) headquarters in London, a "hulking, postmodern fortress" influenced by 1930s industrial modernist design and Mayan and Aztec temples.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-2)

**Postmodernism** encompasses a variety of [artistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artistic "Artistic"), [cultural](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culture "Culture"), and [philosophical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophical "Philosophical") movements that claim to mark a break from [modernism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modernism "Modernism"). They have in common the conviction that it is no longer possible to rely upon previous ways of depicting the world. Still, there is disagreement among experts about its more precise meaning even within narrow contexts.

The term began to acquire its current range of meanings in literary criticism and architectural theory during the 1950s–1960s. In opposition to modernism's alleged self-seriousness, postmodernism is characterized by its playful use of [eclectic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eclecticism "Eclecticism") styles and performative [irony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony "Irony"), among other features. Critics claim it supplants [moral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morality "Morality"), [political](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics "Politics"), and [aesthetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aesthetics "Aesthetics") ideals with mere style and spectacle.

In the 1990s, "postmodernism" came to denote a general – and, in general, celebratory – response to [cultural pluralism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_pluralism "Cultural pluralism"). Proponents align themselves with [feminism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feminism "Feminism"), [multiculturalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiculturalism "Multiculturalism"), and [postcolonialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postcolonialism "Postcolonialism"). Building upon [poststructural](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poststructural "Poststructural") theory, postmodern thought defined itself by the rejection of any single, foundational historical narrative. This called into question the legitimacy of the [Enlightenment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment "Age of Enlightenment") account of progress and rationality. Critics allege that its premises lead to a [nihilistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nihilistic "Nihilistic") form of [relativism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativism "Relativism"). In this sense, it has become a term of abuse in [popular culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Popular_culture "Popular culture").

"Postmodernism" is "a highly contested term",[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuchanan2018-3) referring to "a particularly unstable concept",[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertens199511-4) that "names many different kinds of cultural objects and phenomena in many different ways".[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConnor2013567-5) It may be described simply as a general mood or _[Zeitgeist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeitgeist "Zeitgeist")_.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTESpencer2011217-6)[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-13)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-14)

Although postmodernists are generally united in their effort to transcend the perceived limits of modernism, "modernism" also means different things to different critics in various arts.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertens19954%E2%80%935-15) Further, there are outliers on even this basic stance; for instance, literary critic [William Spanos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Spanos "William Spanos") conceives postmodernism not in period terms but in terms of a certain kind of literary imagination so that pre-modern texts such as [Euripides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euripides "Euripides")' _[Orestes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orestes "Orestes")_ or [Cervantes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cervantes "Cervantes")' _[Don Quixote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Don\_Quixote "Don Quixote")_ count as postmodern.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertens199546-16)

According to scholar [Louis Menand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Menand "Louis Menand"), "Postmodernism is the [Swiss Army knife](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swiss_Army_knife "Swiss Army knife") of critical concepts. It's definitionally overloaded, and it can do almost any job you need done."[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-17) From an opposing perspective, media theorist [Dick Hebdige](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dick_Hebdige "Dick Hebdige") criticized the vagueness of the term, enumerating a long list of otherwise unrelated concepts that people have designated as postmodernism, from "the décor of a room" or "a 'scratch' video", to fear of [nuclear armageddon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_armageddon "Nuclear armageddon") and the "implosion of meaning", and stated that anything that could signify all of those things was "a buzzword".[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-Hebdige-18)

All this notwithstanding, scholar Hans Bertens offers the following:

> If there is a common denominator to all these postmodernisms, it is that of a crisis in representation: a deeply felt loss of faith in our ability to represent the real, in the widest sense. No matter whether they are aesthestic [sic], epistemological, moral, or political in nature, the representations that we used to rely on can no longer be taken for granted.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertens199510-19)

In practical terms, postmodernisms share an attitude[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-20)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-21) of skepticism towards grand explanations and established ways of doing things. In art, literature, and architecture, this attitude blurs boundaries between styles and genres, and encourages freely mixing elements, challenging traditional distinctions like [high art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_culture "High culture") versus [popular art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Popular_culture "Popular culture").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-22) In science, it emphasizes multiple ways of seeing things, and how our cultural and personal backgrounds shape how we see the world, making it impossible to be completely [objective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subjectivity_and_objectivity_(philosophy) "Subjectivity and objectivity (philosophy)").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-23) In philosophy, education, history, politics, and many other fields, it encourages critical re-examination of established institutions and social norms, embracing diversity, and breaking down disciplinary boundaries.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-24)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-25) Though these ideas weren't strictly new, postmodernism amplified them, using an often playful, at times deeply critical, attitude of pervasive skepticism to turn them into defining features.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-26)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-27)

Historical overview
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Postmodernism&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Historical overview")]

Two broad cultural movements, modernism and postmodernism, emerged in response to profound changes in the Western world. The [Industrial Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_Revolution "Industrial Revolution"), [urbanization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urbanization "Urbanization"), [secularization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secularization "Secularization"), [technological advances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technological_change "Technological change"), two [world wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_war "World war"), and [globalization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Globalization "Globalization") deeply disrupted the social order. Modernism emerged in the late 1800s, seeking to redefine fundamental truths and values through a radical rethinking of traditional ideas and forms across many fields. Postmodernism emerged in the mid-20th century with a skeptical perspective that questioned the notion of universal truths and reshaped modernist approaches by embracing the complexity and contradictions of modern life.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBestKellner19912-28)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-29)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-30)

The term "postmodernism" first appeared in print in 1870,[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWelschSandbothe199776-31)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHassan198712ff-32) but it only began to enter circulation with its current range of meanings in the 1950s—60s.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrooker2003202-33)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuchanan2018-3)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertens19954-34)

The term "postmodern" was first used in 1870 by the artist John Watkins Chapman, who described "a Postmodern style of painting" as a departure from French [Impressionism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impressionism "Impressionism").[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWelschSandbothe199776-31)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHassan198712ff-32) Similarly, the first citation given by the _[Oxford English Dictionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxford\_English\_Dictionary "Oxford English Dictionary")_ is dated to 1916, describing [Gus Mager](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gus_Mager "Gus Mager") as "one of the few 'post' modern painters whose style is convincing".[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-35)

[Episcopal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Episcopal_Church_(United_States) "Episcopal Church (United States)") priest and cultural commentator J. M. Thompson, in a 1914 article, uses the term to describe changes in attitudes and beliefs in the critique of religion, writing, "the _raison d'être_ of Post-Modernism is to escape from the double-mindedness of [modernism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modernism_(Roman_Catholicism) "Modernism (Roman Catholicism)") by being thorough in its criticism by extending it to religion as well as theology, to [Catholic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic "Catholic") feeling as well as to Catholic tradition".[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEThompson1914733-36) Cultural critic [Randolph Bourne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Randolph_Bourne "Randolph Bourne") used the word to describe Japan in his essay "Trans-National America."[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-37) In 1926, [Bernard Iddings Bell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernard_Iddings_Bell "Bernard Iddings Bell"), president of [St. Stephen's College](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bard_College "Bard College") and also an Episcopal priest, published _Postmodernism and Other Essays_, which marks the first use of the term to describe an historical period following modernity.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-38)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMadsen1995-39) The essay criticizes lingering socio-cultural norms, attitudes, and practices of the [Enlightenment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment "Age of Enlightenment"). It is also critical of a purported cultural shift away from traditional Christian beliefs.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBell1926-40)[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBirzer2015-41)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTERussello2007-42)

The term "postmodernity" was first used in an academic historical context as a general concept for a movement by [Arnold J. Toynbee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arnold_J._Toynbee "Arnold J. Toynbee") in a 1939 essay, which states that "Our own Post-Modern Age has been inaugurated by the general war of 1914–1918".[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEToynbee196143-43)

In 1942, the literary critic and author H. R. Hays describes postmodernism as a new literary form.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-44) Also in the arts, the term was first used in 1949 to describe a dissatisfaction with the [modernist architectural movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_architecture "Modern architecture") known as the [International Style](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Style_(architecture) "International Style (architecture)").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConnor2013567-5)

Although these early uses anticipate some of the concerns of the debate in the second part of the 20th century, there is little direct continuity in the discussion.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertens199519-45) Just when the new discussion begins, however, is also a matter of dispute. Various authors place its beginnings in the 1950s, 1960s, 1970s, and 1980s.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrooker2003203-46)

### Theoretical development

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Postmodernism&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Theoretical development")]

In the mid-1970s, the American sociologist [Daniel Bell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daniel_Bell "Daniel Bell") provided a general account of the postmodern as an effectively [nihilistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nihilistic "Nihilistic") response to modernism's alleged assault on the [Protestant work ethic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protestant_work_ethic "Protestant work ethic") and its rejection of what he upheld as traditional values.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertens199530-47) The ideals of modernity, per his diagnosis, were degraded to the level of consumer choice.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConnor20045-48) This research project, however, was not taken up in a significant way by others until the mid-1980s when the work of [Jean Baudrillard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Baudrillard "Jean Baudrillard") and [Fredric Jameson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fredric_Jameson "Fredric Jameson"), building upon art and literary criticism, reintroduced the term to sociology.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertens1995201-49)

Discussion about the postmodern in the second part of the 20th century was most articulate in areas with a large body of critical discourse around the [modernist movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modernism "Modernism"). Even here, however, there continued to be disagreement about such basic issues as whether postmodernism is a break with modernism, a renewal and intensification of modernism,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConnor2013567-5) or even, both at once, a rejection and a radicalization of its historical predecessor.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertens19954%E2%80%935-15)

While discussions in the 1970s were dominated by literary criticism, these were supplanted by architectural theory in the 1980s.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConnor200412-50) Some of these conversations made use of French poststructuralist thought, but only after these innovations and critical discourse in the arts did postmodernism emerge as a philosophical term in its own right.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAylesworth2015Introduction_&_%C2%A72-51)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuchanan2018-3)

#### In literary and architectural theory

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Postmodernism&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: In literary and architectural theory")]

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Creeley.jpg/250px-Creeley.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Creeley.jpg)

The poet Robert Creeley in 1972

According to Hans Bertens and [Perry Anderson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perry_Anderson "Perry Anderson"), the [Black Mountain poets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Mountain_poets "Black Mountain poets")[Charles Olson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Olson "Charles Olson") and [Robert Creeley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Creeley "Robert Creeley") first introduced the term "postmodern" in its current sense during the 1950s.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-52)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuchanan2018-3) Their stance against modernist poetry – and Olson's [Heideggerian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heideggerian "Heideggerian") orientation – were influential in the identification of postmodernism as a polemical position opposed to the [rationalist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rationalism "Rationalism") values championed by the [Enlightenment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Enlightenment "Age of Enlightenment") project.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertens199519-45)

During the 1960s, this affirmative use gave way to a pejorative use by the [New Left](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Left "New Left"), who used it to describe a waning commitment among youth to the political ideals [socialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialism "Socialism") and [communism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communism "Communism").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuchanan2018-3) The literary critic [Irving Howe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irving_Howe "Irving Howe"), for instance, denounced postmodern literature for being content to merely reflect, rather than actively attempt to refashion, what he saw as the "increasingly shapeless" character of contemporary society.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertens199521-53)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuchanan2018-3)

In the 1970s, this changed again, largely under the influence of the literary critic [Ihab Hassan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ihab_Hassan "Ihab Hassan")'s large-scale survey of works that he said could no longer be called modern. Taking the Black Mountain poets an exemplary instance of the new postmodern type, Hassan celebrates its [Nietzschean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nietzschean "Nietzschean") playfulness and cheerfully anarchic spirit, which he sets off against the high seriousness of modernism.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuchanan2018-3)[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertens199524-54)

(Yet, from another perspective, [Friedrich Nietzsche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche "Friedrich Nietzsche")'s attack on Western philosophy and [Martin Heidegger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Heidegger "Martin Heidegger")'s critique of metaphysics posed deep theoretical problems not necessarily a cause for aesthetic celebration. Their further influence on the conversation about postmodernism, however, would be largely mediated by French [poststructuralism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poststructuralism "Poststructuralism").[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBestKellner199122%E2%80%9323-55))

If literature were at the center of the discussion in the 1970s, architecture was at the center in the 1980s.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConnor200412-50) The architectural theorist [Charles Jencks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Jencks "Charles Jencks"), in particular, connected the artistic [avant-garde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avant-garde "Avant-garde") to social change in a way that captured attention outside of academia.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuchanan2018-3) Jencks, much influenced by the American architect [Robert Venturi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Venturi "Robert Venturi"),[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertens199555-56) celebrated a plurality of forms and encourages participation and active engagement with the local context of the built environment.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertens199559%E2%80%9360-57) He presented this as in opposition to the "authoritarian style" of International Modernism.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConnor2013567-5)

#### The influence of poststructuralism

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Postmodernism&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: The influence of poststructuralism")]

In the 1970s, postmodern criticism increasingly came to incorporate poststructuralist theory, particularly the [deconstructive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deconstruction "Deconstruction") approach to texts most strongly associated with [Jacques Derrida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Derrida "Jacques Derrida"), who attempted to demonstrate that the whole [foundationalist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foundationalist "Foundationalist") approach to language and knowledge was untenable and misguided.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBestKellner199121-58) It is during this period that postmodernism came to be particularly equated with a kind of anti-representational self-reflexivity.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertens199570-59)[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-62)

In the 1980s, some critics began to take an interest in the work of [Michel Foucault](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michel_Foucault "Michel Foucault"). This introduced a political concern about social power-relations into discussions about postmodernism.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertens19957,_79-63) This was also the beginning of the affiliation of postmodernism with [feminism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feminism "Feminism") and [multiculturalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiculturalism "Multiculturalism").[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertens19958,_70-64) The art critic [Craig Owens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Craig_Owens_(critic) "Craig Owens (critic)"), in particular, not only made the connection to feminism explicit, but went so far as to claim feminism for postmodernism wholesale,[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertens199592-65) a broad claim resisted by even many sympathetic feminists such as [Nancy Fraser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nancy_Fraser "Nancy Fraser") and Linda Nicholson.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertens1995190%E2%80%93196-66)

Although postmodern criticism and thought drew on philosophical ideas from early on, "postmodernism" was only introduced to the expressly philosophical lexicon by [Jean-François Lyotard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Fran%C3%A7ois_Lyotard "Jean-François Lyotard") in his 1979[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-67)_[The Postmodern Condition: A Report on Knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Postmodern\_Condition:\_A\_Report\_on\_Knowledge "The Postmodern Condition: A Report on Knowledge")_. This work served as a catalyst for many of the subsequent intellectual debates around the term.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAylesworth2015Introduction_&_%C2%A72-51)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuchanan2018-3)

By the 1990s, postmodernism had become increasingly identified with critical and philosophical discourse directly about postmodernity or the postmodern idiom itself.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConnor20044-68) No longer centered on any particular art or even the arts in general, it instead turned to address the more general problems posed to society in general by a new proliferation of cultures and forms.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConnor200412-50) It is during this period that it also came to be associated with [postcolonialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postcolonialism "Postcolonialism") and [identity politics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_politics "Identity politics").[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConnor20045-48)

Around this time, postmodernism also began to be conceived in popular culture as a general "philosophical disposition" associated with a loose sort of [relativism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativism "Relativism"). In this sense, the term also started to appear as a "casual term of abuse" in non-academic contexts.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConnor20045-48) Others identified it as an aesthetic "lifestyle" of eclecticism and playful self-irony.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrooker2003203-46)

The basis for what became known later as the [Science Wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_Wars "Science Wars") was the 1962 publication of _[The Structure of Scientific Revolutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Structure\_of\_Scientific\_Revolutions "The Structure of Scientific Revolutions")_ by the physicist and historian of science [Thomas Kuhn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Kuhn "Thomas Kuhn").[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoldman2021208-69) Kuhn presented the direction of scientific inquiry — the kind of questions that can be asked, and what counts as a correct answer — as governed by a "paradigm" defining what counts as "normal science" during any given period.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoldman2021201-70) While not based on postmodern ideas or [Continental philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_philosophy "Continental philosophy"), Kuhn's intervention set the agenda for much of _The Postmodern Condition_ and has subsequently been presented as the beginning of "postmodern epistemology" in the philosophy of science.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJameson1984vii-71)[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrant201195%E2%80%9396-72)

In Kuhn's 1962 framework, the assumptions introduced by new paradigms make them "mutually incommensurable" with previous ones, although they may provide improved explanations of the material world.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoldman2021203%E2%80%93206-73)[[d]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-75) A more radical version of incommensurablity, introduced by the philosopher of science [Paul Feyerabend](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Feyerabend "Paul Feyerabend"), made stronger claims that connected the largely Anglo-American debate about science to the development of poststructuralism in France.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoldman2021218-76)

To some, the stakes were more than epistemological.[[e]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-79) The philosopher [Israel Scheffler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israel_Scheffler "Israel Scheffler"), for instance, argued that the ever-expanding body of scientific knowledge embodies a sort of "moral principle" protecting society from its authoritarian and tribal tendencies.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoldman2021209%E2%80%93210-80) In this way, with the addition of the poststructuralist influence, the debate about science expanded into a debate about Western culture in general.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoldman2021243-81)

The French political philosophers [Alain Renaut](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Alain_Renaut&action=edit&redlink=1 "Alain Renaut (page does not exist)")[[fr](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alain_Renaut "fr:Alain Renaut")] and [Luc Ferry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luc_Ferry "Luc Ferry") began a series of responses to this interpretation of postmodernism, and these inspired the physicist [Alan Sokal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alan_Sokal "Alan Sokal") to submit a deliberately nonsensical paper to a postmodernist journal, where it was accepted and published in 1996.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoldman2021244%E2%80%93247-82) Although the so-called [Sokal hoax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sokal_hoax "Sokal hoax") proved nothing about postmodernism or science, it added to the public perception of a high-stakes intellectual "war" that had already been introduced to the general public by popular books published in the late '80s and '90s.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoldman2021244%E2%80%93245-83)[[f]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-84) By the late '90s, however, the debate had largely subsided, in part due to the recognition that it had been staged between [strawman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strawman "Strawman") versions of postmodernism and science alike.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrossmann202155-78)

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/95/Warhol-Campbell_Soup-1-screenprint-1968.jpg/250px-Warhol-Campbell_Soup-1-screenprint-1968.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Warhol-Campbell_Soup-1-screenprint-1968.jpg)

Andy Warhol's _Campbell's Soup I_ (1968)

Postmodernism encompasses a wide range of artistic movements and styles. In visual arts, [pop art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pop_art "Pop art"), [conceptual art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conceptual_art "Conceptual art"), [feminist art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feminist_art "Feminist art"), [video art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Video_art "Video art"), [minimalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimalism "Minimalism"), and [neo-expressionism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neo-expressionism "Neo-expressionism") are among the approaches recognized as postmodern.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-85) The label extends to diverse musical genres and artists: [John Cage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Cage "John Cage"), [Madonna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madonna "Madonna"), and [punk rock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punk_rock "Punk rock") all meet postmodern definitions. Literature, film, architecture, theater, fashion, dance, and many other creative disciplines saw postmodern expression. As an example, [Andy Warhol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andy_Warhol "Andy Warhol")'s pop art across multiple mediums challenged traditional distinctions between high and low culture, and blurred the lines between fine art and commercial design. His work, exemplified by the iconic _[Campbell's Soup Cans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campbell%27s\_Soup\_Cans "Campbell's Soup Cans")_ series during the 1960s, brought the postmodernist sensibility to mainstream attention.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:14-86)[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:13-87)

Criticism of postmodernist movements in the arts include objections to departure from beauty, the reliance on language for the art to have meaning, a lack of coherence or comprehensibility, deviation from clear structure, and consistent use of dark and negative themes.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-paglia-88)[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-89)

[![Image 4: Michael Graves "Portland Building" (1982)](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Portland_Building_1982.jpg/250px-Portland_Building_1982.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Portland_Building_1982.jpg)

[Portland Building](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portland_Building "Portland Building") (1982) by [Michael Graves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Graves "Michael Graves"), considered the first built example of postmodern architecture in a tall building[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-trib-nov20112-90) and "a seminal Postmodern work"[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-D-GravesDies2-91)

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/Espicopal_Acad_int.JPG/250px-Espicopal_Acad_int.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Espicopal_Acad_int.JPG)

Interior of the Chapel at the [Episcopal Academy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Episcopal_Academy "Episcopal Academy") near [Newtown Square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newtown_Square "Newtown Square"), PA by alumnus of the Academy architect [Robert Venturi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Venturi "Robert Venturi")

Scholarship regarding postmodernism and architecture is closely linked with the writings of critic-turned-architect [Charles Jencks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Jencks "Charles Jencks"), beginning with lectures in the early 1970s and his essay "The Rise of Post-Modern Architecture" from 1975.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-92) His _magnum opus_, however, is the book _The Language of Post-Modern Architecture_, first published in 1977, and since running to seven editions[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-93) (in which he famously wrote: "Modern architecture died in St. Louis, Missouri, on 15 July 1972 at 3:32 p.m. (or thereabouts) when the infamous [Pruitt–Igoe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pruitt%E2%80%93Igoe "Pruitt–Igoe") scheme, or rather several of its slab blocks, were given the final coup de grâce by dynamite."[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-94)).

Jencks makes the point that postmodernism (like modernism) varies for each field of art, and that for architecture it is not just a reaction to modernism but what he terms _double coding_: "Double Coding: the combination of Modern techniques with something else (usually traditional building) in order for architecture to communicate with the public and a concerned minority, usually other architects."[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-95)

In their book, "Revisiting Postmodernism", [Terry Farrell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terry_Farrell_(architect) "Terry Farrell (architect)") and Adam Furman argue that postmodernism brought a more joyous and sensual experience to the culture, particularly in architecture.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-96) For instance, in response to the modernist slogan of [Ludwig Mies van der Rohe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Mies_van_der_Rohe "Ludwig Mies van der Rohe") that "less is more", the postmodernist Robert Venturi rejoined that "less is a bore".[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-97)

The term "postmodern dance" is most strongly associated with the [Judson Dance Theater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judson_Dance_Theater "Judson Dance Theater"), located in New York's [Greenwich Village](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenwich_Village "Greenwich Village") during the 1960s and 1970s. Perhaps its most important principle is taken from the composer [John Cage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Cage "John Cage")'s efforts to break down the distinction between art and life,[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBanes2008-98)[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:0-99) developed in particular by the American dancer and choreographer [Merce Cunningham](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Merce_Cunningham "Merce Cunningham"), Cage's partner.[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:0-99) The Judson dancers "[stripped] dance of its theatrical conventions such as virtuoso technique, fanciful costumes, complex storylines, and the traditional stage [and] drew on everyday movements (sitting, walking, kneeling, and other gestures) to create their pieces, often performing them in ordinary spaces."[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-100)[Anna Halprin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anna_Halprin "Anna Halprin")'s San Francisco Dancers' Workshop, established in the 1950s to explore beyond the technical constraints of modern dance, pioneered ideas later developed at Judson;[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-101) Halprin, [Simone Forti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simone_Forti "Simone Forti"), and [Yvonne Rainer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yvonne_Rainer "Yvonne Rainer") are considered "giants of the field".[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-102)

The Judson collective included trained dancers, visual artists, filmmakers, writers, and composers, exchanging approaches, and critiquing traditional dance,[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-103) with a focus "more on the intellectual process of creating dance than the end result".[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-104) The end of the 1970s saw a distancing from this analytical postmodern dance, and a return to the expression of meaning.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:22-105) In the 1980s and 1990s, dance began to incorporate other typically postmodern features such as the mixing of genres, challenging high–low cultural distinctions, and incorporating a political dimension.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBanes2008-98)

Postmodern film aims to subvert the mainstream [conventions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convention_(norm) "Convention (norm)") of [narrative structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Narrative_structure "Narrative structure") and [characterization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characterization "Characterization"), and to test the audience's [suspension of disbelief](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suspension_of_disbelief "Suspension of disbelief").[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-Susan12-106)[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-Laurent13-107)[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-Linda22-108) Typically, such films also break down the cultural divide between [high](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_culture "High culture") and [low](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_culture "Low culture")[art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Art "Art") and often upend typical portrayals of [gender](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gender "Gender"), [race](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Race_(classification_of_humans) "Race (classification of humans)"), [class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_class "Social class"), [genre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genre "Genre"), and [time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time "Time") with the goal of creating something that does not abide by traditional narrative expression.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-109)

Certain key characteristics are used to separate the postmodern from modernist cinema and traditional narrative film.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:2-110)[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-archive.org-111) One is an extensive use of [homage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homage_(arts) "Homage (arts)") or [pastiche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pastiche "Pastiche"), imitating the style or character of other artistic works. A second is [meta-reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meta-reference "Meta-reference") or self-reference, highlighting the relation of the image to other images in media and not to any kind of external reality.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:2-110) Viewers are reminded that the film itself is only a film, perhaps through the use of [intertextuality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intertextuality "Intertextuality"), in which the film's characters reference other works of fiction. A third characteristic is stories that [unfold out of chronological order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlinear_narrative "Nonlinear narrative"), deconstructing or fragmenting time to emphasize the constructed nature of film. Another common element is a bridging of the gap between [highbrow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Highbrow "Highbrow") and [lowbrow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_culture "Low culture"),.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-Laurent13-107)[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-Linda22-108)[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:2-110)[Contradictions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contradictions "Contradictions") of all sorts are crucial to postmodernism.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-Laurent13-107)[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-Mary1-112)

[Ridley Scott](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ridley_Scott "Ridley Scott")'s _[Blade Runner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blade\_Runner "Blade Runner")_ (1982) has been widely studied as a prime example of postmodernism. The setting is a future [dystopia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dystopia "Dystopia") where "replicants", enhanced [android](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Android_(robot) "Android (robot)") workers nearly indistinguishable from humans, are hunted down when they escape from their jobs. The film blurs boundaries between genres and cultures, and fuses disparate styles and periods: futuristic visuals "mingle with drab 1940s clothes and offices, punk rock hairstyles, pop Egyptian styles and oriental culture."[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:2-110)[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-Laurent13-107) The blending of [film noir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Film_noir "Film noir") and [science-fiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science-fiction "Science-fiction") into [tech noir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tech_noir "Tech noir") illustrates the deconstruction of both cinema and genre.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-113) The film can also be seen as an example of major studios using the "mystique and cachet of the term 'postmodern' as a sales pitch", resulting in [Hollywood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cinema_of_the_United_States "Cinema of the United States") movies that "demonstrate all the postmodern characteristics".[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:2-110) From another perspective, "critical responses to _Blade Runner_ fall on either side of a modern/postmodern line" – critical analysis from "modernist" and "postmodernist" approaches produce entirely different interpretations.[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-114)

In 1971, the American literary theorist [Ihab Hassan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ihab_Hassan "Ihab Hassan") made "postmodernism" popular in literary studies with his influential book, _The Dismemberment of Orpheus: Toward a Postmodern Literature_. According to scholar David Herwitz, American writers such as [John Barth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Barth "John Barth") (who had controversially declared that the novel was "exhausted" as a genre), [Donald Barthelme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Barthelme "Donald Barthelme"), and [Thomas Pynchon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Pynchon "Thomas Pynchon") responded in various ways to the stylistic innovations of _[Finnegans Wake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finnegans\_Wake "Finnegans Wake")_ and the late work of [Samuel Beckett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samuel_Beckett "Samuel Beckett"). Postmodern literature often calls attention to issues regarding its own complicated connection to reality. The postmodern novel plays with language, twisted plots, multiple narrators, and unresolved endings, unsettling the conventional idea of the novel as faithfully reflecting the world.[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHerwitz2008History_of_Postmodernism-115)

In _Postmodernist Fiction_ (1987), [Brian McHale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brian_McHale "Brian McHale") details the shift from modernism to postmodernism, arguing that postmodernist works developed out of modernism, moving from concern with questions about the nature and limits of knowledge about one's "world" ("[epistemological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epistemology "Epistemology") dominant") to concern with questions of modes of being and existence in relation to "different kinds of worlds" ("[ontological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ontology "Ontology") dominant").[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-116) McHale's "What Was Postmodernism?" (2007)[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-117) follows [Raymond Federman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raymond_Federman "Raymond Federman")'s lead in now using the past tense when discussing postmodernism. Others argue that postmodernism in literature utilizes compositional and semantic practices such as inclusivity, intentional indiscrimination, nonselection, and "logical impossibility."[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-118)

American singer-songwriter [Madonna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madonna "Madonna")

Postmodern influence extends across all areas of music; its accessibility to a general audience requires an understanding of references, irony, and pastiche that varies widely between artists and their works.[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:8-119) In [popular music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Popular_music "Popular music"), [Madonna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madonna "Madonna"), [David Bowie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Bowie "David Bowie"), and [Talking Heads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Talking_Heads "Talking Heads") have been singled out by critics and scholars as postmodern icons. The belief that [art music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Art_music "Art music") – serious, classical music – holds higher cultural and technical value than [folk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Folk_music "Folk music") and popular traditions, lost influence under postmodern analysis, as musical hybrids and crossovers attracted scholarly attention.[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:8-119)[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:9-120)

Across musical traditions, postmodernism can be identified through several core characteristics: genre mixing; irony, humor, and self-parody; "surface" exploration with less concern for formal structure than in modernist approaches; and a return to tonality.[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:9-120) This represents a loss of authority of the Eurocentric perspective on music and the rise of [world music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_music "World music") as influenced by postmodern values. Composers took different routes: some returned to traditional modes over experimentation, others challenged the authority of dominant musical structures, others intermingled disparate sources.[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:8-119)

The composer [Jonathan Kramer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jonathan_Kramer "Jonathan Kramer") has written that avant-garde musical compositions (which some would consider modernist rather than postmodernist) "defy more than seduce the listener, and they extend by potentially unsettling means the very idea of what music is."[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-121) In the 1960s, composers such as [Henryk Górecki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henryk_G%C3%B3recki "Henryk Górecki") and [Philip Glass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_Glass "Philip Glass") reacted to the perceived elitism and dissonant sound of atonal academic modernism by producing music with simple textures and relatively consonant harmonies,[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] whilst others, most notably [John Cage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Cage "John Cage") challenged the modernist account of structure by including the contingent in the structure of his compositions themselves.[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-122)

In 2023, music critic Andy Cush described Talking Heads as "New York art-punks" whose "blend of nervy postmodernism and undeniable groove made them one of the defining rock bands of the late 1970s and '80s."[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-123) Media theorist [Dick Hebdige](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dick_Hebdige "Dick Hebdige"), examining the "[Road to Nowhere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Road_to_Nowhere "Road to Nowhere")" music video in 1989, said the group "draw eclectically on a wide range of visual and aural sources to create a distinctive pastiche or hybrid 'house style' which they have used since their formation in the mid-1970s deliberately to stretch received (industrial) definitions of what rock/pop/video/Art/performance/audience are", calling them "a properly postmodernist band."[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-124) According to lead vocalist/guitarist/songwriter [David Byrne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Byrne "David Byrne"), commenting in 2011, "Anything could be mixed and matched – or [mashed up](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mashup_(culture) "Mashup (culture)"), as is said today – and anything was fair game for inspiration."[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-125)

Avant-garde academics labelled American singer Madonna a "personification of the postmodern" and created a sub-discipline of [cultural studies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_studies "Cultural studies") known as [Madonna studies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madonna_studies "Madonna studies").[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-Brown2003-126) Her self-aware constructs of gender and identity, and classic film references in music videos for "[Material Girl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Material_Girl#Music_video "Material Girl")" (1984) and "[Express Yourself](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Express_Yourself_(Madonna_song)#Music_video "Express Yourself (Madonna song)")" (1989), made her a favorite of cultural theorists, who saw her as "enacting postmodernist models of subjectivity."[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-127) Madonna was seen to embody fragmentation, pastiche, retrospection, anti-foundationalism, and de-differentiation; her "subversion of the subversion of the subversion of the [male gaze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Male_gaze "Male gaze")" in the "Material Girl" video was analyzed.[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-Brown2003-126)

### Performance and theater

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Postmodernism&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Performance and theater")]

Postmodern theater emerged as a reaction against modernist theater. Most postmodern productions are centered on highlighting the fallibility of definite truth, instead encouraging the audience to reach their own individual understanding. Essentially, thus, postmodern theater raises questions rather than attempting to supply answers.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

"Trowel" (1976) by Claes Oldenburg.[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-128)

Sculptor [Claes Oldenberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claes_Oldenburg "Claes Oldenburg"), at the forefront of the [pop art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pop_art "Pop art") movement, declared in 1961: "I am for an art that is political-erotical-mystical … I am for an art that embroils itself with everyday crap and still comes out on top."[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-129) That year, he opened _The Store_ in a [dime store](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variety_store "Variety store") area of [New York's Lower East Side](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Village,_Manhattan "East Village, Manhattan"), where he blurred the line between art and commerce by producing and selling brightly painted plaster replicas of hamburgers and cans of soda, dresses, underwear, and other everyday objects: "Museum in b[ourgeois] concept equals store in mine".[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-130)[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-131)

### Poststructuralist precursors

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Postmodernism&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Poststructuralist precursors")]

In the 1970s, a disparate group of French theorists – often grouped together as "poststructuralists" – developed a critique of modern philosophy with roots discernible in [Friedrich Nietzsche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche "Friedrich Nietzsche") and [Martin Heidegger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Heidegger "Martin Heidegger")'s critique of [metaphysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metaphysics "Metaphysics").[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBestKellner199122-132) Although few themselves relied upon the term, they became known to many as postmodern theorists.[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBernstein199211-133) Poststructuralism is sometimes treated as distinct from or a subcategory of postmodernism and sometimes is treated as having been subsumed by postmodernism.[[g]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-134) While their ideas exerted a great influence on debates about the postmodern, the French poststucturalists themselves did not intervene or attempt to provide their own definitions of the postmodern.[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBestKellner199131-135)

Poststructuralists, like [structuralists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structuralism "Structuralism"), start from the assumption that people's identities, values, and economic conditions determine each other as parts of a common whole, rather than having intrinsic properties that can be understood in isolation.[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBestKellner199118-136) While structuralism explores how meaning is produced by a set of essential relationships in an overarching quasi-linguistic system, poststructuralism accepts this premise, but rejects the assumption that such systems can ever be fixed or centered.[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrooker2003205-137) Instead, poststructuralists stress the various ways that cultural structures are produced in history.[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBestKellner199120-138) They also emphasize how meaning is generated, rather than discovered, and they replace the traditional concept of "representation" (according to which meaning is determined by the objected signified) to focus instead upon the elastic potentialities of language to generate new meanings.[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBestKellner199120-138)[[h]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-139)

Politically, all of them began with Marxist sympathies, became disillusioned, and eventually opposed the [French Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Communist_Party "French Communist Party") and its application of theory.[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPoster19894-140) The chaos following the briefly successful communist revolution of [May '68](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/May_%2768 "May '68") in France was a particular point of rupture.[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBestKellner199117-141)

#### Jacques Derrida and deconstruction

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Postmodernism&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Jacques Derrida and deconstruction")]

[Deconstruction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deconstruction "Deconstruction") is a practice in philosophy, literary criticism, and close reading developed by [Jacques Derrida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Derrida "Jacques Derrida"). It is based on the assumption, which it seeks to validate by textual analysis, that any text harbors inherent points of "undecidability" that undermine any stable meaning intended by the author. The process of writing inevitably, he aims to show, reveals suppressed elements, challenging the oppositions that are thought to sustain the text.[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReynolds6=%C2%A72._Deconstructive_Strategy-142) Nevertheless, Derrida does not wish to do away with such concepts as "origin" or "truth". What he challenges is any claim to finality. Such metaphysical concepts are, as he puts it, "under erasure", and this, he says, makes deconstructive reading a kind of "double play".[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrooker200366-143)

From this perspective, Derrida argues that the practice of metaphysics in the Western tradition depends upon hierarchies and orders of subordination within various dualisms that it does not acknowledge. It prioritizes presence and purity over the contingent and complicated, dismissing them as aberrations irrelevant to philosophical analysis. In essence, according to Derrida, metaphysical thought prioritizes one side of an opposition while ignoring or marginalizing the alternative.[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReynolds6=%C2%A72.a_Metaphysics_of_Presence/Logocentrism-144) He uses the term [metaphysics of presence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metaphysics_of_presence "Metaphysics of presence") to describe the foundationalist approach to knowledge, taking himself to have demonstrated that we do not have unmediated access to reality. This project of deconstructing and challenging the assumptions of modern philosophy was influential for many postmodern thinkers.[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBestKellner199120-138)

#### Michel Foucault on power relations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Postmodernism&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Michel Foucault on power relations")]

French philosopher and social theorist [Michel Foucault](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michel_Foucault "Michel Foucault") argued that power operates according to the logics of social institutions that have become unmoored from the intentions of any actual individuals. Individuals, according to Foucault, are both products and participants in these dynamics. Among other strategies, he employed a Nietzsche-inspired "[genealogical method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genealogy_(philosophy) "Genealogy (philosophy)")" to analyze power-relations across their historical permutations.[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKellylead_section-145)

Both Foucault's political orientation and the consistency of his positions continue to be debated among critics and defenders alike. Nevertheless, Foucault's political works share two common elements: a historical perspective and a discursive methodology. He analyzed social phenomena in historical contexts and focused on how they have evolved over time. Additionally, he employed the study of written texts, usually academic texts, as the material for his inquiries. In this way, Foucault sought to understand how the historical formation of discourses has shaped contemporary political thinking and institutions.[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKellylead_section-145)

#### Jean Baudrillard on hyperreality

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Postmodernism&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Jean Baudrillard on hyperreality")]

Although trained in sociology, [Jean Baudrillard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Baudrillard "Jean Baudrillard") worked across many disciplines. Drawing upon [some of the technical vocabulary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Lacan#Three_orders_(plus_one) "Jacques Lacan") of the psychoanalyst [Jacques Lacan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Lacan "Jacques Lacan"), Baudrillard argued that social production had shifted from creating real objects to instead producing signs and symbols. This system of symbolic exchange, detached from the real, constitutes hyperreality. In the words of one commentator, "the hyperreal is a system of simulation that simulates itself."[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAylesworth2015%C2%A76._Hyperreality-146)

Postmodernity, Baudrillard said, is the condition in which the domain of reality has become so heavily mediated by signs as to become inaccessible in itself, leaving us entirely in the domain of the [simulacra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simulacra "Simulacra"), images that bear no relation to anything outside of themselves.[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConnor2004568%E2%80%93569-147) This hyperreality is presented as the terminal stage of simulation, where signs and images become entirely self-referential.[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAylesworth2015%C2%A76._Hyperreality-146)

Baudrillard's vision of postmodernity has been described as "apocalyptic",[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConstable200443%E2%80%9347-148)[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertens1995109-149) and scholars disagree about whether his later works are intended as [science fiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_fiction "Science fiction") or truthful theoretical claims.[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKellner2020%C2%A76._Concluding_Assessment-150) Another interpretation is that Baudrillard deliberately adopts the role of _[agent provocateur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agent\_provocateur "Agent provocateur")_.[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConstable200447-151)

### A crisis of legitimacy

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Postmodernism&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: A crisis of legitimacy")]

At the center of the intellectual debate about postmodernism is the question of what, if anything, grounds theory. What establishes that a statement is true or that an action is right? This foundational debate is most prominently on display in Habermas's rejoinder to Lyotard's anti-foundational, postmodern challenge to Habermas's own foundational version of modernism.[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPoster198912%E2%80%9316-152)

#### _The Postmodern Condition_

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Postmodernism&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: The Postmodern Condition")]

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Jean-Francois_Lyotard_cropped.jpg/250px-Jean-Francois_Lyotard_cropped.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Jean-Francois_Lyotard_cropped.jpg)

Philosopher Jean-François Lyotard, photo by [Bracha L. Ettinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracha_L._Ettinger "Bracha L. Ettinger"), 1995

[Jean-François Lyotard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Fran%C3%A7ois_Lyotard "Jean-François Lyotard") is credited with being the first to use the term "postmodern" in a philosophical context. This appeared in his 1979 _[The Postmodern Condition: A Report on Knowledge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Postmodern\_Condition:\_A\_Report\_on\_Knowledge "The Postmodern Condition: A Report on Knowledge")_. In this influential work, Lyotard provided the following definition: "Simplifying to the extreme, I define postmodern as incredulity towards metanarratives".[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTELyotard1984xxiv-153)[[i]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-154)

By "metanarratives", Lyotard meant such overarching narrative frameworks as those provided by [Christianity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity "Christianity"), [G. W. F. Hegel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G._W._F._Hegel "G. W. F. Hegel"), and [Karl Marx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Marx "Karl Marx") that unite and determine our basic sense of our place and significance in the world.[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHerwitz2008Theories_of_the_Postmodern-155) It was his early disillusionment with his early Marxism that would later be generalized into the universal claim about metanarratives.[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGratton2018%C2%A71-156) In a society with no unifying narrative, he argued, we are left with heterogeneous, group-specific narratives (or "[language games](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Language_game_(philosophy) "Language game (philosophy)")", as adopted from [Ludwig Wittgenstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Wittgenstein "Ludwig Wittgenstein")[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuchanan2018-3)) with no universal perspective from which to adjudicate among them.[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAylesworth2015%C2%A72_The_Postmodern_Condition-157)

According to Lyotard, this introduced a general crisis of legitimacy, a theme he adopts from the philosopher [Jürgen Habermas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J%C3%BCrgen_Habermas "Jürgen Habermas"), whose theory of [communicative rationality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communicative_rationality "Communicative rationality") Lyotard rejected.[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertens1995111-158)[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTELyotard198465%E2%80%9366-159) While he was particularly concerned in that report with the way that this insight undermined claims of scientific objectivity, Lyotard's argument undermines the entire principle of transcendent legitimization.[[151]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertens1995119%E2%80%93121-160)[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTELyotard1984xxiii%E2%80%93xxv-161) Instead, proponents of a language game must make the case for their legitimacy with reference to such considerations as efficiency or practicality.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuchanan2018-3) Far from celebrating the apparently relativistic consequences of this argument, however, Lyotard focused much of his subsequent work on how links among games could be established, particularly with respect to ethics and politics.[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGratton2018%C2%A7%C2%A73.2%E2%80%933.4-162)

#### The philosophical criticism of Jürgen Habermas

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Postmodernism&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: The philosophical criticism of Jürgen Habermas")]

The philosopher [Jürgen Habermas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J%C3%BCrgen_Habermas "Jürgen Habermas"), a prominent critic of philosophical postmodernism, argued in his 1985 work _[The Philosophical Discourse of Modernity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Philosophical\_Discourse\_of\_Modernity "The Philosophical Discourse of Modernity")_[[j]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-164) that postmodern thinkers were caught in a performative contradiction, more specifically, that their critiques of modernity rely on concepts and methods that are themselves products of modern reason.[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAylesworth2015%C2%A79-165)

Habermas criticized these thinkers for their rejection of the subject and their embrace of experimental, avant-garde strategies. He asserted that their critiques of modernism ultimately lead to a longing for the very subject they seek to dismantle. Habermas also took issue with postmodernists' leveling of the distinction between philosophy and literature. He argued that such rhetorical strategies undermine the importance of argument and [communicative reason](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communicative_rationality "Communicative rationality").[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAylesworth2015%C2%A79-165)

Habermas's critique of postmodernism set the stage for much of the subsequent debate by clarifying some of its key underlying issues. According to scholar Gary Aylesworth – against those who would dismiss postmodernist discourse as simple nonsense – the fact that Habermas was "able to read postmodernist texts closely and discursively testifies to their intelligibility". His engagement with their ideas has led some postmodern philosophers, following Lyotard, to similarly engage with Habermas's criticisms.[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAylesworth2015%C2%A79-165)

### Frederic Jameson's Marxist rejoinder

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Postmodernism&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: Frederic Jameson's Marxist rejoinder")]

The appearance of linguistic relativism also inspired an extensive rebuttal by the [Marxist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marxism "Marxism") critic [Fredric Jameson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fredric_Jameson "Fredric Jameson").[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertens1995108-166) Building upon the theoretical foundations laid out by the Marxist economist [Ernst Mandel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernst_Mandel "Ernst Mandel")[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuchanan2018-3) and observations in the early work of the sociologist [Jean Baudrillard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Baudrillard "Jean Baudrillard"),[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConnor20043-167) Jameson developed his own conception of the postmodern as "the cultural logic of [late capitalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_capitalism "Late capitalism")" in the form of an enormous cultural expansion into an economy of spectacle and style, rather than the production of goods.[[158]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConnor20043%E2%80%934-168)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBuchanan2018-3) According to Jameson, because the postmodernism is result of political and historical circumstances that make up the social world, it is not something that can be simply embraced or condemned. Instead, it must be analyzed and understood so that we may confront the world as it is.[[159]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoberts2000120-169)

Jameson categorizes a variety of features of the postmodern. One is the elision of the distinction between high culture and mass culture.[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoberts2000121-170) Also, because of our loss of a unified "bourgeois ego", subjectivity is less focused, and we experience what he terms a "waning of the affect", an emotional disengagement from the social world.[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoberts2000124-171) This loss of significance leads to what he calls "depthlessness", a difficulty in getting beneath the surfaces of cultural objects to find any deeper significance than is offered directly to the subject.[[162]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoberts2000126-172) Reduced to a set of styles, history looses its political force.[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoberts2000128-173) This phenomenon finds expression, for instance, in the shift from "parody", in which styles are mixed in the interest of making a point, to "pastiche", in which styles are mixed together without attention to their original contexts.[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoberts2000133-174)

### Richard Rorty's neopragmatism

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Postmodernism&action=edit&section=26 "Edit section: Richard Rorty's neopragmatism")]

[Richard Rorty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Rorty "Richard Rorty") was an American philosopher known for his linguistic form of [neopragmatism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neopragmatism "Neopragmatism"). Initially attracted to [analytic philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_philosophy "Analytic philosophy"), Rorty later rejected its representationalism. His major influences, rather than the poststructuralists, include [Charles Darwin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Darwin "Charles Darwin"), [Hans Georg Gadamer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Georg_Gadamer "Hans Georg Gadamer"), [G. W. F. Hegel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G._W._F._Hegel "G. W. F. Hegel"), and Martin Heidegger.[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrippelead_section-175)

Rorty challenged the notion of a mind-independent, language-independent reality. He argued that language is a tool used to adapt to the environment and achieve desired ends. This [naturalistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naturalism_(philosophy) "Naturalism (philosophy)") approach led him to abandon the traditional quest for a privileged mental power that allows direct access to things-in-themselves.[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrippelead_section-175)

Instead, Rorty advocated for a focus on imaginative alternatives to present beliefs rather than the pursuit of independently grounded truths. He believed that creative, [secular humanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secular_humanism "Secular humanism"), free from authoritarian assertions about truth and goodness, is the key to a better future. Rorty saw his neopragmatism as a continuation of the Enlightenment project, aiming to demystify human life and replace traditional power relations with those based on tolerance and freedom.[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrippelead_section-175)

Postmodernism is more fully understood by observing its effects in such diverse fields as law, education, urban planning, religious studies, politics and many others.[[166]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTELinn1996xiv-176) Its influence varies widely across disciplines, reflecting the extent to which postmodern theories and ideas have been integrated into actual practices.

Postmodern theory in [anthropology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthropology "Anthropology") originated in the 1960s, alongside the literary postmodern movement.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_][Reflexivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflexivity_(social_theory) "Reflexivity (social theory)") is central to postmodern anthropology, a continuous practice of critical self-awareness that attempts to address the subjectivity inherent in interpretation.[[167]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-177) Other key practices are an emphasis on including the perspectives of the people being studied;[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:5-178)[cultural relativism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_relativism "Cultural relativism"), which considers values and beliefs within their cultural context;[[169]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-Garder1996-179) skepticism towards the notion that science can produce objective and universally valid knowledge;[[170]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-Spiro1996-180) and rejection of grand narratives or theories that attempt to explain other cultures.[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:5-178)

Anthropologists working in a postmodern vein seek to dissect, interpret, and write cultural critiques, analyzing of cultural texts and practices, rather than relying on empirical observation.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] The issue of subjectivity is a concern: as [ethnographies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnography "Ethnography") are influenced by the perspective of the author, the question arises in the study of individual cultures as to whether the author's opinions should be considered scientific.[[171]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-181)[Clifford Geertz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_Geertz "Clifford Geertz"), considered a founding member of postmodernist anthropology,[[172]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-182) holds that, "anthropological writings are themselves interpretations, and second and third order ones to boot. (By definition, only a 'native' makes first order ones: it's _his_ culture.)"[[173]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-183) In the 21st century, some anthropologists use a form of [standpoint theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standpoint_theory "Standpoint theory"), which prioritizes the perspectives of the subject over the perspective of the observer in cultural interpretation.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Postmodern feminism mixes [postmodern theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodern_philosophy "Postmodern philosophy") and [French feminism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_feminism "French feminism")[[174]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:3-184) that rejects a universal female subject.[[175]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:1-185)[[176]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:4-186) The goal is to destabilize the [patriarchal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patriarchy "Patriarchy") norms entrenched in society that have led to gender inequality.[[175]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:1-185)[Essentialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Essentialism "Essentialism"), philosophy, and universal truths are opposed, in favor of embracing the differences that exist amongst women to demonstrate that not all women are the same.[[176]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:4-186) Applying universal truths to all women in a society minimizes individual experience; ideas displayed as the norm in society stem from masculine notions of how women should be portrayed.[[177]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-187)

Postmodern feminism seeks to analyze notions that have led to gender inequality, and attempts to promote equality through critiquing [logocentrism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logocentrism "Logocentrism"), supporting multiple discourses, deconstructing texts, and seeking to promote [subjectivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subjectivity "Subjectivity").[[174]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:3-184)[[176]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:4-186) This approach is not readily accepted by all feminists—some believe postmodern thought undermines the attacks that [feminist theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feminist_theory "Feminist theory") attempts to create, while other feminists are in favor of the union.[[174]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:3-184)

In response to the perceived shortcomings of [legal formalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_formalism "Legal formalism") and [positivism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_positivism "Legal positivism"), postmodern legal scholars developed several new approaches to address both formal and ethical issues in [jurisprudence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jurisprudence "Jurisprudence"). In particular, they emphasize the inequalities introduced to the legal system by such matters as race, gender, and economic status.[[178]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDouzinas2004197-188)

In 1992, the _[Los Angeles Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los\_Angeles\_Times "Los Angeles Times")_ reported on "a group of increasingly influential psychologists – postmodern psychologists seems to be the name that is sticking", who had come to the conclusion that "the American conception of an isolated, unified self" does not exist. People are composed of many different selves, constructed for different situations.[[179]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-189) In this way, postmodernism challenges the [modernist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modernist "Modernist") view of psychology as the science of the individual,[[180]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-190) in favor of seeing humans as a cultural/communal product, dominated by language rather than by an inner self.[[181]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-191)

In 2001, [Kenneth Gergen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenneth_J._Gergen "Kenneth J. Gergen"), a pioneer in postmodern psychological theory,identified "emphasis on the individual mind, an objectively knowable world, and language as carrier of truth" as the cornerstones of traditional modernist psychology. He noted criticism of these assumptions coming from "every quarter of the humanities and the sciences", and the emergence of a psychology in which "colonialist universalism is replaced by a global conversation among equals". He also considered the "strong critical reservation", including the realist argument that a socially constructed world cannot negate a clearly observable objective reality; the claim of incoherence, wherein postmodernism denies truth and objectivity while simultaneously making truth claims; and its moral relativism, which fails to take a principled ethical stand. Ultimately, he concluded that psychology's future is "hanging in the balance".[[182]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-192)

In 2021, psychologist [Jan Smedslund](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_Smedslund "Jan Smedslund") discussed how psychology tried for decades to emulate the [natural sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_science "Natural science") and address unpredictable individual behavior. He described how the dominant methodology came to rely exclusively on statistical analysis of group-level data and average findings, whereby it "lost contact with the psychological processes going on in individual persons." He advocated for abandoning the natural science approach that had "led into a clearly discernible blind alley."[[183]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-193)

In 2024, American psychology professor Edwin Gantt wrote that psychology remains in a state of continual struggle "to decide whether its true intellectual home is to be found among the humanities, especially philosophy and literature, or among the STEM disciplines." He finds psychology "a key site where the intellectual tug-of-war between modernism and postmodernism plays itself out in academia."[[184]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGantt2024-194)

Modernism sought to design and plan cities that followed the logic of the new model of industrial [mass production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_production "Mass production"); reverting to large-scale solutions, aesthetic standardization, and [prefabricated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prefabrication "Prefabrication") design solutions.[[185]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoodchild1990119%E2%80%93137-195) This approach was found to have eroded urban living by its failure to recognize differences and aim towards homogeneous landscapes.[[186]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTESimonsen199057-196)[Jane Jacobs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jane_Jacobs "Jane Jacobs")'s 1961 book _[The Death and Life of Great American Cities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Death\_and\_Life\_of\_Great\_American\_Cities "The Death and Life of Great American Cities"),_[[187]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-197) was a sustained critique of urban planning as it had developed within modernism,[[188]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIrving1993479-198) and played a major role in turning public opinion against modernist planners, notably [Robert Moses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Moses "Robert Moses").[[189]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-199)

Postmodern urban planning involves theories that embrace and aim to create diversity, elevating uncertainty, flexibility, and change, and rejecting utopianism while embracing a utopian way of thinking and acting.[[190]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHatukad'Hooghe200720%E2%80%9327-200) The postmodernity of "resistance" seeks to deconstruct modernism, a critique of the origins without necessarily returning to them.[[191]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIrving1993460-201)[[192]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-202)

The postmodern theological movement interprets [Christian theology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_theology "Christian theology") in light of postmodern theory and various forms of post-[Heideggerian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Heidegger "Martin Heidegger") thought, using approaches such as [poststructuralism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poststructuralism "Poststructuralism"), [phenomenology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenomenology_(philosophy) "Phenomenology (philosophy)"), and [deconstruction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deconstruction "Deconstruction") to question fixed interpretations, explore the role of [lived experience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lived_experience "Lived experience"), and uncover hidden textual assumptions and contradictions.[[193]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-203) The movement emerged in the 1980s and 1990s when a handful of philosophers who took philosopher [Martin Heidegger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martin_Heidegger "Martin Heidegger") as a common point of departure began publishing books engaging with Christian theology.[[194]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVanhoozer200322%E2%80%9325-204)[[195]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-205)

Theologian [Kevin J. Vanhoozer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kevin_J._Vanhoozer "Kevin J. Vanhoozer") combines and expands on other scholarly classifications to present seven types of postmodern theology: postliberal, postmetaphysical, deconstructive, reconstructive, [feminist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feminist_theology "Feminist theology"), Anglo-American postmodernity, and [radical orthodoxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_orthodoxy "Radical orthodoxy"). He notes that the typology should be considered "provisional and fallible [yet] not entirely arbitrary", having met two main criteria: each is an approach taken by more than one theologian, and each "believes itself to be responding to, rejecting, or passing through modernity, not inhabiting it."[[196]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEVanhoozer200320-206)

[![Image 9: Dresses by Rei Kawakubo (1997)](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Dresses_by_Rei_Kawakubo_-_Sarah_Stierch_03.jpg/250px-Dresses_by_Rei_Kawakubo_-_Sarah_Stierch_03.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Dresses_by_Rei_Kawakubo_-_Sarah_Stierch_03.jpg)

Padded dresses by Rei Kawakubo (1997)

One manifestation of postmodernism in fashion explored alternatives to conventional concepts of elegance: [Rei Kawakubo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rei_Kawakubo "Rei Kawakubo")’s Spring/Summer 1997 collection featured "dresses asymmetrically padded with goose down, creating bumps in unexpected areas of the body". [Issey Miyake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Issey_Miyake "Issey Miyake")'s 1985 [dreadlocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dreadlocks "Dreadlocks") hat "offered an immediate, yet impermanent, '[multi-culti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiculturalism "Multiculturalism")' fashion experience". [Vivienne Westwood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vivienne_Westwood "Vivienne Westwood") took "an extremely polyglot approach", from early work with copies of 1950s clothes, to exploration of historic modes and cultural influences. In 1981, her first runway show, "Pirate", merged British history, 18th- and 19th-century dress, and African textile design, with a rap and ethnic music soundtrack.[[197]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:6-207)[[198]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:02-208)

The postmodern fashion sensibility appeared also through the subcultures of the 1960s and 1970s. [Hippies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hippie "Hippie"), [punks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punk_subculture "Punk subculture") and other countercultural groups constructed their own nonconformist identities through choices in music, drugs, slang, and appearance. As these styles gained mainstream popularity, critics claim they lost their deeper meaning: "the adoption of surface attributes offers the frisson of rebellion without a commitment to a subcultural lifestyle."[[197]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-:6-207)

Early mention of postmodernism in [graphic design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphic_design "Graphic design") appeared in the British magazine, _Design_, during the late 1960s.[[199]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPoynor200318-209) The discussion took a pragmatic if not entirely comfortable view of graphic design as engaging with the economic necessities of a changing world. Graphic design had the role of "active stylization of product surfaces (such as those of packaging and promotion)", engaging without moralizing with consumer desires. Editor Corin Hughes-Stanton concluded, "Post-Modernism' is an attitude that takes the form of a creative response to unfolding developments in the socio-economic sphere; it is a sign of active engagement rather than an academic retreat from its commercial and professional concerns."[[200]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-210)

Postmodernism in marketing focuses on customized experiences where broad market generalizations are no longer applied.[[201]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-sbrown-pg192-211) According to academic Stephen Brown, "Marketers know about consumers, consumers know about marketers, marketers know consumers know about marketers, and consumers know marketers know consumers know about marketers." Brown, writing in 1993, stated that the postmodern approach in many ways rejects attempts to impose order and work in silos. Instead marketers should work collectively with "artistic" attributes of intuition, creativity, spontaneity, speculation, emotion, and involvement.[[201]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-sbrown-pg192-211)

Since the late 1990s, there has been a growing sentiment in popular culture and in academia that postmodernism "has gone out of fashion".[[202]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-212) Others argue that postmodernism is dead in the context of current cultural production.[[203]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-213)[[204]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-214)[[205]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-215)

A 2020 study investigated the reported transition from postmodernism to post-postmodernism, those "changing social conditions that lead the consumer to consume in a particular manner". Song lyrics were selected from Madonna (postmodern), [Taylor Swift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor_Swift "Taylor Swift") (post-postmodern), and [Lady Gaga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lady_Gaga "Lady Gaga") as a transitional example. Five postmodern characteristics consistently found in marketing literature were compared to their post-postmodern counterparts: [anti-foundationalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-foundationalism "Anti-foundationalism") to rewriting; [dedifferentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dedifferentiation "Dedifferentiation") to redifferentiation; [fragmentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fragmentation_(sociology) "Fragmentation (sociology)") to reengagement; reversal of production and consumption to rebalancing of production and consumption; and [hyperreality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperreality "Hyperreality") to alternative reality. Postmodernism, it finds, "remains vibrant, re-inventive, and calls for its demise may be somewhat overblown." Swift's success "suggests a significant shift from deconstructive to reconstructive positions regarding the self and its surroundings", noting that her "post-postmodern engagement, enthusiasm and sincerity" appeared to be "somewhat superficial, sociopathic, and couched in fabulation."[[206]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-216)

The connection between postmodernism, [posthumanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Posthumanism "Posthumanism"), and [cyborgism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transhumanism "Transhumanism") has led to a challenge to postmodernism, for which the terms _[Post-postmodernism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post-postmodernism "Post-postmodernism")_ and _postpoststructuralism_ were first coined in 2003.[[207]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-217)[[208]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-218)[[209]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-219) A small group of critics has put forth a range of theories that aim to describe culture or society in the alleged aftermath of postmodernism, most notably Raoul Eshelman (performatism), [Gilles Lipovetsky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gilles_Lipovetsky "Gilles Lipovetsky") ([hypermodernity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypermodernity "Hypermodernity")), [Nicolas Bourriaud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolas_Bourriaud "Nicolas Bourriaud") ([altermodern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altermodern "Altermodern")), and Alan Kirby (digimodernism, formerly called pseudo-modernism). None of these new theories or labels have so far gained very widespread acceptance.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Writing in 2022, [Steven Connor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steven_Connor "Steven Connor") argues that, despite continuing reports of its death or imminent demise, postmodernism has instead undergone a kind of disappearance into our culture by way of assimilation. He notes there is little that can now be called postmodern style because "the clashing or commingling of styles has become entirely routine at all levels of culture." The energizing antagonism between high and low culture has been "pestled into a tepid porridge." And the general postmodern condition is now "universal, irreversible and metastable, embodied above all in the massive increase in digitally mediated information technologies." According to Connor, postmodernism in the 2020s is a sensibility that has been integrated into everyday life, having been subject to a considerable degree of shifting, perhaps temporarily, from irony, pluralism and ambivalence to urgency, indignation, and reductive absolutism.[[210]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postmodernism#cite_note-220)
