**Metric time** is the measure of time intervals using the [metric system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_system "Metric system"). The modern [SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI "SI") system defines the [second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second "Second") as the [base unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_base_unit "SI base unit") of time, and forms multiples and submultiples with [metric prefixes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_prefix "Metric prefix") such as kiloseconds and milliseconds. Other units of time – [minute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minute "Minute"), [hour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hour "Hour"), and [day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Day "Day") – are [accepted for use with SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-SI_units_mentioned_in_the_SI "Non-SI units mentioned in the SI"), but are not part of it. Metric time is a measure of time intervals, while [decimal time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_time "Decimal time") is a means of recording [time of day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_of_day "Time of day").

History
-------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_time&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: History")]

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/60px-Question_book-new.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg)This section **needs additional citations for [verification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability "Wikipedia:Verifiability")**. Please help [improve this article](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:EditPage/Metric_time "Special:EditPage/Metric time") by [adding citations to reliable sources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners "Help:Referencing for beginners") in this section. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed._(October 2024)_ _([Learn how and when to remove this message](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Maintenance\_template\_removal "Help:Maintenance template removal"))_

The second is derived from the [sexagesimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexagesimal "Sexagesimal") system, which originated with the [Sumerians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sumer "Sumer") and [Babylonians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylonia "Babylonia"). This system divides a base unit into sixty minutes, each minute into sixty seconds, and each second into sixty [tierces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexagesimal#Modern_usage "Sexagesimal"). The word "minute" comes from the Latin _pars minuta prima_, meaning "first small part", and "second" from _pars minuta secunda_ or "second small part". [Angular measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_measure "Angular measure") also uses sexagesimal units; there, it is the [degree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_(angle) "Degree (angle)") that is subdivided into minutes and seconds, while in time, it is the hour.

In 1790, French diplomat [Charles Maurice de Talleyrand-Périgord](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Maurice_de_Talleyrand-P%C3%A9rigord "Charles Maurice de Talleyrand-Périgord") proposed that the fundamental unit of length for the metric system should be the length of a [pendulum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum "Pendulum") with a one-second period, measured at sea level on the 45th parallel (50 grades in the new angular measures), thus basing the metric system on the value of the second. A Commission of Weights and Measures was formed within the French Academy of Sciences to develop the system. The commission rejected the seconds-pendulum definition of the [metre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre "Metre") the following year because the second of time was an arbitrary period equal to 1/86,400 day, rather than a decimal fraction of a natural unit. Instead, the metre would be defined as a decimal fraction of the length of the [Paris Meridian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris_Meridian "Paris Meridian") between the [equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator "Equator") and the [North Pole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Pole "North Pole").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_time#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_time#cite_note-Meter-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_time#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_time#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_time#cite_note-5)

The commission initially proposed the decimal time units later enacted as part of the new [Republican calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_calendar "French Republican calendar"). In January, 1791, [Jean-Charles de Borda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Charles_de_Borda "Jean-Charles de Borda") commissioned Louis Berthoud to manufacture a decimal chronometer displaying these units. On March 28, 1794, the commission's president, [Joseph Louis Lagrange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Louis_Lagrange "Joseph Louis Lagrange"), proposed using the day (French _jour_) as the base unit of time, with divisions _[déci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deci "Deci")-jour_ and _[centi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centi "Centi")-jour,_ and suggested representing 4 _déci-jours_ and 5 _centi-jours_ as "4,5", "4/5", or just "45".[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_time#cite_note-6) The final system, as introduced in 1795, included units for length, area, dry volume, liquid capacity, weight or mass, and currency, but not time. [Decimal time of day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_time_of_day "Decimal time of day") had been introduced in France two years earlier, but mandatory use was suspended at the same time the metric system was inaugurated, and did not follow the metric pattern of a base unit and prefixed units.

Base units equivalent to decimal divisions of the day, such as 1/10, 1/100, 1/1,000, or 1/100,000 day, or other divisions of the day, such as 1/20 or 1/40 day, have also been proposed, with various names. Such alternative units did not gain any notable acceptance. In China, during the [Song dynasty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Song_dynasty "Song dynasty"), a day was divided into smaller units, called _kè_ ([刻](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E5%88%BB "wikt:刻")). One _kè_ was usually defined as 1⁄100 of a day until 1628, though there were short periods before then where days had 96, 108 or 120 _kè_.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_time#cite_note-Units-7) A kè is about 14.4 minutes, or 14 minutes 24 seconds. In the 19th century, Joseph Charles François de Rey-Pailhade endorsed Lagrange’s proposal of using _centijours,_ but abbreviated _cé_, and divided into 10 _decicés_, 100 _centicés_, 1,000 _millicés_,[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_time#cite_note-8) and 10,000 _dimicés_.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_time#cite_note-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_time#cite_note-10)

[James Clerk Maxwell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell "James Clerk Maxwell") and [Elihu Thomson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elihu_Thomson "Elihu Thomson") (through the [British Association for the Advancement of Science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science "British Association for the Advancement of Science"), or BAAS) introduced the [Centimetre gram second system of units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centimetre_gram_second_system_of_units "Centimetre gram second system of units") in 1874 to derive electric and magnetic metric units, following the recommendation of [Carl Friedrich Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss "Carl Friedrich Gauss") in 1832.

In 1897, the _Commission de décimalisation du temps_ was created by the French [Bureau of Longitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bureau_of_Longitude "Bureau of Longitude"), with the mathematician [Henri Poincaré](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9 "Henri Poincaré") as secretary. The commission proposed making the standard [hour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hour "Hour") the base unit of metric time, but the proposal did not gain acceptance and was eventually abandoned.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_time#cite_note-11)

When the modern [SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI "SI") system was defined at the 10th [General Conference on Weights and Measures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conf%C3%A9rence_G%C3%A9n%C3%A9rale_des_Poids_et_Mesures "Conférence Générale des Poids et Mesures") (CGPM) in 1954, the ephemeris second (1/86400 of a mean solar day) was made one of the system's base units. Because the Earth's rotation is slowly decelerating at an irregular rate and was thus unsuitable as a reference point for precise measurements, the SI second was later redefined more precisely as the duration of 9,192,631,770 periods of the radiation corresponding to the transition between the two hyperfine levels of the [ground state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_state "Ground state") of the [caesium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium "Caesium")-133 atom. The international standard [atomic clocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_clock "Atomic clock") use caesium-133 measurements as their main benchmark.

In computing
------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_time&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: In computing")]

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/60px-Question_book-new.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg)This section **does not [cite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources "Wikipedia:Citing sources") any [sources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability "Wikipedia:Verifiability")**. Unsourced material may be challenged and [removed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence "Wikipedia:Verifiability")._(October 2024)_ _([Learn how and when to remove this message](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Maintenance\_template\_removal "Help:Maintenance template removal"))_

In computing, at least internally, metric time gained widespread use for ease of computation. [Unix time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unix_time "Unix time") gives date and time as the number of seconds since January 1, 1970, and Microsoft's [NTFS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NTFS "NTFS") FILETIME as multiples of 100 ns since January 1, 1601. [VAX/VMS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OpenVMS#Timekeeping "OpenVMS") uses the number of 100 ns since November 17, 1858, and [RISC OS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RISC_OS "RISC OS") the number of centiseconds since January 1, 1900. [Microsoft Excel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsoft_Excel "Microsoft Excel") uses number of days (with decimals, [floating point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Floating-point_arithmetic "Floating-point arithmetic")) since January 1, 1900.

All these systems present time for the user using traditional units. None of these systems is strictly linear, as they each have discontinuities at [leap seconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leap_second "Leap second").

Metric prefixes for subdivisions of a second are commonly used in science and technology. Milliseconds and microseconds are particularly common. Prefixes for multiples of a second are rarely used:

| Multiple | Name of unit | Seconds | In common units |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 10 1 | decasecond | 10 | 0.17 minutes |
| 10 2 | hectosecond | 100 | 1.67 minutes (or 1 minute 40 seconds) |
| 10 3 | kilosecond | 1 000 | 16.7 minutes (or 16 minutes and 40 seconds) |
| 10 6 | megasecond | 1 000 000 | 11.6 days (or 11 days, 13 hours, 46 minutes and 40 seconds) |
| 10 9 | gigasecond | 1 000 000 000 | 31.7 years (or 31 years, 252 days, 1 hour, 46 minutes, 40 seconds, assuming that there are 7 leap years in the interval) |
