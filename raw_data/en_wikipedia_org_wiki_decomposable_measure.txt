This is a generalization of Ïƒ-finite measures , which are the same as those that are a disjoint union of countably many finite measures. There are several theorems in measure theory such as the Radonâ€“Nikodym theorem that are not true for arbitrary measures but are true for Ïƒ-finite measures. Several such theorems remain true for the more general class of decomposable measures. This extra generality is not used much as most decomposable measures that occur in practice are Ïƒ-finite.

Examples [ edit ] Counting measure on an uncountable measure space with all subsets measurable is a decomposable measure that is not Ïƒ-finite.

Fubini's theorem and Tonelli's theorem hold for Ïƒ-finite measures but can fail for this measure.

Counting measure on an uncountable measure space with not all subsets measurable is generally not a decomposable measure.

The one-point space of measure infinity is not decomposable.
