**Carbonyl sulfide** is the [chemical compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_compound "Chemical compound") with the [linear formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")[O](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen")=[C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon")=[S](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur "Sulfur"). It is a colorless flammable [gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas "Gas") with an unpleasant [odor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odor "Odor"). It is a linear molecule consisting of a [carbonyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl "Carbonyl")[double bonded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_bond "Double bond") to a sulfur atom. Carbonyl sulfide can be considered to be intermediate between [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") and [carbon disulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_disulfide "Carbon disulfide"), both of which are [valence isoelectronic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valence_isoelectronic "Valence isoelectronic") with it.

Carbonyl sulfide is the most abundant sulfur compound naturally present in the atmosphere, at 0.5±0.05[ppb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts_per_billion "Parts per billion"), because it is emitted from oceans, [volcanoes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcano "Volcano") and [deep sea vents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_sea_vent "Deep sea vent"). As such, it is a significant compound in the global [sulfur cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_cycle "Sulfur cycle"). Measurements on the [Antarctica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antarctica "Antarctica")[ice cores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_core "Ice core") and from air trapped in snow above glaciers ([firn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Firn "Firn") air) have provided a detailed picture of OCS concentrations from 1640 to the present day and allow an understanding of the relative importance of [anthropogenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment "Human impact on the environment") and non-anthropogenic sources of this gas to the atmosphere.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-3) Some carbonyl sulfide that is transported into the [stratospheric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stratosphere "Stratosphere") sulfate layer is oxidized to sulfuric acid.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-4) Sulfuric acid forms particulate which affects [energy balance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_warming "Global warming") due to [light scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_scattering "Light scattering").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-Seinfeld_2006-5) The long atmospheric lifetime of COS makes it the major source of stratospheric sulfate, though [sulfur dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide "Sulfur dioxide") from volcanic activity can be significant too.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-Seinfeld_2006-5) Carbonyl sulfide is also removed from the atmosphere by terrestrial vegetation by enzymes associated with the uptake of carbon dioxide during photosynthesis, and by hydrolysis in ocean waters.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-8) Loss processes, such as these, limit the persistence (or lifetime) of a molecule of COS in the atmosphere to a few years.

The largest man-made sources of carbonyl sulfide release include its primary use as a chemical intermediate and as a byproduct of carbon disulfide production; however, it is also released from automobiles and their [tire wear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tire_wear "Tire wear"),[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-9) coal-fired power plants, [coking ovens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coke_(fuel)#Industrial_coke_furnaces "Coke (fuel)"), [biomass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomass "Biomass") combustion, fish processing, combustion of refuse and plastics, petroleum manufacture, and manufacture of synthetic fibers, starch, and rubber.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-hsdb94-10) The average total worldwide release of carbonyl sulfide to the atmosphere has been estimated[_[when?](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual\_of\_Style/Dates\_and\_numbers#Chronological\_items "Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers")_] at about 3 million tons per year, of which less than one third was related to human activity.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-hsdb94-10) It is also a significant sulfur-containing impurity in many [fuel gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fuel_gas "Fuel gas") such as [synthesis gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthesis_gas "Synthesis gas"), which are produced from sulfur-containing feedstocks.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-syngasetc-11)

Carbonyl sulfide is present in [foodstuffs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foodstuff "Foodstuff"), such as [cheese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cheese "Cheese") and prepared [vegetables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vegetable "Vegetable") of the [cabbage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cabbage "Cabbage") family. Traces of COS are naturally present in [grains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grain "Grain") and [seeds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seed "Seed") in the range of 0.05–0.1 mg/kg.

Carbonyl sulfide has been observed in the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium") (see also [List of molecules in interstellar space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_molecules_in_interstellar_space "List of molecules in interstellar space")), in [comet 67P](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet_67P "Comet 67P")[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-12) and in the [atmosphere of Venus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Venus "Atmosphere of Venus"), where, because of the difficulty of producing COS inorganically, it is considered a possible indicator of life.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-Landis2003b-13)

Reactions and applications
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbonyl_sulfide&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Reactions and applications")]

Carbonyl sulfide is used as an intermediate in the production of thiocarbamate herbicides.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-epa94-14)

The hydrolysis of carbonyl sulfide is promoted by chromium-based catalysts:[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-syngasetc-11)

COS + H 2 O → CO 2 + H 2 S
This conversion is catalyzed in solution by [carbonic anhydrase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonic_anhydrase "Carbonic anhydrase") enzymes in plants and mammals. Because of this chemistry, the release of carbonyl sulfide from small organic molecules has been identified as a strategy for delivering hydrogen sulfide, which is [gaseous signaling molecule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaseous_signaling_molecules "Gaseous signaling molecules").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-16)

This compound is found to [catalyze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalysis "Catalysis") the formation of [peptides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peptide "Peptide") from [amino acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid "Amino acid"). This finding is an extension of the [Miller–Urey experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miller%E2%80%93Urey_experiment "Miller–Urey experiment"), and it is suggested that carbonyl sulfide played a significant role in the [origin of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_of_life "Origin of life").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-17)

In ecosystem science, [[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-18) are increasingly being used to describe the rate of [photosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis "Photosynthesis").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-20)

Carbonyl sulfide was first described in 1841,[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-21) but was apparently mischaracterized as a mixture of carbon dioxide and [hydrogen sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide "Hydrogen sulfide"). [Carl von Than](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_von_Than "Carl von Than") first characterized the substance in 1867. It forms when [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide") reacts with molten sulfur:

CO +⁠1/8⁠S 8 → COS
This reaction reverses above 1200 K (930°C; 1700°F).

A laboratory synthesis entails the reaction [potassium thiocyanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_thiocyanate "Potassium thiocyanate") and [sulfuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid"):

KSCN + 2 H 2 SO 4 + H 2 O → KHSO 4 + NH 4 HSO 4 + COS
The resulting gas contains significant amounts of byproducts and requires purification.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-22)

Hydrolysis of [isothiocyanates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isothiocyanates "Isothiocyanates") in [hydrochloric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid "Hydrochloric acid") solution also affords COS.

Carbonyl sulfide has the "rotten egg" odor characteristic of sulfides, although some sources suggest that the associated odor is due to impurities, and not present in the pure compound.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-CDC-23) The detectability threshold is estimated at 135μg/m![Image 19: {\displaystyle ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73c05e3410279c99fc3d8b9fcdaa15b71a98772a).[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-CDC-23)

As of 1994, limited information existed on the acute toxicity of carbonyl sulfide in humans and in animals.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-epa94-14) High concentrations (above 1000 ppm) can cause sudden collapse, convulsions, and death from respiratory paralysis.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-hsdb94-10)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-epa94-14) Occasional fatalities have been reported, practically without local irritation or olfactory warning.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-epa94-14) In tests with rats, 50% animals died when exposed to 1400 ppm of COS for 90 minutes, or at 3000 ppm for 9 minutes.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-epa94-14) Limited studies with laboratory animals also suggest that continued inhalation of low concentrations (around 50 ppm for up to 12 weeks) does not affect the lungs or the heart.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-epa94-14)

Carbonyl sulfide is a potential alternative [fumigant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fumigant "Fumigant")[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonyl_sulfide#cite_note-24) to [methyl bromide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_bromide "Methyl bromide") and [phosphine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine "Phosphine"). In some cases, however, residues on the grain result in flavours that are unacceptable to consumers, such as in barley used for brewing.
