This article is about integers in mathematics. For integers as a data type, see [Integer (computer science)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer_(computer_science) "Integer (computer science)").

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/NumberLineIntegers.svg/330px-NumberLineIntegers.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:NumberLineIntegers.svg)

The integers arranged on a [number line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_line "Number line")

An **integer** is the [number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number "Number") zero ([0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0 "0")), a positive [natural number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number") (1, 2, 3, ...), or the negation of a positive natural number ([−1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E2%88%921 "−1"), −2, −3, ...).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-1) The negations or [additive inverses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_inverse "Additive inverse") of the positive natural numbers are referred to as **negative integers**.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-2) The [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") of all integers is often denoted by the [boldface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boldface "Boldface")**Z** or [blackboard bold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blackboard_bold "Blackboard bold")![Image 2: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-earliest-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-Cameron1998-4)

The set of natural numbers ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbb {N} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdf9a96b565ea202d0f4322e9195613fb26a9bed) is a [subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset") of ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc), which in turn is a subset of the set of all [rational numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number "Rational number")![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5909f0b54e4718fa24d5fd34d54189d24a66e9a), itself a subset of the [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number")⁠![Image 6: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc)⁠.[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-7) Like the set of natural numbers, the set of integers ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) is [countably infinite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Countable_set "Countable set"). An integer may be regarded as a real number that can be written without a [fractional component](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fraction "Fraction"). For example, 21, 4, 0, and −2048 are integers, while 9.75, ⁠5+1/2⁠, 5/4, and the [square root of 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_root_of_2 "Square root of 2") are not.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-8)

The integers form the smallest [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") and the smallest [ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)") containing the [natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number"). In [algebraic number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_number_theory "Algebraic number theory"), the integers are sometimes qualified as **rational integers** to distinguish them from the more general [algebraic integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_integer "Algebraic integer"). In fact, (rational) integers are algebraic integers that are also [rational numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number "Rational number").

History
-------

The word integer comes from the [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin")[_integer_](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/integer#Latin "wikt:integer") meaning "whole" or (literally) "untouched", from _in_ ("not") plus _tangere_ ("to touch"). "[Entire](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/entire "wikt:entire")" derives from the same origin via the [French](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_language "French language") word _[entier](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/entier "wikt:entier")_, which means both _entire_ and _integer_.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-9) Historically the term was used for a [number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number "Number") that was a multiple of 1,[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-10)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-11) or to the whole part of a [mixed number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed_number "Mixed number").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-12)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-13) Only positive integers were considered, making the term synonymous with the [natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number"). The definition of integer expanded over time to include [negative numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negative_number "Negative number") as their usefulness was recognized.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-negmath-14) For example [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler") in his 1765 _[Elements of Algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elements\_of\_Algebra "Elements of Algebra")_ defined integers to include both positive and negative numbers.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-15)

The phrase _the set of the integers_ was not used before the end of the 19th century, when [Georg Cantor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Cantor "Georg Cantor") introduced the concept of [infinite sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_set "Infinite set") and [set theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_theory "Set theory"). The use of the letter Z to denote the set of integers comes from the [German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_language "German language") word _[Zahlen](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Zahlen "wikt:Zahlen")_ ("numbers")[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-earliest-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-Cameron1998-4) and has been attributed to [David Hilbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Hilbert "David Hilbert").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-16) The earliest known use of the notation in a textbook occurs in [Algèbre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89l%C3%A9ments_de_math%C3%A9matique "Éléments de mathématique") written by the collective [Nicolas Bourbaki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolas_Bourbaki "Nicolas Bourbaki"), dating to 1947.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-earliest-3)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-17) The notation was not adopted immediately. For example, another textbook used the letter J,[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-18) and a 1960 paper used Z to denote the non-negative integers.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-19) But by 1961, Z was generally used by modern algebra texts to denote the positive and negative integers.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-20)

The symbol ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) is often annotated to denote various sets, with varying usage amongst different authors: ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} ^{+}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/628778fcf14bd3629e9b9ebacffa172b0ad6ce41), ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} _{+}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16cea652d9aba69b6accb9af09548981c2b4e24f), or ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} ^{>}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b674b889017afcd24e7b7d296f6d1746cefd2d94) for the positive integers, ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} ^{0+}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81a3b6ad192693a6aeb684d1876f82d852fcc228) or ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} ^{\geq }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d1683538868e87548cd2a6dd28c28dfab75ff44) for non-negative integers, and ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} ^{\neq }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0413be677c88a720f7f2d2593da069808bbc252a) for non-zero integers. Some authors use ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} ^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45aa6a166e406bf9e192b7a32d6b140f27cd8538) for non-zero integers, while others use it for non-negative integers, or for {−1,1} (the [group of units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_of_units "Group of units") of ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc)). Additionally, ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} _{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dbc1df7227ef11fe88dccd2dae3adc7bbdeae5f4) is used to denote either the set of [integers modulo _p_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integers_modulo_n "Integers modulo n") (i.e., the set of [congruence classes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_relation "Congruence relation") of integers), or the set of [_p_-adic integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P-adic_integer "P-adic integer").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-edexcelc1-21)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-22)

The _whole numbers_ were synonymous with the integers up until the early 1950s.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-23)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-24)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-25) In the late 1950s, as part of the [New Math](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Math "New Math") movement,[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-26) American elementary school teachers began teaching that _whole numbers_ referred to the [natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number"), excluding negative numbers, while _integer_ included the negative numbers.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-27)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-28) The _whole numbers_ remain ambiguous to the present day.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-29)

Algebraic properties
--------------------

[![Image 18](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Number-line.svg/330px-Number-line.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Number-line.svg)

Integers can be thought of as discrete, equally spaced points on an infinitely long [number line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_line "Number line"). In the above, non-[negative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sign_(mathematics)#Terminology_for_signs "Sign (mathematics)") integers are shown in blue and negative integers in red.

Like the [natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_numbers "Natural numbers"), ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) is [closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closure_(mathematics) "Closure (mathematics)") under the [operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_operation "Binary operation") of addition and [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication "Multiplication"), that is, the sum and product of any two integers is an integer. However, with the inclusion of the negative natural numbers (and importantly, [0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0_(number) "0 (number)")), ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc), unlike the natural numbers, is also closed under [subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction "Subtraction").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-30)

The integers form a [ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)") which is the most basic one, in the following sense: for any ring, there is a unique [ring homomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_homomorphism "Ring homomorphism") from the integers into this ring. This [universal property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_property "Universal property"), namely to be an [initial object](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Initial_object "Initial object") in the [category of rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_of_rings "Category of rings"), characterizes the ring![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc). This unique homomorphism is [injective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injective "Injective") if and only if the [characteristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_(algebra) "Characteristic (algebra)") of the ring is zero. It follows that every ring of characteristic zero contains a subring isomorphic to ⁠![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc)⁠, which is its smallest subring.

![Image 23: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) is not closed under [division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_(mathematics) "Division (mathematics)"), since the quotient of two integers (e.g., 1 divided by 2) need not be an integer. Although the natural numbers are closed under [exponentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponentiation "Exponentiation"), the integers are not (since the result can be a fraction when the exponent is negative).

The following table lists some of the basic properties of addition and multiplication for any integers _a_, _b_, and _c_:

Properties of addition and multiplication on integers |  | Addition | Multiplication |
| --- | --- | --- |
| [Closure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closure_(mathematics) "Closure (mathematics)"): | _a_ + _b_ is an integer | _a_ × _b_ is an integer |
| [Associativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associativity "Associativity"): | _a_ + (_b_ + _c_) = (_a_ + _b_) + _c_ | _a_ × (_b_ × _c_) = (_a_ × _b_) × _c_ |
| [Commutativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutativity "Commutativity"): | _a_ + _b_ = _b_ + _a_ | _a_ × _b_ = _b_ × _a_ |
| Existence of an [identity element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_element "Identity element"): | _a_ + 0 = _a_ | _a_ × 1 = _a_ |
| Existence of [inverse elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_element "Inverse element"): | _a_ + (−_a_) = 0 | The only invertible integers (called [units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_(ring_theory) "Unit (ring theory)")) are −1 and 1. |
| [Distributivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributivity "Distributivity"): | _a_ × (_b_ + _c_) = (_a_ × _b_) + (_a_ × _c_) and (_a_ + _b_) × _c_ = (_a_ × _c_) + (_b_ × _c_) |
| No [zero divisors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_divisor "Zero divisor"): |  | If _a_ × _b_ = 0, then _a_ = 0 or _b_ = 0 (or both) |

The first five properties listed above for addition say that ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc), under addition, is an [abelian group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group"). It is also a [cyclic group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_group "Cyclic group"), since every non-zero integer can be written as a finite sum 1 + 1 + ... + 1 or (−1) + (−1) + ... + (−1). In fact, ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) under addition is the _only_ infinite cyclic group—in the sense that any infinite cyclic group is [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_isomorphism "Group isomorphism") to ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc).

The first four properties listed above for multiplication say that ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) under multiplication is a [commutative monoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_monoid "Commutative monoid"). However, not every integer has a multiplicative inverse (as is the case of the number 2), which means that ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) under multiplication is not a group.

All the rules from the above property table (except for the last), when taken together, say that ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) together with addition and multiplication is a [commutative ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring "Commutative ring") with [unity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplicative_identity "Multiplicative identity"). It is the prototype of all objects of such [algebraic structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_structure "Algebraic structure"). Only those [equalities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equality_(mathematics) "Equality (mathematics)") of [expressions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_expression "Algebraic expression") are true in![Image 30: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc)[for all](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/For_all "For all") values of variables, which are true in any unital commutative ring. Certain non-zero integers map to [zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_identity "Additive identity") in certain rings.

The lack of [zero divisors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_divisor "Zero divisor") in the integers (last property in the table) means that the commutative ring![Image 31: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) is an [integral domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_domain "Integral domain").

The lack of multiplicative inverses, which is equivalent to the fact that ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) is not closed under division, means that ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) is _not_ a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)"). The smallest field containing the integers as a [subring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subring "Subring") is the field of [rational numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number "Rational number"). The process of constructing the rationals from the integers can be mimicked to form the [field of fractions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_of_fractions "Field of fractions") of any integral domain. And back, starting from an [algebraic number field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_number_field "Algebraic number field") (an extension of rational numbers), its [ring of integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_of_integers "Ring of integers") can be extracted, which includes ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) as its [subring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subring "Subring").

Although ordinary division is not defined on ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc), the division "with remainder" is defined on them. It is called [Euclidean division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_division "Euclidean division"), and possesses the following important property: given two integers _a_ and _b_ with _b_ ≠ 0, there exist unique integers _q_ and _r_ such that _a_ = _q_ × _b_ + _r_ and 0 ≤ _r_< |_b_|, where |_b_| denotes the [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value") of _b_. The integer _q_ is called the _quotient_ and _r_ is called the _[remainder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remainder "Remainder")_ of the division of _a_ by _b_. The [Euclidean algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_algorithm "Euclidean algorithm") for computing [greatest common divisors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greatest_common_divisor "Greatest common divisor") works by a sequence of Euclidean divisions.

The above says that ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) is a [Euclidean domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_domain "Euclidean domain"). This implies that ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) is a [principal ideal domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_ideal_domain "Principal ideal domain"), and any positive integer can be written as the products of [primes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_number "Prime number") in an [essentially unique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Essentially_unique "Essentially unique") way.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-31) This is the [fundamental theorem of arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_arithmetic "Fundamental theorem of arithmetic").

Order-theoretic properties
--------------------------

![Image 38: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) is a [totally ordered set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_order "Total order") without [upper or lower bound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upper_and_lower_bounds "Upper and lower bounds"). The ordering of ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) is given by: :... −3 < −2 < −1 < 0 < 1 < 2 < 3 < .... An integer is _positive_ if it is greater than [zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0 "0"), and _negative_ if it is less than zero. Zero is defined as neither negative nor positive.

The ordering of integers is compatible with the algebraic operations in the following way:

1.   If _a_<_b_ and _c_<_d_, then _a_ + _c_<_b_ + _d_
2.   If _a_<_b_ and 0 <_c_, then _ac_<_bc_

Thus it follows that ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) together with the above ordering is an [ordered ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_ring "Ordered ring").

The integers are the only nontrivial [totally ordered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Totally_ordered "Totally ordered")[abelian group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group") whose positive elements are [well-ordered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Well-ordered "Well-ordered").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-32) This is equivalent to the statement that any [Noetherian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noetherian_ring "Noetherian ring")[valuation ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valuation_ring "Valuation ring") is either a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)")—or a [discrete valuation ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_valuation_ring "Discrete valuation ring").

Construction
------------

### Traditional development

In elementary school teaching, integers are often intuitively defined as the union of the (positive) natural numbers, [zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero "Zero"), and the negations of the natural numbers. This can be formalized as follows.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-33) First construct the set of natural numbers according to the [Peano axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peano_axioms "Peano axioms"), call this ![Image 41: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a). Then construct a set ![Image 42: {\displaystyle P^{-}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df5e7f2104dcc4156c6db49c5fe233e2f9e3fd91) which is [disjoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disjoint_sets "Disjoint sets") from ![Image 43: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) and in one-to-one correspondence with ![Image 44: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) via a function ![Image 45: {\displaystyle \psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45e5789e5d9c8f7c79744f43ecaaf8ba42a8553a). For example, take ![Image 46: {\displaystyle P^{-}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df5e7f2104dcc4156c6db49c5fe233e2f9e3fd91) to be the [ordered pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair "Ordered pair")![Image 47: {\displaystyle (1,n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e432fa1ab0d210a280dc37e0b7118565ed55946) with the mapping ![Image 48: {\displaystyle \psi =n\mapsto (1,n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8d453d0cfbd77129b34032455b63590a96e28714). Finally let 0 be some object not in ![Image 49: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) or ![Image 50: {\displaystyle P^{-}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df5e7f2104dcc4156c6db49c5fe233e2f9e3fd91), for example the ordered pair (0,0). Then the integers are defined to be the union ![Image 51: {\displaystyle P\cup P^{-}\cup \{0\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/373648483c45baf3647c3bfb81f55a8fd22e11d7).

The traditional arithmetic operations can then be defined on the integers in a [piecewise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piecewise "Piecewise") fashion, for each of positive numbers, negative numbers, and zero. For example [negation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negation "Negation") is defined as follows:

![Image 52: {\displaystyle -x={\begin{cases}\psi (x),&{\text{if }}x\in P\\\psi ^{-1}(x),&{\text{if }}x\in P^{-}\\0,&{\text{if }}x=0\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/419206f23502e746a700939a1a4e57017144a0c6)

The traditional style of definition leads to many different cases (each arithmetic operation needs to be defined on each combination of types of integer) and makes it tedious to prove that integers obey the various laws of arithmetic.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-34)

### Equivalence classes of ordered pairs

[![Image 53: Representation of equivalence classes for the numbers −5 to 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Relative_numbers_representation.svg/500px-Relative_numbers_representation.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Relative_numbers_representation.svg)

Red points represent ordered pairs of [natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number"). Linked red points are equivalence classes representing the blue integers at the end of the line.

In modern set-theoretic mathematics, a more abstract construction[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-35)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-36) allowing one to define arithmetical operations without any case distinction is often used instead.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-37) The integers can thus be formally constructed as the [equivalence classes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_class "Equivalence class") of [ordered pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair "Ordered pair") of [natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number")(_a_,_b_).[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-Campbell-1970-p83-38)

The intuition is that (_a_,_b_) stands for the result of subtracting _b_ from _a_.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-Campbell-1970-p83-38) To confirm our expectation that 1 − 2 and 4 − 5 denote the same number, we define an [equivalence relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_relation "Equivalence relation")~ on these pairs with the following rule:

![Image 54: {\displaystyle (a,b)\sim (c,d)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9eed2e08692e7ce42d13803af10157e550d6328)
precisely when

![Image 55: {\displaystyle a+d=b+c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/452fe2e66cb2e61da56ebdb3f8b8521557709cf6).
Addition and multiplication of integers can be defined in terms of the equivalent operations on the natural numbers;[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-Campbell-1970-p83-38) by using [(_a_,_b_)] to denote the equivalence class having (_a_,_b_) as a member, one has:

![Image 56: {\displaystyle [(a,b)]+[(c,d)]:=[(a+c,b+d)]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/30cad410af4dd3435f2f9d35c9775cf0bf2f12b4).![Image 57: {\displaystyle [(a,b)]\cdot [(c,d)]:=[(ac+bd,ad+bc)]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0f43120f0df09f5fcbc7dea5f5a9eac4acfabe37).
The negation (or additive inverse) of an integer is obtained by reversing the order of the pair:

![Image 58: {\displaystyle -[(a,b)]:=[(b,a)]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/679f1ca47c5c2989051e3707e676604071f65efc).
Hence subtraction can be defined as the addition of the additive inverse:

![Image 59: {\displaystyle [(a,b)]-[(c,d)]:=[(a+d,b+c)]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3d9a769afd448161d58b61f84bc7eb1b601ea448).
The standard ordering on the integers is given by:

![Image 60: {\displaystyle [(a,b)]<[(c,d)]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5f5d9d3f2ef39e8cf90692589f0b97945454536)[if and only if](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If_and_only_if "If and only if")![Image 61: {\displaystyle a+d<b+c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a8b0cf531600818efb3a8967fde95d2ed774e867).
It is easily verified that these definitions are independent of the choice of representatives of the equivalence classes.

Every equivalence class has a unique member that is of the form (_n_,0) or (0,_n_) (or both at once). The natural number _n_ is identified with the class [(_n_,0)] (i.e., the natural numbers are [embedded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embedding "Embedding") into the integers by map sending _n_ to [(_n_,0)]), and the class [(0,_n_)] is denoted −_n_ (this covers all remaining classes, and gives the class [(0,0)] a second time since −0=0.

Thus, [(_a_,_b_)] is denoted by

![Image 62: {\displaystyle {\begin{cases}a-b,&{\mbox{if }}a\geq b\\-(b-a),&{\mbox{if }}a<b\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e3a3ad7ae93b9136af5dd636c43f7c5678257ada)
If the natural numbers are identified with the corresponding integers (using the embedding mentioned above), this convention creates no ambiguity.

This notation recovers the familiar [representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_representation "Group representation") of the integers as {..., −2, −1, 0, 1, 2, ...} .

Some examples are:

![Image 63: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}0&=[(0,0)]&=[(1,1)]&=\cdots &&=[(k,k)]\\1&=[(1,0)]&=[(2,1)]&=\cdots &&=[(k+1,k)]\\-1&=[(0,1)]&=[(1,2)]&=\cdots &&=[(k,k+1)]\\2&=[(2,0)]&=[(3,1)]&=\cdots &&=[(k+2,k)]\\-2&=[(0,2)]&=[(1,3)]&=\cdots &&=[(k,k+2)]\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59af091a8f28053ca4a7a7508509918bd31c9d8f)
### Other approaches

In theoretical computer science, other approaches for the construction of integers are used by [automated theorem provers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automated_theorem_proving "Automated theorem proving") and [term rewrite engines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rewriting "Rewriting"). Integers are represented as [algebraic terms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Term_algebra "Term algebra") built using a few basic operations (e.g., **zero**, **succ**, **pred**) and using [natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number"), which are assumed to be already constructed (using the [Peano approach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peano_axioms "Peano axioms")).

There exist at least ten such constructions of signed integers.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer#cite_note-39) These constructions differ in several ways: the number of basic operations used for the construction, the number (usually, between 0 and 2), and the types of arguments accepted by these operations; the presence or absence of natural numbers as arguments of some of these operations, and the fact that these operations are free constructors or not, i.e., that the same integer can be represented using only one or many algebraic terms.

The technique for the construction of integers presented in the previous section corresponds to the particular case where there is a single basic operation **pair**![Image 64: {\displaystyle (x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41cf50e4a314ca8e2c30964baa8d26e5be7a9386) that takes as arguments two natural numbers ![Image 65: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) and ![Image 66: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d), and returns an integer (equal to ![Image 67: {\displaystyle x-y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3129cb3620bd9f38d0304a0fca719644d7d2d265)). This operation is not free since the integer 0 can be written **pair**(0,0), or **pair**(1,1), or **pair**(2,2), etc.. This technique of construction is used by the [proof assistant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proof_assistant "Proof assistant")[Isabelle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isabelle_(proof_assistant) "Isabelle (proof assistant)"); however, many other tools use alternative construction techniques, notable those based upon free constructors, which are simpler and can be implemented more efficiently in computers.

Computer science
----------------

An integer is often a primitive [data type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Data_type "Data type") in [computer languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_language "Computer language"). However, integer data types can only represent a [subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset") of all integers, since practical computers are of finite capacity. Also, in the common [two's complement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two%27s_complement "Two's complement") representation, the inherent definition of [sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sign_(mathematics) "Sign (mathematics)") distinguishes between "negative" and "non-negative" rather than "negative, positive, and 0". (It is, however, certainly possible for a computer to determine whether an integer value is truly positive.) Fixed length integer approximation data types (or subsets) are denoted _int_ or Integer in several programming languages (such as [Algol68](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algol68 "Algol68"), [C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C_(computer_language) "C (computer language)"), [Java](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Java_(programming_language) "Java (programming language)"), [Delphi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Object_Pascal "Object Pascal"), etc.).

Variable-length representations of integers, such as [bignums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bignum "Bignum"), can store any integer that fits in the computer's memory. Other integer data types are implemented with a fixed size, usually a number of bits which is a power of 2 (4, 8, 16, etc.) or a memorable number of decimal digits (e.g., 9 or 10).

Cardinality
-----------

The set of integers is [countably infinite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Countably_infinite "Countably infinite"), meaning it is possible to pair each integer with a unique natural number. An example of such a pairing is

(0, 1), (1, 2), (−1, 3), (2, 4), (−2, 5), (3, 6), . . . ,(1 − _k_, 2 _k_ − 1), (_k_, 2 _k_ ), . . .
More technically, the [cardinality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality "Cardinality") of ![Image 68: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) is said to equal ℵ 0 ([aleph-null](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aleph_number "Aleph number")). The pairing between elements of ![Image 69: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) and ![Image 70: {\displaystyle \mathbb {N} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdf9a96b565ea202d0f4322e9195613fb26a9bed) is called a [bijection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijection "Bijection").
