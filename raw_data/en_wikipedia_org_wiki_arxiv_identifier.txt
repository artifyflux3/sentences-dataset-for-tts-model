**arXiv** (pronounced as "[archive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archive "Archive")"—the X represents the [Greek letter chi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chi_(letter) "Chi (letter)") ⟨χ⟩)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-Steele-1) is an [open-access repository](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open-access_repository "Open-access repository") of electronic [preprints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preprint "Preprint") and [postprints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postprints "Postprints") (known as [e-prints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E-prints "E-prints")) approved for posting after moderation, but not [peer reviewed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scholarly_peer_review "Scholarly peer review"). It consists of [scientific papers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_papers "Scientific papers") in the fields of [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy"), [electrical engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_engineering "Electrical engineering"), [computer science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science "Computer science"), [quantitative biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantitative_biology "Quantitative biology"), [statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics "Statistics"), [mathematical finance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_finance "Mathematical finance"), and [economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economics "Economics"), which can be accessed online. In many fields of mathematics and physics, almost all scientific papers are [self-archived](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-archiving "Self-archiving") on the arXiv repository before publication in a peer-reviewed journal. Some publishers also grant permission for authors to archive the peer-reviewed [postprint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postprint "Postprint"). Begun on August 14, 1991, arXiv.org passed the half-million-article milestone on October 3, 2008,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-3) had hit a million by the end of 2014[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-dddmag2015-5) and two million by the end of 2021.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-7) As of November 2024, the submission rate is about 24,000 articles per month.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-8)

arXiv was made possible by the compact [TeX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TeX "TeX") file format, which allowed scientific papers to be easily transmitted over the [Internet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internet "Internet") and rendered [client-side](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Client-side "Client-side").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-11) Around 1990, [Joanne Cohn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joanne_Cohn "Joanne Cohn") began emailing [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") preprints to colleagues as TeX files, but the number of papers being sent soon filled mailboxes to capacity.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-12)[Paul Ginsparg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Ginsparg "Paul Ginsparg") recognized the need for central storage, and in August 1991 he created a central [repository](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disciplinary_repository "Disciplinary repository") mailbox stored at the [Los Alamos National Laboratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Alamos_National_Laboratory "Los Alamos National Laboratory") (LANL) that could be accessed from any computer.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-13) Additional modes of access were soon added: [FTP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/FTP "FTP") in 1991, [Gopher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gopher_(protocol) "Gopher (protocol)") in 1992, and the [World Wide Web](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Wide_Web "World Wide Web") in 1993.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-dddmag2015-5)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-14) The term [e-print](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E-print "E-print") was quickly adopted to describe the articles.

It began as a physics archive, called the [LANL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LANL "LANL") preprint archive, but soon expanded to include astronomy, mathematics, computer science, quantitative biology and, most recently, statistics. Its original [domain name](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_name "Domain name") was xxx.lanl.gov. Due to LANL's lack of interest in the rapidly expanding technology, in 2001 Ginsparg changed institutions to [Cornell University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornell_University "Cornell University") and changed the name of the repository to arXiv.org.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-15) Ginsparg brainstormed the new name with his wife; the domain "archive" was already claimed, so "chi" was replaced with "X" standing in as the [Greek letter chi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chi_(letter) "Chi (letter)") and the "e" dropped for symmetry around the "X".[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-wired2025-16)

arXiv was an early adopter and promoter of [preprints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preprints "Preprints").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-SPARC_2021-17) Its success in sharing preprints was one of the precipitating factors that led to the later movement in [scientific publishing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_publishing "Scientific publishing") known as [open access](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_access_(publishing) "Open access (publishing)").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-SPARC_2021-17)[Mathematicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician") and scientists regularly upload their papers to arXiv.org for worldwide access[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-18) and sometimes for reviews before they are published in [peer-reviewed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peer_review "Peer review")[journals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_journal "Academic journal"). Ginsparg was awarded a [MacArthur Fellowship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MacArthur_Fellowship "MacArthur Fellowship") in 2002 for his establishment of arXiv.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-19) The annual budget for arXiv was approximately $826,000 for 2013 to 2017, funded jointly by Cornell University Library, the [Simons Foundation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simons_Foundation "Simons Foundation") (in both gift and [challenge grant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Challenge_grant "Challenge grant") forms) and annual fee income from member institutions.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-20) This model arose in 2010, when Cornell sought to broaden the financial funding of the project by asking institutions to make annual voluntary contributions based on the amount of download usage by each institution. Each member institution pledges a five-year funding commitment to support arXiv. Based on institutional usage ranking, the annual fees are set in four tiers from $1,000 to $4,400. Cornell's goal is to raise at least $504,000 per year through membership fees generated by approximately 220 institutions.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-21)

In September 2011, Cornell University Library took overall administrative and financial responsibility for arXiv's operation and development. Ginsparg was quoted in the _[Chronicle of Higher Education](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronicle\_of\_Higher\_Education "Chronicle of Higher Education")_ as joking that it "was supposed to be a [three-hour tour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gilligan%27s_Island "Gilligan's Island"), not a life sentence".[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-CHE_2011-22) However, Ginsparg remains on the arXiv's Scientific Advisory Board and its Physics Advisory Committee.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-23)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-24)

In January 2022, arXiv began assigning [DOIs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_object_identifier "Digital object identifier") to articles, in collaboration with [DataCite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DataCite "DataCite").[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-25)

Each arXiv paper has a unique identifier:

*   `YYMM.NNNNN`, e.g. `1507.00123`,
*   `YYMM.NNNN`, e.g. `0704.0001`,
*   `arch-ive/YYMMNNN` for older papers, e.g. `hep-th/9901001`.

Different versions of the same paper are specified by a version number at the end. For example, `1709.08980v1`. If no version number is specified, the default is the latest version.

arXiv uses a category system. Each paper is tagged with one or more categories. Some categories have two layers. For example, `q-fin.TR` is the "Trading and Market Microstructure" category within "quantitative finance". Other categories have one layer. For example, `hep-ex` is "high energy physics experiments".

Moderation process and endorsement
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ArXiv&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Moderation process and endorsement")]

Although arXiv is not [peer reviewed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peer_review "Peer review"), a collection of moderators for each area review the [submissions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_submission "Electronic submission"); they may recategorize any that are deemed off-topic,[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-mckinney-26) or reject submissions that are not scientific papers, or sometimes for undisclosed reasons.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-Merali-27) The lists of moderators for many sections of arXiv are publicly available,[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-28) but moderators for most of the physics sections remain unlisted.

Additionally, an "endorsement" system was introduced in 2004 as part of an effort to ensure content is relevant and of interest to current research in the specified disciplines.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-29) Under the system, for categories that use it, an author must be endorsed by an established arXiv author before being allowed to submit papers to those categories. Endorsers are not asked to review the paper for errors but to check whether the paper is appropriate for the intended subject area.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-mckinney-26) New authors from recognized academic institutions generally receive automatic endorsement, which in practice means that they do not need to deal with the endorsement system at all. However, the endorsement system has attracted criticism for allegedly restricting scientific inquiry.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-30)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-31)

A majority of the [e-prints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E-print "E-print") are also submitted to [journals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_journal "Scientific journal") for publication, but some work, including some very influential papers, remain purely as e-prints and are never published in a peer-reviewed journal. A well-known example of the latter is an outline of a proof of [Thurston's geometrization conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thurston%27s_geometrization_conjecture "Thurston's geometrization conjecture"), including the [Poincaré conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_conjecture "Poincaré conjecture") as a particular case, uploaded by [Grigori Perelman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grigori_Perelman "Grigori Perelman") in November 2002.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-32) Perelman appears content to forgo the traditional peer-reviewed journal process, stating: "If anybody is interested in my way of solving the problem, it's all there [on the arXiv]– let them go and read about it".[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-33) Despite this non-traditional method of publication, other mathematicians recognized this work by offering the [Fields Medal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fields_Medal "Fields Medal") and [Clay Mathematics Millennium Prizes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millennium_Prize_Problems "Millennium Prize Problems") to Perelman, both of which he refused.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-34)

While arXiv does contain some dubious e-prints, such as those claiming to refute famous theorems or proving famous conjectures such as [Fermat's Last Theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermat%27s_Last_Theorem "Fermat's Last Theorem") using only high-school mathematics, a 2002 article which appeared in _[Notices of the American Mathematical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Notices\_of\_the\_American\_Mathematical\_Society "Notices of the American Mathematical Society")_ described those as "surprisingly rare".[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-35) arXiv generally re-classifies these works, e.g. in "General mathematics", rather than deleting them;[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-36) however, some authors have voiced concern over the lack of transparency in the arXiv screening process.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-Merali-27)

### Withdrawn preprints

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ArXiv&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Withdrawn preprints")]

It has been reported that 14,000 preprints have been withdrawn at arXiv, most commonly due to "crucial errors".[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-h426-37) A lesser number of the withdrawals were due to the preprint being subsumed by another publication. The report itself was posted at arXiv December, 2024.

Papers can be submitted in any of several formats, including [LaTeX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LaTeX "LaTeX"), and [PDF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PDF "PDF") printed from a [word processor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word_processor "Word processor") other than TeX or LaTeX. The [submission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_submission "Electronic submission") is rejected by the arXiv software if generating the final [PDF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PDF "PDF") file fails, if any image file is too large, or if the total size of the submission is too large. arXiv now allows one to store and modify an incomplete submission, and only finalize the submission when ready. The time stamp on the article is set when the submission is finalized.

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dd/Arxiv.org_abstract_view.png/330px-Arxiv.org_abstract_view.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Arxiv.org_abstract_view.png)

A screenshot of viewing a paper's abstract on arxiv.org in 2021

The standard access route is through the arXiv.org website, which is publicly accessible and does not require an account. Other interfaces and access routes have also been created by other un-associated organisations.

[Metadata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metadata "Metadata") for arXiv is made available through [OAI-PMH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OAI-PMH "OAI-PMH"), the standard for [open access repositories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_access_repositories "Open access repositories").[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-38) Content is therefore indexed in all major consumers of such data, such as [BASE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BASE_(search_engine) "BASE (search engine)"), [CORE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CORE_(research_service) "CORE (research service)") and [Unpaywall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unpaywall "Unpaywall"). As of 2020, the Unpaywall dump links over 500,000 arxiv URLs as the [open access](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_access "Open access") version of a work found in [CrossRef](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CrossRef "CrossRef") data from the publishers, making arXiv a top 10 global host of [green open access](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_open_access "Green open access").

Finally, researchers can select sub-fields and receive daily e-mailings or [RSS feeds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RSS_feed "RSS feed") of all submissions in them.

Copyright status of files
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ArXiv&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Copyright status of files")]

Files on arXiv can have a number of different copyright statuses:[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ArXiv_(identifier)#cite_note-39)

1.   Some are [public domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_domain "Public domain"), in which case they will have a statement saying so.
2.   Some are available under either the [Creative Commons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creative_Commons "Creative Commons") 4.0 [Attribution-ShareAlike license](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creative_Commons_licenses "Creative Commons licenses") or the Creative Commons 4.0 [Attribution-Noncommercial-ShareAlike license](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creative_Commons_licenses "Creative Commons licenses").
3.   Some are copyright to the publisher, but the author has the right to distribute them and has given arXiv a non-exclusive irrevocable license to distribute them.
4.   Most are copyright to the author, and arXiv has only a non-exclusive irrevocable license to distribute them.
