From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

The **classical limit** or **correspondence limit** is the ability of a [physical theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoretical_physics "Theoretical physics") to approximate or "recover" [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics") when considered over special values of its parameters.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_limit#cite_note-1) The classical limit is used with physical theories that predict non-classical behavior.

A [heuristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heuristic "Heuristic") postulate called the [correspondence principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Correspondence_principle "Correspondence principle") was introduced to [quantum theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohr_model "Bohr model") by [Niels Bohr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niels_Bohr "Niels Bohr"): in effect it states that some kind of continuity argument should apply to the classical limit of quantum systems as the value of the [Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant") normalized by the action of these systems becomes very small. Often, this is approached through "quasi-classical" techniques (cf. [WKB approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WKB_approximation "WKB approximation")).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_limit#cite_note-2)

More rigorously,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_limit#cite_note-3) the mathematical operation involved in classical limits is a [group contraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_contraction "Group contraction"), approximating physical systems where the relevant action is much larger than the reduced Planck constant ħ, so the "deformation parameter" ħ/S can be effectively taken to be zero (cf. [Weyl quantization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weyl_quantization "Weyl quantization").) Thus typically, quantum commutators (equivalently, [Moyal brackets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moyal_bracket "Moyal bracket")) reduce to [Poisson brackets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poisson_bracket "Poisson bracket"),[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_limit#cite_note-4) in a [group contraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_contraction "Group contraction").

In [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), due to [Werner Heisenberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg "Werner Heisenberg")'s [uncertainty principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncertainty_principle "Uncertainty principle"), an [electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") can never be at rest; it must always have a non-zero [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy"), a result not found in classical mechanics. For example, if we consider something very large relative to an electron, like a baseball, the uncertainty principle predicts that it cannot really have zero kinetic energy, but the uncertainty in kinetic energy is so small that the baseball can effectively appear to be at rest, and hence it appears to obey classical mechanics. In general, if large energies and large objects (relative to the size and energy levels of an electron) are considered in quantum mechanics, the result will appear to obey classical mechanics. The typical [occupation numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occupation_number "Occupation number") involved are huge: a macroscopic [harmonic oscillator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_oscillator "Harmonic oscillator") with ω=2 Hz, m=10 g, and maximum [amplitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amplitude "Amplitude")x 0=10 cm, has _S_≈_E_/_ω_≈ _mωx_ 2

0/2≈ 10−4 kg·m 2/s=ħn, so that n≃10 30. Further see [coherent states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coherent_states#The_wavefunction_of_a_coherent_state "Coherent states"). It is less clear, however, how the classical limit applies to chaotic systems, a field known as [quantum chaos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_chaos "Quantum chaos").

Quantum mechanics and classical mechanics are usually treated with entirely different formalisms: quantum theory using [Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space"), and classical mechanics using a representation in [phase space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_space "Phase space"). One can bring the two into a common mathematical framework in various ways. In the [phase space formulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_space_formulation "Phase space formulation") of quantum mechanics, which is statistical in nature, logical connections between quantum mechanics and classical statistical mechanics are made, enabling natural comparisons between them, including the violations of [Liouville's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liouville%27s_theorem_(Hamiltonian) "Liouville's theorem (Hamiltonian)") upon quantization.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_limit#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_limit#cite_note-6)

In a crucial paper (1933), [Paul Dirac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Dirac "Paul Dirac")[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_limit#cite_note-7) explained how classical mechanics is an [emergent phenomenon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emergence#Non-living,_physical_systems "Emergence") of quantum mechanics: [destructive interference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Destructive_interference#Quantum_interference "Destructive interference") among paths with non-[extremal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extremal "Extremal") macroscopic actions S»ħ obliterate amplitude contributions in the [path integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Path_integral_formulation "Path integral formulation") he introduced, leaving the extremal action S class, thus the classical action path as the dominant contribution, an observation further elaborated by [Richard Feynman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Feynman "Richard Feynman") in his 1942 PhD dissertation.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_limit#cite_note-8) (Further see [quantum decoherence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_decoherence "Quantum decoherence").)

Time-evolution of expectation values
------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Classical_limit&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Time-evolution of expectation values")]

One simple way to compare classical to quantum mechanics is to consider the time-evolution of the _expected_ position and _expected_ momentum, which can then be compared to the time-evolution of the ordinary position and momentum in classical mechanics. The quantum expectation values satisfy the [Ehrenfest theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ehrenfest_theorem "Ehrenfest theorem"). For a one-dimensional quantum particle moving in a potential ![Image 1: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845), the Ehrenfest theorem says[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_limit#cite_note-9)

![Image 2: {\displaystyle m{\frac {d}{dt}}\langle x\rangle =\langle p\rangle ;\quad {\frac {d}{dt}}\langle p\rangle =-\left\langle V'(X)\right\rangle .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24d5e514f985a955617f92c1c99cc8e1493f51a4)
Although the first of these equations is consistent with the classical mechanics, the second is not: If the pair ![Image 3: {\displaystyle (\langle X\rangle ,\langle P\rangle )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a208b4fe89bcbccd675e91ada6ab2f734def8b4) were to satisfy Newton's second law, the right-hand side of the second equation would have read

![Image 4: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}\langle p\rangle =-V'\left(\left\langle X\right\rangle \right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/111e4cac8a53a827ffc92edf505f01f1d22a789d).
But in most cases,

![Image 5: {\displaystyle \left\langle V'(X)\right\rangle \neq V'(\left\langle X\right\rangle )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b2188696ef5c10aa37b82e3c5492cb76562072c).
If for example, the potential ![Image 6: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) is cubic, then ![Image 7: {\displaystyle V'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff31fe992a31b4c954a933f1be91e2739a1c0ae7) is quadratic, in which case, we are talking about the distinction between ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \langle X^{2}\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54c709b0821e6cdff54da65be02b03d0708bc185) and ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \langle X\rangle ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c11f0def5713baa82e390942e3dc4a78956dd6c0), which differ by ![Image 10: {\displaystyle (\Delta X)^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/577d0ad84bcf442f30c44c35506a863c980f2291).

An exception occurs in case when the classical equations of motion are linear, that is, when ![Image 11: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) is quadratic and ![Image 12: {\displaystyle V'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff31fe992a31b4c954a933f1be91e2739a1c0ae7) is linear. In that special case, ![Image 13: {\displaystyle V'\left(\left\langle X\right\rangle \right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a4334cb83aa21b56c0941d539772062e8c6cd79) and ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \left\langle V'(X)\right\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db61e58cc63308d55d3cea8ab0ea1c35213082f5) do agree. In particular, for a [free particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_particle "Free particle") or a [quantum harmonic oscillator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_harmonic_oscillator "Quantum harmonic oscillator"), the expected position and expected momentum exactly follows solutions of Newton's equations.

For general systems, the best we can hope for is that the expected position and momentum will _approximately_ follow the classical trajectories. If the wave function is highly concentrated around a point ![Image 15: {\displaystyle x_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86f21d0e31751534cd6584264ecf864a6aa792cf), then ![Image 16: {\displaystyle V'\left(\left\langle X\right\rangle \right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a4334cb83aa21b56c0941d539772062e8c6cd79) and ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \left\langle V'(X)\right\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db61e58cc63308d55d3cea8ab0ea1c35213082f5) will be _almost_ the same, since both will be approximately equal to ![Image 18: {\displaystyle V'(x_{0})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/597d8921d945c06cd6f9c31c4547c1fbc824b9ed). In that case, the expected position and expected momentum will remain very close to the classical trajectories, at least _for as long as_ the wave function remains highly localized in position.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_limit#cite_note-10)

Now, if the initial state is very localized in position, it will be very spread out in momentum, and thus we expect that the wave function will rapidly spread out, and the connection with the classical trajectories will be lost. When the Planck constant is small, however, it is possible to have a state that is well localized in _both_ position and momentum. The small uncertainty in momentum ensures that the particle _remains_ well localized in position for a long time, so that expected position and momentum continue to closely track the classical trajectories for a long time.

Relativity and other deformations
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Classical_limit&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Relativity and other deformations")]

Other familiar deformations in physics involve:

*   The deformation of classical Newtonian into relativistic mechanics ([special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity")), with deformation parameter _v_/_c_; the classical limit involves small speeds, so _v_/_c_→0, and the systems appear to obey Newtonian mechanics.
*   Similarly for the deformation of Newtonian gravity into [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), with deformation parameter Schwarzschild-radius/characteristic-dimension, we find that objects once again appear to obey classical mechanics (flat space), when the mass of an object times the square of the [Planck length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_length "Planck length") is much smaller than its size and the sizes of the problem addressed. See [Newtonian limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newtonian_limit "Newtonian limit").
*   Wave optics might also be regarded as a deformation of [ray optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical_optics "Geometrical optics") for deformation parameter _λ_/_a_.
*   Likewise, [thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamics "Thermodynamics") deforms to [statistical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_mechanics "Statistical mechanics") with deformation parameter 1/_N_.
