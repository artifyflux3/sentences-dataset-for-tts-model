The **Laniakea Supercluster** ([/ˌ l ɑː n i.ə ˈ k eɪ.ə/](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English "Help:IPA/English"); [Hawaiian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawaiian_language "Hawaiian language") for "open skies" or "immense heaven")[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster#cite_note-2) or the **Local Supercluster** (**LSC** or **LS**) is the [galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy")[supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercluster "Supercluster") that is home to the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") and approximately 100,000 other nearby galaxies.

It was defined in September 2014, when a group of astronomers including [R. Brent Tully](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R._Brent_Tully "R. Brent Tully") of the [University of Hawaiʻi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Hawai%CA%BBi_at_M%C4%81noa "University of Hawaiʻi at Mānoa"), [Hélène Courtois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H%C3%A9l%C3%A8ne_Courtois "Hélène Courtois") of the [University of Lyon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Lyon "University of Lyon"), Yehuda Hoffman of the [Hebrew University of Jerusalem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebrew_University_of_Jerusalem "Hebrew University of Jerusalem"), and [Daniel Pomarède](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daniel_Pomar%C3%A8de "Daniel Pomarède") of CEA [Université Paris-Saclay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universit%C3%A9_Paris-Saclay "Université Paris-Saclay") published a new way of defining superclusters according to the [relative velocities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radial_velocity "Radial velocity") of galaxies.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster#cite_note-:0-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster#cite_note-4) The new definition of the local supercluster subsumes the prior defined [Virgo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster "Virgo Supercluster") and [Hydra-Centaurus Supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydra-Centaurus_Supercluster "Hydra-Centaurus Supercluster") as appendages, the former being the prior defined local supercluster.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster#cite_note-NRAO-2014-09-03-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster#cite_note-Reuters-2014-09-03-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster#cite_note-Nature-2014-09-03-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster#cite_note-NYT-20140903-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster#cite_note-SandT-2014-09-03-9)

Follow-up studies suggest that the Laniakea Supercluster is not gravitationally bound. It will disperse rather than continue to maintain itself as an overdensity relative to surrounding areas.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster#cite_note-chon-10)

Name
----

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Laniakea_Supercluster&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Name")]

The name _laniākea_ ([[ˈlɐnijaːˈkɛjə]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Hawaiian "Help:IPA/Hawaiian")) means 'immense heaven' in [Hawaiian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawaiian_language "Hawaiian language"), from _lani_'heaven' and _ākea_'spacious, immeasurable'. The name was suggested by [Nawaʻa Napoleon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nawa%CA%BBa_Napoleon "Nawaʻa Napoleon"), an associate professor of Hawaiian language at [Kapiolani Community College](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kapiolani_Community_College "Kapiolani Community College").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster#cite_note-11) The name honors [Polynesian navigators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynesian_navigation "Polynesian navigation"), who used knowledge of the sky to navigate the [Pacific Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pacific_Ocean "Pacific Ocean").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster#cite_note-12)

Characteristics
---------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Laniakea_Supercluster&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Characteristics")]

A video showing in 3D Laniakea and other nearby superclusters of galaxies

The Laniakea Supercluster encompasses approximately 100,000 galaxies stretched out over 160[Mpc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec#Megaparsecs_and_gigaparsecs "Parsec") (520 million[ly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year "Light-year")). It has the approximate mass of 10 17 solar masses, or 100,000 times that of our galaxy, which is almost the same as that of the [Horologium Supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horologium_Supercluster "Horologium Supercluster").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster#cite_note-:0-3) It consists of four subparts, which were known previously as separate superclusters:

*   [Virgo Supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster "Virgo Supercluster"), the part in which the Milky Way resides.
*   [Hydra–Centaurus Supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydra%E2%80%93Centaurus_Supercluster "Hydra–Centaurus Supercluster")
    *   the [Great Attractor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Attractor "Great Attractor"), Laniakea's central gravitational point near [Norma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norma_Cluster "Norma Cluster")
    *   [Antlia Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antlia_Wall "Antlia Wall"), known as [Hydra Supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydra_Supercluster "Hydra Supercluster")
    *   [Centaurus Supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centaurus_Supercluster "Centaurus Supercluster")

*   [Pavo–Indus Supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pavo%E2%80%93Indus_Supercluster "Pavo–Indus Supercluster")
*   [Southern Supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Supercluster "Southern Supercluster"), including [Fornax Cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fornax_Cluster "Fornax Cluster") (S373), [Dorado](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dorado_Cluster "Dorado Cluster") and [Eridanus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eridanus_Cluster "Eridanus Cluster") clouds.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster#cite_note-13)

The most massive galaxy clusters of the Laniakea Supercluster are [Virgo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Cluster "Virgo Cluster"), [Hydra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydra_Cluster "Hydra Cluster"), [Centaurus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centaurus_Cluster "Centaurus Cluster"), [Abell 3565](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Abell_clusters "List of Abell clusters"), [Abell 3574](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Abell_3574&action=edit&redlink=1 "Abell 3574 (page does not exist)"), [Abell 3521](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Abell_3521&action=edit&redlink=1 "Abell 3521 (page does not exist)"), [Fornax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fornax_Cluster "Fornax Cluster"), [Eridanus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eridanus_Cluster "Eridanus Cluster"), and [Norma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norma_Cluster "Norma Cluster"). The entire supercluster consists of approximately 300 to 500 known galaxy clusters and groups. The real number may be much larger because some of these are traversing the [Zone of Avoidance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zone_of_Avoidance "Zone of Avoidance"), an area of the sky that is partially obscured by [gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas "Gas") and [dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_dust "Cosmic dust") from the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") galaxy, making them essentially undetectable.

Superclusters are some of the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe")'s largest structures and have boundaries that are difficult to define, especially from the inside. Within a given supercluster, most galaxy motions will be directed inward, toward the [center of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_mass "Center of mass"). This gravitational focal point, in the case of Laniakea, is called the [Great Attractor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Attractor "Great Attractor"), and influences the motions of the [Local Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_Group "Local Group") of galaxies, where the Milky Way galaxy resides, and all others throughout the supercluster. Unlike its constituent clusters, Laniakea is not [gravitationally bound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_binding_energy "Gravitational binding energy") and is projected to be torn apart by [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster#cite_note-Nature-2014-09-03-7)

Although the confirmation of the existence of the Laniakea Supercluster emerged in 2014,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster#cite_note-:0-3) early studies in the 1980s already suggested that several of the superclusters then known might be connected. For example, South African astronomer [Tony Fairall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthony_Patrick_Fairall "Anthony Patrick Fairall") stated in 1988 that redshifts suggested that the Virgo and Hydra–Centaurus superclusters may be connected.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster#cite_note-14)

Location
--------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Laniakea_Supercluster&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Location")]

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Laniakea.gif/250px-Laniakea.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Laniakea.gif)

A map of superclusters within the nearby [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe"), with Laniakea shown in yellow

The neighboring superclusters to the Laniakea Supercluster are the [Shapley Supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shapley_Supercluster "Shapley Supercluster"), [Hercules Supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hercules_Supercluster "Hercules Supercluster"), [Coma Supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coma_Supercluster "Coma Supercluster"), and [Perseus–Pisces Supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perseus%E2%80%93Pisces_Supercluster "Perseus–Pisces Supercluster"). The edges of the superclusters and Laniakea were not clearly known at the time of Laniakea's definition.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster#cite_note-Reuters-2014-09-03-6) Since then, the study of the edges of the supercluster and of structures beyond them has substantially improved.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster#cite_note-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster#cite_note-16)

Laniakea is itself a constituent part of the [Pisces–Cetus Supercluster Complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pisces%E2%80%93Cetus_Supercluster_Complex "Pisces–Cetus Supercluster Complex"), a [galaxy filament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament "Galaxy filament").
