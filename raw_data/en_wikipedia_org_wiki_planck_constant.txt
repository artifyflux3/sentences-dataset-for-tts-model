The **Planck constant**, or **Planck's constant**, denoted by ![Image 6: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a), is a fundamental [physical constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_constant "Physical constant") of foundational importance in [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"): a [photon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon")'s energy is equal to its [frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency") multiplied by the Planck constant, and a particle's [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") is equal to the [wavenumber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavenumber "Wavenumber") of the associated [matter wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter_wave "Matter wave") (the reciprocal of its [wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength "Wavelength")) multiplied by the Planck constant.

The constant was postulated by [Max Planck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Planck "Max Planck") in 1900 as a [proportionality constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportionality_constant "Proportionality constant") needed to explain experimental [black-body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black-body "Black-body") radiation.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-Planck01-1) Planck later referred to the constant as the "quantum of [action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics) "Action (physics)")".[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-2) In 1905, [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") associated the "quantum" or minimal element of the energy to the electromagnetic wave itself. Max Planck received the 1918 [Nobel Prize in Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics "Nobel Prize in Physics") "in recognition of the services he rendered to the advancement of Physics by his discovery of energy quanta".

In [metrology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metrology "Metrology"), the Planck constant is used, together with other constants, to define the [kilogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram "Kilogram"), the [SI unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_unit "SI unit") of mass.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-3) The SI units are defined such that it has the exact value ![Image 7: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a) = 6.626 070 15×10−34 J⋅Hz−1[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-physconst-h-4) when the Planck constant is expressed in SI units.

The closely related **reduced Planck constant**, denoted ![Image 8: {\textstyle \hbar }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9d68c123c2f0f7e66f5a7a890e93cca598247a6) (h-bar), equal to the Planck constant divided by [2π](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tau_(mathematics) "Tau (mathematics)"): ![Image 9: {\textstyle \hbar ={\frac {h}{2\pi }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/788c1a8a0024c8e4f4e46bb932e1dabb5c1ae78d), is commonly used in quantum physics equations. It relates the energy of a photon to its [angular frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_frequency "Angular frequency"), and the linear momentum of a particle to the [angular wavenumber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_wavenumber "Angular wavenumber") of its associated matter wave. As ![Image 10: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a) has an exact defined value, the value of ![Image 11: {\textstyle \hbar }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9d68c123c2f0f7e66f5a7a890e93cca598247a6) can be calculated to arbitrary precision: ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \hbar }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de68de3a92517953436c93b5a76461d49160cc41) = 1.054 571 817...×10−34 J⋅s.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-physconst-hbar-5) As a [proportionality constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportionality_constant "Proportionality constant") in relationships involving angular quantities, the unit of ![Image 13: {\textstyle \hbar }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9d68c123c2f0f7e66f5a7a890e93cca598247a6) may be given as J·s/rad, with the same numerical value, as the [radian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian") is the natural [dimensionless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensionless_quantity "Dimensionless quantity") unit of [angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle").

### Origin of the constant

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Planck_constant&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Origin of the constant")]

[![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/16/Max_Planck_Wirkungsquantums_20050815.jpg/330px-Max_Planck_Wirkungsquantums_20050815.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Max_Planck_Wirkungsquantums_20050815.jpg)

Plaque at the [Humboldt University of Berlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humboldt_University_of_Berlin "Humboldt University of Berlin"): "In this edifice taught Max Planck, the discoverer of the elementary quantum of action _h_, from 1889 to 1928."

[![Image 15](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Wiens_law.svg/500px-Wiens_law.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Wiens_law.svg)

Intensity of light emitted from a [black body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_body "Black body"). Each curve represents behavior at different body temperatures. The Planck constant _h_ is used to explain the shape of these curves.

The Planck constant was formulated as part of Max Planck's successful effort to produce a mathematical expression that accurately predicted the observed spectral distribution of [black-body radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black-body_radiation "Black-body radiation").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-Bitter-6) This expression is now known as Planck's law.

In the last years of the 19th century, Max Planck was investigating the problem of black-body radiation first posed by [Kirchhoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustav_Kirchhoff "Gustav Kirchhoff") some 40 years earlier. Every [physical body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_body "Physical body") spontaneously and continuously emits [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation"). There was no expression or explanation for the overall shape of the observed emission spectrum. At the time, [Wien's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wien_approximation "Wien approximation") fit the data for short wavelengths and high temperatures, but failed for long wavelengths.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-Bitter-6): 141  Also around this time, but unknown to Planck, [Lord Rayleigh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lord_Rayleigh "Lord Rayleigh") had derived theoretically a formula, now known as the [Rayleigh–Jeans law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rayleigh%E2%80%93Jeans_law "Rayleigh–Jeans law"), that could reasonably predict long wavelengths but failed dramatically at short wavelengths.

Approaching this problem, Planck hypothesized that the equations of motion for light describe a set of [harmonic oscillators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_oscillator "Harmonic oscillator"), one for each possible frequency. He examined how the [entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy "Entropy") of the oscillators varied with the temperature of the body, trying to match Wien's law, and was able to derive an approximate mathematical function for the black-body spectrum,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-Planck01-1) which gave a simple empirical formula for long wavelengths.

Planck tried to find a mathematical expression that could reproduce Wien's law (for short wavelengths) and the empirical formula (for long wavelengths). This expression included a constant, ![Image 16: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a), which is thought to be for _Hilfsgröße_ (auxiliary quantity),[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-7) and subsequently became known as the Planck constant. The expression formulated by Planck showed that the spectral radiance per unit frequency of a body for [frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency")_ν_ at [absolute temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_temperature "Absolute temperature")_T_ is given by ![Image 17: {\displaystyle B_{\nu }(\nu ,T)d\nu ={\frac {2h\nu ^{3}}{c^{2}}}{\frac {1}{e^{\frac {h\nu }{k_{\mathrm {B} }T}}-1}}d\nu ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07b471728a02846c9c1c539ed02aad13cd461c68) where ![Image 18: {\displaystyle k_{\text{B}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9582c7c795d2def2c061f0dfa3a6f0fb3dd2de44) is the [Boltzmann constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_constant "Boltzmann constant"), ![Image 19: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a) is the Planck constant, and ![Image 20: {\displaystyle c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a67b81c2de995bd608d5b2df50cd8cd7d92455) is the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light") in the medium, whether material or vacuum.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-Planck_1914_6_168-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-Chan8-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-Rybicki_1979_22-10)

Planck soon realized that his solution was not unique. There were several different solutions, each of which gave a different value for the entropy of the oscillators.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-Planck01-1) To save his theory, Planck resorted to using the then-controversial theory of [statistical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_mechanics "Statistical mechanics"),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-Planck01-1) which he described as "an act of desperation".[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-Kragh-11) One of his new boundary conditions was

> to interpret _U_ _N_ ['the vibrational energy of _N_ oscillators'] not as a continuous, infinitely divisible quantity, but as a discrete quantity composed of an integral number of finite equal parts. Let us call each such part the energy element _ε_;

— Planck, "On the Law of Distribution of Energy in the Normal Spectrum"[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-Planck01-1)

With this new condition, Planck had imposed the quantization of the energy of the oscillators, in his own words, "a purely formal assumption ... actually I did not think much about it",[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-12) but one that would revolutionize physics. Applying this new approach to Wien's displacement law showed that the "energy element" must be proportional to the frequency of the oscillator, the first version of what is now sometimes termed the _[Planck–Einstein relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck%E2%80%93Einstein\_relation "Planck–Einstein relation")_: ![Image 21: {\displaystyle E=hf.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d8e54b560a268cc5eecb4ce42db2ade6ec1b8cd)

Planck was able to calculate the value of ![Image 22: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a) from experimental data on black-body radiation: his result, 6.55×10−34 J⋅s, is within 1.2% of the currently defined value.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-Planck01-1) He also made the first determination of the [Boltzmann constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_constant "Boltzmann constant")![Image 23: {\displaystyle k_{\text{B}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9582c7c795d2def2c061f0dfa3a6f0fb3dd2de44) from the same data and theory.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-PlanckNobel-13)

### Development and application

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Planck_constant&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Development and application")]

The black-body problem was revisited in 1905, when [Lord Rayleigh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_William_Strutt,_3rd_Baron_Rayleigh "John William Strutt, 3rd Baron Rayleigh") and [James Jeans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Jeans "James Jeans") (together) and [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") independently proved that classical electromagnetism could _never_ account for the observed spectrum. These proofs are commonly known as the "[ultraviolet catastrophe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet_catastrophe "Ultraviolet catastrophe")", a name coined by [Paul Ehrenfest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Ehrenfest "Paul Ehrenfest") in 1911. They contributed greatly (along with Einstein's work on the [photoelectric effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect "Photoelectric effect")) in convincing physicists that Planck's postulate of quantized energy levels was more than a mere mathematical formalism. The first [Solvay Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvay_Conference "Solvay Conference") in 1911 was devoted to "the theory of radiation and quanta".[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-14)

#### Photoelectric effect

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Planck_constant&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Photoelectric effect")]

The photoelectric effect is the emission of electrons (called "photoelectrons") from a surface when light is shone on it. It was first observed by [Alexandre Edmond Becquerel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexandre_Edmond_Becquerel "Alexandre Edmond Becquerel") in 1839, although credit is usually reserved for [Heinrich Hertz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinrich_Hertz "Heinrich Hertz"),[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-Nobel21-15) who published the first thorough investigation in 1887. Another particularly thorough investigation was published by [Philipp Lenard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philipp_Lenard "Philipp Lenard") (Lénárd Fülöp) in 1902.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-Lenard-16) Einstein's 1905 paper[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-17) discussing the effect in terms of light quanta would earn him the Nobel Prize in 1921,[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-Nobel21-15) after his predictions had been confirmed by the experimental work of [Robert Andrews Millikan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Andrews_Millikan "Robert Andrews Millikan").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-Millikan-18) The Nobel committee awarded the prize for his work on the photo-electric effect, rather than relativity, both because of a bias against purely theoretical physics not grounded in discovery or experiment, and dissent amongst its members as to the actual proof that relativity was real.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-20)

Before Einstein's paper, electromagnetic radiation such as visible light was considered to behave as a wave: hence the use of the terms "frequency" and "wavelength" to characterize different types of radiation. The energy transferred by a wave in a given time is called its [intensity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intensity_(physics) "Intensity (physics)"). The light from a theatre spotlight is more _intense_ than the light from a domestic lightbulb; that is to say that the spotlight gives out more energy per unit time and per unit space (and hence consumes more electricity) than the ordinary bulb, even though the color of the light might be very similar. Other waves, such as sound or the waves crashing against a seafront, also have their intensity. However, the energy account of the photoelectric effect did not seem to agree with the wave description of light.

The "photoelectrons" emitted as a result of the photoelectric effect have a certain [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy"), which can be measured. This kinetic energy (for each photoelectron) is _independent_ of the intensity of the light,[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-Lenard-16) but depends linearly on the frequency;[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-Millikan-18) and if the frequency is too low (corresponding to a photon energy that is less than the [work function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work_function "Work function") of the material), no photoelectrons are emitted at all, unless a plurality of photons, whose energetic sum is greater than the energy of the photoelectrons, acts virtually simultaneously (multiphoton effect).[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-21)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-22) Assuming the frequency is high enough to cause the photoelectric effect, a rise in intensity of the light source causes more photoelectrons to be emitted with the same kinetic energy, rather than the same number of photoelectrons to be emitted with higher kinetic energy.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-Lenard-16)

Einstein's explanation for these observations was that light itself is quantized; that the energy of light is not transferred continuously as in a classical wave, but only in small "packets" or quanta. The size of these "packets" of energy, which would later be named [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon"), was to be the same as Planck's "energy element", giving the modern version of the Planck–Einstein relation: ![Image 25: {\displaystyle E=hf.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d8e54b560a268cc5eecb4ce42db2ade6ec1b8cd)

Einstein's postulate was later proven experimentally: the constant of proportionality between the frequency of incident light ![Image 26: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) and the kinetic energy of photoelectrons ![Image 27: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b) was shown to be equal to the Planck constant ![Image 28: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a).[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-Millikan-18)

[![Image 29](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Bohr_atom_model.svg/250px-Bohr_atom_model.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bohr_atom_model.svg)

A schematization of the Bohr model of the hydrogen atom. The transition shown from the _n_ = 3 level to the _n_ = 2 level gives rise to visible light of wavelength 656 nm (red), as the model predicts.

In 1912 [John William Nicholson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_William_Nicholson "John William Nicholson") developed[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-23) an atomic model and found the angular momentum of the electrons in the model were related by _h_/2 π.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-HeilbronPath-24)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-McCormmach-25) Nicholson's nuclear quantum atomic model influenced the development of [Niels Bohr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niels_Bohr "Niels Bohr") 's atomic model[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-26)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-27)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-McCormmach-25) and Bohr quoted him in his 1913 paper of the Bohr model of the atom.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-Bohr-28) Bohr's model went beyond Planck's abstract harmonic oscillator concept: an electron in a Bohr atom could only have certain defined energies ![Image 30: {\displaystyle E_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad6b82f2a00af6c9efd4c16d4e99329605645c0c), defined by ![Image 31: {\displaystyle E_{n}=-{\frac {hcR_{\infty }}{n^{2}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/46b4a5f29957dddbfcfe312e291d144e2d3b9d7b) where ![Image 32: {\displaystyle c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a67b81c2de995bd608d5b2df50cd8cd7d92455) is the speed of light in vacuum, ![Image 33: {\displaystyle R_{\infty }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/639146705058e9d335aed47b55a9f802bc290b5f) is an experimentally determined constant (the [Rydberg constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rydberg_constant "Rydberg constant")) and ![Image 34: {\displaystyle n\in \{1,2,3,...\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3e97f8c9bf0deb3e92c46a93ea68c9ea970330e). This approach also allowed Bohr to account for the [Rydberg formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rydberg_formula "Rydberg formula"), an empirical description of the atomic spectrum of hydrogen, and to account for the value of the Rydberg constant ![Image 35: {\displaystyle R_{\infty }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/639146705058e9d335aed47b55a9f802bc290b5f) in terms of other fundamental constants. In discussing angular momentum of the electrons in his model Bohr introduced the quantity ![Image 36: {\textstyle {\frac {h}{2\pi }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d91adb268f891436e5499d126c8a68414506b99f), now known as the [reduced Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#Reduced_Planck_constant) as the quantum of [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum").[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-Bohr-28)

#### Uncertainty principle

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Planck_constant&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Uncertainty principle")]

The Planck constant also occurs in statements of [Werner Heisenberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg "Werner Heisenberg")'s uncertainty principle. Given numerous particles prepared in the same state, the [uncertainty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncertainty "Uncertainty") in their position, ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \Delta x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f3890eb866b6258d7a304fc34c70ee3fb3a81a70), and the uncertainty in their momentum, ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \Delta p_{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a84011ce3f13232ac79d38f73b7b48c463144cdc), obey ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \Delta x\,\Delta p_{x}\geq {\frac {\hbar }{2}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db6810da090450946c8eaed9712d17d740043617) where the uncertainty is given as the [standard deviation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_deviation "Standard deviation") of the measured value from its [expected value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expected_value "Expected value"). There are several other such pairs of physically measurable [conjugate variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjugate_variable "Conjugate variable") which obey a similar rule. One example is time vs. energy. The inverse relationship between the uncertainty of the two conjugate variables forces a tradeoff in quantum experiments, as measuring one quantity more precisely results in the other quantity becoming imprecise.

In addition to some assumptions underlying the interpretation of certain values in the quantum mechanical formulation, one of the fundamental cornerstones to the entire theory lies in the [commutator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutator "Commutator") relationship between the [position operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_operator "Position operator")![Image 40: {\displaystyle {\hat {x}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18d95a7845e4e16ffb7e18ab37a208d0ab18e0e0) and the [momentum operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum_operator "Momentum operator")![Image 41: {\displaystyle {\hat {p}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8bd4c026f1b3413adc58b9b65e89e62bce92c85a): ![Image 42: {\displaystyle [{\hat {p}}_{i},{\hat {x}}_{j}]=-i\hbar \delta _{ij},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a6de152aa445b7ca6653b9dd087ad604c2b8bf0e) where ![Image 43: {\displaystyle \delta _{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa75d04c11480d976e1396951e02cbb3c4f71568) is the [Kronecker delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta "Kronecker delta").

The [Planck relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_relation "Planck relation") connects the particular [photon energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon_energy "Photon energy")_E_ with its associated wave frequency _f_: ![Image 44: {\displaystyle E=hf.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d8e54b560a268cc5eecb4ce42db2ade6ec1b8cd) This energy is extremely small in terms of ordinarily perceived everyday objects.

Since the frequency _f_, [wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength "Wavelength")_λ_, and [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light")_c_ are related by ![Image 45: {\displaystyle f={\frac {c}{\lambda }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4dc0fb0b3be8b879ff12bb534c0b82ff36f7eb7b), the relation can also be expressed as ![Image 46: {\displaystyle E={\frac {hc}{\lambda }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7688f3f579790110ee154ffd6f242c291f7e1365)

#### de Broglie wavelength

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Planck_constant&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: de Broglie wavelength")]

In 1923, [Louis de Broglie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_de_Broglie "Louis de Broglie") generalized the Planck–Einstein relation by postulating that the Planck constant represents the proportionality between the momentum and the quantum wavelength of not just the photon, but the quantum wavelength of any particle. This was confirmed by experiments soon afterward. This holds throughout the quantum theory, including [electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrodynamics "Electrodynamics"). The [de Broglie wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Broglie_wavelength "De Broglie wavelength")_λ_ of the particle is given by ![Image 47: {\displaystyle \lambda ={\frac {h}{p}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea61dba4f570d474341b6f5ff22b0f26f52c1a87) where _p_ denotes the linear [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") of a particle, such as a photon, or any other [elementary particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle").

The [energy of a photon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon_energy "Photon energy") with angular frequency _ω_ = 2 _πf_ is given by ![Image 48: {\displaystyle E=\hbar \omega ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/74fe860e612d188411925adb0b1bd9280e852747) while its linear momentum relates to ![Image 49: {\displaystyle p=\hbar k,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0675af104e0ca277ba5bcac0661aca5a5f9221e5) where _k_ is an [angular wavenumber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavenumber "Wavenumber").

These two relations are the temporal and spatial parts of the special relativistic expression using [4-vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-Vector "Four-Vector"). ![Image 50: {\displaystyle P^{\mu }=\left({\frac {E}{c}},{\vec {p}}\right)=\hbar K^{\mu }=\hbar \left({\frac {\omega }{c}},{\vec {k}}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/958a2bb58475775f93394f5ee2dfe57d9450b49b)

#### Statistical mechanics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Planck_constant&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Statistical mechanics")]

Classical [statistical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_mechanics "Statistical mechanics") requires the existence of _h_ (but does not define its value).[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-29) Eventually, following upon Planck's discovery, it was speculated that physical [action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics) "Action (physics)") could not take on an arbitrary value, but instead was restricted to integer multiples of a very small quantity, the "[elementary] [quantum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum "Quantum") of action", now called the _Planck constant_.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-30) This was a significant conceptual part of the so-called "[old quantum theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_quantum_theory "Old quantum theory")" developed by physicists including [Bohr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niels_Bohr "Niels Bohr"), [Sommerfeld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arnold_Sommerfeld "Arnold Sommerfeld"), and [Ishiwara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jun_Ishiwara "Jun Ishiwara"), in which particle trajectories exist but are [hidden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hidden_variable_theory "Hidden variable theory"), but quantum laws constrain them based on their action. This view has been replaced by fully modern quantum theory, in which definite trajectories of motion do not even exist; rather, the particle is represented by a wavefunction spread out in space and in time.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-31): 373  Related to this is the concept of energy quantization which existed in old quantum theory and also exists in altered form in modern quantum physics. Classical physics cannot explain quantization of energy.

Dimension and value
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Planck_constant&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Dimension and value")]

The Planck constant has the same [dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(physics) "Dimension (physics)") as [action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics) "Action (physics)") and as [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum") (both with unit J·s=kg·m 2·s−1). The Planck constant is fixed at ![Image 51: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a) = 6.626 070 15×10−34 J⋅Hz−1[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-physconst-h-4) as part of the definition of the [SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") units.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-SIbrochure9th-32) Alternatively, if the [radian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian") were considered a [base unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_unit_of_measurement "Base unit of measurement"), then ![Image 52: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a) would have the dimension of action (unit J·s), while ![Image 53: {\displaystyle \hbar }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de68de3a92517953436c93b5a76461d49160cc41) would have the dimension of angular momentum (unit J·s·rad−1), instead.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-r763-33)

This value is used to define the SI unit of mass, the [kilogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram "Kilogram"): "the kilogram [...] is defined by taking the fixed numerical value of _h_ to be 6.626 070 15×10−34 when expressed in the unit J⋅s, which is equal to kg⋅m 2⋅s−1, where the [metre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre "Metre") and the [second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second "Second") are defined in terms of [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light")_c_ and duration of [hyperfine transition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperfine_structure "Hyperfine structure") of the [ground state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_state "Ground state") of an unperturbed [caesium-133](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium-133 "Caesium-133") atom Δ _ν_ Cs."[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-SIbrochure9th-32) Technologies of mass [metrology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metrology "Metrology") such as the [Kibble balance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kibble_balance "Kibble balance") measure the kilogram by fixing the Planck constant.

As _![Image 54: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a)_ has an exact defined value, the value of the reduced Planck constant _![Image 55: {\displaystyle \hbar }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/de68de3a92517953436c93b5a76461d49160cc41)_ can be calculated to arbitrary precision without any limiting uncertainty:

> ![Image 56: {\displaystyle \hbar ={\frac {h}{2\pi }}={\frac {6.626\,070\,15}{2\pi }}\times 10^{-34}\,\mathrm {J{\cdot }s} =1.054\,571\,817...\times 10^{-34}\,\mathrm {J{\cdot }s} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d59c27be238ce99df9de173cea811910add21809)

As a [proportionality constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportionality_constant "Proportionality constant") in relationships involving angular quantities, the unit of ![Image 57: {\textstyle \hbar }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9d68c123c2f0f7e66f5a7a890e93cca598247a6) may be given as J·s/rad, with the same numerical value, as the radian is the natural [dimensionless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensionless_quantity "Dimensionless quantity") unit of [angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle"). This is analogous to the use of [hertz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hertz "Hertz") (Hz) for ordinary [frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency") and [radians per second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian_per_second "Radian per second") (rad/s) for [angular frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_frequency "Angular frequency"), both dimensionally equal to s−1.

### Significance of the value

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Planck_constant&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Significance of the value")]

The Planck constant is one of the smallest constants used in physics. This reflects the fact that on a [scale adapted to humans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_scale "Human scale"), where energies are typical of the order of kilojoules and times are typical of the order of seconds or minutes, the Planck constant is very small. When the [product of energy and time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics) "Action (physics)") for a physical event approaches the Planck constant, [quantum effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_effects "Quantum effects") dominate.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-FeynmanII-34)

Equivalently, the order of the Planck constant reflects the fact that everyday objects and systems are made of a _large_ number of [microscopic particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microscopic_scale "Microscopic scale"). For example, in [green](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green "Green") light (with a [wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength "Wavelength") of 555[nanometres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanometre "Nanometre") or a [frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency") of 540 THz) each [photon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") has an [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy")_E_ = _hf_ = 3.58×10−19 J. This is a very small amount of energy in terms of everyday experience, but everyday experience is not concerned with individual photons any more than with individual [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom") or [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule"). An amount of [light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light "Light") more typical in everyday experience (though much larger than the smallest amount perceivable by the [human eye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_eye "Human eye")) is the energy of one [mole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mole_(unit) "Mole (unit)") of photons, which can be computed by multiplying the photon energy by the [Avogadro number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avogadro_constant "Avogadro constant"), 6.022 140 76×10 23,[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-physconst-NA-35) with the result of 216 kJ, about equal to the [food energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_energy "Food energy") in a small fresh [apple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple "Apple").[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant#cite_note-36)
