[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/Fungi_of_Saskatchewan.JPG/250px-Fungi_of_Saskatchewan.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Fungi_of_Saskatchewan.JPG)

An example of the biodiversity of [fungi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungi "Fungi") in a forest in [North Saskatchewan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Saskatchewan_Administration_District "Northern Saskatchewan Administration District") (in this photo, there are also leaf [lichens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lichen "Lichen") and [mosses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moss "Moss")).

**Biodiversity** refers to the variety and variability of [life on Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life "Life"). It can be measured at multiple levels, including [genetic variability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_variability "Genetic variability"), [species diversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species_diversity "Species diversity"), [ecosystem diversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem_diversity "Ecosystem diversity") and [phylogenetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phylogenetics "Phylogenetics") diversity.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Faith_19922-1) Diversity is unevenly distributed across the planet and is highest in the [tropics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropics "Tropics"), largely due to the region's warm climate and high [primary productivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_productivity "Primary productivity"). Although tropical forests cover less than one-fifth of Earth's land surface, they host approximately half of the world's species.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-2) Patterns such as the [latitudinal gradients in species diversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitudinal_gradients_in_species_diversity "Latitudinal gradients in species diversity") are observed in both marine and terrestrial organisms.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-auto2-3)

Since the emergence of [life on Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenesis "Abiogenesis"), biodiversity has undergone significant changes, including six major [mass extinctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_extinctions "Mass extinctions") and several smaller events. The [Phanerozoic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phanerozoic "Phanerozoic") eon (the past 540 million years) saw a rapid expansion of biodiversity, notably during the [Cambrian explosion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambrian_explosion "Cambrian explosion"), when many [multicellular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multicellular_organism "Multicellular organism")[phyla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phylum "Phylum") first appeared. Over the next 400 million years, biodiversity repeatedly declined due to [mass extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_extinction "Mass extinction") events. These included the [Carboniferous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboniferous "Carboniferous")[rainforest collapse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboniferous_rainforest_collapse "Carboniferous rainforest collapse") and the [Permian–Triassic extinction event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permian%E2%80%93Triassic_extinction_event "Permian–Triassic extinction event") 251 million years ago—which caused the most severe biodiversity loss in Earth's history. Recovery from that event took about 30 million years.

Currently, [human activities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment "Human impact on the environment") are driving a rapid decline in [biodiversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity_loss "Biodiversity loss"), often referred to as the [Holocene extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holocene_extinction "Holocene extinction") or the _sixth mass extinction_. It was estimated in 2007 that up to 30% of all species could be extinct by 2050.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-:03-4)[Habitat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat_destruction "Habitat destruction") destruction—particularly for agriculture—is a primary driver of this decline. [Climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change "Climate change") is also a major contributor, affecting entire biomes.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-:12-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-:32-6) This anthropogenic extinction may have begun during the late [Pleistocene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pleistocene "Pleistocene"), as some studies suggest that the [megafaunal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megafauna "Megafauna") extinction that took place around the end of the last ice age partly resulted from overhunting.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Brook_&_Bowman_20042-7)

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Biodiversity_by_Dano.jpg/250px-Biodiversity_by_Dano.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Biodiversity_by_Dano.jpg)

Shown in a museum, various models of species across various taxa and orders visualize the variety of life on earth.

Biologists commonly define _biodiversity_ as the "totality of [genes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene "Gene"), [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species "Species"), and [ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystems "Ecosystems") in a region".[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Davis2-9) This definition helps unify several dimensions of biological variety:

*   [Taxonomic diversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxonomic_diversity "Taxonomic diversity") (typically measured at the species level)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Sahney,_S._20102-10)
*   [Ecological diversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecological_diversity "Ecological diversity") (often interpreted through [ecosystem diversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem_diversity "Ecosystem diversity"))[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Sahney,_S._20102-10)
*   Morphological diversity (which stems from [genetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_diversity "Genetic diversity") and [molecular diversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_biology "Molecular biology")[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-11))
*   [Functional diversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_group_(ecology) "Functional group (ecology)") (which assesses the number of functionally disparate species within a population—e.g. different feeding mechanism, different motility, predator vs prey, etc.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-12))

_Biodiversity_ as a term is often used in a broader replacement for more specific terms like [species diversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species_diversity "Species diversity") and [species richness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species_richness "Species richness").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-13) However, the definition of biodiversity remains fluid and continues to evolve. For instance, the [Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_and_Agriculture_Organization "Food and Agriculture Organization") (FAO) defined biodiversity in 2019 as "the variability that exists among living organisms (both within and between species) and the ecosystems of which they are part." The [World Health Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Health_Organization "World Health Organization") updated their website's definition of biodiversity to be the "variability among living organisms from all sources."[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-14) These definitions reflect current understanding while emphasizing the breadth of the concept.

According to estimates by Mora et al. (2011), there are approximately 8.7 million terrestrial species and 2.2 million oceanic species. The authors note that these estimates are strongest for eukaryotic organisms and likely represent the lower bound of prokaryote diversity.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-mora2011-15) Other estimates include:

*   220,000 [vascular plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vascular_plants "Vascular plants"), estimated using the species-area relation method[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-16)
*   0.7–1 million marine species[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-17)
*   10–30 million [insects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insect "Insect");[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-18) (of some 0.9 million we know today)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Le_Monde_newspaper_article-19)
*   5–10 million [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria "Bacteria");[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-20)
*   1.5-3 million [fungi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungi "Fungi"), estimates based on data from the tropics, long-term non-tropical sites and molecular studies that have revealed [cryptic speciation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptic_species "Cryptic species").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-21) Some 0.075 million species of fungi had been documented by 2001;[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Hawksworth-22)
*   1 million [mites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mite "Mite")[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-23)
*   The number of [microbial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbial "Microbial") species is not reliably known, but the [Global Ocean Sampling Expedition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Ocean_Sampling_Expedition "Global Ocean Sampling Expedition") dramatically increased the estimates of genetic diversity by identifying an enormous number of new genes from near-surface [plankton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plankton "Plankton") samples at various marine locations, initially over the 2004–2006 period.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-24) The findings may eventually cause a significant change in the way science defines [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species "Species") and other taxonomic categories.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-25)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-26)

Since the rate of extinction has increased, many extant species may become extinct before they are described.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-27) Not surprisingly, in the [Animalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal "Animal") the most studied groups are [birds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birds "Birds") and [mammals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mammals "Mammals"), whereas [fishes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fishes "Fishes") and [arthropods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthropods "Arthropods") are the least studied animal groups.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-28)

Current biodiversity loss
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Current biodiversity loss")]

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a4/1970-_Decline_in_species_populations_-_Living_Planet_Index.svg/250px-1970-_Decline_in_species_populations_-_Living_Planet_Index.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:1970-_Decline_in_species_populations_-_Living_Planet_Index.svg)

The World Wildlife Fund's Living Planet Report 2024 found that wildlife populations declined by an average 73% since 1970.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-LivingPlanetIndex_2018-29)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-WEforum_20221017-30)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-LPI_by_Region_1970-31)

During the last century, decreases in biodiversity have been increasingly observed. It was estimated in 2007 that up to 30% of all species will be extinct by 2050.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-:0-32) Of these, about one eighth of known plant species are threatened with [extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction "Extinction").[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Reid_Reversing_loss_of_Biodiversity-33) Estimates reach as high as 140,000 species per year (based on [Species-area theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species-area_curve "Species-area curve")).[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-34) This figure indicates [unsustainable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sustainability "Sustainability") ecological practices, because few species emerge each year.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-35) The rate of species loss is greater now than at any time in human history, with extinctions occurring at rates hundreds of times higher than [background extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Background_extinction "Background extinction") rates.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Reid_Reversing_loss_of_Biodiversity-33)[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-36)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Dasgupta-37) and expected to still grow in the upcoming years.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Dasgupta-37)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-38)[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-39) As of 2012, some studies suggest that 25% of all mammal species could be extinct in 20 years.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-40)

In absolute terms, the planet has lost 58% of its biodiversity since 1970 according to a 2016 study by the World Wildlife Fund.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-41) The Living Planet Report 2014 claims that "the number of mammals, birds, reptiles, amphibians, and fish across the globe is, on average, about half the size it was 40 years ago". Of that number, 39% accounts for the terrestrial wildlife gone, 39% for the marine wildlife gone and 76% for the freshwater wildlife gone. Biodiversity took the biggest hit in [Latin America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_America "Latin America"), plummeting 83 percent. High-income countries showed a 10% increase in biodiversity, which was canceled out by a loss in low-income countries. This is despite the fact that high-income countries use five times the ecological resources of low-income countries, which was explained as a result of a process whereby wealthy nations are outsourcing [resource depletion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resource_depletion "Resource depletion") to poorer nations, which are suffering the greatest ecosystem losses.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-LivingPlanetReport2014-42)

A 2017 study published in _[PLOS One](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PLOS\_One "PLOS One")_ found that the biomass of insect life in Germany had declined by three-quarters in the last 25 years.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-43) Dave Goulson of [Sussex University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sussex_University "Sussex University") stated that their study suggested that humans "appear to be making vast tracts of land inhospitable to most forms of life, and are currently on course for ecological Armageddon. If we lose the insects then everything is going to collapse."[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-44)

In 2020 the [World Wildlife Foundation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Wildlife_Foundation "World Wildlife Foundation") published a report saying that "biodiversity is being destroyed at a rate unprecedented in human history". The report claims that 68% of the population of the examined species were destroyed in the years 1970 – 2016.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-45)

Of 70,000 monitored species, around 48% are experiencing population declines from human activity (in 2023), whereas only 3% have increasing populations.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-46)[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-47)[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-48)

Summary of major biodiversity-related environmental-change categories expressed as a percentage of human-driven change (in red) relative to baseline (blue)

Rates of [decline in biodiversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity_loss "Biodiversity loss") in the current [sixth mass extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sixth_mass_extinction "Sixth mass extinction") match or exceed rates of loss in the five previous [mass extinction events](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_event "Extinction event") in the [fossil record](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil_record "Fossil record").[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-58) Biodiversity loss is in fact "one of the most critical manifestations of the [Anthropocene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthropocene "Anthropocene")" (since around the 1950s); the continued decline of biodiversity constitutes "an unprecedented threat" to the continued existence of human civilization.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Dirzo-2022-59) The reduction is caused primarily by [human impacts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment "Human impact on the environment"), particularly [habitat destruction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat_destruction "Habitat destruction").

Since the [Stone Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stone_Age "Stone Age"), species loss has accelerated above the average basal rate, driven by human activity. Estimates of species losses are at a rate 100–10,000 times as fast as is typical in the fossil record.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Hassan2005-60)

Loss of biodiversity results in the loss of [natural capital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_capital "Natural capital") that supplies [ecosystem goods and services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem_services "Ecosystem services"). Species today are being wiped out at a rate 100 to 1,000 times higher than baseline, and the rate of extinctions is increasing. This process destroys the resilience and adaptability of life on Earth.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-61)

In 2006, many species were formally classified as [rare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rare_species "Rare species") or [endangered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endangered_species "Endangered species") or [threatened](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Threatened_species "Threatened species"); moreover, scientists have estimated that millions more species are at risk which have not been formally recognized. About 40 percent of the 40,177 species assessed using the [IUCN Red List](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUCN_Red_List "IUCN Red List") criteria are now listed as threatened with [extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction "Extinction")—a total of 16,119.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-62) As of late 2022 9251 species were considered part of the IUCN's [critically endangered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critically_Endangered "Critically Endangered").[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-63)

Numerous scientists and the [IPBES](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IPBES "IPBES")_[Global Assessment Report on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global\_Assessment\_Report\_on\_Biodiversity\_and\_Ecosystem\_Services "Global Assessment Report on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services")_ assert that [human population growth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_population_growth "Human population growth") and [overconsumption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overconsumption "Overconsumption") are the primary factors in this decline.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-64)[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-65)[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-66)[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-67)[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-68) However, other scientists have criticized this finding and say that loss of habitat caused by "the growth of commodities for export" is the main driver.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-69) A 2025 study found that human activities are responsible for biodiversity loss across all species and ecosystems.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-70)

Some studies have however pointed out that habitat destruction for the expansion of agriculture and the [overexploitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overexploitation "Overexploitation") of wildlife are the more significant drivers of contemporary biodiversity loss, not [climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change "Climate change").[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-:1-71)[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-:3-72)

Biodiversity is not evenly distributed, rather it varies greatly across the globe as well as within regions and seasons. Among other factors, the diversity of all living things ([biota](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biota_(ecology) "Biota (ecology)")) depends on [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature"), [precipitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precipitation "Precipitation"), [altitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altitude "Altitude"), [soils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil "Soil"), [geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography "Geography") and the interactions between other species.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-73) The study of the spatial distribution of [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism "Organism"), species and [ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem "Ecosystem"), is the science of [biogeography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biogeography "Biogeography").[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-MorandKrasnov2010-74)[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-the-functional-role-of-producer-diversity-75)

Diversity consistently measures higher in the [tropics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropics "Tropics") and in other localized regions such as the [Cape Floristic Region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cape_Floristic_Region "Cape Floristic Region") and lower in polar regions generally. [Rain forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rain_forests "Rain forests") that have had wet climates for a long time, such as [Yasuní National Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yasuni_National_Park "Yasuni National Park") in [Ecuador](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecuador "Ecuador"), have particularly high biodiversity.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-dotearth-76)[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-journal.pone.0008767-77)

There is local biodiversity, which directly impacts daily life, affecting the availability of fresh water, food choices, and fuel sources for humans. Regional biodiversity includes habitats and ecosystems that synergizes and either overlaps or differs on a regional scale. National biodiversity within a country determines the ability for a country to thrive according to its habitats and ecosystems on a national scale. Also, within a country, [endangered species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endangered_species "Endangered species") are initially supported on a national level then internationally. [Ecotourism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecotourism "Ecotourism") may be utilized to support the economy and encourages tourists to continue to visit and support species and ecosystems they visit, while they enjoy the available amenities provided. International biodiversity impacts global livelihood, food systems, and health. Problematic pollution, over consumption, and climate change can devastate international biodiversity. Nature-based solutions are a critical tool for a global resolution. Many species are in danger of becoming extinct and need world leaders to be proactive with the [Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kunming-Montreal_Global_Biodiversity_Framework "Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework").

Terrestrial biodiversity is thought to be up to 25 times greater than ocean biodiversity.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-78) Forests harbour most of Earth's terrestrial biodiversity. The conservation of the world's biodiversity is thus utterly dependent on the way in which we interact with and use the world's forests.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-FAO_&_UNEP-2020-79) A new method used in 2011, put the total number of species on Earth at 8.7 million, of which 2.1 million were estimated to live in the ocean.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Moraplos-80) However, this estimate seems to under-represent the diversity of microorganisms.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Microorganisms_Editorial_Office-2019-81) Forests provide habitats for 80 percent of amphibian [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species "Species"), 75 percent of bird species and 68 percent of mammal species. About 60 percent of all vascular plants are found in tropical forests. Mangroves provide breeding grounds and nurseries for numerous species of fish and shellfish and help trap sediments that might otherwise adversely affect seagrass beds and coral reefs, which are habitats for many more marine species.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-FAO_&_UNEP-2020-79) Forests span around 4 billion acres (nearly a third of the Earth's land mass) and are home to approximately 80% of the world's biodiversity. About 1 billion hectares are covered by primary forests. Over 700 million hectares of the world's woods are officially protected.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-82)[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-83)

The biodiversity of forests varies considerably according to factors such as forest type, geography, climate and soils – in addition to human use.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-FAO_&_UNEP-2020-79) Most forest habitats in temperate regions support relatively few animal and plant species and species that tend to have large geographical distributions, while the montane forests of Africa, South America and Southeast Asia and lowland forests of Australia, coastal Brazil, the Caribbean islands, Central America and insular Southeast Asia have many species with small geographical distributions.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-FAO_&_UNEP-2020-79) Areas with dense human populations and intense agricultural land use, such as [Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europe "Europe"), parts of Bangladesh, China, India and North America, are less intact in terms of their biodiversity. Northern Africa, southern Australia, coastal Brazil, Madagascar and South Africa, are also identified as areas with striking losses in biodiversity intactness.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-FAO_&_UNEP-2020-79) European forests in EU and non-EU nations comprise more than 30% of Europe's land mass (around 227 million hectares), representing an almost 10% growth since 1990.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-84)[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-85)

### Latitudinal gradients

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Latitudinal gradients")]

Generally, there is an increase in biodiversity from the [poles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographical_pole "Geographical pole") to the [tropics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropics "Tropics"). Thus localities at lower [latitudes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitudes "Latitudes") have more species than localities at higher [latitudes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude "Latitude"). This is often referred to as the latitudinal gradient in species diversity. Several ecological factors may contribute to the gradient, but the ultimate factor behind many of them is the greater mean temperature at the equator compared to that at the poles.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-MoraRobertson2003-86)

Even though terrestrial biodiversity declines from the equator to the poles,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-auto2-3) some studies claim that this characteristic is unverified in [aquatic ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aquatic_ecosystems "Aquatic ecosystems"), especially in [marine ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_ecosystems "Marine ecosystems").[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-87) The latitudinal distribution of parasites does not appear to follow this rule.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-MorandKrasnov2010-74) Also, in terrestrial ecosystems the soil bacterial diversity has been shown to be highest in temperate climatic zones,[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-88) and has been attributed to carbon inputs and habitat connectivity.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-89)

In 2016, an alternative hypothesis ("the [fractal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal "Fractal") biodiversity") was proposed to explain the biodiversity latitudinal gradient.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-90) In this study, the [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species "Species") pool size and the fractal nature of ecosystems were combined to clarify some general patterns of this gradient. This hypothesis considers [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature"), [moisture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moisture "Moisture"), and [net primary production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_primary_production "Net primary production") (NPP) as the main variables of an ecosystem niche and as the axis of the ecological [hypervolume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypervolume "Hypervolume"). In this way, it is possible to build fractal hyper volumes, whose [fractal dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension "Fractal dimension") rises to three moving towards the [equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator "Equator").[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-91)

### Biodiversity Hotspots

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Biodiversity Hotspots")]

A [biodiversity hotspot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity_hotspot "Biodiversity hotspot") is a region with a high level of [endemic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endemism "Endemism") species that have experienced great [habitat loss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat_destruction "Habitat destruction").[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-92) The term hotspot was introduced in 1988 by [Norman Myers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norman_Myers "Norman Myers").[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-93)[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-94)[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-95)[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-McKee2004-96) While hotspots are spread all over the world, the majority are forest areas and most are located in the [tropics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropics "Tropics").[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-97)

[Brazil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brazil "Brazil")'s [Atlantic Forest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_Forest "Atlantic Forest") is considered one such hotspot, containing roughly 20,000 plant species, 1,350 vertebrates and millions of insects, about half of which occur nowhere else.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-98)[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-MyersMittermeierMittermeier2000-99) The island of [Madagascar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madagascar "Madagascar") and [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India") are also particularly notable. [Colombia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colombia "Colombia") is characterized by high biodiversity, with the highest rate of species by area unit worldwide and it has the largest number of endemics (species that are not found naturally anywhere else) of any country. About 10% of the species of the Earth can be found in Colombia, including over 1,900 species of bird, more than in Europe and North America combined, Colombia has 10% of the world's mammals species, 14% of the amphibian species and 18% of the bird species of the world.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-100)[Madagascar dry deciduous forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madagascar_dry_deciduous_forests "Madagascar dry deciduous forests") and lowland rainforests possess a high ratio of [endemism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endemism "Endemism").[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-101)[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Harrison-2013-102) Since the island separated from mainland [Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa "Africa") 66 million years ago, many species and ecosystems have evolved independently.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-103)[Indonesia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indonesia "Indonesia")'s 17,000 islands cover 735,355 square miles (1,904,560 km 2) and contain 10% of the world's [flowering plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flowering_plant "Flowering plant"), 12% of mammals and 17% of [reptiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reptile "Reptile"), [amphibians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amphibian "Amphibian") and [birds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bird "Bird")—along with nearly 240 million people.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-104) Many regions of high biodiversity and/or endemism arise from specialized [habitats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat "Habitat") which require unusual adaptations, for example, [alpine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpine_climate "Alpine climate") environments in high [mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mountain "Mountain"), or [Northern European](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Europe "Northern Europe") peat [bogs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bog "Bog").[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Harrison-2013-102)

Accurately measuring differences in biodiversity can be difficult. [Selection bias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Selection_bias "Selection bias") amongst researchers may contribute to biased empirical research for modern estimates of biodiversity. In 1768, Rev. [Gilbert White](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gilbert_White "Gilbert White") succinctly observed of his [Selborne, Hampshire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Selborne "Selborne")_"all nature is so full, that that district produces the most variety which is the most examined."_[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-White1887-105)

Evolution over geologic timeframes
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Evolution over geologic timeframes")]

Biodiversity is the result of 3.5 billion years of [evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution "Evolution").[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-106) The [origin of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_of_life "Origin of life") has not been established by science, however, some evidence suggests that life may already have been well-established only a few hundred million years after the [formation of the Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_the_Earth "Age of the Earth"). Until approximately 2.5 billion years ago, all life consisted of [microorganisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism "Microorganism") – [archaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaea "Archaea"), [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria "Bacteria"), and [single-celled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicellular_organism "Unicellular organism")[protozoans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protozoan "Protozoan") and [protists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protist "Protist").[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Microorganisms_Editorial_Office-2019-81)

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/Phanerozoic_Biodiversity.png/500px-Phanerozoic_Biodiversity.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Phanerozoic_Biodiversity.png)

Apparent marine fossil diversity during the Phanerozoic[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Rosing2010-107)

Biodiversity grew fast during the [Phanerozoic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phanerozoic "Phanerozoic") (the last 540 million years), especially during the so-called [Cambrian explosion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambrian_explosion "Cambrian explosion")—a period during which nearly every [phylum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phylum_(biology) "Phylum (biology)") of [multicellular organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multicellular_organism "Multicellular organism") first appeared.[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Alroy-2001-108) However, recent studies suggest that this diversification had started earlier, at least in the [Ediacaran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ediacaran "Ediacaran"), and that it continued in the [Ordovician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordovician "Ordovician").[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Servais_et_al._2023-109) Over the next 400 million years or so, [invertebrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invertebrate "Invertebrate") diversity showed little overall trend and [vertebrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertebrate "Vertebrate") diversity shows an overall exponential trend.[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Sahney,_S._2010-110) This dramatic rise in diversity was marked by periodic, massive losses of diversity classified as [mass extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_extinction "Mass extinction") events.[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Sahney,_S._2010-110) A significant loss occurred in anamniotic limbed vertebrates when rainforests collapsed in the [Carboniferous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboniferous "Carboniferous"),[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-SahneyBentonFerry2010RainforestCollapse-111) but [amniotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amniote "Amniote") seem to have been little affected by this event; their diversification slowed down later, around the [Asselian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asselian "Asselian")/[Sakmarian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sakmarian "Sakmarian") boundary, in the early [Cisuralian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cisuralian "Cisuralian") (Early [Permian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permian "Permian")), about 293 Ma ago.[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Didier_&_Laurin_2024-112) The worst was the [Permian-Triassic extinction event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permian-Triassic_extinction_event "Permian-Triassic extinction event"), 251 million years ago.[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Viglietti_et_al._2021-113)[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Kammerer_et_al._2023-114) Vertebrates took 30 million years to recover from this event.[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-SahneyBenton2008RecoveryFromProfoundExtinction-115)

The most recent major [mass extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_extinction "Mass extinction") event, the [Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cretaceous%E2%80%93Paleogene_extinction_event "Cretaceous–Paleogene extinction event"), occurred 66 million years ago. This period has attracted more attention than others because it resulted in the extinction of the non-avian[dinosaurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dinosaur "Dinosaur"), which were represented by many lineages at the end of the [Maastrichtian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maastrichtian "Maastrichtian"), just before that extinction event. However, many other taxa were affected by this crisis, which affected even marine taxa, such as [ammonites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonites "Ammonites"), which also became extinct around that time.[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Machalski_2005-116)

The biodiversity of the past is called Paleobiodiversity. The [fossil record](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil_record "Fossil record") suggests that the last few million years featured the greatest biodiversity in [history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Earth "History of Earth").[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Sahney,_S._2010-110) However, not all scientists support this view, since there is uncertainty as to how strongly the fossil record is biased by the greater availability and preservation of recent [geologic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geology "Geology") sections.[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-117) Some scientists believe that corrected for sampling artifacts, modern biodiversity may not be much different from biodiversity 300 million years ago,[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Alroy-2001-108) whereas others consider the fossil record reasonably reflective of the diversification of life.[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Marjanovi%C4%87_&_Lauarin_2008-118)[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Sahney,_S._2010-110) Estimates of the present global macroscopic species diversity vary from 2 million to 100 million, with a best estimate of somewhere near 9 million,[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Moraplos-80) the vast majority [arthropods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthropod "Arthropod").[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-heywood-119) Diversity appears to increase continually in the absence of natural selection.[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-120)

The existence of a _global [carrying capacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carrying\_capacity "Carrying capacity")_, limiting the amount of life that can live at once, is debated, as is the question of whether such a limit would also cap the number of species. While records of life in the sea show a [logistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logistic_function "Logistic function") pattern of growth, life on land (insects, plants and tetrapods) shows an [exponential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_growth "Exponential growth") rise in diversity.[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Sahney,_S._2010-110) As one author states, "Tetrapods have not yet invaded 64 percent of potentially habitable modes and it could be that without human influence the ecological and [taxonomic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology) "Taxonomy (biology)") diversity of tetrapods would continue to increase exponentially until most or all of the available eco-space is filled."[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Sahney,_S._2010-110)

It also appears that the diversity continues to increase over time, especially after mass extinctions.[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-121)

On the other hand, changes through the [Phanerozoic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phanerozoic "Phanerozoic") correlate much better with the [hyperbolic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_growth "Hyperbolic growth") model (widely used in [population biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population_biology "Population biology"), [demography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demography "Demography") and [macrosociology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macrosociology "Macrosociology"), as well as [fossil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil "Fossil") biodiversity) than with exponential and logistic models. The latter models imply that changes in diversity are guided by a first-order [positive feedback](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive_feedback "Positive feedback") (more ancestors, more descendants) and/or a [negative feedback](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negative_feedback "Negative feedback") arising from resource limitation. Hyperbolic model implies a second-order positive feedback.[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Hautmann_et_al._2015-122) Differences in the strength of the second-order feedback due to different intensities of interspecific competition might explain the faster rediversification of [ammonoids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonoids "Ammonoids") in comparison to [bivalves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bivalves "Bivalves") after the [end-Permian extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/End-Permian_extinction "End-Permian extinction").[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Hautmann_et_al._2015-122) The hyperbolic pattern of the [world population](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_population "World population") growth arises from a second-order positive feedback between the population size and the rate of technological growth.[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-pmid18677962-123) The hyperbolic character of biodiversity growth can be similarly accounted for by a feedback between diversity and community structure complexity.[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-pmid18677962-123)[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Markov-2007-124) The similarity between the curves of biodiversity and human population probably comes from the fact that both are derived from the interference of the hyperbolic trend with cyclical and [stochastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stochastic "Stochastic") dynamics.[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-pmid18677962-123)[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Markov-2007-124)

Most biologists agree however that the period since human emergence is part of a new mass extinction, named the [Holocene extinction event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holocene_extinction_event "Holocene extinction event"), caused primarily by the impact humans are having on the environment.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-125) It has been argued that the present rate of extinction is sufficient to eliminate most species on the planet Earth within 100 years.[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Wilson2002-126)

New species are regularly discovered (on average between 5–10,000 new species each year, most of them [insects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insect "Insect")) and many, though discovered, are not yet classified (estimates are that nearly 90% of all [arthropods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthropod "Arthropod") are not yet classified).[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-heywood-119) Most of the terrestrial diversity is found in [tropical forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_forest "Tropical forest") and in general, the land has more species than the ocean; some 8.7 million species may exist on Earth, of which some 2.1 million live in the ocean.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Moraplos-80)

### Species diversity in geologic time frames

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Species diversity in geologic time frames")]

It is estimated that 5 to 50 billion species have existed on the planet.[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-127) Assuming that there may be a maximum of about 50 million species currently alive,[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-128) it stands to reason that greater than 99% of the planet's species went extinct prior to the evolution of humans.[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-129) Estimates on the number of Earth's current [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species "Species") range from 10 million to 14 million, of which about 1.2 million have been documented and over 86% have not yet been described.[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-MillerSpoolman2012-130) However, a May 2016 scientific report estimates that 1 trillion species are currently on Earth, with only one-thousandth of one percent described.[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-NSF-2016002-131) The total amount of related [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA "DNA")[base pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_pair "Base pair") on Earth is estimated at 5.0 x 10 37 and weighs 50 billion [tonnes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tonne "Tonne"). In comparison, the total [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomass_(ecology) "Biomass (ecology)") of the [biosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere "Biosphere") has been estimated to be as much as four trillion tons of [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon").[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-AGCI-2014-132) In July 2016, scientists reported identifying a set of 355 [genes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene "Gene") from the [last universal common ancestor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Last_universal_common_ancestor "Last universal common ancestor") (LUCA) of all [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism "Organism") living on Earth.[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-NYT-20160725-133)

The [age of Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_Earth "Age of Earth") is about 4.54 billion years.[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-USGS1997-134)[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-135)[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-136) The earliest undisputed evidence of [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life "Life") dates at least from 3.7 billion years ago, during the [Eoarchean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eoarchean "Eoarchean") era after a geological [crust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crust_(geology) "Crust (geology)") started to solidify following the earlier molten [Hadean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadean "Hadean") eon.[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Origin1-137)[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Origin2-138)[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-RavenJohnson2002-139) There are [microbial mat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbial_mat "Microbial mat")[fossils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossils "Fossils") found in 3.48 billion-year-old [sandstone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sandstone "Sandstone") discovered in [Western Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Australia "Western Australia"). Other early physical evidence of a [biogenic substance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biogenic_substance "Biogenic substance") is [graphite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphite "Graphite") in 3.7 billion-year-old [meta-sedimentary rocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metasediment "Metasediment") discovered in [Western Greenland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Greenland "Western Greenland").[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-NG-201312083-140)[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-:02-141) More recently, in 2015, "remains of [biotic life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biotic_material "Biotic material")" were found in 4.1 billion-year-old rocks in Western [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia "Australia"). According to one of the researchers, "If life arose relatively quickly on Earth...then it could be common in the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe")."[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-apnews.excite.com_2015-142)

Role and benefits of biodiversity
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Role and benefits of biodiversity")]

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Field_Hamois_Belgium_Luc_Viatour.jpg/250px-Field_Hamois_Belgium_Luc_Viatour.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Field_Hamois_Belgium_Luc_Viatour.jpg)

Summer field in [Belgium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belgium "Belgium") (Hamois). The blue flowers are _[Centaurea cyanus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centaurea\_cyanus "Centaurea cyanus")_ and the red are _[Papaver rhoeas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papaver\_rhoeas "Papaver rhoeas")_.

There have been many claims about biodiversity's effect on the [ecosystem services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem_service "Ecosystem service"), especially _provisioning_ and _regulating services_.[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-diversity-loss-and-its-impact-143) Some of those claims have been validated, some are incorrect and some lack enough evidence to draw definitive conclusions.[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-diversity-loss-and-its-impact-143)

Ecosystem services have been grouped in three types:[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-diversity-loss-and-its-impact-143)

1.   Provisioning services which involve the production of renewable resources (e.g.: food, wood, fresh water)
2.   Regulating services which are those that lessen environmental change (e.g.: climate regulation, pest/disease control)
3.   Cultural services represent human value and enjoyment (e.g.: landscape aesthetics, cultural heritage, outdoor recreation and spiritual significance)[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-144)

Experiments with controlled environments have shown that humans cannot easily build ecosystems to support human needs;[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-145) for example [insect pollination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entomophily "Entomophily") cannot be mimicked, though there have been attempts to create artificial pollinators using [unmanned aerial vehicles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unmanned_aerial_vehicles "Unmanned aerial vehicles").[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-146) The economic activity of pollination alone represented between $2.1–14.6 billion in 2003.[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-147) Other sources have reported somewhat conflicting results and in 1997 [Robert Costanza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Costanza "Robert Costanza") and his colleagues reported the estimated global value of ecosystem services (not captured in traditional markets) at an average of $33 trillion annually.[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-nature.com-148)

#### Provisioning services

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Provisioning services")]

With regards to provisioning services, greater species diversity has the following benefits:

*   Greater species diversity of plants increases fodder yield (synthesis of 271 experimental studies).[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-the-functional-role-of-producer-diversity-75)
*   Greater species diversity of plants (i.e. diversity within a single species) increases overall [crop yield](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crop_yield "Crop yield") (synthesis of 575 experimental studies).[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-grain-yield-increase-in-cereal-variety-mixtures-149) Although another review of 100 experimental studies reported mixed evidence.[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-does-plant-diversity-benefit-agroecosystems-150)
*   Greater species diversity of trees increases overall [wood production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wood_production "Wood production") (synthesis of 53 experimental studies).[[151]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-tree-growth-in-monocultures-and-mixed-151) However, there is not enough data to draw a conclusion about the effect of tree trait diversity on wood production.[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-diversity-loss-and-its-impact-143)

#### Regulating services

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Regulating services")]

With regards to regulating services, greater species diversity has the following benefits:

Greater species diversity

*   of fish increases the stability of [fisheries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fisheries "Fisheries") yield (synthesis of 8 observational studies)[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-diversity-loss-and-its-impact-143)
*   of plants increases [carbon sequestration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_sequestration "Carbon sequestration"), but note that this finding only relates to actual uptake of carbon dioxide and not long-term storage; synthesis of 479 experimental studies)[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-the-functional-role-of-producer-diversity-75)
*   of plants increases [soil nutrient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil_nutrient "Soil nutrient")[remineralization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remineralization "Remineralization") (synthesis of 103 experimental studies), increases soil organic matter (synthesis of 85 experimental studies) and decreases disease prevalence on plants (synthesis of 107 experimental studies)[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-plant-diversity-enhances-provision-of-ecosystem-services-152)
*   of natural pest enemies decreases herbivorous pest populations data from two separate reviews; synthesis of 266 experimental and observational studies;[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-annual-review-ecology-evolution-systematics-vol-40-153) Synthesis of 18 observational studies.[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-functional-richness-and-ecosystem-services-bird-pred-154)[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-birds-as-predators-in-trop-agroforestry-155) Although another review of 38 experimental studies found mixed support for this claim, suggesting that in cases where mutual intraguild predation occurs, a single predatory species is often more effective[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-influence-of-intraguild-predation-156)

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Ueberladewagen_%28jha%29.jpg/250px-Ueberladewagen_%28jha%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ueberladewagen_(jha).jpg)

[Agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agriculture "Agriculture") production, pictured is a [tractor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tractor "Tractor") and a [chaser bin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaser_bin "Chaser bin")

Agricultural diversity can be divided into two categories: [intraspecific diversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_diversity "Genetic diversity"), which includes the genetic variation within a single species, like the potato (_[Solanum tuberosum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solanum\_tuberosum "Solanum tuberosum")_) that is composed of many different forms and types (e.g. in the U.S. they might compare russet potatoes with new potatoes or purple potatoes, all different, but all part of the same species, _S. tuberosum_). The other category of agricultural diversity is called [interspecific diversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species_diversity "Species diversity") and refers to the number and types of different species.

Agricultural diversity can also be divided by whether it is 'planned' diversity or 'associated' diversity. This is a functional classification that we impose and not an intrinsic feature of life or diversity. Planned diversity includes the crops which a farmer has encouraged, planted or raised (e.g. crops, covers, symbionts, and livestock, among others), which can be contrasted with the associated diversity that arrives among the crops, uninvited (e.g. herbivores, weed species and pathogens, among others).[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-ecology-of-agroecosystems-157)

Associated biodiversity can be damaging or beneficial. The beneficial associated biodiversity include for instance wild pollinators such as wild bees and [syrphid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syrphid "Syrphid") flies that pollinate crops[[158]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-IPBES-158) and natural enemies and antagonists to pests and pathogens. Beneficial associated biodiversity occurs abundantly in crop fields and provide multiple [ecosystem services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem_services "Ecosystem services") such as pest control, [nutrient cycling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrient_cycling "Nutrient cycling") and pollination that support crop production.[[159]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-159)

Although about 80 percent of humans' food supply comes from just 20 kinds of plants,[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-160) humans use at least 40,000 species.[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-161) Earth's surviving biodiversity provides resources for increasing the range of food and other products suitable for human use, although the present extinction rate shrinks that potential.[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Wilson2002-126)

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Forest_fruits_from_Barro_Colorado.png/250px-Forest_fruits_from_Barro_Colorado.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Forest_fruits_from_Barro_Colorado.png)

The diverse forest canopy on [Barro Colorado Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barro_Colorado_Island "Barro Colorado Island"), Panama, yielded this display of different fruit

Biodiversity's relevance to human health is becoming an international political issue, as scientific evidence builds on the global health implications of biodiversity loss.[[162]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-162)[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-biodiversity1-163)[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Corval%C3%A1nHales2005-164) This issue is closely linked with the issue of [climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change "Climate change"),[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-165) as many of the anticipated [health risks of climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health_risks_of_climate_change "Health risks of climate change") are associated with changes in biodiversity (e.g. changes in populations and distribution of disease vectors, scarcity of fresh water, impacts on agricultural biodiversity and food resources etc.). This is because the species most likely to disappear are those that buffer against infectious disease transmission, while surviving species tend to be the ones that increase disease transmission, such as that of West Nile Virus, [Lyme disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lyme_disease "Lyme disease") and Hantavirus, according to a study done co-authored by Felicia Keesing, an ecologist at Bard College and Drew Harvell, associate director for Environment of the [Atkinson Center for a Sustainable Future](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atkinson_Center_for_a_Sustainable_Future "Atkinson Center for a Sustainable Future") (ACSF) at [Cornell University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornell_University "Cornell University").[[166]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-166)

Some of the health issues influenced by biodiversity include dietary health and nutrition security, infectious disease, medical science and medicinal resources, social and psychological health.[[167]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-167) Biodiversity is also known to have an important role in reducing disaster risk, including rising sea levels. For example, wetland ecosystems along coastal communities serve as excellent water filtration systems, storage, and ultimately create a buffer region between the ocean and mainland neighborhoods in order to prevent water reaching these communities under climate change pressures or storm storages. Other examples of diverse species or organisms are present around the world, offering their resourceful utilities to provide protection of human survival.[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-168)

Biodiversity provides critical support for drug discovery and the availability of medicinal resources.[[169]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-169)[[170]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-170) A significant proportion of drugs are derived, directly or indirectly, from biological sources: at least 50% of the pharmaceutical compounds on the US market are derived from plants, animals and [microorganisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism "Microorganism"), while about 80% of the world population depends on medicines from nature (used in either modern or traditional medical practice) for primary healthcare.[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-biodiversity1-163) Only a tiny fraction of wild species has been investigated for medical potential.

[Marine ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_ecosystem "Marine ecosystem") are particularly important, especially their chemical and physical properties that have paved the way for numerous pharmaceutical achievements; the immense diversity of marine organisms have led to scientific discoveries including medical treatments to cancer, viral bacteria, AIDS, etc.[[171]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-171) This process of [bioprospecting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bioprospecting "Bioprospecting") can increase biodiversity loss, as well as violating the laws of the communities and states from which the resources are taken.[[172]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-172)[[173]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-173)[[174]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-174)

### Business and industry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Business and industry")]

According to the [Boston Consulting Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_Consulting_Group "Boston Consulting Group"), in 2021, the economic value that biodiversity has on society comes down to four definable terms: regulation, culture, habitat, and provisioning. To sum these up in a relatively short manner, biodiversity helps maintain habitat and animal functions that provide considerable amounts of resources that benefit the economy.[[175]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-:5-175)

Biodiversity's economic resources are worth at around $150 trillion annually which is roughly twice the world's GDP. The loss of biodiversity is actually harming the GDP of the world by costing an estimated $5 trillion annually.[[175]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-:5-175)

Business supply chains rely heavily on ecosystems remaining relatively maintained and nurtured. A disruption to these supply chains would negatively impact many businesses that would end up costing them more than what they are gaining.[[176]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-176)

### Cultural and aesthetic value

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Cultural and aesthetic value")]

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Eaglecreek-28July2006.jpg/250px-Eaglecreek-28July2006.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Eaglecreek-28July2006.jpg)

[Eagle Creek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eagle_Creek_(Multnomah_County,_Oregon) "Eagle Creek (Multnomah County, Oregon)"), Oregon hiking

Philosophically it could be argued that biodiversity has intrinsic aesthetic and spiritual value to [mankind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human "Human")_in and of itself_. This idea can be used as a counterweight to the notion that [tropical forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_forest "Tropical forest") and other ecological realms are only worthy of conservation because of the services they provide.[[177]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-177)

Biodiversity also affords many non-material benefits including spiritual and aesthetic values, knowledge systems and education.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Hassan2005-60)

Measuring biodiversity
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Measuring biodiversity")]

A variety of objective means exist to empirically [measure biodiversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measurement_of_biodiversity "Measurement of biodiversity"). Each measure relates to a particular use of the data, and is likely to be associated with the variety of genes. Biodiversity is commonly measured in terms of taxonomic richness of a geographic area over a time interval. In order to calculate biodiversity, species evenness, species richness, and species diversity are to be obtained first. _Species evenness_ is the relative number of individuals of each species in a given area.[[178]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-178)_Species richness_[[179]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-179) is the number of species present in a given area. _Species diversity_[[180]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-180) is the relationship between species evenness and species richness. There are many ways to measure biodiversity within a given ecosystem. However, the two most popular are [Shannon-Weaver diversity index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shannon-Weaver_diversity_index "Shannon-Weaver diversity index"),[[181]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-181) commonly referred to as Shannon diversity index, and the other is [Simpsons diversity index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simpson_diversity_index "Simpson diversity index").[[182]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-182) Although many scientists prefer to use Shannon's diversity index simply because it takes into account species richness.[[183]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-183)

Less than 1% of all species that have been described have been studied beyond noting their existence.[[184]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-184) The vast majority of Earth's species are microbial. Contemporary biodiversity physics is "firmly fixated on the visible [macroscopic] world".[[185]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-185) For example, microbial life is [metabolically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolically "Metabolically") and environmentally more diverse than multicellular life (see e.g., [extremophile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extremophile "Extremophile")). "On the tree of life, based on analyses of small-subunit [ribosomal RNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ribosomal_RNA "Ribosomal RNA"), visible life consists of barely noticeable twigs. The inverse relationship of size and population recurs higher on the evolutionary ladder—to a first approximation, all multicellular species on Earth are insects".[[186]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-186)[Insect extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decline_in_insect_populations "Decline in insect populations") rates are high—supporting the Holocene extinction hypothesis.[[187]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-187)[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Modern_Insect-56)

Biodiversity changes (other than losses)
----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Biodiversity changes (other than losses)")]

### Natural seasonal variations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Natural seasonal variations")]

Biodiversity naturally varies due to seasonal shifts. Spring's arrival enhances biodiversity as numerous species breed and feed, while winter's onset temporarily reduces it as some insects perish and migrating animals leave. Additionally, the seasonal fluctuation in plant and invertebrate populations influences biodiversity.[[188]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-188)

### Introduced and invasive species

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Introduced and invasive species")]

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2b/Male_Silver_Pheasant.jpg/250px-Male_Silver_Pheasant.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Male_Silver_Pheasant.jpg)

Male _Lophura nycthemera_ ([silver pheasant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silver_pheasant "Silver pheasant")), a native of [East Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Asia "East Asia") that has been introduced into parts of [Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europe "Europe") for ornamental reasons

Barriers such as large [rivers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/River "River"), [seas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea "Sea"), [oceans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oceans "Oceans"), [mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mountains "Mountains") and [deserts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deserts "Deserts") encourage diversity by enabling independent evolution on either side of the barrier, via the process of [allopatric speciation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allopatric_speciation "Allopatric speciation"). The term [invasive species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invasive_species "Invasive species") is applied to species that breach the natural barriers that would normally keep them constrained. Without barriers, such species occupy new territory, often supplanting native species by occupying their niches, or by using resources that would normally sustain native species.

Species are increasingly being moved by humans (on purpose and accidentally). Some studies say that diverse ecosystems are more resilient and resist invasive plants and animals.[[189]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-189) Many studies cite effects of invasive species on natives,[[190]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-190) but not extinctions.

Invasive species seem to increase local ([alpha diversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_diversity "Alpha diversity")) diversity, which decreases turnover of diversity ([beta diversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_diversity "Beta diversity")). Overall [gamma diversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_diversity "Gamma diversity") may be lowered because species are going extinct because of other causes,[[191]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-191) but even some of the most insidious invaders (e.g.: Dutch elm disease, emerald ash borer, chestnut blight in North America) have not caused their host species to become extinct. [Extirpation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_extinction "Local extinction"), [population decline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population_decline "Population decline") and [homogenization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species_homogeneity "Species homogeneity") of regional biodiversity are much more common. Human activities have frequently been the cause of invasive species circumventing their barriers,[[192]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-192) by introducing them for food and other purposes. Human activities therefore allow species to migrate to new areas (and thus become invasive) occurred on time scales much shorter than historically have been required for a species to extend its range.

At present, several countries have already imported so many exotic species, particularly agricultural and ornamental plants, that their indigenous fauna/flora may be outnumbered. For example, the introduction of [kudzu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kudzu "Kudzu") from Southeast Asia to Canada and the United States has threatened biodiversity in certain areas.[[193]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-193) Another example are [pines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pine "Pine"), which have invaded forests, shrublands and grasslands in the southern hemisphere.[[194]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-194)

### Hybridization and genetic pollution

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Hybridization and genetic pollution")]

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Wheat_selection_k10183-1.jpg/250px-Wheat_selection_k10183-1.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Wheat_selection_k10183-1.jpg)

The Yecoro [wheat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wheat "Wheat") (right) [cultivar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultivar "Cultivar") is sensitive to salinity, plants resulting from a hybrid cross with cultivar W4910 (left) show greater tolerance to high salinity

Endemic species can be threatened with [extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction "Extinction")[[195]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-195) through the process of [genetic pollution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_pollution "Genetic pollution"), i.e. uncontrolled [hybridization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hybrid_(biology) "Hybrid (biology)"), [introgression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Introgression "Introgression") and genetic swamping. Genetic pollution leads to homogenization or replacement of local [genomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genomes "Genomes") as a result of either a numerical and/or [fitness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fitness_(biology) "Fitness (biology)") advantage of an introduced species.[[196]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-196)

Hybridization and introgression are side-effects of introduction and invasion. These phenomena can be especially detrimental to [rare species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rare_species "Rare species") that come into contact with more abundant ones. The abundant species can interbreed with the rare species, swamping its [gene pool](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene_pool "Gene pool"). This problem is not always apparent from [morphological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphology_(biology) "Morphology (biology)") (outward appearance) observations alone. Some degree of [gene flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene_flow "Gene flow") is normal adaptation and not all [gene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene "Gene") and [genotype](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genotype "Genotype") constellations can be preserved. However, hybridization with or without introgression may, nevertheless, threaten a rare species' existence.[[197]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-197)[[198]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-PottsBarbour2001-198)

[![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/MEAConservationStrategies.jpg/250px-MEAConservationStrategies.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:MEAConservationStrategies.jpg)

A schematic image illustrating the relationship between biodiversity, ecosystem services, human well-being and poverty.[[199]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-199) The illustration shows where conservation action, strategies, and plans can influence the drivers of the current biodiversity crisis at local, regional, to global scales.

[Conservation biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_biology "Conservation biology") matured in the mid-20th century as [ecologists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecologists "Ecologists"), [naturalists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naturalists "Naturalists") and other [scientists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientists "Scientists") began to research and address issues pertaining to global biodiversity declines.[[200]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Soule86-200)[[201]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Davis96-201)[[202]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Dyke2008-202)

The conservation ethic advocates management of [natural resources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_resource "Natural resource") for the purpose of sustaining biodiversity in [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species "Species"), [ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystems "Ecosystems"), the [evolutionary process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution "Evolution") and human culture and society.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-proceedings1-52)[[200]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Soule86-200)[[202]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Dyke2008-202)[[203]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Hunter1996-203)[[204]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-204)

Conservation biology is reforming around strategic plans to protect biodiversity.[[200]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Soule86-200)[[205]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Soul%C3%A91986-205)[[206]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-206)[[207]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Knozowski-2023-207) Preserving global biodiversity is a priority in strategic conservation plans that are designed to engage public policy and concerns affecting local, regional and global scales of communities, ecosystems and cultures.[[208]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-208) Action plans identify [ways](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecological_economics#Methodology "Ecological economics") of sustaining human well-being, employing [natural capital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_capital "Natural capital"), macroeconomic policies including economic incentives, and [ecosystem services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem_services "Ecosystem services").[[209]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-209)[[210]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-210)

In the [EU Directive 1999/22/EC](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=EU_Directive_1999/22/EC&action=edit&redlink=1 "EU Directive 1999/22/EC (page does not exist)") zoos are described as having a role in the preservation of the biodiversity of wildlife animals by conducting research or participation in [breeding programs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breeding_program "Breeding program").[[211]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-211)

### Protection and restoration techniques

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: Protection and restoration techniques")]

Removal of exotic species will allow the species that they have negatively impacted to recover their ecological niches. Exotic species that have become pests can be identified taxonomically (e.g.,with [Digital Automated Identification SYstem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_Automated_Identification_SYstem "Digital Automated Identification SYstem") (DAISY), using the [barcode of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consortium_for_the_Barcode_of_Life "Consortium for the Barcode of Life")).[[212]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-212)[[213]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-213) Removal is practical only given large groups of individuals due to the economic cost.

As sustainable populations of the remaining native species in an area become assured, "missing" species that are candidates for reintroduction can be identified using databases such as the _[Encyclopedia of Life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclopedia\_of\_Life#Resources\_and\_collaborations "Encyclopedia of Life")_ and the [Global Biodiversity Information Facility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Biodiversity_Information_Facility "Global Biodiversity Information Facility").

*   [Biodiversity banking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity_banking "Biodiversity banking") places a monetary value on biodiversity. One example is the Australian [Native Vegetation Management Framework](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Native_Vegetation_Management_Framework "Native Vegetation Management Framework").[[214]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-214)
*   [Gene banks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene_bank "Gene bank") are collections of specimens and genetic material. Some banks intend to reintroduce banked species to the ecosystem (e.g.,via tree nurseries).[[215]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-215)
*   Reduction and better targeting of pesticides allows more species to survive in agricultural and urbanized areas.
*   Location-specific approaches may be less useful for protecting migratory species. One approach is to create [wildlife corridors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wildlife_corridor "Wildlife corridor") that correspond to the animals' movements. National and other boundaries can complicate corridor creation.[[216]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-216)

[![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/Orang_Utan_%40_Sepilok.jpg/250px-Orang_Utan_%40_Sepilok.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Orang_Utan_@_Sepilok.jpg)

Mother and child at an orangutan rehab facility in Malaysia

Protected areas, including forest reserves and biosphere reserves, serve many functions including for affording protection to wild animals and their habitat.[[217]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-217) Protected areas have been set up all over the world with the specific aim of protecting and conserving plants and animals. Some scientists have called on the global community to designate as protected areas of 30 percent of the planet by 2030, and 50 percent by 2050, in order to mitigate biodiversity loss from anthropogenic causes.[[218]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-218)[[219]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-219) The target of protecting 30% of the area of the planet by the year 2030 ([30 by 30](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/30_by_30 "30 by 30")) was adopted by almost 200 countries in the [2022 United Nations Biodiversity Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_United_Nations_Biodiversity_Conference "2022 United Nations Biodiversity Conference"). At the moment of adoption (December 2022) 17% of land territory and 10% of ocean territory were protected.[[220]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Paddison-220) In a study published 4 September 2020 in [Science Advances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_Advances "Science Advances") researchers mapped out regions that can help meet critical conservation and climate goals.[[221]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-221)

Protected areas safeguard nature and cultural resources and contribute to livelihoods, particularly at local level. There are over 238 563 designated protected areas worldwide, equivalent to 14.9 percent of the earth's land surface, varying in their extension, level of protection, and type of management (IUCN, 2018).[[222]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-222)

The benefits of protected areas extend beyond their immediate environment and time. In addition to conserving nature, protected areas are crucial for securing the long-term delivery of ecosystem services. They provide numerous benefits including the conservation of [genetic resources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_resources "Genetic resources") for food and agriculture, the provision of medicine and health benefits, the provision of water, recreation and tourism, and for acting as a buffer against disaster. Increasingly, there is acknowledgement of the wider socioeconomic values of these natural ecosystems and of the ecosystem services they can provide.[[223]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-223)

### National parks and wildlife sanctuaries

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity&action=edit&section=27 "Edit section: National parks and wildlife sanctuaries")]

A [national park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_park "National park") is a large natural or near natural area set aside to protect large-scale ecological processes, which also provide a foundation for environmentally and culturally compatible, spiritual, scientific, educational, recreational and visitor opportunities. These areas are selected by governments or private organizations to protect natural biodiversity along with its underlying ecological structure and supporting environmental processes, and to promote education and recreation. The [International Union for Conservation of Nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Union_for_Conservation_of_Nature "International Union for Conservation of Nature") (IUCN), and its World Commission on Protected Areas (WCPA), has defined "National Park" as its Category II type of protected areas.[[224]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-224)

[Wildlife sanctuaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wildlife_sanctuary "Wildlife sanctuary") are areas of either shelter for animals who are unable to live in the wild on their own, or they are temporary rehabilitation centers for wildlife to improve in their overall health and wellbeing.[[225]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-225)

Both of these serve as places in which biodiversity can be preserved rather than harmed. According to an article published in the [National Park Service](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Park_Service "National Park Service") website, national parks aim their resources at maintaining animal and habitat integrity through conservation and preservation of their ecosystems. This along with educating the general public on wildlife functions, the aim for an increase in biodiversity is one of many goals trying to be focused on through national parks.[[226]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-226)

### Forest protected areas

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity&action=edit&section=28 "Edit section: Forest protected areas")]

[![Image 15](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Percentage_of_forest_in_legally_protected_areas%2C_2020.svg/250px-Percentage_of_forest_in_legally_protected_areas%2C_2020.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Percentage_of_forest_in_legally_protected_areas,_2020.svg)

Percentage of forest in legally protected areas (as of 2020).[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-FAO_&_UNEP-2020-79)

Forest protected areas are a subset of all protected areas in which a significant portion of the area is forest.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-FAO_&_UNEP-2020-79) This may be the whole or only a part of the protected area.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-FAO_&_UNEP-2020-79) Globally, 18 percent of the world's forest area, or more than 700 million hectares, fall within legally established protected areas such as national parks, conservation areas and game reserves.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-FAO_&_UNEP-2020-79)

There is an estimated 726 million ha of forest in protected areas worldwide. Of the six major world regions, South America has the highest share of forests in protected areas, 31 percent.[[227]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-227) The [forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forest "Forest") play a vital role in harboring more than 45,000 floral and 81,000 faunal species of which 5150 floral and 1837 faunal species are [endemic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endemism "Endemism").[[228]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-228) In addition, there are 60,065 different tree species in the world.[[229]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-229) Plant and animal species confined to a specific geographical area are called endemic species.[[230]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-230)

In [forest reserves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forest_reserves "Forest reserves"), rights to activities like hunting and grazing are sometimes given to communities living on the fringes of the forest, who sustain their livelihood partially or wholly from forest resources or products.

Approximately 50 million hectares (or 24%) of European forest land is protected for biodiversity and landscape protection. Forests allocated for soil, water, and other ecosystem services encompass around 72 million hectares (32% of European forest area).[[231]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-231)[[232]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-232)

### Transformative change

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity&action=edit&section=30 "Edit section: Transformative change")]

In 2019, a summary for policymakers of the largest, most comprehensive study to date of biodiversity and ecosystem services, the _[Global Assessment Report on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global\_Assessment\_Report\_on\_Biodiversity\_and\_Ecosystem\_Services "Global Assessment Report on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services")_, was published by the [Intergovernmental Science-Policy Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intergovernmental_Science-Policy_Platform_on_Biodiversity_and_Ecosystem_Services "Intergovernmental Science-Policy Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services") (IPBES). It stated that "the state of nature has deteriorated at an unprecedented and accelerating rate". To fix the problem, humanity will need a transformative change, including [sustainable agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sustainable_agriculture "Sustainable agriculture"), reductions in [consumption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consumption_(economics) "Consumption (economics)") and waste, fishing quotas and collaborative water management.[[233]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-233)[[234]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-234)

The concept of [nature-positive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature-positive "Nature-positive") is playing a role in mainstreaming the goals of the [Global Biodiversity Framework](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kunming-Montreal_Global_Biodiversity_Framework "Kunming-Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework") (GBF) for biodiversity.[[235]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-:2-235) The aim of mainstreaming is to embed biodiversity considerations into public and private practice to [conserve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_biology "Conservation biology") and [sustainably use](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sustainability "Sustainability") biodiversity on global and local levels.[[236]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-:4-236) The concept of nature-positive refers to the societal goal to halt and reverse biodiversity loss, measured from a baseline of 2020 levels, and to achieve full so-called "nature recovery" by 2050.[[237]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-237)

[Citizen science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citizen_science "Citizen science"), also known as public participation in scientific research, has been widely used in environmental sciences and is particularly popular in a biodiversity-related context. It has been used to enable scientists to involve the general public in biodiversity research, thereby enabling the scientists to collect data that they would otherwise not have been able to obtain.[[238]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-238)

Volunteer observers have made significant contributions to on-the-ground knowledge about biodiversity, and recent improvements in technology have helped increase the flow and quality of occurrences from citizen sources. A 2016 study published in Biological Conservation[[239]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-239) registers the massive contributions that citizen scientists already make to data mediated by the [Global Biodiversity Information Facility (GBIF)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Biodiversity_Information_Facility "Global Biodiversity Information Facility"). Despite some limitations of the dataset-level analysis, it is clear that nearly half of all occurrence records shared through the GBIF network come from datasets with significant volunteer contributions. Recording and sharing observations are enabled by several global-scale platforms, including [iNaturalist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/INaturalist "INaturalist") and [eBird](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EBird "EBird").[[240]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-240)[[241]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-241)

[![Image 16](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Hopetoun_falls.jpg/250px-Hopetoun_falls.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hopetoun_falls.jpg)

A great deal of work is occurring to preserve the natural characteristics of [Hopetoun Falls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hopetoun_Falls "Hopetoun Falls"), [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia "Australia") while continuing to allow visitor access.

*   United Nations [Convention on Biological Diversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convention_on_Biological_Diversity "Convention on Biological Diversity") (1992) and [Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartagena_Protocol_on_Biosafety "Cartagena Protocol on Biosafety");
*   UN BBNJ ([High Seas Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_Seas_Treaty "High Seas Treaty")) 2023 Intergovernmental conference on an international legally binding instrument under the [UNCLOS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Convention_on_the_Law_of_the_Sea "United Nations Convention on the Law of the Sea") on the conservation and sustainable use of marine biological diversity of areas beyond national jurisdiction (GA resolution 72/249)
*   Convention on International Trade in Endangered Species ([CITES](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CITES "CITES"));
*   [Ramsar Convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ramsar_Convention "Ramsar Convention") (Wetlands);
*   [Bonn Convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bonn_Convention "Bonn Convention") on Migratory Species;
*   [UNESCO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNESCO "UNESCO")[Convention concerning the Protection of the World's Cultural and Natural Heritage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Heritage_Site#Convention_and_background "World Heritage Site") (indirectly by protecting biodiversity habitats)
*   [UNESCO Global Geoparks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNESCO_Global_Geoparks "UNESCO Global Geoparks")
*   Regional Conventions such as the Apia Convention
*   Bilateral agreements such as the [Japan-Australia Migratory Bird Agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan-Australia_Migratory_Bird_Agreement "Japan-Australia Migratory Bird Agreement").

Global agreements such as the [Convention on Biological Diversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convention_on_Biological_Diversity "Convention on Biological Diversity"), give "sovereign national rights over biological resources" (not property).[[242]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-242) The agreements commit countries to "conserve biodiversity", "develop resources for sustainability" and "share the benefits" resulting from their use. Biodiverse countries that allow [bioprospecting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bioprospecting "Bioprospecting") or collection of natural products, expect a share of the benefits rather than allowing the individual or institution that discovers/exploits the resource to capture them privately. Bioprospecting can become a type of [biopiracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biopiracy "Biopiracy") when such principles are not respected.[[243]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-243)

Sovereignty principles can rely upon what is better known as [Access and Benefit Sharing Agreements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Treaty_on_Plant_Genetic_Resources_for_Food_and_Agriculture "International Treaty on Plant Genetic Resources for Food and Agriculture") (ABAs).[[244]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-244) The Convention on Biodiversity implies [informed consent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Informed_consent "Informed consent") between the source country and the collector, to establish which resource will be used and for what and to settle on a [fair agreement on benefit sharing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Treaty_on_Plant_Genetic_Resources_for_Food_and_Agriculture "International Treaty on Plant Genetic Resources for Food and Agriculture").

On the 19 of December 2022, during the [2022 United Nations Biodiversity Conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_United_Nations_Biodiversity_Conference "2022 United Nations Biodiversity Conference") every country on earth, with the exception of the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States") and the [Holy See](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holy_See "Holy See"), signed onto the agreement which includes protecting 30% of land and oceans by 2030 ([30 by 30](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/30_by_30 "30 by 30")) and 22 other targets intended to reduce [biodiversity loss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity_loss "Biodiversity loss").[[220]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Paddison-220)[[245]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-245)[[246]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-246) The agreement includes also recovering 30% of earth degraded ecosystems and increasing funding for biodiversity issues.[[247]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-247)

In May 2020, the European Union published its Biodiversity Strategy for 2030. The biodiversity strategy is an essential part of the [climate change mitigation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_mitigation "Climate change mitigation") strategy of the European Union. From the 25% of the European budget that will go to fight climate change, large part will go to restore biodiversity[[207]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-Knozowski-2023-207) and [nature based solutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature-based_solutions "Nature-based solutions").

The [EU Biodiversity Strategy for 2030](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EU_Biodiversity_Strategy_for_2030 "EU Biodiversity Strategy for 2030") include the next targets:

*   Protect 30% of the sea territory and 30% of the land territory especially [Old-growth forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old-growth_forest "Old-growth forest").
*   Plant 3 billion trees by 2030.
*   Restore at least 25,000 kilometers of rivers, so they will become free flowing.
*   Reduce the use of [Pesticides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pesticide "Pesticide") by 50% by 2030.
*   Increase [Organic farming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_farming "Organic farming"). In linked EU program [From Farm to Fork](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=From_Farm_to_Fork&action=edit&redlink=1 "From Farm to Fork (page does not exist)") it is said, that the target is making 25% of EU agriculture organic, by 2030.[[248]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-248)
*   Increase [biodiversity in agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity_in_agriculture "Biodiversity in agriculture").
*   Give €20 billion per year to the issue and make it part of the business practice.

Approximately half of the global [GDP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GDP "GDP") depend on nature. In Europe many parts of the economy that generate trillions of euros per year depend on nature. The benefits of [Natura 2000](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natura_2000 "Natura 2000") alone in Europe are €200 – €300 billion per year.[[249]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-249)

### National level laws

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity&action=edit&section=35 "Edit section: National level laws")]

Biodiversity is taken into account in some political and judicial decisions:

*   The relationship between law and ecosystems is very ancient and has consequences for biodiversity. It is related to private and public property rights. It can define protection for threatened ecosystems, but also some rights and duties (for example, [fishing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fishing "Fishing") and hunting rights).[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]
*   Law regarding species is more recent. It defines species that must be protected because they may be threatened by extinction. The U.S. [Endangered Species Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endangered_Species_Act "Endangered Species Act") is an example of an attempt to address the "law and species" issue.
*   Laws regarding gene pools are only about a century old.[[250]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-250) Domestication and plant breeding methods are not new, but advances in genetic engineering have led to tighter laws covering distribution of [genetically modified organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetically_modified_organisms "Genetically modified organisms"), gene [patents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patent "Patent") and process patents.[[251]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-251)

Uniform approval for use of biodiversity as a legal standard has not been achieved, however. Bosselman argues that biodiversity should not be used as a legal standard, claiming that the remaining areas of scientific uncertainty cause unacceptable administrative waste and increase litigation without promoting preservation goals.[[252]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-252)

India passed the [Biological Diversity Act](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_Diversity_Act "Biological Diversity Act") in 2002 for the conservation of biological diversity in India. The Act also provides mechanisms for equitable sharing of benefits from the use of traditional biological resources and knowledge.

History of the term
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biodiversity&action=edit&section=36 "Edit section: History of the term")]

*   1916 – The term _biological diversity_ was used first by [J. Arthur Harris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._Arthur_Harris "J. Arthur Harris") in "The Variable Desert", _[Scientific American](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific\_American "Scientific American")_: "The bare statement that the region contains a flora rich in genera and species and of diverse geographic origin or affinity is entirely inadequate as a description of its real biological diversity."[[253]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-253)
*   1967 – [Raymond F. Dasmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raymond_F._Dasmann "Raymond F. Dasmann") used the term biological diversity in reference to the richness of living nature that conservationists should protect in his book _A Different Kind of Country_.[[254]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-254)[[255]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-255)
*   1974 – The term _natural diversity_ was introduced by [John Terborgh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Terborgh "John Terborgh").[[256]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-256)
*   1980 – [Thomas Lovejoy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Lovejoy "Thomas Lovejoy") introduced the term _biological diversity_ to the scientific community in a book.[[257]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-ConsBiol80-257) It rapidly became commonly used.[[258]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-258)
*   1986 – According to [Edward O. Wilson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_O._Wilson "Edward O. Wilson"), the contracted form _biodiversity_ was coined by W. G. Rosen: "The National Forum on BioDiversity [held on September 21–24, 1986]... was conceived by Walter G. Rosen ... he introduced the term _biodiversity_".[[259]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-259)[[260]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-260)[[261]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-WilsonSciences1988-261)[[262]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-262)
*   1988 to Present – The [United Nations Environment Programme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Environment_Programme "United Nations Environment Programme") (UNEP) Ad Hoc Working Group of Experts on Biological Diversity in began working in November 1988, leading to the publication of the draft [Convention on Biological Diversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convention_on_Biological_Diversity "Convention on Biological Diversity") in May 1992. Since this time, there have been 16 Conferences of the Parties (COPs) to discuss potential global political responses to biodiversity loss. Most recently [COP 16](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2024_United_Nations_Biodiversity_Conference "2024 United Nations Biodiversity Conference") in [Cali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cali "Cali"), [Colombia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colombia "Colombia") in 2024.[[263]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity#cite_note-263)
