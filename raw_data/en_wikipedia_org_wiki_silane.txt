**Silane** (**Silicane**) is an [inorganic compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inorganic_compound "Inorganic compound") with [chemical formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")SiH 4. It is a colorless, [pyrophoric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrophoric "Pyrophoric")[gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas "Gas") with a sharp, repulsive, [pungent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pungent "Pungent") smell, somewhat similar to that of [acetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid "Acetic acid").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane#cite_note-6) Silane is of practical interest as a precursor to elemental [silicon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon "Silicon"). Silanes with [alkyl groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkyl_group "Alkyl group") are effective water repellents for mineral surfaces such as concrete and masonry. Silanes with both [organic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound") and [inorganic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inorganic_compound "Inorganic compound") attachments are used as coupling agents. They are commonly used to apply coatings to surfaces or as an adhesion promoter.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane#cite_note-7)

### Commercial-scale routes

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Silane&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Commercial-scale routes")]

Silane can be produced by several routes.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane#cite_note-Ullmann-8) Typically, it arises from the reaction of hydrogen chloride with [magnesium silicide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnesium_silicide "Magnesium silicide"):

Mg 2 Si + 4 HCl → 2 MgCl 2 + SiH 4
It is also prepared from metallurgical-grade silicon in a two-step process. First, silicon is treated with [hydrogen chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride "Hydrogen chloride") at about 300°C to produce [trichlorosilane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trichlorosilane "Trichlorosilane"), HSiCl3, along with [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") gas, according to the [chemical equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_equation "Chemical equation")

Si + 3 HCl → HSiCl 3 + H 2
The trichlorosilane is then converted to a mixture of silane and [silicon tetrachloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_tetrachloride "Silicon tetrachloride"):

4 HSiCl 3 → SiH 4 + 3 SiCl 4
This [redistribution reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redistribution_(chemistry) "Redistribution (chemistry)") requires a catalyst.

The most commonly used catalysts for this process are [metal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal "Metal")[halides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halogen "Halogen"), particularly [aluminium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium_chloride "Aluminium chloride"). This is referred to as a redistribution reaction, which is a double displacement involving the same central element. It may also be thought of as a [disproportionation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disproportionation "Disproportionation") reaction, even though there is no change in the oxidation number for silicon (Si has a nominal oxidation number IV in all three species). However, the utility of the oxidation number concept for a covalent molecule[_[vague](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Vagueness "Wikipedia:Vagueness")_], even a polar covalent molecule, is ambiguous.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] The silicon atom could be rationalized as having the highest formal oxidation state and partial positive charge in SiCl 4 and the lowest formal oxidation state in SiH 4, since Cl is far more electronegative than is H.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

An alternative industrial process for the preparation of very high-purity silane, suitable for use in the production of semiconductor-grade silicon, starts with metallurgical-grade silicon, hydrogen, and [silicon tetrachloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_tetrachloride "Silicon tetrachloride") and involves a complex series of redistribution reactions (producing byproducts that are recycled in the process) and distillations. The reactions are summarized below:

1.   Si + 2 H 2 + 3 SiCl 4 → 4 SiHCl 3
2.   2 SiHCl 3 → SiH 2 Cl 2 + SiCl 4
3.   2 SiH 2 Cl 2 → SiHCl 3 + SiH 3 Cl
4.   2 SiH 3 Cl → SiH 4 + SiH 2 Cl 2

The silane produced by this route can be thermally decomposed to produce high-purity silicon and hydrogen in a single pass.

Still other industrial routes to silane involve reduction of [silicon tetrafluoride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_tetrafluoride "Silicon tetrafluoride") (SiF 4) with [sodium hydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_hydride "Sodium hydride") (NaH) or reduction of SiCl 4 with [lithium aluminium hydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium_aluminium_hydride "Lithium aluminium hydride") (LiAlH 4).

Another commercial production of silane involves reduction of [silicon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_dioxide "Silicon dioxide") (SiO 2) under Al and H 2 gas in a mixture of [NaCl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NaCl "NaCl") and [aluminum chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminum_chloride "Aluminum chloride") (AlCl 3) at high pressures:[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane#cite_note-9)

3 SiO 2 + 6 H 2 + 4 Al → 3 SiH 4 + 2 Al 2 O 3

### Laboratory-scale routes

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Silane&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Laboratory-scale routes")]

In 1857, the German chemists [Heinrich Buff](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Heinrich_Buff&action=edit&redlink=1 "Heinrich Buff (page does not exist)") and [Friedrich Woehler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_W%C3%B6hler "Friedrich Wöhler") discovered silane among the products formed by the action of [hydrochloric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid "Hydrochloric acid") on aluminum silicide, which they had previously prepared. They called the compound _siliciuretted hydrogen_.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane#cite_note-10)

For classroom demonstrations, silane can be produced by heating [sand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sand "Sand") with [magnesium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnesium "Magnesium") powder to produce [magnesium silicide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnesium_silicide "Magnesium silicide") (Mg 2 Si), then pouring the mixture into hydrochloric acid. The magnesium silicide reacts with the acid to produce silane gas, which [burns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrophoricity "Pyrophoricity") on contact with air and produces tiny explosions.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane#cite_note-theodoregray-11) This may be classified as a [heterogeneous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneity_and_heterogeneity "Homogeneity and heterogeneity")[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_][acid–base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction "Acid–base reaction") chemical reaction, since the isolated Si 4− ion in the Mg 2 Si[antifluorite structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antifluorite_structure "Antifluorite structure") can serve as a [Brønsted–Lowry base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Br%C3%B8nsted%E2%80%93Lowry_acid%E2%80%93base_theory "Brønsted–Lowry acid–base theory") capable of accepting four protons. It can be written as

4 HCl + Mg 2 Si → SiH 4 + 2 MgCl 2
In general, the alkaline-earth metals form silicides with the following [stoichiometries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoichiometry "Stoichiometry"): M II 2 Si, M II Si, and M II Si 2. In all cases, these substances react with Brønsted–Lowry acids to produce some type of hydride of silicon that is dependent on the Si anion connectivity in the silicide. The possible products include SiH 4 and/or higher molecules in the homologous series Si _n_ H 2 _n_+2, a polymeric silicon hydride, or a [silicic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicic_acid "Silicic acid"). Hence, M II Si with their zigzag chains of Si 2− anions (containing two lone pairs of electrons on each Si anion that can accept protons) yield the polymeric hydride (SiH 2)_x_.

Yet another small-scale route for the production of silane is from the action of [sodium amalgam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_amalgam "Sodium amalgam") on [dichlorosilane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dichlorosilane "Dichlorosilane"), SiH 2 Cl 2, to yield monosilane along with some yellow [polymerized silicon hydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polysilicon_hydride "Polysilicon hydride")(SiH)_x_.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane#cite_note-12)

Silane is the [silicon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon "Silicon")[analogue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analog_(chemistry) "Analog (chemistry)") of [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane"). All four Si−H bonds are equal and their length is 147.98 [pm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Picometre "Picometre").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane#cite_note-cccbdb-13) Because of the greater electronegativity of hydrogen in comparison to silicon, this Si–H bond polarity is the opposite of that in the C–H bonds of methane. One consequence of this reversed polarity is the greater tendency of silane to form complexes with transition metals. A second consequence is that silane is [pyrophoric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrophoric "Pyrophoric")— it undergoes spontaneous combustion in air, without the need for external ignition.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane#cite_note-14) However, the difficulties in explaining the available (often contradictory) combustion data are ascribed to the fact that silane itself is stable and that the natural formation of larger silanes during production, as well as the sensitivity of combustion to impurities such as moisture and to the catalytic effects of container surfaces causes its pyrophoricity.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane#cite_note-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane#cite_note-timms-16) Above 420°C (788°F), silane decomposes into silicon and [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen"); it can therefore be used in the [chemical vapor deposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_vapor_deposition "Chemical vapor deposition") of silicon.

The Si–H [bond strength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_dissociation_energy "Bond dissociation energy") is around 384 kJ/mol, which is about 20% weaker than the H–H bond in H 2. Consequently, compounds containing Si–H bonds are much more reactive than is H 2. The strength of the Si–H bond is modestly affected by other substituents: the Si–H bond strengths are: SiHF 3 419 kJ/mol, SiHCl 3 382 kJ/mol, and SiHMe 3 398 kJ/mol.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane#cite_note-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane#cite_note-18)

Monosilane gas shipping containers in Japan.

While diverse applications exist for [organosilanes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organosilicon_chemistry "Organosilicon chemistry"), silane itself has one dominant application, as a precursor to elemental silicon, particularly in the semiconductor industry. The higher silanes, such as di- and trisilane, are only of academic interest. About 300 [metric tons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_ton "Metric ton") per year of silane were consumed in the late 1990s.[_[needs update](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual\_of\_Style/Dates\_and\_numbers#Chronological\_items "Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers")_][[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane#cite_note-timms-16) Low-cost [solar photovoltaic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_photovoltaic "Solar photovoltaic") module manufacturing has led to substantial consumption of silane for depositing hydrogenated [amorphous silicon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amorphous_silicon "Amorphous silicon") (a-Si:H) on glass and other substrates like metal and plastic. The [plasma-enhanced chemical vapor deposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma-enhanced_chemical_vapor_deposition "Plasma-enhanced chemical vapor deposition") (PECVD) process is relatively inefficient at materials utilization with approximately 85% of the silane being wasted. To reduce the waste and [ecological footprint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecological_footprint "Ecological footprint") of a-Si:H-based solar cells further, several recycling efforts have been developed.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane#cite_note-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane#cite_note-20)

Safety and precautions
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Silane&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Safety and precautions")]

A number of fatal industrial accidents produced by combustion and detonation of leaked silane in air have been reported.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane#cite_note-21)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane#cite_note-22)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane#cite_note-23)

Silane is a pyrophoric gas (capable of autoignition at temperatures below 54°C or 129°F).[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane#cite_note-24)

SiH 4 + 2 O 2 → SiO 2 + 2 H 2 O![Image 18: {\displaystyle \Delta H=-1517{\text{ kJ/mol }}=-47.23{\text{ kJ/g}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/953f0403b82c621c22fff0b613db6a7b4595a5e4)SiH 4 + O 2 → SiO 2 + 2 H 2 SiH 4 + O 2 → SiH 2 O + H 2 O 2 SiH 4 + O 2 → 2 SiH 2 O + 2 H 2 SiH 2 O + O 2 → SiO 2 + H 2 O
For lean mixtures a two-stage reaction process has been proposed, which consists of a silane consumption process and a hydrogen oxidation process. The heat of SiO 2(s) condensation increases the burning velocity due to thermal feedback.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane#cite_note-25)

Diluted silane mixtures with inert gases such as [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") or [argon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argon "Argon") are even more likely to ignite when leaked into open air, compared to pure silane: even a 1% mixture of silane in pure nitrogen easily ignites when exposed to air.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane#cite_note-26)

In Japan, in order to reduce the danger of silane for amorphous silicon solar cell manufacturing, several companies began to dilute silane with [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") gas. This resulted in a symbiotic benefit of making more stable [solar photovoltaic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_photovoltaic "Solar photovoltaic") cells as it reduced the [Staebler–Wronski effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Staebler%E2%80%93Wronski_effect "Staebler–Wronski effect").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Unlike methane, silane is slightly toxic: the lethal concentration in air for rats ([LC 50](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Median_lethal_dose "Median lethal dose")) is 0.96% (9,600 ppm) over a 4-hour exposure. In addition, contact with eyes may form [silicic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicic_acid "Silicic acid") with resultant irritation.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane#cite_note-27)

In regards to occupational exposure of silane to workers, the US [National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health "National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health") has set a [recommended exposure limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recommended_exposure_limit "Recommended exposure limit") of 5 ppm (7 mg/m 3) over an eight-hour time-weighted average.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silane#cite_note-28)
