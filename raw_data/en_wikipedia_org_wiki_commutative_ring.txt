In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **commutative ring** is a [ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)") in which the multiplication operation is [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative "Commutative"). The study of commutative rings is called [commutative algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_algebra "Commutative algebra"). Complementarily, [noncommutative algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noncommutative_algebra "Noncommutative algebra") is the study of ring properties that are not specific to commutative rings. This distinction results from the high number of fundamental properties of commutative rings that do not extend to noncommutative rings.

Commutative rings appear in the following chain of [class inclusions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subclass_(set_theory) "Subclass (set theory)"):

**[rngs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rng_(algebra) "Rng (algebra)")** ⊃ **[rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)")** ⊃ **[commutative rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring)** ⊃ **[integral domains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_domain "Integral domain")** ⊃ **[integrally closed domains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrally_closed_domain "Integrally closed domain")** ⊃ **[GCD domains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GCD_domain "GCD domain")** ⊃ **[unique factorization domains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unique_factorization_domain "Unique factorization domain")** ⊃ **[principal ideal domains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_ideal_domain "Principal ideal domain")** ⊃ **[euclidean domains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_domain "Euclidean domain")** ⊃ **[fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)")** ⊃ **[algebraically closed fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraically_closed_field "Algebraically closed field")**

Definition and first examples
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Commutative_ring&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Definition and first examples")]

A _ring_ is a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)")![Image 1: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) equipped with two [binary operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_operation "Binary operation"), i.e. operations combining any two elements of the ring to a third. They are called _addition_ and _multiplication_ and commonly denoted by "![Image 2: {\displaystyle +}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe6ef363cd19902d1a7a71fb1c8b21e8ede52406)" and "![Image 3: {\displaystyle \cdot }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba2c023bad1bd39ed49080f729cbf26bc448c9ba)"; e.g. ![Image 4: {\displaystyle a+b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a2391acf09244b9dba74eb940e871a6be7e7973a) and ![Image 5: {\displaystyle a\cdot b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/620419d3ed53abc98659a5fc0f3a5eb6177830ae). To form a ring these two operations have to satisfy a number of properties: the ring has to be an [abelian group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group") under addition as well as a [monoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monoid "Monoid") under multiplication, where multiplication [distributes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributive_law "Distributive law") over addition; i.e., ![Image 6: {\displaystyle a\cdot \left(b+c\right)=\left(a\cdot b\right)+\left(a\cdot c\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/09c39ad791abd5747462add406da44b14dd313d9). The identity elements for addition and multiplication are denoted ![Image 7: {\displaystyle 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2aae8864a3c1fec9585261791a809ddec1489950) and ![Image 8: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf), respectively.

If the multiplication is commutative, i.e. ![Image 9: {\displaystyle a\cdot b=b\cdot a,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a2d89d7b6e4965ec503ee4b200abf8b78e8dc148) then the ring ![Image 10: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is called _commutative_. In the remainder of this article, all rings will be commutative, unless explicitly stated otherwise.

An important example, and in some sense crucial, is the [ring of integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer")![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) with the two operations of addition and multiplication. As the multiplication of integers is a commutative operation, this is a commutative ring. It is usually denoted ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) as an abbreviation of the [German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_language "German language") word _Zahlen_ (numbers).

A [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)") is a commutative ring where ![Image 13: {\displaystyle 0\neq 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5add705e86314a6ce57c76d7493896b092661a75) and every [non-zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0_(number) "0 (number)") element ![Image 14: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) is invertible; i.e., has a multiplicative inverse ![Image 15: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3) such that ![Image 16: {\displaystyle a\cdot b=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/943794bd7ed5a52298d3c7e44eceea37724e2d1b). Therefore, by definition, any field is a commutative ring. The [rational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number "Rational number"), [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") and [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") form fields.

If ![Image 17: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is a given commutative ring, then the set of all [polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial") in the variable ![Image 18: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) whose coefficients are in ![Image 19: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) forms the [polynomial ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_ring "Polynomial ring"), denoted ![Image 20: {\displaystyle R\left[X\right]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73de3a1ad1ac968cfce515ecb04c96a8633f60bf). The same holds true for several variables.

If ![Image 21: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) is some [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space"), for example a subset of some ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d), real- or complex-valued [continuous functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function") on ![Image 23: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) form a commutative ring. The same is true for [differentiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_function "Differentiable function") or [holomorphic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holomorphic_function "Holomorphic function"), when the two concepts are defined, such as for ![Image 24: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) a [complex manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_manifold "Complex manifold").

In contrast to fields, where every nonzero element is multiplicatively invertible, the concept of [divisibility for rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divisibility_(ring_theory) "Divisibility (ring theory)") is richer. An element ![Image 25: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) of ring ![Image 26: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is called a [unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_(algebra) "Unit (algebra)") if it possesses a multiplicative inverse. Another particular type of element is the [zero divisors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_divisor "Zero divisor"), i.e. an element ![Image 27: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) such that there exists a non-zero element ![Image 28: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3) of the ring such that ![Image 29: {\displaystyle ab=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a59b821cbfde052e05d53bf5f013cbb97a6bbd6). If ![Image 30: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) possesses no non-zero zero divisors, it is called an [integral domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_domain "Integral domain") (or domain). An element ![Image 31: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) satisfying ![Image 32: {\displaystyle a^{n}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c1b03312f135b5289da188c9a23006fb401e574) for some positive integer ![Image 33: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) is called [nilpotent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nilpotent_element "Nilpotent element").

The _localization_ of a ring is a process in which some elements are rendered invertible, i.e. multiplicative inverses are added to the ring. Concretely, if ![Image 34: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) is a [multiplicatively closed subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplicatively_closed_subset "Multiplicatively closed subset") of ![Image 35: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) (i.e. whenever ![Image 36: {\displaystyle s,t\in S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5cef58c646b1df37d9f7bc0eacc49d8eddac37ea) then so is ![Image 37: {\displaystyle st}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2cdf20afe66602738ef1970158f18cc18f55b4ba)) then the _localization_ of ![Image 38: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) at ![Image 39: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2), or _ring of fractions_ with denominators in ![Image 40: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2), usually denoted ![Image 41: {\displaystyle S^{-1}R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/36f81aa8006deb333465f99113727ab38fa80e04) consists of symbols

![Image 42: {\displaystyle {\frac {r}{s}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f8eed615832363b6ebab6609b7f628281a2cdd9c) with ![Image 43: {\displaystyle r\in R,s\in S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10a285aa4a8820c5a36788a6b09f6c76b7ed96af)

subject to certain rules that mimic the cancellation familiar from rational numbers. Indeed, in this language ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5909f0b54e4718fa24d5fd34d54189d24a66e9a) is the localization of ![Image 45: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) at all nonzero integers. This construction works for any integral domain ![Image 46: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) instead of ![Image 47: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc). The localization ![Image 48: {\displaystyle \left(R\setminus \left\{0\right\}\right)^{-1}R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/78e59a4e5ed7a58302408905f86f9a29ed0d28e5) is a field, called the [quotient field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_field "Quotient field") of ![Image 49: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33).

In the following, R denotes a commutative ring.

Many of the following notions also exist for not necessarily commutative rings, but the definitions and properties are usually more complicated. For example, all ideals in a commutative ring are automatically [two-sided](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-sided_ideal "Two-sided ideal"), which simplifies the situation considerably.

For a ring ![Image 50: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33), an ![Image 51: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33)-_module_![Image 52: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is like what a vector space is to a field. That is, elements in a module can be added; they can be multiplied by elements of ![Image 53: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) subject to the same axioms as for a vector space.

The study of modules is significantly more involved than the one of [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space"), since there are modules that do not have any [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)"), that is, do not contain a [spanning set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanning_set "Spanning set") whose elements are [linearly independents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linearly_independent "Linearly independent"). A module that has a basis is called a [free module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_module "Free module"), and a submodule of a free module needs not to be free.

A [module of finite type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_of_finite_type "Module of finite type") is a module that has a finite spanning set. Modules of finite type play a fundamental role in the theory of commutative rings, similar to the role of the [finite-dimensional vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite-dimensional_vector_space "Finite-dimensional vector space") in [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra"). In particular, [Noetherian rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noetherian_rings "Noetherian rings") (see also _[§Noetherian rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative\_ring#Noetherian\_rings)_, below) can be defined as the rings such that every submodule of a module of finite type is also of finite type.

_Ideals_ of a ring ![Image 54: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) are the [submodules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Submodule "Submodule") of ![Image 55: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33), i.e., the modules contained in ![Image 56: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33). In more detail, an ideal ![Image 57: {\displaystyle I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/535ea7fc4134a31cbe2251d9d3511374bc41be9f) is a non-empty subset of ![Image 58: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) such that for all ![Image 59: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) in ![Image 60: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33), ![Image 61: {\displaystyle i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/add78d8608ad86e54951b8c8bd6c8d8416533d20) and ![Image 62: {\displaystyle j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f461e54f5c093e92a55547b9764291390f0b5d0) in ![Image 63: {\displaystyle I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/535ea7fc4134a31cbe2251d9d3511374bc41be9f), both ![Image 64: {\displaystyle ri}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9bbaf2ab121a373b9a5c18bcfcadd025bc70860b) and ![Image 65: {\displaystyle i+j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea2c2b111f44787702a0e16807f7d66b541d1c59) are in ![Image 66: {\displaystyle I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/535ea7fc4134a31cbe2251d9d3511374bc41be9f). For various applications, understanding the ideals of a ring is of particular importance, but often one proceeds by studying modules in general.

Any ring has two ideals, namely the [zero ideal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0_(number) "0 (number)")![Image 67: {\displaystyle \left\{0\right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4c92fb4ba1dbe6e7512b5fc989753060a7e15261) and ![Image 68: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33), the whole ring. These two ideals are the only ones precisely if ![Image 69: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is a field. Given any subset ![Image 70: {\displaystyle F=\left\{f_{j}\right\}_{j\in J}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f10d49e49cc0d7c12a31c52a0b8620ca724b009c) of ![Image 71: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) (where ![Image 72: {\displaystyle J}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/359e4f407b49910e02c27c2f52e87a36cd74c053) is some index set), the ideal _generated by_![Image 73: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) is the smallest ideal that contains ![Image 74: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57). Equivalently, it is given by finite [linear combinations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_combination "Linear combination")![Image 75: {\displaystyle r_{1}f_{1}+r_{2}f_{2}+\dots +r_{n}f_{n}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/305ed91e7444628c36ee00786be7522769f6c590)

#### Principal ideal domains

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Commutative_ring&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Principal ideal domains")]

If ![Image 76: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) consists of a single element ![Image 77: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538), the ideal generated by ![Image 78: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57) consists of the multiples of ![Image 79: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538), i.e., the elements of the form ![Image 80: {\displaystyle rs}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/009ad13250c948a0ea4089b2548c0175efdd44f3) for arbitrary elements ![Image 81: {\displaystyle s}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01d131dfd7673938b947072a13a9744fe997e632). Such an ideal is called a [principal ideal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_ideal "Principal ideal"). If every ideal is a principal ideal, ![Image 82: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is called a [principal ideal ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_ideal_ring "Principal ideal ring"); two important cases are ![Image 83: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) and ![Image 84: {\displaystyle k\left[X\right]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa1bbc13d0fa33e8950dc1de615eb1d10739d329), the polynomial ring over a field ![Image 85: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40). These two are in addition domains, so they are called [principal ideal domains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_ideal_domain "Principal ideal domain").

Unlike for general rings, for a principal ideal domain, the properties of individual elements are strongly tied to the properties of the ring as a whole. For example, any principal ideal domain ![Image 86: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is a [unique factorization domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unique_factorization_domain "Unique factorization domain") (UFD) which means that any element is a product of irreducible elements, in a (up to reordering of factors) unique way. Here, an element ![Image 87: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) in a domain is called [irreducible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irreducible_element "Irreducible element") if the only way of expressing it as a product ![Image 88: {\displaystyle a=bc,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f84cb51dc6db784dca5664203d73a47ee7b79c63) is by either ![Image 89: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3) or ![Image 90: {\displaystyle c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a67b81c2de995bd608d5b2df50cd8cd7d92455) being a unit. An example, important in [field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)"), are [irreducible polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irreducible_polynomial "Irreducible polynomial"), i.e., irreducible elements in ![Image 91: {\displaystyle k\left[X\right]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa1bbc13d0fa33e8950dc1de615eb1d10739d329), for a field ![Image 92: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40). The fact that ![Image 93: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) is a UFD can be stated more elementarily by saying that any [natural number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number") can be uniquely decomposed as product of powers of prime numbers. It is also known as the [fundamental theorem of arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_arithmetic "Fundamental theorem of arithmetic").

An element ![Image 94: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) is a [prime element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_element "Prime element") if whenever ![Image 95: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) divides a product ![Image 96: {\displaystyle bc}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/729e20113e9029b3d860aab123277d8aa0d7a950), ![Image 97: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) divides ![Image 98: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3) or ![Image 99: {\displaystyle c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a67b81c2de995bd608d5b2df50cd8cd7d92455). In a domain, being prime implies being irreducible. The converse is true in a unique factorization domain, but false in general.

The definition of ideals is such that "dividing" ![Image 100: {\displaystyle I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/535ea7fc4134a31cbe2251d9d3511374bc41be9f) "out" gives another ring, the _factor ring_![Image 101: {\displaystyle R/I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0650c975ee7bf3b39ce3144a5de71179c40ee493): it is the set of [cosets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coset "Coset") of ![Image 102: {\displaystyle I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/535ea7fc4134a31cbe2251d9d3511374bc41be9f) together with the operations ![Image 103: {\displaystyle \left(a+I\right)+\left(b+I\right)=\left(a+b\right)+I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ccf28b9d122442b30f3763648b907908c47f917) and ![Image 104: {\displaystyle \left(a+I\right)\left(b+I\right)=ab+I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b53db218127d0054d8fdc333989b90b12b12d23d). For example, the ring ![Image 105: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} /n\mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f2120ebbc85f91df66c6de5446367bf9fd620844) (also denoted ![Image 106: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} _{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7b729c334a9863c47f0b7e3ad61342c2f0881bdb)), where ![Image 107: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) is an integer, is the ring of integers modulo ![Image 108: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b). It is the basis of [modular arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modular_arithmetic "Modular arithmetic").

An ideal is _proper_ if it is strictly smaller than the whole ring. An ideal that is not strictly contained in any proper ideal is called [maximal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maximal_ideal "Maximal ideal"). An ideal ![Image 109: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) is maximal [if and only if](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If_and_only_if "If and only if")![Image 110: {\displaystyle R/m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/913099f0b37b10bc65bfc2159617805eb85700cd) is a field. Except for the [zero ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_ring "Zero ring"), any ring (with identity) possesses at least one maximal ideal; this follows from [Zorn's lemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zorn%27s_lemma "Zorn's lemma").

A ring is called _Noetherian_ (in honor of [Emmy Noether](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmy_Noether "Emmy Noether"), who developed this concept) if every [ascending chain of ideals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ascending_chain_condition "Ascending chain condition")![Image 111: {\displaystyle 0\subseteq I_{0}\subseteq I_{1}\subseteq \dots \subseteq I_{n}\subseteq I_{n+1}\dots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/56feb8eeea62d0f0b91eeb6314aa0c7995f8b4c9) becomes stationary, i.e. becomes constant beyond some index ![Image 112: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b). Equivalently, any ideal is generated by finitely many elements, or, yet equivalent, [submodules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Submodule "Submodule") of finitely generated modules are finitely generated.

Being Noetherian is a highly important finiteness condition, and the condition is preserved under many operations that occur frequently in geometry. For example, if ![Image 113: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is Noetherian, then so is the polynomial ring ![Image 114: {\displaystyle R\left[X_{1},X_{2},\dots ,X_{n}\right]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/37b22dfd66d71acc5feebeffa02d8de930c1d93c) (by [Hilbert's basis theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert%27s_basis_theorem "Hilbert's basis theorem")), any localization ![Image 115: {\displaystyle S^{-1}R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/36f81aa8006deb333465f99113727ab38fa80e04), and also any factor ring ![Image 116: {\displaystyle R/I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0650c975ee7bf3b39ce3144a5de71179c40ee493).

Any non-Noetherian ring ![Image 117: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is the [union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_(set_theory) "Union (set theory)") of its Noetherian subrings. This fact, known as [Noetherian approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Noetherian_approximation&action=edit&redlink=1 "Noetherian approximation (page does not exist)"), allows the extension of certain theorems to non-Noetherian rings.

A ring is called [Artinian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artinian_ring "Artinian ring") (after [Emil Artin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emil_Artin "Emil Artin")), if every descending chain of ideals ![Image 118: {\displaystyle R\supseteq I_{0}\supseteq I_{1}\supseteq \dots \supseteq I_{n}\supseteq I_{n+1}\dots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/36c697d82d1f7cfb4f85a3f5b6804064139c9148) becomes stationary eventually. Despite the two conditions appearing symmetric, Noetherian rings are much more general than Artinian rings. For example, ![Image 119: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) is Noetherian, since every ideal can be generated by one element, but is not Artinian, as the chain ![Image 120: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} \supsetneq 2\mathbb {Z} \supsetneq 4\mathbb {Z} \supsetneq 8\mathbb {Z} \dots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9b54258e0ed92f08122870a372d235e40268fb18) shows. In fact, by the [Hopkins–Levitzki theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hopkins%E2%80%93Levitzki_theorem "Hopkins–Levitzki theorem"), every Artinian ring is Noetherian. More precisely, Artinian rings can be characterized as the Noetherian rings whose [Krull dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krull_dimension "Krull dimension") is zero.

Spectrum of a commutative ring
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Commutative_ring&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Spectrum of a commutative ring")]

As was mentioned above, ![Image 121: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc) is a [unique factorization domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unique_factorization_domain "Unique factorization domain"). This is not true for more general rings, as algebraists realized in the 19th century. For example, in ![Image 122: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} \left[{\sqrt {-5}}\right]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/828485881660a382d22d27b7ddce858daa2ea760) there are two genuinely distinct ways of writing 6 as a product: ![Image 123: {\displaystyle 6=2\cdot 3=\left(1+{\sqrt {-5}}\right)\left(1-{\sqrt {-5}}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/42c2c4ad5136d7aaa9ab490bad474c3b02668784) Prime ideals, as opposed to prime elements, provide a way to circumvent this problem. A prime ideal is a proper (i.e., strictly contained in ![Image 124: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33)) ideal ![Image 125: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) such that, whenever the product ![Image 126: {\displaystyle ab}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/49337c5cf256196e2292f7047cb5da68c24ca95d) of any two ring elements ![Image 127: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) and ![Image 128: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3) is in ![Image 129: {\displaystyle p,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/393fcf18074cb42eafb26b76c515a1e93e17512c) at least one of the two elements is already in ![Image 130: {\displaystyle p.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88532f4eab1d4cef71ef96c0f8c98cac36fd9257) (The opposite conclusion holds for any ideal, by definition.) Thus, if a prime ideal is principal, it is equivalently generated by a prime element. However, in rings such as ![Image 131: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} \left[{\sqrt {-5}}\right],}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3271816be14eae9d4f40c3843eff106ad948019d) prime ideals need not be principal. This limits the usage of prime elements in ring theory. A cornerstone of algebraic number theory is, however, the fact that in any [Dedekind ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dedekind_ring "Dedekind ring") (which includes ![Image 132: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} \left[{\sqrt {-5}}\right]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/828485881660a382d22d27b7ddce858daa2ea760) and more generally the [ring of integers in a number field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_integers "Algebraic integers")) any ideal (such as the one generated by 6) decomposes uniquely as a product of prime ideals.

Any maximal ideal is a prime ideal or, more briefly, is prime. Moreover, an ideal ![Image 133: {\displaystyle I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/535ea7fc4134a31cbe2251d9d3511374bc41be9f) is prime if and only if the factor ring ![Image 134: {\displaystyle R/I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0650c975ee7bf3b39ce3144a5de71179c40ee493) is an integral domain. Proving that an ideal is prime, or equivalently that a ring has no zero-divisors can be very difficult. Yet another way of expressing the same is to say that the [complement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complement_(set_theory) "Complement (set theory)")![Image 135: {\displaystyle R\setminus p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/46c6d49abb0bcc8c75fdd5f347c8c606c6fff074) is multiplicatively closed. The localisation ![Image 136: {\displaystyle \left(R\setminus p\right)^{-1}R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06d2162a69f9244ebe4c3cd928aba6280de4b651) is important enough to have its own notation: ![Image 137: {\displaystyle R_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f6650a6a5d41e666a0e83553ed13ec10e0d72845). This ring has only one maximal ideal, namely ![Image 138: {\displaystyle pR_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4744f552ec66a5fd2670d15efc55f7daa955fe80). Such rings are called [local](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_ring "Local ring").

[![Image 139](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Spec_Z.png/500px-Spec_Z.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Spec_Z.png)

Spec (**Z**) contains a point for the zero ideal. The closure of this point is the entire space. The remaining points are the ones corresponding to ideals (_p_), where _p_ is a prime number. These points are closed.

The _spectrum of a ring_![Image 140: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33),[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring#cite_note-1) denoted by ![Image 141: {\displaystyle {\text{Spec}}\ R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/37cfe5ad87fd9a6209416aa594a6af0854bc6e19), is the set of all prime ideals of ![Image 142: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33). It is equipped with a topology, the [Zariski topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zariski_topology "Zariski topology"), which reflects the algebraic properties of ![Image 143: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33): a basis of open subsets is given by ![Image 144: {\displaystyle D\left(f\right)=\left\{p\in {\text{Spec}}\ R,f\not \in p\right\},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd4a27722697c82553b5d9ae3d639b37be7e841c) where ![Image 145: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is any ring element. Interpreting ![Image 146: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) as a function that takes the value _f_ mod _p_ (i.e., the image of _f_ in the residue field _R_/_p_), this subset is the locus where _f_ is non-zero. The spectrum also makes precise the intuition that localisation and factor rings are complementary: the natural maps _R_ → _R_ _f_ and _R_ → _R_ / _fR_ correspond, after endowing the spectra of the rings in question with their Zariski topology, to complementary [open](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_immersion "Open immersion") and [closed immersions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_immersion "Closed immersion") respectively. Even for basic rings, such as illustrated for _R_ = **Z** at the right, the Zariski topology is quite different from the one on the set of real numbers.

The spectrum contains the set of maximal ideals, which is occasionally denoted mSpec (_R_). For an [algebraically closed field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraically_closed_field "Algebraically closed field")_k_, mSpec (k[_T_ 1, ..., _T_ _n_] / (_f_ 1, ..., _f_ _m_)) is in bijection with the set

{_x_ =(_x_ 1, ..., _x_ _n_) ∊ _k_ _n_

Thus, maximal ideals reflect the geometric properties of solution sets of polynomials, which is an initial motivation for the study of commutative rings. However, the consideration of non-maximal ideals as part of the geometric properties of a ring is useful for several reasons. For example, the minimal prime ideals (i.e., the ones not strictly containing smaller ones) correspond to the [irreducible components](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irreducible_component "Irreducible component") of Spec _R_. For a Noetherian ring _R_, Spec _R_ has only finitely many irreducible components. This is a geometric restatement of [primary decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_decomposition "Primary decomposition"), according to which any ideal can be decomposed as a product of finitely many [primary ideals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_ideal "Primary ideal"). This fact is the ultimate generalization of the decomposition into prime ideals in Dedekind rings.

The notion of a spectrum is the common basis of commutative algebra and [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"). Algebraic geometry proceeds by endowing Spec _R_ with a [sheaf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheaf_(mathematics) "Sheaf (mathematics)")![Image 147: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {O}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d6ae2ed4058fb748a183d9ada8aea50a00d0c89f) (an entity that collects functions defined locally, i.e. on varying open subsets). The datum of the space and the sheaf is called an [affine scheme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_scheme "Affine scheme"). Given an affine scheme, the underlying ring _R_ can be recovered as the [global sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_section "Global section") of ![Image 148: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {O}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d6ae2ed4058fb748a183d9ada8aea50a00d0c89f). Moreover, this one-to-one correspondence between rings and affine schemes is also compatible with ring homomorphisms: any _f_ : _R_ → _S_ gives rise to a [continuous map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_map "Continuous map") in the opposite direction

Spec _S_ → Spec _R_, _q_ ↦ _f_−1(_q_), i.e. any prime ideal of _S_ is mapped to its [preimage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preimage "Preimage") under _f_, which is a prime ideal of _R_.

The resulting [equivalence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_of_categories "Equivalence of categories") of the two said categories aptly reflects algebraic properties of rings in a geometrical manner.

Similar to the fact that [manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold_(mathematics) "Manifold (mathematics)") are locally given by open subsets of **R**_n_, affine schemes are local models for [schemes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scheme_(mathematics) "Scheme (mathematics)"), which are the object of study in algebraic geometry. Therefore, several notions concerning commutative rings stem from geometric intuition.

The _Krull dimension_ (or dimension) dim _R_ of a ring _R_ measures the "size" of a ring by, roughly speaking, counting independent elements in _R_. The dimension of algebras over a field _k_ can be axiomatized by four properties:

*   The dimension is a local property: dim _R_ = sup p ∊ Spec _R_ dim _R_ _p_.
*   The dimension is independent of nilpotent elements: if _I_ ⊆ _R_ is nilpotent then dim _R_ = dim _R_ / _I_.
*   The dimension remains constant under a finite extension: if _S_ is an _R_-algebra which is finitely generated as an _R_-module, then dim _S_ = dim _R_.
*   The dimension is calibrated by dim _k_[_X_ 1, ..., _X_ _n_] = _n_. This axiom is motivated by regarding the polynomial ring in _n_ variables as an algebraic analogue of [_n_-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space "Affine space").

The dimension is defined, for any ring _R_, as the supremum of lengths _n_ of chains of prime ideals

_p_ 0 ⊊ _p_ 1 ⊊ ... ⊊ _p_ _n_.

For example, a field is zero-dimensional, since the only prime ideal is the zero ideal. The integers are one-dimensional, since chains are of the form (0) ⊊ (_p_), where _p_ is a [prime number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_number "Prime number"). For non-Noetherian rings, and also non-local rings, the dimension may be infinite, but Noetherian local rings have finite dimension. Among the four axioms above, the first two are elementary consequences of the definition, whereas the remaining two hinge on important facts in [commutative algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_algebra "Commutative algebra"), the [going-up theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Going-up_theorem "Going-up theorem") and [Krull's principal ideal theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krull%27s_principal_ideal_theorem "Krull's principal ideal theorem").

A _ring homomorphism_ or, more colloquially, simply a _map_, is a map _f_ : _R_ → _S_ such that

_f_(_a_ + _b_) = _f_(_a_) + _f_(_b_), _f_(_ab_) = _f_(_a_)_f_(_b_) and _f_(1) = 1.

These conditions ensure _f_(0) = 0. Similarly as for other algebraic structures, a ring homomorphism is thus a map that is compatible with the structure of the algebraic objects in question. In such a situation _S_ is also called an _R_-algebra, by understanding that _s_ in _S_ may be multiplied by some _r_ of _R_, by setting

_r_ · _s_:= _f_(_r_) · _s_.

The _kernel_ and _image_ of _f_ are defined by ker(_f_) = {_r_ ∈ _R_, _f_(_r_) = 0} and im(_f_) = _f_(_R_) = {_f_(_r_), _r_ ∈ _R_}. The kernel is an [ideal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_ideal "Ring ideal") of _R_, and the image is a [subring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subring "Subring") of _S_.

A ring homomorphism is called an isomorphism if it is bijective. An example of a ring isomorphism, known as the [Chinese remainder theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_remainder_theorem "Chinese remainder theorem"), is ![Image 149: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Z} /n=\bigoplus _{i=0}^{k}\mathbf {Z} /p_{i},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6f68e5ac6b89da914560a3f706a2a45508bbdcb9) where _n_ = _p_ 1 _p_ 2..._p_ _k_ is a product of pairwise distinct [prime numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_number "Prime number").

Commutative rings, together with ring homomorphisms, form a [category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_(mathematics) "Category (mathematics)"). The ring **Z** is the [initial object](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Initial_object "Initial object") in this category, which means that for any commutative ring _R_, there is a unique ring homomorphism **Z** → _R_. By means of this map, an integer _n_ can be regarded as an element of _R_. For example, the [binomial formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binomial_formula "Binomial formula")![Image 150: {\displaystyle (a+b)^{n}=\sum _{k=0}^{n}{\binom {n}{k}}a^{k}b^{n-k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c261728e25b77e0d253599a7b9d4aa1badf2f273) which is valid for any two elements _a_ and _b_ in any commutative ring _R_ is understood in this sense by interpreting the binomial coefficients as elements of _R_ using this map.

[![Image 151](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/83/Tensor_product_of_algebras.png/250px-Tensor_product_of_algebras.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Tensor_product_of_algebras.png)

The [universal property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_property "Universal property") of _S_ ⊗_R_ _T_ states that for any two maps _S_ → _W_ and _T_ → _W_ which make the outer quadrangle commute, there is a unique map _S_ ⊗_R_ _T_ → _W_ that makes the entire diagram commute.

Given two _R_-algebras _S_ and _T_, their [tensor product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product_of_algebras "Tensor product of algebras")

_S_ ⊗_R_ _T_

is again a commutative _R_-algebra. In some cases, the tensor product can serve to find a _T_-algebra which relates to _Z_ as _S_ relates to _R_. For example,

_R_[_X_] ⊗_R_ _T_ = _T_[_X_].

An _R_-algebra _S_ is called [finitely generated (as an algebra)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finitely_generated_algebra "Finitely generated algebra") if there are finitely many elements _s_ 1, ..., _s_ _n_ such that any element of _s_ is expressible as a polynomial in the _s_ _i_. Equivalently, _S_ is isomorphic to

_R_[_T_ 1, ..., _T_ _n_] / _I_.

A much stronger condition is that _S_ is [finitely generated as an _R_-module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finitely_generated_module "Finitely generated module"), which means that any _s_ can be expressed as a _R_-linear combination of some finite set _s_ 1, ..., _s_ _n_.

A ring is called [local](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_ring "Local ring") if it has only a single maximal ideal, denoted by _m_. For any (not necessarily local) ring _R_, the localization

_R_ _p_

at a prime ideal _p_ is local. This localization reflects the geometric properties of Spec _R_ "around _p_". Several notions and problems in commutative algebra can be reduced to the case when _R_ is local, making local rings a particularly deeply studied class of rings. The [residue field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Residue_field "Residue field") of _R_ is defined as

_k_ = _R_ / _m_.

Any _R_-module _M_ yields a _k_-vector space given by _M_ / _mM_. [Nakayama's lemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nakayama%27s_lemma "Nakayama's lemma") shows this passage is preserving important information: a finitely generated module _M_ is zero if and only if _M_ / _mM_ is zero.

### Regular local rings

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Commutative_ring&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Regular local rings")]

[![Image 152](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Node_%28algebraic_geometry%29.png/250px-Node_%28algebraic_geometry%29.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Node_(algebraic_geometry).png)

The [cubic plane curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubic_plane_curve "Cubic plane curve") (red) defined by the equation _y_ 2 = _x_ 2(_x_ + _1_) is [singular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singularity_(mathematics) "Singularity (mathematics)") at the origin, i.e., the ring _k_[_x_, _y_] / _y_ 2 − _x_ 2(_x_ + _1_), is not a regular ring. The tangent cone (blue) is a union of two lines, which also reflects the singularity.

The _k_-vector space _m_/_m_ 2 is an algebraic incarnation of the [cotangent space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotangent_space "Cotangent space"). Informally, the elements of _m_ can be thought of as functions which vanish at the point _p_, whereas _m_ 2 contains the ones which vanish with order at least 2. For any Noetherian local ring _R_, the inequality

dim _k_ _m_/_m_ 2 ≥ dim _R_

holds true, reflecting the idea that the cotangent (or equivalently the tangent) space has at least the dimension of the space Spec _R_. If equality holds true in this estimate, _R_ is called a [regular local ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_local_ring "Regular local ring"). A Noetherian local ring is regular if and only if the ring (which is the ring of functions on the [tangent cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_cone "Tangent cone")) ![Image 153: {\displaystyle \bigoplus _{n}m^{n}/m^{n+1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba1822a3035ac823ccbce488289985edeb7c3fc4) is isomorphic to a polynomial ring over _k_. Broadly speaking, regular local rings are somewhat similar to polynomial rings. Regular local rings are UFD's.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMatsumura1989%C2%A719,_Theorem_48-3)

[Discrete valuation rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_valuation_ring "Discrete valuation ring") are equipped with a function which assign an integer to any element _r_. This number, called the valuation of _r_ can be informally thought of as a zero or pole order of _r_. Discrete valuation rings are precisely the one-dimensional regular local rings. For example, the ring of germs of holomorphic functions on a [Riemann surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_surface "Riemann surface") is a discrete valuation ring.

### Complete intersections

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Commutative_ring&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Complete intersections")]

[![Image 154](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Twisted_cubic_curve.png/250px-Twisted_cubic_curve.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Twisted_cubic_curve.png)

The [twisted cubic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twisted_cubic "Twisted cubic") (green) is a set-theoretic complete intersection, but not a complete intersection.

By [Krull's principal ideal theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krull%27s_principal_ideal_theorem "Krull's principal ideal theorem"), a foundational result in the [dimension theory of rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_theory_(algebra) "Dimension theory (algebra)"), the dimension of

_R_ = _k_[_T_ 1, ..., _T_ _r_] / (_f_ 1, ..., _f_ _n_)

is at least _r_ − _n_. A ring _R_ is called a [complete intersection ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_intersection_ring "Complete intersection ring") if it can be presented in a way that attains this minimal bound. This notion is also mostly studied for local rings. Any regular local ring is a complete intersection ring, but not conversely.

A ring _R_ is a _set-theoretic_ complete intersection if the [reduced ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reduced_ring "Reduced ring") associated to _R_, i.e., the one obtained by dividing out all nilpotent elements, is a complete intersection. As of 2017, it is in general unknown, whether curves in three-dimensional space are set-theoretic complete intersections.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring#cite_note-FOOTNOTELyubeznik1989-4)

### Cohen–Macaulay rings

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Commutative_ring&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Cohen–Macaulay rings")]

The [depth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Depth_(ring_theory) "Depth (ring theory)") of a local ring _R_ is the number of elements in some (or, as can be shown, any) maximal regular sequence, i.e., a sequence _a_ 1, ..., _a_ _n_ ∈ _m_ such that all _a_ _i_ are non-zero divisors in

_R_ / (_a_ 1, ..., _a_ _i_−1).

For any local Noetherian ring, the inequality

depth(_R_) ≤ dim(_R_)

holds. A local ring in which equality takes place is called a [Cohen–Macaulay ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cohen%E2%80%93Macaulay_ring "Cohen–Macaulay ring"). Local complete intersection rings, and a fortiori, regular local rings are Cohen–Macaulay, but not conversely. Cohen–Macaulay combine desirable properties of regular rings (such as the property of being [universally catenary rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universally_catenary_ring "Universally catenary ring"), which means that the (co)dimension of primes is well-behaved), but are also more robust under taking quotients than regular local rings.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEisenbud1995Corollary_18.10,_Proposition_18.13-5)

Constructing commutative rings
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Commutative_ring&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Constructing commutative rings")]

There are several ways to construct new rings out of given ones. The aim of such constructions is often to improve certain properties of the ring so as to make it more readily understandable. For example, an integral domain that is [integrally closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_element#Equivalent_definitions "Integral element") in its [field of fractions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_of_fractions "Field of fractions") is called [normal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_ring "Normal ring"). This is a desirable property, for example any normal one-dimensional ring is necessarily [regular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_local_ring "Regular local ring"). Rendering[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] a ring normal is known as _normalization_.

If _I_ is an ideal in a commutative ring _R_, the powers of _I_ form [topological neighborhoods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighborhood_(topology) "Neighborhood (topology)") of _0_ which allow _R_ to be viewed as a [topological ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_ring "Topological ring"). This topology is called the [_I_-adic topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/I-adic_topology "I-adic topology"). _R_ can then be completed with respect to this topology. Formally, the _I_-adic completion is the [inverse limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_limit "Inverse limit") of the rings _R_/_I n_. For example, if _k_ is a field, _k_[[_X_]], the [formal power series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formal_power_series "Formal power series") ring in one variable over _k_, is the _I_-adic completion of _k_[_X_] where _I_ is the principal ideal generated by _X_. This ring serves as an algebraic analogue of the disk. Analogously, the ring of [_p_-adic integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P-adic_number "P-adic number") is the completion of **Z** with respect to the principal ideal (_p_). Any ring that is isomorphic to its own completion, is called [complete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_ring "Complete ring").

Complete local rings satisfy [Hensel's lemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hensel%27s_lemma "Hensel's lemma"), which roughly speaking allows extending solutions (of various problems) over the residue field _k_ to _R_.

Homological notions
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Commutative_ring&action=edit&section=26 "Edit section: Homological notions")]

Several deeper aspects of commutative rings have been studied using methods from [homological algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homological_algebra "Homological algebra"). [Hochster (2007)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring#CITEREFHochster2007) lists some open questions in this area of active research.

### Projective modules and Ext functors

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Commutative_ring&action=edit&section=27 "Edit section: Projective modules and Ext functors")]

Projective modules can be defined to be the [direct summands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_summand "Direct summand") of free modules. If _R_ is local, any finitely generated projective module is actually free, which gives content to an analogy between projective modules and [vector bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring#cite_note-6) The [Quillen–Suslin theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quillen%E2%80%93Suslin_theorem "Quillen–Suslin theorem") asserts that any finitely generated projective module over _k_[_T_ 1, ..., _T_ _n_] (_k_ a field) is free, but in general these two concepts differ. A local Noetherian ring is regular if and only if its [global dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_dimension "Global dimension") is finite, say _n_, which means that any finitely generated _R_-module has a [resolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resolution_(homological_algebra) "Resolution (homological algebra)") by projective modules of length at most _n_.

The proof of this and other related statements relies on the usage of homological methods, such as the [Ext functor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ext_functor "Ext functor"). This functor is the [derived functor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derived_functor "Derived functor") of the functor

Hom _R_(_M_, −).

The latter functor is exact if _M_ is projective, but not otherwise: for a surjective map _E_ → _F_ of _R_-modules, a map _M_ → _F_ need not extend to a map _M_ → _E_. The higher Ext functors measure the non-exactness of the Hom-functor. The importance of this standard construction in homological algebra stems can be seen from the fact that a local Noetherian ring _R_ with residue field _k_ is regular if and only if

Ext _n_(_k_, _k_)

vanishes for all large enough _n_. Moreover, the dimensions of these Ext-groups, known as [Betti numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Betti_number "Betti number"), grow polynomially in _n_ if and only if _R_ is a [local complete intersection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_complete_intersection "Local complete intersection") ring.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChristensenStriuliVeliche2010-7) A key argument in such considerations is the [Koszul complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koszul_complex "Koszul complex"), which provides an explicit free resolution of the residue field _k_ of a local ring _R_ in terms of a regular sequence.

The [tensor product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product "Tensor product") is another non-exact functor relevant in the context of commutative rings: for a general _R_-module _M_, the functor

_M_ ⊗_R_ −

is only right exact. If it is exact, _M_ is called [flat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_module "Flat module"). If _R_ is local, any finitely presented flat module is free of finite rank, thus projective. Despite being defined in terms of homological algebra, flatness has profound geometric implications. For example, if an _R_-algebra _S_ is flat, the dimensions of the fibers

_S_ / _pS_ = _S_ ⊗_R_ _R_ / _p_

(for prime ideals _p_ in _R_) have the "expected" dimension, namely dim _S_ − dim _R_ + dim(_R_ / _p_).

By [Wedderburn's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wedderburn%27s_little_theorem "Wedderburn's little theorem"), every finite [division ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_ring "Division ring") is commutative, and therefore a [finite field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field "Finite field"). Another condition ensuring commutativity of a ring, due to [Jacobson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nathan_Jacobson "Nathan Jacobson"), is the following: for every element _r_ of _R_ there exists an integer _n_> 1 such that _r_ _n_ = _r_.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJacobson1945-8) If, _r_ 2 = _r_ for every _r_, the ring is called [Boolean ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boolean_ring "Boolean ring"). More general conditions which guarantee commutativity of a ring are also known.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPinter-Lucke2007-9)

### Graded-commutative rings

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Commutative_ring&action=edit&section=31 "Edit section: Graded-commutative rings")]

[![Image 155](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Pair_of_pants.png/250px-Pair_of_pants.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pair_of_pants.png)

A [pair of pants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pair_of_pants_(mathematics) "Pair of pants (mathematics)") is a [cobordism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cobordism "Cobordism") between a circle and two disjoint circles. Cobordism classes, with the [cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product") as multiplication and [disjoint union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disjoint_union "Disjoint union") as the sum, form the [cobordism ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cobordism_ring "Cobordism ring").

A [graded ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graded_ring "Graded ring")_R_ = ⨁_i_∊**Z**_R_ _i_ is called [graded-commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graded-commutative_ring "Graded-commutative ring") if, for all homogeneous elements _a_ and _b_,

_ab_ = (−1)deg _a_ ⋅ deg _b_ _ba_.

If the _R_ _i_ are connected by differentials ∂ such that an abstract form of the [product rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_rule "Product rule") holds, i.e.,

∂(_ab_) = ∂(_a_)_b_ + (−1)deg _a_ a∂(_b_),

_R_ is called a [commutative differential graded algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_graded_algebra "Differential graded algebra") (cdga). An example is the complex of [differential forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form") on a [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold_(mathematics) "Manifold (mathematics)"), with the multiplication given by the [exterior product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_product "Exterior product"), is a cdga. The cohomology of a cdga is a graded-commutative ring, sometimes referred to as the [cohomology ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cohomology_ring "Cohomology ring"). A broad range examples of graded rings arises in this way. For example, the [Lazard ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lazard%27s_universal_ring "Lazard's universal ring") is the ring of cobordism classes of complex manifolds.

A graded-commutative ring with respect to a grading by **Z**/2 (as opposed to **Z**) is called a [superalgebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superalgebra "Superalgebra").

A related notion is an [almost commutative ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almost_commutative_ring "Almost commutative ring"), which means that _R_ is [filtered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filtration_(mathematics) "Filtration (mathematics)") in such a way that the associated graded ring

gr _R_:= ⨁ _F_ _i_ _R_ / ⨁ _F_ _i_−1 _R_

is commutative. An example is the [Weyl algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weyl_algebra "Weyl algebra") and more general rings of [differential operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_operator "Differential operator").

### Simplicial commutative rings

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Commutative_ring&action=edit&section=32 "Edit section: Simplicial commutative rings")]

A [simplicial commutative ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplicial_commutative_ring "Simplicial commutative ring") is a [simplicial object](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplicial_object "Simplicial object") in the category of commutative rings. They are building blocks for (connective) [derived algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derived_algebraic_geometry "Derived algebraic geometry"). A closely related but more general notion is that of [E∞-ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E-infinity_ring "E-infinity ring").
