In [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") and related areas of [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a [subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset")_A_ of a [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space")_X_ is said to be **dense** in _X_ if every point of _X_ either belongs to _A_ or else is arbitrarily "close" to a member of _A_ â€” for instance, the [rational numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number "Rational number") are a dense subset of the [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") because every real number either is a rational number or has a rational number arbitrarily close to it (see [Diophantine approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diophantine_approximation "Diophantine approximation")). ### Density in metric spaces

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dense_set&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Density in metric spaces")]

An alternative definition of dense set in the case of [metric spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space") is the following. When the [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology_(structure) "Topology (structure)") of ![Image 38: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is given by a [metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_(mathematics) "Metric (mathematics)"), the [closure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_closure "Topological closure")![Image 39: {\displaystyle {\overline {A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92efef0e89bdc77f6a848764195ef5b9d9bfcc6a) of ![Image 40: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) in ![Image 41: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is the [union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_(set_theory) "Union (set theory)") of ![Image 42: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) and the set of all [limits of sequences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_of_a_sequence#Topological_spaces "Limit of a sequence") of elements in ![Image 43: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) (its _limit points_), ![Image 44: {\displaystyle {\overline {A}}=A\cup \left\{\lim _{n\to \infty }a_{n}:a_{n}\in A{\text{ for all }}n\in \mathbb {N} \right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/03b7365796b309a17aed6826157c185a7de44baa)

Then ![Image 45: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) is dense in ![Image 46: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) if ![Image 47: {\displaystyle {\overline {A}}=X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bc0e1ebaef0ac4f63dee909eab22e5a78223e31)

If ![Image 48: {\displaystyle \left\{U_{n}\right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c6338d5f9a99854ba89c1731718aa373116da5f) is a sequence of dense [open](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set") sets in a complete metric space, ![Image 49: {\displaystyle X,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/09ba32eeb405f7f5f2bac1eb12987c47d2fd42df) then ![Image 50: {\textstyle \bigcap _{n=1}^{\infty }U_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2566b2f5d87a545d28cdc216362d19f81b272c71) is also dense in ![Image 51: {\displaystyle X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ba76c5a460c4a0bb1639a193bc1830f0a773e03) This fact is one of the equivalent forms of the [Baire category theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baire_category_theorem "Baire category theorem").

The [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") with the usual topology have the [rational numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number "Rational number") as a [countable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Countable_set "Countable set") dense subset which shows that the [cardinality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality "Cardinality") of a dense subset of a topological space may be strictly smaller than the cardinality of the space itself. The [irrational numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irrational_number "Irrational number") are another dense subset which shows that a topological space may have several [disjoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disjoint_sets "Disjoint sets") dense subsets (in particular, two dense subsets may be each other's complements), and they need not even be of the same cardinality. Perhaps even more surprisingly, both the rationals and the irrationals have empty interiors, showing that dense sets need not contain any non-empty open set. The intersection of two dense open subsets of a topological space is again dense and open.[[proof 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dense_set#cite_note-2) The [empty set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empty_set "Empty set") is a dense subset of itself. But every dense subset of a non-empty space must also be non-empty.

By the [Weierstrass approximation theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weierstrass_approximation_theorem "Weierstrass approximation theorem"), any given [complex-valued](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number")[continuous function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function") defined on a [closed interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_interval "Closed interval")![Image 52: {\displaystyle [a,b]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c4b788fc5c637e26ee98b45f89a5c08c85f7935) can be [uniformly approximated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_convergence "Uniform convergence") as closely as desired by a [polynomial function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_function "Polynomial function"). In other words, the polynomial functions are dense in the space ![Image 53: {\displaystyle C[a,b]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7c1625217aad8c105c50c975599e45192b2bfbec) of continuous complex-valued functions on the interval ![Image 54: {\displaystyle [a,b],}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d493b840f8326ba81ff9d95b4edf1effd5f2842) equipped with the [supremum norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supremum_norm "Supremum norm").

Every [metric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space") is dense in its [completion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Completion_(metric_space) "Completion (metric space)").

Every [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space") is a dense subset of itself. For a set ![Image 55: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) equipped with the [discrete topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_topology "Discrete topology"), the whole space is the only dense subset. Every non-empty subset of a set ![Image 56: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) equipped with the [trivial topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trivial_topology "Trivial topology") is dense, and every topology for which every non-empty subset is dense must be trivial.

Denseness is [transitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transitive_relation "Transitive relation"): Given three subsets ![Image 57: {\displaystyle A,B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/96c3298ea9aa77c226be56a7d8515baaa517b90b) and ![Image 58: {\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029) of a topological space ![Image 59: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) with ![Image 60: {\displaystyle A\subseteq B\subseteq C\subseteq X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bbdf2c4a99cbe382ba3abf288b9c294603d135a2) such that ![Image 61: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) is dense in ![Image 62: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) and ![Image 63: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) is dense in ![Image 64: {\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029) (in the respective [subspace topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subspace_topology "Subspace topology")) then ![Image 65: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) is also dense in ![Image 66: {\displaystyle C.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/067be67e68f60c53ce83241748d0d6249675c58d)

The [image](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_(mathematics) "Image (mathematics)") of a dense subset under a [surjective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surjective_function "Surjective function")[continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function_(topology) "Continuous function (topology)") function is again dense. The density of a topological space (the least of the [cardinalities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality "Cardinality") of its dense subsets) is a [topological invariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_invariant "Topological invariant").

A topological space with a [connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connected_space "Connected space") dense subset is necessarily connected itself.

Continuous functions into [Hausdorff spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_space "Hausdorff space") are determined by their values on dense subsets: if two continuous functions ![Image 67: {\displaystyle f,g:X\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a2979ff5d39cd2f0db5eda37f7ec54baae4c9909) into a [Hausdorff space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_space "Hausdorff space")![Image 68: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) agree on a dense subset of ![Image 69: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) then they agree on all of ![Image 70: {\displaystyle X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ba76c5a460c4a0bb1639a193bc1830f0a773e03)

For metric spaces there are universal spaces, into which all spaces of given density can be [embedded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embedding "Embedding"): a metric space of density ![Image 71: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) is isometric to a subspace of ![Image 72: {\displaystyle C\left([0,1]^{\alpha },\mathbb {R} \right),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/809452f4d7ae5513ace8ea8b9f7045218657f271) the space of real continuous functions on the [product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product#Infinite_products "Cartesian product") of ![Image 73: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) copies of the [unit interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_interval "Unit interval"). A point ![Image 74: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) of a subset ![Image 75: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) of a topological space ![Image 76: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is called a [limit point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_point "Limit point") of ![Image 77: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) (in ![Image 78: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab)) if every neighbourhood of ![Image 79: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) also contains a point of ![Image 80: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) other than ![Image 81: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) itself, and an [isolated point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isolated_point "Isolated point") of ![Image 82: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) otherwise. A subset without isolated points is said to be [dense-in-itself](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dense-in-itself "Dense-in-itself").

A subset ![Image 83: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) of a topological space ![Image 84: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is called [nowhere dense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nowhere_dense_set "Nowhere dense set") (in ![Image 85: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab)) if there is no neighborhood in ![Image 86: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) on which ![Image 87: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) is dense. Equivalently, a subset of a topological space is nowhere dense [if and only if](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If_and_only_if "If and only if") the interior of its closure is empty. The interior of the complement of a nowhere dense set is always dense. The complement of a closed nowhere dense set is a dense open set. Given a topological space ![Image 88: {\displaystyle X,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/09ba32eeb405f7f5f2bac1eb12987c47d2fd42df) a subset ![Image 89: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) of ![Image 90: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) that can be expressed as the union of countably many nowhere dense subsets of ![Image 91: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is called [meagre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meagre_set "Meagre set"). The rational numbers, while dense in the real numbers, are meagre as a subset of the reals.

A topological space with a countable dense subset is called [separable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separable_space "Separable space"). A topological space is a [Baire space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baire_space "Baire space") if and only if the intersection of countably many dense open sets is always dense. A topological space is called [resolvable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resolvable_space "Resolvable space") if it is the union of two disjoint dense subsets. More generally, a topological space is called Îº-resolvable for a [cardinal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinal_number "Cardinal number") Îº if it contains Îº pairwise disjoint dense sets.
