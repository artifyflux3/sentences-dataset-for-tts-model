The **number density** (symbol: _n_ or _ρ_ N) is an [intensive quantity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intensive_quantity "Intensive quantity") used to describe the degree of [concentration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concentration "Concentration") of [countable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Countable "Countable") objects ([particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle"), [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecules "Molecules"), [phonons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phonon "Phonon"), [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(biology) "Cell (biology)"), [galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy"), etc.) in physical space: [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space")**volumetric number density**, [two-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(mathematics) "Plane (mathematics)")**areal number density**, or [one-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-dimensional_space "One-dimensional space")**linear number density**. _[Population density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population\_density "Population density")_ is an example of areal number density. The term **number concentration** (symbol: [lowercase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lowercase "Lowercase")_n_, or _C_, to avoid confusion with [amount of substance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amount_of_substance "Amount of substance") indicated by [uppercase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uppercase "Uppercase")_[N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Letter\_N "Letter N")_) is sometimes used in chemistry for the same quantity, particularly when comparing with other [concentrations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concentration "Concentration").

Volume number density is the number of specified objects per unit [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume"):[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_density#cite_note-1)![Image 1: {\displaystyle n={\frac {N}{V}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/634a08a3807ca46766f55801fed39291af431db1) where _N_ is the total number of objects in a volume _V_.

Here it is assumed[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_density#cite_note-2) that _N_ is large enough that [rounding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rounding "Rounding") of the count to the nearest [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer") does not introduce much of an [error](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_error "Random error"), however _V_ is chosen to be small enough that the resulting _n_ does not depend much on the [size](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume") or [shape](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape "Shape") of the volume _V_ because of large-scale features.

Area number density is the number of specified objects per unit [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area"), _A_: ![Image 2: {\displaystyle n'={\frac {N}{A}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/534e3fabc6529be26a11051d00db5851c9cb5efd) Similarly, linear number density is the number of specified objects per unit [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length "Length"), _L_: ![Image 3: {\displaystyle n''={\frac {N}{L}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c947d04ee2aedab3f2efdc32070fd2fba51621d)

_Column number density_ is a kind of areal density, the number or count of a substance per unit area, obtained integrating volumetric number density along a vertical path: ![Image 4: {\displaystyle n'_{c}=\int n\,\mathrm {d} s.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ac343f5ee65d0527acffd54202cedc0613bc3e4e) It's related to [column mass density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Column_mass_density "Column mass density"), with the volumetric number density replaced by the volume mass density.

In [SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") units, number density is measured in m−3, although cm−3 is often used. However, these units are not quite practical when dealing with atoms or molecules of [gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas "Gas"), [liquids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid "Liquid") or [solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid "Solid") at [room temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Room_temperature "Room temperature") and [atmospheric pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure "Atmospheric pressure"), because the resulting numbers are extremely large (on the order of 10 20). Using the number density of an [ideal gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas "Ideal gas") at 0 [°C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celsius "Celsius") and 1 [atm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit) "Atmosphere (unit)") as a [yardstick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yardstick "Yardstick"): [_n_ 0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loschmidt_constant "Loschmidt constant") = 1 [amg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amagat "Amagat") = 2.686 7774 × 10 25 m−3 is often introduced as a unit of number density, for any substances at any conditions (not necessarily limited to an ideal gas at 0 °C and 1 atm).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_density#cite_note-3)

Using the number density as a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") of [spatial coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system"), the total number of objects _N_ in the entire volume _V_ can be calculated as ![Image 5: {\displaystyle N=\iiint _{V}n(x,\,y,\,z)\,\mathrm {d} V,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ee2e6cbd4fa947c4e72bc9e2e3689339acddb65) where d _V_ = d _x_ d _y_ d _z_ is a volume element. If each object possesses the same [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass")_m_ 0, the total mass _m_ of all the objects in the volume _V_ can be expressed as ![Image 6: {\displaystyle m=\iiint _{V}m_{0}n(x,\,y,\,z)\,\mathrm {d} V.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f46376c1c7c73a95a3c48663342414beeff293cc)

Similar expressions are valid for [electric charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge") or any other [extensive quantity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extensive_quantity "Extensive quantity") associated with countable objects. For example, replacing _m_ with _q_ (total charge) and _m_ 0 with _q_ 0 (charge of each object) in the above equation will lead to a correct expression for charge.

The number density of [solute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solute "Solute") molecules in a [solvent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvent "Solvent") is sometimes called [concentration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concentration "Concentration"), although usually concentration is expressed as a number of [moles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mole_(unit) "Mole (unit)") per unit volume (and thus called [molar concentration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molar_concentration "Molar concentration")).

Relation to other quantities
----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Number_density&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Relation to other quantities")]

### Molar concentration

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Number_density&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Molar concentration")]

For any substance, the number density can be expressed in terms of its [amount concentration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amount_concentration "Amount concentration")_c_ (in [mol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mole_(unit) "Mole (unit)")/m 3) as ![Image 7: {\displaystyle n=N_{\rm {A}}c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2bad59373a11d5831c2854205a4d6c8b5bfd6acb) where _N_ A is the [Avogadro constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avogadro_constant "Avogadro constant"). This is still true if the [spatial dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spatial_dimension "Spatial dimension") unit, metre, in both _n_ and _c_ is consistently replaced by any other spatial dimension unit, e.g. if _n_ is in cm−3 and _c_ is in mol/cm 3, or if _n_ is in [L](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Litre "Litre")−1 and _c_ is in mol/L, etc.

For [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom") or [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule") of a well-defined [molar mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molar_mass "Molar mass")_M_ (in [kg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram "Kilogram")/mol), the number density can sometimes be expressed in terms of their [mass density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_density "Mass density")_ρ_ m (in kg/m 3) as ![Image 8: {\displaystyle n={\frac {N_{\rm {A}}}{M}}\rho _{\mathrm {m} }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f66b623054734347a239ad338f5a95450ff65dca) Note that the ratio _M_/_N_ A is the mass of a single atom or molecule in kg.
