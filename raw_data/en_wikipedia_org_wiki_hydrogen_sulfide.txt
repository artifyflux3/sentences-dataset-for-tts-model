**Hydrogen sulfide** is a [chemical compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_compound "Chemical compound") with the [formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")[H](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen")2[S](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur "Sulfur"). It is a colorless [chalcogen-hydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chalcogen_hydride "Chalcogen hydride")[gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas "Gas"), and is toxic, corrosive, and flammable. Trace amounts in ambient atmosphere have a characteristic foul odor of rotten eggs.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-11) Swedish chemist [Carl Wilhelm Scheele](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Scheele "Carl Wilhelm Scheele") is credited with having discovered the chemical composition of purified hydrogen sulfide in 1777.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-americansci-12)

Hydrogen sulfide is toxic to humans and most other animals by inhibiting [cellular respiration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellular_respiration "Cellular respiration") in a manner similar to [hydrogen cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide "Hydrogen cyanide"). When it is inhaled or its salts are ingested in high amounts, damage to organs occurs rapidly with symptoms ranging from breathing difficulties to convulsions and death.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-13)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-14) Despite this, the [human body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_body "Human body") produces small amounts of this sulfide and its mineral salts, and uses it as a [signalling molecule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signaling_molecule "Signaling molecule").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-15)

Hydrogen sulfide is often produced from the [microbial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prokaryotic "Prokaryotic") breakdown of organic matter in the absence of oxygen, such as in swamps and sewers; this process is commonly known as [anaerobic digestion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion "Anaerobic digestion"), which is done by [sulfate-reducing microorganisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfate-reducing_microorganisms "Sulfate-reducing microorganisms"). It also occurs in [volcanic gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcanic_gas "Volcanic gas"), natural gas deposits, and sometimes in well-drawn water.

Hydrogen sulfide is slightly denser than air. A mixture of H 2 S and air can be explosive.

In general, hydrogen sulfide acts as a [reducing agent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reducing_agent "Reducing agent"), as indicated by its ability to reduce sulfur dioxide in the [Claus process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claus_process "Claus process"). Hydrogen sulfide burns in oxygen with a blue flame to form [sulfur dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide "Sulfur dioxide") (SO 2) and [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_(molecule) "Water (molecule)"):

2 H 2 S + 3 O 2 → 2 SO 2 + 2 H 2 O
If an excess of oxygen is present, [sulfur trioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_trioxide "Sulfur trioxide") (SO 3) is formed, which quickly hydrates to [sulfuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid"):

H 2 S + 2 O 2 → H 2 SO 4

### Acid-base properties

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_sulfide&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Acid-base properties")]

It is slightly soluble in water and acts as a [weak acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_acid "Weak acid") ([p _K_ a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid_dissociation_constant "Acid dissociation constant")=6.9 in 0.01–0.1 mol/litre solutions at 18°C), giving the [hydrosulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrosulfide "Hydrosulfide") ion HS−. Hydrogen sulfide and its solutions are colorless. When exposed to air, it slowly oxidizes to form elemental sulfur, which is not soluble in water. The [sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfide "Sulfide") anion S 2− is not formed in aqueous solution.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-16)

H 2 S and H 2 O exchange protons rapidly. This behavior is the basis of technologies for the purification of deuterium oxide ("heavy water" or D 2 O), which exploits the easy distillation of these compounds.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-17)

### Extreme temperatures and pressures

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_sulfide&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Extreme temperatures and pressures")]

At pressures above 90 GPa ([gigapascal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gigapascal "Gigapascal")), hydrogen sulfide becomes a metallic conductor of electricity. When cooled below a [critical temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superconductivity#Superconducting_phase_transition "Superconductivity") this high-pressure phase exhibits [superconductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superconductivity "Superconductivity"). The critical temperature increases with pressure, ranging from 23 K at 100 GPa to 150 K at 200 GPa.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Drozdov2014-18) If hydrogen sulfide is pressurized at higher temperatures, then cooled, the critical temperature reaches 203 K (−70°C), which was the highest accepted superconducting critical temperature until the discovery of [Lanthanum decahydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lanthanum_decahydride "Lanthanum decahydride") in 2019. By substituting a small part of sulfur with phosphorus and using even higher pressures, it has been predicted that it may be possible to raise the critical temperature to above 0°C (273 K) and achieve [room-temperature superconductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Room-temperature_superconductor "Room-temperature superconductor").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-cart15-19)

Under atmospheric pressure and in the absence of a catalyst, hydrogen sulfide decomposes around 1200 °C into hydrogen and sulfur.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-20)

### Reactions with metals

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_sulfide&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Reactions with metals")]

Hydrogen sulfide reacts with metal ions to form metal sulfides, which are insoluble, often dark colored solids. This behavior is the basis of the use of hydrogen sulfide as a reagent in the [qualitative inorganic analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qualitative_inorganic_analysis "Qualitative inorganic analysis") of metal ions. In these analyses, heavy metal (and [nonmetal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonmetal_(chemistry) "Nonmetal (chemistry)")) ions (e.g., Pb(II), Cu(II), Hg(II), As(III)) are precipitated from solution upon exposure to H 2 S. The components of the resulting solid are then identified by their reactivity. [Lead(II) acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lead(II)_acetate "Lead(II) acetate") paper is used to detect hydrogen sulfide because it readily converts to [lead(II) sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lead(II)_sulfide "Lead(II) sulfide"), which is black.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-King-21)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-22)

Hydrogen sulfide is also responsible for [tarnishing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tarnish "Tarnish") on various metals including [copper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper "Copper") and [silver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silver "Silver"); the chemical responsible for black [toning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toning_(coin) "Toning (coin)") found on silver coins is [silver sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silver_sulfide "Silver sulfide") (Ag 2 S), which is produced when the silver on the surface of the coin reacts with atmospheric hydrogen sulfide.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-23) Coins that have been subject to toning by hydrogen sulfide and other sulfur-containing compounds may have the toning add to the numismatic value of a coin based on aesthetics, as the toning may produce [thin-film interference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thin-film_interference "Thin-film interference"), resulting in the coin taking on an attractive coloration.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-24) Coins can also be intentionally treated with hydrogen sulfide to induce toning, though artificial toning can be distinguished from natural toning, and is generally criticised among collectors.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-25)

Hydrogen sulfide is most commonly obtained by its separation from [sour gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sour_gas "Sour gas"), which is natural gas with a high content of H 2 S. It can also be produced by treating [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") with molten elemental sulfur at about 450°C. Hydrocarbons can serve as a source of hydrogen in this process.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-ullmann-26)

S + H 2 → H 2 S
The very favorable thermodynamics for the hydrogenation of sulfur implies that the dehydrogenation (or [cracking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cracking_(chemistry) "Cracking (chemistry)")) of hydrogen sulfide would require very high temperatures.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-27)

A standard lab preparation is to treat [ferrous sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron(II)_sulfide "Iron(II) sulfide") with a strong acid in a [Kipp generator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kipp_generator "Kipp generator"):

FeS + 2 HCl → FeCl 2 + H 2 S
For use in [qualitative inorganic analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qualitative_inorganic_analysis "Qualitative inorganic analysis"), [thioacetamide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thioacetamide "Thioacetamide") is used to generate H 2 S:

CH 3 C(S)NH 2 + H 2 O → CH 3 C(O)NH 2 + H 2 S
Many metal and nonmetal sulfides, e.g. [aluminium sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium_sulfide "Aluminium sulfide"), [phosphorus pentasulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_pentasulfide "Phosphorus pentasulfide"), [silicon disulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_disulfide "Silicon disulfide") liberate hydrogen sulfide upon exposure to water:[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-28)

6 H 2 O + Al 2 S 3 → 3 H 2 S + 2 Al(OH)3
This gas is also produced by heating sulfur with solid organic compounds and by reducing sulfurated organic compounds with hydrogen. It can also be produced by mixing ammonium thiocyanate to concentrated sulphuric acid and adding water to it.

Hydrogen sulfide can be generated in cells via enzymatic or non-enzymatic pathways. Three enzymes catalyze formation of H

2 S: [cystathionine γ-lyase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cystathionine_gamma-lyase "Cystathionine gamma-lyase") (CSE), [cystathionine β-synthetase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cystathionine_beta_synthase "Cystathionine beta synthase") (CBS), and [3-mercaptopyruvate sulfurtransferase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3-mercaptopyruvate_sulfurtransferase "3-mercaptopyruvate sulfurtransferase") (3-MST).[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Huang-2015-29) CBS and CSE are the main proponents of H 2 S biogenesis, which follows the trans-sulfuration pathway.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Kabil-2011-30) These enzymes have been identified in a breadth of biological cells and tissues, and their activity is induced by a number of disease states.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Kabil-2014-31) These enzymes are characterized by the transfer of a sulfur atom from methionine to serine to form a cysteine molecule.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Kabil-2011-30) 3-MST also contributes to hydrogen sulfide production by way of the cysteine catabolic pathway.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Kabil-2014-31)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Kabil-2011-30) Dietary amino acids, such as methionine and cysteine serve as the primary substrates for the transulfuration pathways and in the production of hydrogen sulfide. Hydrogen sulfide can also be derived from proteins such as [ferredoxins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferredoxin "Ferredoxin") and [Rieske proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rieske_protein "Rieske protein").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Kabil-2014-31)

[Sulfate-reducing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfate-reducing_bacteria "Sulfate-reducing bacteria") (resp. [sulfur-reducing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur-reducing_bacteria "Sulfur-reducing bacteria")) bacteria generate usable energy under low-oxygen conditions by using sulfates (resp. elemental sulfur) to [oxidize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redox "Redox") organic compounds or hydrogen; this produces hydrogen sulfide as a waste product.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-barton-32)

H 2 S in the body acts as a [gaseous signaling molecule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaseous_signaling_molecule "Gaseous signaling molecule") with implications for health and in diseases.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Huang-2015-29)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-33)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-powell-34)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-35)

Hydrogen sulfide is involved in [vasodilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vasodilation "Vasodilation") in animals, as well as in increasing seed germination and stress responses in plants.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Hancock-2017-36) Hydrogen sulfide signaling is moderated by [reactive oxygen species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reactive_oxygen_species "Reactive oxygen species") (ROS) and [reactive nitrogen species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reactive_nitrogen_species "Reactive nitrogen species") (RNS).[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Hancock-2017-36)H 2 S has been shown to interact with the NO pathway resulting in several different cellular effects, including the inhibition of cGMP phosphodiesterases,[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-37) as well as the formation of another signal called nitrosothiol.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Hancock-2017-36) Hydrogen sulfide is also known to increase the levels of glutathione, which acts to reduce or disrupt ROS levels in cells.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Hancock-2017-36)

The field of H 2 S biology has advanced from environmental toxicology to investigate the roles of endogenously produced H 2 S in physiological conditions and in various pathophysiological states.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-38)H 2 S has been implicated in cancer, in Down syndrome and in vascular disease.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-39)[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-40)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-41)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-42)

At lower concentrations, it stimulates mitochondrial function via multiple mechanisms including direct electron donation.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-43)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-44) However, at higher concentrations, it inhibits Complex IV of the mitochondrial electron transport chain, which effectively reduces ATP generation and biochemical activity within cells.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Hancock-2017-36)

### Production of sulfur

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_sulfide&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Production of sulfur")]

Hydrogen sulfide is mainly consumed as a precursor to elemental sulfur. This conversion, called the [Claus process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claus_process "Claus process"), involves partial oxidation to sulfur dioxide. The latter reacts with hydrogen sulfide to give elemental sulfur. The conversion is catalyzed by alumina.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-45)

2H 2 S + SO 2→ 3S + 2H 2 O

### Production of thioorganic compounds

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_sulfide&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Production of thioorganic compounds")]

Many fundamental [organosulfur compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organosulfur_compound "Organosulfur compound") are produced using hydrogen sulfide. These include [methanethiol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanethiol "Methanethiol"), [ethanethiol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanethiol "Ethanethiol"), and [thioglycolic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thioglycolic_acid "Thioglycolic acid").[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-ullmann-26) Hydrosulfides can be used in the production of [thiophenol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thiophenol "Thiophenol").[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-46)

### Production of metal sulfides

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_sulfide&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Production of metal sulfides")]

Upon combining with [alkali metal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkali_metal "Alkali metal") bases, hydrogen sulfide converts to alkali hydrosulfides such as [sodium hydrosulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_hydrosulfide "Sodium hydrosulfide") and [sodium sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_sulfide "Sodium sulfide"):

H 2 S + NaOH → NaSH + H 2 O NaSH + NaOH → Na 2 S + H 2 O
Sodium sulfides are used in the [paper making](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paper_making "Paper making") industry. Specifically, salts of SH− break bonds between lignin and cellulose components of [pulp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulp_(paper) "Pulp (paper)") in the [Kraft process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kraft_process "Kraft process").[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-ullmann-26)

As indicated above, many metal ions react with hydrogen sulfide to give the corresponding metal sulfides. Oxidic ores are sometimes treated with hydrogen sulfide to give the corresponding metal sulfides which are more readily purified by [flotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flotation_process "Flotation process").Metal parts are sometimes [passivated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Passivation_(chemistry) "Passivation (chemistry)") with hydrogen sulfide. Catalysts used in [hydrodesulfurization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrodesulfurization "Hydrodesulfurization") are routinely activated with hydrogen sulfide.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-ullmann-26)

[![Image 21](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Deposit_from_hydrogen_sulphide.jpg/250px-Deposit_from_hydrogen_sulphide.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Deposit_from_hydrogen_sulphide.jpg)

Deposit of [sulfur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur "Sulfur") on a rock, caused by [volcanic gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcanic_gas "Volcanic gas")

[Volcanoes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcano "Volcano") and some [hot springs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hot_spring "Hot spring") (as well as [cold springs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mineral_spring "Mineral spring")) emit some H 2 S. Hydrogen sulfide can be present naturally in well water, often as a result of the action of [sulfate-reducing bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfate-reducing_bacteria "Sulfate-reducing bacteria").[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-47)[_[better source needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS "Wikipedia:NOTRS")_] Hydrogen sulfide is produced by the human body in small quantities through bacterial breakdown of proteins containing sulfur in the intestinal tract; it therefore contributes to the characteristic odor of flatulence. It is also produced in the mouth ([halitosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halitosis "Halitosis")).[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-ATSDR-48)

A portion of global H 2 S emissions are due to human activity. By far the largest industrial source of H 2 S is [petroleum refineries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_refinery "Oil refinery"): The [hydrodesulfurization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrodesulfurization "Hydrodesulfurization") process liberates sulfur from [petroleum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petroleum "Petroleum") by the action of hydrogen. The resulting H 2 S is converted to elemental sulfur by partial combustion via the [Claus process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claus_process "Claus process"), which is a major source of elemental sulfur. Other anthropogenic sources of hydrogen sulfide include [coke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coke_(fuel) "Coke (fuel)") ovens, [paper mills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paper_mill "Paper mill") (using the Kraft process), tanneries and [sewerage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sewerage "Sewerage"). H 2 S arises from virtually anywhere where elemental sulfur comes in contact with organic material, especially at high temperatures. Depending on environmental conditions, it is responsible for deterioration of material through the action of some sulfur oxidizing microorganisms. It is called [biogenic sulfide corrosion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biogenic_sulfide_corrosion "Biogenic sulfide corrosion").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

In 2011 it was reported that increased concentrations of H 2 S were observed in the [Bakken formation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bakken_formation "Bakken formation") crude, possibly due to oil field practices, and presented challenges such as "health and environmental risks, corrosion of wellbore, added expense with regard to materials handling and pipeline equipment, and additional refinement requirements".[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-49)

Besides living near gas and oil drilling operations, ordinary citizens can be exposed to hydrogen sulfide by being near [waste water treatment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sewage_treatment "Sewage treatment") facilities, [landfills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landfill "Landfill") and farms with manure storage. Exposure occurs through breathing contaminated air or drinking contaminated water.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-50)

In [municipal waste landfill sites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landfill "Landfill"), the burial of [organic material](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_matter "Organic matter") rapidly leads to the production of [anaerobic digestion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion "Anaerobic digestion") within the waste mass and, with the humid atmosphere and relatively high temperature that accompanies [biodegradation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodegradation "Biodegradation"), [biogas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biogas "Biogas") is produced as soon as the air within the waste mass has been reduced. If there is a source of sulfate bearing material, such as plasterboard or natural [gypsum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gypsum "Gypsum") (calcium sulfate dihydrate), under anaerobic conditions [sulfate reducing bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfate-reducing_microorganisms "Sulfate-reducing microorganisms") converts this to hydrogen sulfide. These bacteria cannot survive in air but the moist, warm, anaerobic conditions of buried waste that contains a high source of carbon – in inert landfills, paper and glue used in the fabrication of products such as [plasterboard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drywall "Drywall") can provide a rich source of carbon[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-51) – is an excellent environment for the formation of hydrogen sulfide.

In industrial anaerobic digestion processes, such as [waste water treatment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wastewater_treatment "Wastewater treatment") or the [digestion of organic waste from agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion "Anaerobic digestion"), hydrogen sulfide can be formed from the reduction of sulfate and the degradation of amino acids and proteins within organic compounds.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-52) Sulfates are relatively non-inhibitory to [methane forming bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanobacteria "Methanobacteria") but can be reduced to H 2 S by [sulfate reducing bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfate-reducing_microorganisms "Sulfate-reducing microorganisms"), of which there are several genera.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-53)

A number of processes have been designed to remove hydrogen sulfide from [drinking water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drinking_water "Drinking water").[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-54)

Continuous chlorination For levels up to 75 mg/L [chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine") is used in the purification process as an oxidizing chemical to react with hydrogen sulfide. This reaction yields insoluble solid sulfur. Usually the chlorine used is in the form of [sodium hypochlorite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_hypochlorite "Sodium hypochlorite").[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-55)Aeration For concentrations of hydrogen sulfide less than 2 mg/L [aeration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aeration "Aeration") is an ideal treatment process. Oxygen is added to water and a reaction between oxygen and hydrogen sulfide react to produce odorless sulfate.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-56)

### Removal from fuel gases

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_sulfide&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Removal from fuel gases")]

Hydrogen sulfide is commonly found in raw natural gas and biogas. It is typically removed by [amine gas treating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amine_gas_treating "Amine gas treating") technologies. In such processes, the hydrogen sulfide is first converted to an ammonium salt, whereas the natural gas is unaffected.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Kohl-57)[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Gary-58)

RNH 2 + H 2 S ⇌ [RNH 3]+ + SH−
The bisulfide anion is subsequently regenerated by heating of the amine sulfide solution. Hydrogen sulfide generated in this process is typically converted to elemental sulfur using the [Claus Process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claus_Process "Claus Process").

[![Image 22](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f9/AmineTreating.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:AmineTreating.png)

[Process flow diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Process_flow_diagram "Process flow diagram") of a typical amine treating process used in petroleum refineries, natural gas processing plants and other industrial facilities

The underground mine gas term for foul-smelling hydrogen sulfide-rich gas mixtures is _stinkdamp_. Hydrogen sulfide is a highly [toxic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxic "Toxic") and flammable gas ([flammable range](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flammability_limit "Flammability limit"): 4.3–46%). It can poison several systems in the body, although the [nervous system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nervous_system "Nervous system") is most affected.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] The toxicity of H 2 S is comparable with that of [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide").[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-pmid23111938-59) It binds with [iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron "Iron") in the [mitochondrial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mitochondria "Mitochondria")[cytochrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytochrome "Cytochrome")[enzymes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enzyme "Enzyme"), thus preventing [cellular respiration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellular_respiration "Cellular respiration"). Its toxic properties were described in detail in 1843 by [Justus von Liebig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Justus_von_Liebig "Justus von Liebig").[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-60)

Even before hydrogen sulfide was discovered, Italian physician [Bernardino Ramazzini](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernardino_Ramazzini "Bernardino Ramazzini") hypothesized in his 1713 book [De Morbis Artificum Diatriba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Morbis_Artificum_Diatriba "De Morbis Artificum Diatriba") that occupational diseases of sewer-workers and blackening of coins in their clothes may be caused by an unknown invisible volatile acid (moreover, in late 18th century toxic gas emanation from [Paris sewers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris_sewers "Paris sewers") became a problem for the citizens and authorities).[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-61)

Although very pungent at first (it smells like rotten eggs[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-62)), it quickly deadens the sense of smell, creating temporary [anosmia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anosmia "Anosmia"),[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-63) so victims may be unaware of its presence until it is too late. Safe handling procedures are provided by its [safety data sheet (SDS)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MSDS "MSDS").[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-64)

Since hydrogen sulfide occurs naturally in the body, the environment, and the gut, enzymes exist to metabolize it. At some threshold level, believed to average around 300–350 ppm, the oxidative enzymes become overwhelmed. Many personal safety gas detectors, such as those used by utility, sewage and petrochemical workers, are set to alarm at as low as 5 to 10 ppm and to go into high alarm at 15 ppm. Metabolism causes oxidation to sulfate, which is harmless.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-65) Hence, low levels of hydrogen sulfide may be tolerated indefinitely.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Exposure to lower concentrations can result in [eye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_eye "Human eye") irritation, a sore throat and [cough](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cough "Cough"), nausea, shortness of breath, and [fluid in the lungs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_edema "Pulmonary edema").[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-pmid23111938-59) These effects are believed to be due to hydrogen sulfide combining with [alkali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkali "Alkali") present in moist surface tissues to form [sodium sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_sulfide "Sodium sulfide"), a [caustic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corrosive_substance "Corrosive substance").[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-66) These symptoms usually subside in a few weeks.

Long-term, low-level exposure may result in [fatigue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatigue_(physical) "Fatigue (physical)"), loss of appetite, [headaches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Headache "Headache"), irritability, poor memory, and [dizziness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dizziness "Dizziness"). Chronic exposure to low level H 2 S (around 2 [ppm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts_per_million "Parts per million")) has been implicated in increased miscarriage and reproductive health issues among Russian and Finnish wood pulp workers,[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-67) but the reports have not (as of 1995) been replicated.

### High-level exposure

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_sulfide&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: High-level exposure")]

Short-term, high-level exposure can induce immediate collapse, with loss of breathing and a high probability of death. If death does not occur, high exposure to hydrogen sulfide can lead to [cortical pseudolaminar necrosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cortical_pseudolaminar_necrosis "Cortical pseudolaminar necrosis"), degeneration of the [basal ganglia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basal_ganglia "Basal ganglia") and [cerebral edema](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cerebral_edema "Cerebral edema").[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-pmid23111938-59) Although respiratory paralysis may be immediate, it can also be delayed up to 72 hours.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-68)

Inhalation of H 2 S resulted in about 7 workplace deaths per year in the U.S. (2011–2017 data), second only to carbon monoxide (17 deaths per year) for workplace chemical inhalation deaths.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-%22BLS_2019%22-69)

#### Exposure thresholds

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_sulfide&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Exposure thresholds")]

*   Exposure limits stipulated by the United States government:[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-70)
    *   10 [ppm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts_per_million "Parts per million")[REL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recommended_exposure_limit "Recommended exposure limit")-Ceiling ([NIOSH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health "National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health")): recommended permissible exposure ceiling (the recommended level that must not be exceeded, except once for 10 min. in an 8-hour shift, if no other measurable exposure occurs)
    *   20 ppm [PEL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit "Permissible exposure limit")-Ceiling ([OSHA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration "Occupational Safety and Health Administration")): permissible exposure ceiling (the level that must not be exceeded, except once for 10 min. in an 8-hour shift, if no other measurable exposure occurs)
    *   50 ppm PEL-Peak (OSHA): peak permissible exposure (the level that must never be exceeded)
    *   100 ppm [IDLH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immediately_dangerous_to_life_or_health "Immediately dangerous to life or health") (NIOSH): immediately dangerous to life and health (the level that interferes with the ability to escape)

*   0.00047 ppm or 0.47[ppb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts_per_billion "Parts per billion") is the odor threshold, the point at which 50% of a human panel can detect the presence of an odor without being able to identify it.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-IA_State-71)
*   10–20 ppm is the borderline concentration for eye irritation.
*   50–100 ppm leads to eye damage.
*   At 100–150 ppm the [olfactory nerve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olfactory_nerve "Olfactory nerve") is paralyzed after a few inhalations, and the [sense of smell disappears](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anosmia "Anosmia"), often together with awareness of danger.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-72)[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-73)
*   320–530 ppm leads to [pulmonary edema](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_edema "Pulmonary edema") with the possibility of death.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-pmid23111938-59)
*   530–1000 ppm causes strong stimulation of the [central nervous system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_nervous_system "Central nervous system") and rapid breathing, leading to loss of breathing.
*   800 ppm is the lethal concentration for 50% of humans for 5 minutes' exposure ([LC50](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LC50 "LC50")).
*   Concentrations over 1000 ppm cause immediate collapse with loss of breathing, even after inhalation of a single breath.

Treatment involves immediate inhalation of [amyl nitrite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amyl_nitrite "Amyl nitrite"), injections of [sodium nitrite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_nitrite "Sodium nitrite"), or administration of [4-dimethylaminophenol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-dimethylaminophenol "4-dimethylaminophenol") in combination with inhalation of pure oxygen, administration of [bronchodilators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronchodilator "Bronchodilator") to overcome eventual [bronchospasm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronchospasm "Bronchospasm"), and in some cases [hyperbaric oxygen therapy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbaric_oxygen_therapy "Hyperbaric oxygen therapy") (HBOT).[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-pmid23111938-59) HBOT has clinical and anecdotal support.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-74)[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-75)[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-76)

Hydrogen sulfide was used by the [British Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Army "British Army") as a [chemical weapon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_weapon "Chemical weapon") during [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I"). It was not considered to be an ideal war gas, partially due to its flammability and because the distinctive smell could be detected from even a small leak, alerting the enemy to the presence of the gas. It was nevertheless used on two occasions in 1916 when other gases were in short supply.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-77)

On September 2, 2005, a leak in the propeller room of a [Royal Caribbean Cruise Liner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Caribbean_International "Royal Caribbean International") docked in [Los Angeles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Los_Angeles "Los Angeles") resulted in the deaths of 3 crewmen due to a [sewage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sewage "Sewage") line leak. As a result, all such compartments are now required to have a ventilation system.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-78)[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-79)

A dump of toxic waste containing hydrogen sulfide is believed to have caused 17 deaths and thousands of illnesses in [Abidjan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abidjan "Abidjan"), on the [West African](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/West_Africa "West Africa") coast, in the [2006 Côte d'Ivoire toxic waste dump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2006_C%C3%B4te_d%27Ivoire_toxic_waste_dump "2006 Côte d'Ivoire toxic waste dump").[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-80)

In September 2008, three workers were killed and two suffered serious injury, including long term brain damage, at a mushroom growing company in [Langley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Langley,_British_Columbia_(city) "Langley, British Columbia (city)"), [British Columbia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Columbia "British Columbia"). A valve to a pipe that carried chicken [manure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manure "Manure"), [straw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Straw "Straw") and [gypsum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gypsum "Gypsum") to the compost fuel for the mushroom growing operation became clogged, and as workers unclogged the valve in a confined space without proper ventilation the hydrogen sulfide that had built up due to anaerobic decomposition of the material was released, poisoning the workers in the surrounding area.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-81) An investigator said there could have been more fatalities if the pipe had been fully cleared and/or if the wind had changed directions.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-82)

In 2014, levels of hydrogen sulfide as high as 83 ppm were detected at a recently built mall in [Thailand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thailand "Thailand") called Siam Square One at the [Siam Square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siam_Square "Siam Square") area. Shop tenants at the mall reported health complications such as sinus inflammation, breathing difficulties and eye irritation. After investigation it was determined that the large amount of gas originated from imperfect treatment and disposal of waste water in the building.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-83)

In 2014, hydrogen sulfide gas killed workers at the Promenade shopping center in North [Scottsdale, Arizona](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scottsdale,_Arizona "Scottsdale, Arizona"), USA [[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-84) after climbing into 15 ft deep chamber without wearing [personal protective gear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Personal_protective_equipment "Personal protective equipment"). "Arriving crews recorded high levels of [hydrogen cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide "Hydrogen cyanide") and hydrogen sulfide coming out of the sewer."

In November 2014, a substantial amount of hydrogen sulfide gas shrouded the central, eastern and southeastern parts of [Moscow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moscow "Moscow"). Residents living in the area were urged to stay indoors by the emergencies ministry. Although the exact source of the gas was not known, blame had been placed on a Moscow oil refinery.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-85)

In June 2016, a mother and her daughter were found dead in their still-running 2006 [Porsche Cayenne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Porsche_Cayenne "Porsche Cayenne")[SUV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SUV "SUV") against a guardrail on [Florida's Turnpike](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Florida%27s_Turnpike#Ft._Pierce_to_Wildwood "Florida's Turnpike"), initially thought to be victims of [carbon monoxide poisoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide_poisoning "Carbon monoxide poisoning").[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-86)[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Salinger-87) Their deaths remained unexplained as the medical examiner waited for results of toxicology tests on the victims,[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Lotan-88) until urine tests revealed that hydrogen sulfide was the cause of death. A report from the Orange-Osceola Medical Examiner's Office indicated that toxic fumes came from the Porsche's [starter battery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automotive_battery "Automotive battery"), located under the front passenger seat.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-89)[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-90)

In January 2017, three utility workers in [Key Largo, Florida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Key_Largo,_Florida "Key Largo, Florida"), died one by one within seconds of descending into a narrow space beneath a [manhole cover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manhole_cover "Manhole cover") to check a section of paved street.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-91) In an attempt to save the men, a firefighter who entered the hole without his air tank (because he could not fit through the hole with it) collapsed within seconds and had to be rescued by a colleague.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Goodhue-92) The firefighter was airlifted to [Jackson Memorial Hospital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jackson_Memorial_Hospital "Jackson Memorial Hospital") and later recovered.[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-93)[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-94) A Monroe County Sheriff officer initially determined that the space contained hydrogen sulfide and [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane") gas produced by decomposing vegetation.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Rabin-95)

On May 24, 2018, two workers were killed, another seriously injured, and 14 others hospitalized by hydrogen sulfide inhalation at a [Norske Skog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norske_Skog "Norske Skog") paper mill in [Albury, New South Wales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albury,_New_South_Wales "Albury, New South Wales").[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Clantar_2020-96)[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-97) An investigation by [SafeWork NSW](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Safe_Work_Australia "Safe Work Australia") found that the gas was released from a tank used to hold [process water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paper_machine#Forming_section_or_wet_end "Paper machine"). The workers were exposed at the end of a 3-day maintenance period. Hydrogen sulfide had built up in an upstream tank, which had been left stagnant and untreated with [biocide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biocide "Biocide") during the maintenance period. These conditions allowed sulfate-reducing bacteria to grow in the upstream tank, as the water contained small quantities of [wood pulp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulp_(paper)#Wood_pulp "Pulp (paper)") and [fiber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wood_fibre "Wood fibre"). The high rate of pumping from this tank into the tank involved in the incident caused hydrogen sulfide gas to escape from various openings around its top when pumping was resumed at the end of the maintenance period. The area above it was sufficiently enclosed for the gas to pool there, despite not being identified as a [confined space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confined_space "Confined space") by Norske Skog. One of the workers who was killed was exposed while investigating an apparent fluid leak in the tank, while the other who was killed and the worker who was badly injured were attempting to rescue the first after he collapsed on top of it. In a resulting [criminal case](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Criminal_case "Criminal case"), Norske Skog was accused of failing to ensure the health and safety of its workforce at the plant to a reasonably practicable extent. It pleaded guilty, and was fined AU$1,012,500 and ordered to fund the production of an anonymized educational video about the incident.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-98)[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-99)[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Clantar_2020-96)[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-100)

In October 2019, an [Odessa, Texas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odessa,_Texas "Odessa, Texas") employee of Aghorn Operating Inc. and his wife were killed due to a water pump failure. [Produced water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Produced_water "Produced water") with a high concentration of hydrogen sulfide was released by the pump. The worker died while responding to an automated phone call he had received alerting him to a mechanical failure in the pump, while his wife died after driving to the facility to check on him.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-101) A [CSB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Chemical_Safety_and_Hazard_Investigation_Board "U.S. Chemical Safety and Hazard Investigation Board") investigation cited lax safety practices at the facility, such as an informal [lockout-tagout](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lockout-tagout "Lockout-tagout") procedure and a nonfunctioning hydrogen sulfide alert system.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-102)

The gas, produced by mixing certain household ingredients, was used in a [suicide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suicide "Suicide") wave in 2008 in Japan.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-103) The wave prompted staff at Tokyo's [suicide prevention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suicide_prevention "Suicide prevention") center to set up a special hotline during "[Golden Week](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_Week_(Japan) "Golden Week (Japan)")", as they received an increase in calls from people wanting to kill themselves during the annual May holiday.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-104)

As of 2010, this phenomenon has occurred in a number of US cities, prompting warnings to those arriving at the site of the suicide.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-105)[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-106)[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-107)[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-108)[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-109)

In 2020, H 2 S ingestion was used as a suicide method by Japanese pro wrestler [Hana Kimura](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hana_Kimura "Hana Kimura").[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-110)

In 2024, Lucy-Bleu Knight, stepdaughter of famed musician [Slash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slash_(musician) "Slash (musician)"), also used H 2 S ingestion to commit suicide.[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-111)

Hydrogen sulfide in the natural environment
-------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_sulfide&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Hydrogen sulfide in the natural environment")]

### Microbial: The sulfur cycle

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_sulfide&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Microbial: The sulfur cycle")]

[![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Teichschlamm1.jpg/250px-Teichschlamm1.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Teichschlamm1.jpg)

A drained pond, showing the layer of [sludge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sludge "Sludge") on the bottom; its black colour is due to the presence of metal sulfides, the result of reactions with hydrogen sulfide produced by bacteria

Hydrogen sulfide is a central participant in the [sulfur cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_cycle "Sulfur cycle"), the [biogeochemical cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biogeochemical_cycle "Biogeochemical cycle") of sulfur on Earth.[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-112)

In the absence of [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen"), [sulfur-reducing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur-reducing_bacteria "Sulfur-reducing bacteria") and [sulfate-reducing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfate-reducing_bacteria "Sulfate-reducing bacteria") bacteria derive energy from [oxidizing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redox "Redox") hydrogen or organic molecules by reducing elemental sulfur or sulfate to hydrogen sulfide. Other bacteria liberate hydrogen sulfide from sulfur-containing [amino acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid "Amino acid"); this gives rise to the odor of rotten eggs and contributes to the odor of [flatulence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flatulence "Flatulence").

As organic matter decays under low-oxygen (or [hypoxic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypoxia_(environmental) "Hypoxia (environmental)")) conditions (such as in swamps, [eutrophic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eutrophic "Eutrophic") lakes or [dead zones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dead_zone_(ecology) "Dead zone (ecology)") of oceans), sulfate-reducing bacteria will use the sulfates present in the water to oxidize the organic matter, producing hydrogen sulfide as waste. Some of the hydrogen sulfide will react with metal ions in the water to produce metal sulfides, which are not water-soluble. These metal sulfides, such as ferrous sulfide FeS, are often black or brown, leading to the dark color of [sludge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sludge "Sludge").

Several groups of bacteria can use hydrogen sulfide as fuel, oxidizing it to elemental sulfur or to sulfate by using dissolved oxygen, metal oxides (e.g., [iron oxyhydroxides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron(III)_oxide-hydroxide "Iron(III) oxide-hydroxide") and [manganese oxides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manganese_oxide "Manganese oxide")), or nitrate as electron acceptors.[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-113)

The [purple sulfur bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purple_sulfur_bacteria "Purple sulfur bacteria") and the [green sulfur bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_sulfur_bacteria "Green sulfur bacteria") use hydrogen sulfide as an [electron donor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_donor "Electron donor") in [photosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis "Photosynthesis"), thereby producing elemental sulfur. This mode of photosynthesis is older than the mode of [cyanobacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanobacteria "Cyanobacteria"), [algae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algae "Algae"), and [plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant "Plant"), which uses water as electron donor and liberates oxygen.

The biochemistry of hydrogen sulfide is a key part of the chemistry of the [iron-sulfur world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron-sulfur_world_theory "Iron-sulfur world theory"). In this model of the [origin of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_of_life "Origin of life") on Earth, geologically produced hydrogen sulfide is postulated as an electron donor driving the reduction of carbon dioxide.[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-114)

Hydrogen sulfide is lethal to most animals, but a few highly specialized species ([extremophiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extremophile "Extremophile")) do thrive in habitats that are rich in this compound.[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Tobler2008-115)

In the deep sea, [hydrothermal vents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent "Hydrothermal vent") and [cold seeps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_seep "Cold seep") with high levels of hydrogen sulfide are home to a number of extremely specialized lifeforms, ranging from bacteria to fish.[_[which?](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Avoid\_weasel\_words "Wikipedia:Avoid weasel words")_][[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-116) Because of the absence of sunlight at these depths, these ecosystems rely on [chemosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemosynthesis "Chemosynthesis") rather than [photosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis "Photosynthesis").[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-117)

Freshwater springs rich in hydrogen sulfide are mainly home to invertebrates, but also include a small number of fish: _[Cyprinodon bobmilleri](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cyprinodon\_bobmilleri&action=edit&redlink=1 "Cyprinodon bobmilleri (page does not exist)")_ (a [pupfish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pupfish "Pupfish") from Mexico), _[Limia sulphurophila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limia\_sulphurophila "Limia sulphurophila")_ (a [poeciliid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poeciliid "Poeciliid") from the [Dominican Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dominican_Republic "Dominican Republic")), _[Gambusia eurystoma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gambusia\_eurystoma "Gambusia eurystoma")_ (a poeciliid from Mexico), and a few _[Poecilia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poecilia "Poecilia")_ (poeciliids from Mexico).[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Tobler2008-115)[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-118) Invertebrates and microorganisms in some cave systems, such as [Movile Cave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Movile_Cave "Movile Cave"), are adapted to high levels of hydrogen sulfide.[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-119)

### Interstellar and planetary occurrence

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_sulfide&action=edit&section=26 "Edit section: Interstellar and planetary occurrence")]

Hydrogen sulfide has often been detected in the interstellar medium.[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-120) It also occurs in the clouds of planets in the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System").[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-121)[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-lissauer2019-122)

[![Image 24](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/39/Hydrogen_Sulfide_Emissions_off_of_Africa.jpg/250px-Hydrogen_Sulfide_Emissions_off_of_Africa.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hydrogen_Sulfide_Emissions_off_of_Africa.jpg)

A hydrogen sulfide bloom (green) stretching for about 150km along the coast of Namibia. As oxygen-poor water reaches the coast, bacteria in the organic-matter rich sediment produce hydrogen sulfide, which is toxic to fish.

Hydrogen sulfide has been implicated in several [mass extinctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extinction_event "Extinction event") that have occurred in the Earth's past. In particular, a buildup of hydrogen sulfide in the atmosphere may have caused, or at least contributed to, the [Permian-Triassic extinction event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permian-Triassic_extinction_event "Permian-Triassic extinction event") 252 million years ago.[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-sciam-123)[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-124)[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-Kump2005-125)

Organic residues from these extinction boundaries indicate that the oceans were anoxic (oxygen-depleted) and had species of shallow plankton that metabolized H 2 S. The formation of H 2 S may have been initiated by massive volcanic eruptions, which emitted [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") and [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane") into the atmosphere, which warmed the oceans, lowering their capacity to absorb oxygen that would otherwise oxidize H 2 S. The increased levels of hydrogen sulfide could have killed oxygen-generating plants as well as depleted the ozone layer, causing further stress. Small H 2 S blooms have been detected in modern times in the [Dead Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dead_Sea "Dead Sea") and in the [Atlantic Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean "Atlantic Ocean") off the coast of [Namibia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Namibia "Namibia").[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide#cite_note-sciam-123)
