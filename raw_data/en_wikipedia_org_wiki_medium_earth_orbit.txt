Space of Medium Earth orbits (MEO) as pink area, with [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth") and the [distance of the orbit of the Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_distance "Lunar distance") for reference and to scale.

A **medium Earth orbit** (**MEO**) is an [Earth-centered orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geocentric_orbit "Geocentric orbit") with an altitude above a [low Earth orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit "Low Earth orbit") (LEO) and below a [high Earth orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_Earth_orbit "High Earth orbit") (HEO)– between 2,000 and 35,786 km (1,243 and 22,236 mi) above sea level.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit#cite_note-NASA_EO-3)

The boundary between MEO and LEO is an arbitrary altitude chosen by accepted convention, whereas the boundary between MEO and HEO is the particular altitude of a [geosynchronous orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosynchronous_orbit "Geosynchronous orbit"), in which a satellite takes 24 hours to circle the Earth, the same period as the Earth’s own rotation. All satellites in MEO have an [orbital period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_period "Orbital period") of less than 24 hours, with the minimum period (for a circular orbit at the lowest MEO altitude) about 2 hours.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit#cite_note-nasa20120718-4)

Satellites in MEO orbits are perturbed by solar radiation pressure, which is the dominating non-gravitational perturbing force.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit#cite_note-5) Other perturbing forces include: [Earth's albedo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albedo#Terrestrial_albedo "Albedo"), navigation antenna thrust, and thermal effects related to heat re-radiation.

The MEO region includes the two zones of energetic charged particles above the equator known as the [Van Allen radiation belts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt "Van Allen radiation belt"), which can damage satellites’ electronic systems without special shielding.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit#cite_note-6)

A medium Earth orbit is sometimes called **mid Earth orbit**[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit#cite_note-NASA_EO-3) or **intermediate circular orbit** (**ICO**).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit#cite_note-nasa20120718-4)

A [camera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hasselblad "Hasselblad") photo of Earth from a distance of 29,400 kilometers (18,300 miles), a distance of higher medium Earth orbits (uncropped and unrotated _[The Blue Marble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Blue\_Marble "The Blue Marble")_ image, from [Apollo 17](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo_17 "Apollo 17") during lunar transfer).

Two medium Earth orbits are particularly significant. A satellite in the _[semi-synchronous orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-synchronous\_orbit "Semi-synchronous orbit")_ at an altitude of approximately 20,200 kilometres (12,600 mi) has an orbital period of 12 hours and passes over the same two spots on the equator every day.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit#cite_note-NASA_EO-3) This reliably predictable orbit is used by the [Global Positioning System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Positioning_System "Global Positioning System") (GPS) [constellation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite_constellation "Satellite constellation").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit#cite_note-nasa20120718-4) Other navigation satellite systems use similar medium Earth orbits including [GLONASS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GLONASS "GLONASS") (with an altitude of 19,100 kilometres, 11,900 mi),[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit#cite_note-7)[Galileo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galileo_positioning_system "Galileo positioning system") (with an altitude of 23,222 kilometres, 14,429 mi)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit#cite_note-8) and [BeiDou](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BeiDou "BeiDou") (with an altitude of 21,528 kilometres, 13,377 mi).[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit#cite_note-9)

The _[Molniya orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molniya\_orbit "Molniya orbit")_ has a high [inclination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_inclination "Orbital inclination") of 63.4° and high [eccentricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity "Orbital eccentricity") of 0.722 with a period of 12 hours, so a satellite spends most of its orbit above the chosen area in high latitudes. This orbit was used by the (now defunct) North American [Sirius Satellite Radio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirius_Satellite_Radio "Sirius Satellite Radio") and [XM Satellite Radio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/XM_Satellite_Radio "XM Satellite Radio") satellites and the Russian [Molniya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molniya_(satellite) "Molniya (satellite)") military communications satellites, after which it is named.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit#cite_note-NASA_EO-3)

Communications satellites in MEO include the [O3b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/O3b "O3b") and [O3b mPOWER](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/O3b_mPOWER "O3b mPOWER") constellations for low-[latency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latency_(engineering) "Latency (engineering)")[broadband](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite_Internet_access "Satellite Internet access") and [data backhaul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Backhaul_(telecommunications) "Backhaul (telecommunications)") to maritime, aero and remote locations (with an altitude of 8,063 kilometres, 5,010 mi).[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit#cite_note-10)

Communications satellites to cover the North and South Pole are also put in MEO.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit#cite_note-11)

[Telstar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telstar "Telstar") 1, an experimental communications satellite launched in 1962, orbited in MEO.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit#cite_note-12)

In May 2022, [Kazakhstani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazakhstan "Kazakhstan") mobile network operator, [Kcell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kcell "Kcell"), and satellite owner and operator, [SES](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SES_(company) "SES (company)") used SES's [O3b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/O3b "O3b") MEO [satellite constellation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite_constellation "Satellite constellation") to demonstrate that MEO satellites could be used to provide high-speed mobile internet to remote regions of Kazakhstan for reliable video calling, conferencing and streaming, and web browsing, with a [latency (delay)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latency_(engineering) "Latency (engineering)") five times lower than on the existing platform based on [geostationary orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geostationary_orbit "Geostationary orbit") satellites.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit#cite_note-13)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit#cite_note-14)

In September 2023, satellite operator [SES](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SES_(company) "SES (company)") announced the first satellite internet service to use [satellite constellations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite_constellation "Satellite constellation") in both MEO and [Low Earth Orbit (LEO)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_Earth_Orbit "Low Earth Orbit"). The SES Cruise mPOWERED + Starlink service will use SES's [O3b mPOWER](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/O3b_mPOWER "O3b mPOWER") MEO satellites and [SpaceX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceX "SpaceX")'s [Starlink](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Starlink "Starlink") LEO system to provide cruise ship passengers with internet, social media and video calls at up to 3 Gbps per ship anywhere in the World. Subsequently, in February 2024, [SES](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SES_(company) "SES (company)") announced that [Virgin Voyages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgin_Voyages "Virgin Voyages") will be the first cruise line to deploy the service.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit#cite_note-15)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit#cite_note-16)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medium_Earth_orbit#cite_note-17)

Infographic showing the space debris situation extending from low Earth orbit, across medium Earth orbits, until the lowest high Earth orbits.

[Space debris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_debris "Space debris") in medium Earth orbit stays practically permanently orbiting Earth. Most space debris extends to the lowest high Earth orbits just beyond the edge of medium Earth orbit, where [geostationary satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geostationary_satellite "Geostationary satellite") are and where after their end of use they are parked in similar orbits, so-called [graveyard orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graveyard_orbit "Graveyard orbit").
