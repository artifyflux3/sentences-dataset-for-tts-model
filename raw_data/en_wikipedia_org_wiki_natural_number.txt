[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/10/Three_Baskets_with_Apples.svg/250px-Three_Baskets_with_Apples.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Three_Baskets_with_Apples.svg)

Natural numbers can be used for counting: one apple; two apples are one apple added to another apple, three apples are one apple added to two apples, ...

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **natural numbers** are the numbers [0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0 "0"), [1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1 "1"), [2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2 "2"), [3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3 "3"), and so on, possibly excluding 0.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-Enderton-1) Some start counting with 0, defining the natural numbers as the **non-negative integers**0, 1, 2, 3, ..., while others start with 1, defining them as the **positive integers**1, 2, 3, ... .[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-2) Some authors acknowledge both definitions whenever convenient.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-:1-3) Sometimes, the **whole numbers** are the natural numbers as well as zero. In other cases, the _whole numbers_ refer to all of the [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer"), including negative integers.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-4) The **counting numbers** are another term for the natural numbers, particularly in primary education, and are ambiguous as well although typically start at 1.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-MathWorld_CountingNumber-5)

The natural numbers are used for counting things, like "there are _six_ coins on the table", in which case they are called _[cardinal numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinal\_number "Cardinal number")_. They are also used to put things in order, like "this is the _third_ largest city in the country", which are called _[ordinal numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinal\_number "Ordinal number")_. Natural numbers are also used as labels, like [jersey numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_(sports) "Number (sports)") on a sports team, where they serve as _[nominal numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nominal\_number "Nominal number")_ and do not have mathematical properties.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-6)

The natural numbers form a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)"), commonly symbolized as a bold **N** or [blackboard bold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blackboard_bold "Blackboard bold")⁠![Image 2: {\displaystyle \mathbb {N} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdf9a96b565ea202d0f4322e9195613fb26a9bed)⁠. Many other [number sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_set "Number set") are built from the natural numbers. For example, the [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer") are made by adding 0 and negative numbers. The [rational numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number "Rational number") add fractions, and the [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") add all infinite decimals. [Complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") add the [square root of −1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit "Imaginary unit"). This chain of extensions canonically [embeds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embedding "Embedding") the natural numbers in the other number systems.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-7)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-8)

Natural numbers are studied in different areas of math. [Number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory") looks at things like how numbers divide evenly ([divisibility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divisibility "Divisibility")), or how [prime numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_number "Prime number") are spread out. [Combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combinatorics "Combinatorics") studies counting and arranging numbered objects, such as [partitions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partition_(number_theory) "Partition (number theory)") and [enumerations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enumerative_combinatorics "Enumerative combinatorics").

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Ishango_bone_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Ishango_bone_%28cropped%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ishango_bone_(cropped).jpg)

The [Ishango bone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ishango_bone "Ishango bone") (on exhibition at the [Royal Belgian Institute of Natural Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Belgian_Institute_of_Natural_Sciences "Royal Belgian Institute of Natural Sciences"))[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-RBINS_intro-9)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-RBINS_flash-10)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-UNESCO-11) is believed to have been used 20,000 years ago for natural number arithmetic.

The most primitive method of representing a natural number is to use one's fingers, as in [finger counting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finger_counting "Finger counting"). Putting down a [tally mark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tally_mark "Tally mark") for each object is another primitive method. Later, a set of objects could be tested for equality, excess or shortage—by striking out a mark and removing an object from the set.

The first major advance in abstraction was the use of [numerals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numeral_system "Numeral system") to represent numbers. This allowed systems to be developed for recording large numbers. The ancient [Egyptians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Ancient_Egypt "History of Ancient Egypt") developed a powerful system of numerals with distinct [hieroglyphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptian_hieroglyphs "Egyptian hieroglyphs") for 1, 10, and all powers of 10 up to over 1 million. A stone carving from [Karnak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karnak "Karnak"), dating back from around 1500 BCE and now at the [Louvre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louvre "Louvre") in Paris, depicts 276 as 2 hundreds, 7 tens, and 6 ones; and similarly for the number 4,622. The [Babylonians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylonia "Babylonia") had a [place-value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positional_notation "Positional notation") system based essentially on the numerals for 1 and 10, using base sixty, so that the symbol for sixty was the same as the symbol for one—its value being determined from context.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-12)

A much later advance was the development of the idea that[0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0_(number) "0 (number)") can be considered as a number, with its own numeral. The use of a 0 [digit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerical_digit "Numerical digit") in place-value notation (within other numbers) dates back as early as 700 BCE by the Babylonians, who omitted such a digit when it would have been the last symbol in the number.[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-14) The [Olmec](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olmec "Olmec") and [Maya civilizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_civilization "Maya civilization") used 0 as a separate number as early as the 1st century BCE, but this usage did not spread beyond [Mesoamerica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesoamerica "Mesoamerica").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-15)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-16) The use of a numeral 0 in modern times originated with the Indian mathematician [Brahmagupta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahmagupta "Brahmagupta") in 628 CE. However, 0 had been used as a number in the medieval [computus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computus "Computus") (the calculation of the date of Easter), beginning with [Dionysius Exiguus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dionysius_Exiguus "Dionysius Exiguus") in 525 CE, without being denoted by a numeral. Standard [Roman numerals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_numerals "Roman numerals") do not have a symbol for 0; instead, _nulla_ (or the genitive form _nullae_) from _nullus_, the Latin word for "none", was employed to denote a 0 value.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-17)

The first systematic study of numbers as [abstractions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstraction "Abstraction") is usually credited to the [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece") philosophers [Pythagoras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras "Pythagoras") and [Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes"). Some Greek mathematicians treated the number 1 differently than larger numbers, sometimes even not as a number at all.[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-19)[Euclid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid "Euclid"), for example, defined a unit first and then a number as a multitude of units, thus by his definition, a unit is not a number and there are no unique numbers (e.g., any two units from indefinitely many units is a 2).[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-Mueller_2006_p._58-20) However, in the definition of [perfect number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perfect_number "Perfect number") which comes shortly afterward, Euclid treats 1 as a number like any other.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-21)

Independent studies on numbers also occurred at around the same time in [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/India "India"), China, and [Mesoamerica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesoamerica "Mesoamerica").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-22)

### Emergence as a term

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Natural_number&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Emergence as a term")]

[Nicolas Chuquet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolas_Chuquet "Nicolas Chuquet") used the term _progression naturelle_ (natural progression) in 1484.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-23) The earliest known use of "natural number" as a complete English phrase is in 1763.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-24)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-MacTutor-25) The 1771 Encyclopaedia Britannica defines natural numbers in the logarithm article.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-MacTutor-25)

Starting at 0 or 1 has long been a matter of definition. In 1727, [Bernard Le Bovier de Fontenelle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernard_Le_Bovier_de_Fontenelle "Bernard Le Bovier de Fontenelle") wrote that his notions of distance and element led to defining the natural numbers as including or excluding 0.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-26) In 1889, [Giuseppe Peano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giuseppe_Peano "Giuseppe Peano") used N for the positive integers and started at 1,[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-27) but he later changed to using N 0 and N 1.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-Peano1901-28) Historically, most definitions have excluded 0,[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-MacTutor-25)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-29)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-30) but many mathematicians such as [George A. Wentworth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_A._Wentworth "George A. Wentworth"), [Bertrand Russell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bertrand_Russell "Bertrand Russell"), [Nicolas Bourbaki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolas_Bourbaki "Nicolas Bourbaki"), [Paul Halmos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Halmos "Paul Halmos"), [Stephen Cole Kleene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephen_Cole_Kleene "Stephen Cole Kleene"), and [John Horton Conway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Horton_Conway "John Horton Conway") have preferred to include 0.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-31)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-MacTutor-25)

Mathematicians have noted tendencies in which definition is used, such as algebra texts including 0,[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-MacTutor-25)[[d]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-MacLaneBirkhoff1999p15-32) number theory and analysis texts excluding 0,[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-MacTutor-25)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-K%C5%99%C3%AD%C5%BEek-33)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-34) logic and set theory texts including 0,[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-35)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-36)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-37) dictionaries excluding 0,[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-MacTutor-25)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-38) school books (through high-school level) excluding 0, and upper-division college-level books including 0.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-Enderton-1) There are exceptions to each of these tendencies and as of 2023 no formal survey has been conducted. Arguments raised include [division by zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_by_zero "Division by zero")[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-K%C5%99%C3%AD%C5%BEek-33) and the size of the [empty set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empty_set "Empty set"). [Computer languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_language "Computer language") often [start from zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero-based_numbering "Zero-based numbering") when enumerating items like [loop counters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/For_loop "For loop") and [string-](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_(computer_science) "String (computer science)") or [array-elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Array_data_structure "Array data structure").[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-39)[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-40) Including 0 began to rise in popularity in the 1960s.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-MacTutor-25) The [ISO 31-11](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_31-11 "ISO 31-11") standard included 0 in the natural numbers in its first edition in 1978 and this has continued through its present edition as [ISO 80000-2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO/IEC_80000 "ISO/IEC 80000").[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-ISO80000-41)

### Formal construction

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Natural_number&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Formal construction")]

In 19th century Europe, there was mathematical and philosophical discussion about the exact nature of the natural numbers. [Henri Poincaré](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9 "Henri Poincaré") stated that axioms can only be demonstrated in their finite application, and concluded that it is "the power of the mind" which allows conceiving of the indefinite repetition of the same act.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-42)[Leopold Kronecker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leopold_Kronecker "Leopold Kronecker") summarized his belief as "God made the integers, all else is the work of man".[[e]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-45)

The [constructivists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constructivism_(mathematics) "Constructivism (mathematics)") saw a need to improve upon the logical rigor in the [foundations of mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foundations_of_mathematics "Foundations of mathematics").[[f]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-46) In the 1860s, [Hermann Grassmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Grassmann "Hermann Grassmann") suggested a [recursive definition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recursive_definition "Recursive definition") for natural numbers, thus stating they were not really natural—but a consequence of definitions. Later, two classes of such formal definitions emerged, using set theory and Peano's axioms respectively. Later still, they were shown to be equivalent in most practical applications.

[Set-theoretical definitions of natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set-theoretical_definitions_of_natural_numbers "Set-theoretical definitions of natural numbers") were initiated by [Frege](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottlob_Frege "Gottlob Frege"). He initially defined a natural number as the class of all sets that are in one-to-one correspondence with a particular set. However, this definition turned out to lead to paradoxes, including [Russell's paradox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russell%27s_paradox "Russell's paradox"). To avoid such paradoxes, the formalism was modified so that a natural number is defined as a particular set, and any set that can be put into one-to-one correspondence with that set is said to have that number of elements.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-47)

In 1881, [Charles Sanders Peirce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Sanders_Peirce "Charles Sanders Peirce") provided the first [axiomatization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiomatic_system#Axiomatization "Axiomatic system") of natural-number arithmetic.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-48)[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-49) In 1888, [Richard Dedekind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Dedekind "Richard Dedekind") proposed another axiomatization of natural-number arithmetic,[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-50) and in 1889, Peano published a simplified version of Dedekind's axioms in his book _The principles of arithmetic presented by a new method_ ([Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_language "Latin language"): _[Arithmetices principia, nova methodo exposita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetices\_principia,\_nova\_methodo\_exposita "Arithmetices principia, nova methodo exposita")_). This approach is now called [Peano arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peano_arithmetic "Peano arithmetic"). It is based on an [axiomatization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiomatization "Axiomatization") of the properties of [ordinal numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinal_number "Ordinal number"): each natural number has a successor and every non-zero natural number has a unique predecessor. Peano arithmetic is [equiconsistent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equiconsistent "Equiconsistent") with several weak systems of [set theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_theory "Set theory"). One such system is [ZFC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ZFC "ZFC") with the [axiom of infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_of_infinity "Axiom of infinity") replaced by its negation.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-51) Theorems that can be proved in ZFC but cannot be proved using the Peano Axioms include [Goodstein's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goodstein%27s_theorem "Goodstein's theorem").[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-52)

The [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") of all natural numbers is standardly denoted **N** or ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \mathbb {N} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/682f44bd6a1ea39ecf1e21a8290b9d5b2f504505)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-:1-3)[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-53) Older texts have occasionally employed _J_ as the symbol for this set.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-54)

Since natural numbers may contain 0 or not, it may be important to know which version is referred to. This is often specified by the context, but may also be done by using a subscript or a superscript in the notation, possibly with reference to the [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer")![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc). Examples include:

*   Naturals with zero (![Image 15: {\displaystyle \;\{0,1,2,...\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90ad1bccead06565c740a0c981c2a0e3a65cb57a)): ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} ^{\geq 0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07e9fa6f2ee6c67ecf72fc7898c4d5d7a02a156a), ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \mathbb {N} ^{\geq 0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2785d17ebf296abd324dd63df5152933ceeb9e2d),[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-Smula2015-55)![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mathbb {N} _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/77ab7e98123f0def29a1cd3df96a0b7a58f4202c),[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-58)![Image 19: {\displaystyle \mathbb {N} ^{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c266661ca769727f799db89ef85b1353f2b1d71),[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-59)![Image 20: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} ^{0+}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81a3b6ad192693a6aeb684d1876f82d852fcc228).[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-60)

This section uses the convention ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathbb {N} =\mathbb {N} _{0}=\mathbb {N} ^{*}\cup \{0\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ddbef60796e9c37a573367bb6bcf04715861db92).

Given the set ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathbb {N} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdf9a96b565ea202d0f4322e9195613fb26a9bed) of natural numbers and the [successor function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Successor_function "Successor function")![Image 23: {\displaystyle S\colon \mathbb {N} \to \mathbb {N} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d8d6e80a52918c3b75f7062fe383b1e09b5e2c8) sending each natural number to the next one, one can define [addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition_in_N "Addition in N") of natural numbers recursively by setting _a_ + 0 = _a_ and _a_ + _S_(_b_) = _S_(_a_ + _b_) for all _a_, _b_. Thus, _a_ + 1 = _a_ + S(0) = S(_a_+0) = S(_a_), _a_ + 2 = _a_ + S(1) = S(_a_+1) = S(S(_a_)), and so on. The [algebraic structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_structure "Algebraic structure")![Image 24: {\displaystyle (\mathbb {N} ,+)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0072a6ee0ab943ce24dc44083bd60d50739a0b1f) is a [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative "Commutative")[monoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monoid "Monoid") with [identity element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_element "Identity element")0. It is a [free monoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_object "Free object") on one generator. This commutative monoid satisfies the [cancellation property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cancellation_property "Cancellation property"), so it can be embedded in a [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)"). The smallest group containing the natural numbers is the [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer").

If 1 is defined as _S_(0), then _b_ + 1 = _b_ + _S_(0) = _S_(_b_ + 0) = _S_(_b_). That is, _b_ + 1 is simply the successor of _b_.

Analogously, given that addition has been defined, a [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication "Multiplication") operator ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \times }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ffafff1ad26cbe49045f19a67ce532116a32703) can be defined via _a_ × 0 = 0 and _a_ × S(_b_) = (_a_ × _b_) + _a_. This turns ![Image 26: {\displaystyle (\mathbb {N} ^{*},\times )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd9f6d877853c0e1b4e6477b70cac9644a5fe14) into a [free commutative monoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_commutative_monoid "Free commutative monoid") with identity element 1; a generator set for this monoid is the set of [prime numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_number "Prime number").

### Relationship between addition and multiplication

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Natural_number&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Relationship between addition and multiplication")]

Addition and multiplication are compatible, which is expressed in the [distribution law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributivity "Distributivity"): _a_ × (_b_ + _c_) = (_a_ × _b_) + (_a_ × _c_). These properties of addition and multiplication make the natural numbers an instance of a [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative "Commutative")[semiring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiring "Semiring"). Semirings are an algebraic generalization of the natural numbers where multiplication is not necessarily commutative. The lack of additive inverses, which is equivalent to the fact that ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \mathbb {N} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdf9a96b565ea202d0f4322e9195613fb26a9bed) is not [closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closure_(mathematics) "Closure (mathematics)") under subtraction (that is, subtracting one natural from another does not always result in another natural), means that ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \mathbb {N} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdf9a96b565ea202d0f4322e9195613fb26a9bed) is _not_ a [ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)"); instead it is a [semiring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiring "Semiring") (also known as a _rig_).

If the natural numbers are taken as "excluding 0", and "starting at 1", the definitions of + and × are as above, except that they begin with _a_ + 1 = _S_(_a_) and _a_ × 1 = _a_. Furthermore, ![Image 29: {\displaystyle (\mathbb {N^{*}} ,+)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22ad68f9dd3f2e7653a596f2e8d647a5de6b0bda) has no identity element.

In this section, juxtaposed variables such as _ab_ indicate the product _a_ × _b_,[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-61) and the standard [order of operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_of_operations "Order of operations") is assumed.

A [total order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_order "Total order") on the natural numbers is defined by letting _a_ ≤ _b_ if and only if there exists another natural number _c_ where _a_ + _c_ = _b_. This order is compatible with the [arithmetical operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetical_operations "Arithmetical operations") in the following sense: if _a_, _b_ and _c_ are natural numbers and _a_ ≤ _b_, then _a_ + _c_ ≤ _b_ + _c_ and _ac_ ≤ _bc_.

An important property of the natural numbers is that they are [well-ordered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Well-order "Well-order"): every non-empty set of natural numbers has a least element. The rank among well-ordered sets is expressed by an [ordinal number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinal_number "Ordinal number"); for the natural numbers, this is denoted as [_ω_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omega_(ordinal) "Omega (ordinal)") (omega).

In this section, juxtaposed variables such as _ab_ indicate the product _a_ × _b_, and the standard [order of operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_of_operations "Order of operations") is assumed.

While it is in general not possible to divide one natural number by another and get a natural number as result, the procedure of _division with remainder_ or [Euclidean division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_division "Euclidean division") is available as a substitute: for any two natural numbers _a_ and _b_ with _b_ ≠ 0 there are natural numbers _q_ and _r_ such that

![Image 30: {\displaystyle a=bq+r{\text{ and }}r<b.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6eb374cf44fed4f1fad58fbf031da726405c28b6)
The number _q_ is called the _[quotient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient "Quotient")_ and _r_ is called the _[remainder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remainder "Remainder")_ of the division of _a_ by _b_. The numbers _q_ and _r_ are uniquely determined by _a_ and _b_. This Euclidean division is key to the several other properties ([divisibility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divisibility "Divisibility")), algorithms (such as the [Euclidean algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_algorithm "Euclidean algorithm")), and ideas in number theory.

### Algebraic properties satisfied by the natural numbers

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Natural_number&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Algebraic properties satisfied by the natural numbers")]

The addition (+) and multiplication (×) operations on natural numbers as defined above have several algebraic properties:

*   [Closure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closure_(mathematics) "Closure (mathematics)") under addition and multiplication: for all natural numbers _a_ and _b_, both _a_ + _b_ and _a_ × _b_ are natural numbers.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-62)
*   [Associativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associativity "Associativity"): for all natural numbers _a_, _b_, and _c_, _a_ + (_b_ + _c_) = (_a_ + _b_) + _c_ and _a_ × (_b_ × _c_) = (_a_ × _b_) × _c_.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-63)
*   [Commutativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutativity "Commutativity"): for all natural numbers _a_ and _b_, _a_ + _b_ = _b_ + _a_ and _a_ × _b_ = _b_ × _a_.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-64)
*   Existence of [identity elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_element "Identity element"): for every natural number _a_, _a_ + 0 = _a_ and _a_ × 1 = _a_. 
    *   If the natural numbers are taken as "excluding 0", and "starting at 1", then for every natural number _a_, _a_ × 1 = _a_. However, the "existence of additive identity element" property is not satisfied

*   [Distributivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributivity "Distributivity") of multiplication over addition for all natural numbers _a_, _b_, and _c_, _a_ × (_b_ + _c_) = (_a_ × _b_) + (_a_ × _c_).
*   No nonzero [zero divisors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_divisor "Zero divisor"): if _a_ and _b_ are natural numbers such that _a_ × _b_ = 0, then _a_ = 0 or _b_ = 0 (or both).

Two important generalizations of natural numbers arise from the two uses of counting and ordering: [cardinal numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinal_number "Cardinal number") and [ordinal numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinal_number "Ordinal number").

*   A natural number can be used to express the size of a finite set; more precisely, a cardinal number is a measure for the size of a set, which is even suitable for infinite sets. The numbering of cardinals usually begins at zero, to accommodate the [empty set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empty_set "Empty set")![Image 31: {\displaystyle \emptyset }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6af50205f42bb2ec3c666b7b847d2c7f96e464c7). This concept of "size" relies on maps between sets, such that two sets have [the same size](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equinumerosity "Equinumerosity"), exactly if there exists a [bijection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijection "Bijection") between them. The set of natural numbers itself, and any bijective image of it, is said to be _[countably infinite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Countable\_set "Countable set")_ and to have [cardinality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality "Cardinality")[aleph-null](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aleph_number#Aleph-null "Aleph number") (ℵ 0).
*   Natural numbers are also used as [linguistic ordinal numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinal_numbers_(linguistics) "Ordinal numbers (linguistics)"): "first", "second", "third", and so forth. The numbering of ordinals usually begins at zero, to accommodate the order type of the [empty set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empty_set "Empty set")![Image 32: {\displaystyle \emptyset }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6af50205f42bb2ec3c666b7b847d2c7f96e464c7). This way they can be assigned to the elements of a totally ordered finite set, and also to the elements of any [well-ordered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Well-order "Well-order") countably infinite set without [limit points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_points "Limit points"). This assignment can be generalized to general well-orderings with a cardinality beyond countability, to yield the ordinal numbers. An ordinal number may also be used to describe the notion of "size" for a well-ordered set, in a sense different from cardinality: if there is an [order isomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_isomorphism "Order isomorphism") (more than a bijection) between two well-ordered sets, they have the same ordinal number. The first ordinal number that is not a natural number is expressed as ω; this is also the ordinal number of the set of natural numbers itself.

The least ordinal of cardinality ℵ 0 (that is, the [initial ordinal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Von_Neumann_cardinal_assignment "Von Neumann cardinal assignment") of ℵ 0) is ω but many well-ordered sets with cardinal number ℵ 0 have an ordinal number greater than ω.

For [finite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_set "Finite set") well-ordered sets, there is a one-to-one correspondence between ordinal and cardinal numbers; therefore they can both be expressed by the same natural number, the number of elements of the set. This number can also be used to describe the position of an element in a larger finite, or an infinite, [sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence "Sequence").

A countable [non-standard model of arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-standard_model_of_arithmetic "Non-standard model of arithmetic") satisfying the Peano Arithmetic (that is, the first-order Peano axioms) was developed by [Skolem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skolem "Skolem") in 1933. The [hypernatural](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypernatural "Hypernatural") numbers are an uncountable model that can be constructed from the ordinary natural numbers via the [ultrapower construction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultrapower_construction "Ultrapower construction"). Other generalizations are discussed in [Number §Extensions of the concept](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number#Extensions_of_the_concept "Number").

[Georges Reeb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georges_Reeb "Georges Reeb") used to claim provocatively that "The naïve integers don't fill up ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \mathbb {N} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdf9a96b565ea202d0f4322e9195613fb26a9bed)".[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-65)

There are two standard methods for formally defining natural numbers. The first one, named for [Giuseppe Peano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giuseppe_Peano "Giuseppe Peano"), consists of an autonomous [axiomatic theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiomatic_theory "Axiomatic theory") called [Peano arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peano_arithmetic "Peano arithmetic"), based on few axioms called [Peano axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peano_axioms "Peano axioms").

The second definition is based on [set theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_theory "Set theory"). It defines the natural numbers as specific [sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)"). More precisely, each natural number n is defined as an explicitly defined set, whose elements allow counting the elements of other sets, in the sense that the sentence "a set S has n elements" means that there exists a [one to one correspondence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_to_one_correspondence "One to one correspondence") between the two sets n and S.

The sets used to define natural numbers satisfy Peano axioms. It follows that every [theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorem "Theorem") that can be stated and proved in Peano arithmetic can also be proved in set theory. However, the two definitions are not equivalent, as there are theorems that can be stated in terms of Peano arithmetic and proved in set theory, which are not _provable_ inside Peano arithmetic. A probable example is [Fermat's Last Theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermat%27s_Last_Theorem "Fermat's Last Theorem").

The definition of the integers as sets satisfying Peano axioms provide a [model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Model_(mathematical_logic) "Model (mathematical logic)") of Peano arithmetic inside set theory. An important consequence is that, if set theory is [consistent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consistent "Consistent") (as it is usually guessed), then Peano arithmetic is consistent. In other words, if a contradiction could be proved in Peano arithmetic, then set theory would be contradictory, and every theorem of set theory would be both true and wrong.

The five Peano axioms are the following:[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-66)[[g]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-67)

1.   0 is a natural number.
2.   Every natural number has a successor which is also a natural number.
3.   0 is not the successor of any natural number.
4.   If the successor of ![Image 34: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) equals the successor of ![Image 35: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d), then ![Image 36: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) equals ![Image 37: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d).
5.   The [axiom of induction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_of_induction "Axiom of induction"): If a statement is true of 0, and if the truth of that statement for a number implies its truth for the successor of that number, then the statement is true for every natural number.

These are not the original axioms published by Peano, but are named in his honor. Some forms of the Peano axioms have 1 in place of 0. In ordinary arithmetic, the successor of ![Image 38: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) is ![Image 39: {\displaystyle x+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16df430ed7a23df9b160a5bbd957f306a0c3baa7).

### Set-theoretic definition

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Natural_number&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Set-theoretic definition")]

Intuitively, the natural number n is the common property of all [sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") that have n elements. So, it seems natural to define n as an [equivalence class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_class "Equivalence class") under the relation "can be made in [one to one correspondence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_to_one_correspondence "One to one correspondence")". This does not work in all [set theories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_theory "Set theory"), as such an equivalence class would not be a set[[h]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-68) (because of [Russell's paradox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russell%27s_paradox "Russell's paradox")). The standard solution is to define a particular set with n elements that will be called the natural number n.

The following definition was first published by [John von Neumann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_von_Neumann "John von Neumann"),[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-vonNeumann1923pp199-208-69) although Levy attributes the idea to unpublished work of Zermelo in 1916.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-Levy-70) As this definition extends to [infinite set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_set "Infinite set") as a definition of [ordinal number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinal_number "Ordinal number"), the sets considered below are sometimes called [von Neumann ordinals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Von_Neumann_ordinals "Von Neumann ordinals").

The definition proceeds as follows:

*   Call 0 = { }, the [empty set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empty_set "Empty set").
*   Define the _successor_ _S_(_a_) of any set a by _S_(_a_) = _a_ ∪ {_a_}.
*   By the [axiom of infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_of_infinity "Axiom of infinity"), there exist sets which contain 0 and are [closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closure_(mathematics) "Closure (mathematics)") under the successor function. Such sets are said to be _inductive_. The intersection of all inductive sets is still an inductive set.
*   This intersection is the set of the _natural numbers_.

It follows that the natural numbers are defined iteratively as follows:

*   0 = { },
*   1 = 0 ∪ {0} = {0} = {{ }},
*   2 = 1 ∪ {1} = {0, 1} = {{ }, {{ }}},
*   3 = 2 ∪ {2} = {0, 1, 2}= {{ }, {{ }}, {{ }, {{ }}}},
*   _n_ = _n_−1 ∪ {_n_−1} = {0, 1, ..., _n_−1}= {{ }, {{ }}, ..., {{ }, {{ }}, ...}},
*   etc.

It can be checked that the natural numbers satisfy the [Peano axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peano_axioms "Peano axioms").

With this definition, given a natural number _n_, the sentence "a set S has n elements" can be formally defined as "there exists a [bijection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijection "Bijection") from n to S." This formalizes the operation of _counting_ the elements of S. Also, _n_ ≤ _m_ if and only if _n_ is a [subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset") of _m_. In other words, the [set inclusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_inclusion "Set inclusion") defines the usual [total order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_order "Total order") on the natural numbers. This order is a [well-order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Well-order "Well-order").

It follows from the definition that each natural number is equal to the set of all natural numbers less than it. This definition, can be extended to the [von Neumann definition of ordinals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Von_Neumann_ordinal "Von Neumann ordinal") for defining all [ordinal numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinal_number "Ordinal number"), including the infinite ones: "each ordinal is the well-ordered set of all smaller ordinals."

If one [does not accept the axiom of infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finitism "Finitism"), the natural numbers may not form a set. Nevertheless, the natural numbers can still be individually defined as above, and they still satisfy the Peano axioms.

There are other set theoretical constructions. In particular, [Ernst Zermelo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernst_Zermelo "Ernst Zermelo") provided a construction that is nowadays only of historical interest, and is sometimes referred to as **Zermelo ordinals**.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_note-Levy-70) It consists in defining 0 as the empty set, and _S_(_a_) = {_a_}.

With this definition each nonzero natural number is a [singleton set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singleton_set "Singleton set"). So, the property of the natural numbers to represent [cardinalities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinalities "Cardinalities") is not directly accessible; only the ordinal property (being the n th element of a sequence) is immediate. Unlike von Neumann's construction, the Zermelo ordinals do not extend to infinite ordinals.

[Halmos (1974](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#CITEREFHalmos1974), p.46) uses the language of set theory instead of the language of arithmetic for his five axioms. He begins with "(I)0 ∈ ω (where, of course, 0 = ∅" (ω is the set of all natural numbers).

[Morash (1991)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#CITEREFMorash1991) gives "a two-part axiom" in which the natural numbers begin with 1. (Section 10.1: _An Axiomatization for the System of Positive Integers_)
8.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number#cite_ref-68)**In some set theories, e.g., [New Foundations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Foundations "New Foundations"), a [universal set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_set "Universal set") exists and Russel's paradox cannot be formulated.
