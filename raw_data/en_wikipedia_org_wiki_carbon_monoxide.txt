**Carbon monoxide** ([chemical formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")**CO**) is a poisonous, flammable gas that is colorless, odorless, tasteless, and slightly less dense than air. Carbon monoxide consists of one [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") atom and one [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") atom connected by a [triple bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple_bond "Triple bond"). It is the simplest [carbon oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxocarbon "Oxocarbon"). In [coordination complexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordination_complex "Coordination complex"), the carbon monoxide [ligand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ligand "Ligand") is called _[carbonyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal\_carbonyl "Metal carbonyl")_. It is a key ingredient in many processes in industrial chemistry.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Ull-5)

The most common source of carbon monoxide is the partial [combustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combustion "Combustion") of carbon-containing compounds. Numerous environmental and biological sources generate carbon monoxide. In industry, carbon monoxide is important in the production of many compounds, including drugs, fragrances, and fuels.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-6)

Indoors CO is one of the most acutely toxic contaminants affecting [indoor air quality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indoor_air_quality "Indoor air quality"). CO may be emitted from tobacco smoke and generated from malfunctioning fuel-burning stoves (wood, kerosene, natural gas, propane) and fuel-burning heating systems (wood, oil, natural gas) and from blocked [flues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flue "Flue") connected to these appliances.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Myers-2022-7)[Carbon monoxide poisoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide_poisoning "Carbon monoxide poisoning") is the most common type of fatal air poisoning in many countries.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-UK_Health_Security_Agency-8)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Myers-2022-7)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Dent-2024-9)

Carbon monoxide has important biological roles across phylogenetic kingdoms. It is produced by many organisms, including humans. In mammalian physiology, carbon monoxide is a classical example of [hormesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hormesis "Hormesis") where low concentrations serve as an endogenous [neurotransmitter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neurotransmitter "Neurotransmitter") ([gasotransmitter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gasotransmitter "Gasotransmitter")) and high concentrations are [toxic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxic "Toxic"), resulting in [carbon monoxide poisoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide_poisoning "Carbon monoxide poisoning"). It is [isoelectronic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isoelectronic "Isoelectronic") with both [cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanide "Cyanide") anion CN− and molecular [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen")N 2.

Physical and chemical properties
--------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbon_monoxide&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Physical and chemical properties")]

Carbon monoxide is the simplest [oxocarbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxocarbon "Oxocarbon") and is [isoelectronic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isoelectronic "Isoelectronic") with other triply bonded [diatomic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic "Diatomic") species possessing 10 valence electrons, including the [cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanide "Cyanide") anion, the [nitrosonium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrosonium "Nitrosonium") cation, [boron monofluoride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boron_monofluoride "Boron monofluoride") and molecular [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen"). It has a [molar mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molar_mass "Molar mass") of 28.0, which, according to the [ideal gas law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas_law "Ideal gas law"), makes it slightly less dense than air, whose average molar mass is 28.8.

The carbon and oxygen are connected by a [triple bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple_bond "Triple bond") that consists of a net two [pi bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi_bond "Pi bond") and one [sigma bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigma_bond "Sigma bond"). The [bond length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_length "Bond length") between the carbon atom and the oxygen atom is 112.8[pm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Picometre "Picometre").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-gilliam-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-11) This bond length is consistent with a triple bond, as in molecular nitrogen (N 2), which has a similar bond length (109.76 pm) and nearly the same [molecular mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_mass "Molecular mass"). Carbon–oxygen double bonds are significantly longer, 120.8 pm in [formaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde "Formaldehyde"), for example.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-12) The boiling point (82 K) and melting point (68 K) are very similar to those of N 2 (77 K and 63 K, respectively). The [bond-dissociation energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond-dissociation_energy "Bond-dissociation energy") of 1072 kJ/mol is stronger than that of N 2 (942 kJ/mol) and represents the strongest chemical bond known.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-13)

The [ground](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_state "Ground state")[electronic state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_state "Electronic state") of carbon monoxide is a [singlet state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singlet_state "Singlet state")[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-14) since there are no unpaired electrons.

### Bonding and dipole moment

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbon_monoxide&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Bonding and dipole moment")]

The strength of the C≡O bond in carbon monoxide is indicated by the high frequency of its vibration, 2143 cm−1.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-nuCO-17) For comparison, organic carbonyls such as ketones and esters absorb at around 1700 cm−1.

Carbon and oxygen together have a total of 10 [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrons "Electrons") in the [valence shell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valence_shell "Valence shell"). Following the [octet rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octet_rule "Octet rule") for both carbon and oxygen, the two atoms form a [triple bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple_bond "Triple bond"), with six shared electrons in three bonding molecular orbitals, rather than the usual double bond found in organic carbonyl compounds. Since four of the shared electrons come from the oxygen atom and only two from carbon, one bonding orbital is occupied by two electrons from oxygen, forming a dative or [dipolar bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dipolar_bond "Dipolar bond"). This causes a C←O [polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_polarity "Chemical polarity") of the molecule, with a small negative charge on carbon and a small positive charge on oxygen. The other two bonding orbitals are each occupied by one electron from carbon and one from oxygen, forming (polar) covalent bonds with a reverse C→O polarization since oxygen is more [electronegative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronegativity "Electronegativity") than carbon. In the free carbon monoxide molecule, a net negative charge δ− remains at the carbon end and the molecule has a small [dipole moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_dipole_moment "Bond dipole moment") of 0.122[D](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debye "Debye").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-18)

The molecule is therefore asymmetric: oxygen is more electron dense than carbon and is also slightly positively charged compared to carbon being negative.

[![Image 20](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Carbon-monoxide-resonance-2D.png/250px-Carbon-monoxide-resonance-2D.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Carbon-monoxide-resonance-2D.png)

The most important resonance form of carbon monoxide is −C≡O+. An important minor resonance contributor is the non-octet carbenic structure :C=O.

Carbon monoxide has a computed fractional bond order of 2.6, indicating that the "third" bond is important but constitutes somewhat less than a full bond.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-19) Thus, in valence bond terms, −C≡O+ is the most important structure, while :C=O is non-octet, but has a neutral formal charge on each atom and represents the second most important resonance contributor. Because of the lone pair and divalence of carbon in this resonance structure, carbon monoxide is often considered to be an extraordinarily stabilized [carbene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbene "Carbene").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-20)[Isocyanides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isocyanide "Isocyanide") are compounds in which the O is replaced by an NR (R = alkyl or aryl) group and have a similar bonding scheme.

If carbon monoxide acts as a [ligand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal_carbonyl "Metal carbonyl"), the polarity of the dipole may reverse with a net negative charge on the oxygen end, depending on the structure of the [coordination complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordination_complex "Coordination complex").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-21) See also the section _["Coordination chemistry"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon\_monoxide#Coordination\_chemistry)_ below.

### Bond polarity and oxidation state

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbon_monoxide&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Bond polarity and oxidation state")]

Theoretical and experimental studies show that, despite the greater electronegativity of oxygen, the dipole moment points from the more-negative carbon end to the more-positive oxygen end.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-22)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-23) The three bonds are in fact [polar covalent bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_covalent_bond "Polar covalent bond") that are strongly polarized. The calculated polarization toward the oxygen atom is 71% for the [σ-bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigma_bond "Sigma bond") and 77% for both [π-bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi_bond "Pi bond").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Stefan-24)

The [oxidation state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidation_state "Oxidation state") of carbon in carbon monoxide is +2 in each of these structures. It is calculated by counting all the bonding electrons as belonging to the more electronegative oxygen. Only the two non-bonding electrons on carbon are assigned to carbon. In this count, carbon then has only two valence electrons in the molecule compared to four in the free atom.

Monthly averages of global concentrations of tropospheric carbon monoxide at an altitude of about 12,000 feet. Data were collected by the MOPITT (Measurements Of Pollution In The Troposphere) sensor on NASA's Terra satellite.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-25)

### Atmospheric presence

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbon_monoxide&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Atmospheric presence")]

The streak of red, orange, and yellow across [South America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_America "South America"), [Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa "Africa"), and the [Atlantic Ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atlantic_Ocean "Atlantic Ocean") in this animation points to high levels of carbon monoxide on September 30, 2005.

[![Image 21](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/Carbon_Monoxide_concentrations_in_spring..jpg/250px-Carbon_Monoxide_concentrations_in_spring..jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Carbon_Monoxide_concentrations_in_spring..jpg)

Carbon monoxide concentrations in Northern Hemisphere spring as measured with the MOPITT instrument

Carbon monoxide (CO) is present in small amounts (about 80 [ppb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts_per_billion "Parts per billion")) in the [Earth's atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth "Atmosphere of Earth"). Most of the rest comes from chemical reactions with [organic compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound") emitted by human activities and natural origins due to [photochemical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photochemistry "Photochemistry") reactions in the [troposphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Troposphere "Troposphere") that generate about 5 × 10 12 kilograms per year.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Weinstock-1972-35) Other natural sources of CO include volcanoes, [forest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forest_fires "Forest fires") and [bushfires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bushfire "Bushfire"), and other miscellaneous forms of combustion such as [fossil fuels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil_fuel "Fossil fuel").[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Seinfeld2006-36) Small amounts are also emitted from the ocean, and from geological activity because carbon monoxide occurs dissolved in molten volcanic rock at high pressures in the Earth's [mantle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mantle_(geology) "Mantle (geology)").[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-37) Because natural sources of carbon monoxide vary from year to year, it is difficult to accurately measure natural emissions of the gas.

Carbon monoxide has an indirect effect on [radiative forcing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiative_forcing "Radiative forcing") by elevating concentrations of direct [greenhouse gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas "Greenhouse gas"), including [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane") and [tropospheric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Troposphere "Troposphere")[ozone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone "Ozone"). CO can react chemically with other atmospheric constituents (primarily the [hydroxyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl "Hydroxyl")[radical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_(chemistry) "Radical (chemistry)"), •OH) that would otherwise destroy methane.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-38) Through natural processes in the atmosphere, it is oxidized to [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") and ozone. Carbon monoxide is short-lived in the atmosphere (with an average lifetime of about one to two months), and spatially variable in concentration.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-39)

Due to its long lifetime in the mid-troposphere, carbon monoxide is also used as a tracer for pollutant plumes.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-40)

Beyond Earth, carbon monoxide is the second-most common diatomic molecule in the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium"), after [molecular hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_hydrogen "Molecular hydrogen"). Because of its asymmetry, this [polar molecule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_molecule "Polar molecule") produces far brighter [spectral lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line "Spectral line") than the hydrogen molecule, making CO much easier to detect. Interstellar CO was first detected with [radio telescopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_telescope "Radio telescope") in 1970. It is now the most commonly used tracer of molecular gas in general in the interstellar medium of galaxies, as molecular hydrogen can only be detected using ultraviolet light, which requires [space telescopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_telescopes "Space telescopes"). Carbon monoxide observations provide much of the information about the [molecular clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_cloud "Molecular cloud") in which most [stars form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_formation "Star formation").[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-41)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-42)

[Beta Pictoris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_Pictoris "Beta Pictoris"), the second brightest star in the constellation [Pictor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pictor "Pictor"), shows an [excess of infrared emission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared_excess "Infrared excess") compared to normal stars of its type, which is caused by large quantities of dust and gas (including carbon monoxide)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-LAT-20140308-43)[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-SCI-20140306-44) near the star.

In the [atmosphere of Venus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Venus "Atmosphere of Venus") carbon monoxide occurs as a result of the photodissociation of carbon dioxide by electromagnetic radiation of wavelengths shorter than 169 [nm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanometre "Nanometre"). It has also been identified spectroscopically on the surface of Neptune's moon [Triton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triton_(moon) "Triton (moon)").[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Lellouchde_Bergh2010-45)

Solid carbon monoxide is a component of [comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet "Comet").[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-46) The [volatile or "ice"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volatile_(astrogeology) "Volatile (astrogeology)") component of [Halley's Comet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halley%27s_Comet "Halley's Comet") is about 15% CO.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Yeomans-47) At room temperature and at atmospheric pressure, carbon monoxide is actually only metastable (see [Boudouard reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boudouard_reaction "Boudouard reaction")) and the same is true at low temperatures where CO and CO 2 are solid, but nevertheless it can exist for billions of years in comets. There is very little CO in the atmosphere of [Pluto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pluto "Pluto"), which seems to have been formed from comets. This may be because there is (or was) liquid water inside Pluto.

Carbon monoxide can react with water to form carbon dioxide and hydrogen:

CO + H 2 O → H 2 + CO 2
This is called the [water-gas shift reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water-gas_shift_reaction "Water-gas shift reaction") when occurring in the gas phase, but it can also take place (very slowly) in an aqueous solution. If the hydrogen partial pressure is high enough (for instance in an underground sea), [formic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid "Formic acid") will be formed:

CO + H 2 O → HCOOH
These reactions can take place in a few million years even at temperatures such as found on Pluto.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-48)

Pollution and health effects
----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbon_monoxide&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Pollution and health effects")]

Carbon monoxide is a temporary atmospheric pollutant in some urban areas, chiefly from the exhaust of [internal combustion engines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine "Internal combustion engine") (including vehicles, portable and back-up generators, lawnmowers, power washers, etc.), but also from incomplete combustion of various other fuels (including wood, coal, charcoal, oil, paraffin, propane, natural gas, and trash).

Large CO pollution events can be observed from space over cities.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-49)

#### Role in ground level ozone formation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbon_monoxide&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Role in ground level ozone formation")]

Carbon monoxide is, along with [aldehydes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aldehyde "Aldehyde"), part of the series of cycles of chemical reactions that form [photochemical smog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photochemical_smog "Photochemical smog"). It reacts with hydroxyl radical (•OH) to produce a radical intermediate •HOCO, which rapidly transfers its radical hydrogen to O 2 to form [peroxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peroxy "Peroxy") radical (HO 2•) and carbon dioxide (CO 2).[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-50) Peroxy radical subsequently reacts with [nitrogen oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide "Nitrogen oxide") (NO) to form [nitrogen dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide "Nitrogen dioxide") (NO 2) and hydroxyl radical. NO 2 gives O(3 P) via photolysis, thereby forming O 3 following reaction with O 2. Since hydroxyl radical is formed during the formation of NO 2, the balance of the sequence of chemical reactions starting with carbon monoxide and leading to the formation of ozone is:

CO + 2 O 2 + hν → CO 2 + O 3
(where hν refers to the [photon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") of light absorbed by the NO 2 molecule in the sequence)

Although the creation of NO 2 is the critical step leading to low level [ozone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone "Ozone") formation, it also increases this ozone in another, somewhat mutually exclusive way, by reducing the quantity of NO that is available to react with ozone.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-NA-51)

### Indoor air pollution

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbon_monoxide&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Indoor air pollution")]

Carbon monoxide is one of the most acutely toxic [indoor air contaminants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indoor_air_quality "Indoor air quality"). Carbon monoxide may be emitted from tobacco smoke and generated from malfunctioning fuel burning stoves (wood, kerosene, natural gas, propane) and fuel burning heating systems (wood, oil, natural gas) and from blocked [flues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flue "Flue") connected to these appliances.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Myers-2022-7) In [developed countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developed_country "Developed country") the main sources of indoor CO emission come from cooking and heating devices that burn [fossil fuels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil_fuel "Fossil fuel") and are faulty, incorrectly installed or poorly maintained.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Penney-2010-52) Appliance malfunction may be due to faulty installation or lack of maintenance and proper use.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Myers-2022-7) In [low- and middle-income countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developing_country "Developing country") the most common sources of CO in homes are burning [biomass fuels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biofuel "Biofuel") and cigarette smoke.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Penney-2010-52)

Miners refer to carbon monoxide as "[whitedamp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitedamp "Whitedamp")" or the "silent killer". It can be found in confined areas of poor ventilation in both surface mines and underground mines. The most common sources of carbon monoxide in mining operations are the internal combustion engine and explosives; however, in coal mines, carbon monoxide can also be found due to the low-temperature oxidation of coal.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-53) The idiom "[Canary in the coal mine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canary_in_the_coal_mine "Canary in the coal mine")" pertained to an early warning of a carbon monoxide presence.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Hopper2021-54)

[Carbon monoxide poisoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide_poisoning "Carbon monoxide poisoning") is the most common type of fatal air poisoning in many countries. Acute exposure can also lead to long-term neurological effects such as cognitive and behavioural changes. Severe CO poisoning may lead to unconsciousness, coma and death. Chronic exposure to low concentrations of carbon monoxide may lead to lethargy, headaches, nausea, flu-like symptoms and neuropsychological and cardiovascular issues.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-UK_Health_Security_Agency-8)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Myers-2022-7)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Dent-2024-9)

Carbon monoxide has a wide range of functions across all disciplines of chemistry. The four premier categories of reactivity involve [metal-carbonyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal_carbonyl "Metal carbonyl") catalysis, [radical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_(chemistry) "Radical (chemistry)") chemistry, [cation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cation "Cation") and [anion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anion "Anion") chemistries.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-55)

### Coordination chemistry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbon_monoxide&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Coordination chemistry")]

[![Image 22](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/MO_COeng.svg/250px-MO_COeng.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:MO_COeng.svg)

Energy level scheme of the σ and π orbitals of carbon monoxide

[![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Carbon-monoxide-HOMO-phase-3D-balls.svg/250px-Carbon-monoxide-HOMO-phase-3D-balls.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Carbon-monoxide-HOMO-phase-3D-balls.svg)

The [HOMO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HOMO/LUMO "HOMO/LUMO") of CO is a σ [MO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_orbital "Molecular orbital").

[![Image 24](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Carbon-monoxide-LUMO-phase-3D-balls.png/250px-Carbon-monoxide-LUMO-phase-3D-balls.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Carbon-monoxide-LUMO-phase-3D-balls.png)

The [LUMO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HOMO/LUMO "HOMO/LUMO") of CO is a π* [antibonding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antibonding "Antibonding")[MO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_orbital "Molecular orbital").

Most metals form [coordination complexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordination_complex "Coordination complex") containing covalently attached carbon monoxide. These derivatives, which are called [metal carbonyls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal_carbonyls "Metal carbonyls"), tend to be more robust when the metal is in lower oxidation states. For example [iron pentacarbonyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_pentacarbonyl "Iron pentacarbonyl") (Fe(CO)5) is an air-stable, distillable liquid. [Nickel carbonyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nickel_carbonyl "Nickel carbonyl") is a [metal carbonyl complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal_carbonyl_complex "Metal carbonyl complex") that forms by the direct combination of carbon monoxide with the metal:[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-56)

Ni + 4 CO → Ni(CO)4 (1 [bar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bar_(unit) "Bar (unit)"), 55 °C)
These volatile complexes are often highly toxic. Some metal–CO complexes are prepared by decarbonylation of organic solvents, not from CO. For instance, [iridium trichloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iridium(III)_chloride "Iridium(III) chloride") and [triphenylphosphine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triphenylphosphine "Triphenylphosphine") react in boiling [2-methoxyethanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2-Methoxyethanol "2-Methoxyethanol") or [DMF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimethylformamide "Dimethylformamide") to afford [IrCl(CO)(PPh 3)2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vaska%27s_complex "Vaska's complex").

As a ligand, CO binds through carbon, forming a kind of triple bond. The lone pair on the carbon atom donates electron density to form a M-CO [sigma bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigma_bond "Sigma bond"). The two π* orbitals on CO bind to filled metal orbitals. The effect is related to the [Dewar-Chatt-Duncanson model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dewar-Chatt-Duncanson_model "Dewar-Chatt-Duncanson model"). The effects of the quasi-triple M-C bond is reflected in the [infrared spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared_spectrum "Infrared spectrum") of these complexes. Whereas free CO vibrates at 2143 cm−1, its complexes tend to absorb near 1950 cm−1.

[![Image 25: Structure of iron pentacarbonyl.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/IronPentacarbonylStructure.png/250px-IronPentacarbonylStructure.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:IronPentacarbonylStructure.png "Structure of iron pentacarbonyl.")

### Organic and main group chemistry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbon_monoxide&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Organic and main group chemistry")]

In the presence of strong acids, [alkenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkene "Alkene") react with [carboxylic acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboxylic_acids "Carboxylic acids"). Hydrolysis of this species (an [acylium ion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acylium_ion "Acylium ion")) gives the carboxylic acid, a net process known as the [Koch–Haaf reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koch%E2%80%93Haaf_reaction "Koch–Haaf reaction").[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-koch-57) In the [Gattermann–Koch reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gattermann%E2%80%93Koch_reaction "Gattermann–Koch reaction"), [arenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aromatic_hydrocarbon "Aromatic hydrocarbon") are converted to [benzaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzaldehyde "Benzaldehyde") derivatives in the presence of CO, [AlCl 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium_chloride "Aluminium chloride"), and [HCl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride "Hydrogen chloride").[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-coleman-58)

A mixture of hydrogen gas and CO reacts with [alkenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkene "Alkene") to give aldehydes. The process requires the presence of metal catalysts.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-59)

With main group reagents, CO undergoes several noteworthy reactions. [Chlorination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halogenation "Halogenation") of CO is the industrial route to the important compound [phosgene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosgene "Phosgene"). With [borane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borane "Borane") CO forms the adduct [H 3 BCO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borane_carbonyl "Borane carbonyl"), which is [isoelectronic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isoelectronic "Isoelectronic") with the [acylium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acylium "Acylium") cation [H 3 CCO]+. CO reacts with [sodium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium "Sodium") to give products resulting from C−C coupling such as [sodium acetylenediolate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylenediol "Acetylenediol")2Na+·C 2 O 2−2. It reacts with molten [potassium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium "Potassium") to give a mixture of an organometallic compound, [potassium acetylenediolate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylenediol "Acetylenediol")2K+·C 2 O 2−2, [potassium benzenehexolate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzenehexol "Benzenehexol")6K+·C 6 O 6−6,[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-wbuechIV-60) and [potassium rhodizonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhodizonic_acid "Rhodizonic acid")2K+·C 6 O 2−6.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-61)

The compounds [cyclohexanehexone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclohexanehexone "Cyclohexanehexone") or triquinoyl (C 6 O 6) and [cyclopentanepentone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclopentanepentone "Cyclopentanepentone") or leuconic acid (C 5 O 5), which so far have been obtained only in trace amounts, can be regarded as polymers of carbon monoxide. At pressures exceeding 5 [GPa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit) "Pascal (unit)"), carbon monoxide converts to [polycarbonyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycarbonyl "Polycarbonyl"), a solid polymer that is metastable at atmospheric pressure but is explosive.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-62)[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-63)

#### Laboratory preparation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbon_monoxide&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Laboratory preparation")]

Carbon monoxide is conveniently produced in the laboratory by the [dehydration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dehydration_reaction "Dehydration reaction") of [formic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid "Formic acid") or [oxalic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxalic_acid "Oxalic acid"), for example with concentrated [sulfuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid").[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-koch-57)[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-coleman-58)[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Georg-64) Another method is heating an intimate mixture of powdered [zinc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zinc "Zinc") metal and [calcium carbonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_carbonate "Calcium carbonate"), which releases CO and leaves behind [zinc oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zinc_oxide "Zinc oxide") and [calcium oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_oxide "Calcium oxide"):

Zn + CaCO 3 → ZnO + CaO + CO
[Silver nitrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silver_nitrate "Silver nitrate") and [iodoform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iodoform "Iodoform") also afford carbon monoxide:

CHI 3 + 3 AgNO 3 + H 2 O → 3 HNO 3 + CO + 3 AgI
Finally, metal [oxalate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxalate "Oxalate") salts release CO upon heating, leaving a [carbonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonate "Carbonate") as byproduct:

Na 2 C 2 O 4 → Na 2 CO 3 + CO
Thermal [combustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combustion "Combustion") is the most common source for carbon monoxide. Carbon monoxide is produced from the partial oxidation of [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon")-containing compounds; it forms when there is not enough oxygen to produce [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") (CO 2), such as when operating a [stove](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stove "Stove") or an [internal combustion engine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine "Internal combustion engine") in an enclosed space.

A large quantity of CO byproduct is formed during the oxidative processes for the production of chemicals. For this reason, the process off-gases have to be purified.

Many methods have been developed for carbon monoxide production.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-65)

### Industrial production

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbon_monoxide&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Industrial production")]

A major industrial source of CO is [producer gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Producer_gas "Producer gas"), a mixture containing mostly carbon monoxide and nitrogen, formed by combustion of carbon in air at high temperature when there is an excess of carbon. In an oven, air is passed through a bed of [coke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coke_(fuel) "Coke (fuel)"). The initially produced CO 2 equilibrates with the remaining hot carbon to give CO.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-66) The reaction of CO 2 with carbon to give CO is described as the [Boudouard reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boudouard_reaction "Boudouard reaction").[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-67) Above 800°C, CO is the predominant product:

CO 2 (g) + C (s) → 2 CO (g) (Δ _H_ r = 170 kJ/mol)
Another source is "[water gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_gas "Water gas")", a mixture of [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") and carbon monoxide produced via the endothermic reaction of [steam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steam "Steam") and carbon:

H 2 O (g) + C (s) → H 2 (g) + CO (g) (Δ _H_ r = 131 kJ/mol)
Other similar "[synthesis gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syngas "Syngas")" can be obtained from [natural gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_gas "Natural gas") and other fuels.

Carbon monoxide can also be produced by [high-temperature electrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-temperature_electrolysis "High-temperature electrolysis") of carbon dioxide with [solid oxide electrolyzer cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_oxide_electrolyzer_cell "Solid oxide electrolyzer cell").[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-68) One method developed at DTU Energy uses a cerium oxide catalyst and does not have any issues of fouling of the catalyst.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-69)[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-70)

2 CO 2 → 2 CO + O 2
Carbon monoxide is also a byproduct of the reduction of metal [oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxide "Oxide")[ores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ore "Ore") with carbon, shown in a simplified form as follows:

MO + C → M + CO
Carbon monoxide is also produced by the direct oxidation of carbon in a limited supply of oxygen or air.

2 C + O 2 → 2 CO
Since CO is a gas, the reduction process can be driven by heating, exploiting the positive (favorable) [entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy "Entropy") of reaction. The [Ellingham diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellingham_diagram "Ellingham diagram") shows that CO formation is favored over CO 2 in high temperatures.

Carbon monoxide is an [industrial gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_gas "Industrial gas") that has many applications in bulk chemicals manufacturing.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-71) Large quantities of aldehydes are produced by the [hydroformylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroformylation "Hydroformylation") reaction of [alkenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkene "Alkene"), carbon monoxide, and H 2. Hydroformylation is coupled to the [Shell higher olefin process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shell_higher_olefin_process "Shell higher olefin process") to give precursors to [detergents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detergent "Detergent").

[Phosgene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosgene "Phosgene"), useful for preparing isocyanates, polycarbonates, and polyurethanes, is produced by passing purified carbon monoxide and [chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine") gas through a bed of porous [activated carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Activated_carbon "Activated carbon"), which serves as a [catalyst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalyst "Catalyst"). World production of this compound was estimated to be 2.74 million tonnes in 1989.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Ullmann-72)

CO + Cl 2 → COCl 2
[Methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol") is produced by the [hydrogenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogenation "Hydrogenation") of carbon monoxide. In a related reaction, the hydrogenation of carbon monoxide is coupled to C−C bond formation, as in the [Fischer–Tropsch process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fischer%E2%80%93Tropsch_process "Fischer–Tropsch process") where carbon monoxide is hydrogenated to liquid hydrocarbon fuels. This technology allows [coal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal "Coal") or biomass to be converted to diesel.

In the [Cativa process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cativa_process "Cativa process"), carbon monoxide and methanol react in the presence of a homogeneous [iridium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iridium "Iridium")[catalyst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalyst "Catalyst") and [hydroiodic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroiodic_acid "Hydroiodic acid") to give [acetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid "Acetic acid"). This process is responsible for most of the industrial production of acetic acid.

Carbon monoxide is a strong reductive agent and has been used in [pyrometallurgy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrometallurgy "Pyrometallurgy") to reduce [metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal "Metal") from [ores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ore "Ore") since ancient times. Carbon monoxide strips oxygen off metal oxides, reducing them to pure metal in high temperatures, forming [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") in the process. Carbon monoxide is not usually supplied as is, in the gaseous phase, in the reactor, but rather it is formed in high temperature in presence of oxygen-carrying ore, or a carboniferous agent such as coke, and high temperature. The [blast furnace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blast_furnace "Blast furnace") process is a typical example of a process of reduction of metal from ore with carbon monoxide.

Likewise, [blast furnace gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blast_furnace_gas "Blast furnace gas") collected at the top of blast furnace, still contains some 10% to 30% of carbon monoxide, and is used as fuel on [Cowper stoves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cowper_stove "Cowper stove") and on Siemens-Martin furnaces on [open hearth steelmaking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_hearth_furnace "Open hearth furnace").

### Proposed use as a rocket fuel

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbon_monoxide&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Proposed use as a rocket fuel")]

Carbon monoxide has been proposed for use as a fuel on Mars by NASA researcher [Geoffrey Landis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geoffrey_Landis "Geoffrey Landis"). [Carbon monoxide/oxygen engines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide/oxygen_engine "Carbon monoxide/oxygen engine") have been suggested for early surface transportation use as both carbon monoxide and oxygen can be straightforwardly produced from the carbon dioxide [atmosphere of Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars "Atmosphere of Mars") by [zirconia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zirconia "Zirconia")[electrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrolysis "Electrolysis"), without using any [Martian water resources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_on_Mars "Water on Mars") to obtain hydrogen, which would be needed to make methane or any hydrogen-based fuel.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-landis2001-73)

Landis also proposed manufacturing the fuel from the similar carbon dioxide atmosphere of Venus for a sample return mission, in combination with solar-powered UAVs and rocket balloon ascent.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-o253-74)

Carbon monoxide is used in [electrochemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrochemistry "Electrochemistry") to study the structure of [electrodes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrode "Electrode") thanks to its strong affinity to some metals used as [electrocatalysts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrocatalyst "Electrocatalyst"), through a technique known as [CO stripping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CO_stripping "CO stripping").[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-75)

Biological and physiological properties
---------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbon_monoxide&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Biological and physiological properties")]

Carbon monoxide is a bioactive molecule which acts as a [gaseous signaling molecule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaseous_signaling_molecules "Gaseous signaling molecules"). It is naturally produced by many enzymatic and non-enzymatic pathways,[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Hopper-2020-76) the best understood of which is the catabolic action of [heme oxygenase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heme_oxygenase "Heme oxygenase") on the [heme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heme "Heme") derived from [hemoproteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemoprotein "Hemoprotein") such as [hemoglobin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemoglobin "Hemoglobin").[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-77) Following the first report that carbon monoxide is a normal neurotransmitter in 1993,[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Hopper2021-54) carbon monoxide has received significant clinical attention as a biological regulator.

Because of carbon monoxide's role in the body, abnormalities in its metabolism have been linked to a variety of diseases, including neurodegenerations, hypertension, heart failure, and pathological inflammation.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-endogenous_co-78) In many tissues, carbon monoxide acts as [anti-inflammatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-inflammatories "Anti-inflammatories"), [vasodilatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vasodilators "Vasodilators"), and encouragers of [neovascular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neovascular "Neovascular") growth.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-79) In animal model studies, carbon monoxide reduced the severity of experimentally induced bacterial [sepsis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sepsis "Sepsis"), pancreatitis, hepatic [ischemia/reperfusion injury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ischemia/reperfusion_injury "Ischemia/reperfusion injury"), colitis, osteoarthritis, lung injury, lung transplantation rejection, and neuropathic pain while promoting skin wound healing. Therefore, there is significant interest in the therapeutic potential of carbon monoxide becoming pharmaceutical agent and clinical standard of care.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Motterlini-2010-80)

Studies involving carbon monoxide have been conducted in many laboratories throughout the world for its anti-inflammatory and cytoprotective properties.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-81) These properties have the potential to be used to prevent the development of a series of pathological conditions including ischemia reperfusion injury, transplant rejection, atherosclerosis, severe sepsis, severe malaria, or autoimmunity.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Motterlini-2010-80) Many pharmaceutical drug delivery initiatives have developed methods to safely administer carbon monoxide, and subsequent controlled clinical trials have evaluated the therapeutic effect of carbon monoxide.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-82)

Microbiota may also utilize carbon monoxide as a [gasotransmitter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gasotransmitter "Gasotransmitter").[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-83) Carbon monoxide sensing is a signaling pathway facilitated by proteins such as [CooA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CooA "CooA").[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-84)[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-85)[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-86) The scope of the biological roles for carbon monoxide sensing is still unknown.

The human microbiome produces, consumes, and responds to carbon monoxide.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Hopper-2020-76) For example, in certain bacteria, carbon monoxide is produced via the [reduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redox "Redox") of carbon dioxide by the enzyme [carbon monoxide dehydrogenase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide_dehydrogenase "Carbon monoxide dehydrogenase") with favorable [bioenergetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bioenergetics "Bioenergetics") to power downstream cellular operations.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-87)[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Hopper-2020-76) In another example, carbon monoxide is a nutrient for [methanogenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanogen "Methanogen") archaea which reduce it to methane using hydrogen.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-88)

Carbon monoxide has certain antimicrobial properties which have been studied to treat against infectious diseases.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Hopper-2020-76)

Carbon monoxide is used in [modified atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modified_atmosphere "Modified atmosphere") packaging systems in the US, mainly with fresh meat products such as beef, pork, and fish to keep them looking fresh. The benefit is two-fold: carbon monoxide protects against microbial spoilage and it enhances the meat color for consumer appeal.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Van_Rooyen-89) The carbon monoxide combines with [myoglobin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myoglobin "Myoglobin") to form carboxymyoglobin, a bright-cherry-red pigment. Carboxymyoglobin is more stable than the oxygenated form of myoglobin, oxymyoglobin, which can become oxidized to the brown pigment [metmyoglobin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metmyoglobin "Metmyoglobin"). This stable red color can persist much longer than in normally packaged meat. Typical levels of carbon monoxide used in the facilities that use this process are between 0.4% and 0.5%.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Van_Rooyen-89)

The technology was first given "[generally recognized as safe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generally_recognized_as_safe "Generally recognized as safe")" (GRAS) status by the [U.S. Food and Drug Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Food_and_Drug_Administration "U.S. Food and Drug Administration") (FDA) in 2002 for use as a secondary packaging system, and does not require labeling. In 2004, the FDA approved CO as primary packaging method, declaring that CO does not mask spoilage odor.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Meatsci2005_eilert-90) The process is currently unauthorized in many other countries, including Japan, [Singapore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singapore "Singapore"), and the [European Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Union "European Union").[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-91)[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-92)[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-93)

In ancient history, [Hannibal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hannibal "Hannibal") executed [Roman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Rome "Ancient Rome") prisoners with coal fumes during the [Second Punic War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Punic_War "Second Punic War").[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Hopper2021-54)

Carbon monoxide had been used for [genocide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genocide "Genocide") during [the Holocaust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Holocaust "The Holocaust") at some [extermination camps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extermination_camps_in_the_Holocaust "Extermination camps in the Holocaust"), the most notable by [gas vans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_gas_van "Nazi gas van") in [Chełmno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Che%C5%82mno_extermination_camp "Chełmno extermination camp"), and in the [Action T4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_T4 "Action T4") "[euthanasia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euthanasia "Euthanasia")" program.[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-94)

Humans have maintained a complex relationship with carbon monoxide since first learning to control fire circa 800,000 BC. Early humans probably discovered the toxicity of carbon monoxide poisoning upon introducing fire into their dwellings. The early development of [metallurgy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metallurgy "Metallurgy") and [smelting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smelting "Smelting") technologies emerging circa 6,000 BC through the [Bronze Age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronze_Age "Bronze Age") likewise plagued humankind from carbon monoxide exposure. Apart from the toxicity of carbon monoxide, indigenous [Native Americans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States "Native Americans in the United States") may have experienced the neuroactive properties of carbon monoxide through [shamanistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shamanistic "Shamanistic") fireside rituals.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Hopper2021-54)

Early civilizations developed mythological tales to explain the origin of fire, such as [Prometheus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prometheus "Prometheus") from [Greek mythology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_mythology "Greek mythology") who shared fire with humans. [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") (384–322 BC) first recorded that burning coals produced toxic fumes. Greek physician [Galen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galen "Galen") (129–199 AD) speculated that there was a change in the composition of the air that caused harm when inhaled, and many others of the era developed a basis of knowledge about carbon monoxide in the context of [coal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal "Coal") fume toxicity. [Cleopatra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cleopatra "Cleopatra") may have [died](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Death_of_Cleopatra "Death of Cleopatra") from [carbon monoxide poisoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide_poisoning "Carbon monoxide poisoning").[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Hopper2021-54)

### Pre–industrial revolution

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbon_monoxide&action=edit&section=33 "Edit section: Pre–industrial revolution")]

[Georg Ernst Stahl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Ernst_Stahl "Georg Ernst Stahl") mentioned _carbonarii halitus_ in 1697 in reference to toxic vapors thought to be carbon monoxide. [Friedrich Hoffmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Hoffmann "Friedrich Hoffmann") conducted the first modern scientific investigation into carbon monoxide poisoning from coal in 1716. [Herman Boerhaave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herman_Boerhaave "Herman Boerhaave") conducted the first scientific experiments on the effect of carbon monoxide (coal fumes) on animals in the 1730s.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Hopper2021-54)

[Joseph Priestley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Priestley "Joseph Priestley") is considered to have first synthesized carbon monoxide in 1772. [Carl Wilhelm Scheele](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Scheele "Carl Wilhelm Scheele") similarly isolated carbon monoxide from charcoal in 1773 and thought it could be the carbonic entity making fumes toxic. [Torbern Bergman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torbern_Bergman "Torbern Bergman") isolated carbon monoxide from [oxalic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxalic_acid "Oxalic acid") in 1775. Later in 1776, the French chemist [de Lassone](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=De_Lassone&action=edit&redlink=1 "De Lassone (page does not exist)")[[fr](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph-Marie-Fran%C3%A7ois_de_Lassone "fr:Joseph-Marie-François de Lassone")] produced CO by heating [zinc oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zinc_oxide "Zinc oxide") with [coke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coke_(fuel) "Coke (fuel)"), but mistakenly concluded that the gaseous product was [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen"), as it burned with a blue flame. In the presence of oxygen, including atmospheric concentrations, carbon monoxide burns with a blue flame, producing carbon dioxide. [Antoine Lavoisier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antoine_Lavoisier "Antoine Lavoisier") conducted similar inconclusive experiments to Lassone in 1777. The gas was identified as a compound containing [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") and [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") by [William Cruickshank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Cruickshank_(chemist) "William Cruickshank (chemist)") in 1800.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Hopper2021-54)[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-95)

[Thomas Beddoes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Beddoes "Thomas Beddoes") and [James Watt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Watt "James Watt") recognized carbon monoxide (as [hydrocarbonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbonate_(gas) "Hydrocarbonate (gas)")) to brighten venous blood in 1793. Watt suggested coal fumes could act as an antidote to the oxygen in blood, and Beddoes and Watt likewise suggested hydrocarbonate has a greater affinity for animal fiber than oxygen in 1796. In 1854, [Adrien Chenot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adrien_Chenot "Adrien Chenot") similarly suggested carbon monoxide to remove the oxygen from blood and then be oxidized by the body to carbon dioxide.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Hopper2021-54) The mechanism for carbon monoxide poisoning is widely credited to [Claude Bernard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claude_Bernard "Claude Bernard") whose memoirs beginning in 1846 and published in 1857 phrased, "prevents arterials blood from becoming venous". [Felix Hoppe-Seyler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Hoppe-Seyler "Felix Hoppe-Seyler") independently published similar conclusions in the following year.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Hopper2021-54)

### Advent of industrial chemistry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carbon_monoxide&action=edit&section=34 "Edit section: Advent of industrial chemistry")]

Carbon monoxide gained recognition as an essential reagent in the 1900s.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-Ull-5) Three industrial processes illustrate its evolution in industry. In the [Fischer–Tropsch process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fischer%E2%80%93Tropsch_process "Fischer–Tropsch process"), coal and related carbon-rich feedstocks are converted into liquid fuels via the intermediacy of CO. Originally developed as part of the German war effort to compensate for their lack of domestic petroleum, this technology continues today. Also in Germany, a mixture of CO and hydrogen was found to combine with [olefins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olefin "Olefin") to give [aldehydes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aldehyde "Aldehyde"). This process, called [hydroformylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroformylation "Hydroformylation"), is used to produce many large scale chemicals such as [surfactants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surfactant "Surfactant") as well as specialty compounds that are popular fragrances and drugs. For example, CO is used in the production of [vitamin A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitamin_A "Vitamin A").[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide#cite_note-96) In a third major process, attributed to researchers at [Monsanto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monsanto "Monsanto"), CO combines with methanol to give [acetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid "Acetic acid"). Most acetic acid is produced by the [Cativa process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cativa_process "Cativa process"). Hydroformylation and the acetic acid syntheses are two of myriad [carbonylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonylation "Carbonylation") processes.
