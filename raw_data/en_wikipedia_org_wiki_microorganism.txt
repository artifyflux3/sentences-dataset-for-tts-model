A [cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colony_(biology)#Microbial_colonies "Colony (biology)") of _[Escherichia coli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Escherichia\_coli "Escherichia coli")_ bacteria magnified 10,000 times

A **microorganism**, or **microbe**,[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-1) is an [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism "Organism") of [microscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microscopic_scale "Microscopic scale") size, which may exist in its [single-celled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicellular_organism "Unicellular organism") form or as a [colony of cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colony_(biology)#Microbial_colonies "Colony (biology)"). The possible existence of unseen microbial life was suspected from antiquity, with an early attestation in [Jain literature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jain_literature "Jain literature") authored in 6th-century BC India. The scientific study of microorganisms began with their observation under the [microscope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microscope "Microscope") in the 1670s by [Anton van Leeuwenhoek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anton_van_Leeuwenhoek "Anton van Leeuwenhoek"). In the 1850s, [Louis Pasteur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Pasteur "Louis Pasteur") found that microorganisms caused [food spoilage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_spoilage "Food spoilage"), debunking the theory of [spontaneous generation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spontaneous_generation "Spontaneous generation"). In the 1880s, [Robert Koch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Koch "Robert Koch") discovered that microorganisms caused the diseases [tuberculosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuberculosis "Tuberculosis"), [cholera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cholera "Cholera"), [diphtheria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diphtheria "Diphtheria"), and [anthrax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthrax "Anthrax").

Microorganisms are extremely diverse, representing most [unicellular organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicellular_organism "Unicellular organism") in all [three domains of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_domains_of_life "Three domains of life"): two of the three domains, [Archaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaea "Archaea") and [Bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria "Bacteria"), only contain microorganisms. The third domain, [Eukaryota](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryota "Eukaryota"), includes all [multicellular organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multicellular_organism "Multicellular organism") as well as many unicellular [protists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protist "Protist") and [protozoans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protozoa "Protozoa") that are microbes. Some protists are related to [animals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animals "Animals") and some to [green plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_plants "Green plants"). Many multicellular organisms are also microscopic, namely [micro-animals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micro-animal "Micro-animal"), some [fungi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungus "Fungus"), and some [algae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algae "Algae").

Microorganisms can have very different [habitats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat "Habitat"), and live everywhere from the [poles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographic_pole "Geographic pole") to the [equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator "Equator"), in [deserts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desert "Desert"), [geysers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geyser "Geyser"), [rocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock_(geology) "Rock (geology)"), and the [deep sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_sea "Deep sea"). Some are [adapted to extremes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extremophiles "Extremophiles") such as [very hot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperthermophiles "Hyperthermophiles") or [very cold conditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychrophile "Psychrophile"), others to [high pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piezophile "Piezophile"), and a few, such as _[Deinococcus radiodurans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deinococcus\_radiodurans "Deinococcus radiodurans")_, to [high radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioresistance "Radioresistance") environments. Microorganisms also make up the [microbiota](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbiota "Microbiota") found in and on all [multicellular organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multicellular_organism "Multicellular organism"). There is evidence that 3.45-billion-year-old Australian rocks once contained microorganisms, the earliest direct evidence of life on Earth.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-WU-20171218-2)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-PNAS-2017-3)

Microbes are [important in human culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbes_in_human_culture "Microbes in human culture") and [health](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Health "Health") in many ways, serving to [ferment foods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermentation_in_food_processing "Fermentation in food processing") and [treat sewage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sewage_treatment "Sewage treatment"), and to [produce fuel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algae_fuel "Algae fuel"), [enzymes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enzyme "Enzyme"), and other [bioactive compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bioactive_compound "Bioactive compound"). Microbes are essential tools in [biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biology "Biology") as [model organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Model_organism "Model organism") and have been put to use in [biological warfare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_warfare "Biological warfare") and [bioterrorism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bioterrorism "Bioterrorism"). Microbes are a vital [component of fertile soil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil_microbiology "Soil microbiology"). In the [human body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_body "Human body"), microorganisms make up the [human microbiota](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_microbiota "Human microbiota"), including the essential [gut flora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gut_flora "Gut flora"). The [pathogens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathogen "Pathogen") responsible for many [infectious diseases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infectious_disease "Infectious disease") are microbes and, as such, are the target of [hygiene measures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hygiene "Hygiene").

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Spallanzani.jpg/250px-Spallanzani.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Spallanzani.jpg)

[Lazzaro Spallanzani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lazzaro_Spallanzani "Lazzaro Spallanzani") showed that boiling a broth stopped it from decaying.

The possible existence of microscopic organisms was discussed for many centuries before their discovery in the 17th century. By the 6th century BC, the [Jains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jain "Jain") of present-day India postulated the existence of tiny organisms called _[nigodas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nigoda "Nigoda")_.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-JDL_2013-4) These nigodas are said to be born in clusters; they live everywhere, including the bodies of plants, animals, and people; and their life lasts only for a fraction of a second.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-5) According to [Mahavira](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahavira "Mahavira"), the 24th preacher of Jainism, the humans destroy these nigodas on a massive scale, when they eat, breathe, sit, and move.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-JDL_2013-4) Many modern Jains assert that Mahavira's teachings presage the existence of microorganisms as discovered by modern science.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-6)

The earliest known idea to indicate the possibility of diseases spreading by yet unseen organisms was that of the Roman scholar [Marcus Terentius Varro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marcus_Terentius_Varro "Marcus Terentius Varro") in a 1st-century BC book entitled _On Agriculture_ in which he called the unseen creatures animalia minuta, and warns against locating a homestead near a swamp:[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-Varro-7)

> … and because there are bred certain minute creatures that cannot be seen by the eyes, which float in the air and enter the body through the mouth and nose and they cause serious diseases.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-Varro-7)

In _[The Canon of Medicine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Canon\_of\_Medicine "The Canon of Medicine")_ (1020), [Avicenna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avicenna "Avicenna") suggested that [tuberculosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuberculosis "Tuberculosis") and other diseases might be contagious.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-8)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-9)

Turkish scientist [Akshamsaddin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Akshamsaddin "Akshamsaddin") mentioned the microbe in his work _Maddat ul-Hayat_ (The Material of Life) about two centuries prior to Leeuwenhoek's experimental discovery:

> It is a mistake to assume that diseases appear in individuals one by one. Diseases are transmitted from person to person. This transmission takes place through small seeds that are invisible to the eye, but are still alive.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-10)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-11)

In [1546](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1546_in_science#Medicine "1546 in science"), [Girolamo Fracastoro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Girolamo_Fracastoro "Girolamo Fracastoro") proposed that [epidemic diseases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epidemic_diseases "Epidemic diseases") were caused by transferable seedlike entities that could transmit infection by direct or indirect contact, or even without contact over long distances.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-12)

[Antonie van Leeuwenhoek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antonie_van_Leeuwenhoek "Antonie van Leeuwenhoek") is considered to be one of the [fathers of microbiology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_people_considered_father_or_mother_of_a_scientific_field "List of people considered father or mother of a scientific field"). He was the first in 1673 to discover and conduct scientific experiments with microorganisms, using simple single-lensed [microscopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microscope "Microscope") of his own design.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-13)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-14)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-NickLane_RS-15)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-16)[Robert Hooke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Hooke "Robert Hooke"), a contemporary of Leeuwenhoek, also used [microscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microscopy "Microscopy") to observe microbial life in the form of the fruiting bodies of [moulds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mold_(fungus) "Mold (fungus)"). In his [1665](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1665#July%E2%80%93December "1665") book _[Micrographia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrographia "Micrographia")_, he made drawings of studies, and he coined the term _[cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell\_(biology) "Cell (biology)")_.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-17)

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Albert_Edelfelt_-_Louis_Pasteur_-_1885.jpg/250px-Albert_Edelfelt_-_Louis_Pasteur_-_1885.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Albert_Edelfelt_-_Louis_Pasteur_-_1885.jpg)

[Louis Pasteur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Pasteur "Louis Pasteur") showed that Spallanzani's findings held even if air could enter through a filter that kept particles out.

[Louis Pasteur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Pasteur "Louis Pasteur") (1822–1895) exposed boiled broths to the air, in vessels that contained a filter to prevent particles from passing through to the [growth medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Growth_medium "Growth medium"), and also in vessels without a filter, but with air allowed in via a curved tube so dust particles would settle and not come in contact with the broth. By boiling the broth beforehand, Pasteur ensured that no microorganisms survived within the broths at the beginning of his experiment. Nothing grew in the broths in the course of Pasteur's experiment. This meant that the living organisms that grew in such broths came from outside, as [spores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spore "Spore") on dust, rather than spontaneously generated within the broth. Thus, Pasteur refuted the theory of [spontaneous generation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spontaneous_generation "Spontaneous generation") and supported the [germ theory of disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germ_theory_of_disease "Germ theory of disease").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-18)

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Robert_Koch.jpg/250px-Robert_Koch.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Robert_Koch.jpg)

[Robert Koch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Koch "Robert Koch") showed that microorganisms caused [disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disease "Disease").

In 1876, [Robert Koch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Koch "Robert Koch") (1843–1910) established that microorganisms can cause disease. He found that the blood of cattle that were infected with [anthrax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthrax "Anthrax") always had large numbers of _[Bacillus anthracis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacillus\_anthracis "Bacillus anthracis")_. Koch found that he could transmit anthrax from one animal to another by taking a small sample of blood from the infected animal and injecting it into a healthy one, and this caused the healthy animal to become sick. He also found that he could grow the bacteria in a nutrient broth, then inject it into a healthy animal, and cause illness. Based on these experiments, he devised criteria for establishing a causal link between a microorganism and a disease and these are now known as [Koch's postulates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koch%27s_postulates "Koch's postulates").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-19) Although these postulates cannot be applied in all cases, they do retain historical importance to the development of scientific thought and are still being used today.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-20)

The discovery of microorganisms such as _[Euglena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euglena "Euglena")_ that did not fit into either the [animal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal "Animal") or [plant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant "Plant") kingdoms, since they were [photosynthetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthetic "Photosynthetic") like plants, but [motile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motile "Motile") like animals, led to the naming of a third kingdom in the 1860s. In 1860 [John Hogg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Hogg_(biologist) "John Hogg (biologist)") called this the [Protoctista](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protoctista "Protoctista"), and in 1866 [Ernst Haeckel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernst_Haeckel "Ernst Haeckel") named it the [Protista](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protista "Protista").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-Scamardella1999-21)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-Rothschild1989-22)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-Pearl2005-23)

The work of Pasteur and Koch did not accurately reflect the true diversity of the microbial world because of their exclusive focus on microorganisms having direct medical relevance. It was not until the work of [Martinus Beijerinck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martinus_Beijerinck "Martinus Beijerinck") and [Sergei Winogradsky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sergei_Winogradsky "Sergei Winogradsky") in the late 19th century that the true breadth of microbiology was revealed.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-Brock-24) Beijerinck made two major contributions to microbiology: the discovery of [viruses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virus "Virus") and the development of [enrichment culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enrichment_culture "Enrichment culture") techniques.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-25) While his work on the [tobacco mosaic virus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tobacco_mosaic_virus "Tobacco mosaic virus") established the basic principles of virology, it was his development of enrichment culturing that had the most immediate impact on microbiology by allowing for the cultivation of a wide range of microbes with wildly different physiologies. Winogradsky was the first to develop the concept of [chemolithotrophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemolithotrophy "Chemolithotrophy") and to thereby reveal the essential role played by microorganisms in geochemical processes.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-26) He was responsible for the first isolation and description of both [nitrifying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrifying_bacteria "Nitrifying bacteria") and [nitrogen-fixing bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen-fixing_bacteria "Nitrogen-fixing bacteria").[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-Brock-24) French-Canadian microbiologist [Félix d'Hérelle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/F%C3%A9lix_d%27H%C3%A9relle "Félix d'Hérelle") co-discovered [bacteriophages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteriophage "Bacteriophage") and was one of the earliest applied microbiologists.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-Keen-27)

Classification and structure
----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Microorganism&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Classification and structure")]

Microorganisms can be found almost anywhere on [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth"). [Bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria "Bacteria") and [archaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaea "Archaea") are almost always microscopic, while a number of [eukaryotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryote "Eukaryote") are also microscopic, including most [protists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protista "Protista"), some [fungi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungus "Fungus"), as well as some [micro-animals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micro-animal "Micro-animal") and plants. [Viruses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virus "Virus") are generally regarded as [not living](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-cellular_life "Non-cellular life") and therefore not considered to be microorganisms, although a subfield of [microbiology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbiology "Microbiology") is [virology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virology "Virology"), the study of viruses.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-28)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-29)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-30)

![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Phylogenetic_tree.svg/330px-Phylogenetic_tree.svg.png)

[Carl Woese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Woese "Carl Woese")'s 1990 [phylogenetic tree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phylogenetic_tree "Phylogenetic tree") based on [rRNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RRNA "RRNA") data shows the domains of [Bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria "Bacteria"), [Archaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaea "Archaea"), and [Eukaryota](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryota "Eukaryota"). All are microorganisms except some eukaryote groups.

Single-celled microorganisms were the [first forms of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_of_life "Origin of life") to develop on Earth, approximately 3.5 billion years ago.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-31)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-32)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-33) Further evolution was slow,[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-34) and for about 3 billion years in the [Precambrian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precambrian "Precambrian")[eon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eon_(geology) "Eon (geology)"), (much of the history of [life on Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life "Life")), all [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism "Organism") were microorganisms.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-35)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-36) Bacteria, algae and fungi have been identified in [amber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amber "Amber") that is 220 million years old, which shows that the [morphology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphology_(biology) "Morphology (biology)") of microorganisms has changed little since at least the [Triassic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triassic "Triassic") period.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-37) The newly discovered [biological role played by nickel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nickel#Biological_role "Nickel"), however – especially that brought about by [volcanic eruptions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Types_of_volcanic_eruption "Types of volcanic eruption") from the [Siberian Traps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siberian_Traps "Siberian Traps") – may have accelerated the evolution of [methanogens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanogen "Methanogen") towards the end of the [Permian–Triassic extinction event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permian%E2%80%93Triassic_extinction_event "Permian–Triassic extinction event").[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-38)

Microorganisms tend to have a relatively fast rate of evolution. Most microorganisms can reproduce rapidly, and bacteria are also able to freely exchange genes through [conjugation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacterial_conjugation "Bacterial conjugation"), [transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformation_(genetics) "Transformation (genetics)") and [transduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transduction_(genetics) "Transduction (genetics)"), even between widely divergent species.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-39) This [horizontal gene transfer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizontal_gene_transfer "Horizontal gene transfer"), coupled with a high [mutation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutation "Mutation") rate and other means of transformation, allows microorganisms to swiftly [evolve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_evolution "Biological evolution") (via [natural selection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_selection "Natural selection")) to survive in new environments and respond to [environmental stresses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stressors "Stressors"). This rapid evolution is important in medicine, as it has led to the development of [multidrug resistant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multidrug_resistance "Multidrug resistance")[pathogenic bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathogenic_bacteria "Pathogenic bacteria"), _superbugs_, that are [resistant to antibiotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antimicrobial_resistance "Antimicrobial resistance").[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-40)

A possible transitional form of microorganism between a prokaryote and a eukaryote was discovered in 2012 by Japanese scientists. _[Parakaryon myojinensis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parakaryon\_myojinensis "Parakaryon myojinensis")_ is a unique microorganism larger than a typical prokaryote, but with nuclear material enclosed in a membrane as in a eukaryote, and the presence of endosymbionts. This is seen to be the first plausible evolutionary form of microorganism, showing a stage of development from the prokaryote to the eukaryote.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-Parakaryon-41)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-Yamaguchi-42)

Archaea are [prokaryotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prokaryote "Prokaryote") unicellular organisms, and form the first domain of life in [Carl Woese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Woese "Carl Woese")'s [three-domain system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-domain_system "Three-domain system"). A prokaryote is defined as having no [cell nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_nucleus "Cell nucleus") or other [membrane bound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipid_bilayer "Lipid bilayer")-[organelle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organelle "Organelle"). Archaea share this defining feature with the bacteria with which they were once grouped. In 1990 the microbiologist Woese proposed the three-domain system that divided living things into bacteria, archaea and eukaryotes,[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-43) and thereby split the prokaryote domain.

Archaea differ from bacteria in both their genetics and biochemistry. For example, while bacterial [cell membranes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_membrane "Cell membrane") are made from [phosphoglycerides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phospholipid "Phospholipid") with [ester](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ester "Ester") bonds, Achaean membranes are made of [ether lipids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ether_lipid "Ether lipid").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-44) Archaea were originally described as [extremophiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extremophile "Extremophile") living in [extreme environments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extreme_environment "Extreme environment"), such as [hot springs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hot_spring "Hot spring"), but have since been found in all types of [habitats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat "Habitat").[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-45) Only now are scientists beginning to realize how common archaea are in the environment, with [Thermoproteota](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermoproteota "Thermoproteota") (formerly Crenarchaeota) being the most common form of life in the ocean, dominating ecosystems below 150 metres (490 ft) in depth.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-46)[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-47) These organisms are also common in soil and play a vital role in [ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia") oxidation.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-LeiningerUrich2006-48)

The combined domains of archaea and bacteria make up the most diverse and abundant group of [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism "Organism") on Earth and inhabit practically all environments where the temperature is below +140°C (284°F). They are found in [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water "Water"), [soil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil "Soil"), [air](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere "Earth's atmosphere"), as the [microbiome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbiome "Microbiome") of an organism, [hot springs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hot_spring "Hot spring") and even deep beneath the Earth's crust in [rocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock_(geology) "Rock (geology)").[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-Gold-49) The number of prokaryotes is estimated to be around five nonillion, or 5 × 10 30, accounting for at least half the [biomass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomass_(ecology) "Biomass (ecology)") on Earth.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-50)

The biodiversity of the prokaryotes is unknown, but may be very large. A May 2016 estimate, based on laws of scaling from known numbers of species against the size of organism, gives an estimate of perhaps 1 trillion species on the planet, of which most would be microorganisms. Currently, only one-thousandth of one percent of that total have been described.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-NSF-2016002-51)[Archael cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaea "Archaea") of some species aggregate and transfer [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA "DNA") from one cell to another through direct contact, particularly under stressful environmental conditions that cause [DNA damage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA_damage_(naturally_occurring) "DNA damage (naturally occurring)").[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-52)[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-53)

_[Staphylococcus aureus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Staphylococcus\_aureus "Staphylococcus aureus")_ bacteria magnified about 10,000×

Like archaea, bacteria are prokaryotic – unicellular, and having no cell nucleus or other membrane-bound organelle. Bacteria are microscopic, with a few extremely rare exceptions, such as _[Thiomargarita namibiensis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thiomargarita\_namibiensis "Thiomargarita namibiensis")_.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-54) Bacteria function and reproduce as individual cells, but they can often aggregate in multicellular [colonies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colony_(biology)#Microbial_colony "Colony (biology)").[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-55) Some species such as [myxobacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myxobacteria "Myxobacteria") can aggregate into complex [swarming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swarm "Swarm") structures, operating as multicellular groups as part of their [life cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_life_cycle "Biological life cycle"),[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-56) or form clusters in [bacterial colonies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colony_(biology) "Colony (biology)") such as _[E. coli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E.\_coli "E. coli")_.

Their [genome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genome "Genome") is usually a [circular bacterial chromosome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_bacterial_chromosome "Circular bacterial chromosome") – a single loop of [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA "DNA"), although they can also harbor small pieces of DNA called [plasmids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasmid "Plasmid"). These plasmids can be transferred between cells through [bacterial conjugation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacterial_conjugation "Bacterial conjugation"). Bacteria have an enclosing [cell wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacterial_cell_structure#Cell_wall "Bacterial cell structure"), which provides strength and rigidity to their cells. They reproduce by [binary fission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_fission "Binary fission") or sometimes by [budding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Budding "Budding"), but do not undergo [meiotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meiosis "Meiosis")[sexual reproduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexual_reproduction "Sexual reproduction"). However, many bacterial species can transfer DNA between individual cells by a [horizontal gene transfer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizontal_gene_transfer "Horizontal gene transfer") process referred to as natural [transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformation_(genetics) "Transformation (genetics)").[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-57) Some species form extraordinarily resilient [spores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endospore "Endospore"), but for bacteria this is a mechanism for survival, not reproduction. Under optimal conditions bacteria can grow extremely rapidly and their numbers can double as quickly as every 20 minutes.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-58)

Most living things that are visible to the naked eye in their adult form are [eukaryotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryote "Eukaryote"), including [humans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human "Human"). However, many eukaryotes are also microorganisms. Unlike [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria "Bacteria") and [archaea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaea "Archaea"), eukaryotes contain [organelles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organelle "Organelle") such as the [cell nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_nucleus "Cell nucleus"), the [Golgi apparatus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golgi_apparatus "Golgi apparatus") and [mitochondria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mitochondrion "Mitochondrion") in their [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(biology) "Cell (biology)"). The nucleus is an organelle that houses the [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA "DNA") that makes up a cell's genome. DNA (Deoxyribonucleic acid) itself is arranged in complex [chromosomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromosome "Chromosome").[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-59)[Mitochondria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mitochondrion "Mitochondrion") are organelles vital in [metabolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism "Metabolism") as they are the site of the [citric acid cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citric_acid_cycle "Citric acid cycle") and [oxidative phosphorylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidative_phosphorylation "Oxidative phosphorylation"). They evolved from [symbiotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbiotic "Symbiotic") bacteria and retain a remnant genome.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-Dyall-60) Like bacteria, [plant cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant_cell "Plant cell") have [cell walls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_wall "Cell wall"), and contain organelles such as [chloroplasts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloroplast "Chloroplast") in addition to the organelles in other eukaryotes. Chloroplasts produce energy from light by [photosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis "Photosynthesis"), and were also originally symbiotic bacteria.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-Dyall-60)

Unicellular eukaryotes consist of a single [cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(biology) "Cell (biology)") throughout their life cycle. This qualification is significant since most [multicellular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multicellular_organism "Multicellular organism") eukaryotes consist of a single cell called a [zygote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zygote "Zygote") only at the beginning of their life cycles. Microbial eukaryotes can be either [haploid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haploid "Haploid") or [diploid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diploid "Diploid"), and some organisms have multiple [cell nuclei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_nucleus "Cell nucleus").

Unicellular eukaryotes usually reproduce asexually by [mitosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mitosis "Mitosis") under favorable conditions. However, under stressful conditions such as nutrient limitations and other conditions associated with DNA damage, they tend to reproduce sexually by [meiosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meiosis "Meiosis") and [syngamy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fertilization "Fertilization").[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-Bernstein-61)

_[Euglena mutabilis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euglena "Euglena")_, a [photosynthetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthetic "Photosynthetic")[flagellate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flagellate "Flagellate")

Of [eukaryotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryote "Eukaryote") groups, the [protists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protists "Protists") are most commonly [unicellular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicellular "Unicellular") and microscopic. This is a highly diverse group of organisms that are not easy to classify.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-62)[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-63) Several [algae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algae "Algae")[species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species "Species") are [multicellular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multicellular "Multicellular") protists, and [slime molds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slime_mold "Slime mold") have unique life cycles that involve switching between unicellular, colonial, and multicellular forms.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-64) The number of species of protists is unknown since only a small proportion has been identified. Protist diversity is high in oceans, deep sea-vents, river sediment and an acidic river, suggesting that many eukaryotic microbial communities may yet be discovered.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-65)[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-66)

The [fungi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungus "Fungus") have several unicellular species, such as baker's yeast (_[Saccharomyces cerevisiae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saccharomyces\_cerevisiae "Saccharomyces cerevisiae")_) and fission yeast (_[Schizosaccharomyces pombe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schizosaccharomyces\_pombe "Schizosaccharomyces pombe")_). Some fungi, such as the pathogenic yeast _[Candida albicans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Candida\_albicans "Candida albicans")_, can undergo [phenotypic switching](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenotypic_switching "Phenotypic switching") and grow as single cells in some environments, and [filamentous hyphae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypha "Hypha") in others.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-67)

The [green algae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_algae "Green algae") are a large group of photosynthetic eukaryotes that include many microscopic organisms. Although some green algae are classified as [protists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protist "Protist"), others such as [charophyta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charophyta "Charophyta") are classified with [embryophyte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embryophyte "Embryophyte") plants, which are the most familiar group of land plants. Algae can grow as single cells, or in long chains of cells. The green algae include unicellular and colonial [flagellates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flagellate "Flagellate"), usually but not always with two [flagella](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flagellum "Flagellum") per cell, as well as various colonial, [coccoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorococcales "Chlorococcales"), and filamentous forms. In the [Charales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charales "Charales"), which are the algae most closely related to higher plants, cells differentiate into several distinct tissues within the organism. There are about 6000 species of green algae.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-68)

Microorganisms are found in almost every [habitat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat_(ecology) "Habitat (ecology)") present in nature, including hostile environments such as the [North and South poles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographic_poles "Geographic poles"), [deserts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desert "Desert"), [geysers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geyser "Geyser"), and [rocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock_(geology) "Rock (geology)"). They also include all the [marine microorganisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_microorganism "Marine microorganism") of the [oceans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_ocean "World ocean") and [deep sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_sea "Deep sea"). Some types of microorganisms have adapted to [extreme environments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extreme_environment "Extreme environment") and sustained colonies; these organisms are known as [extremophiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extremophiles "Extremophiles"). Extremophiles have been isolated from rocks as much as 7 kilometres below the Earth's surface,[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-69) and it has been suggested that the amount of organisms living below the Earth's surface is comparable with the amount of life on or above the surface.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-Gold-49) Extremophiles have been known to survive for a prolonged time in a [vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum "Vacuum"), and can be highly resistant to [radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet_radiation "Ultraviolet radiation"), which may even allow them to survive in space.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-70) Many types of microorganisms have intimate [symbiotic relationships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbiosis "Symbiosis") with other larger organisms; some of which are mutually beneficial ([mutualism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutualism_(biology) "Mutualism (biology)")), while others can be damaging to the [host](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Host_(biology) "Host (biology)") organism ([parasitism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parasitism "Parasitism")). If microorganisms can cause [disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disease "Disease") in a host they are known as _[pathogens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathogen "Pathogen")_. Microorganisms play critical roles in Earth's [biogeochemical cycles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biogeochemical_cycle "Biogeochemical cycle") as they are responsible for [decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decomposition "Decomposition") and [nitrogen fixation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation "Nitrogen fixation").[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-71)

Bacteria use [regulatory networks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene_regulatory_network "Gene regulatory network") that allow them to adapt to almost every environmental niche on earth.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-FillouxAAM-72)[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-GrossRBeierD-73) A network of interactions among diverse types of molecules including DNA, RNA, proteins and metabolites, is utilised by the bacteria to achieve [regulation of gene expression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regulation_of_gene_expression "Regulation of gene expression"). In bacteria, the principal function of regulatory networks is to control the response to environmental changes, for example nutritional status and environmental stress.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-RequenaJM-74) A complex organization of networks permits the microorganism to coordinate and integrate multiple environmental signals.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-FillouxAAM-72)

[Extremophiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extremophiles "Extremophiles") are microorganisms that have adapted so that they can survive and even thrive in [extreme environments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extreme_environment "Extreme environment") that are normally fatal to most life-forms. [Thermophiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermophile "Thermophile") and [hyperthermophiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperthermophiles "Hyperthermophiles") thrive in high [temperatures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature"). [Psychrophiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychrophile "Psychrophile") thrive in extremely low temperatures. – Temperatures as high as 130°C (266°F),[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-75) as low as −17°C [vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum "Vacuum") of [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space "Outer space").[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-81) A few [Gy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gray_(unit) "Gray (unit)"). Extremophiles are significant in different ways. They extend terrestrial life into much of the Earth's [hydrosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrosphere "Hydrosphere"), [crust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crust_(geology) "Crust (geology)") and [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere "Atmosphere"), their specific evolutionary adaptation mechanisms to their extreme environment can be exploited in [biotechnology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biotechnology "Biotechnology"), and their very existence under such extreme conditions increases the potential for [extraterrestrial life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life "Extraterrestrial life").[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-83)

The [nitrogen cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle "Nitrogen cycle") in soils depends on the [fixation of atmospheric nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation "Nitrogen fixation"). This is achieved by a number of [diazotrophs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diazotroph "Diazotroph"). One way this can occur is in the [root nodules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root_nodule "Root nodule") of [legumes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legumes "Legumes") that contain symbiotic [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria "Bacteria") of the genera _[Rhizobium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhizobium "Rhizobium")_, _[Mesorhizobium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesorhizobium "Mesorhizobium")_, _[Sinorhizobium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sinorhizobium "Sinorhizobium")_, _[Bradyrhizobium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bradyrhizobium "Bradyrhizobium")_, and _[Azorhizobium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azorhizobium "Azorhizobium")_.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-84)

The [roots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root "Root") of plants create a narrow region known as the [rhizosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhizosphere "Rhizosphere") that supports many microorganisms known as the [root microbiome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root_microbiome "Root microbiome").[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-Gottel-85)

These microorganisms in the [root microbiome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Root_microbiome "Root microbiome") are able to interact with each other and surrounding plants through signals and cues. For example, [mycorrhizal fungi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mycorrhizal_fungi "Mycorrhizal fungi") are able to communicate with the root systems of many plants through chemical signals between both the plant and fungi. This results in a mutualistic [symbiosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbiosis "Symbiosis") between the two. However, these signals can be eavesdropped by other microorganisms, such as the [soil bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil_bacteria "Soil bacteria"), _Myxococcus xanthus_, which preys on other bacteria. Eavesdropping, or the interception of signals from unintended receivers, such as plants and microorganisms, can lead to large-scale, evolutionary consequences. For example, signaler-receiver pairs, like plant-microorganism pairs, may lose the ability to communicate with neighboring populations because of variability in eavesdroppers. In adapting to avoid local eavesdroppers, signal divergence could occur and thus, lead to the isolation of plants and microorganisms from the inability to communicate with other populations.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-86)

The photosynthetic [cyanobacterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanobacterium "Cyanobacterium")_Hyella caespitosa_ (round shapes) with fungal hyphae (translucent threads) in the [lichen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lichen "Lichen")_Pyrenocollema halodytes_

A [lichen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lichen "Lichen") is a [symbiosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbiosis "Symbiosis") of a macroscopic fungus with [photosynthetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthetic "Photosynthetic") microbial [algae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alga "Alga") or [cyanobacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanobacteria "Cyanobacteria").[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-87)[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-88)

Microorganisms are useful in producing foods, treating waste water, creating biofuels and a wide range of chemicals and enzymes. They are invaluable in research as [model organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Model_organism "Model organism"). They have been [weaponised](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_agent "Biological agent") and sometimes used in [warfare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_warfare "Biological warfare") and [bioterrorism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bioterrorism "Bioterrorism"). They are vital to agriculture through their roles in maintaining [soil fertility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil_fertility "Soil fertility") and in decomposing organic matter. They also have applications in aquaculture, such as in [biofloc technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biofloc_Technology "Biofloc Technology").

Microorganisms are used in a [fermentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermentation_(food) "Fermentation (food)") process to make [yoghurt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoghurt "Yoghurt"), [cheese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cheese "Cheese"), [curd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curd "Curd"), [kefir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kefir "Kefir"), [ayran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ayran "Ayran"), [xynogala](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermented_milk_products "Fermented milk products"), and other types of food. Fermentation cultures provide flavour and aroma, and inhibit undesirable organisms.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-89) They are used to [leaven](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leavening_agent "Leavening agent")[bread](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bread "Bread"), and to convert [sugars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sugar "Sugar") to [alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol "Ethanol") in [wine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wine "Wine") and [beer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beer "Beer"). Microorganisms are used in [brewing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brewing "Brewing"), [wine making](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wine_making "Wine making"), [baking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baking "Baking"), [pickling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pickling "Pickling") and other [food](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food "Food")-making processes.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-HuiMeunier-Goddik2004-90)

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e8/WWTP_Antwerpen-Zuid.jpg/250px-WWTP_Antwerpen-Zuid.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:WWTP_Antwerpen-Zuid.jpg)

[Wastewater treatment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wastewater_treatment "Wastewater treatment") treatment plants rely largely on microorganisms to [oxidise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidise "Oxidise") organic matter.

These depend for their ability to clean up water contaminated with organic material on microorganisms that can respire dissolved substances. Respiration may be aerobic, with a well-oxygenated filter bed such as a [slow sand filter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slow_sand_filter "Slow sand filter").[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-91)[Anaerobic digestion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaerobic_digestion "Anaerobic digestion") by [methanogens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanogen "Methanogen") generate useful [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane") gas as a by-product.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-92)

Microorganisms are used in [fermentation to produce ethanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol_fermentation "Ethanol fermentation"),[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-93) and in [biogas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biogas "Biogas") reactors to produce [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane").[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-94) Scientists are researching the use of [algae to produce liquid fuels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algae_fuel "Algae fuel"),[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-95) and bacteria to convert various forms of agricultural and urban waste into [usable fuels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellulosic_ethanol "Cellulosic ethanol").[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-96)

Microorganisms are used to produce many commercial and industrial chemicals, [enzymes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enzymes "Enzymes") and other bioactive molecules. Organic acids produced on a large industrial scale by microbial fermentation include [acetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid "Acetic acid") produced by [acetic acid bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid_bacteria "Acetic acid bacteria") such as _[Acetobacter aceti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetobacter\_aceti "Acetobacter aceti")_, [butyric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butyric_acid "Butyric acid") made by the bacterium _[Clostridium butyricum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clostridium\_butyricum "Clostridium butyricum")_, [lactic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lactic_acid "Lactic acid") made by _[Lactobacillus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lactobacillus "Lactobacillus")_ and other [lactic acid bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lactic_acid_bacteria "Lactic acid bacteria"),[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-Sauer-97) and [citric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citric_acid "Citric acid") produced by the mould fungus _[Aspergillus niger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aspergillus\_niger "Aspergillus niger")_.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-Sauer-97)

Microorganisms are used to prepare bioactive molecules such as [Streptokinase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Streptokinase "Streptokinase") from the bacterium _[Streptococcus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Streptococcus "Streptococcus")_,[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-98)[Cyclosporin A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclosporin_A "Cyclosporin A") from the ascomycete fungus _[Tolypocladium inflatum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tolypocladium\_inflatum "Tolypocladium inflatum")_,[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-99) and [statins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statins "Statins") produced by the yeast _[Monascus purpureus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monascus\_purpureus "Monascus purpureus")_.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-100)

[![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/Biofermentor.jpeg/250px-Biofermentor.jpeg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Biofermentor.jpeg)

A laboratory [fermentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermentation "Fermentation") vessel

Microorganisms are essential tools in [biotechnology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biotechnology "Biotechnology"), [biochemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biochemistry "Biochemistry"), [genetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetics "Genetics"), and [molecular biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_biology "Molecular biology"). The [yeasts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yeast "Yeast")_[Saccharomyces cerevisiae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saccharomyces\_cerevisiae "Saccharomyces cerevisiae")_ and _[Schizosaccharomyces pombe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schizosaccharomyces\_pombe "Schizosaccharomyces pombe")_ are important [model organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Model_organism "Model organism") in science, since they are simple eukaryotes that can be grown rapidly in large numbers and are easily manipulated.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-101) They are particularly valuable in [genetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetics "Genetics"), [genomics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genomics "Genomics") and [proteomics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proteomics "Proteomics").[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-102)[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-103) Microorganisms can be harnessed for uses such as creating steroids and treating skin diseases. Scientists are also considering using microorganisms for living [fuel cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fuel_cells "Fuel cells"),[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-104) and as a solution for pollution.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-105)

In the [Middle Ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Ages "Middle Ages"), as an early example of [biological warfare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_warfare "Biological warfare"), diseased corpses were thrown into castles during [sieges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege "Siege") using catapults or other [siege engines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siege_engine "Siege engine"). Individuals near the corpses were exposed to the pathogen and were likely to spread that pathogen to others.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-106)

In modern times, [bioterrorism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bioterrorism "Bioterrorism") has included the [1984 Rajneeshee bioterror attack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1984_Rajneeshee_bioterror_attack "1984 Rajneeshee bioterror attack")[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-107) and the 1993 release of [anthrax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthrax "Anthrax") by [Aum Shinrikyo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aum_Shinrikyo "Aum Shinrikyo") in Tokyo.[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-108)

Microbes can make [nutrients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrient "Nutrient") and minerals in the soil available to plants, produce [hormones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hormones "Hormones") that spur growth, stimulate the plant [immune system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system "Immune system") and trigger or dampen stress responses. In general a more diverse set of [soil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil_biology "Soil biology") microbes results in fewer plant diseases and higher yield.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-:02-109)

Microorganisms can form an [endosymbiotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endosymbiont "Endosymbiont") relationship with other, larger organisms. For example, [microbial symbiosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbial_symbiosis_and_immunity "Microbial symbiosis and immunity") plays a crucial role in the immune system. The microorganisms that make up the [gut flora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gut_flora "Gut flora") in the [gastrointestinal tract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract "Gastrointestinal tract") contribute to gut immunity, synthesize [vitamins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitamin "Vitamin") such as [folic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Folic_acid "Folic acid") and [biotin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biotin "Biotin"), and ferment complex indigestible [carbohydrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbohydrate "Carbohydrate").[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-110) Some microorganisms that are seen to be beneficial to health are termed [probiotics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probiotic "Probiotic") and are available as [dietary supplements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dietary_supplement "Dietary supplement"), or [food additives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_additive#Fortifying_agents "Food additive").[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-who-111)

[![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Plasmodium.jpg/250px-Plasmodium.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Plasmodium.jpg)

The [eukaryotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryotic "Eukaryotic")[parasite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parasite "Parasite")_[Plasmodium falciparum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasmodium\_falciparum "Plasmodium falciparum")_ (spiky blue shapes), a causative agent of [malaria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malaria "Malaria"), in human blood

Microorganisms are the causative agents ([pathogens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathogen "Pathogen")) in many [infectious diseases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infection "Infection"). The organisms involved include [pathogenic bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathogenic_bacteria "Pathogenic bacteria"), causing diseases such as [plague](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bubonic_plague "Bubonic plague"), [tuberculosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuberculosis "Tuberculosis") and [anthrax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthrax "Anthrax"); [protozoan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protozoa "Protozoa")[parasites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parasite "Parasite"), causing diseases such as [malaria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malaria "Malaria"), [sleeping sickness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/African_trypanosomiasis "African trypanosomiasis"), [dysentery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dysentery "Dysentery") and [toxoplasmosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxoplasmosis "Toxoplasmosis"); and also fungi causing diseases such as [ringworm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ringworm "Ringworm"), [candidiasis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Candidiasis "Candidiasis") or [histoplasmosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Histoplasmosis "Histoplasmosis"). However, other diseases such as [influenza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Influenza "Influenza"), [yellow fever](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yellow_fever "Yellow fever") or [AIDS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AIDS "AIDS") are caused by [pathogenic viruses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathogenic_viruses "Pathogenic viruses"), which are not usually classified as living organisms and are not, therefore, microorganisms by the strict definition. No clear examples of archaean pathogens are known,[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-112) although a relationship has been proposed between the presence of some archaean methanogens and human [periodontal disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodontal_disease "Periodontal disease").[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-113) Numerous microbial pathogens are capable of sexual processes that appear to facilitate their survival in their infected host.[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-114)

Hygiene is a set of practices to avoid [infection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infection "Infection") or [food spoilage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_spoilage "Food spoilage") by eliminating microorganisms from the surroundings. As microorganisms, in particular bacteria, are found virtually everywhere, [harmful microorganisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathogen "Pathogen") may be reduced to acceptable levels rather than actually eliminated. In food preparation, microorganisms are reduced by [preservation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_preservation "Food preservation") methods such as cooking, cleanliness of utensils, short storage periods, or by low temperatures. If complete sterility is needed, as with surgical equipment, an [autoclave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autoclave "Autoclave") is used to kill microorganisms with heat and pressure.[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-115)[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#cite_note-116)
