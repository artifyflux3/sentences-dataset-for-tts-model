From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Alternative width characters in East Asian typography

For the Unicode block, see [Halfwidth and Fullwidth Forms (Unicode block)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halfwidth_and_Fullwidth_Forms_(Unicode_block) "Halfwidth and Fullwidth Forms (Unicode block)").

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a8/Command_Prompt_on_Windows_XP_%28Korean%29.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Command_Prompt_on_Windows_XP_(Korean).png)

A command prompt ([cmd.exe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cmd.exe "Cmd.exe")) with Korean localisation, showing halfwidth and fullwidth characters

In [CJK](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CJK_characters "CJK characters") (Chinese, Japanese, and Korean) computing, [graphic characters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphic_character "Graphic character") are traditionally classed into **fullwidth**[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halfwidth_and_fullwidth_forms#cite_note-1) and **halfwidth**[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halfwidth_and_fullwidth_forms#cite_note-2) characters. Unlike [monospaced fonts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monospaced_font "Monospaced font"), a halfwidth character occupies half the width of a fullwidth character, hence the name.

_[Halfwidth and Fullwidth Forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halfwidth\_and\_Fullwidth\_Forms\_(Unicode\_block) "Halfwidth and Fullwidth Forms (Unicode block)")_ is also the name of a [Unicode block](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode_block "Unicode block") U+FF00–FFEF, provided so that older encodings containing both halfwidth and fullwidth characters can have lossless translation to and from Unicode.

Rationale
---------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Halfwidth_and_fullwidth_forms&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Rationale")]

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/60px-Question_book-new.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg)This section **needs additional citations for [verification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability "Wikipedia:Verifiability")**. Please help [improve this article](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:EditPage/Halfwidth_and_fullwidth_forms "Special:EditPage/Halfwidth and fullwidth forms") by [adding citations to reliable sources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners "Help:Referencing for beginners") in this section. Unsourced material may be challenged and removed._(April 2021)_ _([Learn how and when to remove this message](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Maintenance\_template\_removal "Help:Maintenance template removal"))_
[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Alternative_names_of_JIS_X_0213.svg/250px-Alternative_names_of_JIS_X_0213.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Alternative_names_of_JIS_X_0213.svg)

Characters which appear in both [JIS X 0201](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JIS_X_0201 "JIS X 0201") (single byte) and [JIS X 0208](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JIS_X_0208 "JIS X 0208") / [JIS X 0213](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JIS_X_0213 "JIS X 0213") (double byte) have both a halfwidth and a fullwidth form in [Shift JIS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shift_JIS "Shift JIS").

In the days of [text mode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Text_mode "Text mode") computing, Western characters were normally laid out in a grid on the screen, often 80 columns by 24 or 25 lines. Each character was displayed as a small [dot matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_matrix "Dot matrix"), often about 8 [pixels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pixel "Pixel") wide, and an [SBCS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SBCS "SBCS") (single-byte character set) was generally used to encode characters of Western languages.

For aesthetic reasons and readability, it is preferable for [Chinese characters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_characters "Chinese characters") to be approximately square-shaped, therefore twice as wide as these fixed-width SBCS characters. As these were typically encoded in a [DBCS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double-byte_character_set "Double-byte character set") (double-byte character set), this also meant that their width on screen in a [duospaced font](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duospaced_font "Duospaced font") was proportional to their byte length. Some terminals and editing programs could not deal with double-byte characters starting at odd columns, only even ones (some could not even put double-byte and single-byte characters in the same line). So the DBCS sets generally included Roman characters and digits also, for use alongside the CJK characters in the same line.

On the other hand, early Japanese computing used a single-byte code page called [JIS X 0201](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JIS_X_0201 "JIS X 0201") for [katakana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Katakana "Katakana"). These would be rendered at the same width as the other single-byte characters, making them [half-width kana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Half-width_kana "Half-width kana") characters rather than normally proportioned kana. Although the JIS X 0201 standard itself did not specify half-width display for katakana, this became the visually distinguishing feature in [Shift JIS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shift_JIS "Shift JIS") between the single-byte JIS X 0201 and double-byte [JIS X 0208](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JIS_X_0208 "JIS X 0208") katakana. Some IBM code pages used a similar treatment for [Korean jamo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hangul#Letters "Hangul"),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halfwidth_and_fullwidth_forms#cite_note-ibm933-3) based on the [N-byte Hangul code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KS_C_5601#1974 "KS C 5601") and its [EBCDIC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EBCDIC "EBCDIC") translation.

In Unicode
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Halfwidth_and_fullwidth_forms&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: In Unicode")]

See also: [Halfwidth and Fullwidth Forms (Unicode block)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halfwidth_and_Fullwidth_Forms_(Unicode_block) "Halfwidth and Fullwidth Forms (Unicode block)")

For compatibility with existing character sets that contained both half- and fullwidth versions of the same character, [Unicode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode "Unicode") allocated a single block at U+FF00–FFEF containing the necessary "alternative width" characters. This includes a fullwidth version of all the [ASCII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASCII "ASCII") characters and some non-ASCII punctuation such as the Yen sign, halfwidth versions of katakana and [hangul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hangul "Hangul"), and halfwidth versions of some other symbols such as circles. Only characters needed for lossless round trip to existing character sets were allocated, rather than (for instance) making a fullwidth version of every Latin accented character.

Unicode assigns _every_ code point an "East Asian width" [property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode_character_property "Unicode character property"). This may be:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halfwidth_and_fullwidth_forms#cite_note-uax11-4)

Unicode character properties based on width | Abbreviation | Name | Description |
| --- | --- | --- |
| W | Wide | Naturally wide character, e.g. [Hiragana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hiragana "Hiragana"). |
| Na | Narrow | Naturally narrow character, e.g. [ISO Basic Latin alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_Basic_Latin_alphabet "ISO Basic Latin alphabet"). |
| F | Fullwidth | Wide variant with [compatibility normalisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NFKC "NFKC") to naturally narrow character, e.g. fullwidth Latin script. |
| H | Halfwidth | Narrow variant with [compatibility normalisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NFKC "NFKC") to naturally wide character, e.g. [half-width kana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Half-width_kana "Half-width kana"). Includes U+20A9 ([₩](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Won_sign "Won sign")) as an exception. |
| A | Ambiguous | Characters included in East Asian DBCS codes but also in European SBCS codes, e.g. [Greek alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_alphabet "Greek alphabet"). Duospaced behaviour can consequently vary. |
| N | Neutral | Characters which do not appear in East Asian DBCS codes, e.g. [Devanagari](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Devanagari "Devanagari"). |

[Terminal emulators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terminal_emulator "Terminal emulator") can use this property to decide whether a character should consume one or two "columns" when figuring out tabs and cursor position.

In OpenType
-----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Halfwidth_and_fullwidth_forms&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: In OpenType")]

[OpenType](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OpenType "OpenType") has the `fwid`, `halt`, `hwid`, and `vhal` feature tags to be used to reproduce fullwidth or halfwidth form of a character. [CSS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CSS "CSS") provides control over these features using `font-variant-east-asian` and `font-feature-settings` properties.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halfwidth_and_fullwidth_forms#cite_note-5)
