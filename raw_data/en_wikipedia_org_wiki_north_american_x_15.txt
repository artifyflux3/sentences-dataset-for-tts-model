The **North American X-15** is a [hypersonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersonic_speed "Hypersonic speed")[rocket-powered aircraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket-powered_aircraft "Rocket-powered aircraft") which was operated by the [United States Air Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Air_Force "United States Air Force") and the [National Aeronautics and Space Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Aeronautics_and_Space_Administration "National Aeronautics and Space Administration") (NASA) as part of the [X-plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_X-planes "List of X-planes") series of [experimental aircraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Experimental_aircraft "Experimental aircraft"). The X-15 set speed and altitude records in the 1960s, crossing the [edge of outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line "Kármán line") and returning with valuable data used in [aircraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aircraft "Aircraft") and [spacecraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacecraft "Spacecraft") design. The X-15's highest speed, 4,520 miles per hour (7,274 km/h; 2,021 m/s),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-nasa20140228-1)[was achieved on](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-15_Flight_188 "X-15 Flight 188") 3 October 1967,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-2) when [William J. Knight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_J._Knight "William J. Knight") flew at [Mach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mach_number "Mach number")6.7 at an altitude of 102,100 feet (31,120 m), or 19.34 miles. This set the [official world record](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flight_airspeed_record "Flight airspeed record") for the highest speed ever recorded by a crewed, powered aircraft, which remains unbroken.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-Fastest-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-4)

During the X-15 program, 12 pilots flew a combined 199 flights.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-nasa20140228-1) Of these, eight pilots flew a combined 13 flights which met the Air Force [spaceflight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_spaceflight "Human spaceflight") criterion by exceeding the altitude of 50 miles (80 km), thus qualifying these pilots as being [astronauts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronaut "Astronaut"); of those 13 flights, two (flown by the same civilian pilot) met the [FAI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_A%C3%A9ronautique_Internationale "Fédération Aéronautique Internationale") definition (100 kilometres (62 mi)) of [outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space "Outer space"). The 5 Air Force pilots qualified for military [astronaut wings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronaut_wings "Astronaut wings") immediately, while the 3 civilian pilots were eventually awarded NASA astronaut wings in 2005, 35 years after the last X-15 flight.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJenkins200110-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-NASA-6)

Design and development
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=North_American_X-15&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Design and development")]

[![Image 26](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d6/X-15_in_flight.jpg/250px-X-15_in_flight.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:X-15_in_flight.jpg)

X-15 after igniting rocket engine

[![Image 27](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4d/X-15A2_NB-52B_3.jpg/250px-X-15A2_NB-52B_3.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:X-15A2_NB-52B_3.jpg)

X-15A-2, with sealed ablative coating, [external fuel tanks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drop_tank "Drop tank"), and ramjet dummy test

The X-15 was based on a concept study from [Walter Dornberger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Dornberger "Walter Dornberger") for the [National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Advisory_Committee_for_Aeronautics "National Advisory Committee for Aeronautics") (NACA) of a [hypersonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersonic_speed "Hypersonic speed") research aircraft.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-FOOTNOTEK%C3%A4smann1999105-7) The [requests for proposal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Request_for_proposal "Request for proposal") (RFPs) were published on 30 December 1954 for the airframe and on 4 February 1955 for the [rocket engine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket_engine "Rocket engine"). The X-15 was built by two manufacturers: [North American Aviation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_Aviation "North American Aviation") was contracted for the airframe in November 1955, and [Reaction Motors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reaction_Motors "Reaction Motors") was contracted for building the engines in 1956.

Like many [X-series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_X-planes "List of X-planes") aircraft, the X-15 was designed to be carried aloft and [drop launched](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drop_test "Drop test") from under the wing of a [B-52](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boeing_B-52_Stratofortress "Boeing B-52 Stratofortress")[mother ship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mother_ship "Mother ship"). Air Force NB-52A, "The High and Mighty One" (serial 52-0003), and NB-52B, "The Challenger" (serial 52-0008, also known as _[Balls 8](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balls\_8 "Balls 8")_) served as carrier planes for all X-15 flights. Release of the X-15 from NB-52A took place at an altitude of about 8.5 miles (13.7 km) (45,000 feet) and a speed of about 500 miles per hour (805 km/h).[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-E-4942-8) The X-15 fuselage was long and cylindrical, with rear [fairings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aircraft_fairing "Aircraft fairing") that flattened its appearance, and thick, dorsal and ventral wedge-fin stabilizers. Parts of the fuselage (the outer skin[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-NASA-FS-9)) were heat-resistant [nickel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nickel "Nickel")[alloy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alloy "Alloy") ([Inconel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inconel "Inconel")-X 750).[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-FOOTNOTEK%C3%A4smann1999105-7) The retractable [landing gear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landing_gear "Landing gear") comprised a nose-wheel carriage and two rear skids. The skids did not extend beyond the ventral [fin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fin "Fin"), which required the pilot to jettison the lower fin just before landing. The lower fin was recovered by parachute.

### Cockpit and pilot systems

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=North_American_X-15&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Cockpit and pilot systems")]

[![Image 28](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5a/North_American_X-15A-2_cockpit_2_USAF.jpg/250px-North_American_X-15A-2_cockpit_2_USAF.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:North_American_X-15A-2_cockpit_2_USAF.jpg)

Cockpit of an X-15

The X-15 was the product of developmental research, and changes were made to various systems over the course of the program and between the different models. The X-15 was operated under several different scenarios, including attachment to a launch aircraft, drop, main engine start and acceleration, ballistic flight into thin air/space, re-entry into thicker air, unpowered glide to landing, and direct landing without a main-engine start. The main rocket engine operated only for a relatively short part of the flight but boosted the X-15 to its high speeds and altitudes. Without the main rocket engine thrust, the X-15's instruments and control surfaces remained functional, but the aircraft could not maintain altitude.

As the X-15 also had to be controlled in an environment where there was too little air for aerodynamic [flight control surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flight_control_surface "Flight control surface"), it had a [reaction control system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reaction_control_system "Reaction control system") (RCS) that used rocket thrusters.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-paul-10) There were two different X-15 pilot control setups: one used three joysticks, the other, one joystick.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-jarvis-11)

The X-15 type with multiple control sticks for the pilot placed a traditional center stick between a left 3-axis joystick that sent commands to the Reaction Control System,[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-paul2-12) and a third joystick on the right used during high-G maneuvers to augment the center stick.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-paul2-12) In addition to pilot input, the X-15 "[Stability Augmentation System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stability_Augmentation_System "Stability Augmentation System")" (SAS) sent inputs to the aerodynamic controls to help the pilot maintain [attitude control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacecraft_attitude_control "Spacecraft attitude control").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-paul2-12) The Reaction Control System (RCS) could be operated in two modes– manual and automatic.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-jarvis-11) The automatic mode used a feature called "Reaction Augmentation System" (RAS) that helped stabilize the vehicle at high altitude.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-jarvis-11) The RAS was typically used for approximately three minutes of an X-15 flight before automatic power off.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-jarvis-11)

The alternative control setup used the MH-96 flight control system, which allowed one joystick in place of three and simplified pilot input.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-goleta-13) The MH-96 could automatically blend aerodynamic and rocket controls, depending on how effective each system was at controlling the aircraft.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-goleta-13)

Among the many controls were the rocket engine throttle and a control for jettisoning the ventral tail fin.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-paul2-12) Other features of the cockpit included heated windows to prevent icing and a forward headrest for periods of high deceleration.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-paul2-12)

The X-15 had an ejection seat designed to operate at speeds up to Mach 4 (4,500 km/h; 2,800 mph) and/or 120,000 feet (37 km) (23 miles) altitude, although it was never used during the program.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-paul2-12) In the event of ejection, the seat was designed to deploy fins, which were used until it reached a safer speed/altitude at which to deploy its main parachute.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-paul2-12) Pilots wore pressure suits, which could be pressurized with nitrogen gas.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-paul2-12) Above 35,000 feet (11 km) altitude, the cockpit was pressurized to 3.5 psi (24 kPa; 0.24 atm) with nitrogen gas, while oxygen for breathing was fed separately to the pilot.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-paul2-12)

### Propulsion

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=North_American_X-15&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Propulsion")]

[![Image 29](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/NASM-SI-2008-5570.jpg/250px-NASM-SI-2008-5570.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:NASM-SI-2008-5570.jpg)

X-15 tail with XLR-99

The initial 24 powered flights used two [Reaction Motors XLR11](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reaction_Motors_XLR11 "Reaction Motors XLR11")[liquid-propellant rocket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid-propellant_rocket "Liquid-propellant rocket") engines, enhanced to provide a total of 16,000 pounds-force (71 kN) of thrust as compared to the 6,000 pounds-force (27 kN) that a single XLR11 provided in 1947 to make the [Bell X-1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bell_X-1 "Bell X-1") the first aircraft to fly faster than the [speed of sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_sound "Speed of sound"). The XLR11 used [ethyl alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethyl_alcohol "Ethyl alcohol") and [liquid oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_oxygen "Liquid oxygen").

By November 1960, Reaction Motors delivered the [XLR99](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reaction_Motors_XLR99 "Reaction Motors XLR99") rocket engine, generating 57,000 pounds-force (250 kN) of thrust. The remaining 175 flights of the X-15 used XLR99 engines, in a single engine configuration. The XLR99 used [anhydrous ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia") and liquid oxygen as propellant, and hydrogen peroxide to drive the high-speed [turbopump](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turbopump "Turbopump") that delivered propellants to the engine.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-paul-10) It could burn 15,000 pounds (6,804 kg) of propellant in 80 seconds;[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-paul-10)[Jules Bergman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jules_Bergman "Jules Bergman") titled his book on the program _Ninety Seconds to Space_ to describe the total powered flight time of the aircraft.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-gale196110-14)

The X-15 [reaction control system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reaction_control_system "Reaction control system") (RCS), for maneuvering in the low-pressure/density environment, used [high-test peroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-test_peroxide "High-test peroxide") (HTP), which decomposes into water and oxygen in the presence of a catalyst and could provide a [specific impulse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_impulse "Specific impulse") of 140 s (1.4 km/s).[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-jarvis-11)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDavies20038.28-15) The HTP also fueled a turbopump for the main engines and [auxiliary power units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auxiliary_power_unit "Auxiliary power unit") (APUs).[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-paul-10) Additional tanks for helium and liquid nitrogen performed other functions; the fuselage interior was [purged](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purging_(gas) "Purging (gas)") with helium gas, and liquid nitrogen was used as coolant for various systems.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-paul-10)

### Wedge tail and hypersonic stability

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=North_American_X-15&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Wedge tail and hypersonic stability")]

[![Image 30](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/X-15_and_B-52_Mother_ship.jpg/250px-X-15_and_B-52_Mother_ship.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:X-15_and_B-52_Mother_ship.jpg)

X-15 attached to its B-52 mother ship with a [T-38](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northrop_T-38_Talon "Northrop T-38 Talon") flying nearby

The X-15 had a thick wedge tail to enable it to fly in a steady manner at hypersonic speeds.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-wendell-16) This produced a significant amount of base drag at lower speeds;[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-wendell-16) the blunt end at the rear of the X-15 could produce as much drag as an entire [F-104 Starfighter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lockheed_F-104_Starfighter "Lockheed F-104 Starfighter").[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-wendell-16)

> A wedge shape was used because it is more effective than the conventional tail as a stabilizing surface at hypersonic speeds. A vertical-tail area equal to 60 percent of the wing area was required to give the X-15 adequate directional stability.

— Wendell H. Stillwell, [X-15 Research Results (SP-60)](https://history.nasa.gov/SP-60/ch-3.html)

Stability at hypersonic speeds was aided by side panels that could be extended from the tail to increase the overall surface area, and these panels doubled as air brakes.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-wendell-16)

Operational history
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=North_American_X-15&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Operational history")]

[Newsreel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newsreel "Newsreel") showing a test flight of the X-15 in 1959

Before 1958, [United States Air Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Air_Force "United States Air Force") (USAF) and [NACA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NACA "NACA") officials discussed an orbital X-15 [spaceplane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spaceplane "Spaceplane"), the **X-15B** that would launch into [outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space "Outer space") from atop an [SM-64 Navaho](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SM-64_Navaho "SM-64 Navaho") missile. This was canceled when the NACA became [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA") and adopted [Project Mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_Mercury "Project Mercury") instead.

By 1959, the [Boeing X-20 Dyna-Soar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boeing_X-20_Dyna-Soar "Boeing X-20 Dyna-Soar") space-glider program was to become the USAF's preferred means for launching military crewed spacecraft into orbit. This program was canceled in the early 1960s before an operational vehicle could be built.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJenkins200110-5) Various configurations of the Navaho were considered, and another proposal involved a Titan I stage.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-wade-17)

Three X-15s were built, flying 199 test flights, the last on 24 October 1968.

The first X-15 flight was an unpowered glide flight by [Scott Crossfield](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scott_Crossfield "Scott Crossfield"), on 8 June 1959. Crossfield also piloted the first powered flight on 17 September 1959, and his first flight with the XLR-99 [rocket engine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket_engine "Rocket engine") on 15 November 1960. Twelve test pilots flew the X-15. Among these were [Neil Armstrong](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neil_Armstrong "Neil Armstrong"), later a NASA [astronaut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronaut "Astronaut") and the first man to set foot on the Moon, and [Joe Engle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joe_Engle "Joe Engle"), later a commander of [NASA Space Shuttle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Shuttle_program "Space Shuttle program") missions.

In a 1962 proposal, NASA considered using the B-52/X-15 as a launch platform for a [Blue Scout](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blue_Scout "Blue Scout") rocket to place satellites weighing up to 150 pounds (68 kg) into orbit.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-wade-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-cis20120321-18)

In July and August 1963, pilot [Joe Walker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_A._Walker "Joseph A. Walker") exceeded 100 km in altitude, joining NASA astronauts and Soviet [cosmonauts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmonaut "Cosmonaut") as the first human beings to cross that line on their way to [outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space "Outer space"). The USAF awarded [astronaut wings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronaut_wings "Astronaut wings") to anyone achieving an altitude of 50 miles (80 km), while the [FAI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/F%C3%A9d%C3%A9ration_A%C3%A9ronautique_Internationale "Fédération Aéronautique Internationale") set the [limit of space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line "Kármán line") at 100 kilometers (62.1 mi).

On 15 November 1967, U.S. Air Force test pilot Major [Michael J. Adams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_J._Adams "Michael J. Adams") was killed during [X-15 Flight 191](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-15_Flight_3-65-97 "X-15 Flight 3-65-97") when X-15-3, AF Ser. No. 56-6672, entered a hypersonic spin while descending, then oscillated violently as aerodynamic forces increased after re-entry. As his aircraft's flight control system operated the control surfaces to their limits, acceleration built to 15[_g_ 0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_gravity "Standard gravity") (150[m/s 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre_per_second_squared "Metre per second squared")) vertical and 8.0[_g_ 0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_gravity "Standard gravity") (78[m/s 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre_per_second_squared "Metre per second squared")) lateral. The airframe broke apart at 60,000 feet (18 km) altitude, scattering the X-15's wreckage across 50 square miles (130 km 2). On 8 May 2004, a monument was erected at the cockpit's locale, near [Johannesburg, California](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannesburg,_California "Johannesburg, California").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-xpress2004-19) Major Adams was posthumously awarded Air Force astronaut wings for his final flight in X-15-3, which had reached an altitude of 50.4 miles (81.1 km). In 1991, his name was added to the [Astronaut Memorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Mirror_Memorial "Space Mirror Memorial").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-xpress2004-19)

[![Image 31](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/X-15_Crash_at_Mud_Lake%2C_Nevada_-_GPN-2000-000120.jpg/250px-X-15_Crash_at_Mud_Lake%2C_Nevada_-_GPN-2000-000120.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:X-15_Crash_at_Mud_Lake,_Nevada_-_GPN-2000-000120.jpg)

X-15-2 crash at Mud Lake, Nevada

The second plane, X-15-2, was rebuilt [[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-20) after a landing accident on 9 November 1962 which damaged the craft and injured its pilot, [John McKay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_B._McKay "John B. McKay").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-21) The new plane renamed **X-15A-2**, had a new 28 -in. fuselage extension to carry liquid hydrogen.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-nasa20140228-1) It was lengthened by 2.4 feet (73 cm), had a pair of auxiliary fuel tanks attached beneath its [fuselage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fuselage "Fuselage") and wings, and a complete heat-resistant [ablative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_entry#Ablative "Atmospheric entry") coating was added. It took flight for the first time on 25 June 1964. It reached its maximum speed of 4,520 miles per hour (7,274 km/h) in October 1967 with pilot [William "Pete" Knight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_%22Pete%22_Knight "William \"Pete\" Knight") of the [U.S. Air Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Air_Force "U.S. Air Force") in control.

Five principal aircraft were used during the X-15 program: three X-15 planes and two modified "nonstandard" [NB-52](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boeing_B-52_Stratofortress "Boeing B-52 Stratofortress") bombers:

*   **X-15-1** –_56-6670_, 81 free flights
*   **X-15-2** (later **X-15A-2**) –_56-6671_, 31 free flights as X-15-2, 22 free flights as X-15A-2; 53 in total
*   **X-15-3** –_56-6672_, 65 free flights, including the [Flight 191](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-15_Flight_3-65-97 "X-15 Flight 3-65-97") disaster
*   **NB-52A** –_52-003_ nicknamed _The High and Mighty One_ (retired in October 1969)
*   **NB-52B** –_[52-008](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balls\_8 "Balls 8")_ nicknamed _The Challenger_, later _Balls 8_ (retired in November 2004)

Additionally, [F-100](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_F-100_Super_Sabre "North American F-100 Super Sabre"), [F-104](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lockheed_F-104_Starfighter "Lockheed F-104 Starfighter") and [F5D](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Douglas_F5D_Skylancer "Douglas F5D Skylancer")[chase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chase_plane "Chase plane") aircraft and [C-130](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lockheed_C-130_Hercules "Lockheed C-130 Hercules") and [C-47](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Douglas_C-47_Skytrain "Douglas C-47 Skytrain") transports supported the program.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-22)

The 200th flight over Nevada was first scheduled for 21 November 1968, to be flown by William "Pete" Knight. Numerous technical problems and outbreaks of bad weather delayed this proposed flight six times, and it was permanently canceled on 20 December 1968. This X-15 (56-6670) was detached from the B-52 and then put into indefinite storage. The aircraft was later donated to the Smithsonian Air & Space Museum for display.

Both surviving X-15s are currently on display at museums in the United States. In addition, three mockups and both [B-52 Stratofortresses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B-52_Stratofortress "B-52 Stratofortress") used as motherships are on display as well.

*   X-15-1 (AF Ser. No. 56-6670) is on display in the [National Air and Space Museum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Air_and_Space_Museum "National Air and Space Museum") "Milestones of Flight" gallery, [Washington, D.C.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Washington,_D.C. "Washington, D.C.")
*   X-15A-2 (AF Ser. No. 56-6671) is at the [National Museum of the United States Air Force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Museum_of_the_United_States_Air_Force "National Museum of the United States Air Force"), at [Wright-Patterson Air Force Base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wright-Patterson_Air_Force_Base "Wright-Patterson Air Force Base"), near [Dayton, Ohio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dayton,_Ohio "Dayton, Ohio"). It was retired to the museum in October 1969.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUSAF_Museum_Guidebook197573-23) The aircraft is displayed in the museum's Research and Development Gallery alongside other "X-planes", including the [Bell X-1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bell_X-1 "Bell X-1")B and [Douglas X-3 Stiletto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Douglas_X-3_Stiletto "Douglas X-3 Stiletto").

Record flights
--------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=North_American_X-15&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Record flights")]

Main article: [List of X-15 flights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_X-15_flights "List of X-15 flights")

### Highest flights

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=North_American_X-15&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Highest flights")]

During 13 of the 199 total X-15 flights, eight pilots flew above 264,000 feet (50.0 mi; 80 km), thereby qualifying as astronauts according to the [US Armed Forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/US_Armed_Forces "US Armed Forces") definition of the [space border](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karman_line "Karman line"). All five Air Force pilots flew above 50 miles and were awarded military [astronaut wings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronaut_wings "Astronaut wings") contemporaneously with their achievements, including Adams, who received the distinction posthumously following the flight[191 disaster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-15_Flight_3-65-97 "X-15 Flight 3-65-97").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-24) However the other three were NASA employees and did not receive a comparable decoration at the time. In 2004, the [Federal Aviation Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federal_Aviation_Administration "Federal Aviation Administration") conferred its first-ever commercial astronaut wings on [Mike Melvill](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mike_Melvill "Mike Melvill") and [Brian Binnie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brian_Binnie "Brian Binnie"), pilots of the commercial [SpaceShipOne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SpaceShipOne "SpaceShipOne"), another spaceplane with a flight profile comparable to the X-15's. Following this in 2005, NASA retroactively awarded its civilian astronaut wings to Dana (then living), and to McKay and Walker (posthumously).[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-25)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-26) Forrest S. Petersen, the only Navy pilot in the X-15 program, never took the aircraft above the requisite altitude and thus never earned astronaut wings.

Of the thirteen flights, only two–flights 90 and 91, piloted by Walker–exceeded the 100 km (62 mi) altitude used by the FAI to denote the [Kármán line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line "Kármán line").

**General characteristics**

*   **Crew:** One
*   **Length:** 49 ft 2 in (14.99 m) [[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-35)
*   **Wingspan:** 22 ft 4 in (6.81 m) [[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-36)
*   **Height:** 13 ft 1 in (3.99 m) [[d]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-37)
*   **Wing area:** 200 sq ft (19 m 2)
*   **Empty weight:** 14,600 lb (6,622 kg) [[e]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15#cite_note-38)
*   **Gross weight:** 33,500 lb (15,195 kg)
*   **Powerplant:** 1 × [Reaction Motors XLR99](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reaction_Motors_XLR99 "Reaction Motors XLR99")-RM-2 [liquid-fuelled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid-propellant_rocket "Liquid-propellant rocket")[rocket engine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket_engine "Rocket engine"), 70,400 lbf (313 kN) thrust

**Performance**

*   **Maximum speed:** 4,520 mph (7,270 km/h, 3,930 kn)
*   **Range:** 280 mi (450 km, 240 nmi)
*   **Service ceiling:** 354,330 ft (108,000 m)
*   **Rate of climb:** 60,000 ft/min (300 m/s)
*   **[Thrust/weight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thrust-to-weight_ratio "Thrust-to-weight ratio"):** 2.07
