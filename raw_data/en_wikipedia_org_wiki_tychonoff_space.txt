In [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") and related branches of [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), **Tychonoff spaces** and **completely regular spaces** are kinds of [topological spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space"). These conditions are examples of [separation axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_axiom "Separation axiom"). A Tychonoff space is any completely regular space that is also a [Hausdorff space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_space "Hausdorff space"); there exist completely regular spaces that are not Tychonoff (i.e. not Hausdorff).

[Paul Urysohn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Urysohn "Paul Urysohn") had used the notion of completely regular space in a 1925 paper[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tychonoff_space#cite_note-1) without giving it a name. But it was [Andrey Tychonoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrey_Tychonoff "Andrey Tychonoff") who introduced the terminology _completely regular_ in 1930.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tychonoff_space#cite_note-tychonoff-1930-2)

Separation of a point from a closed set via a continuous function.

A topological space ![Image 2: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is called **_completely regular_** if points can be [separated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separated_sets "Separated sets") from closed sets via (bounded) continuous real-valued functions. In technical terms this means: for any [closed set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_set "Closed set")![Image 3: {\displaystyle A\subseteq X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dce86da0107830a9a97287f9486d9b4ff022875) and any [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)")![Image 4: {\displaystyle x\in X\setminus A,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c8da9f47f2502d14711392ad73d6a62698ac7ca2) there exists a [real-valued](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_line "Real line")[continuous function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function_(topology) "Continuous function (topology)")![Image 5: {\displaystyle f:X\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/669fa4832da4b0b229d77eadb270e95188f2eb10) such that ![Image 6: {\displaystyle f(x)=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea78f54e69b72f398cf6077e61c50a05b532d4c0) and ![Image 7: {\displaystyle f\vert _{A}=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a488eeed65bbcd19a45087c3f7cd94f04d1cac5) (Equivalently one can choose any two values instead of ![Image 8: {\displaystyle 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2aae8864a3c1fec9585261791a809ddec1489950) and ![Image 9: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf) and even require that ![Image 10: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) be a bounded function.)

A topological space is called a **_Tychonoff space_** (alternatively: **_T 3½ space_**, or _T π space_, or _completely T 3 space_) if it is a completely regular [Hausdorff space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_space "Hausdorff space").

**Remark.** Completely regular spaces and Tychonoff spaces are related through the notion of [Kolmogorov equivalence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kolmogorov_equivalence "Kolmogorov equivalence"). A topological space is Tychonoff if and only if it's both completely regular and [T 0](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kolmogorov_space "Kolmogorov space"). On the other hand, a space is completely regular if and only if its [Kolmogorov quotient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kolmogorov_quotient "Kolmogorov quotient") is Tychonoff.

Across mathematical literature different conventions are applied when it comes to the term "completely regular" and the "T"-Axioms. The definitions in this section are in typical modern usage. Some authors, however, switch the meanings of the two kinds of terms, or use all terms interchangeably. In Wikipedia, the terms "completely regular" and "Tychonoff" are used freely and the "T"-notation is generally avoided. In standard literature, caution is thus advised, to find out which definitions the author is using. For more on this issue, see [History of the separation axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_the_separation_axioms "History of the separation axioms").

Almost every topological space studied in [mathematical analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_analysis "Mathematical analysis") is Tychonoff, or at least completely regular. For example, the [real line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_line "Real line") is Tychonoff under the standard [Euclidean topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"). Other examples include:

*   Every [metric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space") is Tychonoff; every [pseudometric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudometric_space "Pseudometric space") is completely regular.
*   Every [locally compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Locally_compact "Locally compact")[regular space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_space "Regular space") is completely regular, and therefore every locally compact Hausdorff space is Tychonoff.
*   In particular, every [topological manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_manifold "Topological manifold") is Tychonoff.
*   Every [totally ordered set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Totally_ordered_set "Totally ordered set") with the [order topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_topology "Order topology") is Tychonoff.
*   Every [topological group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_group "Topological group") is completely regular.
*   Every [pseudometrizable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudometrizable "Pseudometrizable") space is completely regular, but not Tychonoff if the space is not Hausdorff.
*   Every [seminormed space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seminormed_space "Seminormed space") is completely regular (both because it is pseudometrizable and because it is a [topological vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_vector_space "Topological vector space"), hence a topological group). But it will not be Tychonoff if the seminorm is not a norm.
*   Generalizing both the metric spaces and the topological groups, every [uniform space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_space "Uniform space") is completely regular. The converse is also true: every completely regular space is uniformisable.
*   Every [CW complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CW_complex "CW complex") is Tychonoff.
*   Every [normal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_space "Normal space") regular space is completely regular, and every normal Hausdorff space is Tychonoff.
*   The [Niemytzki plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niemytzki_plane "Niemytzki plane") is an example of a Tychonoff space that is not [normal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_space "Normal space").

There are regular Hausdorff spaces that are not completely regular, but such examples are complicated to construct. One of them is the so-called _Tychonoff corkscrew_,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tychonoff_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWillard1970Problem_18G-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tychonoff_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTESteenSeebach1995Example_90-4) which contains two points such that any continuous real-valued function on the space has the same value at these two points. An even more complicated construction starts with the Tychonoff corkscrew and builds a regular Hausdorff space called _Hewitt's condensed corkscrew_,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tychonoff_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTESteenSeebach1995Example_92-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tychonoff_space#cite_note-6) which is not completely regular in a stronger way, namely, every continuous real-valued function on the space is constant.

Complete regularity and the Tychonoff property are well-behaved with respect to [initial topologies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Initial_topology "Initial topology"). Specifically, complete regularity is preserved by taking arbitrary initial topologies and the Tychonoff property is preserved by taking point-separating initial topologies. It follows that:

*   Every [subspace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subspace_(topology) "Subspace (topology)") of a completely regular or Tychonoff space has the same property.
*   A nonempty [product space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_space "Product space") is completely regular (respectively Tychonoff) if and only if each factor space is completely regular (respectively Tychonoff).

Like all separation axioms, complete regularity is not preserved by taking [final topologies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Final_topology "Final topology"). In particular, [quotients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_space_(topology) "Quotient space (topology)") of completely regular spaces need not be [regular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_space "Regular space"). Quotients of Tychonoff spaces need not even be [Hausdorff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hausdorff_space "Hausdorff space"), with one elementary counterexample being the [line with two origins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_with_two_origins "Line with two origins"). There are closed quotients of the [Moore plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moore_plane "Moore plane") that provide counterexamples.

The algebraic theory of these rings is therefore subject of intensive studies. A vast generalization of this class of rings that still resembles many properties of Tychonoff spaces, but is also applicable in [real algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_algebraic_geometry "Real algebraic geometry"), is the class of [real closed rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_closed_ring "Real closed ring").

Tychonoff spaces are precisely those spaces that can be [embedded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_embedding "Topological embedding") in [compact Hausdorff spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_Hausdorff_space "Compact Hausdorff space"). More precisely, for every Tychonoff space ![Image 38: {\displaystyle X,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/09ba32eeb405f7f5f2bac1eb12987c47d2fd42df) there exists a compact Hausdorff space ![Image 39: {\displaystyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76fce82a62ed5461908f0dc8f037de4e3686b0) such that ![Image 40: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is [homeomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphic "Homeomorphic") to a subspace of ![Image 41: {\displaystyle K.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1bb0e178e42abf16ef4e4c0b0f22aa235ad6e6e5)

In fact, one can always choose ![Image 42: {\displaystyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76fce82a62ed5461908f0dc8f037de4e3686b0) to be a [Tychonoff cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tychonoff_cube "Tychonoff cube") (i.e. a possibly infinite product of [unit intervals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_interval "Unit interval")). Every Tychonoff cube is compact Hausdorff as a consequence of [Tychonoff's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tychonoff%27s_theorem "Tychonoff's theorem"). Since every subspace of a compact Hausdorff space is Tychonoff one has:

_A topological space is Tychonoff if and only if it can be embedded in a Tychonoff cube_.

Among those Hausdorff compactifications, there is a unique "most general" one, the [Stone–Čech compactification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stone%E2%80%93%C4%8Cech_compactification "Stone–Čech compactification")![Image 51: {\displaystyle \beta X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99bec224d9a06a54dd7c53ed1c06652c0615e38c) It is characterized by the [universal property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_property "Universal property") that, given a continuous map ![Image 52: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) from ![Image 53: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) to any other compact Hausdorff space ![Image 54: {\displaystyle Y,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3765557b7effa1a5f2f4dce9c80a25973b7009f) there is a [unique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unique_(mathematics) "Unique (mathematics)") continuous map ![Image 55: {\displaystyle g:\beta X\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/26d67dbfd1aeb7c86778b1e46d82627a80f68a28) that extends ![Image 56: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) in the sense that ![Image 57: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is the [composition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composition_(functions) "Composition (functions)") of ![Image 58: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) and ![Image 59: {\displaystyle j.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a24040d2c50c228edf9b031ce3db3d04101cb22)

Complete regularity is exactly the condition necessary for the existence of [uniform structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_structure "Uniform structure") on a topological space. In other words, every [uniform space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_space "Uniform space") has a completely regular topology and every completely regular space ![Image 60: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is [uniformizable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniformizable "Uniformizable"). A topological space admits a separated uniform structure if and only if it is Tychonoff.

Given a completely regular space ![Image 61: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) there is usually more than one uniformity on ![Image 62: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) that is compatible with the topology of ![Image 63: {\displaystyle X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ba76c5a460c4a0bb1639a193bc1830f0a773e03) However, there will always be a finest compatible uniformity, called the [fine uniformity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fine_uniformity "Fine uniformity") on ![Image 64: {\displaystyle X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ba76c5a460c4a0bb1639a193bc1830f0a773e03) If ![Image 65: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is Tychonoff, then the uniform structure can be chosen so that ![Image 66: {\displaystyle \beta X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aa7f1e80cfd73969b45d11c6223b6004acf28ab5) becomes the [completion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Completion_(topology) "Completion (topology)") of the uniform space ![Image 67: {\displaystyle X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ba76c5a460c4a0bb1639a193bc1830f0a773e03)
