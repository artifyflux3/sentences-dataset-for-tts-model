In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), **circular motion** is [movement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion "Motion") of an object along the [circumference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumference "Circumference") of a [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle") or [rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation") along a [circular arc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_arc "Circular arc"). It can be uniform, with a constant [rate of rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rate_of_rotation "Rate of rotation") and constant [tangential speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangential_speed "Tangential speed"), or non-uniform with a changing rate of rotation. The [rotation around a fixed axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_around_a_fixed_axis "Rotation around a fixed axis") of a three-dimensional body involves the circular motion of its parts. The equations of motion describe the movement of the [center of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_mass "Center of mass") of a body, which remains at a constant distance from the [axis of rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axis_of_rotation "Axis of rotation"). In circular motion, the distance between the body and a fixed point on its surface remains the same, i.e., the body is assumed [rigid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body "Rigid body").

Examples of circular motion include: special satellite orbits around the Earth ([circular orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_orbit "Circular orbit")), a [ceiling fan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ceiling_fan "Ceiling fan")'s blades rotating around a hub, a stone that is tied to a rope and is being swung in circles, a car turning through a curve in a [race track](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Race_track "Race track"), an electron moving perpendicular to a uniform [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field"), and a [gear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gear "Gear") turning inside a mechanism.

Since the object's [velocity vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity_vector "Velocity vector") is constantly changing direction, the moving object is undergoing [acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration") by a [centripetal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force "Centripetal force") in the direction of the center of rotation. Without this acceleration, the object would move in a straight line, according to [Newton's laws of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion "Newton's laws of motion").

Uniform circular motion
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Circular_motion&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Uniform circular motion")]

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Uniform_circular_motion.svg/250px-Uniform_circular_motion.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Uniform_circular_motion.svg)

Figure 1: Velocity **v** and acceleration **a** in uniform circular motion at angular rate ω; the speed is constant, but the velocity is always tangential to the orbit; the acceleration has constant magnitude, but always points toward the center of rotation.

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9b/Velocity-acceleration.svg/330px-Velocity-acceleration.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Velocity-acceleration.svg)

Figure 2: The velocity vectors at time t and time _t_ + _dt_ are moved from the orbit on the left to new positions where their tails coincide, on the right. Because the velocity is fixed in magnitude at _v_ = _r_ _ω_, the velocity vectors also sweep out a circular path at angular rate ω. As _dt_ → 0, the acceleration vector **a** becomes perpendicular to **v**, which means it points toward the center of the orbit in the circle on the left. Angle _ω_ _dt_ is the very small angle between the two velocities and tends to zero as _dt_ → 0.

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Breaking_String.PNG/500px-Breaking_String.PNG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Breaking_String.PNG)

Figure 3: (Left) Ball in a circular motion – rope provides centripetal force to keep the ball in a circle (Right) Rope is cut and the ball continues in a straight line with the velocity at the time of cutting the rope, in accord with Newton's law of inertia, because centripetal force is no longer there.

In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), **uniform circular motion** describes the motion of a body traversing a [circular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle") path at a constant [speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed "Speed"). Since the body describes circular motion, its [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance "Distance") from the axis of rotation remains constant at all times. Though the body's speed is constant, its [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") is not constant: velocity, a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") quantity, depends on both the body's speed and its direction of travel. This changing velocity indicates the presence of an acceleration; this [centripetal acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_acceleration "Centripetal acceleration") is of constant magnitude and directed at all times toward the axis of rotation. This acceleration is, in turn, produced by a [centripetal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force "Centripetal force") which is also constant in magnitude and directed toward the axis of rotation.

In the case of [rotation around a fixed axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_around_a_fixed_axis "Rotation around a fixed axis") of a [rigid body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body "Rigid body") that is not negligibly small compared to the radius of the path, each particle of the body describes a uniform circular motion with the same angular velocity, but with velocity and acceleration varying with the position with respect to the axis.

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Circular_motion_vectors.svg/330px-Circular_motion_vectors.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Circular_motion_vectors.svg)

Figure 1: Vector relationships for uniform circular motion; vector **Ω** representing the rotation is normal to the plane of the orbit.

For motion in a circle of [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius")r, the circumference of the circle is _C_ = 2 _πr_. If the period for one rotation is T, the angular rate of rotation, also known as [angular velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity"), ω is: [![Image 5: {\displaystyle \omega ={\frac {2\pi }{T}}=2\pi f={\frac {d\theta }{dt}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/78049c36120f9bc65a2f17d6665e3154f22a83ea)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q161635) and the units are radians/second.

The speed of the object traveling the circle is: [![Image 6: {\displaystyle v={\frac {2\pi r}{T}}=\omega r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3d779be46b45bf2597acbd9c491bc5584ae19636)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q3711325)

The angle θ swept out in a time t is: [![Image 7: {\displaystyle \theta =2\pi {\frac {t}{T}}=\omega t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/796abe9fab8852cec649b3986dd27cd750e63e9b)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q11352)

The [angular acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_acceleration "Angular acceleration"), α, of the particle is: [![Image 8: {\displaystyle \alpha ={\frac {d\omega }{dt}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d74ed1b36112c1096f5398c490469be030425553)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q186300)

In the case of uniform circular motion, α will be zero.

The acceleration due to change in the direction is: [![Image 9: {\displaystyle a_{c}={\frac {v^{2}}{r}}=\omega ^{2}r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/93fe057c91594d78799543c67480203edb9acff7)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q2248131)

The [centripetal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force "Centripetal force") and [centrifugal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centrifugal_force_(rotating_reference_frame) "Centrifugal force (rotating reference frame)") force can also be found using acceleration: [![Image 10: {\displaystyle F_{c}={\dot {p}}\mathrel {\overset {{\dot {m}}=0}{=}} ma_{c}={\frac {mv^{2}}{r}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f8fc7f7e1e85fd7c1e05f2463ddfb33e554f1de8)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q172881)

The vector relationships are shown in Figure 1. The axis of rotation is shown as a vector _**ω**_ perpendicular to the plane of the orbit and with a magnitude _ω_ = _dθ_ / _dt_. The direction of _**ω**_ is chosen using the [right-hand rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-hand_rule "Right-hand rule"). With this convention for depicting rotation, the velocity is given by a vector [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product") as ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} ={\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times \mathbf {r} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/09840d976dc8dd5a5d575e763a4d93456b7f17ef) which is a vector perpendicular to both _**ω**_ and **r**(_t_), tangential to the orbit, and of magnitude _ω_ _r_. Likewise, the acceleration is given by ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} ={\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times \mathbf {v} ={\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times \left({\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times \mathbf {r} \right),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b308a1fa1ad572ebebedca22d234432e5b9ec80) which is a vector perpendicular to both _**ω**_ and **v**(_t_) of magnitude _ω_ |**v**| = _ω_ 2 _r_ and directed exactly opposite to **r**(_t_).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_motion#cite_note-1)

In the simplest case the speed, mass, and radius are constant.

Consider a body of one kilogram, moving in a circle of [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius") one metre, with an [angular velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity") of one [radian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian") per [second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second "Second").

*   The [speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed "Speed") is 1 metre per second.
*   The inward [acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration") is 1 metre per square second, _v_ 2/_r_.
*   It is subject to a [centripetal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force "Centripetal force") of 1 kilogram metre per square second, which is 1 [newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_(unit) "Newton (unit)").
*   The [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") of the body is 1 kg·m·s−1.
*   The [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia") is 1 kg·m 2.
*   The [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum") is 1 kg·m 2·s−1.
*   The [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy") is 0.5[joule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joule "Joule").
*   The [circumference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumference "Circumference") of the [orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit "Orbit") is 2[π](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi "Pi") (~6.283) metres.
*   The period of the motion is 2 π seconds.
*   The [frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency") is (2 π)−1[hertz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hertz "Hertz").

During circular motion, the body moves on a curve that can be described in the [polar coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinate_system "Polar coordinate system") as a fixed distance _R_ from the center of the orbit taken as the origin, oriented at an angle _θ_(_t_) from some reference direction. See Figure 4. The displacement _vector_![Image 20: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1) is the radial vector from the origin to the particle location: ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} (t)=R{\hat {\mathbf {u} }}_{R}(t)\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43322df20fed9f49cf7f6ea5f8487c73f9794791) where ![Image 22: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {u} }}_{R}(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/788f3bcaf3261df84988cd50401f924a4a22123d) is the [unit vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector") parallel to the radius vector at time t and pointing away from the origin. It is convenient to introduce the unit vector [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonality_(mathematics)#Euclidean_vector_spaces "Orthogonality (mathematics)") to ![Image 23: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {u} }}_{R}(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/788f3bcaf3261df84988cd50401f924a4a22123d) as well, namely ![Image 24: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {u} }}_{\theta }(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2910be7941133c32c50b0cf475f55e0a0fa5f8a8). It is customary to orient ![Image 25: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {u} }}_{\theta }(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2910be7941133c32c50b0cf475f55e0a0fa5f8a8) to point in the direction of travel along the orbit.

The velocity is the time derivative of the displacement: ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} (t)={\frac {d}{dt}}\mathbf {r} (t)={\frac {dR}{dt}}{\hat {\mathbf {u} }}_{R}(t)+R{\frac {d{\hat {\mathbf {u} }}_{R}}{dt}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b9ea848c5ad89bbc15554dbc237655879b812a)

Because the radius of the circle is constant, the radial component of the velocity is zero. The unit vector ![Image 27: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {u} }}_{R}(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/788f3bcaf3261df84988cd50401f924a4a22123d) has a time-invariant magnitude of unity, so as time varies its tip always lies on a circle of unit radius, with an angle θ the same as the angle of ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} (t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7216e69d28fdb11c29c859f015a284c1ec6f06ac). If the particle displacement rotates through an angle _dθ_ in time _dt_, so does ![Image 29: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {u} }}_{R}(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/788f3bcaf3261df84988cd50401f924a4a22123d), describing an arc on the unit circle of magnitude _dθ_. See the unit circle at the left of Figure 4. Hence: ![Image 30: {\displaystyle {\frac {d{\hat {\mathbf {u} }}_{R}}{dt}}={\frac {d\theta }{dt}}{\hat {\mathbf {u} }}_{\theta }(t)\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/371f90101e07e7171d07b52e9ded2bb7a0bcacd9) where the direction of the change must be perpendicular to ![Image 31: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {u} }}_{R}(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/788f3bcaf3261df84988cd50401f924a4a22123d) (or, in other words, along ![Image 32: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {u} }}_{\theta }(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2910be7941133c32c50b0cf475f55e0a0fa5f8a8)) because any change ![Image 33: {\displaystyle d{\hat {\mathbf {u} }}_{R}(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/36f01e72efb62aff1b61aab23bc380ca651fa995) in the direction of ![Image 34: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {u} }}_{R}(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/788f3bcaf3261df84988cd50401f924a4a22123d) would change the size of ![Image 35: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {u} }}_{R}(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/788f3bcaf3261df84988cd50401f924a4a22123d). The sign is positive because an increase in _dθ_ implies the object and ![Image 36: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {u} }}_{R}(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/788f3bcaf3261df84988cd50401f924a4a22123d) have moved in the direction of ![Image 37: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {u} }}_{\theta }(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2910be7941133c32c50b0cf475f55e0a0fa5f8a8). Hence the velocity becomes: ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} (t)={\frac {d}{dt}}\mathbf {r} (t)=R{\frac {d{\hat {\mathbf {u} }}_{R}}{dt}}=R{\frac {d\theta }{dt}}{\hat {\mathbf {u} }}_{\theta }(t)=R\omega {\hat {\mathbf {u} }}_{\theta }(t)\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec65b4b12711f02c3817ac8189a98ca8ef38add8)

#### Using complex numbers

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Circular_motion&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Using complex numbers")]

Circular motion can be described using [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number"). Let the x axis be the real axis and the ![Image 55: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) axis be the imaginary axis. The position of the body can then be given as ![Image 56: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98), a complex "vector": ![Image 57: {\displaystyle z=x+iy=R\left(\cos[\theta (t)]+i\sin[\theta (t)]\right)=Re^{i\theta (t)}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5f70473a1d45582449264bfb173bcd862be3dae7) where _i_ is the [imaginary unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit "Imaginary unit"), and ![Image 58: {\displaystyle \theta (t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e92e767769c09cc676d3b32facf194677e467fc1) is the argument of the complex number as a function of time, t.

Since the radius is constant: ![Image 59: {\displaystyle {\dot {R}}={\ddot {R}}=0\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d8444821b40804940f05a3d7acc8e0a07f315127) where a _dot_ indicates differentiation in respect of time.

With this notation, the velocity becomes: ![Image 60: {\displaystyle v={\dot {z}}={\frac {d}{dt}}\left(Re^{i\theta [t]}\right)=R{\frac {d}{dt}}\left(e^{i\theta [t]}\right)=Re^{i\theta (t)}{\frac {d}{dt}}\left(i\theta [t]\right)=iR{\dot {\theta }}(t)e^{i\theta (t)}=i\omega Re^{i\theta (t)}=i\omega z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bfe1c9630c24006fe24bf7de9194566b0aff4ca9) and the acceleration becomes: ![Image 61: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}a&={\dot {v}}=i{\dot {\omega }}z+i\omega {\dot {z}}=\left(i{\dot {\omega }}-\omega ^{2}\right)z\\&=\left(i{\dot {\omega }}-\omega ^{2}\right)Re^{i\theta (t)}\\&=-\omega ^{2}Re^{i\theta (t)}+{\dot {\omega }}e^{i{\frac {\pi }{2}}}Re^{i\theta (t)}\,.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/caccdce0c6640065cf4cfed1e10f689a28efdc6d)

The first term is opposite in direction to the displacement vector and the second is perpendicular to it, just like the earlier results shown before.

Figure 1 illustrates velocity and acceleration vectors for uniform motion at four different points in the orbit. Because the velocity **v** is tangent to the circular path, no two velocities point in the same direction. Although the object has a constant _speed_, its _direction_ is always changing. This change in velocity is caused by an acceleration **a**, whose magnitude is (like that of the velocity) held constant, but whose direction also is always changing. The [acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration") points radially inwards ([centripetally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal "Centripetal")) and is perpendicular to the velocity. This acceleration is known as centripetal acceleration.

For a path of radius r, when an angle θ is swept out, the distance traveled on the [periphery](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/periphery "wikt:periphery") of the orbit is _s_ = _rθ_. Therefore, the speed of travel around the orbit is ![Image 62: {\displaystyle v=r{\frac {d\theta }{dt}}=r\omega ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02c67eb95d8102b82f91d2de26bb90e57177f3cd) where the angular rate of rotation is _ω_. (By rearrangement, _ω_ = _v_/_r_.) Thus, _v_ is a constant, and the velocity vector **v** also rotates with constant magnitude _v_, at the same angular rate _ω_.

Non-uniform circular motion
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Circular_motion&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Non-uniform circular motion")]

[![Image 68](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Nonuniform_circular_motion.svg/250px-Nonuniform_circular_motion.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Nonuniform_circular_motion.svg)

Velocity and acceleration in non-uniform circular motion.

In **non-uniform circular motion**, an object moves in a circular path with varying [speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed "Speed"). Since the speed is changing, there is [tangential acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangential_acceleration "Tangential acceleration") in addition to normal acceleration.

The net acceleration is directed towards the interior of the circle (but does not pass through its center).

The net acceleration may be resolved into two components: tangential acceleration and centripetal acceleration. Unlike tangential acceleration, centripetal acceleration is present in both uniform and non-uniform circular motion.

[![Image 69](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/Freebody_circular.svg/250px-Freebody_circular.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Freebody_circular.svg)

This diagram shows the normal force (n) pointing in other directions rather than opposite to the weight force.

In non-uniform circular motion, the [normal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_force "Normal force") does not always point to the opposite direction of [weight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weight "Weight").

[![Image 70](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Freebody_object.svg/220px-Freebody_object.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Freebody_object.svg)

Here, 'n' is the normal force.

The normal force is actually the sum of the radial and tangential forces. The component of weight force is responsible for the tangential force (when we neglect friction). The centripetal force is due to the change in the direction of velocity.

The normal force and weight may also point in the same direction. Both forces can point downwards, yet the object will remain in a circular path without falling down.

[![Image 71](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Normal_and_weight.svg/220px-Normal_and_weight.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Normal_and_weight.svg)

The normal force can point downwards.

The normal force _can_ point downwards. Considering that the object is a person sitting inside a plane moving in a circle, the two forces (weight and normal force) will point down only when the plane reaches the top of the circle. The reason for this is that the normal force is the sum of the tangential force and centripetal force. The tangential force is zero at the top (as no work is performed when the motion is perpendicular to the direction of force). Since weight is perpendicular to the direction of motion of the object at the top of the circle and the centripetal force points downwards, the normal force will point down as well.

From a logical standpoint, a person travelling in that plane will be upside down at the top of the circle. At that moment, the person's seat is actually pushing down on the person, which is the normal force.

The reason why an object does not fall down when subjected to only downward forces is a simple one. Once an object is thrown into the air, there is only the downward gravitational force that acts on the object. That does not mean that once an object is thrown into the air, it will fall instantly. The [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") of the object keeps it up in the air. The first of [Newton's laws of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion "Newton's laws of motion") states that an object's [inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertia "Inertia") keeps it in motion; since the object in the air has a velocity, it will tend to keep moving in that direction.

A varying [angular speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_speed "Angular speed") for an object moving in a circular path can also be achieved if the rotating body does not have a homogeneous mass distribution.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_motion#cite_note-2)

Solving applications dealing with non-uniform circular motion involves force analysis. With a uniform circular motion, the only force acting upon an object traveling in a circle is the centripetal force. In a non-uniform circular motion, there are additional forces acting on the object due to a non-zero tangential acceleration. Although there are forces acting upon the object, the sum of all the forces acting on the object will have to be equal to the centripetal force. ![Image 82: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}F_{\text{net}}&=ma\\&=ma_{r}\\&={\frac {mv^{2}}{r}}\\&=F_{c}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b44d3636b60a123ae683806792cffcfe4e01b11e)

Radial acceleration is used when calculating the total force. Tangential acceleration is not used in calculating total force because it is not responsible for keeping the object in a circular path. The only acceleration responsible for keeping an object moving in a circle is the radial acceleration. Since the sum of all forces is the centripetal force, drawing centripetal force into a free body diagram is not necessary and usually not recommended.

Using ![Image 83: {\displaystyle F_{\text{net}}=F_{c}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/17006af7e285247a55b45f93cc5fed57afd869f3), we can draw free body diagrams to list all the forces acting on an object and then set it equal to ![Image 84: {\displaystyle F_{c}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/60eebab5c416247ae9c8b7d317d03b23b0b31c4b). Afterward, we can solve for whatever is unknown (this can be mass, velocity, radius of curvature, coefficient of friction, normal force, etc.). For example, the visual above showing an object at the top of a semicircle would be expressed as ![Image 85: {\displaystyle F_{c}=n+mg}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/44fb251b32dcab89de4050f52280075aece71343).

In a uniform circular motion, the total acceleration of an object in a circular path is equal to the radial acceleration. Due to the presence of tangential acceleration in a non uniform circular motion, that does not hold true any more. To find the total acceleration of an object in a non uniform circular, find the vector sum of the tangential acceleration and the radial acceleration. ![Image 86: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {a_{r}^{2}+a_{t}^{2}}}=a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/929c495d7fe7f0fb1baeb66ad66a69010648aed5)

Radial acceleration is still equal to ![Image 87: {\textstyle {\frac {v^{2}}{r}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/550e8ec049d270c9cd718e8aea135a0a9790e849). Tangential acceleration is simply the derivative of the speed at any given point: ![Image 88: {\textstyle a_{t}={\frac {dv}{dt}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/825c13257c0b0cf7ca54ad4fdcddc512cc5a0823). This root sum of squares of separate radial and tangential accelerations is only correct for circular motion; for general motion within a plane with polar coordinates ![Image 89: {\displaystyle (r,\theta )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed8396fdc359fb06c93722137c959e7496e47ed6), the Coriolis term ![Image 90: {\textstyle a_{c}=2\left({\frac {dr}{dt}}\right)\left({\frac {d\theta }{dt}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68d74c41564c20b137deb7ab293b1b90686d8490) should be added to ![Image 91: {\displaystyle a_{t}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/77fce84b535e9e195e3d30ce5ae09b372d87e2e9), whereas radial acceleration then becomes ![Image 92: {\textstyle a_{r}={\frac {-v^{2}}{r}}+{\frac {d^{2}r}{dt^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df9ea48c0314f1b446eb65b34ed4d7999a9f96dc).
