Cartesian product of the sets {_x_,_y_,_z_} and {1,2,3}

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), specifically [set theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_theory "Set theory"), the **Cartesian product** of two [sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)")A and B, denoted _A_ × _B_, is the set of all [ordered pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair "Ordered pair")(_a_, _b_) where a is an element of A and b is an element of B.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product#cite_note-:1-1) In terms of [set-builder notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set-builder_notation "Set-builder notation"), that is ![Image 2: {\displaystyle A\times B=\{(a,b)\mid a\in A\ {\mbox{ and }}\ b\in B\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e04811beec4644d6b1c183b823f7cfd96f8ff6de)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product#cite_note-3)

A table can be created by taking the Cartesian product of a set of rows and a set of columns. If the Cartesian product _rows_ × _columns_ is taken, the cells of the table contain ordered pairs of the form (row value, column value).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product#cite_note-:2-4)

One can similarly define the Cartesian product of n sets, also known as an **n-fold Cartesian product**, which can be represented by an n-dimensional array, where each element is an n-[tuple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple "Tuple"). An ordered pair is a [2-tuple or couple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple#Etymology "Tuple"). More generally still, one can define the Cartesian product of an [indexed family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indexed_family "Indexed family") of sets.

The Cartesian product is named after [René Descartes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes "René Descartes"),[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product#cite_note-5) whose formulation of [analytic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_geometry "Analytic geometry") gave rise to the concept, which is further generalized in terms of [direct product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_product "Direct product").

Set-theoretic definition
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cartesian_product&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Set-theoretic definition")]

A rigorous definition of the Cartesian product requires a domain to be specified in the [set-builder notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set-builder_notation "Set-builder notation"). In this case the domain would have to contain the Cartesian product itself. For defining the Cartesian product of the sets ![Image 3: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) and ![Image 4: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a), with the typical [Kuratowski's definition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair#Kuratowski's_definition "Ordered pair") of a pair ![Image 5: {\displaystyle (a,b)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d7e5710198f33b00695903460983021e75860e2c) as ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \{\{a\},\{a,b\}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/15f7ce8f7bf3626c994984995df42875fe90db40), an appropriate domain is the set ![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {P}}({\mathcal {P}}(A\cup B))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2154ae19497b00bd2b958c07d11dfdc7e22233a7) where ![Image 8: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {P}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10d6ec962de5797ba4f161c40e66dca74ae95cc6) denotes the [power set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_set "Power set"). Then the Cartesian product of the sets ![Image 9: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) and ![Image 10: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) would be defined as[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product#cite_note-6)![Image 11: {\displaystyle A\times B=\{x\in {\mathcal {P}}({\mathcal {P}}(A\cup B))\mid \exists a\in A\ \exists b\in B:x=(a,b)\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b77ff19919bf3db0f0a405ccce0cd32718c34cb9)

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Piatnikcards.jpg/250px-Piatnikcards.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Piatnikcards.jpg)

Standard 52-card deck

An illustrative example is the [standard 52-card deck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_52-card_deck "Standard 52-card deck"). The [standard playing card](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Playing_cards#Anglo-American "Playing cards") ranks {A, K, Q, J, 10, 9, 8, 7, 6, 5, 4, 3, 2} form a 13-element set. The card suits {♠, ♥, ♦, ♣} form a four-element set. The Cartesian product of these sets returns a 52-element set consisting of 52 [ordered pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair "Ordered pair"), which correspond to all 52 possible playing cards.

_Ranks_ × _Suits_ returns a set of the form {(A,♠), (A,♥), (A,♦), (A,♣), (K,♠), ..., (3,♣), (2,♠), (2,♥), (2,♦), (2,♣)}.

_Suits_ × _Ranks_ returns a set of the form {(♠,A), (♠,K), (♠,Q), (♠,J), (♠,10), ..., (♣,6), (♣,5), (♣,4), (♣,3), (♣,2)}.

These two sets are distinct, even [disjoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disjoint_sets "Disjoint sets"), but there is a natural [bijection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijection "Bijection") between them, under which (3,♣) corresponds to (♣,3) and so on.

### A two-dimensional coordinate system

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cartesian_product&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: A two-dimensional coordinate system")]

[![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0e/Cartesian-coordinate-system.svg/250px-Cartesian-coordinate-system.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cartesian-coordinate-system.svg)

Cartesian coordinates of example points

The main historical example is the [Cartesian plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_plane "Cartesian plane") in [analytic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_geometry "Analytic geometry"). In order to represent geometrical shapes in a numerical way, and extract numerical information from shapes' numerical representations, [René Descartes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes "René Descartes") assigned to each point in the plane a pair of [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number"), called its [coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinates "Coordinates"). Usually, such a pair's first and second components are called its x and y coordinates, respectively (see picture). The set of all such pairs (i.e., the Cartesian product ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} \times \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eb99f01c438a62e4ac5af8cff4eb402739ed67a8), with ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) denoting the real numbers) is thus assigned to the set of all points in the plane.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product#cite_note-7)

Most common implementation (set theory)
---------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cartesian_product&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Most common implementation (set theory)")]

A formal definition of the Cartesian product from [set-theoretical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_theory "Set theory") principles follows from a definition of [ordered pair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair "Ordered pair"). The most common definition of ordered pairs, [Kuratowski's definition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair#Kuratowski's_definition "Ordered pair"), is ![Image 16: {\displaystyle (x,y)=\{\{x\},\{x,y\}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2e04588c90083e1dd7520b3beabbb0a87342ed2a). Under this definition, ![Image 17: {\displaystyle (x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41cf50e4a314ca8e2c30964baa8d26e5be7a9386) is an element of ![Image 18: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {P}}({\mathcal {P}}(X\cup Y))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b1c8b26dbf72969e13e23d7fa6c6f4224271bd55), and ![Image 19: {\displaystyle X\times Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1613c1ff4b6fbfb6c80a8da83e90ad28f0ab3483) is a subset of that set, where ![Image 20: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {P}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10d6ec962de5797ba4f161c40e66dca74ae95cc6) represents the [power set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_set "Power set") operator. Therefore, the existence of the Cartesian product of any two sets in [ZFC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ZFC "ZFC") follows from the axioms of [pairing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_of_pairing "Axiom of pairing"), [union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_of_union "Axiom of union"), [power set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_of_power_set "Axiom of power set"), and [specification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_schema_of_specification "Axiom schema of specification"). Since [functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") are usually defined as a special case of [relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relation_(mathematics) "Relation (mathematics)"), and relations are usually defined as subsets of the Cartesian product, the definition of the two-set Cartesian product is necessarily prior to most other definitions.

### Non-commutativity and non-associativity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cartesian_product&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Non-commutativity and non-associativity")]

Let A, B, and C be sets.

The Cartesian product _A_ × _B_ is not [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative "Commutative"), ![Image 21: {\displaystyle A\times B\neq B\times A,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/298d14a15d0185ba56a4cc0843c05f5338fd2042)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product#cite_note-:2-4) because the [ordered pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair "Ordered pair") are reversed unless at least one of the following conditions is satisfied:[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product#cite_note-cnx-8)

*   A is equal to B, or
*   A or B is the [empty set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empty_set "Empty set").

For example:

_A_ = {1,2}; _B_ = {3,4}_A_ × _B_ = {1,2} × {3,4} = {(1,3), (1,4), (2,3), (2,4)}_B_ × _A_ = {3,4} × {1,2} = {(3,1), (3,2), (4,1), (4,2)}_A_ = _B_ = {1,2}_A_ × _B_ = _B_ × _A_ = {1,2} × {1,2} = {(1,1), (1,2), (2,1), (2,2)}_A_ = {1,2}; _B_ = ∅_A_ × _B_ = {1,2} × ∅ = ∅_B_ × _A_ = ∅ × {1,2} = ∅
Strictly speaking, the Cartesian product is not [associative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative "Associative") (unless one of the involved sets is empty). ![Image 22: {\displaystyle (A\times B)\times C\neq A\times (B\times C)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af4db9124b350a6b7bf22af04347b141aa848eae) If for example _A_ = {1}, then (_A_ × _A_) × _A_ = {((1, 1), 1)} ≠{(1, (1, 1))} = _A_ × (_A_ × _A_).

### Intersections, unions, and subsets

[![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3e/CartDistr_svg.svg/250px-CartDistr_svg.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:CartDistr_svg.svg)

_C_ = [4,7], demonstrating

_A_ × (_B_∩_C_) = (_A_×_B_) ∩ (_A_×_C_),

_A_ × (_B_∪_C_) = (_A_×_B_) ∪ (_A_×_C_), and

_A_ × (_B_ \ _C_) = (_A_×_B_) \ (_A_×_C_)

[![Image 24](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/CartInts_svg.svg/250px-CartInts_svg.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:CartInts_svg.svg)

Example sets

_A_ = [2,5], _B_ = [3,7], _C_ = [1,3],

_D_ = [2,4], demonstrating

(_A_∩_B_) × (_C_∩_D_) = (_A_×_C_) ∩ (_B_×_D_).

[![Image 25](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/CartUnion_svg.svg/250px-CartUnion_svg.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:CartUnion_svg.svg)

(_A_∪_B_) × (_C_∪_D_) ≠ (_A_×_C_) ∪ (_B_×_D_) can be seen from the same example.

The Cartesian product satisfies the following property with respect to [intersections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intersection_(set_theory) "Intersection (set theory)") (see middle picture). ![Image 26: {\displaystyle (A\cap B)\times (C\cap D)=(A\times C)\cap (B\times D)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/80fcc10c9d5e8cae71c50d578881faeb3c518430)

In most cases, the above statement is not true if we replace intersection with [union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_(set_theory) "Union (set theory)") (see rightmost picture). ![Image 27: {\displaystyle (A\cup B)\times (C\cup D)\neq (A\times C)\cup (B\times D)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b71587d69d860f6dd4e3aefd0f9dd13bbbd9b639)

In fact, we have that: ![Image 28: {\displaystyle (A\times C)\cup (B\times D)=[(A\setminus B)\times C]\cup [(A\cap B)\times (C\cup D)]\cup [(B\setminus A)\times D]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/67cfa315894265914c23ed2d555d05e6255d98a4)

For the set difference, we also have the following identity: ![Image 29: {\displaystyle (A\times C)\setminus (B\times D)=[A\times (C\setminus D)]\cup [(A\setminus B)\times C]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/401c029889c8cdaaa16d20a38c311158b98cfd41)

Here are some rules demonstrating distributivity with other operators (see leftmost picture):[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product#cite_note-cnx-8)![Image 30: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}A\times (B\cap C)&=(A\times B)\cap (A\times C),\\A\times (B\cup C)&=(A\times B)\cup (A\times C),\\A\times (B\setminus C)&=(A\times B)\setminus (A\times C),\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31ab07a4954f68032c0bb8adb45f598e84cdc525)![Image 31: {\displaystyle (A\times B)^{\complement }=\left(A^{\complement }\times B^{\complement }\right)\cup \left(A^{\complement }\times B\right)\cup \left(A\times B^{\complement }\right)\!,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/19fc2dd5f0fea9a06528accd980f5f549a27249e) where ![Image 32: {\displaystyle A^{\complement }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e02346e3f6e10452080e6f3a7326e0aa2185dc2a) denotes the [absolute complement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_complement "Absolute complement") of A.

Other properties related with [subsets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset") are:

![Image 33: {\displaystyle {\text{if }}A\subseteq B{\text{, then }}A\times C\subseteq B\times C;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d89f47eb024f952d5fb0fc73e48d86016c7bc3b6)
![Image 34: {\displaystyle {\text{if both }}A,B\neq \emptyset {\text{, then }}A\times B\subseteq C\times D\!\iff \!A\subseteq C{\text{ and }}B\subseteq D.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea1fcf4b32eb8ecabd0ebef4ae0cb108b691dc7f)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product#cite_note-9)

The [cardinality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality "Cardinality") of a set is the number of elements of the set. For example, defining two sets: _A_ = {a, b} and _B_ = {5, 6}. Both set A and set B consist of two elements each. Their Cartesian product, written as _A_ × _B_, results in a new set which has the following elements:

_A_ × _B_ = {(a,5), (a,6), (b,5), (b,6)}.
where each element of A is paired with each element of B, and where each pair makes up one element of the output set. The number of values in each element of the resulting set is equal to the number of sets whose Cartesian product is being taken; 2 in this case. The cardinality of the output set is equal to the product of the cardinalities of all the input sets. That is,

|_A_ × _B_| = |_A_| · |_B_|.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product#cite_note-:2-4)
In this case, |_A_ × _B_| = 4

Similarly,

|_A_ × _B_ × _C_| = |_A_| · |_B_| · |_C_|
and so on.

The set _A_ × _B_ is [infinite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_set "Infinite set") if either A or B is infinite, and the other set is not the empty set.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product#cite_note-10)

Cartesian products of several sets

### _n_-ary Cartesian product

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cartesian_product&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: n-ary Cartesian product")]

The Cartesian product can be generalized to the **n-ary Cartesian product** over n sets _X_ 1, ..., _X_ _n_ as the set ![Image 35: {\displaystyle X_{1}\times \cdots \times X_{n}=\{(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n})\mid x_{i}\in X_{i}\ {\text{for every}}\ i\in \{1,\ldots ,n\}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f7129d5c21a7d804029e9c4b85fc831f215668f9)

of [n-tuples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple "Tuple"). If tuples are defined as [nested ordered pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple#Tuples_as_nested_ordered_pairs "Tuple"), it can be identified with (_X_ 1 × ... × _X_ _n_−1) × _X n_. If a tuple is defined as a function on {1, 2, ..., _n_} that takes its value at i to be the i-th element of the tuple, then the Cartesian product _X_ 1 × ... × _X_ _n_ is the set of functions ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \{x:\{1,\ldots ,n\}\to X_{1}\cup \cdots \cup X_{n}\ |\ x(i)\in X_{i}\ {\text{for every}}\ i\in \{1,\ldots ,n\}\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61762bce51ae481400c98b10483773a4e126c497)

### _n_-ary Cartesian power

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cartesian_product&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: n-ary Cartesian power")]

The **Cartesian square** of a set X is the Cartesian product _X_ 2 = _X_ × _X_. An example is the 2-dimensional [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(mathematics) "Plane (mathematics)")**R**2 = **R** × **R** where **R** is the set of [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number"):[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product#cite_note-:1-1)**R**2 is the set of all points (_x_,_y_) where x and y are real numbers (see the [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system")).

The **n-ary Cartesian power** of a set X, denoted ![Image 37: {\displaystyle X^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/268db8293666fefd75cfb00513706171948edf09), can be defined as ![Image 38: {\displaystyle X^{n}=\underbrace {X\times X\times \cdots \times X} _{n}=\{(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n})\ |\ x_{i}\in X\ {\text{for every}}\ i\in \{1,\ldots ,n\}\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d31c44c67052aed5ae0d3865240381b2dff2eefe)

An example of this is **R**3 = **R** × **R** × **R**, with **R** again the set of real numbers,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product#cite_note-:1-1) and more generally **R**_n_.

The n-ary Cartesian power of a set X is [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism "Isomorphism") to the space of functions from an n-element set to X. As a special case, the 0-ary Cartesian power of X may be taken to be a [singleton set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singleton_set "Singleton set"), corresponding to the [empty function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empty_function "Empty function") with [codomain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codomain "Codomain")X.

### Intersections, unions, complements and subsets

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cartesian_product&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Intersections, unions, complements and subsets")]

2.   ![Image 44: {\displaystyle A\cap B=(A_{1}\cap B_{1})\times \dots \times (A_{n}\cap B_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b768f1bae44fce3acda0adff5786f27ace8e0c9), at the same time, if there exists at least one ![Image 45: {\displaystyle i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/add78d8608ad86e54951b8c8bd6c8d8416533d20) such that ![Image 46: {\displaystyle A_{i}\cap B_{i}=\varnothing }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae63b1c247a46a4c8eb2e00fc0c3cfcc1bf738ea), then ![Image 47: {\displaystyle A\cap B=\varnothing }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1325e582d7bc7ad1e30dfef4ee67747895af068e);[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product#cite_note-:3-11)
3.   ![Image 48: {\displaystyle A\cup B\subseteq (A_{1}\cup B_{1})\times \dots \times (A_{n}\cup B_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/486f621453731c1d3394d79d887854a4ef0d7710), moreover, equality is possible only in the following cases:[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product#cite_note-:4-12)
    1.   ![Image 49: {\displaystyle A\subseteq B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b09068bd2f7ba899aeb883ebe670b2ad07b0c851) or ![Image 50: {\displaystyle B\subseteq A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb8124cb68686ede7083aa2a5a821f262eb62954);
    2.   for all ![Image 51: {\displaystyle i=1,2,\ldots ,n\quad A_{i}=B_{i}\quad }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7463823ef975523ce019a83dc5a4e52babf110ee) except for one from ![Image 52: {\displaystyle i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/add78d8608ad86e54951b8c8bd6c8d8416533d20).

4.   The complement of a Cartesian product ![Image 53: {\displaystyle A=A_{1}\times \dots \times A_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/117f883d87da4dbb6792962912a9a192dc013b2c) can be calculated,[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product#cite_note-:4-12) if a _universe_ is defined ![Image 54: {\displaystyle U=X_{1}\times \dots \times X_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1237d938bf6fafa7ba343f0ffa1bd112bf87f794). To simplify the expressions, we introduce the following notation. Let us denote the Cartesian product as a tuple bounded by square brackets; this tuple includes the sets from which the Cartesian product is formed, e.g.:

![Image 55: {\displaystyle A=A_{1}\times A_{2}\times \dots \times A_{n}=[A_{1}\quad A_{2}\quad \dots \quad A_{n}]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f263e27a488d5c2b133c1511ac31c0742b54937).
In _n-tuple algebra_ (NTA), [[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product#cite_note-:4-12) such a matrix-like representation of Cartesian products is called a _C-n-tuple_.

With this in mind, the union of some Cartesian products given in the same universe can be expressed as a matrix bounded by square brackets, in which the rows represent the Cartesian products involved in the union:

![Image 56: {\displaystyle A\cup B=(A_{1}\times A_{2}\times \dots \times A_{n})\cup (B_{1}\times B_{2}\times \dots \times B_{n})=\left[{\begin{array}{cccc}A_{1}&A_{2}&\dots &A_{n}\\B_{1}&B_{2}&\dots &B_{n}\end{array}}\right]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4bc8ec1086612b829272b95bb0ddb3ee54fba7a1).
Such a structure is called a _C-system_ in NTA.

Then the complement of the Cartesian product ![Image 57: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) will look like the following _C_-system expressed as a matrix of the dimension ![Image 58: {\displaystyle n\times n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59d2b4cb72e304526cf5b5887147729ea259da78):

![Image 59: {\displaystyle A^{\complement }=\left[{\begin{array}{ccccc}A_{1}^{\complement }&X_{2}&\dots &X_{n-1}&X_{n}\\X_{1}&A_{2}^{\complement }&\dots &X_{n-1}&X_{n}\\\dots &\dots &\dots &\dots &\dots \\X_{1}&X_{2}&\dots &A_{n-1}^{\complement }&X_{n}\\X_{1}&X_{2}&\dots &X_{n-1}&A_{n}^{\complement }\end{array}}\right]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b1872e97a8f0ed419fe732d9e81da248057d7401).
The diagonal components of this matrix ![Image 60: {\displaystyle A_{i}^{\complement }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d1661fa2c41803d1dd2d4d3054fde665ba60df32) are equal correspondingly to ![Image 61: {\displaystyle X_{i}\setminus A_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0976cd9152a420eece8fc0bcfb63295c37cbd560).

In NTA, a diagonal _C_-system ![Image 62: {\displaystyle A^{\complement }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e02346e3f6e10452080e6f3a7326e0aa2185dc2a), that represents the complement of a _C-n_-tuple ![Image 63: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3), can be written concisely as a tuple of diagonal components bounded by inverted square brackets:

![Image 64: {\displaystyle A^{\complement }=]A_{1}^{\complement }\quad A_{2}^{\complement }\quad \dots \quad A_{n}^{\complement }[}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a913f7ef1e9150299787df629e9daefeace55f08).
This structure is called a _D-n-tuple_. Then the complement of the _C_-system ![Image 65: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is a structure ![Image 66: {\displaystyle R^{\complement }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d05a991ba542b47b7538ac245d56e829d2da8c5), represented by a matrix of the same dimension and bounded by inverted square brackets, in which all components are equal to the complements of the components of the initial matrix ![Image 67: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33). Such a structure is called a _D_-system and is calculated, if necessary, as the intersection of the _D-n_-tuples contained in it. For instance, if the following _C_-system is given:

![Image 68: {\displaystyle R_{1}=\left[{\begin{array}{cccc}A_{1}&A_{2}&\dots &A_{n}\\B_{1}&B_{2}&\dots &B_{n}\end{array}}\right]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f8707632efacd6f90bf7e6412c58f60b92256164),
then its complement will be the _D_-system

![Image 69: {\displaystyle R_{1}^{\complement }=\left]{\begin{array}{cccc}A_{1}^{\complement }&A_{2}^{\complement }&\dots &A_{n}^{\complement }\\B_{1}^{\complement }&B_{2}^{\complement }&\dots &B_{n}^{\complement }\end{array}}\right[}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d2cb4acf40aa40bc7ca0b187501922b0691f319d).
Let us consider some new relations for structures with Cartesian products obtained in the process of studying the properties of NTA.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product#cite_note-:4-12) The structures defined in the same universe are called _homotypic_ ones.

1.   _The intersection of C-systems_. Assume the homotypic _C_-systems are given ![Image 70: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) and ![Image 71: {\displaystyle Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8752c7023b4b3286800fe3238271bbca681219ed). 2.   _Checking the inclusion of a C-n-tuple into a D-n-tuple_. For the _C-n_-tuple ![Image 74: {\displaystyle P=[P_{1}\quad P_{2}\quad \cdots \quad P_{N}]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1990bbdbf2f5b6f9d8a4136c28a52bdc919d7c73) and the _D-n_-tuple ![Image 75: {\displaystyle Q=]Q_{1}\quad Q_{2}\quad \cdots \quad Q_{N}[}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2e24a3c1abe35d23b1ae203c96c653eb496b5081) holds ![Image 76: {\displaystyle P\subseteq Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e34254b0983c5c0c24431161c2392c54f2b03ae), if and only if, at least for one ![Image 77: {\displaystyle i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/add78d8608ad86e54951b8c8bd6c8d8416533d20) holds ![Image 78: {\displaystyle P_{i}\subseteq Q_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e1f88b27b9af212aea14116d9cb19b3bc8bb867a).

For the _C-n_-tuple ![Image 79: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) and the _D_-system ![Image 80: {\displaystyle Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8752c7023b4b3286800fe3238271bbca681219ed) is true ![Image 81: {\displaystyle P\subseteq Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e34254b0983c5c0c24431161c2392c54f2b03ae), if and only if, for every _D-n_-tuple ![Image 82: {\displaystyle Q_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b9f7193081d440425e522698e80817b5d558df03) from ![Image 83: {\displaystyle Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8752c7023b4b3286800fe3238271bbca681219ed) holds ![Image 84: {\displaystyle P\subseteq Q_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c815542160ab367ce5bdc330d5d8fe42438c89c4).

### Infinite Cartesian products

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cartesian_product&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Infinite Cartesian products")]

It is possible to define the Cartesian product of an arbitrary (possibly [infinite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinity "Infinity")) [indexed family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indexed_family "Indexed family") of sets. If I is any [index set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Index_set "Index set"), and ![Image 85: {\displaystyle \{X_{i}\}_{i\in I}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c0beca1beee1298c4b0545d7f554749ce2d7f36e) is a family of sets indexed by I, then the Cartesian product of the sets in ![Image 86: {\displaystyle \{X_{i}\}_{i\in I}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c0beca1beee1298c4b0545d7f554749ce2d7f36e) is defined to be ![Image 87: {\displaystyle \prod _{i\in I}X_{i}=\left\{\left.f:I\to \bigcup _{i\in I}X_{i}\ \right|\ \forall i\in I.\ f(i)\in X_{i}\right\},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ceb166017e6c7b48f0373cf236d98c1b4eae20d5) that is, the set of all functions defined on the [index set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Index_set "Index set")I such that the value of the function at a particular index i is an element of _X i_. Even if each of the _X i_ is nonempty, the Cartesian product may be empty if the [axiom of choice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_of_choice "Axiom of choice"), which is equivalent to the statement that every such product is nonempty, is not assumed. ![Image 88: {\displaystyle \prod _{i\in I}X_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e4cf2ee0af61dd971a31005ff6d10fb3fe3267f) may also be denoted ![Image 89: {\displaystyle {\mathsf {X}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/525dc42f6e3522443cd90cb029c222181ea663aa)![Image 90: {\displaystyle {}_{i\in I}X_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52925707cc01bb3d8e5151b2954d3f2a96c78684).[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product#cite_note-13)

For each j in I, the function ![Image 91: {\displaystyle \pi _{j}:\prod _{i\in I}X_{i}\to X_{j},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9ad18e7edcf27a9b3c9885bc399e679fbe077edf) defined by ![Image 92: {\displaystyle \pi _{j}(f)=f(j)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16ef2fa207f17b276d5a9c440c8330ac8120cddb) is called the **j-th [projection map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projection_(mathematics) "Projection (mathematics)")**.

**Cartesian power** is a Cartesian product where all the factors _X i_ are the same set X. In this case, ![Image 93: {\displaystyle \prod _{i\in I}X_{i}=\prod _{i\in I}X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d12cd53d15a26081fd13aba1a0afe46553147b0f) is the set of all functions from I to X, and is frequently denoted _X I_. This case is important in the study of [cardinal exponentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinal_exponentiation "Cardinal exponentiation"). An important special case is when the index set is ![Image 94: {\displaystyle \mathbb {N} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdf9a96b565ea202d0f4322e9195613fb26a9bed), the [natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number"): this Cartesian product is the set of all infinite sequences with the i-th term in its corresponding set _X i_. For example, each element of ![Image 95: {\displaystyle \prod _{n=1}^{\infty }\mathbb {R} =\mathbb {R} \times \mathbb {R} \times \cdots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a211a7f15269873e5e07ce7b682aec66f806de0) can be visualized as a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") with countably infinite real number components. This set is frequently denoted ![Image 96: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{\omega }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0881ff53d61be4b3ed806e41af0d2ffba29c09b), or ![Image 97: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{\mathbb {N} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2608c429068c69675f1144f0ba8249603a8ceaf4).

If several sets are being multiplied together (e.g., _X_ 1, _X_ 2, _X_ 3, ...), then some authors[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product#cite_note-14) choose to abbreviate the Cartesian product as simply ×_X_ _i_.

### Cartesian product of functions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cartesian_product&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Cartesian product of functions")]

If f is a function from X to A and g is a function from Y to B, then their Cartesian product _f_ × _g_ is a function from _X_ × _Y_ to _A_ × _B_ with ![Image 98: {\displaystyle (f\times g)(x,y)=(f(x),g(y)).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ccc6c61d8b2c712f0e9b1c734439c045c9385858)

This can be extended to [tuples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple "Tuple") and infinite collections of functions. This is different from the standard Cartesian product of functions considered as sets.

Let ![Image 99: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) be a set and ![Image 100: {\displaystyle B\subseteq A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb8124cb68686ede7083aa2a5a821f262eb62954). Then the _cylinder_ of ![Image 101: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) with respect to ![Image 102: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) is the Cartesian product ![Image 103: {\displaystyle B\times A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b1f7f117a7b4c40424838eb9f4717e683c46b2a) of ![Image 104: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) and ![Image 105: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3).

Normally, ![Image 106: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) is considered to be the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe_(mathematics) "Universe (mathematics)") of the context and is left away. For example, if ![Image 107: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) is a subset of the natural numbers ![Image 108: {\displaystyle \mathbb {N} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdf9a96b565ea202d0f4322e9195613fb26a9bed), then the cylinder of ![Image 109: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) is ![Image 110: {\displaystyle B\times \mathbb {N} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ebaf6a75a8339a0af1af01ed8a6e86d56b64a47).

Definitions outside set theory
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cartesian_product&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Definitions outside set theory")]

Although the Cartesian product is traditionally applied to sets, [category theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_theory "Category theory") provides a more general interpretation of the [product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_(category_theory) "Product (category theory)") of mathematical structures. This is distinct from, although related to, the notion of a [Cartesian square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_square_(category_theory) "Cartesian square (category theory)") in category theory, which is a generalization of the [fiber product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_product "Fiber product").

[Exponentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_object "Exponential object") is the [right adjoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_adjoint "Right adjoint") of the Cartesian product; thus any category with a Cartesian product (and a [final object](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Final_object "Final object")) is a [Cartesian closed category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_closed_category "Cartesian closed category").

In [graph theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_theory "Graph theory"), the [Cartesian product of two graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product_of_graphs "Cartesian product of graphs")G and H is the graph denoted by _G_ × _H_, whose [vertex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(graph_theory) "Vertex (graph theory)") set is the (ordinary) Cartesian product _V_(_G_) × _V_(_H_) and such that two vertices (_u_,_v_) and (_u_′,_v_′) are adjacent in _G_ × _H_, if and only if _u_ = _u_′ and v is adjacent with v′ in H, _or_ _v_ = _v_′ and u is adjacent with u′ in G. The Cartesian product of graphs is not a [product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_(category_theory) "Product (category theory)") in the sense of category theory. Instead, the categorical product is known as the [tensor product of graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product_of_graphs "Tensor product of graphs").
