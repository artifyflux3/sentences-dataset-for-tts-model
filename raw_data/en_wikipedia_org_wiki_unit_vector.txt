In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **unit vector** in a [normed vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normed_vector_space "Normed vector space") is a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(mathematics_and_physics) "Vector (mathematics and physics)") (often a [spatial vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(geometry) "Vector (geometry)")) of [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_(mathematics) "Norm (mathematics)") 1. A unit vector is often denoted by a lowercase letter with a [circumflex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumflex "Circumflex"), or "hat", as in ![Image 1: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {v} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07d20e4ec673485a4ba42dc927be6ce1c00184a) (pronounced "v-hat"). The term _normalized vector_ is sometimes used as a synonym for _unit vector_.

The **normalized vector û** of a non-zero vector **u** is the unit vector in the direction of **u**, i.e.,

![Image 2: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\hat {u}} ={\frac {\mathbf {u} }{\|\mathbf {u} \|}}=\left({\frac {u_{1}}{\|\mathbf {u} \|}},{\frac {u_{2}}{\|\mathbf {u} \|}},...,{\frac {u_{n}}{\|\mathbf {u} \|}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7a2adfbeccccb2aa72d367b4d491399025a42461)
where ‖**u**‖ is the [norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_(mathematics) "Norm (mathematics)") (or length) of **u** and ![Image 3: {\textstyle \mathbf {u} =(u_{1},u_{2},...,u_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e002e7700b3e6f6d857c0fdd325cd87172ac1fd7).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector#cite_note-:0-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector#cite_note-2)

The proof is the following: ![Image 4: {\textstyle \|\mathbf {\hat {u}} \|={\sqrt {{\frac {u_{1}}{\sqrt {u_{1}^{2}+...+u_{n}^{2}}}}^{2}+...+{\frac {u_{n}}{\sqrt {u_{1}^{2}+...+u_{n}^{2}}}}^{2}}}={\sqrt {\frac {u_{1}^{2}+...+u_{n}^{2}}{u_{1}^{2}+...+u_{n}^{2}}}}={\sqrt {1}}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/703ba405d17301af7fc30e0a4d2c9ca542bbf503)

A unit vector is often used to represent [directions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direction_(geometry) "Direction (geometry)"), such as [normal directions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_direction "Normal direction"). Unit vectors are often chosen to form the [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)") of a vector space, and every vector in the space may be written as a [linear combination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_combination "Linear combination") form of unit vectors.

### Cartesian coordinates

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Unit_vector&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Cartesian coordinates")]

Unit vectors may be used to represent the axes of a [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system"). For instance, the standard _unit vectors_ in the direction of the _x_, _y_, and _z_ axes of a [three dimensional Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") are

![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\hat {x}} ={\begin{bmatrix}1\\0\\0\end{bmatrix}},\,\,\mathbf {\hat {y}} ={\begin{bmatrix}0\\1\\0\end{bmatrix}},\,\,\mathbf {\hat {z}} ={\begin{bmatrix}0\\0\\1\end{bmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/736952e6a93a07c6203cab8fb65a3d3402796663)
They form a set of mutually [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal "Orthogonal")_unit vectors_, typically referred to as a [standard basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_basis "Standard basis") in [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra").

They are often denoted using common [vector notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_notation "Vector notation") (e.g., **x** or ![Image 6: {\displaystyle {\vec {x}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db2dc6ced9cc3bc7e8b9f2707cbec033f6d3759c)) rather than standard unit vector notation (e.g., **x̂**). In most contexts it can be assumed that **x**, **y**, and **z**, (or ![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\vec {x}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bee05dc98bb8d7ba15b4ec913c320d47ca8be2ef)![Image 8: {\displaystyle {\vec {y}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/573b97c49ae3a4f9a3c10644f344171a1df165ef) and ![Image 9: {\displaystyle {\vec {z}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/790d3f4970e0b9cdd15408437b2f6df1b498c9c1)) are versors of a 3-D [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system"). The notations (**î**, **ĵ**, **k̂**), (**x̂1**, **x̂2**, **x̂3**), (**ê x**, **ê y**, **ê z**), or (**ê 1**, **ê 2**, **ê 3**), with or without [hat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hat_notation "Hat notation"), are also used,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector#cite_note-:0-1) particularly in contexts where **i**, **j**, **k** might lead to confusion with another quantity (for instance with [index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indexed_family "Indexed family") symbols such as _i_, _j_, _k_, which are used to identify an element of a set or array or sequence of variables).

When a unit vector in space is expressed in [Cartesian notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system#Representing_a_vector_with_Cartesian_notation "Cartesian coordinate system") as a linear combination of **x**, **y**, **z**, its three scalar components can be referred to as [direction cosines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direction_cosines "Direction cosines"). The value of each component is equal to the cosine of the angle formed by the unit vector with the respective basis vector. This is one of the methods used to describe the [orientation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation_(mathematics) "Orientation (mathematics)") (angular position) of a straight line, segment of straight line, oriented axis, or segment of oriented axis ([vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(geometry) "Vector (geometry)")).

Curvilinear coordinates
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Unit_vector&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Curvilinear coordinates")]

In general, a coordinate system may be uniquely specified using a number of [linearly independent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_independence "Linear independence") unit vectors ![Image 56: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\hat {e}} _{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be354d0bd70ecdbe586049962afa0bf042276020)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector#cite_note-:0-1) (the actual number being equal to the degrees of freedom of the space). For ordinary 3-space, these vectors may be denoted ![Image 57: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\hat {e}} _{1},\mathbf {\hat {e}} _{2},\mathbf {\hat {e}} _{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59c2304f094c9195f68273363426944087cbef07). It is nearly always convenient to define the system to be orthonormal and [right-handed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-hand_rule "Right-hand rule"):

![Image 58: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\hat {e}} _{i}\cdot \mathbf {\hat {e}} _{j}=\delta _{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/896cf83684ca3b7e871fa25f274677f4e420f447)![Image 59: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\hat {e}} _{i}\cdot (\mathbf {\hat {e}} _{j}\times \mathbf {\hat {e}} _{k})=\varepsilon _{ijk}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3d941282105cb29946ca1f62b3a0ff2df3eda04c)
where ![Image 60: {\displaystyle \delta _{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa75d04c11480d976e1396951e02cbb3c4f71568) is the [Kronecker delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta "Kronecker delta") (which is 1 for _i_ = _j_, and 0 otherwise) and ![Image 61: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{ijk}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21525193117bdfc0f3ac71b8ec46e3b6d0637daf) is the [Levi-Civita symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol "Levi-Civita symbol") (which is 1 for permutations ordered as _ijk_, and −1 for permutations ordered as _kji_).

A unit vector in ![Image 62: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) was called a **right versor** by [W. R. Hamilton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W._R._Hamilton "W. R. Hamilton"), as he developed his [quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion "Quaternion")![Image 63: {\displaystyle \mathbb {H} \subset \mathbb {R} ^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a93946a6b9bc8191686e1667fffbd49b9536d9c). In fact, he was the originator of the term _vector_, as every quaternion ![Image 64: {\displaystyle q=s+v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4250659806da9aa44694598a61160f7f3c99857d) has a scalar part _s_ and a vector part _v_. If _v_ is a unit vector in ![Image 65: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5), then the square of _v_ in quaternions is −1. Thus by [Euler's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_formula "Euler's formula"), ![Image 66: {\displaystyle \exp(\theta v)=\cos \theta +v\sin \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dcbda3751eadfb705b5dad97752ea8072e55e1bf) is a [versor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor "Versor") in the [3-sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3-sphere "3-sphere"). When _θ_ is a [right angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle "Right angle"), the versor is a right versor: its scalar part is zero and its vector part _v_ is a unit vector in ![Image 67: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5).

Thus the right versors extend the notion of [imaginary units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit "Imaginary unit") found in the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane"), where the right versors now range over the [2-sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2-sphere "2-sphere")![Image 68: {\displaystyle \mathbb {S} ^{2}\subset \mathbb {R} ^{3}\subset \mathbb {H} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5929a32c1cd9713cc4bb43f97d56820c8dbed5b8) rather than the pair {_i_, −_i_} in the complex plane.

By extension, a **right quaternion** is a real multiple of a right versor.
