Infrared spectrum of [HH 46/47](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HH_46/47 "HH 46/47") (image in inset), with [vibrational bands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vibrational_band "Vibrational band") of several molecules labelled in colour

This is a list of [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule") that have been detected in the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium") and [circumstellar envelopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumstellar_envelope "Circumstellar envelope"), grouped by the number of component [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom"). The [chemical formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula") is listed for each detected compound, along with any ionized form that has also been observed.

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Rotational_spectrum_example.png/330px-Rotational_spectrum_example.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Rotational_spectrum_example.png)

Idealised example of the rotational spectrum (bottom) produced by transitions between different rotational energy levels (top) of a simple [linear molecule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_molecule "Linear molecule"). ![Image 4: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) is the [rotational constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_constant "Rotational constant") of the molecule, ![Image 5: {\displaystyle J}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/359e4f407b49910e02c27c2f52e87a36cd74c053) is the rotational [quantum number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_number "Quantum number"), ![Image 6: {\displaystyle J'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c76d5f08e40c83bfc959e27a75dd387278214e9c) is the upper level and ![Image 7: {\displaystyle J''}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec133a827a3cecaf5299d1fcad5053868439056a) is the lower level.

The molecules listed below were detected through [astronomical spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy "Astronomical spectroscopy"). Their spectral features arise because molecules either absorb or emit a [photon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") of light when they transition between two molecular [energy levels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_levels "Energy levels"). The energy (and thus the [wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength "Wavelength")) of the photon matches the energy difference between the levels involved. [Molecular electronic transitions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_electronic_transition "Molecular electronic transition") occur when one of the molecule's [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") moves between [molecular orbitals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_orbitals "Molecular orbitals"), producing a [spectral line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line "Spectral line") in the [ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet"), [optical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visible_light "Visible light") or [near-infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near-infrared "Near-infrared") parts of the [electromagnetic spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum "Electromagnetic spectrum"). Alternatively, a [vibrational transition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vibrational_transition "Vibrational transition") transfers [quanta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum "Quantum") of energy to (or from) vibrations of [molecular bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_bonds "Molecular bonds"), producing signatures in the mid- or far-[infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared"). Gas-phase molecules also have [quantised rotational levels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_transition "Rotational transition"), leading to transitions at [microwave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave "Microwave") or [radio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio "Radio") wavelengths.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules#cite_note-shu82-1)

Sometimes a transition can involve more than one of these types of energy level e.g. [ro-vibrational spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ro-vibrational_spectroscopy "Ro-vibrational spectroscopy") changes both the rotational and vibrational energy level. Occasionally all three occur together, as in the Phillips band of C 2 ([diatomic carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_carbon "Diatomic carbon")), in which an electronic transition produces a line in the near-infrared, which is then split into several [vibronic bands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vibronic_band "Vibronic band") by a simultaneous change in vibrational level, which in turn are split again into [rotational branches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_spectroscopy "Rotational spectroscopy").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules#cite_note-2)

The spectrum of a particular molecule is governed by the [selection rules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Selection_rules "Selection rules") of [quantum chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_chemistry "Quantum chemistry") and by its [molecular symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_symmetry "Molecular symmetry"). Some molecules have simple spectra which are easy to identify, whilst others (even some small molecules) have extremely complex spectra with flux spread among many different lines, making them far harder to detect.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules#cite_note-mcguire_review-3) Interactions between the atomic nuclei and the electrons sometimes cause further [hyperfine structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperfine_structure "Hyperfine structure") of the spectral lines. If the molecule exists in multiple [isotopologues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotopologues "Isotopologues") (versions containing different atomic [isotopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope "Isotope")), the spectrum is further complicated by [isotope shifts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope_shift "Isotope shift").

Detection of a new interstellar or circumstellar molecule requires identifying a suitable astronomical object where it is likely to be present, then observing it with a [telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telescope "Telescope") equipped with a [spectrograph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectrograph "Spectrograph") working at the required wavelength, [spectral resolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_resolution "Spectral resolution") and sensitivity. The first molecule detected in the interstellar medium was the [methylidyne radical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylidyne_radical "Methylidyne radical") (CH•) in 1937, through its strong electronic transition at 4300 [angstroms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angstroms "Angstroms") (in the optical).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules#cite_note-woon05-4) Advances in [astronomical instrumentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_instrumentation "Astronomical instrumentation") have led to increasing numbers of new detections. From the 1950s onwards, [radio astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_astronomy "Radio astronomy") began to dominate new detections, with [sub-mm astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sub-mm_astronomy "Sub-mm astronomy") also becoming important from the 1990s.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules#cite_note-mcguire_review-3)

The inventory of detected molecules is highly biased towards certain types which are easier to detect. For example, radio astronomy is most sensitive to small linear molecules with a high [molecular dipole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_dipole "Molecular dipole").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules#cite_note-mcguire_review-3) The most common molecule in the Universe, H 2 ([molecular hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_hydrogen "Molecular hydrogen")), is completely invisible to radio telescopes because it has no dipole;[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules#cite_note-mcguire_review-3) its electronic transitions are too energetic for optical telescopes, so detection of H 2 required ultraviolet observations with a [sounding rocket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sounding_rocket "Sounding rocket").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules#cite_note-apj161-5) Vibrational lines are often not specific to an individual molecule, allowing only the general class to be identified. For example, the vibrational lines of [polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycyclic_aromatic_hydrocarbon "Polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbon") (PAHs) were identified in 1984,[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules#cite_note-leger_puget-6) showing the class of molecules is very common in space,[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules#cite_note-7) but it took until 2021 to identify any specific PAHs through their rotational lines.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules#cite_note-mcguire_pah-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules#cite_note-burkhardt_indene-9)

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/CW_Leonis_-_HST_-_Heic2112a.jpg/330px-CW_Leonis_-_HST_-_Heic2112a.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:CW_Leonis_-_HST_-_Heic2112a.jpg)

The carbon star [CW Leonis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CW_Leonis "CW Leonis"). The visible shells of circumstellar material were ejected by the central star over thousands of years.

One of the richest sources for detecting interstellar molecules is [Sagittarius B2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sagittarius_B2 "Sagittarius B2") (Sgr B2), a [giant molecular cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_molecular_cloud "Giant molecular cloud") near the centre of the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way"). About half of the molecules listed below were first found in Sgr B2, and many of the others have been subsequently detected there.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules#cite_note-ajss60-10) Many of the largest molecules were first detected in another molecular cloud, [TMC-1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TMC-1 "TMC-1"). A rich source of circumstellar molecules is [CW Leonis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CW_Leonis "CW Leonis") (also known as IRC+10216), a nearby [carbon star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_star "Carbon star"), where about 50 molecules have been identified.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules#cite_note-kaler2002-11) There is no clear boundary between interstellar and circumstellar media, so both are included in the tables below.

The discipline of [astrochemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrochemistry "Astrochemistry") includes understanding how these molecules form and explaining their abundances. The extremely low density of the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium") is not conducive to the formation of molecules, making conventional [gas-phase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas-phase "Gas-phase") reactions between neutral species (atoms or molecules) inefficient. Many regions also have very low temperatures (typically 10 [kelvin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin") inside a molecular cloud), further reducing the reaction rates, or high ultraviolet radiation fields, which destroy molecules through [photochemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photochemistry "Photochemistry").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules#cite_note-brown_pais95-12) Explaining the observed abundances of interstellar molecules requires calculating the balance between formation and destruction rates using [gas-phase ion chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas-phase_ion_chemistry "Gas-phase ion chemistry") (often driven by [cosmic rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_ray "Cosmic ray")), [surface chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_chemistry "Surface chemistry") on [cosmic dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_dust "Cosmic dust"), [radiative transfer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiative_transfer "Radiative transfer") including [interstellar extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_extinction "Interstellar extinction"), and sophisticated [reaction networks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_reaction_network_theory "Chemical reaction network theory").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules#cite_note-pnas103d-13) The use of molecular lines to determine the physical properties of astronomical objects is known as [molecular astrophysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_astrophysics "Molecular astrophysics").

The following tables list molecules that have been detected in the interstellar medium or circumstellar matter, grouped by the number of component [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom"). Neutral molecules and their [molecular ions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_ions "Molecular ions") are listed in separate columns; if there is no entry in the molecule column, only the ionized form has been detected. Designations (names of molecules) are those used in the scientific literature describing the detection; if none was given that field is left empty. Mass is listed in [daltons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dalton_(unit) "Dalton (unit)"). [Deuterated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuterated "Deuterated") molecules, which contain at least one [deuterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuterium "Deuterium") (2 H) atom, have slightly different masses and are listed in a separate table. The total number of unique species, including distinct ionization states, is indicated in each section header.

Most of the molecules detected so far are [organic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_molecule "Organic molecule"). The only detected [inorganic molecule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inorganic_molecule "Inorganic molecule") with five or more atoms is SiH 4.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules#cite_note-Klemperer2011-14) Molecules larger than that all have at least one carbon atom, with no N−N or O−O bonds.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules#cite_note-Klemperer2011-14)

The [H+ 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonated_molecular_hydrogen "Protonated molecular hydrogen") cation is one of the most abundant ions in the universe. [![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Formaldehyde-3D-vdW.png/250px-Formaldehyde-3D-vdW.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Formaldehyde-3D-vdW.png)

[Formaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde "Formaldehyde") is an organic molecule that is widely distributed in the interstellar medium.[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules#cite_note-prl61-95)

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/Methane-3D-space-filling.svg/250px-Methane-3D-space-filling.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Methane-3D-space-filling.svg)

[Methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane"), the primary component of [natural gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_gas "Natural gas"), has also been detected on [comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet "Comet") and in the atmosphere of several [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet") in the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System").[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules#cite_note-mnras347-122)

[![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Formamide-3D-vdW.png/250px-Formamide-3D-vdW.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Formamide-3D-vdW.png)

In the ISM, [formamide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formamide "Formamide") (above) can combine with [methylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylene_radical "Methylene radical") to form [acetamide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetamide "Acetamide").[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules#cite_note-apj643hl-146)

[![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/Acetaldehyde-3D-vdW.png/250px-Acetaldehyde-3D-vdW.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Acetaldehyde-3D-vdW.png)

[Acetaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde "Acetaldehyde") (above) and its [isomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomer "Isomer")[vinyl alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethenol "Ethenol") and [ethylene oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_oxide "Ethylene oxide") have all been detected in interstellar space.[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules#cite_note-rnao011001-159)

The radio signature of [acetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid "Acetic acid"), a compound found in [vinegar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinegar "Vinegar"), was confirmed in 1997.[[169]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules#cite_note-acetic_acid-171)

Deuterated molecules (22)
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Deuterated molecules (22)")]

These molecules all contain one or more [deuterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuterium "Deuterium") atoms, a heavier [isotope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope "Isotope") of [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen").

Evidence for the existence of the following molecules has been reported in the scientific literature, but the detections either are described as tentative by the authors, or have been challenged by other researchers. They await independent confirmation.

*   [Molecular astrophysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_astrophysics "Molecular astrophysics")

*   [Molecules in stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecules_in_stars "Molecules in stars")

*   [Tholin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tholin "Tholin")
