From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Surface of revolution with a hole in the middle

For other uses, see [Toroid (disambiguation)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toroid_(disambiguation) "Toroid (disambiguation)").

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Toroid_by_Zureks.svg/250px-Toroid_by_Zureks.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Toroid_by_Zureks.svg)

A toroid using a square.

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Torus.png/250px-Torus.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Torus.png)

A [torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torus "Torus") is a type of toroid.

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **toroid** is a [surface of revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_of_revolution "Surface of revolution") with a hole in the middle. The axis of revolution passes through the hole and so does not intersect the surface.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toroid#cite_note-1) For example, when a rectangle is rotated around an axis parallel to one of its edges, then a hollow rectangle-section ring is produced. If the revolved figure is a [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle"), then the object is called a [torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torus "Torus").

The term _toroid_ is also used to describe a [toroidal polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toroidal_polyhedron "Toroidal polyhedron"). In this context a toroid need not be circular and may have any number of holes. A _g_-holed _toroid_ can be seen as approximating the surface of a [torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torus "Torus") having a [topological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology_(Mathematics) "Topology (Mathematics)")[genus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genus_(mathematics) "Genus (mathematics)"), _g_, of 1 or greater. The [Euler characteristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_characteristic "Euler characteristic") χ of a _g_ holed toroid is 2(1−_g_).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toroid#cite_note-2)

The torus is an example of a toroid, which is the surface of a [doughnut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doughnut "Doughnut"). Doughnuts are an example of a [solid torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_torus "Solid torus") created by rotating a disk, and are not toroids.

Toroidal structures occur in both natural and synthetic materials.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toroid#cite_note-3)

Equations
---------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Toroid&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Equations")]

A toroid is specified by the radius of revolution _R_ measured from the center of the section rotated. For symmetrical sections volume and surface of the body may be computed (with circumference _C_ and area _A_ of the section):

### Square toroid

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Toroid&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Square toroid")]

The volume (V) and surface area (S) of a toroid are given by the following equations, where A is the area of the square section of side, and R is the radius of revolution.

V=2 π R A{\displaystyle V=2\pi RA}![Image 13: {\displaystyle V=2\pi RA}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c4de05a390d4841e8c2add9a14245f66b1305495)S=2 π R C{\displaystyle S=2\pi RC}![Image 14: {\displaystyle S=2\pi RC}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9abcf41b0fde5c9a22c093c39b3198ed408dde76)

### Circular toroid

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Toroid&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Circular toroid")]

The volume (V) and surface area (S) of a toroid are given by the following equations, where r is the radius of the circular section, and R is the radius of the overall shape.

V=2 π 2 r 2 R{\displaystyle V=2\pi ^{2}r^{2}R}![Image 15: {\displaystyle V=2\pi ^{2}r^{2}R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad200b46057ffcaffd614154e528ab265529d123)S=4 π 2 r R{\displaystyle S=4\pi ^{2}rR}![Image 16: {\displaystyle S=4\pi ^{2}rR}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5dc86216943f9e7c3ba56b2b88476ccafc5e5c3b)
[Pappus's centroid theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pappus%27s_centroid_theorem "Pappus's centroid theorem") generalizes the formulas here to arbitrary surfaces of revolution.
