In [logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic "Logic"), **negation**, also called the **logical not** or **logical complement**, is an [operation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_(mathematics) "Operation (mathematics)") that takes a [proposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proposition_(mathematics) "Proposition (mathematics)")![Image 8: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) to another proposition "not ![Image 9: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a)", written ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \neg P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5eb0d6c8752f8c7256d69c62e77dfe4c466dbe58), ![Image 11: {\displaystyle {\mathord {\sim }}P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9daca81525792364a725ec311d9d762a8a6956a9), ![Image 12: {\displaystyle P^{\prime }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/56ad46bf6f18ec059e93caf8cc3d2db843366b76)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negation#cite_note-1) or ![Image 13: {\displaystyle {\overline {P}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5e1bed5bc42d4e46dd9e5c7d2fc327927b87169).[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] It is interpreted intuitively as being true when ![Image 14: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) is false, and false when ![Image 15: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) is true.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negation#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negation#cite_note-3) For example, if ![Image 16: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) is "Spot runs", then "not ![Image 17: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a)" is "Spot does not run". An operand of a negation is called a _**negand**_ or _**negatum**_.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negation#cite_note-:212-4)

Negation is a [unary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unary_operation "Unary operation")[logical connective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_connective "Logical connective"). It may furthermore be applied not only to propositions, but also to [notions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Notion_(philosophy) "Notion (philosophy)"), [truth values](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth_value "Truth value"), or [semantic values](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interpretation_(logic) "Interpretation (logic)") more generally. In [classical logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_logic "Classical logic"), negation is normally identified with the [truth function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truth_function "Truth function") that takes _truth_ to _falsity_ (and vice versa). In [intuitionistic logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intuitionistic_logic "Intuitionistic logic"), according to the [Brouwer–Heyting–Kolmogorov interpretation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brouwer%E2%80%93Heyting%E2%80%93Kolmogorov_interpretation "Brouwer–Heyting–Kolmogorov interpretation"), the negation of a proposition ![Image 18: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) is the proposition whose proofs are the refutations of ![Image 19: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a).

_Classical negation_ is an [operation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_operation "Logical operation") on one [logical value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_value "Logical value"), typically the value of a [proposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proposition "Proposition"), that produces a value of _true_ when its operand is false, and a value of _false_ when its operand is true. Thus if statement ![Image 20: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) is true, then ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \neg P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5eb0d6c8752f8c7256d69c62e77dfe4c466dbe58) (pronounced "not P") would then be false; and conversely, if ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \neg P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5eb0d6c8752f8c7256d69c62e77dfe4c466dbe58) is true, then ![Image 23: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) would be false.

Negation can be defined in terms of other logical operations. For example, ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \neg P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5eb0d6c8752f8c7256d69c62e77dfe4c466dbe58) can be defined as ![Image 26: {\displaystyle P\rightarrow \bot }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21af5d568c2fd7384626959ef1f5985e2f59c298) (where ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \rightarrow }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53e574cc3aa5b4bf5f3f5906caf121a378eef08b) is [logical consequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_consequence "Logical consequence") and ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \bot }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f282c7bc331cc3bfcf1c57f1452cc23c022f58de) is [absolute falsehood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/False_(logic) "False (logic)")). Conversely, one can define ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \bot }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f282c7bc331cc3bfcf1c57f1452cc23c022f58de) as ![Image 30: {\displaystyle Q\land \neg Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cdaf497bd9328824019118ed8188eccc249803c6) for any proposition Q (where ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \land }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d6823e5a222eb3ca49672818ac3d13ec607052c4) is [logical conjunction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_conjunction "Logical conjunction")). The idea here is that any [contradiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contradiction "Contradiction") is false, and while these ideas work in both classical and intuitionistic logic, they do not work in [paraconsistent logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paraconsistent_logic "Paraconsistent logic"), where contradictions are not necessarily false. As a further example, negation can be defined in terms of NAND and can also be defined in terms of NOR.

Algebraically, classical negation corresponds to [complementation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complement_(order_theory) "Complement (order theory)") in a [Boolean algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boolean_algebra_(structure) "Boolean algebra (structure)"), and intuitionistic negation to pseudocomplementation in a [Heyting algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heyting_algebra "Heyting algebra"). These algebras provide a [semantics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_semantics_(mathematical_logic) "Algebraic semantics (mathematical logic)") for classical and intuitionistic logic.

Within a system of [classical logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_logic "Classical logic"), double negation, that is, the negation of the negation of a proposition ![Image 50: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a), is [logically equivalent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logically_equivalent "Logically equivalent") to ![Image 51: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a). Expressed in symbolic terms, ![Image 52: {\displaystyle \neg \neg P\equiv P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3bbf8c22800dfe80e6343d84718c229b41aff1d9). In [intuitionistic logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intuitionistic_logic "Intuitionistic logic"), a proposition implies its double negation, but not conversely. This marks one important difference between classical and intuitionistic negation. Algebraically, classical negation is called an [involution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Involution_(mathematics) "Involution (mathematics)") of period two.

However, in [intuitionistic logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intuitionistic_logic "Intuitionistic logic"), the weaker equivalence ![Image 53: {\displaystyle \neg \neg \neg P\equiv \neg P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/17089637b8b9487eac521d8650f18e10e1abd327) does hold. This is because in intuitionistic logic, ![Image 54: {\displaystyle \neg P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5eb0d6c8752f8c7256d69c62e77dfe4c466dbe58) is just a shorthand for ![Image 55: {\displaystyle P\rightarrow \bot }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21af5d568c2fd7384626959ef1f5985e2f59c298), and we also have ![Image 56: {\displaystyle P\rightarrow \neg \neg P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dc01898cc16ec321ac4b565e2fabbd988cf70f18). Composing that last implication with triple negation ![Image 57: {\displaystyle \neg \neg P\rightarrow \bot }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b4439d01da18810c6313f85f867c21ff42c64d2) implies that ![Image 58: {\displaystyle P\rightarrow \bot }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21af5d568c2fd7384626959ef1f5985e2f59c298) .

As a result, in the propositional case, a sentence is classically provable if its double negation is intuitionistically provable. This result is known as [Glivenko's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double-negation_translation "Double-negation translation").

[De Morgan's laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Morgan%27s_laws "De Morgan's laws") provide a way of [distributing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributive_property "Distributive property") negation over [disjunction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disjunction "Disjunction") and [conjunction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_conjunction "Logical conjunction"):

Let ![Image 61: {\displaystyle \oplus }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b16e2bdaefee9eed86d866e6eba3ac47c710f60) denote the logical [xor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xor "Xor") operation. In [Boolean algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boolean_algebra "Boolean algebra"), a linear function is one such that:

If there exists ![Image 62: {\displaystyle a_{0},a_{1},\dots ,a_{n}\in \{0,1\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/224cb631d2e9cbcf4d7f496df0123fa74be206c9), ![Image 63: {\displaystyle f(b_{1},b_{2},\dots ,b_{n})=a_{0}\oplus (a_{1}\land b_{1})\oplus \dots \oplus (a_{n}\land b_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da68343e4aaa71e605d556df9e0612fef2372f2d), for all ![Image 64: {\displaystyle b_{1},b_{2},\dots ,b_{n}\in \{0,1\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d87cb0f3f85beecfefaa6b41810a8b7c2d80e35f).

### Negations of quantifiers

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Negation&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Negations of quantifiers")]

In [first-order logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First-order_logic "First-order logic"), there are two quantifiers, one is the universal quantifier ![Image 67: {\displaystyle \forall }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bfc1a1a9c4c0f8d5df989c98aa2773ed657c5937) (means "for all") and the other is the existential quantifier ![Image 68: {\displaystyle \exists }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/77ed842b6b90b2fdd825320cf8e5265fa937b583) (means "there exists"). The negation of one quantifier is the other quantifier (![Image 69: {\displaystyle \neg \forall xP(x)\equiv \exists x\neg P(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d93041045df4feb6cce740a7773e31dd9576c1e0) and ![Image 70: {\displaystyle \neg \exists xP(x)\equiv \forall x\neg P(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fd301c4f840a19ec7bff3606ed9160b011bd19d8)). For example, with the predicate _P_ as "_x_ is mortal" and the domain of x as the collection of all humans, ![Image 71: {\displaystyle \forall xP(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/25873948fc98344950ea1b91f88dd52239cf9c87) means "a person x in all humans is mortal" or "all humans are mortal". The negation of it is ![Image 72: {\displaystyle \neg \forall xP(x)\equiv \exists x\neg P(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d93041045df4feb6cce740a7773e31dd9576c1e0), meaning "there exists a person _x_ in all humans who is not mortal", or "there exists someone who lives forever".

There are a number of equivalent ways to formulate rules for negation. One usual way to formulate classical negation in a [natural deduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_deduction "Natural deduction") setting is to take as primitive rules of inference _negation introduction_ (from a derivation of ![Image 73: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) to both ![Image 74: {\displaystyle Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8752c7023b4b3286800fe3238271bbca681219ed) and ![Image 75: {\displaystyle \neg Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fad34798abb0bbbc063c906e459f103a09b1660e), infer ![Image 76: {\displaystyle \neg P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5eb0d6c8752f8c7256d69c62e77dfe4c466dbe58); this rule also being called _[reductio ad absurdum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reductio\_ad\_absurdum "Reductio ad absurdum")_), _negation elimination_ (from ![Image 77: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) and ![Image 78: {\displaystyle \neg P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5eb0d6c8752f8c7256d69c62e77dfe4c466dbe58) infer ![Image 79: {\displaystyle Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8752c7023b4b3286800fe3238271bbca681219ed); this rule also being called _ex falso quodlibet_), and _double negation elimination_ (from ![Image 80: {\displaystyle \neg \neg P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53d7b8bafb9762c3a07924b6c06be6e08cb5680f) infer ![Image 81: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a)). One obtains the rules for intuitionistic negation the same way but by excluding double negation elimination.

Negation introduction states that if an absurdity can be drawn as conclusion from ![Image 82: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) then ![Image 83: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) must not be the case (i.e. ![Image 84: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) is false (classically) or refutable (intuitionistically) or etc.). Negation elimination states that anything follows from an absurdity. Sometimes negation elimination is formulated using a primitive absurdity sign ![Image 85: {\displaystyle \bot }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f282c7bc331cc3bfcf1c57f1452cc23c022f58de). In this case the rule says that from ![Image 86: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) and ![Image 87: {\displaystyle \neg P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5eb0d6c8752f8c7256d69c62e77dfe4c466dbe58) follows an absurdity. Together with double negation elimination one may infer our originally formulated rule, namely that anything follows from an absurdity.

Typically the intuitionistic negation ![Image 88: {\displaystyle \neg P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5eb0d6c8752f8c7256d69c62e77dfe4c466dbe58) of ![Image 89: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) is defined as ![Image 90: {\displaystyle P\rightarrow \bot }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21af5d568c2fd7384626959ef1f5985e2f59c298). Then negation introduction and elimination are just special cases of implication introduction ([conditional proof](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conditional_proof "Conditional proof")) and elimination (_[modus ponens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modus\_ponens "Modus ponens")_). In this case one must also add as a primitive rule _ex falso quodlibet_.

Programming language and ordinary language
------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Negation&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Programming language and ordinary language")]

As in mathematics, negation is used in [computer science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science "Computer science") to construct logical statements.

if (!(r == t))
{
 /*...statements executed when r does NOT equal t...*/
}

The [exclamation mark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exclamation_mark "Exclamation mark") "`!`" signifies logical NOT in [B](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B_(programming_language) "B (programming language)"), [C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C_Programming_Language "C Programming Language"), and languages with a C-inspired syntax such as [C++](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C%2B%2B "C++"), [Java](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Java_(programming_language) "Java (programming language)"), [JavaScript](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JavaScript "JavaScript"), [Perl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perl "Perl"), and [PHP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PHP "PHP"). "`NOT`" is the operator used in [ALGOL 60](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ALGOL_60 "ALGOL 60"), [BASIC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BASIC "BASIC"), and languages with an ALGOL- or BASIC-inspired syntax such as [Pascal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_programming_language "Pascal programming language"), [Ada](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ada_programming_language "Ada programming language"), [Eiffel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eiffel_(programming_language) "Eiffel (programming language)") and [Seed7](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seed7 "Seed7"). Some languages (C++, Perl, etc.) provide more than one operator for negation. A few languages like [PL/I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PL/I "PL/I") and [Ratfor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ratfor "Ratfor") use `¬` for negation. Most modern languages allow the above statement to be shortened from `if (!(r == t))` to `if (r != t)`, which allows sometimes, when the compiler/interpreter is not able to optimize it, faster programs.

In computer science there is also _[bitwise negation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitwise\_negation "Bitwise negation")_. This takes the value given and switches all the [binary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_numeral_system "Binary numeral system") 1s to 0s and 0s to 1s. This is often used to create [ones' complement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signed_number_representations "Signed number representations") (or "`~`" in C or C++) and [two's complement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two%27s_complement "Two's complement") (just simplified to "`-`" or the [negative sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negative_sign "Negative sign"), as this is equivalent to taking the [arithmetic negation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negation_(arithmetic) "Negation (arithmetic)") of the number).

To get the absolute (positive equivalent) value of a given integer the following would work as the "`-`" changes it from negative to positive (it is negative because "`x < 0`" yields true)

unsigned int abs(int x)
{
 if (x < 0)
 return -x;
 else
 return x;
}

To demonstrate logical negation:

unsigned int abs(int x)
{
 if (!(x < 0))
 return x;
 else
 return -x;
}

Inverting the condition and reversing the outcomes produces code that is logically equivalent to the original code, i.e. will have identical results for any input (depending on the compiler used, the actual instructions performed by the computer may differ).

In C (and some other languages descended from C), double negation (`!!x`) is used as an [idiom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Programming_idiom "Programming idiom") to convert `x` to a canonical Boolean, ie. an integer with a value of either 0 or 1 and no other. Although any integer other than 0 is logically true in C and 1 is not special in this regard, it is sometimes important to ensure that a canonical value is used, for example for printing or if the number is subsequently used for arithmetic operations.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negation#cite_note-7)

### Usage in ordinary language

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Negation&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Usage in ordinary language")]

The convention of using `!` to signify negation occasionally surfaces in [ordinary language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_language "Natural language"), as computer-related [slang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slang "Slang") for _not_. For example, the phrase `!clue` is used as a synonym for "no-clue" or "clueless".[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negation#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negation#cite_note-9)

Another example is the expression `!vote` which means "not a vote".[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negation#cite_note-slate-10) This "!vote" expression is used at [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia "Wikipedia") to negate "majority rule” in order “to have a consensus-building discussion, where the proper course is determined by the strength of the respective arguments."[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negation#cite_note-slate-10)

In [Kripke semantics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kripke_semantics "Kripke semantics") where the semantic values of formulae are sets of [possible worlds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Possible_world "Possible world"), negation can be taken to mean [set-theoretic complementation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set-theoretic_complement "Set-theoretic complement")[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] (see also [possible world semantics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Possible_world_semantics "Possible world semantics") for more).
