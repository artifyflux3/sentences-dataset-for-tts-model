**Entropy** is a [scientific](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science "Science") concept, most commonly associated with states of disorder, randomness, or uncertainty. The term and the concept are used in diverse fields, from [classical thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_thermodynamics "Classical thermodynamics"), where it was first recognized, to the microscopic description of nature in [statistical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_physics "Statistical physics"), and to the principles of [information theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_theory "Information theory"). It has found far-ranging applications in [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry") and [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), in biological systems and their relation to life, in [cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmology "Cosmology"), [economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economics "Economics"), [sociology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sociology "Sociology"), [weather science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_science "Atmospheric science"), [climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change "Climate change") and [information systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_system "Information system") including the transmission of information in [telecommunication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telecommunications "Telecommunications").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-1)

Entropy is central to the [second law of thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics "Second law of thermodynamics"), which states that the entropy of an isolated system left to spontaneous evolution cannot decrease with time. As a result, isolated systems evolve toward [thermodynamic equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium "Thermodynamic equilibrium"), where the entropy is highest. A consequence of the second law of thermodynamics is that certain processes are [irreversible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irreversible_process "Irreversible process").

The thermodynamic concept was referred to by Scottish scientist and engineer [William Rankine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Rankine "William Rankine") in 1850 with the names _thermodynamic function_ and _heat-potential_.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-2) In 1865, German physicist [Rudolf Clausius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolf_Clausius "Rudolf Clausius"), one of the leading founders of the field of thermodynamics, defined it as the quotient of an infinitesimal amount of [heat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat "Heat") to the instantaneous [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature"). He initially described it as _transformation-content_, in German _Verwandlungsinhalt_, and later coined the term _entropy_ from a Greek word for _transformation_.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-brush1976-3)

Austrian physicist [Ludwig Boltzmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Boltzmann "Ludwig Boltzmann") explained entropy as the measure of the number of possible microscopic arrangements or states of individual atoms and molecules of a system that comply with the macroscopic condition of the system. He thereby introduced the concept of statistical disorder and [probability distributions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_distribution "Probability distribution") into a new field of thermodynamics, called [statistical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_mechanics "Statistical mechanics"), and found the link between the microscopic interactions, which fluctuate about an average configuration, to the macroscopically observable behaviour, in form of a simple [logarithmic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logarithm "Logarithm") law, with a [proportionality constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportionality_(mathematics) "Proportionality (mathematics)"), the [Boltzmann constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_constant "Boltzmann constant"), which has become one of the defining universal constants for the modern [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-4)

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/40/Clausius.jpg/250px-Clausius.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Clausius.jpg)

[Rudolf Clausius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolf_Clausius "Rudolf Clausius") (1822–1888), originator of the concept of entropy

In his 1803 paper _Fundamental Principles of Equilibrium and Movement_, the French mathematician [Lazare Carnot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lazare_Carnot "Lazare Carnot") proposed that in any machine, the accelerations and shocks of the moving parts represent losses of _moment of activity_; in any natural process there exists an inherent tendency towards the dissipation of useful energy. In 1824, building on that work, Lazare's son, [Sadi Carnot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolas_L%C3%A9onard_Sadi_Carnot "Nicolas Léonard Sadi Carnot"), published _[Reflections on the Motive Power of Fire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflections\_on\_the\_Motive\_Power\_of\_Fire "Reflections on the Motive Power of Fire")_, which posited that in all heat-engines, whenever "[caloric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caloric_theory "Caloric theory")" (what is now known as heat) falls through a temperature difference, work or [motive power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work_(physics) "Work (physics)") can be produced from the actions of its fall from a hot to cold body. He used an analogy with how water falls in a [water wheel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_wheel "Water wheel"). That was an early insight into the [second law of thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics "Second law of thermodynamics").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-5) Carnot based his views of heat partially on the early 18th-century "Newtonian hypothesis" that both heat and light were types of indestructible forms of matter, which are attracted and repelled by other matter, and partially on the contemporary views of [Count Rumford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Count_Rumford "Count Rumford"), who showed in 1789 that heat could be created by friction, as when cannon bores are machined.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-6) Carnot reasoned that if the body of the working substance, such as a body of steam, is returned to its original state at the end of a complete [engine cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engine_cycle "Engine cycle"), "no change occurs in the condition of the working body".

The [first law of thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_law_of_thermodynamics "First law of thermodynamics"), deduced from the heat-friction experiments of [James Joule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Joule "James Joule") in 1843, expresses the concept of energy and its [conservation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_energy "Conservation of energy") in all processes; the first law, however, is unsuitable to separately quantify the effects of [friction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friction "Friction") and [dissipation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissipation "Dissipation").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

In the 1850s and 1860s, German physicist [Rudolf Clausius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolf_Clausius "Rudolf Clausius") objected to the supposition that no change occurs in the working body, and gave that change a mathematical interpretation, by questioning the nature of the inherent loss of usable heat when work is done, e.g., heat produced by friction.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Clausius-7) He described his observations as a dissipative use of energy, resulting in a _transformation-content_ (_Verwandlungsinhalt_ in German), of a [thermodynamic system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_system "Thermodynamic system") or [working body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Working_body "Working body") of [chemical species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_species "Chemical species") during a change of [state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_state "Thermodynamic state").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Clausius-7) That was in contrast to earlier views, based on the theories of [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton"), that heat was an indestructible particle that had mass. Clausius discovered that the non-usable energy increases as steam proceeds from inlet to exhaust in a steam engine. From the prefix _en-_, as in 'energy', and from the Greek word τροπή [tropē], which is translated in an established lexicon as _turning_ or _change_[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-8) and that he rendered in German as _Verwandlung_, a word often translated into English as _transformation_, in 1865 Clausius coined the name of that property as _entropy_.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Clausius_German-9) The word was adopted into the English language in 1868.

Later, scientists such as [Ludwig Boltzmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Boltzmann "Ludwig Boltzmann"), [Josiah Willard Gibbs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Josiah_Willard_Gibbs "Josiah Willard Gibbs"), and [James Clerk Maxwell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell "James Clerk Maxwell") gave entropy a statistical basis. In 1877, Boltzmann visualized a probabilistic way to measure the entropy of an ensemble of [ideal gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas "Ideal gas") particles, in which he defined entropy as proportional to the [natural logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_logarithm "Natural logarithm") of the number of microstates such a gas could occupy. The [proportionality constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportionality_(mathematics) "Proportionality (mathematics)") in this definition, called the [Boltzmann constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_constant "Boltzmann constant"), has become one of the defining universal constants for the modern [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") (SI). Henceforth, the essential problem in [statistical thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_thermodynamics "Statistical thermodynamics") has been to determine the distribution of a given amount of energy _E_ over _N_ identical systems. [Constantin Carathéodory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantin_Carath%C3%A9odory "Constantin Carathéodory"), a Greek mathematician, linked entropy with a mathematical definition of irreversibility, in terms of trajectories and integrability.

In 1865, Clausius named the concept of "the differential of a quantity which depends on the configuration of the system", _[entropy](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/entropy "wikt:entropy")_ (_Entropie_) after the Greek word for 'transformation'.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Gil399-10) He gave "transformational content" (_Verwandlungsinhalt_) as a synonym, paralleling his "thermal and ergonal content" (_Wärme- und Werkinhalt_) as the name of _U_, but preferring the term _entropy_ as a close parallel of the word _energy_, as he found the concepts nearly "analogous in their physical significance".[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Gil399-10) This term was formed by replacing the root of ἔργον ('ergon', 'work') by that of [τροπή](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%84%CF%81%CE%BF%CF%80%CE%AE "wikt:τροπή") ('tropy', 'transformation').[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Clausius_German-9)

In more detail, Clausius explained his choice of "entropy" as a name as follows:[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Cooper-11)

> I prefer going to the ancient languages for the names of important scientific quantities, so that they may mean the same thing in all living tongues. I propose, therefore, to call _S_ the _entropy_ of a body, after the Greek word "transformation". I have designedly coined the word _entropy_ to be similar to energy, for these two quantities are so analogous in their physical significance, that an analogy of denominations seems to me helpful.

[Leon Cooper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leon_Cooper "Leon Cooper") added that in this way "he succeeded in coining a word that meant the same thing to everybody: nothing".[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Cooper-11)

Definitions and descriptions
----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Entropy&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Definitions and descriptions")]

> Any method involving the notion of entropy, the very existence of which depends on the second law of thermodynamics, will doubtless seem to many far-fetched, and may repel beginners as obscure and difficult of comprehension.

[Willard Gibbs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Willard_Gibbs "Willard Gibbs"), _Graphical Methods in the Thermodynamics of Fluids_[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-12)

The concept of entropy is described by two principal approaches, the macroscopic perspective of [classical thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_thermodynamics "Classical thermodynamics"), and the microscopic description central to [statistical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_mechanics "Statistical mechanics"). The classical approach defines entropy in terms of macroscopically measurable physical properties, such as bulk mass, volume, pressure, and temperature. The statistical definition of entropy defines it in terms of the statistics of the motions of the microscopic constituents of a system — modelled at first classically, e.g. Newtonian particles constituting a gas, and later quantum-mechanically (photons, [phonons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phonons "Phonons"), spins, etc.). The two approaches form a consistent, unified view of the same phenomenon as expressed in the second law of thermodynamics, which has found universal applicability to physical processes.

### State variables and functions of state

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Entropy&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: State variables and functions of state")]

Many [thermodynamic properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_properties "Thermodynamic properties") are defined by physical variables that define a state of [thermodynamic equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium "Thermodynamic equilibrium"), which essentially are [state variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_variable "State variable"). State variables depend only on the equilibrium condition, not on the path evolution to that state. State variables can be functions of state, also called [state functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_function "State function"), in a sense that one state variable is a [mathematical function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") of other state variables. Often, if some properties of a system are determined, they are sufficient to determine the state of the system and thus other properties' values. For example, temperature and pressure of a given quantity of gas determine its state, and thus also its volume via the [ideal gas law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas_law "Ideal gas law"). A system composed of a pure substance of a single [phase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_(matter) "Phase (matter)") at a particular uniform temperature and pressure is determined, and is thus a particular state, and has a particular volume. The fact that entropy is a function of state makes it useful. In the [Carnot cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carnot_cycle "Carnot cycle"), the working fluid returns to the same state that it had at the start of the cycle, hence the change or [line integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_integral "Line integral") of any state function, such as entropy, over this reversible cycle is zero.

The entropy change _![Image 2: {\textstyle \mathrm {d} S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/718e231010cb37a900d5601df0b18bf7e67f6d18)_ of a system can be well-defined as a small portion of [heat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat "Heat")_![Image 3: {\textstyle \delta Q\_{\mathsf {rev}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/19bdf0f74f57d968ccbce6a08833e4ce80246056)_ transferred from the surroundings to the system during a reversible process divided by the [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature")_![Image 4: {\textstyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/db299e88e5485f250f4ba15530469c8c6080a8cb)_ of the system during this [heat transfer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_transfer "Heat transfer"):![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} S={\frac {\delta Q_{\mathsf {rev}}}{T}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10a4a9acafcbf124bb52ab133950b737d1c0e00f)The reversible process is [quasistatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasistatic_process "Quasistatic process") (i.e., it occurs without any dissipation, deviating only infinitesimally from the thermodynamic equilibrium), and it may conserve total entropy. For example, in the [Carnot cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carnot_cycle "Carnot cycle"), while the heat flow from a hot reservoir to a cold reservoir represents the increase in the entropy in a cold reservoir, the work output, if reversibly and perfectly stored, represents the decrease in the entropy which could be used to operate the heat engine in reverse, returning to the initial state; thus the total entropy change may still be zero at all times if the entire process is reversible.

In contrast, an irreversible process increases the total entropy of the system and surroundings.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-13) Any process that happens quickly enough to deviate from the thermal equilibrium cannot be reversible; the total entropy increases, and the potential for maximum work to be done during the process is lost.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-14)

The concept of entropy arose from [Rudolf Clausius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolf_Clausius "Rudolf Clausius")'s study of the [Carnot cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carnot_cycle "Carnot cycle") which is a [thermodynamic cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_cycle "Thermodynamic cycle") performed by a Carnot heat engine as a reversible heat engine.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-15) In a Carnot cycle, the heat ![Image 6: {\textstyle Q_{\mathsf {H}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e567d3f63b876a033a195828783c65afc5cfc11) is transferred from a hot reservoir to a working gas at the constant temperature ![Image 7: {\textstyle T_{\mathsf {H}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e87b23dce59ae71fdd59a470ac0d1d01484defe) during [isothermal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isothermal_process "Isothermal process") expansion stage and the heat ![Image 8: {\textstyle Q_{\mathsf {C}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de01c48357d3b7b3ae90a2115e2303ffb40370c1) is transferred from a working gas to a cold reservoir at the constant temperature ![Image 9: {\textstyle T_{\mathsf {C}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bdf6786e4b802f481f9ebd3645cae0690e97001) during [isothermal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isothermal_process "Isothermal process") compression stage. According to [Carnot's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carnot%27s_theorem_(thermodynamics) "Carnot's theorem (thermodynamics)"), a heat engine with two thermal reservoirs can produce a [work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work_(physics) "Work (physics)")![Image 10: {\textstyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e95737ee2530885a10b104e9cd5331077e1c88d5) if and only if there is a temperature difference between reservoirs. Originally, Carnot did not distinguish between heats ![Image 11: {\textstyle Q_{\mathsf {H}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e567d3f63b876a033a195828783c65afc5cfc11) and ![Image 12: {\textstyle Q_{\mathsf {C}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de01c48357d3b7b3ae90a2115e2303ffb40370c1), as he assumed [caloric theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caloric_theory "Caloric theory") to be valid and hence that the total heat in the system was conserved. But in fact, the magnitude of heat ![Image 13: {\textstyle Q_{\mathsf {H}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e567d3f63b876a033a195828783c65afc5cfc11) is greater than the magnitude of heat ![Image 14: {\textstyle Q_{\mathsf {C}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de01c48357d3b7b3ae90a2115e2303ffb40370c1).[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-16)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-17) Through the efforts of [Clausius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolf_Clausius "Rudolf Clausius") and [Kelvin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lord_Kelvin "Lord Kelvin"), the work ![Image 15: {\textstyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e95737ee2530885a10b104e9cd5331077e1c88d5) done by a reversible heat engine was found to be the product of the Carnot efficiency (i.e., the efficiency of all reversible heat engines with the same pair of thermal reservoirs) and the heat ![Image 16: {\textstyle Q_{\mathsf {H}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e567d3f63b876a033a195828783c65afc5cfc11) absorbed by a working body of the engine during isothermal expansion:![Image 17: {\displaystyle W={\frac {T_{\mathsf {H}}-T_{\mathsf {C}}}{T_{\mathsf {H}}}}\cdot Q_{\mathsf {H}}=\left(1-{\frac {T_{\mathsf {C}}}{T_{\mathsf {H}}}}\right)Q_{\mathsf {H}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b816bc4732dd5f0b3ce48910f773cadbd1cffe5)To derive the Carnot efficiency Kelvin had to evaluate the ratio of the work output to the heat absorbed during the isothermal expansion with the help of the Carnot–Clapeyron equation, which contained an unknown function called the Carnot function. The possibility that the Carnot function could be the temperature as measured from a zero point of temperature was suggested by [Joule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Prescott_Joule "James Prescott Joule") in a letter to Kelvin. This allowed Kelvin to establish his absolute temperature scale.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-18)

It is known that a work ![Image 18: {\textstyle W>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5503151615be2cf8d4e3cec47cbe952d99b87879) produced by an engine over a cycle equals to a net heat ![Image 19: {\textstyle Q_{\Sigma }=\left\vert Q_{\mathsf {H}}\right\vert -\left\vert Q_{\mathsf {C}}\right\vert }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d94d2ed5349e1ea1db801fff438de38d0f6480d) absorbed over a cycle.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-PlanckBook-19) Thus, with the sign convention for a heat ![Image 20: {\textstyle Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/131bb2da649dd6c113517c5ae8c26370654ee8fa) transferred in a thermodynamic process (![Image 21: {\textstyle Q>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c60d3884fe5bb310f5578c3b0825b9128f902d64) for an absorption and ![Image 22: {\textstyle Q<0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c24c4c775e38d92ae80baf26d41eb4981540c5b) for a dissipation) we get:![Image 23: {\displaystyle W-Q_{\Sigma }=W-\left\vert Q_{\mathsf {H}}\right\vert +\left\vert Q_{\mathsf {C}}\right\vert =W-Q_{\mathsf {H}}-Q_{\mathsf {C}}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fca9db41d4ab9f02d38516e36eeeb5acfb268baf)Since this equality holds over an entire Carnot cycle, it gave Clausius the hint that at each stage of the cycle the difference between a work and a net heat would be conserved, rather than a net heat itself. Which means there exists a [state function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_function "State function")![Image 24: {\textstyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/087a7d8a39fe35012cbf2f561879f9e975cb4555) with a change of ![Image 25: {\textstyle \mathrm {d} U=\delta Q-\mathrm {d} W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d402e3a80cc11bde30d4117d4157558d5b05739). It is called an [internal energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_energy "Internal energy") and forms a central concept for the [first law of thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_law_of_thermodynamics "First law of thermodynamics").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Clausius1867-20)

Finally, comparison for both the representations of a work output in a Carnot cycle gives us:[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-PlanckBook-19)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-FermiBook-21)![Image 26: {\displaystyle {\frac {\left\vert Q_{\mathsf {H}}\right\vert }{T_{\mathsf {H}}}}-{\frac {\left\vert Q_{\mathsf {C}}\right\vert }{T_{\mathsf {C}}}}={\frac {Q_{\mathsf {H}}}{T_{\mathsf {H}}}}+{\frac {Q_{\mathsf {C}}}{T_{\mathsf {C}}}}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e3ed7ed96cbd4962d76f48842214f8ee52e7d08)Similarly to the derivation of internal energy, this equality implies existence of a [state function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_function "State function")![Image 27: {\textstyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e10a3c52d186162ec8910ebc0288ce982aef842f) with a change of ![Image 28: {\textstyle \mathrm {d} S=\delta Q/T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe54e0dd95e4c4a985b24a19dfaf69b56934e70d) and which is conserved over an entire cycle. Clausius called this state function _entropy_.

In addition, the total change of entropy in both thermal reservoirs over Carnot cycle is zero too, since the inversion of a heat transfer direction means a sign inversion for the heat transferred during isothermal stages:![Image 29: {\displaystyle -{\frac {Q_{\mathsf {H}}}{T_{\mathsf {H}}}}-{\frac {Q_{\mathsf {C}}}{T_{\mathsf {C}}}}=\Delta S_{\mathsf {r,H}}+\Delta S_{\mathsf {r,C}}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba2be8ff092eee51159dd42e679dfc79a1c2c234)Here we denote the entropy change for a thermal reservoir by ![Image 30: {\textstyle \Delta S_{{\mathsf {r}},i}=-Q_{i}/T_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f64caa819ac819183d6e52d24ef5d285f0d5c4b1), where ![Image 31: {\textstyle i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5b0f327332497b21a059c479e7b2ce098baa1a7e) is either ![Image 32: {\textstyle {\mathsf {H}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/845b6a74908337de339b19adf5396b38614bb03e) for a hot reservoir or ![Image 33: {\textstyle {\mathsf {C}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53a79bb7664553ac657092e5455d1b5fa3581afb) for a cold one.

If we consider a heat engine which is less effective than Carnot cycle (i.e., the work ![Image 34: {\textstyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e95737ee2530885a10b104e9cd5331077e1c88d5) produced by this engine is less than the maximum predicted by Carnot's theorem), its work output is capped by Carnot efficiency as:![Image 35: {\displaystyle W<\left(1-{\frac {T_{\mathsf {C}}}{T_{\mathsf {H}}}}\right)Q_{\mathsf {H}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f32c6737af50a1f821050ebe57a2c3887fcb1477)Substitution of the work ![Image 36: {\textstyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e95737ee2530885a10b104e9cd5331077e1c88d5) as the net heat into the inequality above gives us:![Image 37: {\displaystyle {\frac {Q_{\mathsf {H}}}{T_{\mathsf {H}}}}+{\frac {Q_{\mathsf {C}}}{T_{\mathsf {C}}}}<0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e93612c8013305a19ec685601ab88e79f593084d)or in terms of the entropy change ![Image 38: {\textstyle \Delta S_{{\mathsf {r}},i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bec235568184be259c01c1a756769b15a6a0ca33):![Image 39: {\displaystyle \Delta S_{\mathsf {r,H}}+\Delta S_{\mathsf {r,C}}>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/509b8770e43372a8376bb091b9ff556f1be6a3f1)A [Carnot cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carnot_cycle "Carnot cycle") and an entropy as shown above prove to be useful in the study of any classical thermodynamic heat engine: other cycles, such as an [Otto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otto_cycle "Otto cycle"), [Diesel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diesel_cycle "Diesel cycle") or [Brayton cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brayton_cycle "Brayton cycle"), could be analysed from the same standpoint. Notably, any machine or cyclic process converting heat into work (i.e., heat engine) that is claimed to produce an efficiency greater than the one of Carnot is not viable — due to violation of [the second law of thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics "Second law of thermodynamics").

For further analysis of sufficiently discrete systems, such as an assembly of particles, [statistical thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_mechanics "Statistical mechanics") must be used. Additionally, descriptions of devices operating near the limit of [de Broglie waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter_wave "Matter wave"), e.g. [photovoltaic cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_cell "Solar cell"), have to be consistent with [quantum statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_statistical_mechanics "Quantum statistical mechanics").

### Classical thermodynamics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Entropy&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Classical thermodynamics")]

The thermodynamic definition of entropy was developed in the early 1850s by [Rudolf Clausius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolf_Clausius "Rudolf Clausius") and essentially describes how to measure the entropy of an [isolated system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isolated_system "Isolated system") in [thermodynamic equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium "Thermodynamic equilibrium") with its parts. Clausius created the term entropy as an [extensive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intensive_and_extensive_properties "Intensive and extensive properties") thermodynamic variable that was shown to be useful in characterizing the [Carnot cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carnot_cycle "Carnot cycle"). Heat transfer in the isotherm steps (isothermal expansion and isothermal compression) of the Carnot cycle was found to be proportional to the temperature of a system (known as its [absolute temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_temperature "Absolute temperature")). This relationship was expressed in an increment of entropy that is equal to incremental heat transfer divided by temperature. Entropy was found to vary in the thermodynamic cycle but eventually returned to the same value at the end of every cycle. Thus it was found to be a [function of state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_of_state "Function of state"), specifically a thermodynamic state of the system.

While Clausius based his definition on a reversible process, there are also irreversible processes that change entropy. Following the [second law of thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics "Second law of thermodynamics"), entropy of an isolated [system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_system "Thermodynamic system") always increases for irreversible processes. The difference between an isolated system and closed system is that energy may _not_ flow to and from an isolated system, but energy flow to and from a closed system is possible. Nevertheless, for both closed and isolated systems, and indeed, also in open systems, irreversible thermodynamics processes may occur.

According to the [Clausius equality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clausius_theorem "Clausius theorem"), for a reversible cyclic thermodynamic process: ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \oint {\frac {\delta Q_{\mathsf {rev}}}{T}}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a42e924fb8a85fbe2211659c64f8c793fd2c259f)which means the line integral ![Image 41: {\textstyle \int _{L}{\delta Q_{\mathsf {rev}}/T}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b15ce922427258390bdf7bd004e7c39709bb2f64) is [path-independent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_function "State function"). Thus we can define a state function ![Image 42: {\textstyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e10a3c52d186162ec8910ebc0288ce982aef842f), called _entropy_:![Image 43: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} S={\frac {\delta Q_{\mathsf {rev}}}{T}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10a4a9acafcbf124bb52ab133950b737d1c0e00f)Therefore, thermodynamic entropy has the dimension of energy divided by temperature, and the unit [joule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joule "Joule") per [kelvin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin") (J/K) in the International System of Units (SI).

To find the entropy difference between any two states of the system, the integral must be evaluated for some reversible path between the initial and final states.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-22) Since an entropy is a state function, the entropy change of the system for an irreversible path is the same as for a reversible path between the same two states.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-23) However, the heat transferred to or from the surroundings is different as well as its entropy change.

We can calculate the change of entropy only by integrating the above formula. To obtain the absolute value of the entropy, we consider the [third law of thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third_law_of_thermodynamics "Third law of thermodynamics"): perfect crystals at the [absolute zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_zero "Absolute zero") have an entropy ![Image 44: {\textstyle S=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9df4f28e3ae85198fc00d555ba703995ec12077f).

From a macroscopic perspective, in [classical thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_thermodynamics "Classical thermodynamics") the entropy is interpreted as a [state function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_function "State function") of a [thermodynamic system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_system "Thermodynamic system"): that is, a property depending only on the current state of the system, independent of how that state came to be achieved. In any process, where the system gives up ![Image 45: {\displaystyle \Delta E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/345f0ed5ff6ec6eddc5f908d379f032c52f119c2) of energy to the surrounding at the temperature ![Image 46: {\textstyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db299e88e5485f250f4ba15530469c8c6080a8cb), its entropy falls by ![Image 47: {\textstyle \Delta S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8077bc9da71b897e3eaaa7678e439fa7a3ae5b14) and at least ![Image 48: {\textstyle T\cdot \Delta S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/80d61dd347d4765e56b77aee6a16949081752a3d) of that energy must be given up to the system's surroundings as a heat. Otherwise, this process cannot go forward. In classical thermodynamics, the entropy of a system is defined if and only if it is in a [thermodynamic equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium "Thermodynamic equilibrium") (though a [chemical equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_equilibrium "Chemical equilibrium") is not required: for example, the entropy of a mixture of two moles of hydrogen and one mole of oxygen in [standard conditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure "Standard temperature and pressure") is well-defined).

### Statistical mechanics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Entropy&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Statistical mechanics")]

The statistical definition was developed by [Ludwig Boltzmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Boltzmann "Ludwig Boltzmann") in the 1870s by analysing the statistical behaviour of the microscopic components of the system. Boltzmann showed that this definition of entropy was equivalent to the thermodynamic entropy to within a constant factor—known as the [Boltzmann constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_constant "Boltzmann constant"). In short, the thermodynamic definition of entropy provides the experimental verification of entropy, while the statistical definition of entropy extends the concept, providing an explanation and a deeper understanding of its nature.

The [interpretation of entropy in statistical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy_(statistical_thermodynamics) "Entropy (statistical thermodynamics)") is the measure of uncertainty, disorder, or _mixedupness_ in the phrase of [Gibbs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Josiah_Willard_Gibbs "Josiah Willard Gibbs"), which remains about a system after its observable macroscopic properties, such as temperature, pressure and volume, have been taken into account. For a given set of macroscopic variables, the entropy measures the degree to which the probability of the system is spread out over different possible [microstates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microstate_(statistical_mechanics) "Microstate (statistical mechanics)"). In contrast to the macrostate, which characterizes plainly observable average quantities, a microstate specifies all molecular details about the system including the position and momentum of every molecule. The more such states are available to the system with appreciable probability, the greater the entropy. In statistical mechanics, entropy is a measure of the number of ways a system can be arranged, often taken to be a measure of "disorder" (the higher the entropy, the higher the disorder).[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-McH-24)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Sethna78-25)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-26) This definition describes the entropy as being proportional to the natural logarithm of the number of possible microscopic configurations of the individual atoms and molecules of the system ([microstates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microstate_(statistical_mechanics) "Microstate (statistical mechanics)")) that could cause the observed macroscopic state ([macrostate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macrostate "Macrostate")) of the system. The constant of proportionality is the [Boltzmann constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_constant "Boltzmann constant").

The Boltzmann constant, and therefore entropy, have [dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(physics) "Dimension (physics)") of energy divided by temperature, which has a unit of [joules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joule "Joule") per [kelvin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin") (J⋅K−1) in the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") (or kg⋅m 2⋅s−2⋅K−1 in terms of base units). The entropy of a substance is usually given as an [intensive property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intensive_and_extensive_properties#Intensive_properties "Intensive and extensive properties") — either entropy per unit [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") (SI unit: J⋅K−1⋅kg−1) or entropy per unit [amount of substance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amount_of_substance "Amount of substance") (SI unit: J⋅K−1⋅mol−1).

Specifically, entropy is a [logarithmic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logarithmic_scale "Logarithmic scale") measure for the system with a number of states, each with a probability ![Image 49: {\textstyle p_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/187041073ab405322dd8186181730a59ed788a10) of being occupied (usually given by the [Boltzmann distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_distribution "Boltzmann distribution")):![Image 50: {\displaystyle S=-k_{\mathsf {B}}\sum _{i}{p_{i}\ln {p_{i}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8173560c45cf61ff40ae8bc50de9c9c15de7dacc)where ![Image 51: {\textstyle k_{\mathsf {B}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/acb221305e6831ab0d1545bba020675c08eae6bd) is the [Boltzmann constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_constant "Boltzmann constant") and the summation is performed over all possible microstates of the system.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Perplexed-27)

In case states are defined in a continuous manner, the summation is replaced by an [integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral") over all possible states, or equivalently we can consider the [expected value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expected_value "Expected value") of [the logarithm of the probability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy_(information_theory)#Rationale "Entropy (information theory)") that a microstate is occupied:![Image 52: {\displaystyle S=-k_{\mathsf {B}}\left\langle \ln {p}\right\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54d867f72747851c565819dfc39cc8cce8a38cd7)This definition assumes the basis states to be picked in a way that there is no information on their relative phases. In a general case the expression is:![Image 53: {\displaystyle S=-k_{\mathsf {B}}\ \mathrm {tr} {\left({\hat {\rho }}\times \ln {\hat {\rho }}\right)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a63066d5913dad2dbde1b8e50704ed80101aaa42)where ![Image 54: {\textstyle {\hat {\rho }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/788ee23b4cf1b2708223fc79a153a0329f58cdf6) is a [density matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_matrix "Density matrix"), ![Image 55: {\displaystyle \mathrm {tr} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/812681cabb4e0ad561bc0e91a24e09d9ae15d6f6) is a [trace operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trace_class "Trace class") and ![Image 56: {\displaystyle \ln }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c0de5ba4f372ede555d00035e70c50ed0b9625d0) is a [matrix logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_logarithm "Matrix logarithm"). The density matrix formalism is not required if the system is in thermal equilibrium so long as the basis states are chosen to be [eigenstates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state "Quantum state") of the [Hamiltonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_(quantum_mechanics) "Hamiltonian (quantum mechanics)"). For most practical purposes it can be taken as the fundamental definition of entropy since all other formulae for ![Image 57: {\textstyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e10a3c52d186162ec8910ebc0288ce982aef842f) can be derived from it, but not vice versa.

In what has been called _[the fundamental postulate in statistical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental\_postulate\_of\_statistical\_mechanics "Fundamental postulate of statistical mechanics")_, among system microstates of the same energy (i.e., [degenerate microstates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degenerate_energy_levels "Degenerate energy levels")) each microstate is assumed to be populated with equal probability ![Image 58: {\textstyle p_{i}=1/\Omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ea785e189016649a84275d5f62adec319f184c0), where ![Image 59: {\textstyle \Omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f0f531159ee31e33b4f75988d5abba38abadb3e2) is the number of microstates whose energy equals that of the system. Usually, this assumption is justified for an isolated system in a thermodynamic equilibrium.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-28) Then in case of an isolated system the previous formula reduces to:![Image 60: {\displaystyle S=k_{\mathsf {B}}\ln {\Omega }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5b33fbc6830f6e384a2098f47bc0f7f132b0724)In thermodynamics, such a system is one with a fixed volume, number of molecules, and internal energy, called a [microcanonical ensemble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microcanonical_ensemble "Microcanonical ensemble").

The most general interpretation of entropy is as a measure of the extent of uncertainty about a system. The [equilibrium state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilibrium_state "Equilibrium state") of a system maximizes the entropy because it does not reflect all information about the initial conditions, except for the conserved variables. This uncertainty is not of the everyday subjective kind, but rather the uncertainty inherent to the experimental method and interpretative model.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-29)

The interpretative model has a central role in determining entropy. The qualifier "for a given set of macroscopic variables" above has deep implications when two observers use different sets of macroscopic variables. For example, consider observer A using variables ![Image 61: {\textstyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/087a7d8a39fe35012cbf2f561879f9e975cb4555), ![Image 62: {\textstyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d67b50b7ba03a56fea637093cf80e12807852d19), ![Image 63: {\textstyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e95737ee2530885a10b104e9cd5331077e1c88d5) and observer B using variables ![Image 64: {\textstyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/087a7d8a39fe35012cbf2f561879f9e975cb4555), ![Image 65: {\textstyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d67b50b7ba03a56fea637093cf80e12807852d19), ![Image 66: {\textstyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e95737ee2530885a10b104e9cd5331077e1c88d5), ![Image 67: {\textstyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8d80c41192705e1a6c6de1d65e16d7f70fbac391). If observer B changes variable ![Image 68: {\textstyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8d80c41192705e1a6c6de1d65e16d7f70fbac391), then observer A will see a violation of the second law of thermodynamics, since he does not possess information about variable ![Image 69: {\textstyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8d80c41192705e1a6c6de1d65e16d7f70fbac391) and its influence on the system. In other words, one must choose a complete set of macroscopic variables to describe the system, i.e. every independent parameter that may change during experiment.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-30)

Entropy can also be defined for any [Markov processes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Markov_process "Markov process") with [reversible dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reversible_dynamics "Reversible dynamics") and the [detailed balance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detailed_balance "Detailed balance") property.

In Boltzmann's 1896 _Lectures on Gas Theory_, he showed that this expression gives a measure of entropy for systems of atoms and molecules in the gas phase, thus providing a measure for the entropy of classical thermodynamics.

### Entropy of a system

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Entropy&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Entropy of a system")]

[![Image 70](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/System_boundary.svg/250px-System_boundary.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:System_boundary.svg)

A [thermodynamic system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_system "Thermodynamic system")

[![Image 71](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Temperature-entropy_chart_for_steam%2C_imperial_units.svg/250px-Temperature-entropy_chart_for_steam%2C_imperial_units.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Temperature-entropy_chart_for_steam,_imperial_units.svg)

A [temperature–entropy diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature%E2%80%93entropy_diagram "Temperature–entropy diagram") for steam. The vertical axis represents uniform temperature, and the horizontal axis represents specific entropy. Each dark line on the graph represents constant pressure, and these form a mesh with light grey lines of constant volume. (Dark-blue is liquid water, light-blue is liquid-steam mixture, and faint-blue is steam. Grey-blue represents supercritical liquid water.)

In a [thermodynamic system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_system "Thermodynamic system"), pressure and temperature tend to become uniform over time because the [equilibrium state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilibrium_state "Equilibrium state") has higher [probability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability "Probability") (more possible [combinations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combination "Combination") of [microstates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microstate_(statistical_mechanics) "Microstate (statistical mechanics)")) than any other state. As an example, for a glass of ice water in air at [room temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Room_temperature "Room temperature"), the difference in temperature between the warm room (the surroundings) and the cold glass of ice and water (the system and not part of the room) decreases as portions of the [thermal energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_energy "Thermal energy") from the warm surroundings spread to the cooler system of ice and water. Over time the temperature of the glass and its contents and the temperature of the room become equal. In other words, the entropy of the room has decreased as some of its energy has been dispersed to the ice and water, of which the entropy has increased.

However, as calculated in the example, the entropy of the system of ice and water has increased more than the entropy of the surrounding room has decreased. In an [isolated system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isolated_system "Isolated system") such as the room and ice water taken together, the dispersal of energy from warmer to cooler always results in a net increase in entropy. Thus, when the "universe" of the room and ice water system has reached a temperature equilibrium, the entropy change from the initial state is at a maximum. The entropy of the [thermodynamic system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_system "Thermodynamic system") is a measure of how far the equalisation has progressed.

Thermodynamic entropy is a non-conserved [state function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_function "State function") that is of great importance in the sciences of [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") and [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-McH-24)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Wiley91-31) Historically, the concept of entropy evolved to explain why some processes (permitted by conservation laws) occur spontaneously while their [time reversals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T-symmetry "T-symmetry") (also permitted by conservation laws) do not; systems tend to progress in the direction of increasing entropy.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-McQuarrie817-32)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-33) For [isolated systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isolated_system "Isolated system"), entropy never decreases.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Wiley91-31) This fact has several important consequences in science: first, it prohibits "[perpetual motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpetual_motion "Perpetual motion")" machines; and second, it implies the [arrow of entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy_(arrow_of_time) "Entropy (arrow of time)") has the same direction as the [arrow of time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arrow_of_time "Arrow of time"). Increases in the total entropy of system and surroundings correspond to irreversible changes, because some energy is expended as waste heat, limiting the amount of work a system can do.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-McH-24)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Sethna78-25)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-OxSci-34)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-35)

Unlike many other functions of state, entropy cannot be directly observed but must be calculated. Absolute [standard molar entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_molar_entropy "Standard molar entropy") of a substance can be calculated from the measured temperature dependence of its [heat capacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_capacity "Heat capacity"). The molar entropy of ions is obtained as a difference in entropy from a reference state defined as zero entropy. The [second law of thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics "Second law of thermodynamics") states that the entropy of an [isolated system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isolated_system "Isolated system") must increase or remain constant. Therefore, entropy is not a conserved quantity: for example, in an isolated system with non-uniform temperature, heat might irreversibly flow and the temperature become more uniform such that entropy increases.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-36) Chemical reactions cause changes in entropy and system entropy, in conjunction with [enthalpy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enthalpy "Enthalpy"), plays an important role in determining in which direction a chemical reaction spontaneously proceeds.

One dictionary definition of entropy is that it is "a measure of thermal energy per unit temperature that is not available for useful work" in a cyclic process. For instance, a substance at uniform temperature is at maximum entropy and cannot drive a heat engine. A substance at non-uniform temperature is at a lower entropy (than if the heat distribution is allowed to even out) and some of the thermal energy can drive a heat engine.

A special case of entropy increase, the [entropy of mixing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy_of_mixing "Entropy of mixing"), occurs when two or more different substances are mixed. If the substances are at the same temperature and pressure, there is no net exchange of heat or work – the entropy change is entirely due to the mixing of the different substances. At a statistical mechanical level, this results due to the change in available volume per particle with mixing.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-37)

### Equivalence of definitions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Entropy&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Equivalence of definitions")]

Proofs of equivalence between the entropy in statistical mechanics — the [Gibbs entropy formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy_(statistical_thermodynamics)#Gibbs_entropy_formula "Entropy (statistical thermodynamics)"):![Image 72: {\displaystyle S=-k_{\mathsf {B}}\sum _{i}{p_{i}\ln {p_{i}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8173560c45cf61ff40ae8bc50de9c9c15de7dacc)and the entropy in classical thermodynamics:![Image 73: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} S={\frac {\delta Q_{\mathsf {rev}}}{T}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10a4a9acafcbf124bb52ab133950b737d1c0e00f)together with the [fundamental thermodynamic relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_thermodynamic_relation "Fundamental thermodynamic relation") are known for the [microcanonical ensemble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microcanonical_ensemble "Microcanonical ensemble"), the [canonical ensemble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_ensemble "Canonical ensemble"), the [grand canonical ensemble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_canonical_ensemble "Grand canonical ensemble"), and the [isothermal–isobaric ensemble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isothermal%E2%80%93isobaric_ensemble "Isothermal–isobaric ensemble"). These proofs are based on the probability density of microstates of the generalised [Boltzmann distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_distribution "Boltzmann distribution") and the identification of the thermodynamic internal energy as the ensemble average ![Image 74: {\textstyle U=\left\langle E_{i}\right\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd328c7ffd2c4024d6aa154f3c01aa1ca520f0b0).[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-38) Thermodynamic relations are then employed to derive the well-known [Gibbs entropy formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gibbs_entropy_formula "Gibbs entropy formula"). However, the equivalence between the Gibbs entropy formula and the thermodynamic definition of entropy is not a fundamental thermodynamic relation but rather a consequence of the form of the [generalized Boltzmann distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_distribution#Generalized_Boltzmann_distribution "Boltzmann distribution").[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-39)

Furthermore, it has been shown that the definitions of entropy in statistical mechanics is the only entropy that is equivalent to the classical thermodynamics entropy under the following postulates:[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Gao2022-40)

1.   The probability density function is proportional to some function of the ensemble parameters and random variables.
2.   Thermodynamic state functions are described by ensemble averages of random variables.
3.   At infinite temperature, all the microstates have the same probability.

Second law of thermodynamics
----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Entropy&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Second law of thermodynamics")]

The [second law of thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics "Second law of thermodynamics") requires that, in general, the total entropy of any system does not decrease other than by increasing the entropy of some other system. Hence, in a system isolated from its environment, the entropy of that system tends not to decrease. It follows that heat cannot flow from a colder body to a hotter body without the application of work to the colder body. Secondly, it is impossible for any device operating on a cycle to produce net work from a single temperature reservoir; the production of net work requires flow of heat from a hotter reservoir to a colder reservoir, or a single expanding reservoir undergoing [adiabatic cooling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adiabatic_cooling "Adiabatic cooling"), which performs [adiabatic work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adiabatic_process "Adiabatic process"). As a result, there is no possibility of a [perpetual motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpetual_motion "Perpetual motion") machine. It follows that a reduction in the increase of entropy in a specified process, such as a [chemical reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_reaction "Chemical reaction"), means that it is energetically more efficient.

It follows from the second law of thermodynamics that the entropy of a system that is not isolated may decrease. An [air conditioner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_conditioner "Air conditioner"), for example, may cool the air in a room, thus reducing the entropy of the air of that system. The heat expelled from the room (the system), which the air conditioner transports and discharges to the outside air, always makes a bigger contribution to the entropy of the environment than the decrease of the entropy of the air of that system. Thus, the total of entropy of the room plus the entropy of the environment increases, in agreement with the second law of thermodynamics.

In mechanics, the second law in conjunction with the [fundamental thermodynamic relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_thermodynamic_relation "Fundamental thermodynamic relation") places limits on a system's ability to do [useful work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work_(thermodynamics) "Work (thermodynamics)").[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Daintith-41) The entropy change of a system at temperature ![Image 75: {\textstyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db299e88e5485f250f4ba15530469c8c6080a8cb) absorbing an infinitesimal amount of heat ![Image 76: {\textstyle \delta q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c646ab729d45a4fbd2157f73993dcdd21a0c2168) in a reversible way, is given by ![Image 77: {\textstyle \delta q/T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb2259699f303a76e59b3b24814498a447f9f125). More explicitly, an energy ![Image 78: {\textstyle T_{R}S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5d57f28eba913382ec33bf906b4aa7b5829b78f) is not available to do useful work, where ![Image 79: {\textstyle T_{R}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/067651e2409348c1022477644e9262b74afe2e7b) is the temperature of the coldest accessible reservoir or heat sink external to the system. For further discussion, see _[Exergy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exergy "Exergy")_.

Statistical mechanics demonstrates that entropy is governed by probability, thus allowing for a decrease in disorder even in an isolated system. Although this is possible, such an event has a small probability of occurring, making it unlikely.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-42)

The applicability of a second law of thermodynamics is limited to systems in or sufficiently near [equilibrium state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium "Thermodynamic equilibrium"), so that they have defined entropy.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-43) Some inhomogeneous systems out of thermodynamic equilibrium still satisfy the hypothesis of [local thermodynamic equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium#local_and_global_equilibrium "Thermodynamic equilibrium"), so that entropy density is locally defined as an intensive quantity. For such systems, there may apply a principle of maximum time rate of entropy production.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-44)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-45) It states that such a system may evolve to a steady state that maximises its time rate of entropy production. This does not mean that such a system is necessarily always in a condition of maximum time rate of entropy production; it means that it may evolve to such a steady state.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-46)[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-47)

### The fundamental thermodynamic relation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Entropy&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: The fundamental thermodynamic relation")]

The entropy of a system depends on its internal energy and its external parameters, such as its volume. In the thermodynamic limit, this fact leads to an equation relating the change in the internal energy ![Image 80: {\textstyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/087a7d8a39fe35012cbf2f561879f9e975cb4555) to changes in the entropy and the external parameters. This relation is known as the _fundamental thermodynamic relation_. If external pressure ![Image 81: {\textstyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad87bd7009e2a5c52bd0fb5a9bda9d8c1c23a79b) bears on the volume ![Image 82: {\textstyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d67b50b7ba03a56fea637093cf80e12807852d19) as the only external parameter, this relation is:![Image 83: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} U=T\ \mathrm {d} S-p\ \mathrm {d} V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9bb99a46034178ce716fb18edfd06c059fc24aff)Since both internal energy and entropy are monotonic functions of temperature ![Image 84: {\textstyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db299e88e5485f250f4ba15530469c8c6080a8cb), implying that the internal energy is fixed when one specifies the entropy and the volume, this relation is valid even if the change from one state of thermal equilibrium to another with infinitesimally larger entropy and volume happens in a non-quasistatic way (so during this change the system may be very far out of thermal equilibrium and then the whole-system entropy, pressure, and temperature may not exist).

The fundamental thermodynamic relation implies many thermodynamic identities that are valid in general, independent of the microscopic details of the system. Important examples are the [Maxwell relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell_relations "Maxwell relations") and the [relations between heat capacities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relations_between_heat_capacities "Relations between heat capacities").

### Entropy in chemical thermodynamics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Entropy&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Entropy in chemical thermodynamics")]

Thermodynamic entropy is central in [chemical thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_thermodynamics "Chemical thermodynamics"), enabling changes to be quantified and the outcome of reactions predicted. The [second law of thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics "Second law of thermodynamics") states that entropy in an [isolated system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isolated_system "Isolated system") — the combination of a subsystem under study and its surroundings — increases during all spontaneous chemical and physical processes. The [Clausius equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clausius_theorem "Clausius theorem") introduces the measurement of entropy change which describes the direction and quantifies the magnitude of simple changes such as heat transfer between systems — always from hotter body to cooler one spontaneously.

Thermodynamic entropy is an [extensive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intensive_and_extensive_properties "Intensive and extensive properties") property, meaning that it scales with the size or extent of a system. In many processes it is useful to specify the entropy as an [intensive property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intensive_and_extensive_properties "Intensive and extensive properties") independent of the size, as a specific entropy characteristic of the type of system studied. Specific entropy may be expressed relative to a unit of mass, typically the kilogram (unit: J⋅kg−1⋅K−1). Alternatively, in chemistry, it is also referred to one [mole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mole_(unit) "Mole (unit)") of substance, in which case it is called the _molar entropy_ with a unit of J⋅mol−1⋅K−1.

Thus, when one mole of substance at about 0 K is warmed by its surroundings to 298 K, the sum of the incremental values of ![Image 85: {\textstyle q_{\mathsf {rev}}/T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d858596d74dd13b88b2b363989f670eccf5bd955) constitute each element's or compound's standard molar entropy, an indicator of the amount of energy stored by a substance at 298 K.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-ctms-48)[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Jungermann-49) Entropy change also measures the mixing of substances as a summation of their relative quantities in the final mixture.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-50)

Entropy is equally essential in predicting the extent and direction of complex chemical reactions. For such applications, ![Image 86: {\textstyle \Delta S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8077bc9da71b897e3eaaa7678e439fa7a3ae5b14) must be incorporated in an expression that includes both the system and its surroundings: ![Image 87: {\displaystyle \Delta S_{\mathsf {universe}}=\Delta S_{\mathsf {surroundings}}+\Delta S_{\mathsf {system}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ab823abc77b90165563f72d52546da99cfc94a7)Via additional steps this expression becomes the equation of [Gibbs free energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gibbs_free_energy "Gibbs free energy") change ![Image 88: {\textstyle \Delta G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d7eac2b6aae5d0b728a47600e91c320ddd74930d) for reactants and products in the system at the constant pressure and temperature ![Image 89: {\textstyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db299e88e5485f250f4ba15530469c8c6080a8cb):![Image 90: {\displaystyle \Delta G=\Delta H-T\ \Delta S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b412d8e9ab468e0b9c428792c854aff0eca799c)where ![Image 91: {\textstyle \Delta H}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f3598ef8fbeacedc1dc2445a9cc3da0d96c13da6) is the [enthalpy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enthalpy "Enthalpy") change and ![Image 92: {\textstyle \Delta S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8077bc9da71b897e3eaaa7678e439fa7a3ae5b14) is the entropy change.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-ctms-48)

| **ΔH** | **ΔS** | **Spontaneity** | **Example** |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| + | + | Spontaneous **at high _T_** | Ice melting |
| – | – | Spontaneous **at low _T_** | Water freezing |
| – | + | Spontaneous **at all _T_** | Propane combustion |
| + | – | **Non-spontaneous** at all _T_ | Ozone formation |

The spontaneity of a chemical or physical process is governed by the [Gibbs free energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gibbs_free_energy "Gibbs free energy") change (ΔG), as defined by the equation ΔG = ΔH − TΔS, where ΔH represents the enthalpy change, ΔS the entropy change, and T the temperature in Kelvin. A negative ΔG indicates a thermodynamically favorable ([spontaneous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spontaneous_process "Spontaneous process")) process, while a positive ΔG denotes a non-spontaneous one. When both ΔH and ΔS are positive ([endothermic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endothermic_process "Endothermic process"), entropy-increasing), the reaction becomes spontaneous at sufficiently high temperatures, as the TΔS term dominates. Conversely, if both ΔH and ΔS are negative (exothermic, entropy-decreasing), spontaneity occurs only at low temperatures, where the enthalpy term prevails. Reactions with ΔH < 0 and ΔS > 0 ([exothermic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exothermic_process "Exothermic process") and entropy-increasing) are spontaneous at all temperatures, while those with ΔH > 0 and ΔS < 0 (endothermic and entropy-decreasing) are non-spontaneous regardless of temperature. These principles underscore the interplay between energy exchange, disorder, and temperature in determining the direction of natural processes, from phase transitions to biochemical reactions.

### World's technological capacity to store and communicate entropic information

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Entropy&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: World's technological capacity to store and communicate entropic information")]

A 2011 study in _[Science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science\_(journal) "Science (journal)")_ estimated the world's technological capacity to store and communicate optimally compressed information normalised on the most effective compression algorithms available in the year 2007, therefore estimating the entropy of the technologically available sources.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-HilbertLopez2011-51) The author's estimate that humankind's technological capacity to store information grew from 2.6 (entropically compressed) [exabytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exabytes "Exabytes") in 1986 to 295 (entropically compressed) [exabytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exabytes "Exabytes") in 2007. The world's technological capacity to receive information through one-way broadcast networks was 432 [exabytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exabytes "Exabytes") of (entropically compressed) information in 1986, to 1.9 [zettabytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zettabytes "Zettabytes") in 2007. The world's effective capacity to exchange information through two-way telecommunication networks was 281 [petabytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petabytes "Petabytes") of (entropically compressed) information in 1986, to 65 (entropically compressed) [exabytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exabytes "Exabytes") in 2007.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-HilbertLopez2011-51)

During [steady-state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steady-state_(chemical_engineering) "Steady-state (chemical engineering)") continuous operation, an entropy balance applied to an open system accounts for system entropy changes related to heat flow and mass flow across the system boundary.

In [chemical engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_engineering "Chemical engineering"), the principles of thermodynamics are commonly applied to "[open systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_system_(systems_theory) "Open system (systems theory)")", i.e. those in which heat, [work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work_(thermodynamics) "Work (thermodynamics)"), and [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") flow across the system boundary. ### Isothermal expansion or compression of an ideal gas

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Entropy&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Isothermal expansion or compression of an ideal gas")]

For the expansion (or compression) of an [ideal gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas "Ideal gas") from an initial volume ![Image 121: {\textstyle V_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5c84c0cf70006bfc9528b3a99b69c618e225e731) and pressure ![Image 122: {\textstyle P_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/34b871251e9775e022b8072367147161d1fb92cb) to a final volume ![Image 123: {\textstyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d67b50b7ba03a56fea637093cf80e12807852d19) and pressure ![Image 124: {\textstyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/038590207af1024a629c1a08c855e9ac46bf5610) at any constant temperature, the change in entropy is given by:![Image 125: {\displaystyle \Delta S=nR\ln {\frac {V}{V_{0}}}=-nR\ln {\frac {P}{P_{0}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9bb8fc47320ca06b91f883c5d0c6eab79ec2a5c8)Here ![Image 126: {\textstyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc6e1f880981346a604257ebcacdef24c0aca2d6) is the amount of gas (in [moles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mole_(unit) "Mole (unit)")) and ![Image 127: {\textstyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/197e66194eb64577670e2a100026bff6fb15d236) is the [ideal gas constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas_constant "Ideal gas constant"). These equations also apply for expansion into a finite vacuum or a [throttling process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Throttling_process_(thermodynamics) "Throttling process (thermodynamics)"), where the temperature, internal energy and enthalpy for an ideal gas remain constant.

### Cooling and heating

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Entropy&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Cooling and heating")]

For pure heating or cooling of any system (gas, liquid or solid) at constant pressure from an initial temperature ![Image 128: {\textstyle T_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1b932d5250530ef31de714cf496072501fc6a0) to a final temperature ![Image 129: {\textstyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db299e88e5485f250f4ba15530469c8c6080a8cb), the entropy change is:

![Image 130: {\textstyle \Delta S=nC_{\mathrm {P} }\ln {\frac {T}{T_{0}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/718f7e39e45cac80d3a89a001097c0e38870fa9f)
provided that the constant-pressure molar [heat capacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_capacity "Heat capacity") (or specific heat) ![Image 131: {\textstyle C_{\mathrm {P} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dcc5af6d8a4fbfde116a258213bb29a894d19f52) is constant and that no [phase transition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_transition "Phase transition") occurs in this temperature interval.

Similarly at constant volume, the entropy change is:![Image 132: {\displaystyle \Delta S=nC_{\mathrm {V} }\ln {\frac {T}{T_{0}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5df3261b15fbc770de5eb513562a06111385c56)where the constant-volume molar heat capacity ![Image 133: {\textstyle C_{\mathrm {V} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c67486cb436e559f3320497d6bff9c04179748d1) is constant and there is no phase change.

At low temperatures near absolute zero, [heat capacities of solids quickly drop off to near zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debye_T3_law "Debye T3 law"), so the assumption of constant heat capacity does not apply.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-58)

Since entropy is a [state function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functions_of_state "Functions of state"), the entropy change of any process in which temperature and volume both vary is the same as for a path divided into two steps – heating at constant volume and expansion at constant temperature. For an ideal gas, the total entropy change is:[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-59)![Image 134: {\displaystyle \Delta S=nC_{\mathrm {V} }\ln {\frac {T}{T_{0}}}+nR\ln {\frac {V}{V_{0}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f8ea70c28acb463172ac9e7acb17b2d3568d5a6)Similarly if the temperature and pressure of an ideal gas both vary:![Image 135: {\displaystyle \Delta S=nC_{\mathrm {P} }\ln {\frac {T}{T_{0}}}-nR\ln {\frac {P}{P_{0}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/692e37366ea47ec0e6fccfff47a055ff93fa7c63)

Reversible [phase transitions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_transition "Phase transition") occur at constant temperature and pressure. The reversible heat is the enthalpy change for the transition, and the entropy change is the enthalpy change divided by the thermodynamic temperature.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-60) For fusion (i.e., [melting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melting "Melting")) of a solid to a liquid at the melting point ![Image 136: {\textstyle T_{\mathsf {m}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e47b6b6ce76eb77a098880c0fb30bcd8faf81947), the [entropy of fusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy_of_fusion "Entropy of fusion") is:![Image 137: {\displaystyle \Delta S_{\mathsf {fus}}={\frac {\Delta H_{\mathsf {fus}}}{T_{\mathsf {m}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64d6b1a882393173948633085c68ca5567775e58)Similarly, for [vaporisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vaporisation "Vaporisation") of a liquid to a gas at the boiling point ![Image 138: {\displaystyle T_{\mathsf {b}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/36241694a0fe40fcd1ab1a04ade607520b416876), the [entropy of vaporisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy_of_vaporization "Entropy of vaporization") is:![Image 139: {\displaystyle \Delta S_{\mathsf {vap}}={\frac {\Delta H_{\mathsf {vap}}}{T_{\mathsf {b}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/28c6ad20366d9df5f7cf39d7f96458b13917522a)

Approaches to understanding entropy
-----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Entropy&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Approaches to understanding entropy")]

As a fundamental aspect of thermodynamics and physics, several different approaches to entropy beyond that of Clausius and Boltzmann are valid.

### Standard textbook definitions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Entropy&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Standard textbook definitions")]

The following is a list of additional definitions of entropy from a collection of textbooks:

*   a measure of [energy dispersal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_dispersal "Energy dispersal") at a specific temperature.
*   a measure of disorder in the universe or of the availability of the energy in a system to do work.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-61)
*   a measure of a system's [thermal energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_energy "Thermal energy") per unit temperature that is unavailable for doing useful [work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work_(thermodynamics) "Work (thermodynamics)").[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-62)

In Boltzmann's analysis in terms of constituent particles, entropy is a measure of the number of possible microscopic states (or microstates) of a system in thermodynamic equilibrium.

Entropy is often loosely associated with the amount of [order](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/order "wikt:order") or [disorder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Randomness "Randomness"), or of [chaos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory "Chaos theory"), in a [thermodynamic system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_system "Thermodynamic system"). The traditional qualitative description of entropy is that it refers to changes in the state of the system and is a measure of "molecular disorder" and the amount of wasted energy in a dynamical energy transformation from one state or form to another. In this direction, several recent authors have derived exact entropy formulas to account for and measure disorder and order in atomic and molecular assemblies.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Brooks-63)[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Landsberg-A-64)[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Landsberg-B-65) One of the simpler entropy order/disorder formulas is that derived in 1984 by thermodynamic physicist Peter Landsberg, based on a combination of [thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamics "Thermodynamics") and [information theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_theory "Information theory") arguments. He argues that when constraints operate on a system, such that it is prevented from entering one or more of its possible or permitted states, as contrasted with its forbidden states, the measure of the total amount of "disorder" and "order" in the system are each given by:[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Brooks-63): 69 [[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Landsberg-A-64)[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Landsberg-B-65)

![Image 140: {\displaystyle {\mathsf {Disorder}}={\frac {C_{\mathsf {D}}}{C_{\mathsf {I}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb7ebd1fea6279d94fed12c808debbe2ca9b8e16)![Image 141: {\displaystyle {\mathsf {Order}}=1-{\frac {C_{\mathsf {O}}}{C_{\mathsf {I}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07bd99512506d65e4d04ff98d5e427feb94c2db6)

Here, ![Image 142: {\textstyle C_{\mathsf {D}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca86db7ae2d81fe62d9075dca74b8888ef85d1e8) is the "disorder" capacity of the system, which is the entropy of the parts contained in the permitted ensemble, ![Image 143: {\textstyle C_{\mathsf {I}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/986331d953d9480f710a9d0414d9697b6dbf0820) is the "information" capacity of the system, an expression similar to Shannon's [channel capacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Channel_capacity "Channel capacity"), and ![Image 144: {\textstyle C_{\mathsf {O}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9ebd81ba8a75ebb8cb2edd55797c632df0cc4969) is the "order" capacity of the system.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Brooks-63)

[](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ultra_slow-motion_video_of_glass_tea_cup_smashed_on_concrete_floor.webm "Play video")[](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ultra_slow-motion_video_of_glass_tea_cup_smashed_on_concrete_floor.webm)

Slow motion video of a glass cup smashing on a concrete floor. In the very short time period of the breaking process, the entropy of the mass making up the glass cup rises sharply, as the matter and energy of the glass disperse.

The concept of entropy can be described qualitatively as a measure of energy dispersal at a specific temperature.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-66) Similar terms have been in use from early in the history of [classical thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_thermodynamics "Classical thermodynamics"), and with the development of [statistical thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_thermodynamics "Statistical thermodynamics") and [quantum theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), entropy changes have been described in terms of the mixing or "spreading" of the total energy of each constituent of a system over its particular quantised energy levels.

Ambiguities in the terms _disorder_ and _chaos_, which usually have meanings directly opposed to equilibrium, contribute to widespread confusion and hamper comprehension of entropy for most students.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-67) As the [second law of thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_law_of_thermodynamics "Second law of thermodynamics") shows, in an [isolated system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isolated_system "Isolated system") internal portions at different temperatures tend to adjust to a single uniform temperature and thus produce equilibrium. A recently developed educational approach avoids ambiguous terms and describes such spreading out of energy as dispersal, which leads to loss of the differentials required for work even though the total energy remains constant in accordance with the [first law of thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_law_of_thermodynamics "First law of thermodynamics")[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-68) (compare discussion in next section). Physical chemist [Peter Atkins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Atkins "Peter Atkins"), in his textbook _Physical Chemistry_, introduces entropy with the statement that "spontaneous changes are always accompanied by a dispersal of energy or matter and often both".[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-AtkinsPaula2019-69)

### Relating entropy to energy _usefulness_

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Entropy&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: Relating entropy to energy usefulness")]

It is possible (in a thermal context) to regard lower entropy as a measure of the _effectiveness_ or _usefulness_ of a particular quantity of energy.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-70) Energy supplied at a higher temperature (i.e. with low entropy) tends to be more useful than the same amount of energy available at a lower temperature. Mixing a hot parcel of a fluid with a cold one produces a parcel of intermediate temperature, in which the overall increase in entropy represents a "loss" that can never be replaced.

As the entropy of the universe is steadily increasing, its total energy is becoming less useful. Eventually, this is theorised to lead to the [heat death of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_death_of_the_universe "Heat death of the universe").[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-71)

### Entropy and adiabatic accessibility

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Entropy&action=edit&section=26 "Edit section: Entropy and adiabatic accessibility")]

A definition of entropy based entirely on the relation of [adiabatic accessibility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adiabatic_accessibility "Adiabatic accessibility") between equilibrium states was given by [E.H.Lieb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliott_H._Lieb "Elliott H. Lieb") and [J.Yngvason](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jakob_Yngvason "Jakob Yngvason") in 1999.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-72) This approach has several predecessors, including the pioneering work of [Constantin Carathéodory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantin_Carath%C3%A9odory "Constantin Carathéodory") from 1909[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-73) and the monograph by R.Giles.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-74) In the setting of Lieb and Yngvason, one starts by picking, for a unit amount of the substance under consideration, two reference states ![Image 145: {\textstyle X_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/755aa8ed987704b165dc585958b0acfca8c82984) and ![Image 146: {\textstyle X_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8988aef95eb5600d6730ece0631d654408f194d6) such that the latter is adiabatically accessible from the former but not conversely. Defining the entropies of the reference states to be 0 and 1 respectively, the entropy of a state ![Image 147: {\textstyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8d80c41192705e1a6c6de1d65e16d7f70fbac391) is defined as the largest number ![Image 148: {\textstyle \lambda }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f801b46dadd4b4d0ba4c6592b232053bd79e080b) such that ![Image 149: {\textstyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8d80c41192705e1a6c6de1d65e16d7f70fbac391) is adiabatically accessible from a composite state consisting of an amount ![Image 150: {\textstyle \lambda }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f801b46dadd4b4d0ba4c6592b232053bd79e080b) in the state ![Image 151: {\textstyle X_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8988aef95eb5600d6730ece0631d654408f194d6) and a complementary amount, ![Image 152: {\textstyle (1-\lambda )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/efdf958f3fc36175fff9c759e88ce107363c8600), in the state ![Image 153: {\textstyle X_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/755aa8ed987704b165dc585958b0acfca8c82984). A simple but important result within this setting is that entropy is uniquely determined, apart from a choice of unit and an additive constant for each chemical element, by the following properties: it is monotonic with respect to the relation of adiabatic accessibility, additive on composite systems, and extensive under scaling.

### Entropy in quantum mechanics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Entropy&action=edit&section=27 "Edit section: Entropy in quantum mechanics")]

In [quantum statistical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_statistical_mechanics "Quantum statistical mechanics"), the concept of entropy was developed by [John von Neumann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_von_Neumann "John von Neumann") and is generally referred to as "[von Neumann entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Von_Neumann_entropy "Von Neumann entropy")":![Image 154: {\displaystyle S=-k_{\mathsf {B}}\ \mathrm {tr} {\left({\hat {\rho }}\times \ln {\hat {\rho }}\right)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a63066d5913dad2dbde1b8e50704ed80101aaa42)where ![Image 155: {\textstyle {\hat {\rho }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/788ee23b4cf1b2708223fc79a153a0329f58cdf6) is the [density matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_matrix "Density matrix"), ![Image 156: {\textstyle \mathrm {tr} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47827384ae728b5e9e0d21fce126f513b0f5e090) is the [trace operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trace_class "Trace class") and ![Image 157: {\textstyle k_{\mathsf {B}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/acb221305e6831ab0d1545bba020675c08eae6bd) is the [Boltzmann constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_constant "Boltzmann constant").

This upholds the [correspondence principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Correspondence_principle "Correspondence principle"), because in the [classical limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_limit "Classical limit"), when the phases between the basis states are purely random, this expression is equivalent to the familiar classical definition of entropy for states with classical probabilities ![Image 158: {\textstyle p_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/187041073ab405322dd8186181730a59ed788a10):![Image 159: {\displaystyle S=-k_{\mathsf {B}}\sum _{i}{p_{i}\ln {p_{i}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8173560c45cf61ff40ae8bc50de9c9c15de7dacc)i.e. in such a basis the density matrix is diagonal.

Von Neumann established a rigorous mathematical framework for quantum mechanics with his work _Mathematische Grundlagen der Quantenmechanik_. He provided in this work a theory of measurement, where the usual notion of [wave function collapse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_function_collapse "Wave function collapse") is described as an irreversible process (the so-called von Neumann or [projective measurement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_measurement "Projective measurement")). Using this concept, in conjunction with the [density matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_matrix "Density matrix") he extended the classical concept of entropy into the quantum domain.

> I thought of calling it "information", but the word was overly used, so I decided to call it "uncertainty". [...] Von Neumann told me, "You should call it entropy, for two reasons. In the first place your uncertainty function has been used in statistical mechanics under that name, so it already has a name. In the second place, and more important, nobody knows what entropy really is, so in a debate you will always have the advantage.

When viewed in terms of [information theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_theory "Information theory"), the entropy state function is the amount of information in the system that is needed to fully specify the microstate of the system. [Entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy_(information_theory) "Entropy (information theory)") is the measure of the amount of missing information before reception.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-76) Often called _Shannon entropy_, it was originally devised by [Claude Shannon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claude_Shannon "Claude Shannon") in 1948 to study the size of information of a transmitted message. The definition of information entropy is expressed in terms of a discrete set of probabilities ![Image 160: {\textstyle p_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/187041073ab405322dd8186181730a59ed788a10) so that:![Image 161: {\displaystyle H(X)=-\sum _{i=1}^{n}{p(x_{i})\log {p(x_{i})}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ccc557c1ad035877ae960e24387c53bf1700e71c)where the base of the logarithm determines the units (for example, the [binary logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_logarithm "Binary logarithm") corresponds to [bits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bit "Bit")).

In the case of transmitted messages, these probabilities were the probabilities that a particular message was actually transmitted, and the entropy of the message system was a measure of the average size of information of a message. For the case of equal probabilities (i.e. each message is equally probable), the Shannon entropy (in bits) is just the number of binary questions needed to determine the content of the message.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Perplexed-27)

Most researchers consider information entropy and thermodynamic entropy directly linked to the same concept,[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-77)[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Georgescu-Roegen_1971-78)[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-79)[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-80)[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-81) while others argue that they are distinct.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-82) Both expressions are mathematically similar. If ![Image 162: {\textstyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e95737ee2530885a10b104e9cd5331077e1c88d5) is the number of microstates that can yield a given macrostate, and each microstate has the same _[a priori](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A\_priori\_knowledge "A priori knowledge")_ probability, then that probability is ![Image 163: {\textstyle p=1/W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3049bd887de3e69c88cc19a84483a739ea031762). The Shannon entropy (in [nats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nat_(unit) "Nat (unit)")) is:![Image 164: {\displaystyle H=-\sum _{i=1}^{W}{p_{i}\ln {p_{i}}}=\ln {W}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c78271c2df3fd9b889e3093904fc00de83aafa73)and if entropy is measured in units of ![Image 165: {\textstyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d5595fc0c47452f8fc2aa6e29c3611f047714b0) per nat, then the entropy is given by:![Image 166: {\displaystyle H=k\ln {W}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b35e757c42e6502517d1dbf0e629c762594944f0)which is the [Boltzmann entropy formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann%27s_entropy_formula "Boltzmann's entropy formula"), where ![Image 167: {\textstyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d5595fc0c47452f8fc2aa6e29c3611f047714b0) is the Boltzmann constant, which may be interpreted as the thermodynamic entropy per nat. Some authors argue for dropping the word entropy for the ![Image 168: {\textstyle H}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2435ce1b360a5f6f5a0ab5acbb3c672bf229dac7) function of information theory and using Shannon's other term, "uncertainty", instead.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-83)

The entropy of a substance can be measured, although in an indirect way. The measurement, known as entropymetry,[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-84) is done on a closed system with constant number of particles ![Image 169: {\textstyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d21d55fc102ec49600d3d5522a59ae4561acc22) and constant volume ![Image 170: {\textstyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d67b50b7ba03a56fea637093cf80e12807852d19), and it uses the definition of temperature[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-85) in terms of entropy, while limiting energy exchange to heat ![Image 171: {\textstyle \mathrm {d} U\rightarrow \mathrm {d} Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a104e43eb19851cdc70ba3d3454c961e14d581cc):![Image 172: {\displaystyle T:={\left({\frac {\partial U}{\partial S}}\right)}_{V,N}\ \Rightarrow \ \cdots \ \Rightarrow \ \mathrm {d} S={\frac {\mathrm {d} Q}{T}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6b8244ea315303cf3d00f8aba7de39c70cc8501)The resulting relation describes how entropy changes ![Image 173: {\textstyle \mathrm {d} S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/718e231010cb37a900d5601df0b18bf7e67f6d18) when a small amount of energy ![Image 174: {\textstyle \mathrm {d} Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8f56459ffbc11a61aac385e5c115a04be5a7fa19) is introduced into the system at a certain temperature ![Image 175: {\textstyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db299e88e5485f250f4ba15530469c8c6080a8cb).

The process of measurement goes as follows. First, a sample of the substance is cooled as close to absolute zero as possible. At such temperatures, the entropy approaches zero– due to the definition of temperature. Then, small amounts of heat are introduced into the sample and the change in temperature is recorded, until the temperature reaches a desired value (usually 25°C). The obtained data allows the user to integrate the equation above, yielding the absolute value of entropy of the substance at the final temperature. This value of entropy is called calorimetric entropy.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-86)

Interdisciplinary applications
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Entropy&action=edit&section=30 "Edit section: Interdisciplinary applications")]

Although the concept of entropy was originally a thermodynamic concept, it has been adapted in other fields of study,[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Pokrovskii_2020-54) including [information theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_theory "Information theory"), [psychodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychodynamics "Psychodynamics"), [thermoeconomics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermoeconomics "Thermoeconomics")/[ecological economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecological_economics "Ecological economics"), and [evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution "Evolution").[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Brooks-63)[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-87)[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-88)[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-89)[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-90)

### Philosophy and theoretical physics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Entropy&action=edit&section=31 "Edit section: Philosophy and theoretical physics")]

Entropy is the only quantity in the physical sciences that seems to imply a particular direction of progress, sometimes called an [arrow of time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arrow_of_time "Arrow of time"). As time progresses, the second law of thermodynamics states that the entropy of an [isolated system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isolated_system "Isolated system") never decreases in large systems over significant periods of time. Hence, from this perspective, entropy measurement is thought of as a clock in these conditions.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-91) Since the 19th century, a number the philosophers have drawn upon the concept of entropy to develop novel metaphysical and ethical systems. Examples of this work can be found in the thought of [Friedrich Nietzsche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche "Friedrich Nietzsche") and [Philipp Mainländer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philipp_Mainl%C3%A4nder "Philipp Mainländer"), [Claude Lévi-Strauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claude_L%C3%A9vi-Strauss "Claude Lévi-Strauss"), [Isabelle Stengers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isabelle_Stengers "Isabelle Stengers"), Shannon Mussett, and [Drew M. Dalton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drew_Dalton "Drew Dalton").

Chiavazzo _et al._ proposed that where cave spiders choose to lay their eggs can be explained through entropy minimisation.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-92)

Entropy has been proven useful in the analysis of base pair sequences in DNA. Many entropy-based measures have been shown to distinguish between different structural regions of the genome, differentiate between coding and non-coding regions of DNA, and can also be applied for the recreation of evolutionary trees by determining the evolutionary distance between different species.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-93)

Assuming that a finite universe is an isolated system, the second law of thermodynamics states that its total entropy is continually increasing. It has been speculated, since the 19th century, that the universe is fated to a [heat death](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_death_of_the_universe "Heat death of the universe") in which all the energy ends up as a homogeneous distribution of thermal energy so that no more work can be extracted from any source.

If the universe can be considered to have generally increasing entropy, then – as [Roger Penrose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roger_Penrose "Roger Penrose") has pointed out – [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity") plays an important role in the increase because gravity causes dispersed matter to accumulate into stars, which collapse eventually into [black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole "Black hole"). [The entropy of a black hole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole_entropy "Black hole entropy") is proportional to the surface area of the black hole's [event horizon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Event_horizon "Event horizon").[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-94)[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-95)[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-96)[Jacob Bekenstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacob_Bekenstein "Jacob Bekenstein") and [Stephen Hawking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephen_Hawking "Stephen Hawking") have shown that black holes have the maximum possible entropy of any object of equal size. This makes them likely end points of all entropy-increasing processes, if they are totally effective matter and energy traps.[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-97) However, the escape of energy from black holes might be possible due to quantum activity (see [Hawking radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hawking_radiation "Hawking radiation")).

The role of entropy in cosmology remains a controversial subject since the time of [Ludwig Boltzmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Boltzmann "Ludwig Boltzmann"). Recent work has cast some doubt on the heat death hypothesis and the applicability of any simple thermodynamic model to the universe in general. Although entropy does increase in the model of an expanding universe, the maximum possible entropy rises much more rapidly, moving the universe further from the heat death with time, not closer.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-98)[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-99)[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-100) This results in an "entropy gap" pushing the system further away from the posited heat death equilibrium.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-101) Other complicating factors, such as the energy density of the vacuum and macroscopic [quantum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") effects, are difficult to reconcile with thermodynamical models, making any predictions of large-scale thermodynamics extremely difficult.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-102)

Current theories suggest the entropy gap to have been originally opened up by [the early rapid exponential expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflation_(cosmology) "Inflation (cosmology)") of the universe.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Albrecht-103)

[Romanian American](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanian_American "Romanian American") economist [Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicholas_Georgescu-Roegen "Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen"), a [progenitor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_people_considered_father_or_mother_of_a_scientific_field "List of people considered father or mother of a scientific field") in economics and a [paradigm founder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paradigm_shift#Kuhnian_paradigm_shifts "Paradigm shift") of [ecological economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecological_economics "Ecological economics"), made extensive use of the entropy concept in his [magnum opus on _The Entropy Law and the Economic Process_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicholas_Georgescu-Roegen#Magnum_opus_on_The_Entropy_Law_and_the_Economic_Process "Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen").[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-Georgescu-Roegen_1971-78) Due to Georgescu-Roegen's work, the [laws of thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laws_of_thermodynamics "Laws of thermodynamics") form an [integral part of the ecological economics school](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecological_economics#Methodology "Ecological economics").[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-104): 204f [[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-105): 29–35  Although his work was [blemished somewhat by mistakes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicholas_Georgescu-Roegen#Mistakes_and_controversies "Nicholas Georgescu-Roegen"), a full chapter on the economics of Georgescu-Roegen has approvingly been included in one elementary physics textbook on the historical development of thermodynamics.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-106): 95–112

In economics, Georgescu-Roegen's work has generated the term ['entropy pessimism'](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pessimism#Entropy_pessimism "Pessimism").[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-107): 116  Since the 1990s, leading ecological economist and [steady-state theorist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steady-state_economy#Herman_Daly's_concept_of_a_steady-state_economy "Steady-state economy")[Herman Daly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herman_Daly "Herman Daly") – a student of Georgescu-Roegen – has been the economics profession's most influential proponent of the entropy pessimism position.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-108): 545f [[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy#cite_note-109)
