**Acetonitrile**, often abbreviated **MeCN** (**methyl cyanide**), is the [chemical compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_compound "Chemical compound") with the [formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")CH 3 CN and [structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_structure "Chemical structure")H 3 C−C≡N. This colourless liquid is the simplest organic [nitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrile "Nitrile") ([hydrogen cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide "Hydrogen cyanide") is a simpler nitrile, but the [cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanide "Cyanide") anion is not classed as [organic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound")). It is produced mainly as a byproduct of [acrylonitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrylonitrile "Acrylonitrile") manufacture. It is used as a [polar aprotic solvent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_aprotic_solvent "Polar aprotic solvent") in [organic synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_synthesis "Organic synthesis") and in the purification of [butadiene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butadiene "Butadiene").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-ashford-5) The N≡C−C skeleton is [linear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_molecular_geometry "Linear molecular geometry") with a short C≡N[distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_length "Bond length") of 1.16[Å](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angstrom "Angstrom").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-6)

Acetonitrile was first prepared in 1847 by the French chemist [Jean-Baptiste Dumas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Dumas "Jean-Baptiste Dumas").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-7)

Acetonitrile is used mainly as a solvent in the purification of [butadiene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butadiene "Butadiene") in refineries. Specifically, acetonitrile is fed into the top of a [distillation column](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distillation_column "Distillation column") filled with [hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon") including butadiene, and as the acetonitrile falls down through the column, it absorbs the butadiene which is then sent from the bottom of the tower to a second separating tower. Heat is then employed in the separating tower to separate the butadiene.

In the laboratory, it is used as a medium-polarity [non-protic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protic_solvent "Protic solvent") solvent that is [miscible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miscibility "Miscibility") with water and a range of organic solvents, but not saturated hydrocarbons. It has a convenient range of temperatures at which it is a liquid, and a high [dielectric constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dielectric_constant "Dielectric constant") of 38.8. With a [dipole moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_dipole_moment "Electric dipole moment") of 3.92[D](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debye "Debye"),[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-8) acetonitrile dissolves a wide range of ionic and nonpolar compounds and is useful as a mobile phase in [HPLC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-performance_liquid_chromatography "High-performance liquid chromatography") and [LC–MS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_chromatography%E2%80%93mass_spectrometry "Liquid chromatography–mass spectrometry").

It is widely used in [battery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battery_(electricity) "Battery (electricity)") applications because of its relatively high dielectric constant and ability to dissolve [electrolytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrolyte "Electrolyte"). For similar reasons, it is a popular solvent in [cyclic voltammetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_voltammetry "Cyclic voltammetry").

Its ultraviolet transparency [UV cutoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UV_cutoff "UV cutoff"), low [viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscosity "Viscosity") and low [chemical reactivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_reactivity "Chemical reactivity") make it a popular choice for [high-performance liquid chromatography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-performance_liquid_chromatography "High-performance liquid chromatography") (HPLC).

Acetonitrile plays a significant role as the dominant solvent used in [oligonucleotide synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligonucleotide_synthesis "Oligonucleotide synthesis") from [nucleoside phosphoramidites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleoside_phosphoramidite "Nucleoside phosphoramidite").

Industrially, it is used as a solvent for the manufacture of [pharmaceuticals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pharmaceutical "Pharmaceutical") and [photographic film](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photographic_film "Photographic film").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-ecb-9)

Acetonitrile is a common two-carbon building block in [organic synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_synthesis "Organic synthesis")[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-10) of many useful chemicals, including [acetamidine hydrochloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetamidine_hydrochloride "Acetamidine hydrochloride"), [thiamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thiamine "Thiamine"), and [1-naphthaleneacetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1-naphthaleneacetic_acid "1-naphthaleneacetic acid").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-encyc-toxic-11) Its reaction with [cyanogen chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanogen_chloride "Cyanogen chloride") affords [malononitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malononitrile "Malononitrile").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-ashford-5)

### As an electron pair donor

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetonitrile&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: As an electron pair donor")]

Acetonitrile has a free electron pair at the nitrogen atom, which can form many [transition metal nitrile complexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transition_metal_nitrile_complexes "Transition metal nitrile complexes"). For example, [bis(acetonitrile)palladium dichloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bis(acetonitrile)palladium_dichloride "Bis(acetonitrile)palladium dichloride") is prepared by heating a suspension of [palladium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palladium_chloride "Palladium chloride") in acetonitrile:[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-12)

PdCl 2 + 2 CH 3 CN → PdCl 2(CH 3 CN)2
A related complex is [tetrakis(acetonitrile)copper(I) hexafluorophosphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrakis(acetonitrile)copper(I)_hexafluorophosphate "Tetrakis(acetonitrile)copper(I) hexafluorophosphate")[Cu(CH 3 CN)4]+. The CH 3 CN ligands in these complexes are rapidly displaced.

It also forms Lewis adducts with group 13 [Lewis acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lewis_acid "Lewis acid") like [boron trifluoride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boron_trifluoride "Boron trifluoride").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-13) In [superacids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superacid "Superacid"), it is possible to protonate acetonitrile.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-Christe-14)

Acetonitrile is a byproduct from the manufacture of [acrylonitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrylonitrile "Acrylonitrile") by catalytic [ammoxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammoxidation "Ammoxidation") of [propylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propylene "Propylene"). Most is combusted to support the intended process but an estimated several thousand tons are retained for the above-mentioned applications.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-Ullmann-15) Production trends for acetonitrile thus generally follow those of [acrylonitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrylonitrile "Acrylonitrile"). Acetonitrile can also be produced by many other methods, but these are of no commercial importance as of 2002. Illustrative routes are by dehydration of [acetamide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetamide "Acetamide") or by [hydrogenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogenation "Hydrogenation") of mixtures of [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide") and [ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia").[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-16) In 1992, 14,700 tonnes (16,200 short tons) of acetonitrile were produced in the US.

### Acetonitrile shortage in 2008–2009

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetonitrile&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Acetonitrile shortage in 2008–2009")]

Starting in October 2008, the worldwide supply of acetonitrile was low because Chinese production was shut down for the [Olympics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2008_Olympics "2008 Olympics"). Furthermore, a U.S. factory was damaged in Texas during [Hurricane Ike](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurricane_Ike "Hurricane Ike").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-17) Due to the global economic slowdown, the production of acrylonitrile used in acrylic fibers and [acrylonitrile butadiene styrene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrylonitrile_butadiene_styrene "Acrylonitrile butadiene styrene") (ABS) resins decreased. Acetonitrile is a byproduct in the production of [acrylonitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrylonitrile "Acrylonitrile") and its production also decreased, further compounding the acetonitrile shortage.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-18) The global shortage of acetonitrile continued through early 2009.[_[needs update](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual\_of\_Style/Dates\_and\_numbers#Chronological\_items "Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Dates and numbers")_]

Acetonitrile has only modest toxicity in small doses.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-encyc-toxic-11)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-inrs-19) It can be [metabolised](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism "Metabolism") to produce [hydrogen cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide "Hydrogen cyanide"), which is the source of the observed toxic effects.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-ecb-9)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-who-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-epa-21) Generally the onset of toxic effects is delayed, due to the time required for the body to metabolize acetonitrile to cyanide (generally about 2–12 hours).[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-encyc-toxic-11)

Cases of acetonitrile poisoning in humans (or, to be more specific, of cyanide poisoning after exposure to acetonitrile) are rare but not unknown, by inhalation, ingestion and (possibly) by skin absorption.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-who-20) The symptoms, which do not usually appear for several hours after the exposure, include breathing difficulties, slow [pulse rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulse_rate "Pulse rate"), [nausea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nausea "Nausea"), and vomiting. [Convulsions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convulsion "Convulsion") and [coma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coma "Coma") can occur in serious cases, followed by death from [respiratory failure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_failure "Respiratory failure"). The treatment is as for [cyanide poisoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanide_poisoning "Cyanide poisoning"), with [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen"), [sodium nitrite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_nitrite "Sodium nitrite"), and [sodium thiosulfate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_thiosulfate "Sodium thiosulfate") among the most commonly used emergency treatments.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-who-20)

It has been used in formulations for [nail polish remover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nail_polish_remover "Nail polish remover"), despite its toxicity. At least two cases have been reported of accidental poisoning of young children by acetonitrile-based nail polish remover, one of which was fatal.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-22)[Acetone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone "Acetone") and [ethyl acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethyl_acetate "Ethyl acetate") are often preferred as safer for domestic use, and acetonitrile has been banned in cosmetic products in the [European Economic Area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Economic_Area "European Economic Area") since March 2000.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-23)

#### Metabolism and excretion

In common with other [nitriles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrile "Nitrile"), acetonitrile can be [metabolised](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism "Metabolism") in [microsomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsome "Microsome"), especially in the liver, to produce [hydrogen cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide "Hydrogen cyanide"), as was first shown by Pozzani _et al._ in 1959.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-25) The first step in this pathway is the oxidation of acetonitrile to [glycolonitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycolonitrile "Glycolonitrile") by an [NADPH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NADPH "NADPH")-dependent [cytochrome P450](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytochrome_P450 "Cytochrome P450")[monooxygenase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monooxygenase "Monooxygenase"). The glycolonitrile then undergoes a spontaneous decomposition to give hydrogen cyanide and [formaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde "Formaldehyde").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-inrs-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-who-20) Formaldehyde, a toxin and a carcinogen on its own, is further oxidized to [formic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid "Formic acid"), which is another source of toxicity.

The metabolism of acetonitrile is much slower than that of other nitriles, which accounts for its relatively low toxicity. Hence, one hour after administration of a potentially lethal dose, the concentration of cyanide in the rat brain was 1⁄20 that for a [propionitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propionitrile "Propionitrile") dose 60 times lower (see table).[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-ratdata-24)

The relatively slow metabolism of acetonitrile to hydrogen cyanide allows more of the cyanide produced to be detoxified within the body to [thiocyanate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thiocyanate "Thiocyanate") (the [rhodanese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhodanese "Rhodanese") pathway). It also allows more of the acetonitrile to be excreted unchanged before it is metabolised. The main pathways of excretion are by exhalation and in the urine.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-inrs-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-who-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile#cite_note-epa-21)

*   [Trichloroacetonitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trichloroacetonitrile "Trichloroacetonitrile") – a derivative of acetonitrile used to [protect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protecting_group "Protecting group")[alcohol groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl "Hydroxyl"), and also used as a reagent in the [Overman rearrangement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overman_rearrangement "Overman rearrangement")
