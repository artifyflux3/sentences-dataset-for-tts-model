Example of a regular grid

A **regular grid** is a [tessellation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tessellation "Tessellation") of _**n**_-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") by [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_(geometry) "Congruence (geometry)")[parallelotopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped#Parallelotope "Parallelepiped") (e.g. [bricks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brick "Brick")).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_grid#cite_note-1) Its opposite is [irregular grid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unstructured_grid "Unstructured grid").

Grids of this type appear on [graph paper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_paper "Graph paper") and may be used in [finite element analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_element_analysis "Finite element analysis"), [finite volume methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_volume_method "Finite volume method"), [finite difference methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_difference_method "Finite difference method"), and in general for discretization of parameter spaces. Since the derivatives of field variables can be conveniently expressed as finite differences,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_grid#cite_note-Preface,_P-1.1-2) structured grids mainly appear in finite difference methods. [Unstructured grids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unstructured_grid "Unstructured grid") offer more flexibility than structured grids and hence are very useful in finite element and finite volume methods.

Each cell in the grid can be addressed by index (i, j) in two [dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension") or (i, j, k) in three dimensions, and each [vertex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)") has [coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate "Coordinate")(i⋅d x,j⋅d y){\displaystyle (i\cdot dx,j\cdot dy)}![Image 14: {\displaystyle (i\cdot dx,j\cdot dy)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29a4c6e1e2d9cae4ba926f64d1e0fbd2602bf047) in 2D or (i⋅d x,j⋅d y,k⋅d z){\displaystyle (i\cdot dx,j\cdot dy,k\cdot dz)}![Image 15: {\displaystyle (i\cdot dx,j\cdot dy,k\cdot dz)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1202201864968f3b04bcad85871c8c821684cd1c) in 3D for some real numbers _dx_, _dy_, and _dz_ representing the grid spacing.

Related grids
-------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Regular_grid&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Related grids")]

A **Cartesian grid** is a special case where the elements are [unit squares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_square "Unit square") or [unit cubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_cube "Unit cube"), and the vertices are [points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)") on the [integer lattice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer_lattice "Integer lattice").

A **rectilinear grid** is a tessellation by [rectangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle "Rectangle") or [rectangular cuboids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangular_cuboid "Rectangular cuboid") (also known as [rectangular parallelepipeds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangular_parallelepiped "Rectangular parallelepiped")) that are not, in general, all [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_(geometry) "Congruence (geometry)") to each other. The cells may still be indexed by integers as above, but the mapping from indexes to vertex coordinates is less uniform than in a regular grid. An example of a rectilinear grid that is not regular appears on [logarithmic scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logarithmic_scale "Logarithmic scale")[graph paper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_paper "Graph paper").

A **skewed grid** is a tessellation of [parallelograms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") or [parallelepipeds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped "Parallelepiped"). (If the unit lengths are all equal, it is a tessellation of [rhombi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombus "Rhombus") or [rhombohedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombohedron "Rhombohedron").)

A **curvilinear grid** or **structured grid** is a grid with the same combinatorial structure as a regular grid, in which the cells are [quadrilaterals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral "Quadrilateral") or [[general] cuboids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuboid "Cuboid"), rather than rectangles or rectangular cuboids.
