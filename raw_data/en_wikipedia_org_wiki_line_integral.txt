In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **line integral** is an [integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral") where the [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") to be integrated is evaluated along a [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_integral#cite_note-Tang2006-1) The terms _**path integral**_, _**curve integral**_, and _**curvilinear integral**_ are also used; _[contour integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contour\_integral "Contour integral")_ is used as well, although that is typically reserved for [line integrals in the complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_integral#Complex_line_integral).

The function to be integrated may be a [scalar field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field "Scalar field") or a [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field"). The value of the line integral is the sum of values of the field at all points on the curve, weighted by some scalar function on the curve (commonly [arc length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_length "Arc length") or, for a vector field, the [scalar product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") of the vector field with a [differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_(infinitesimal) "Differential (infinitesimal)") vector in the curve). This weighting distinguishes the line integral from simpler integrals defined on [intervals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)"). Many simple formulae in physics, such as the definition of [work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work_(physics) "Work (physics)") as ![Image 1: {\displaystyle W=\mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {s} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f62e7b41bc9ba33e6f1d9c7e36d77c48d41a2149), have natural continuous analogues in terms of line integrals, in this case ![Image 2: {\textstyle W=\int _{L}\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {s} )\cdot d\mathbf {s} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/adcc983069f7d70133cc2efadc0560f8de7a93f0), which computes the [work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_work "Mechanical work") done on an object moving through an electric or gravitational field **F** along a path ![Image 3: {\displaystyle L}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/103168b86f781fe6e9a4a87b8ea1cebe0ad4ede8).

In qualitative terms, a line integral in vector calculus can be thought of as a measure of the total effect of a given [tensor field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field "Tensor field") along a given curve. For example, the line integral over a scalar field (rank 0 tensor) can be interpreted as the area under the field carved out by a particular curve. This can be visualized as the surface created by _z_ = _f_(_x_,_y_) and a curve _C_ in the _xy_ plane. The line integral of _f_ would be the area of the "curtain" created—when the points of the surface that are directly over _C_ are carved out.

### Line integral of a scalar field

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Line_integral&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Line integral of a scalar field")]

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/4/42/Line_integral_of_scalar_field.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Line_integral_of_scalar_field.gif)

The line integral over a scalar field _f_ can be thought of as the area under the curve _C_ along a surface _z_ = _f_(_x_,_y_), described by the field.

For some [scalar field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field "Scalar field")![Image 5: {\displaystyle f\colon U\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d71097bf5f00c4116453f42be6ed7f74e74b3be8) where ![Image 6: {\displaystyle U\subseteq \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a4ffabb41c43db92055a8fd5be74cd7633756a9), the line integral along a [piecewise smooth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piecewise_smooth "Piecewise smooth")[curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve")![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {C}}\subset U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/37aa7703ab68113f80a4c75db47bd603a37fb3a4) is defined as ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \int _{\mathcal {C}}f\,ds=\int _{a}^{b}f\left(\mathbf {r} (t)\right)\left|\mathbf {r} '(t)\right|\,dt,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d7c8e2e93aa7034de30e26dcde6e9047fd55309c) where ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} \colon [a,b]\to {\mathcal {C}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/00e254c282fe91e1b451afd278d990ae34283d19) is an arbitrary [bijective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijective "Bijective")[parametrization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametric_equation "Parametric equation") of the curve ![Image 10: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {C}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e7b3edab7022ca9e2976651bc59c489513ee9019) such that **r**(_a_) and **r**(_b_) give the endpoints of ![Image 11: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {C}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e7b3edab7022ca9e2976651bc59c489513ee9019) and _a_<_b_. Here, and in the rest of the article, the absolute value bars denote the [standard (Euclidean) norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance "Euclidean distance") of a vector.

The function f is called the integrand, the curve ![Image 12: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {C}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e7b3edab7022ca9e2976651bc59c489513ee9019) is the domain of integration, and the symbol _ds_ may be intuitively interpreted as an elementary [arc length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_length "Arc length") of the curve ![Image 13: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {C}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e7b3edab7022ca9e2976651bc59c489513ee9019) (i.e., a differential length of ![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {C}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e7b3edab7022ca9e2976651bc59c489513ee9019)). Line integrals of scalar fields over a curve ![Image 15: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {C}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e7b3edab7022ca9e2976651bc59c489513ee9019) do not depend on the chosen parametrization **r** of ![Image 16: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {C}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e7b3edab7022ca9e2976651bc59c489513ee9019).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_integral#cite_note-:1-2)

Geometrically, when the scalar field f is defined over a plane (_n_ = 2), its graph is a surface _z_ = _f_(_x_, _y_) in space, and the line integral gives the (signed) [cross-sectional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-section_(geometry) "Cross-section (geometry)") area bounded by the curve ![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {C}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e7b3edab7022ca9e2976651bc59c489513ee9019) and the graph of f. See the animation to the right.

For a line integral over a scalar field, the integral can be constructed from a [Riemann sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_sum "Riemann sum") using the above definitions of f, C and a parametrization **r** of C. This can be done by partitioning the [interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)")[_a_, _b_] into n sub-intervals [_t_ _i_−1, _t_ _i_] of length Δ _t_ = (_b_ − _a_)/_n_, then **r**(_t_ _i_) denotes some point, call it a sample point, on the curve C. We can use the [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") of sample points {**r**(_t_ _i_): 1 ≤ _i_ ≤ _n_} to approximate the curve C as a [polygonal path](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygonal_path "Polygonal path") by introducing the straight line piece between each of the sample points **r**(_t_ _i_−1) and **r**(_t_ _i_). (The approximation of a curve to a polygonal path is called _rectification of a curve,_ see [here](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_length "Arc length") for more details.) We then label the distance of the line segment between adjacent sample points on the curve as Δ _s_ _i_. The product of _f_(**r**(_t_ _i_)) and Δ _s_ _i_ can be associated with the signed area of a rectangle with a height and width of _f_(**r**(_t_ _i_)) and Δ _s_ _i_, respectively. Taking the [limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_of_a_sequence "Limit of a sequence") of the [sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Summation "Summation") of the terms as the length of the partitions approaches zero gives us ![Image 18: {\displaystyle I=\lim _{\Delta s_{i}\to 0}\sum _{i=1}^{n}f(\mathbf {r} (t_{i}))\,\Delta s_{i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85a192f293130034b5a3d2a1b43f36da032832e)

By the [mean value theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_value_theorem "Mean value theorem"), the distance between subsequent points on the curve, is ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \Delta s_{i}=\left|\mathbf {r} (t_{i}+\Delta t)-\mathbf {r} (t_{i})\right|\approx \left|\mathbf {r} '(t_{i})\Delta t\right|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/edeec3b7689fc395f0d18df564ccd7340ddfec11)

Substituting this in the above Riemann sum yields ![Image 20: {\displaystyle I=\lim _{\Delta t\to 0}\sum _{i=1}^{n}f(\mathbf {r} (t_{i}))\left|\mathbf {r} '(t_{i})\right|\Delta t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/680a75e25b4f4b3a762860fdfc4212ff99081523) which is the Riemann sum for the integral ![Image 21: {\displaystyle I=\int _{a}^{b}f(\mathbf {r} (t))\left|\mathbf {r} '(t)\right|dt.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79d896c9f15bed2e25669e6f075582af73b836d0)

### Line integral of a vector field

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Line_integral&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Line integral of a vector field")]

For a [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field")**F**: _U_ ⊆ **R**_n_ → **R**_n_, the line integral along a [piecewise smooth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piecewise_smooth "Piecewise smooth")[curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve")_C_ ⊂ _U_, in the direction of **r**, is defined as ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \int _{C}\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {r} )\cdot d\mathbf {r} =\int _{a}^{b}\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {r} (t))\cdot \mathbf {r} '(t)\,dt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d317ca76dbfae168bf1eb9cea2f843a0549a4789) where · is the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product"), and **r**: [_a_, _b_] → _C_ is a regular [parametrization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametric_equation "Parametric equation") (i.e: ![Image 23: {\displaystyle ||\mathbf {r} '(t)||\neq 0\;\;\forall t\in [a,b]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a845d5294bb81bb2d50c44b4747fd6d6ea9689d2)) of the curve _C_ such that **r**(_a_) and **r**(_b_) give the endpoints of _C_.

A line integral of a scalar field is thus a line integral of a vector field, where the vectors are always [tangential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangential "Tangential") to the line of the integration.

Line integrals of vector fields are independent of the parametrization **r** in [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value"), but they do depend on its [orientation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve_orientation "Curve orientation"). Specifically, a reversal in the orientation of the parametrization changes the sign of the line integral.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_integral#cite_note-:1-2)

From the viewpoint of [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), the line integral of a vector field along a curve is the integral of the corresponding 1-form under the [musical isomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musical_isomorphism "Musical isomorphism") (which takes the vector field to the corresponding [covector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covector "Covector") field), over the curve considered as an [immersed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immersion_(mathematics) "Immersion (mathematics)") 1-manifold.

[![Image 24](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/b/b0/Line_integral_of_vector_field.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Line_integral_of_vector_field.gif)

The trajectory of a particle (in red) along a curve inside a vector field. Starting from _a_, the particle traces the path _C_ along the vector field _F_. The dot product (green line) of its tangent vector (red arrow) and the field vector (blue arrow) defines an area under a curve, which is equivalent to the path's line integral. (Click on image for a detailed description.)

The line integral of a vector field can be derived in a manner very similar to the case of a scalar field, but this time with the inclusion of a dot product. Again using the above definitions of **F**, C and its parametrization **r**(_t_), we construct the integral from a [Riemann sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_sum "Riemann sum"). We partition the [interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)")[_a_, _b_] (which is the range of the values of the [parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter "Parameter")t) into n intervals of length Δ _t_ = (_b_ − _a_)/_n_. Letting _t i_ be the i th point on [_a_, _b_], then **r**(_t_ _i_) gives us the position of the i th point on the curve. However, instead of calculating up the distances between subsequent points, we need to calculate their [displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Displacement_(vector) "Displacement (vector)") vectors, Δ**r**_i_. As before, evaluating **F** at all the points on the curve and taking the dot product with each displacement vector gives us the [infinitesimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal "Infinitesimal") contribution of each partition of **F** on C. Letting the size of the partitions go to zero gives us a sum ![Image 25: {\displaystyle I=\lim _{\Delta t\to 0}\sum _{i=1}^{n}\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {r} (t_{i}))\cdot \Delta \mathbf {r} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/376597a2ceb7c98255401bbf1c19dbff6a7d13b9)

By the [mean value theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_value_theorem "Mean value theorem"), we see that the displacement vector between adjacent points on the curve is ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \Delta \mathbf {r} _{i}=\mathbf {r} (t_{i}+\Delta t)-\mathbf {r} (t_{i})\approx \mathbf {r} '(t_{i})\,\Delta t.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdfa95407800597e5b86905d7caf335bf89f6aa0)

Substituting this in the above Riemann sum yields ![Image 27: {\displaystyle I=\lim _{\Delta t\to 0}\sum _{i=1}^{n}\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {r} (t_{i}))\cdot \mathbf {r} '(t_{i})\,\Delta t,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d78d5e663fea70ca88b45f0293e3741a3fc7acef)

which is the Riemann sum for the integral defined above.

If a vector field **F** is the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient") of a [scalar field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field "Scalar field")_G_ (i.e. if **F** is [conservative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservative_vector_field "Conservative vector field")), that is, ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =\nabla G,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/199b3b0d079b447f7bdbb88274e52a208bf9c9a5) then by the [multivariable chain rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chain_rule#Multivariable_case "Chain rule") the [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") of the [composition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition "Function composition") of _G_ and **r**(_t_) is ![Image 29: {\displaystyle {\frac {dG(\mathbf {r} (t))}{dt}}=\nabla G(\mathbf {r} )\cdot \mathbf {r} '(t)=\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {r} (t))\cdot \mathbf {r} '(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f98250f273f3a54d69dd437ecc674f63659bbec) which happens to be the integrand for the line integral of **F** on **r**(_t_). It follows, given a path _C_, that ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \int _{C}\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {r} )\cdot d\mathbf {r} =\int _{a}^{b}\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {r} (t))\cdot \mathbf {r} '(t)\,dt=\int _{a}^{b}{\frac {dG(\mathbf {r} (t))}{dt}}\,dt=G(\mathbf {r} (b))-G(\mathbf {r} (a)).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53fa5d1a2d56abfccec4cbb31587b5cab8f7759a)

In other words, the integral of **F** over _C_ depends solely on the values of _G_ at the points **r**(_b_) and **r**(_a_), and is thus independent of the path between them. For this reason, a line integral of a conservative vector field is called _path independent_.

The line integral has many uses in physics. For example, the [work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work_(physics) "Work (physics)") done on a particle traveling on a curve _C_ inside a force field represented as a vector field **F** is the line integral of **F** on _C_.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_integral#cite_note-3)

For another example, see [Ampère's circuital law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amp%C3%A8re%27s_circuital_law "Ampère's circuital law").

Flow across a curve
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Line_integral&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Flow across a curve")]

For a [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field")![Image 31: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} \colon U\subseteq \mathbb {R} ^{2}\to \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/70798a4bbae70cabba481760ac949f6a38608413), **F**(_x_, _y_) = (_P_(_x_, _y_), _Q_(_x_, _y_)), the **line integral across a curve**_C_ ⊂ _U_, also called the [_flux integral_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flux "Flux"), is defined in terms of a [piecewise smooth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piecewise_smooth "Piecewise smooth")[parametrization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametric_equation "Parametric equation")**r**: [_a_,_b_] → _C_, **r**(_t_) = (_x_(_t_), _y_(_t_)), as: ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \int _{C}\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {r} )\cdot d\mathbf {r} ^{\perp }=\int _{a}^{b}{\begin{bmatrix}P{\big (}x(t),y(t){\big )}\\Q{\big (}x(t),y(t){\big )}\end{bmatrix}}\cdot {\begin{bmatrix}y'(t)\\-x'(t)\end{bmatrix}}~dt=\int _{a}^{b}\left(-Q~dx+P~dy\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/648ad32b3545893968b09e1fa0768cf2098cb92e)

Here ⋅ is the dot product, and ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} '(t)^{\perp }=(y'(t),-x'(t))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b0661407cd0a57ff967d3b9bad4db5fd59c2f37) is the clockwise perpendicular of the velocity vector ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} '(t)=(x'(t),y'(t))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1761cded64bc1422c874a509a3704ae97152cc6e).

The flow is computed in an oriented sense: the curve C has a specified forward direction from **r**(_a_) to **r**(_b_), and the flow is counted as positive when **F**(**r**(_t_)) is on the clockwise side of the forward velocity vector **r'**(_t_).

Complex line integral
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Line_integral&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Complex line integral")]

In [complex analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis "Complex analysis"), the line integral is defined in terms of [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number#Multiplication "Complex number") and [addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number#Addition_and_subtraction "Complex number") of complex numbers. Suppose _U_ is an [open subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_subset "Open subset") of the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane")**C**, _f_: _U_ → **C** is a function, and ![Image 35: {\displaystyle L\subset U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38ccc5e372e3f9b86bbfb6a0bac51f2f7b274ea6) is a curve of finite length, parametrized by _γ_: [_a_,_b_] → _L_, where _γ_(_t_) = _x_(_t_) + _iy_(_t_). The line integral ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \int _{L}f(z)\,dz}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d6687361b95b6d95787ecd4f1ce97d8d34c8a6fe) may be defined by subdividing the [interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)") [_a_, _b_] into _a_ = _t_ 0<_t_ 1< ... <_t_ _n_ = _b_ and considering the expression ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \sum _{k=1}^{n}f(\gamma (t_{k}))\,[\gamma (t_{k})-\gamma (t_{k-1})]=\sum _{k=1}^{n}f(\gamma _{k})\,\Delta \gamma _{k}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d2bb22683cc64b9d75f8c5c6c3a2d60bde08a515)

The integral is then the limit of this [Riemann sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_sum "Riemann sum") as the lengths of the subdivision intervals approach zero.

If the parametrization γ is [continuously differentiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_function#Differentiability_classes "Differentiable function"), the line integral can be evaluated as an integral of a function of a real variable: ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \int _{L}f(z)\,dz=\int _{a}^{b}f(\gamma (t))\gamma '(t)\,dt.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3d3465068f61ba875b97a58b0a3c21f85705f1d)

When L is a closed curve (initial and final points coincide), the line integral is often denoted ![Image 39: {\textstyle \oint _{L}f(z)\,dz,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dedd2e21de79ca198fc043777090b3079a47228) sometimes referred to in engineering as a _cyclic integral_.

To establish a complete analogy with the line integral of a vector field, one must go back to the definition of differentiability in multivariable calculus. The gradient is defined from [Riesz representation theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riesz_representation_theorem "Riesz representation theorem"), and inner products in complex analysis involve conjugacy (the gradient of a function ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a223c880b0ce3da8f64ee33c4f0010beee400b1a) at some ![Image 41: {\displaystyle z\in \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/169fae60c23a2027ece2aa7fd4b5047492887e91) would be ![Image 42: {\displaystyle {\overline {\gamma '(z)}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ef194c6d8469036a6d89cea595dbb4c41f12047), and the complex inner product would attribute twice a conjugate to ![Image 43: {\displaystyle \gamma '}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f0e2b9e9e12e56bd62af445be6803ec4843e919a) in the vector field definition of a line integral).

The line integral with respect to the conjugate complex differential ![Image 44: {\displaystyle {\overline {dz}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f89d5d601573e933f7f08769e6a8e7ca78692436) is defined[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_integral#cite_note-4) to be ![Image 45: {\displaystyle \int _{L}f(z){\overline {dz}}:={\overline {\int _{L}{\overline {f(z)}}\,dz}}=\int _{a}^{b}f(\gamma (t)){\overline {\gamma '(t)}}\,dt.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/13d1aaa179397451052608d1e9b7ac88d99131ee)

The line integrals of complex functions can be evaluated using a number of techniques. The most direct is to split into real and imaginary parts, reducing the problem to evaluating two real-valued line integrals. The [Cauchy integral theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy_integral_theorem "Cauchy integral theorem") may be used to equate the line integral of an [analytic function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_function "Analytic function") to the same integral over a more convenient curve. It also implies that over a closed curve enclosing a region where _f_(_z_) is analytic without [singularities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_singularity "Mathematical singularity"), the value of the integral is simply zero, or in case the region includes singularities, the [residue theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Residue_theorem "Residue theorem") computes the integral in terms of the singularities. This also implies the path independence of complex line integral for analytic functions.

Consider the function _f_(_z_) = 1/_z_, and let the contour _L_ be the counterclockwise [unit circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_circle "Unit circle") about 0, parametrized by _z_(_t_) = _e_ _it_ with t in [0, 2 _π_] using the [complex exponential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_formula "Euler's formula"). Substituting, we find: ![Image 46: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\oint _{L}{\frac {1}{z}}\,dz&=\int _{0}^{2\pi }{\frac {1}{e^{it}}}ie^{it}\,dt=i\int _{0}^{2\pi }e^{-it}e^{it}\,dt\\&=i\int _{0}^{2\pi }dt=i(2\pi -0)=2\pi i.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5366d542b5b6582deeb49aed6e19002471687356)

This is a typical result of [Cauchy's integral formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy%27s_integral_formula "Cauchy's integral formula") and the [residue theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Residue_theorem "Residue theorem").

### Relation of complex line integral and line integral of vector field

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Line_integral&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Relation of complex line integral and line integral of vector field")]

Viewing complex numbers as 2-dimensional [vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector"), the line integral of a complex-valued function ![Image 47: {\displaystyle f(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d8dd568d570b390c337c0a911f0a1c5c214e8240) has real and complex parts equal to the line integral and the flux integral of the vector field corresponding to the [conjugate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_conjugate "Complex conjugate") function ![Image 48: {\displaystyle {\overline {f(z)}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cf07f37ed4db05d851bca17e5ad0e58030437bd) Specifically, if ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} (t)=(x(t),y(t))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/00cd8d50d7668a850c7fa784958009e08ab87eb3) parametrizes _L_, and ![Image 50: {\displaystyle f(z)=u(z)+iv(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aadcde493bbff896c524f30d2b09a14cb5f0523f) corresponds to the vector field ![Image 51: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} (x,y)={\overline {f(x+iy)}}=(u(x+iy),-v(x+iy)),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a93167e5519e698ef0fb83dff48c917b89f501ed) then: ![Image 52: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\int _{L}f(z)\,dz&=\int _{L}(u+iv)(dx+i\,dy)\\&=\int _{L}(u,-v)\cdot (dx,dy)+i\int _{L}(u,-v)\cdot (dy,-dx)\\&=\int _{L}\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {r} )\cdot d\mathbf {r} +i\int _{L}\mathbf {F} (\mathbf {r} )\cdot d\mathbf {r} ^{\perp }.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/55be47e9c9ebc76a500b08037b0ace94214752f8)

By [Cauchy's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy%27s_integral_theorem "Cauchy's integral theorem"), the left-hand integral is zero when ![Image 53: {\displaystyle f(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d8dd568d570b390c337c0a911f0a1c5c214e8240) is analytic (satisfying the [Cauchy–Riemann equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy%E2%80%93Riemann_equations "Cauchy–Riemann equations")) for any smooth closed curve L. Correspondingly, by [Green's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green%27s_theorem "Green's theorem"), the right-hand integrals are zero when ![Image 54: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} ={\overline {f(z)}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29eec9c6295c30e3c282ae12505df8774f3dcbc0) is [irrotational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservative_vector_field "Conservative vector field") ([curl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curl_(mathematics) "Curl (mathematics)")-free) and [incompressible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incompressible_flow "Incompressible flow") ([divergence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence "Divergence")-free). In fact, the Cauchy-Riemann equations for ![Image 55: {\displaystyle f(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d8dd568d570b390c337c0a911f0a1c5c214e8240) are identical to the vanishing of curl and divergence for **F**.

By [Green's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green%27s_theorem "Green's theorem"), the area of a region enclosed by a smooth, closed, positively oriented curve ![Image 56: {\displaystyle L}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/103168b86f781fe6e9a4a87b8ea1cebe0ad4ede8) is given by the integral ![Image 57: {\textstyle {\frac {1}{2i}}\int _{L}{\overline {z}}\,dz.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/daf66ee4a41dfa444e30e277654b7f30501bd0c6) This fact is used, for example, in the proof of the [area theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area_theorem_(conformal_mapping) "Area theorem (conformal mapping)").

The [path integral formulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Path_integral_formulation "Path integral formulation") of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") actually refers not to path integrals in this sense but to [functional integrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_integration "Functional integration"), that is, integrals over a space of paths, of a function _of_ a possible path. However, path integrals in the sense of this article are important in quantum mechanics; for example, complex contour integration is often used in evaluating [probability amplitudes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_amplitude "Probability amplitude") in quantum [scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scattering "Scattering") theory.
