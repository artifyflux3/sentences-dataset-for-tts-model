**Tangential speed** is the [speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed "Speed") of an object undergoing [circular motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_motion "Circular motion"), i.e., moving along a [circular path](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_path "Circular path").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangential_speed#cite_note-Hewitt_2007,_p._131-1) A point on the outside edge of a [merry-go-round](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Merry-go-round "Merry-go-round") or [turntable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turntable "Turntable") travels a greater distance in one complete [rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_(unit) "Rotation (unit)") than a point nearer the center. Travelling a greater distance in the same time means a greater speed, and so linear speed is greater on the outer edge of a rotating object than it is closer to the axis. This speed along a circular path is known as _tangential speed_ because the direction of motion is [tangent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_lines_to_circles "Tangent lines to circles") to the [circumference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumference "Circumference") of the circle. For circular motion, the terms linear speed and tangential speed are used interchangeably, and is measured in [SI units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_units "SI units") as meters per second (m/s).

Formula
-------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tangential_speed&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Formula")]

[Rotational speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_speed "Rotational speed") (or rotational frequency) measures the number of revolutions per unit of time. All parts of a rigid merry-go-round or turntable turn about the axis of rotation in the same amount of time. Thus, all parts share the same rate of rotation, or the same number of rotations or revolutions per unit of time. When a direction is assigned to rotational speed, it is known as [rotational velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_velocity "Rotational velocity"), a vector whose magnitude is the rotational speed. ([Angular speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_speed "Angular speed") and [angular velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity") are related to the rotational speed and velocity by a factor of 2 π, the number of [radians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radians "Radians") turned in a full rotation.)

Tangential speed and rotational speed are related: the faster an object rotates around an axis, the larger the speed. Tangential speed is directly proportional to rotational speed at any fixed distance from the axis of rotation.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangential_speed#cite_note-Hewitt_2007,_p._131-1) However, tangential speed, unlike rotational speed, depends on radial distance (the distance from the axis). For a platform rotating with a fixed rotational speed, the tangential speed in the centre is zero. Towards the edge of the platform the tangential speed increases proportional to the distance from the axis.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangential_speed#cite_note-2) In equation form: v∝r ω,{\displaystyle v\propto \!\,r\omega \,,}![Image 34: {\displaystyle v\propto \!\,r\omega \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9eccc1073384a6cea6b502291ae14556031a3c6b)

where _v_ is tangential speed and _ω_ (Greek letter [omega](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omega "Omega")) is rotational speed. One moves faster if the rate of rotation increases (a larger value for _ω_), and one also moves faster if movement farther from the axis occurs (a larger value for _r_). Move twice as far from the rotational axis at the centre and you move twice as fast. Move out three times as far, and you have three times as much tangential speed. In any kind of rotating system, tangential speed depends on how far you are from the axis of rotation.

When proper units are used for tangential speed _v_, rotational speed _ω_, and radial distance _r_, the direct proportion of _v_ to both _r_ and _ω_ becomes the exact equation v=r ω.{\displaystyle v=r\omega \,.}![Image 35: {\displaystyle v=r\omega \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a983029bacb4456d2c7d778b4cf2ea26ae921f8c)This comes from the following: the linear (tangential) velocity of an object in rotation is the rate at which it covers the circumference's length:

v=2 π r T{\displaystyle v={\frac {2\pi r}{T}}}![Image 36: {\displaystyle v={\frac {2\pi r}{T}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61a34fbd3e44c668ab82c6016d64d493be0f7596)
The angular velocity ω{\textstyle \omega }![Image 37: {\textstyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1523c4698dfe0e3768ec5569d5d06b94b4119b1f) is defined as 2 π/T{\displaystyle 2\pi /T}![Image 38: {\displaystyle 2\pi /T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b46e96e91f5fa4a8480b0886f8bda891af41d50c), where _T_ is the [rotation period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_period_(physics) "Rotation period (physics)"), hence v=ω r{\displaystyle v=\omega r}![Image 39: {\displaystyle v=\omega r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5edc54db08559dc3e33cb3cc568126e12b46521).

Thus, tangential speed will be directly proportional to _r_ when all parts of a system simultaneously have the same _ω_, as for a wheel, disk, or rigid wand.
