**Clifford Alan Pickover** (born August 15, 1957) is an American author, editor, and columnist in the fields of [science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science "Science"), [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), [science fiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_fiction "Science fiction"), [innovation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Innovation "Innovation"), and [creativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creativity "Creativity"). For many years, he was employed at the [IBM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Business_Machines "International Business Machines")[Thomas J. Watson Research Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_J._Watson_Research_Center "Thomas J. Watson Research Center") in [Yorktown, New York](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yorktown,_New_York "Yorktown, New York"), where he was editor-in-chief of the _[IBM Journal of Research and Development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IBM\_Journal\_of\_Research\_and\_Development "IBM Journal of Research and Development")_. He has been granted more than 700 U.S. patents, is an elected Fellow for the [Committee for Skeptical Inquiry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Committee_for_Skeptical_Inquiry "Committee for Skeptical Inquiry"), and is author of more than 50 books, translated into more than a dozen languages.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-pickover.com-1)

Life, education and career
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Clifford_A._Pickover&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Life, education and career")]

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f4/24-cell.gif/250px-24-cell.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:24-cell.gif)

 Pickover's works have often dealt with higher dimensions, computer art, and visualization.

Pickover graduated first in his class from [Franklin and Marshall College](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franklin_and_Marshall_College "Franklin and Marshall College"), after completing the four-year undergraduate program in three years. He received his [PhD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PhD "PhD") in 1982 from [Yale University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yale_University "Yale University")'s Department of [Molecular Biophysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_Biophysics "Molecular Biophysics") and [Biochemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biochemistry "Biochemistry"), where he conducted research on [X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray") scattering and protein structure.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-BIOG08-2)

Pickover was elected as a Fellow for the [Committee for Skeptical Inquiry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Committee_for_Skeptical_Inquiry "Committee for Skeptical Inquiry") for his "significant contributions to the general public's understanding of science, reason, and critical inquiry through their scholarship, writing, and work in the media."[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-3) Other Fellows have included [Carl Sagan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Sagan "Carl Sagan") and [Isaac Asimov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Asimov "Isaac Asimov"). He has been awarded almost 700 United States patents,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-pickover.com-1) and his _[The Math Book](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Math\_Book "The Math Book")_ was winner of the 2011 [Neumann Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Society_for_the_History_of_Mathematics#Neumann_Prize "British Society for the History of Mathematics").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-4)

He joined IBM at the [Thomas J. Watson Research Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_J._Watson_Research_Center "Thomas J. Watson Research Center") in 1982, as a member of the [speech synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speech_synthesis "Speech synthesis") group and later worked on the [design-automation](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Design-automation&action=edit&redlink=1 "Design-automation (page does not exist)") workstations.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-5) For much of his career, Pickover has published technical articles in the areas of [scientific visualization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_visualization "Scientific visualization"), [computer art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_art "Computer art"), and [recreational mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recreational_mathematics "Recreational mathematics").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-BIOG08-2)

He is currently an associate editor for the scientific journal _[Computers and Graphics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computers\_and\_Graphics "Computers and Graphics")_ and is an editorial board member for _Odyssey_ and _Leonardo_. He is also the Brain-Strain columnist for _Odyssey_ magazine, and, for many years, he was the Brain-Boggler columnist for [_Discover_ magazine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discover_Magazine "Discover Magazine").

Pickover has received more than 100 IBM invention achievement awards, three research division awards, and four external honor awards.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-BIOG08-2)

 In the 1990s, Pickover created virtual caverns from extremely simple numerical simulations that reminded him of the [Lechuguilla Cave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lechuguilla_Cave "Lechuguilla Cave"), pictured here.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-7)

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Pickover-stalks.png/250px-Pickover-stalks.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pickover-stalks.png)

Example of Pickover stalks in a detail of the Mandelbrot set

Pickover's primary interest is in finding new ways to expand creativity by melding art, science, mathematics, and other seemingly disparate areas of human endeavor.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-8) In _The Math Book_ and his companion book _The Physics Book_, Pickover explains that both mathematics and physics "cultivate a perpetual state of wonder about the limits of thoughts, the workings of the universe, and our place in the vast space-time landscape that we call home."[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-9) Pickover is an inventor with over 700 [patents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patent "Patent"), the author of puzzle calendars, and puzzle contributor to magazines geared to children and adults. His Neoreality and Heaven Virus science-fiction series explores the fabric of reality and religion.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-BIOG08-2)

Pickover is author of hundreds of technical papers in diverse fields, ranging from the creative visualizations of fossil seashells,[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-IllertPickover1992-10) genetic sequences,[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-12) cardiac[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-13) and speech sounds, and virtual caverns[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-14) and [lava lamps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lava_lamps "Lava lamps"),[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-Pickover1993-15) to fractal and mathematically based studies.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-Pickover1997-16)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-Pickover1995a-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-Pickover1995b-18)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-Pickover1995c-19) He also has published articles in the areas of skepticism (e.g. ESP and Nostradamus), psychology (e.g. temporal lobe epilepsy and genius), and technical speculation (e.g. "What if scientists had found a computer in 1900?" and "An informal survey on the scientific and social impact of a soda can-sized super-super computer").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-20) Additional visualization work includes topics that involve breathing motions of proteins,[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-21) snow-flake like patterns for speech sounds,[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-22) cartoon-face representations of data,[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-23) and biomorphs.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-24)

Pickover has also written extensively on the reported experiences of people on the psychotropic compound [DMT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimethyltryptamine "Dimethyltryptamine").[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-sprott.physics.wisc.edu-25)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-ReferenceA-26) Such apparent entities as [Machine Elves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine_Elves "Machine Elves") are described as well as "Insects From A Parallel Universe".[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-ReferenceA-26)

On November 4, 2006, he began [Wikidumper.org](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikidumper.org "Wikidumper.org"), a popular blog featuring articles being considered for deletion by [English Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_Wikipedia "English Wikipedia").

[Pickover stalks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pickover_stalks "Pickover stalks") are certain kinds of details that are empirically found in the [Mandelbrot set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandelbrot_set "Mandelbrot set") in the study of [fractal geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_geometry "Fractal geometry"). In the 1980s, Pickover proposed that experimental mathematicians and computer artists examine the behavior of orbit trajectories for the Mandelbrot set in order to study how closely the orbits of [interior points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interior_points "Interior points") come to the x and y axes in the complex plane. In some renditions of this behavior, the closer that the point approaches, the higher up the color scale, with red denoting the closest approach. The logarithm of the distance is taken to accentuate the details. This work grew from his earlier work with Julia sets and "Pickover biomorphs," the latter of which often resembled microbes.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-LV97-27)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-28)

### Frontiers of Scientific Visualization

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Clifford_A._Pickover&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Frontiers of Scientific Visualization")]

In "Frontiers of Scientific Visualization" (1994) Pickover explored "the art and science of making the unseen workings of nature visible". The books contains contributions on "Fluid flow, fractals, plant growth, genetic sequencing, the configuration of distant galaxies, virtual reality to artistic inspiration", and focuses on use of computers as tools for simulation, art and discovery.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-29)

### Visualizing Biological Information

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Clifford_A._Pickover&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Visualizing Biological Information")]

In "Visualizing Biological Information" (1995) Pickover considered "biological data of all kinds, which is proliferating at an incredible rate". According to Pickover, "if humans attempt to read such data in the form of numbers and letters, they will take in the information at a snail's pace. If the information is rendered graphically, however, human analysts can assimilate it and gain insight much faster. The emphasis of this work is on the novel graphical and musical representation of information containing sequences, such as DNA and amino acid sequences, to help us find hidden pattern and meaning".[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-30)

### Vampire numbers and other mathematical highlights

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Clifford_A._Pickover&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Vampire numbers and other mathematical highlights")]

In mathematics, a [vampire number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vampire_number "Vampire number") or _true vampire number_ is a [composite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composite_number "Composite number")[natural number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number")_v_, with an even number of [digits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerical_digit "Numerical digit")_n_, that can be factored into two [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integers "Integers")_x_ and _y_ each with _n_/2 digits and not both with trailing zeroes, where _v_ contains all the digits from _x_ and from _y_, in any order. _x_ and _y_ are called the _fangs_. As an example, 1260 is a vampire number because it can be expressed as 21 × 60 = 1260. Note that the digits of the factors 21 and 60 can be found, in some scrambled order, in 1260. Similarly, 136,948 is a vampire because 136,948 = 146 × 938.

Vampire numbers first appeared in a 1994 post by Clifford A. Pickover to the [Usenet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Usenet "Usenet") group sci.math, and the article he later wrote was published in chapter 30 of his book _Keys to Infinity_.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-31)

In addition to "Vampire numbers",[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-32) a term Pickover actually coined, he has coined the following terms in the area of mathematics: [Leviathan number](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Leviathan_number&action=edit&redlink=1 "Leviathan number (page does not exist)"),[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-33)[factorion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Factorion "Factorion"),[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-34)[Carotid–Kundalini function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carotid%E2%80%93Kundalini_function "Carotid–Kundalini function") and fractal,[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-35) batrachion,[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-36)[Juggler sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juggler_sequence "Juggler sequence"),[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-37) and Legion's number,[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-38) among others. For characterizing noisy data, he has used [Truchet tiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truchet_tiles "Truchet tiles") and Noise spheres,[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-39) the later of which is a term he coined for a particular mapping, and visualization, of noisy data to spherical coordinates.

In 1990, he asked "Is There a Double Smoothly Undulating Integer?",[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-40) and he computed "All Known Replicating Fibonacci Digits Less than One Billion".[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-41) With his colleague [John R. Hendricks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_R._Hendricks "John R. Hendricks"), he was the first to compute the smallest perfect [(nasik) magic tesseract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nasik_magic_hypercube "Nasik magic hypercube").[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-42) The "Pickover sequence" dealing with e and pi was named after him,[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-43) as was the "Cliff random number generator"[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-44) and the Pickover attractor, sometimes also referred to as the Clifford Attractor.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-45)[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-46)

### Culture, religion, belief

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Clifford_A._Pickover&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Culture, religion, belief")]

Starting in about 2001, Pickover's books sometimes began to include topics beyond his traditional focus on science and mathematics. For example, _Dreaming the Future_ discusses various methods of divination that humans have used since stone-age times. _The Paradox of God_ deals with topics in religion. Perhaps the most obvious departure from his earlier works includes _Sex, Drugs, Einstein, and Elves: Sushi, Psychedelics, Parallel Universes, and the Quest for Transcendence_, which explores the "borderlands of science" and is "part memoir and part surrealist perspective on culture.".[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-47) Pickover follows-up his "quest for transcendence" and examination of popular culture with _A Beginner's Guide to Immortality: Extraordinary People, Alien Brains, and Quantum Resurrection_.

### History of science and mathematics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Clifford_A._Pickover&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: History of science and mathematics")]

Starting in 2008, Pickover's books began to focus on the history of science and mathematics, with such titles as _[Archimedes to Hawking](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Archimedes\_to\_Hawking&action=edit&redlink=1 "Archimedes to Hawking (page does not exist)")_, as well as _[The Math Book](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Math\_Book "The Math Book")_, _[The Physics Book](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=The\_Physics\_Book&action=edit&redlink=1 "The Physics Book (page does not exist)")_, and _[The Medical Book](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=The\_Medical\_Book&action=edit&redlink=1 "The Medical Book (page does not exist)")_—a trilogy of more than 1,500 pages that presents various historical milestones, breakthroughs, and curiosities.

[Wikidumper.org](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikidumper.org "Wikidumper.org") is a website created by Pickover that promises to permanently record a snapshot of the "best of the English Wikipedia rejects", articles that are slated for deletion at the English Wikipedia. WikiDumper was launched on November 4, 2006, and accepts user submissions. Although the site doesn't specify its criteria for inclusion, many of its articles don't [cite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citation "Citation") their sources. The site has been criticized as likely to be less accurate than English Wikipedia.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_A._Pickover#cite_note-48)
