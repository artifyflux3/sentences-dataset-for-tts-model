Title: Tensor algebra

Published Time: 2003-11-11T17:11:10Z

Markdown Content:
In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **tensor algebra** of a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space")_V_, denoted _T_(_V_) or _T_•(_V_), is the [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra_over_a_field "Algebra over a field") of [tensors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") on _V_ (of any rank) with multiplication being the [tensor product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product "Tensor product"). It is the [free algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_algebra "Free algebra") on _V_, in the sense of being [left adjoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left_adjoint "Left adjoint") to the [forgetful functor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forgetful_functor "Forgetful functor") from algebras to vector spaces: it is the "most general" algebra containing _V_, in the sense of the corresponding [universal property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_property "Universal property") (see [below](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_algebra#Adjunction_and_universal_property)).

The tensor algebra is important because many other algebras arise as [quotient algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_associative_algebra "Quotient associative algebra") of _T_(_V_). These include the [exterior algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_algebra "Exterior algebra"), the [symmetric algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_algebra "Symmetric algebra"), [Clifford algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_algebra "Clifford algebra"), the [Weyl algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weyl_algebra "Weyl algebra") and [universal enveloping algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_enveloping_algebra "Universal enveloping algebra").

The tensor algebra also has two [coalgebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coalgebra "Coalgebra") structures; one simple one, which does not make it a bi-algebra, but does lead to the concept of a [cofree coalgebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cofree_coalgebra "Cofree coalgebra"), and a more complicated one, which yields a [bialgebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bialgebra "Bialgebra"), and can be extended by giving an antipode to create a [Hopf algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hopf_algebra "Hopf algebra") structure.

_Note_: In this article, all algebras are assumed to be [unital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unital_algebra "Unital algebra") and [associative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_algebra "Associative algebra"). The unit is explicitly required to define the [coproduct](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coproduct "Coproduct").

Let _V_ be a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") over a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)")_K_. For any nonnegative [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer")_k_, we define the **_k_ th tensor power** of _V_ to be the [tensor product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product "Tensor product") of _V_ with itself _k_ times:

![Image 1: {\displaystyle T^{k}V=V^{\otimes k}=V\otimes V\otimes \cdots \otimes V.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f2281dbd92db9b20688a64fb57f5e4e461cc86b)
That is, _T_ _k_ _V_ consists of all tensors on _V_ of [order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_order "Tensor order")_k_. By convention _T_ 0 _V_ is the [ground field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_field "Ground field")_K_ (as a one-dimensional vector space over itself).

We then construct _T_(_V_) as the [direct sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_sum_of_vector_spaces "Direct sum of vector spaces") of _T_ _k_ _V_ for _k_ = 0,1,2,…

![Image 2: {\displaystyle T(V)=\bigoplus _{k=0}^{\infty }T^{k}V=K\oplus V\oplus (V\otimes V)\oplus (V\otimes V\otimes V)\oplus \cdots .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b71d92c0bd0dca559eabe9887d5b538c491aab8c)
The multiplication in _T_(_V_) is determined by the canonical isomorphism

![Image 3: {\displaystyle T^{k}V\otimes T^{\ell }V\to T^{k+\ell }V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e967dfe0bb14b59f198ed2a5c3c43155ab720de2)
given by the tensor product, which is then extended by linearity to all of _T_(_V_). This multiplication rule implies that the tensor algebra _T_(_V_) is naturally a [graded algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graded_algebra "Graded algebra") with _T_ _k_ _V_ serving as the grade-_k_ subspace. This grading can be extended to a **Z**-grading by appending subspaces ![Image 4: {\displaystyle T^{k}V=\{0\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16ea5ab72882afb17c196b7ef07ed56c9dda6fe0) for negative integers _k_.

The construction generalizes in a straightforward manner to the tensor algebra of any [module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_(mathematics) "Module (mathematics)")_M_ over a [_commutative_ ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring "Commutative ring"). If _R_ is a [non-commutative ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-commutative_ring "Non-commutative ring"), one can still perform the construction for any _R_-_R_[bimodule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bimodule "Bimodule")_M_. (It does not work for ordinary _R_-modules because the iterated tensor products cannot be formed.)

Adjunction and universal property
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_algebra&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Adjunction and universal property")]

The tensor algebra _T_(_V_) is also called the **[free algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_algebra "Free algebra")** on the vector space _V_, and is [functorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functor "Functor"); this means that the map ![Image 5: {\displaystyle V\mapsto T(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c477068e69e40d94362cb7a8c2defd3e5d887cfe) extends to [linear maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map") for forming a _functor_ from the [category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_(mathematics) "Category (mathematics)") of K-vector spaces to the category of [associative algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_algebra "Associative algebra"). Similarly with other [free constructions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_object "Free object"), the functor _T_ is [left adjoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left_adjoint "Left adjoint") to the [forgetful functor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forgetful_functor "Forgetful functor") that sends each associative _K_-algebra to its underlying vector space.

Explicitly, the tensor algebra satisfies the following [universal property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_property "Universal property"), which formally expresses the statement that it is the most general algebra containing _V_:

Any [linear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map")![Image 6: {\displaystyle f:V\to A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/776a9bcb7ffd8cadecbc2b650be9748bc3b0e399) from _V_ to an associative algebra _A_ over _K_ can be uniquely extended to an [algebra homomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra_homomorphism "Algebra homomorphism") from _T_(_V_) to _A_ as indicated by the following [commutative diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_diagram "Commutative diagram"):[![Image 7: Universal property of the tensor algebra](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/9/91/TensorAlgebra-01.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:TensorAlgebra-01.png "Universal property of the tensor algebra")

Universal property of the tensor algebra

Here _i_ is the [canonical inclusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inclusion_map "Inclusion map") of _V_ into _T_(_V_). As for other universal properties, the tensor algebra _T_(_V_) can be defined as the unique algebra satisfying this property (specifically, it is unique [up to](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Up_to "Up to") a unique isomorphism), but this definition requires to prove that an object satisfying this property exists.

The above universal property implies that _T_ is a [functor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functor "Functor") from the [category of vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_of_vector_spaces "Category of vector spaces") over _K_, to the category of _K_-algebras. This means that any linear map between _K_-vector spaces _U_ and _W_ extends uniquely to a _K_-algebra homomorphism from _T_(_U_) to _T_(_W_).

Non-commutative polynomials
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_algebra&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Non-commutative polynomials")]

If _V_ has finite dimension _n_, another way of looking at the tensor algebra is as the "algebra of polynomials over _K_ in _n_ non-commuting variables". If we take [basis vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_vector "Basis vector") for _V_, those become non-commuting variables (or [_indeterminates_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indeterminate_(variable) "Indeterminate (variable)")) in _T_(_V_), subject to no constraints beyond [associativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associativity "Associativity"), the [distributive law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributive_law "Distributive law") and _K_-linearity.

Note that the algebra of polynomials on _V_ is not ![Image 8: {\displaystyle T(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5feceaba68f861e58c03bd211c11e4c3689dbf2), but rather ![Image 9: {\displaystyle T(V^{*})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/098cf5c018e4a839edaa0119454c9be3eb80f839): a (homogeneous) linear function on _V_ is an element of ![Image 10: {\displaystyle V^{*},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88213f842ca974a6f411b4965e5a14e51a3cdb24) for example coordinates ![Image 11: {\displaystyle x^{1},\dots ,x^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/462789b52402e9fe9bebc4629d371f8d8ab49a9d) on a vector space are [covectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_vector "Covariant vector"), as they take in a vector and give out a scalar (the given coordinate of the vector).

Because of the generality of the tensor algebra, many other algebras of interest can be constructed by starting with the tensor algebra and then imposing certain relations on the generators, i.e. by constructing certain [quotient algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_associative_algebra "Quotient associative algebra") of _T_(_V_). Examples of this are the [exterior algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_algebra "Exterior algebra"), the [symmetric algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_algebra "Symmetric algebra"), [Clifford algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_algebra "Clifford algebra"), the [Weyl algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weyl_algebra "Weyl algebra") and [universal enveloping algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_enveloping_algebra "Universal enveloping algebra").

The tensor algebra has two different [coalgebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coalgebra "Coalgebra") structures. One is compatible with the tensor product, and thus can be extended to a [bialgebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bialgebra "Bialgebra"), and can be further be extended with an antipode to a [Hopf algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hopf_algebra "Hopf algebra") structure. The other structure, although simpler, cannot be extended to a bialgebra. The first structure is developed immediately below; the second structure is given in the section on the [cofree coalgebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cofree_coalgebra "Cofree coalgebra"), further down.

The development provided below can be equally well applied to the [exterior algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_algebra "Exterior algebra"), using the wedge symbol ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \wedge }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1caa4004cb216ef2930bb12fe805a76870caed94) in place of the tensor symbol ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \otimes }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de29098f5a34ee296a505681a0d5e875070f2aea); a sign must also be kept track of, when permuting elements of the exterior algebra. This correspondence also lasts through the definition of the bialgebra, and on to the definition of a Hopf algebra. That is, the exterior algebra can also be given a Hopf algebra structure.

Similarly, the [symmetric algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_algebra "Symmetric algebra") can also be given the structure of a Hopf algebra, in exactly the same fashion, by replacing everywhere the tensor product ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \otimes }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de29098f5a34ee296a505681a0d5e875070f2aea) by the symmetrized tensor product ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \otimes _{\mathrm {Sym} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/775942c56f60ea3bc6e50a1a67452334e1c985ba), i.e. that product where ![Image 16: {\displaystyle v\otimes _{\mathrm {Sym} }w=w\otimes _{\mathrm {Sym} }v.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2de6dbd3b4408fe6e824cef448345345ac86be24)

In each case, this is possible because the alternating product ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \wedge }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1caa4004cb216ef2930bb12fe805a76870caed94) and the symmetric product ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \otimes _{\mathrm {Sym} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/775942c56f60ea3bc6e50a1a67452334e1c985ba) obey the required consistency conditions for the definition of a bialgebra and Hopf algebra; this can be explicitly checked in the manner below. Whenever one has a product obeying these consistency conditions, the construction goes through; insofar as such a product gave rise to a quotient space, the quotient space inherits the Hopf algebra structure.

In the language of [category theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_theory "Category theory"), one says that there is a [functor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functor "Functor")_T_ from the category of _K_-vector spaces to the category of _K_-associative algebras. But there is also a functor _Λ_ taking vector spaces to the category of exterior algebras, and a functor _Sym_ taking vector spaces to symmetric algebras. There is a [natural map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_transformation "Natural transformation") from _T_ to each of these. Verifying that quotienting preserves the Hopf algebra structure is the same as verifying that the maps are indeed natural.

The coalgebra is obtained by defining a [coproduct](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coproduct "Coproduct") or diagonal operator

It is being used to distinguish it from the "internal" tensor product ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \otimes }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de29098f5a34ee296a505681a0d5e875070f2aea), which is already being used to denote multiplication in the tensor algebra (see the section _Multiplication_, below, for further clarification on this issue). Equivalently,

![Image 57: {\displaystyle \Delta (v_{1}\otimes \cdots \otimes v_{n})=\sum _{S\subseteq \{1,\dots ,n\}}\left(\prod _{k=1 \atop k\in S}^{n}v_{k}\right)\boxtimes \left(\prod _{k=1 \atop k\notin S}^{n}v_{k}\right)\!,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a1f40f5505dfc2e35ff06f896c1417410d8ecf75)
where the products are in ![Image 58: {\displaystyle TV}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d63b391c53d45238bd3fdf4e73a37abc6fe8209f), and where the sum is over all subsets of ![Image 59: {\displaystyle \{1,\dots ,n\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b98091026e0b10ee08a7c77e229c649f81b0a05).

As before, the algebra grading is preserved:

![Image 60: {\displaystyle \Delta :T^{m}V\to \bigoplus _{k=0}^{m}T^{k}V\boxtimes T^{(m-k)}V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/14eb23a9412cf1cd974f9494deb9668a66997455)
The counit ![Image 61: {\displaystyle \epsilon :TV\to K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61cc2d2c48f0033a30074530014a9aea50848c58) is given by the projection of the field component out from the algebra. This can be written as ![Image 62: {\displaystyle \epsilon :v\mapsto 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c24b0675d6e3c9ce20ee1eca7d81b4c9d695fc) for ![Image 63: {\displaystyle v\in V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99886ebbde63daa0224fb9bf56fa11b3c8a6f4fb) and ![Image 64: {\displaystyle \epsilon :k\mapsto k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e9578918c3bfd89f2612104ef92388c2b6a128) for ![Image 65: {\displaystyle k\in K=T^{0}V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ed35748946477c4d838b0f2d83e6dd58913bdb6). By homomorphism under the tensor product ![Image 66: {\displaystyle \otimes }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de29098f5a34ee296a505681a0d5e875070f2aea), this extends to

![Image 67: {\displaystyle \epsilon :x\mapsto 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/824e60a48839dd9ed67b32d1c693d685e1780e47)
for all ![Image 68: {\displaystyle x\in T^{1}V\oplus T^{2}V\oplus \cdots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/179f8593a42570b268738c5dd9eb311943644e69) It is a straightforward matter to verify that this counit satisfies the needed axiom for the coalgebra:

![Image 69: {\displaystyle (\mathrm {id} \boxtimes \epsilon )\circ \Delta =\mathrm {id} =(\epsilon \boxtimes \mathrm {id} )\circ \Delta .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4ec6483755ae886620a410767fea3e33eb41a859)
Working this explicitly, one has

![Image 70: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}((\mathrm {id} \boxtimes \epsilon )\circ \Delta )(x)&=(\mathrm {id} \boxtimes \epsilon )(1\boxtimes x+x\boxtimes 1)\\&=1\boxtimes \epsilon (x)+x\boxtimes \epsilon (1)\\&=0+x\boxtimes 1\\&\cong x\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3dffcfa6e3cb13d1036688018f8c2c99aa194418)
where, for the last step, one has made use of the isomorphism ![Image 71: {\displaystyle TV\boxtimes K\cong TV}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/62f5748e515d32be13e56e0f2db67b3a97ad33fe), as is appropriate for the defining axiom of the counit.

A [bialgebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bialgebra "Bialgebra") defines both multiplication, and comultiplication, and requires them to be compatible.

Multiplication is given by an operator

![Image 72: {\displaystyle \nabla :TV\boxtimes TV\to TV}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71b1ec6257c423c4c05174f7e2842d7410e2fba1)
which, in this case, was already given as the "internal" tensor product. That is,

![Image 73: {\displaystyle \nabla :x\boxtimes y\mapsto x\otimes y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/efff81a91faf9d13fec05ab9950b854de1e6f37e)
That is, ![Image 74: {\displaystyle \nabla (x\boxtimes y)=x\otimes y.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/57b1b820daba5bf5c087ae63dae70ced3a6a71ce) The above should make it clear why the ![Image 75: {\displaystyle \boxtimes }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0cfc27ef694886c0b78697df1cd69558cdf53eff) symbol needs to be used: the ![Image 76: {\displaystyle \otimes }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de29098f5a34ee296a505681a0d5e875070f2aea) was actually one and the same thing as ![Image 77: {\displaystyle \nabla }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3d0e93b78c50237f9ea83d027e4ebbdaef354b2); and notational sloppiness here would lead to utter chaos. To strengthen this: the tensor product ![Image 78: {\displaystyle \otimes }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de29098f5a34ee296a505681a0d5e875070f2aea) of the tensor algebra corresponds to the multiplication ![Image 79: {\displaystyle \nabla }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3d0e93b78c50237f9ea83d027e4ebbdaef354b2) used in the definition of an algebra, whereas the tensor product ![Image 80: {\displaystyle \boxtimes }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0cfc27ef694886c0b78697df1cd69558cdf53eff) is the one required in the definition of comultiplication in a coalgebra. These two tensor products are _not_ the same thing!

The unit for the algebra

![Image 81: {\displaystyle \eta :K\to TV}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/054fe67a3391136bf7a6b960ba15f97f37a7bac3)
is just the embedding, so that

![Image 82: {\displaystyle \eta :k\mapsto k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0bfbee10a5aac43bab5932eb4c6fbb90bac3cc4)
That the unit is compatible with the tensor product ![Image 83: {\displaystyle \otimes }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de29098f5a34ee296a505681a0d5e875070f2aea) is "trivial": it is just part of the standard definition of the tensor product of vector spaces. That is, ![Image 84: {\displaystyle k\otimes x=kx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be270e58c6e05cf8839e2edd3d2ddb7d520258c5) for field element _k_ and any ![Image 85: {\displaystyle x\in TV.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ce26a359c47b3aaf64576ea1c38f5c691b726e4b) More verbosely, the axioms for an [associative algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_algebra "Associative algebra") require the two homomorphisms (or commuting diagrams):

![Image 86: {\displaystyle \nabla \circ (\eta \boxtimes \mathrm {id} _{TV})=\eta \otimes \mathrm {id} _{TV}=\eta \cdot \mathrm {id} _{TV}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa82ea473ca89fd43e904fb21ff2184637b76257)
on ![Image 87: {\displaystyle K\boxtimes TV}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d6e418d3a42a3be623b9bf9cbe15ad06dfffe96), and that symmetrically, on ![Image 88: {\displaystyle TV\boxtimes K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b97f07876431e9f5dab6098b8d4182475ddb012), that

![Image 89: {\displaystyle \nabla \circ (\mathrm {id} _{TV}\boxtimes \eta )=\mathrm {id} _{TV}\otimes \eta =\mathrm {id} _{TV}\cdot \eta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea94cce88bd9c1634e1c0e9d736415658b6070f6)
where the right-hand side of these equations should be understood as the scalar product.

The unit and counit, and multiplication and comultiplication, all have to satisfy compatibility conditions. It is straightforward to see that

![Image 90: {\displaystyle \epsilon \circ \eta =\mathrm {id} _{K}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9babc072998bd53a1f9cf77915de7fb360f2c25d)
Similarly, the unit is compatible with comultiplication:

![Image 91: {\displaystyle \Delta \circ \eta =\eta \boxtimes \eta \cong \eta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b4e8a20ea00dfcf989200430fe03dba18339b6a)
The above requires the use of the isomorphism ![Image 92: {\displaystyle K\boxtimes K\cong K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d35084ae19d25c4b5458fefb296f5b8c46630139) in order to work; without this, one loses linearity. Component-wise,

![Image 93: {\displaystyle (\Delta \circ \eta )(k)=\Delta (k)=k(1\boxtimes 1)\cong k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71f7bc87b72eaaf1d41ee53c4aad865596aa7836)
with the right-hand side making use of the isomorphism.

Multiplication and the counit are compatible:

![Image 94: {\displaystyle (\epsilon \circ \nabla )(x\boxtimes y)=\epsilon (x\otimes y)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b6edee4da9a1c29662badd093ca36cb078b95f71)
whenever _x_ or _y_ are not elements of ![Image 95: {\displaystyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76fce82a62ed5461908f0dc8f037de4e3686b0), and otherwise, one has scalar multiplication on the field: ![Image 96: {\displaystyle k_{1}\otimes k_{2}=k_{1}k_{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9ce11827112fdf86a004167f6b32024562f3e80a) The most difficult to verify is the compatibility of multiplication and comultiplication:

![Image 97: {\displaystyle \Delta \circ \nabla =(\nabla \boxtimes \nabla )\circ (\mathrm {id} \boxtimes \tau \boxtimes \mathrm {id} )\circ (\Delta \boxtimes \Delta )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/942081ee2c4170f5d9f0b17d5c675ae30e9aa931)
where ![Image 98: {\displaystyle \tau (x\boxtimes y)=y\boxtimes x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/42ca04d2678684fb3b77a308fd876747442b25bb) exchanges elements. The compatibility condition only needs to be verified on ![Image 99: {\displaystyle V\subset TV}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/46132ce37027a297f362cc88a6af85b17c154731); the full compatibility follows as a homomorphic extension to all of ![Image 100: {\displaystyle TV.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89e4023ca6ae703f027945d4b955a28aba533869) The verification is verbose but straightforward; it is not given here, except for the final result:

![Image 101: {\displaystyle (\Delta \circ \nabla )(v\boxtimes w)=\Delta (v\otimes w)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e10351b4e0d4b836541a769634a03b5c1d0a0891)
For ![Image 102: {\displaystyle v,w\in V,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/78abee61c96d098e6a98631cd5714036a330d683) an explicit expression for this was given in the coalgebra section, above.

The [Hopf algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hopf_algebra "Hopf algebra") adds an antipode to the bialgebra axioms. The antipode ![Image 103: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) on ![Image 104: {\displaystyle k\in K=T^{0}V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ed35748946477c4d838b0f2d83e6dd58913bdb6) is given by

![Image 105: {\displaystyle S(k)=k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a186247e66bf5e713bf8ecd3e51c2f064c938ad1)
This is sometimes called the "anti-identity". The antipode on ![Image 106: {\displaystyle v\in V=T^{1}V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a7c0af5af4848082711901973d6912d4fc535829) is given by

![Image 107: {\displaystyle S(v)=-v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7eac24d1523e34714703e416e628e01231504a6)
and on ![Image 108: {\displaystyle v\otimes w\in T^{2}V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa7bc899f590fefde7f752999b4428cbb3d37214) by

![Image 109: {\displaystyle S(v\otimes w)=S(w)\otimes S(v)=w\otimes v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a22c949a7cea4a40c232c7d2218ac4e896a21d7)
This extends homomorphically to

![Image 110: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}S(v_{1}\otimes \cdots \otimes v_{m})&=S(v_{m})\otimes \cdots \otimes S(v_{1})\\&=(-1)^{m}v_{m}\otimes \cdots \otimes v_{1}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29969bc0a33acbb2ef618ee9bb09da2952a2c86d)
Compatibility of the antipode with multiplication and comultiplication requires that

![Image 111: {\displaystyle \nabla \circ (S\boxtimes \mathrm {id} )\circ \Delta =\eta \circ \epsilon =\nabla \circ (\mathrm {id} \boxtimes S)\circ \Delta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b98d4f540c8bee931f3d3863b95ed15f6cbc19ff)
This is straightforward to verify componentwise on ![Image 112: {\displaystyle k\in K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3846561ca971f4b17e10153d1f996e08e5ac192):

![Image 113: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}(\nabla \circ (S\boxtimes \mathrm {id} )\circ \Delta )(k)&=(\nabla \circ (S\boxtimes \mathrm {id} ))(1\boxtimes k)\\&=\nabla (1\boxtimes k)\\&=1\otimes k\\&=k\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4e912ae7c8d864f9086c52759af03d3a107b39f9)
Similarly, on ![Image 114: {\displaystyle v\in V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99886ebbde63daa0224fb9bf56fa11b3c8a6f4fb):

![Image 115: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}(\nabla \circ (S\boxtimes \mathrm {id} )\circ \Delta )(v)&=(\nabla \circ (S\boxtimes \mathrm {id} ))(v\boxtimes 1+1\boxtimes v)\\&=\nabla (-v\boxtimes 1+1\boxtimes v)\\&=-v\otimes 1+1\otimes v\\&=-v+v\\&=0\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/711975283335404dd15c589ce0992ad203b1a81c)
Recall that

![Image 116: {\displaystyle (\eta \circ \epsilon )(k)=\eta (k)=k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b29f36e3027496bd229ce4e74e9f885aed1c7229)
and that

![Image 117: {\displaystyle (\eta \circ \epsilon )(x)=\eta (0)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/720b65c58f1180d8d48ea5d91d986d7e92793614)
for any ![Image 118: {\displaystyle x\in TV}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/108ec515e3e5c0ca39f2de85077d6070974c5946) that is _not_ in ![Image 119: {\displaystyle K.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1bb0e178e42abf16ef4e4c0b0f22aa235ad6e6e5)

One may proceed in a similar manner, by homomorphism, verifying that the antipode inserts the appropriate cancellative signs in the shuffle, starting with the compatibility condition on ![Image 120: {\displaystyle T^{2}V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/879f3eed4102a0013caa6378caa971ef13916cd7) and proceeding by induction.

Cofree cocomplete coalgebra
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_algebra&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Cofree cocomplete coalgebra")]

One may define a different coproduct on the tensor algebra, simpler than the one given above. It is given by

![Image 121: {\displaystyle \Delta (v_{1}\otimes \dots \otimes v_{k}):=\sum _{j=0}^{k}(v_{0}\otimes \dots \otimes v_{j})\boxtimes (v_{j+1}\otimes \dots \otimes v_{k+1})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9f9096fed53ac94cedcddcc1aa76770cfaa05012)
Here, as before, one uses the notational trick ![Image 122: {\displaystyle v_{0}=v_{k+1}=1\in K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffc72d185c7fef23382b507ba032ca19b211c9a8) (recalling that ![Image 123: {\displaystyle v\otimes 1=v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec8ac3c2469644424c1c505dc304569c6dc80d33) trivially).

This coproduct gives rise to a coalgebra. It describes a coalgebra that is [dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duality_(linear_algebra) "Duality (linear algebra)") to the algebra structure on _T_(_V_∗), where _V_∗ denotes the [dual vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_vector_space "Dual vector space") of linear maps _V_ → **F**. In the same way that the tensor algebra is a [free algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_algebra "Free algebra"), the corresponding coalgebra is termed cocomplete co-free. With the usual product this is not a bialgebra. It _can_ be turned into a bialgebra with the product ![Image 124: {\displaystyle v_{i}\cdot v_{j}=(i,j)v_{i+j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1288e4c8bf42f4f32133e66ab1d3108d16646e91) where _(i,j)_ denotes the binomial coefficient for ![Image 125: {\displaystyle {\tbinom {i+j}{i}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a1a4279cf05921e16a40aad08c14bc10ac6e573). This bialgebra is known as the [divided power Hopf algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divided_power_structure "Divided power structure").

The difference between this, and the other coalgebra is most easily seen in the ![Image 126: {\displaystyle T^{2}V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/879f3eed4102a0013caa6378caa971ef13916cd7) term. Here, one has that

![Image 127: {\displaystyle \Delta (v\otimes w)=1\boxtimes (v\otimes w)+v\boxtimes w+(v\otimes w)\boxtimes 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40d22b9dd972efdd13ad7e35471f98daad43b8c8)
for ![Image 128: {\displaystyle v,w\in V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4799d1b026506616abad8de636f46738b8a55a96), which is clearly missing a shuffled term, as compared to before.
