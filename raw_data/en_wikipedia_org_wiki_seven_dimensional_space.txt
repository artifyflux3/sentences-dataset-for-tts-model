In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a sequence of _n_[real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") can be understood as a [location](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)") in _n_-[dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension") space. When _n_ = 7, the set of all such locations is called **7-dimensional space**. Often such a space is studied as a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space"), without any notion of distance. Seven-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") is seven-dimensional space equipped with a [Euclidean metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_metric "Euclidean metric"), which is defined by the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product").[_[disputed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Disputed\_statement "Wikipedia:Disputed statement") â€“ [discuss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Talk:Seven-dimensional\_space "Talk:Seven-dimensional space")_]

More generally, the term may refer to a seven-dimensional vector space over any [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)"), such as a seven-dimensional [complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") vector space, which has 14 real dimensions. It may also refer to a seven-dimensional [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold") such as a [7-sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/7-sphere "7-sphere"), or a variety of other geometric constructions.

Seven-dimensional spaces have a number of special properties, many of them related to the [octonions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion "Octonion"). An especially distinctive property is that a [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product") can be defined only in three or seven dimensions. This is related to [Hurwitz's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurwitz%27s_theorem_(composition_algebras) "Hurwitz's theorem (composition algebras)"), which prohibits the existence of algebraic structures like the [quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion "Quaternion") and octonions in dimensions other than 2, 4, and 8. The first [exotic spheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exotic_sphere "Exotic sphere") ever discovered were seven-dimensional.

A [polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytope "Polytope") in seven dimensions is called a 7-polytope. The most studied are the [regular polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polytope "Regular polytope"), of which there are only [three in seven dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_regular_polytopes#Convex_4 "List of regular polytopes"): the [7-simplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/7-simplex "7-simplex"), [7-cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/7-cube "7-cube"), and [7-orthoplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/7-orthoplex "7-orthoplex"). A wider family are the [uniform 7-polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_7-polytope "Uniform 7-polytope"), constructed from fundamental symmetry domains of reflection, each domain defined by a [Coxeter group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group "Coxeter group"). Each uniform polytope is defined by a ringed [Coxeter-Dynkin diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter-Dynkin_diagram "Coxeter-Dynkin diagram"). The [7-demicube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/7-demicube "7-demicube") is a unique polytope from the D 7 family, and [3 21](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3_21_polytope "3 21 polytope"), [2 31](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2_31_polytope "2 31 polytope"), and [1 32](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1_32_polytope "1 32 polytope") polytopes from the E 7 family.

The [6-sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-sphere "N-sphere") or hypersphere in seven-dimensional Euclidean space is the six-dimensional surface equidistant from a point, e.g. the origin. It has symbol _S 6_, with formal definition for the 6-sphere with radius _r_ of

The volume of the space bounded by this 6-sphere is

![Image 105: {\displaystyle V_{7}\,={\frac {16\pi ^{3}}{105}}\,r^{7}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d2907d9d2f9640f9aef50d34c0ab3193239b57a9)
which is 4.72477 Ã— _r_ 7, or 0.0369 of the [7-cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/7-cube "7-cube") that contains the 6-sphere

A cross product, that is a vector-valued, [bilinear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_map "Bilinear map"), [anticommutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticommutativity "Anticommutativity") and [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonality "Orthogonality") product of two vectors, is defined in seven dimensions. Along with the more usual [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product") in three dimensions it is the only such product, except for trivial products.

In 1956, [John Milnor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Milnor "John Milnor") constructed an [exotic sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exotic_sphere "Exotic sphere") in 7 dimensions and showed that there are at least 7 differentiable structures on the 7-sphere. In 1963 he showed that the exact number of such structures is 28.
