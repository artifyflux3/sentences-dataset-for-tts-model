"Living creatures" redirects here. For the class of heavenly beings in Jewish mythology, see [living creatures (Bible)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Living_creatures_(Bible) "Living creatures (Bible)").

An **organism** is any [living](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life "Life") thing that functions as an [individual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Individual "Individual").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Mosby-2017-1) Such a definition raises more problems than it solves, not least because the concept of an individual is also difficult. Several criteria, few of which are widely accepted, have been proposed to define what constitutes an organism. Among the most common is that an organism has autonomous [reproduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reproduction "Reproduction"), [growth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_growth "Cell growth"), and [metabolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism "Metabolism"). This would exclude [viruses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virus "Virus"), even though they [evolve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution "Evolution") like organisms.

Other problematic cases include [colonial organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colonial_organism "Colonial organism"); a colony of [eusocial insects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eusocial_insect "Eusocial insect") is organised adaptively, and has [germ-soma specialisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germ-Soma_Differentiation "Germ-Soma Differentiation"), with some insects reproducing, others not, like cells in an animal's body. The body of a [siphonophore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siphonophore "Siphonophore"), a jelly-like marine animal, is composed of organism-like [zooids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zooid "Zooid"), but the whole structure looks and functions much like an animal such as a [jellyfish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jellyfish "Jellyfish"), the parts collaborating to provide the functions of the colonial organism.

The evolutionary biologists [David Queller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_C._Queller "David C. Queller") and [Joan Strassmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joan_E._Strassmann "Joan E. Strassmann") state that "organismality", the qualities or attributes that define an entity as an organism, has evolved socially as groups of simpler units (from cells upwards) came to cooperate without conflicts. They propose that cooperation should be used as the "defining trait" of an organism. This would treat many types of collaboration, including the [fungus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungus "Fungus")/[alga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algae "Algae") partnership of different species in a [lichen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lichen "Lichen"), or the permanent sexual partnership of an [anglerfish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anglerfish "Anglerfish"), as an organism.

The term "organism" (from the [Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek")[ὀργανισμός](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BD%80%CF%81%CE%B3%CE%B1%CE%BD%CE%B9%CF%83%CE%BC%CF%8C%CF%82#Ancient_Greek "wikt:ὀργανισμός"), derived from _órganon_, meaning 'instrument, implement, tool', 'organ of sense', or 'apprehension')[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Liddell-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Online-Etym-Dict-3) first appeared in the English language in the 1660s with the now-obsolete meaning of an organic structure or organization.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Online-Etym-Dict-3) It is related to the verb "organize".[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Online-Etym-Dict-3) In his 1790 _[Critique of Judgment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critique\_of\_Judgment "Critique of Judgment")_, [Immanuel Kant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immanuel_Kant "Immanuel Kant") defined an organism as "both an organized and a self-organizing being".[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-5)

Whether criteria exist, or are needed
-------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Organism&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Whether criteria exist, or are needed")]

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Basilikumwurzling.jpg/250px-Basilikumwurzling.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Basilikumwurzling.jpg)

One criterion proposes that an organism cannot be divided without losing functionality.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Rosen-1958-6) This [basil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basil "Basil") plant [cutting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cutting_(plant) "Cutting (plant)") is however developing new adventitious roots from a small bit of [stem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant_stem "Plant stem"), forming a new plant.

Among the criteria that have been proposed for being an organism are:

*   autonomous [reproduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reproduction "Reproduction"), [growth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_growth "Cell growth"), and [metabolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism "Metabolism")[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Moreira-2009-7)
*   noncompartmentability – structure cannot be divided without losing functionality.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Rosen-1958-6)[Richard Dawkins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Dawkins "Richard Dawkins") stated this as "the quality of being sufficiently heterogeneous in form to be rendered non-functional if cut in half".[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Wilson-2000-8) However, many organisms can be cut into pieces which then grow into whole organisms.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Wilson-2000-8)
*   [individuality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Individuality "Individuality") – the entity has simultaneous holdings of genetic uniqueness, genetic homogeneity and [autonomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autonomy "Autonomy")[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-9)
*   an [immune response](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_response "Immune response"), separating self from foreign[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-10)
*   "anti-[entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy "Entropy")", the ability to maintain order, a concept first proposed by [Erwin Schrödinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erwin_Schr%C3%B6dinger "Erwin Schrödinger");[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Bailly-2009-11) or in another form, that [Claude Shannon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claude_Shannon "Claude Shannon")'s [information theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_theory "Information theory") can be used to identify organisms as capable of self-maintaining their information content[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Piast-2019-12)

Other scientists think that the concept of the organism is inadequate in biology;[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-13) that the concept of individuality is problematic;[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Clarke-2010-14) and from a philosophical point of view, question whether such a definition is necessary.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Pepper-2008-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Wilson-2007-16)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Wilson-2000-8)

Problematic cases include [colonial organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colony_(biology) "Colony (biology)"): for instance, a colony of [eusocial insects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eusocial_insects "Eusocial insects") fulfills criteria such as adaptive organisation and [germ-soma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germ-Soma_Differentiation "Germ-Soma Differentiation") specialisation.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Folse-2010-17) If so, the same argument, or a criterion of high co-operation and low conflict, would include some [mutualistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutualism_(biology) "Mutualism (biology)") (e.g. lichens) and sexual partnerships (e.g. [anglerfish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anglerfish "Anglerfish")) as organisms.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Queller-2009-18) If [group selection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_selection "Group selection") occurs, then a group could be viewed as a [superorganism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superorganism "Superorganism"), optimized by group [adaptation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptation "Adaptation").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-19)

Another view is that attributes like autonomy, genetic homogeneity and genetic uniqueness should be examined separately, rather than requiring that an organism possess all of them. On this view, there are multiple dimensions to biological individuality, resulting in several types of organism.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-20)

Organisms at differing levels of biological organisation
--------------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Organism&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Organisms at differing levels of biological organisation")]

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Lichen_cross_section_%E2%80%93_heteromeric_thallus_unlabelled.svg/250px-Lichen_cross_section_%E2%80%93_heteromeric_thallus_unlabelled.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Lichen_cross_section_%E2%80%93_heteromeric_thallus_unlabelled.svg)

A [lichen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lichen "Lichen") consists of a body formed mainly by [fungi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungi "Fungi"), with unicellular [algae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algae "Algae") or [cyanobacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanobacteria "Cyanobacteria") (green) interspersed within the structure, and a bacterial [microbiome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbiome "Microbiome"). The [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species "Species") are mutually interdependent, like cells within a multicellular organism.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-L%C3%BCcking-2021-21)

Differing levels of biological organisation give rise to potentially different understandings of the nature of organisms. A [unicellular organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicellular_organism "Unicellular organism") is a [microorganism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism "Microorganism") such as a [protist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protist "Protist"), [bacterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacterium "Bacterium"), or [archaean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaea "Archaea"), composed of a single [cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(biology) "Cell (biology)"), which may contain functional structures called [organelles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organelle "Organelle").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Hine-2008-22) A [multicellular organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multicellular_organism "Multicellular organism") such as an [animal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal "Animal"), [plant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant "Plant"), [fungus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungus "Fungus"), or [alga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alga "Alga") is composed of many cells, often specialised.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Hine-2008-22) A [colonial organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colony_(biology) "Colony (biology)") such as a [siphonophore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siphonophore "Siphonophore") is a being which functions as an individual but is composed of communicating individuals.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Wilson-2000-8) A [superorganism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superorganism "Superorganism") is a colony, such as of [ants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ant "Ant"), consisting of many individuals working together as a single functional or [social unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_group "Social group").[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Kelly-1994-23)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Folse-2010-17) A [mutualism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutualism_(biology) "Mutualism (biology)") is a partnership of two or more [species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species "Species") which each provide some of the needs of the other. A [lichen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lichen "Lichen") consists of [fungi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungus "Fungus") and [algae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algae "Algae") or [cyanobacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanobacteria "Cyanobacteria"), with a bacterial [microbiome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbiome "Microbiome"); together, they are able to flourish as a kind of organism, the components having different functions, in habitats such as dry rocks where neither could grow alone.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Queller-2009-18)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-L%C3%BCcking-2021-21) The evolutionary biologists [David Queller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_C._Queller "David C. Queller") and [Joan Strassmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joan_E._Strassmann "Joan E. Strassmann") state that "organismality" has evolved socially, as groups of simpler units (from cells upwards) came to cooperate without conflicts. They propose that cooperation should be used as the "defining trait" of an organism.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Queller-2009-18)

Samuel Díaz‐Muñoz and colleagues (2016) accept Queller and Strassmann's view that organismality can be measured wholly by degrees of cooperation and of conflict. They state that this situates organisms in evolutionary time, so that organismality is context dependent. They suggest that highly integrated life forms, which are not context dependent, may evolve through context-dependent stages towards complete unification.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-D%C3%ADaz-Mu%C3%B1oz-2016-24)

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8e/TMV_structure_simple.png/250px-TMV_structure_simple.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:TMV_structure_simple.png)

A virus such as [tobacco mosaic virus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tobacco_mosaic_virus "Tobacco mosaic virus") is not a cell; it contains only its genetic material, and a protein coat.

[Viruses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virus "Virus") are not typically considered to be organisms, because they are incapable of autonomous [reproduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reproduction "Reproduction"), [growth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_growth "Cell growth"), [metabolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism "Metabolism"), or [homeostasis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeostasis "Homeostasis"). Although viruses have a few [enzymes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enzyme "Enzyme") and molecules like those in living organisms, they have no metabolism of their own; they cannot synthesize the organic compounds from which they are formed. In this sense, they are similar to inanimate matter.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Moreira-2009-7) Viruses have their own [genes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene "Gene"), and they [evolve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution "Evolution"). Thus, an argument that viruses should be classed as living organisms is their ability to undergo evolution and replicate through self-assembly. However, some scientists argue that viruses neither evolve nor self-reproduce. Instead, viruses are evolved by their host cells, meaning that there was co-evolution of viruses and host cells. If host cells did not exist, viral evolution would be impossible. As for reproduction, viruses rely on hosts' machinery to replicate. The discovery of viruses with genes coding for energy metabolism and protein synthesis fuelled the debate about whether viruses are living organisms, but the genes have a cellular origin. Most likely, they were acquired through [horizontal gene transfer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizontal_gene_transfer "Horizontal gene transfer") from viral hosts.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Moreira-2009-7)

### Evolutionary emergence of organisms

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Organism&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Evolutionary emergence of organisms")]

The [RNA world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA_world "RNA world") is a hypothetical stage in the evolutionary history of life on Earth during which self-replicating RNA molecules reproduced before the evolution of DNA and proteins.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-25) According to this hypothesis "organisms" emerged when RNA chains began to self-replicate, initiating the three mechanisms of Darwinian selection: [heritability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heritability "Heritability"), variation of type and differential reproductive output. The fitness of an RNA replicator (its per capita rate of increase) would presumably have been a function of its intrinsic adaptive capacities, determined by its [nucleotide sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleic_acid_sequence "Nucleic acid sequence"), and the availability of external resources.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Bernstein1983-26)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Michod1999-27) The three primary adaptive capacities of these early "organisms" may have been: (1) replication with moderate fidelity, giving rise to both heritability while allowing variation of type, (2) resistance to decay, and (3) acquisition of and processing of resources[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Bernstein1983-26)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Michod1999-27) The capacities of these "organisms" would have functioned by means of the folded configurations of the RNA replicators resulting from their nucleotide sequences.

### Organism-like colonies

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Organism&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Organism-like colonies")]

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/Apolemia_sp.jpg/250px-Apolemia_sp.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Apolemia_sp.jpg)

_[Apolemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apolemia "Apolemia")_, a colonial [siphonophore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siphonophorae "Siphonophorae") that functions as a single individual

The philosopher Jack A. Wilson examines some boundary cases to demonstrate that the concept of organism is not sharply defined.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Wilson-2000-8) In his view, [sponges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sponge "Sponge"), [lichens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lichen "Lichen"), [siphonophores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siphonophorae "Siphonophorae"), [slime moulds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slime_mold "Slime mold"), and [eusocial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eusociality "Eusociality") colonies such as those of [ants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ant "Ant") or [naked molerats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naked_mole-rat "Naked mole-rat"), all lie in the boundary zone between being definite colonies and definite organisms (or superorganisms).[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Wilson-2000-8)

### Synthetic organisms

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Organism&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Synthetic organisms")]

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Insect_cyborg.jpg/250px-Insect_cyborg.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Insect_cyborg.jpg)

Insect [cyborg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyborg "Cyborg")

Scientists and bio-engineers are experimenting with different types of [synthetic organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthetic_biology "Synthetic biology"), from [chimaeras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chimera_(genetics) "Chimera (genetics)") composed of cells from two or more species, [cyborgs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyborg "Cyborg") including [electromechanical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromechanics "Electromechanics") limbs, [hybrots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hybrot "Hybrot") containing both electronic and biological elements, and other combinations of systems that have variously evolved and been designed.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Clawson-2023-28)

An evolved organism takes its form by the partially understood mechanisms of [evolutionary developmental biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolutionary_developmental_biology "Evolutionary developmental biology"), in which the [genome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genome "Genome") directs an elaborated series of interactions to produce successively more elaborate structures. The existence of chimaeras and hybrids demonstrates that these mechanisms are "intelligently" robust in the face of radically altered circumstances at all levels from molecular to organismal.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Clawson-2023-28)

Synthetic organisms already take diverse forms, and their diversity will increase. What they all have in common is a [teleonomic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teleonomy "Teleonomy") or goal-seeking behaviour that enables them to correct errors of many kinds so as to achieve whatever result they are designed for. Such behaviour is reminiscent of intelligent action by organisms; intelligence is seen as an embodied form of [cognition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cognition "Cognition").[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism#cite_note-Clawson-2023-28)
