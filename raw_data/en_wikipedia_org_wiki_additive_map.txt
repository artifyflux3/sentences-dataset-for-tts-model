In [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_algebra "Abstract algebra"), an **additive map**, **![Image 1: {\displaystyle Z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1cc6b75e09a8aa3f04d8584b11db534f88fb56bd)-linear map** or **additive function** is a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)")![Image 2: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) that preserves the addition operation:[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_map#cite_note-1)![Image 3: {\displaystyle f(x+y)=f(x)+f(y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11e072f8427aa606b95bad4d8fba9cb3da2c0b09) for every pair of elements ![Image 4: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) and ![Image 5: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) in the [domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_of_a_function "Domain of a function") of ![Image 6: {\displaystyle f.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ecb3ed2e17fa8f336dcc0fd4b3eddbfb02a50ef3) For example, any [linear map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map") is additive. When the domain is the [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number"), this is [Cauchy's functional equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy%27s_functional_equation "Cauchy's functional equation"). For a specific case of this definition, see [additive polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_polynomial "Additive polynomial").

More formally, an additive map is a ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc)-[module homomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_homomorphism "Module homomorphism"). Since an [abelian group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group") is a ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc)-[module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_(mathematics) "Module (mathematics)"), it may be defined as a [group homomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_homomorphism "Group homomorphism") between abelian groups.

A map ![Image 9: {\displaystyle V\times W\to X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e5f94fbf1682c7c3ffb91557ec93cf3aa8bcd1c) that is additive in each of two arguments separately is called a **bi-additive map** or a **![Image 10: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc)-bilinear map**.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_map#cite_note-2)

Typical examples include maps between [rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)"), [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space"), or [modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_(mathematics) "Module (mathematics)") that preserve the [additive group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_group "Additive group"). An additive map does not necessarily preserve any other structure of the object; for example, the product operation of a ring.

If ![Image 11: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) and ![Image 12: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) are additive maps, then the map ![Image 13: {\displaystyle f+g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4d94a24abd865f6f9fd67a7df7e531cae1c769b3) (defined [pointwise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pointwise "Pointwise")) is additive.

**Homogeneity over the integers**

If ![Image 32: {\displaystyle f:X\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abd1e080abef4bbdab67b43819c6431e7561361c) is an additive map between additive groups then ![Image 33: {\displaystyle f(0)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8d308c32c9894b88115262081194321ae7d9bbf3) and for all ![Image 34: {\displaystyle x\in X,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e3ebdb0a09f0721ccdd0b779e0a21caf386be82a)![Image 35: {\displaystyle f(-x)=-f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b022ffe516cf5bc26a68fd954753aa2bddf508f1) (where negation denotes the additive inverse) and[[proof 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_map#cite_note-3)![Image 36: {\displaystyle f(nx)=nf(x)\quad {\text{ for all }}n\in \mathbb {Z} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7005b6b57d7393c87b49bfeae5e991438c526170) Consequently, ![Image 37: {\displaystyle f(x-y)=f(x)-f(y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1923d4019d3d710301e6b9ab2da07bfacdb97cd4) for all ![Image 38: {\displaystyle x,y\in X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d72f66ab332ed430aa9b34ff18c9723c4fea2a1) (where by definition, ![Image 39: {\displaystyle x-y:=x+(-y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e5dbf4e384d0d631c9ca9e6b5df3943fbe40d62)).

In other words, every additive map is [homogeneous over the integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_function "Homogeneous function"). Consequently, every additive map between [abelian groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group") is a [homomorphism of ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc)-modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_homomorphism "Module homomorphism").

**Homomorphism of ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5909f0b54e4718fa24d5fd34d54189d24a66e9a)-modules**

If the additive [abelian groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group")![Image 42: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and ![Image 43: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) are also a [unital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unital_module "Unital module")[modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_(mathematics) "Module (mathematics)") over the rationals ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5909f0b54e4718fa24d5fd34d54189d24a66e9a) (such as real or complex [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space")) then an additive map ![Image 45: {\displaystyle f:X\to Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abd1e080abef4bbdab67b43819c6431e7561361c) satisfies:[[proof 2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_map#cite_note-4)![Image 46: {\displaystyle f(qx)=qf(x)\quad {\text{ for all }}q\in \mathbb {Q} {\text{ and }}x\in X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16148cde1d7d8af770b56cbb5e352aee565fd314) In other words, every additive map is [homogeneous over the rational numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_over_the_rational_numbers "Homogeneous over the rational numbers"). Consequently, every additive maps between unital [![Image 47: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5909f0b54e4718fa24d5fd34d54189d24a66e9a)-modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_(mathematics) "Module (mathematics)") is a [homomorphism of ![Image 48: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5909f0b54e4718fa24d5fd34d54189d24a66e9a)-modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_homomorphism "Module homomorphism").

Despite being homogeneous over ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91185244fbdded6ea99a5e9e6603299128b10928) as described in the article on [Cauchy's functional equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy%27s_functional_equation "Cauchy's functional equation"), even when ![Image 50: {\displaystyle X=Y=\mathbb {R} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/62b3ea61474ac7c5cea150e562b4e520e2f1f3e4) it is nevertheless still possible for the additive function ![Image 51: {\displaystyle f:\mathbb {R} \to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e3a10a3ad05781f5cf9c2d875a02227e21a8448) to _not_ be [homogeneous over the real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_homogeneity "Real homogeneity"); said differently, there exist additive maps ![Image 52: {\displaystyle f:\mathbb {R} \to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e3a10a3ad05781f5cf9c2d875a02227e21a8448) that are _not_ of the form ![Image 53: {\displaystyle f(x)=s_{0}x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/19bd246acb5f4f540bdbe08e97040d5115916d5d) for some constant ![Image 54: {\displaystyle s_{0}\in \mathbb {R} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2c25abc53c9a31a894c1d60d796a9a7dfee1c8da) In particular, there exist additive maps that are not [linear maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map").
