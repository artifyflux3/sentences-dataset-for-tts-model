A **photon** (from [Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language")[φῶς](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%86%E1%BF%B6%CF%82#Ancient_Greek "wikt:φῶς")_,_[φωτός](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%86%CF%89%CF%84%CF%8C%CF%82#Ancient_Greek "wikt:φωτός")_(\_phôs, phōtós\_)_'light') is an [elementary particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle") that is a [quantum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum "Quantum") of the [electromagnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_field "Electromagnetic field"), including [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation") such as [light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light "Light") and [radio waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_wave "Radio wave"), and the [force carrier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force_carrier "Force carrier") for the [electromagnetic force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_force "Electromagnetic force"). Photons are [massless particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massless_particle "Massless particle") that can move no faster than the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light") measured in vacuum. The photon belongs to the class of [boson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boson "Boson") particles.

As with other elementary particles, photons are best explained by [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") and exhibit [wave–particle duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave%E2%80%93particle_duality "Wave–particle duality"), their behavior featuring properties of both [waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave "Wave") and [particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle "Particle").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-2) The modern photon concept originated during the first two decades of the 20th century with the work of [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein"), who built upon the research of [Max Planck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Planck "Max Planck"). While Planck was trying to explain how [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter") and electromagnetic radiation could be in [thermal equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_equilibrium "Thermal equilibrium") with one another, he proposed that the energy stored within a [material](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Material "Material") object should be regarded as composed of an [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer") number of discrete, equal-sized parts. To explain the [photoelectric effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect "Photoelectric effect"), Einstein introduced the idea that light itself is made of discrete units of energy. In 1926, [Gilbert N. Lewis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gilbert_N._Lewis "Gilbert N. Lewis") popularized the term _photon_ for these energy units.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-www.aps.org-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-kragh-5) Subsequently, many other experiments validated Einstein's approach.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-compton-lecture-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-8)

In the [Standard Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model "Standard Model") of [particle physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_physics "Particle physics"), photons and other elementary particles are described as a necessary consequence of physical laws having a certain [symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_(physics) "Symmetry (physics)") at every point in [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime"). The intrinsic properties of particles, such as [charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge"), [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_mass "Invariant mass"), and [spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)"), are determined by [gauge symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauge_symmetry "Gauge symmetry"). The photon concept has led to momentous advances in experimental and theoretical physics, including [lasers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser "Laser"), [Bose–Einstein condensation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_condensation "Bose–Einstein condensation"), [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory"), and the [probabilistic interpretation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_amplitude "Probability amplitude") of quantum mechanics. It has been applied to [photochemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photochemistry "Photochemistry"), [high-resolution microscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-photon_excitation_microscopy "Two-photon excitation microscopy"), and [measurements of molecular distances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorescence_resonance_energy_transfer "Fluorescence resonance energy transfer"). Moreover, photons have been studied as elements of [quantum computers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_computer "Quantum computer"), and for applications in [optical imaging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_imaging "Optical imaging") and [optical communication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_communication "Optical communication") such as [quantum cryptography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_cryptography "Quantum cryptography").

Physical properties
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photon&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Physical properties")]

The photon has no [electric charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge"),[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-chargeless-10) is generally considered to have zero [rest mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rest_mass "Rest mass")[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-11) and is a [stable particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_particle "Stable particle"). The experimental upper limit on the photon mass[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-13) is very small, on the order of 10−50 kg; its lifetime would be more than 10 18 years.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-14) For comparison the [age of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_the_universe "Age of the universe") is about 1.38×10 10 years.

In a vacuum, a photon has two possible [polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon_polarization "Photon polarization") states.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Schwartz2014-15) The photon is the [gauge boson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauge_boson "Gauge boson") for [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism"),[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-16): 29–30  and therefore all other quantum numbers of the photon (such as [lepton number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lepton_number "Lepton number"), [baryon number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon_number "Baryon number"), and [flavour quantum numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flavor_(particle_physics)#Flavour_quantum_numbers "Flavor (particle physics)")) are zero.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-17) Also, the photon obeys [Bose–Einstein statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_statistics "Bose–Einstein statistics"), and not [Fermi–Dirac statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_statistics "Fermi–Dirac statistics"). That is, they do _not_ obey the [Pauli exclusion principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauli_exclusion_principle "Pauli exclusion principle")[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Halliday-18): 1221  and more than one can occupy the same bound quantum state.

Photons are emitted in many natural processes. For example, when a charge is [accelerated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration") it emits [synchrotron radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synchrotron_radiation "Synchrotron radiation"). During a [molecular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule"), [atomic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom") or [nuclear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus") transition to a lower [energy level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_level "Energy level"), photons of various energy will be emitted, ranging from [radio waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_wave "Radio wave") to [gamma rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_ray "Gamma ray"). Photons can also be emitted when a particle and its corresponding [antiparticle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiparticle "Antiparticle") are [annihilated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annihilation "Annihilation") (for example, [electron–positron annihilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron%E2%80%93positron_annihilation "Electron–positron annihilation")).[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Halliday-18): 572, 1114, 1172

### Polarization and spin angular momentum

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photon&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Polarization and spin angular momentum")]

The photon also carries [spin angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_angular_momentum_of_light "Spin angular momentum of light"), which is related to [photon polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon_polarization "Photon polarization"). (Beams of light also exhibit properties described as [orbital angular momentum of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_angular_momentum_of_light "Orbital angular momentum of light")).

The angular momentum of the photon has two possible values, either +ħ or −ħ. These two possible values correspond to the two possible pure states of [circular polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_polarization "Circular polarization"). Collections of photons in a light beam may have mixtures of these two values; a linearly polarized light beam will act as if it were composed of equal numbers of the two possible angular momenta.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Hecht-19): 325

The spin angular momentum of light does not depend on its frequency, and was experimentally verified by [C. V. Raman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C._V._Raman "C. V. Raman") and [Suri Bhagavantam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suri_Bhagavantam "Suri Bhagavantam") in 1931.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-spin-22)

### Antiparticle annihilation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photon&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Antiparticle annihilation")]

The collision of a particle with its antiparticle can create photons. In free space at least _two_ photons must be created since, in the [center of momentum frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_momentum_frame "Center of momentum frame"), the colliding antiparticles have no net momentum, whereas a single photon always has momentum (determined by the photon's frequency or wavelength, which cannot be zero). Hence, [conservation of momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") (or equivalently, [translational invariance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translational_invariance "Translational invariance")) requires that at least two photons are created, with zero net momentum.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Griffiths2008-23): 64–65  The energy of the two photons, or, equivalently, their frequency, may be determined from [conservation of four-momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_law_(physics) "Conservation law (physics)").

Seen another way, the photon can be considered as its own antiparticle (thus an "antiphoton" is simply a normal photon with opposite momentum, equal polarization, and 180° out of phase). The reverse process, [pair production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pair_production "Pair production"), is the dominant mechanism by which high-energy photons such as [gamma rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_ray "Gamma ray") lose energy while passing through matter.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-24) That process is the reverse of "annihilation to one photon" allowed in the electric field of an atomic nucleus.

The classical formulae for the energy and momentum of [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation") can be re-expressed in terms of photon events. For example, the [pressure of electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation_pressure "Radiation pressure") on an object derives from the transfer of photon momentum per unit time and unit area to that object, since pressure is force per unit area and force is the change in [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") per unit time.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-25)

### Experimental checks on photon mass

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photon&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Experimental checks on photon mass")]

Current commonly accepted physical theories imply or assume the photon to be strictly massless. If photons were not purely massless, their speeds would vary with frequency, with lower-energy (redder) photons moving slightly slower than higher-energy photons. Relativity would be unaffected by this; the so-called speed of light, _c_, would then not be the actual speed at which light moves, but a constant of nature which is the [upper bound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Upper_bound "Upper bound") on speed that any object could theoretically attain in spacetime.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-26) Thus, it would still be the speed of spacetime ripples ([gravitational waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_waves "Gravitational waves") and [gravitons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graviton "Graviton")), but it would not be the speed of photons.

If a photon did have non-zero mass, there would be other effects as well. [Coulomb's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coulomb%27s_law "Coulomb's law") would be modified and the [electromagnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_field "Electromagnetic field") would have an extra physical [degree of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_of_freedom "Degree of freedom"). These effects yield more sensitive experimental probes of the photon mass than the frequency dependence of the speed of light. If Coulomb's law is not exactly valid, then that would allow the presence of an [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field") to exist within a hollow conductor when it is subjected to an external electric field. This provides a means for precision [tests of Coulomb's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_electromagnetism "Tests of electromagnetism").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-27) A null result of such an experiment has set a limit of _m_ ≲ 10−14 eV/_c_ 2.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-28)

Sharper upper limits on the mass of light have been obtained in experiments designed to detect effects caused by the galactic [vector potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_vector_potential "Magnetic vector potential"). Although the galactic vector potential is large because the galactic [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field") exists on great length scales, only the magnetic field would be observable if the photon is massless. In the case that the photon has mass, the mass term ⁠1/2⁠_m_ 2 _A_ _μ_ _A_ _μ_ would affect the galactic plasma. The fact that no such effects are seen implies an upper bound on the photon mass of _m_<3×10−27 eV/_c_ 2.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-29) The galactic vector potential can also be probed directly by measuring the torque exerted on a magnetized ring.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-30) Such methods were used to obtain the sharper upper limit of 1.07×10−27 eV/_c_ 2 (10−36[Da](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dalton_(unit) "Dalton (unit)")) given by the [Particle Data Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_Data_Group "Particle Data Group").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-amsler-31)

These sharp limits from the non-observation of the effects caused by the galactic vector potential have been shown to be model-dependent.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-32) If the photon mass is generated via the [Higgs mechanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higgs_mechanism "Higgs mechanism") then the upper limit of _m_ ≲ 10−14 eV/_c_ 2 from the test of Coulomb's law is valid.

Historical development
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photon&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Historical development")]

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Young_Diffraction.png/250px-Young_Diffraction.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Young_Diffraction.png)

[Thomas Young](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Young_(scientist) "Thomas Young (scientist)")'s [double-slit experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double-slit_experiment "Double-slit experiment") in 1801 showed that light can act as a [wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave "Wave"), helping to invalidate early [particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle") theories of light.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Halliday-18): 964 

In most theories up to the eighteenth century, light was pictured as being made of particles. Since [particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle "Subatomic particle") models cannot easily account for the [refraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refraction "Refraction"), [diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffraction "Diffraction") and [birefringence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birefringence "Birefringence") of light, wave theories of light were proposed by [René Descartes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes "René Descartes") (1637),[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-33)[Robert Hooke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Hooke "Robert Hooke") (1665),[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-34) and [Christiaan Huygens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens "Christiaan Huygens") (1678);[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-35) however, particle models remained dominant, chiefly due to the influence of [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton").[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Newton1730-36) In the early 19th century, [Thomas Young](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Young_(scientist) "Thomas Young (scientist)") and [August Fresnel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustin-Jean_Fresnel "Augustin-Jean Fresnel") clearly demonstrated the [interference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interference_(wave_propagation) "Interference (wave propagation)") and diffraction of light, and by 1850 wave models were generally accepted.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-37)[James Clerk Maxwell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell "James Clerk Maxwell")'s 1865 [prediction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations "Maxwell's equations")[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-maxwell-38) that light was an electromagnetic wave – which was confirmed experimentally in 1888 by [Heinrich Hertz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinrich_Hertz "Heinrich Hertz")'s detection of [radio waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio "Radio")[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-hertz-39) – seemed to be the final blow to particle models of light.

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Light-wave.svg/330px-Light-wave.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Light-wave.svg)

In 1900, [Maxwell's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell "James Clerk Maxwell")[theoretical model of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations "Maxwell's equations") as oscillating [electric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field") and [magnetic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field") seemed complete. However, several observations could not be explained by any wave model of [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation"), leading to the idea that light-energy was packaged into _quanta_ described by E = hν. Later experiments showed that these light-quanta also carry momentum and, thus, can be considered [particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle"): The _photon_ concept was born, leading to a deeper understanding of the electric and magnetic fields themselves.

The [Maxwell wave theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_wave_equation "Electromagnetic wave equation"), however, does not account for _all_ properties of light. The Maxwell theory predicts that the energy of a light wave depends only on its [intensity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intensity_(physics) "Intensity (physics)"), not on its [frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency"); nevertheless, several independent types of experiments show that the energy imparted by light to atoms depends only on the light's frequency, not on its intensity. For example, [some chemical reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photochemistry "Photochemistry") are provoked only by light of frequency higher than a certain threshold; light of frequency lower than the threshold, no matter how intense, does not initiate the reaction. Similarly, electrons can be ejected from a metal plate by shining light of sufficiently high frequency on it (the [photoelectric effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect "Photoelectric effect")); the energy of the ejected electron is related only to the light's frequency, not to its intensity.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-40)

At the same time, investigations of [black-body radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black-body_radiation "Black-body radiation") carried out over four decades (1860–1900) by various researchers[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Wien1911-41) culminated in [Max Planck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Planck "Max Planck")'s [hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant")[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Planck1901-42)[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Planck1918-43) that the energy of _any_ system that absorbs or emits electromagnetic radiation of frequency ν is an integer multiple of an energy quantum E = hν . As shown by [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein"),[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Einstein1905-44)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Einstein1909-45) some form of energy quantization _must_ be assumed to account for the thermal equilibrium observed between matter and [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation"); for this explanation of the photoelectric effect, Einstein received the 1921 [Nobel Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize "Nobel Prize") in physics.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-46)

Since the Maxwell theory of light allows for all possible energies of electromagnetic radiation, most physicists assumed initially that the energy quantization resulted from some unknown constraint on the matter that absorbs or emits the radiation. In 1905, Einstein was the first to propose that energy quantization was a property of electromagnetic radiation itself.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Einstein1905-44) Although he accepted the validity of Maxwell's theory, Einstein pointed out that many anomalous experiments could be explained if the _energy_ of a Maxwellian light wave were localized into point-like quanta that move independently of one another, even if the wave itself is spread continuously over space.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Einstein1905-44) In 1909[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Einstein1909-45) and 1916,[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Einstein1916b-47) Einstein showed that, if [Planck's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck%27s_law "Planck's law") regarding black-body radiation is accepted, the energy quanta must also carry [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") p = ⁠ h / λ ⁠, making them full-fledged particles.

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/93/Bohr_atom_model.svg/250px-Bohr_atom_model.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bohr_atom_model.svg)

Up to 1923, most physicists were reluctant to accept that light itself was quantized. Instead, they tried to explain photon behaviour by quantizing only _matter_, as in the [Bohr model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohr_model "Bohr model") of the [hydrogen atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_atom "Hydrogen atom") (shown here). Even though these semiclassical models were only a first approximation, they were accurate for simple systems and they led to [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics").

As recounted in [Robert Millikan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Millikan "Robert Millikan")'s 1923 Nobel lecture, Einstein's 1905 predicted energy relationship was verified experimentally by 1916 but the local concept of the quanta remained unsettled.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Millikan1923-48) Most physicists were reluctant to believe that electromagnetic radiation itself might be particulate and thus an example of wave-particle duality.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-49) Then in 1922 [Arthur Compton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Compton "Arthur Compton") experiment[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Compton1923-50) showed that photons carried momentum proportional to their [wave number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_number "Wave number") (1922) in an experiment now called [Compton scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compton_scattering "Compton scattering") that appeared to clearly support a localized quantum model. At least for Millikan, this settled the matter.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Millikan1923-48) Compton received the Nobel Prize in 1927 for his scattering work.

Even after Compton's experiment, [Niels Bohr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niels_Bohr "Niels Bohr"), [Hendrik Kramers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hendrik_Anthony_Kramers "Hendrik Anthony Kramers") and [John Slater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_C._Slater "John C. Slater") made one last attempt to preserve the Maxwellian continuous electromagnetic field model of light, the so-called [BKS theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BKS_theory "BKS theory").[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Bohr1924-51) An important feature of the BKS theory is how it treated the [conservation of energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_energy "Conservation of energy") and the [conservation of momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_momentum "Conservation of momentum"). In the BKS theory, energy and momentum are only conserved on the average across many interactions between matter and radiation. However, refined Compton experiments showed that the conservation laws hold for individual interactions.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-52) Accordingly, Bohr and his co-workers gave their model "as honorable a funeral as possible".[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Pais1982-53) Nevertheless, the failures of the BKS model inspired [Werner Heisenberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg "Werner Heisenberg") in his development of [matrix mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_mechanics "Matrix mechanics").[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Heisenberg1932-54)

By the late 1920, the pivotal question was how to unify Maxwell's wave theory of light with its experimentally observed particle nature. The answer to this question occupied Albert Einstein for the rest of his life,[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Pais1982-53) and was solved in [quantum electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics "Quantum electrodynamics") and its successor, the [Standard Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model "Standard Model"). (See _[§Quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#Quantum\_field\_theory)_ and _[§As a gauge boson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#As\_a\_gauge\_boson)_, below.)

A few physicists persisted[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Mandel1976-55) in developing semiclassical models in which electromagnetic radiation is not quantized, but matter appears to obey the laws of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"). Although the evidence from chemical and physical experiments for the existence of photons was overwhelming by the 1970s, this evidence could not be considered as _absolutely_ definitive; since it relied on the interaction of light with matter, and a sufficiently complete theory of matter could in principle account for the evidence.

In the 1970s and 1980s photon-correlation experiments definitively demonstrated quantum photon effects. These experiments produce results that cannot be explained by any classical theory of light, since they involve anticorrelations that result from the [quantum measurement process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measurement_in_quantum_mechanics "Measurement in quantum mechanics"). In 1974, the first such experiment was carried out by Clauser, who reported a violation of a classical [Cauchy–Schwarz inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy%E2%80%93Schwarz_inequality "Cauchy–Schwarz inequality"). In 1977, Kimble _et al._ demonstrated an analogous anti-bunching effect of photons interacting with a beam splitter; this approach was simplified and sources of error eliminated in the photon-anticorrelation experiment of Grangier, Roger, & Aspect (1986);[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-56) This work is reviewed and simplified further in Thorn, Neel, _et al._ (2004).[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-57)

[![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Photoelectric_effect_in_a_solid_-_diagram.svg/250px-Photoelectric_effect_in_a_solid_-_diagram.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Photoelectric_effect_in_a_solid_-_diagram.svg)

[Photoelectric effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect "Photoelectric effect"): the emission of electrons from a metal plate caused by light quanta – photons

The word [_quanta_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum "Quantum") (singular _quantum,_ Latin for _[how much](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/quantum "wikt:quantum")_) was used before 1900 to mean particles or amounts of different [quantities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantity "Quantity"), including [electricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron"). In 1900, the German physicist [Max Planck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Planck "Max Planck") was studying [black-body radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black-body_radiation "Black-body radiation"), and he suggested that the experimental observations, specifically at [shorter wavelengths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet_catastrophe "Ultraviolet catastrophe"), would be explained if the energy was "made up of a completely determinate number of finite equal parts", which he called "energy elements".[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-58) In 1905, [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") published a paper in which he proposed that many light-related phenomena—including black-body radiation and the [photoelectric effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect "Photoelectric effect")—would be better explained by modelling electromagnetic waves as consisting of spatially localized, discrete energy quanta.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Einstein1905-44) He called these _a light quantum_ (German: _ein Lichtquant_).[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-59)

The name _photon_ derives from the [Greek word](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language") for light, _φῶς_ (transliterated _phôs_). The name was used 1916 by the American physicist and psychologist [Leonard T. Troland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonard_T._Troland "Leonard T. Troland") for a unit of illumination of the [retina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retina "Retina") and in several other contexts before being adopted for physics.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-kragh-5) The use of the term _photon_ for the light quantum was popularized by [Gilbert N. Lewis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gilbert_N._Lewis "Gilbert N. Lewis"), who used the term in a letter to _[Nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature\_(journal) "Nature (journal)")_ on 18 December 1926.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-60) Arthur Compton, who had performed a key experiment demonstrating light quanta, cited Lewis in the 1927 [Solvay conference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvay_conference "Solvay conference") proceedings for suggesting the name _photon_. Einstein never did use the term.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-kragh-5)

In physics, a photon is usually denoted by the symbol [γ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma "Gamma") (the [Greek letter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_alphabet "Greek alphabet")[gamma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma "Gamma")). This symbol for the photon probably derives from [gamma rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_ray "Gamma ray"), which were discovered in 1900 by [Paul Villard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Ulrich_Villard "Paul Ulrich Villard"),[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-61)[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-62) named by [Ernest Rutherford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernest_Rutherford "Ernest Rutherford") in 1903, and shown to be a form of [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation") in 1914 by Rutherford and [Edward Andrade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Andrade "Edward Andrade").[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-63) In [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry") and [optical engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_engineering "Optical engineering"), photons are usually symbolized by hν, which is the [photon energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon_energy "Photon energy"), where h is the [Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant") and the [Greek letter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_alphabet "Greek alphabet")ν ([nu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nu_(letter) "Nu (letter)")) is the photon's [frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency").[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Liddle2015-64)

Wave–particle duality and uncertainty principles
------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photon&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Wave–particle duality and uncertainty principles")]

Photons obey the laws of quantum mechanics, and so their behavior has both wave-like and particle-like aspects. When a photon is detected by a measuring instrument, it is registered as a single, particulate unit. However, the _probability_ of detecting a photon is calculated by equations that describe waves. This combination of aspects is known as [wave–particle duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave%E2%80%93particle_duality "Wave–particle duality"). For example, the [probability distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_distribution "Probability distribution") for the location at which a photon might be detected displays clearly wave-like phenomena such as [diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffraction "Diffraction") and [interference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interference_(wave_propagation) "Interference (wave propagation)"). A single photon passing through a [double slit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double-slit_experiment "Double-slit experiment") has its energy received at a point on the screen with a probability distribution given by its interference pattern determined by [Maxwell's wave equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations "Maxwell's equations").[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Taylor1909-65) However, experiments confirm that the photon is _not_ a short pulse of electromagnetic radiation; a photon's Maxwell waves will diffract, but photon energy does not spread out as it propagates, nor does this energy divide when it encounters a [beam splitter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beam_splitter "Beam splitter").[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Saleh-66) Rather, the received photon acts like a [point-like particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point-like_particle "Point-like particle") since it is absorbed or emitted _as a whole_ by arbitrarily small systems, including systems much smaller than its wavelength, such as an atomic nucleus (≈10−15 m across) or even the point-like [electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron").

While many introductory texts treat photons using the mathematical techniques of non-relativistic quantum mechanics, this is in some ways an awkward oversimplification, as photons are by nature intrinsically relativistic. Because photons have zero [rest mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rest_mass "Rest mass"), no [wave function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_function "Wave function") defined for a photon can have all the properties familiar from wave functions in non-relativistic quantum mechanics.[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-71) In order to avoid these difficulties, physicists employ the second-quantized theory of photons described below, [quantum electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics "Quantum electrodynamics"), in which photons are quantized excitations of electromagnetic modes.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-scully1997-72)

Another difficulty is finding the proper analogue for the [uncertainty principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncertainty_principle "Uncertainty principle"), an idea frequently attributed to Heisenberg, who introduced the concept in analyzing a [thought experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thought_experiment "Thought experiment") involving [an electron and a high-energy photon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heisenberg%27s_microscope "Heisenberg's microscope"). However, Heisenberg did not give precise mathematical definitions of what the "uncertainty" in these measurements meant. The precise mathematical statement of the position–momentum uncertainty principle is due to [Kennard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earle_Hesse_Kennard "Earle Hesse Kennard"), [Pauli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Pauli "Wolfgang Pauli"), and [Weyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Weyl "Hermann Weyl").[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-73)[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-74) The uncertainty principle applies to situations where an experimenter has a choice of measuring either one of two "canonically conjugate" quantities, like the position and the momentum of a particle. According to the uncertainty principle, no matter how the particle is prepared, it is not possible to make a precise prediction for both of the two alternative measurements: if the outcome of the position measurement is made more certain, the outcome of the momentum measurement becomes less so, and vice versa.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-L&L-75) A [coherent state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coherent_state "Coherent state") minimizes the overall uncertainty as far as quantum mechanics allows.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-scully1997-72)[Quantum optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_optics "Quantum optics") makes use of coherent states for modes of the electromagnetic field. There is a tradeoff, reminiscent of the position–momentum uncertainty relation, between measurements of an electromagnetic wave's amplitude and its phase.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-scully1997-72) This is sometimes informally expressed in terms of the uncertainty in the number of photons present in the electromagnetic wave, ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \Delta N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f2fca110bac4b3427ef640cdf356ae92a7432214), and the uncertainty in the phase of the wave, ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \Delta \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/125968846e9883e0b0ddef5f1d70f53023ec74b0). However, this cannot be an uncertainty relation of the Kennard–Pauli–Weyl type, since unlike position and momentum, the phase ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4) cannot be represented by a [Hermitian operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermitian_operator "Hermitian operator").[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-76)

Bose–Einstein model of a photon gas
-----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photon&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Bose–Einstein model of a photon gas")]

In 1924, [Satyendra Nath Bose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satyendra_Nath_Bose "Satyendra Nath Bose") derived [Planck's law of black-body radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck%27s_law_of_black-body_radiation "Planck's law of black-body radiation") without using any electromagnetism, but rather by using a modification of coarse-grained counting of [phase space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_space "Phase space").[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Bose1924-77) Einstein showed that this modification is equivalent to assuming that photons are rigorously identical and that it implied a "mysterious non-local interaction",[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Einstein1924-78)[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Einstein1925-79) now understood as the requirement for a [symmetric quantum mechanical state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identical_particles "Identical particles"). This work led to the concept of [coherent states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coherent_state "Coherent state") and the development of the laser. In the same papers, Einstein extended Bose's formalism to material particles (bosons) and predicted that they would condense into their lowest [quantum state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state "Quantum state") at low enough temperatures; this [Bose–Einstein condensation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_condensate "Bose–Einstein condensate") was observed experimentally in 1995.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-80) It was later used by [Lene Hau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lene_Hau "Lene Hau") to slow, and then completely stop, light in 1999[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-81) and 2001.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-82)

The modern view on this is that photons are, by virtue of their integer spin, bosons (as opposed to [fermions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermion "Fermion") with half-integer spin). By the [spin-statistics theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin-statistics_theorem "Spin-statistics theorem"), all bosons obey Bose–Einstein statistics (whereas all fermions obey [Fermi–Dirac statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_statistics "Fermi–Dirac statistics")).[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-83)

Stimulated and spontaneous emission
-----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photon&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Stimulated and spontaneous emission")]

[![Image 17](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8a/Stimulatedemission.png/500px-Stimulatedemission.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Stimulatedemission.png)

[Stimulated emission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stimulated_emission "Stimulated emission") (in which photons "clone" themselves) was predicted by Einstein in his kinetic analysis, and led to the development of the [laser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser "Laser"). Einstein's derivation inspired further developments in the quantum treatment of light, which led to the statistical interpretation of quantum mechanics.

In 1916, Albert Einstein showed that Planck's radiation law could be derived from a semi-classical, statistical treatment of photons and atoms, which implies a link between the rates at which atoms emit and absorb photons. The condition follows from the assumption that functions of the emission and absorption of radiation by the atoms are independent of each other, and that thermal equilibrium is made by way of the radiation's interaction with the atoms. Consider a cavity in [thermal equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_equilibrium "Thermal equilibrium") with all parts of itself and filled with [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation") and that the atoms can emit and absorb that radiation. Thermal equilibrium requires that the energy density ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \rho (\nu )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/771818acdb33bf67ca39d6f197a871dae92752b3) of photons with frequency ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c15bbbb971240cf328aba572178f091684585468) (which is proportional to their [number density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_density "Number density")) is, on average, constant in time; hence, the rate at which photons of any particular frequency are _emitted_ must equal the rate at which they are _absorbed_.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Einstein1916a-84)

Einstein began by postulating simple proportionality relations for the different reaction rates involved. In his model, the rate ![Image 20: {\displaystyle R_{ji}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07aa016887d6fd7c4d7f5041899cb34d8ba1154e) for a system to _absorb_ a photon of frequency ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c15bbbb971240cf328aba572178f091684585468) and transition from a lower energy ![Image 22: {\displaystyle E_{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a01babf2293d37d9ff73b61202cfc3a3933eaf19) to a higher energy ![Image 23: {\displaystyle E_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8ba9f6e3041b052cf13a0ede4ecf35fb4c9cd16c) is proportional to the number ![Image 24: {\displaystyle N_{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e42efe207e17a74b4c17b1aa0cced7a84501bec9) of atoms with energy ![Image 25: {\displaystyle E_{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a01babf2293d37d9ff73b61202cfc3a3933eaf19) and to the energy density ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \rho (\nu )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/771818acdb33bf67ca39d6f197a871dae92752b3) of ambient photons of that frequency,

![Image 27: {\displaystyle R_{ji}=N_{j}B_{ji}\rho (\nu )\!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6c77cd3b1d4802a0110925a4cfe934b76a66a45b)
where ![Image 28: {\displaystyle B_{ji}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/baf9e62f616a2934a55bccd69e744f38035d8b93) is the [rate constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rate_constant "Rate constant") for absorption. For the reverse process, there are two possibilities: spontaneous emission of a photon, or the emission of a photon initiated by the interaction of the atom with a passing photon and the return of the atom to the lower-energy state. The ![Image 53: {\displaystyle A_{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8272b28f5aae6dbb8d6f829d58bab353b21bde20) and ![Image 54: {\displaystyle B_{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/57c134504644caba8b83f479c9f27dbfa990b79a) are collectively known as the _Einstein coefficients_.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-85)

Einstein could not fully justify his rate equations, but claimed that it should be possible to calculate the coefficients ![Image 55: {\displaystyle A_{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8272b28f5aae6dbb8d6f829d58bab353b21bde20), ![Image 56: {\displaystyle B_{ji}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/baf9e62f616a2934a55bccd69e744f38035d8b93) and ![Image 57: {\displaystyle B_{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/57c134504644caba8b83f479c9f27dbfa990b79a) once physicists had obtained "mechanics and electrodynamics modified to accommodate the quantum hypothesis".[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-86) Not long thereafter, in 1926, [Paul Dirac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Dirac "Paul Dirac") derived the ![Image 58: {\displaystyle B_{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/57c134504644caba8b83f479c9f27dbfa990b79a) rate constants by using a semiclassical approach,[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Dirac1926-87) and, in 1927, succeeded in deriving _all_ the rate constants from first principles within the framework of quantum theory.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Dirac1927a-88)[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Dirac1927b-89) Dirac's work was the foundation of quantum electrodynamics, i.e., the quantization of the electromagnetic field itself. Dirac's approach is also called _second quantization_ or [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory");[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Heisenberg1929-90)[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Heisenberg1930-91)[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Fermi1932-92) earlier quantum mechanical treatments only treat material particles as quantum mechanical, not the electromagnetic field.

Einstein was troubled by the fact that his theory seemed incomplete, since it did not determine the _direction_ of a spontaneously emitted photon. A probabilistic nature of light-particle motion was first considered by [Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton") in his treatment of [birefringence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birefringence "Birefringence") and, more generally, of the splitting of light beams at interfaces into a transmitted beam and a reflected beam. Newton hypothesized that hidden variables in the light particle determined which of the two paths a single photon would take.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Newton1730-36) Similarly, Einstein hoped for a more complete theory that would leave nothing to chance, beginning his separation[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Pais1982-53) from quantum mechanics. Ironically, [Max Born](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Born "Max Born")'s [probabilistic interpretation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_amplitude "Probability amplitude") of the [wave function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_function "Wave function")[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Born1926a-93)[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Born1926b-94) was inspired by Einstein's later work searching for a more complete theory.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-ghost_field-95)

### Quantization of the electromagnetic field

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photon&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Quantization of the electromagnetic field")]

[![Image 59](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/VisibleEmrWavelengths.svg/330px-VisibleEmrWavelengths.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:VisibleEmrWavelengths.svg)

Different _electromagnetic modes_ (such as those depicted here) can be treated as independent [simple harmonic oscillators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_harmonic_oscillator "Quantum harmonic oscillator"). A photon corresponds to a unit of energy _E_=_hν_ in its electromagnetic mode.

In 1910, [Peter Debye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Debye "Peter Debye") derived [Planck's law of black-body radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck%27s_law_of_black-body_radiation "Planck's law of black-body radiation") from a relatively simple assumption.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Debye1910-96) He decomposed the electromagnetic field in a cavity into its [Fourier modes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_series "Fourier series"), and assumed that the energy in any mode was an integer multiple of ![Image 60: {\displaystyle h\nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3cc23768f43b26085f80e1882a94b31d24abd653), where ![Image 61: {\displaystyle \nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c15bbbb971240cf328aba572178f091684585468) is the frequency of the electromagnetic mode. Planck's law of black-body radiation follows immediately as a geometric sum. However, Debye's approach failed to give the correct formula for the energy fluctuations of black-body radiation, which were derived by Einstein in 1909.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Einstein1909-45)

In 1925, [Born](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Born "Max Born"), [Heisenberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg "Werner Heisenberg") and [Jordan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascual_Jordan "Pascual Jordan") reinterpreted Debye's concept in a key way.[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Born1925-97) As may be shown classically, the [Fourier modes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_series "Fourier series") of the [electromagnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_four-potential "Electromagnetic four-potential")—a complete set of electromagnetic plane waves indexed by their wave vector _**k**_ and polarization state—are equivalent to a set of uncoupled [simple harmonic oscillators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_harmonic_oscillator "Simple harmonic oscillator"). Treated quantum mechanically, the energy levels of such oscillators are known to be ![Image 62: {\displaystyle E=nh\nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5a53b66a9439e07293b61135c915a7b19c33096), where ![Image 63: {\displaystyle \nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c15bbbb971240cf328aba572178f091684585468) is the oscillator frequency. The key new step was to identify an electromagnetic mode with energy ![Image 64: {\displaystyle E=nh\nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5a53b66a9439e07293b61135c915a7b19c33096) as a state with ![Image 65: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) photons, each of energy ![Image 66: {\displaystyle h\nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3cc23768f43b26085f80e1882a94b31d24abd653). This approach gives the correct energy fluctuation formula.

[![Image 67](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c7/Electron-scattering.svg/250px-Electron-scattering.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Electron-scattering.svg)

[Feynman diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feynman_diagram "Feynman diagram") of two electrons interacting by exchange of a virtual photon.

[Dirac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Dirac "Paul Dirac") took this one step further.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Dirac1927a-88)[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Dirac1927b-89) He treated the interaction between a charge and an electromagnetic field as a small perturbation that induces transitions in the photon states, changing the numbers of photons in the modes, while conserving energy and momentum overall. Dirac was able to derive Einstein's ![Image 68: {\displaystyle A_{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8272b28f5aae6dbb8d6f829d58bab353b21bde20) and ![Image 69: {\displaystyle B_{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/57c134504644caba8b83f479c9f27dbfa990b79a) coefficients from first principles, and showed that the Bose–Einstein statistics of photons is a natural consequence of quantizing the electromagnetic field correctly (Bose's reasoning went in the opposite direction; he derived [Planck's law of black-body radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck%27s_law_of_black-body_radiation "Planck's law of black-body radiation") by _assuming_ B–E statistics). In Dirac's time, it was not yet known that all bosons, including photons, must obey Bose–Einstein statistics.

Dirac's second-order [perturbation theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perturbation_theory_(quantum_mechanics) "Perturbation theory (quantum mechanics)") can involve [virtual photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtual_particle "Virtual particle"), transient intermediate states of the electromagnetic field; the static [electric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coulomb%27s_law "Coulomb's law") and [magnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetism "Magnetism") interactions are mediated by such virtual photons. In such [quantum field theories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory"), the [probability amplitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_amplitude "Probability amplitude") of observable events is calculated by summing over _all_ possible intermediate steps, even ones that are unphysical; hence, virtual photons are not constrained to satisfy ![Image 70: {\displaystyle E=pc}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5498982f39427c7573448da10de26a7b3209e47a), and may have extra [polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polarization_(waves) "Polarization (waves)") states; depending on the [gauge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauge_fixing "Gauge fixing") used, virtual photons may have three or four polarization states, instead of the two states of real photons. Although these transient virtual photons can never be observed, they contribute measurably to the probabilities of observable events.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-98)

Indeed, such second-order and higher-order perturbation calculations can give apparently [infinite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinity "Infinity") contributions to the sum. Such unphysical results are corrected for using the technique of [renormalization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renormalization "Renormalization").[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-99)

Other virtual particles may contribute to the summation as well; for example, two photons may interact indirectly through virtual [electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron")–[positron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positron "Positron")[pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pair_production "Pair production").[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-100) Such photon–photon scattering (see [two-photon physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-photon_physics "Two-photon physics")), as well as electron–photon scattering, is meant to be one of the modes of operations of the planned particle accelerator, the [International Linear Collider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Linear_Collider "International Linear Collider").[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-101)

In [modern physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_physics "Modern physics") notation, the [quantum state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state "Quantum state") of the electromagnetic field is written as a [Fock state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fock_state "Fock state"), a [tensor product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product "Tensor product") of the states for each electromagnetic mode

![Image 71: {\displaystyle |n_{k_{0}}\rangle \otimes |n_{k_{1}}\rangle \otimes \dots \otimes |n_{k_{n}}\rangle \dots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1bf1d8ffa71f6f78681ad32f6ff5870627b1cd79)
where ![Image 72: {\displaystyle |n_{k_{i}}\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/261582c861f317f3e8cd9e33c36c8d10e0d62a49) represents the state in which ![Image 73: {\displaystyle \,n_{k_{i}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a6945fb83c5b12e2c726c9a26195feb2207b0d1d) photons are in the mode ![Image 74: {\displaystyle k_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f29138ed3ad54ffce527daccadc49c520459b0b0). In this notation, the creation of a new photon in mode ![Image 75: {\displaystyle k_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f29138ed3ad54ffce527daccadc49c520459b0b0) (e.g., emitted from an atomic transition) is written as ![Image 76: {\displaystyle |n_{k_{i}}\rangle \rightarrow |n_{k_{i}}+1\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/98ad7c2b75079bb41e6e1719263ff9fc799e8161). This notation merely expresses the concept of Born, Heisenberg and Jordan described above, and does not add any physics.

The electromagnetic field can be understood as a [gauge field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauge_field "Gauge field"), i.e., as a field that results from requiring that a gauge symmetry holds independently at every position in [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime").[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Ryder-102) For the [electromagnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_field "Electromagnetic field"), this gauge symmetry is the [Abelian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group")[U(1) symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_group "Unitary group") of [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") of absolute value 1, which reflects the ability to vary the [phase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_geometry "Complex geometry") of a complex field without affecting [observables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable "Observable") or [real valued functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") made from it, such as the [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy") or the [Lagrangian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_(field_theory) "Lagrangian (field theory)").

The quanta of an [Abelian gauge field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauge_theory "Gauge theory") must be massless, uncharged bosons, as long as the symmetry is not broken; hence, the photon is predicted to be massless, and to have zero [electric charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge") and integer spin. The particular form of the [electromagnetic interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_interaction "Electromagnetic interaction") specifies that the photon must have [spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)") ±1; thus, its [helicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helicity_(particle_physics) "Helicity (particle physics)") must be ![Image 77: {\displaystyle \pm \hbar }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5c578ae2b02e515c92a2981d6db0ffb5943e3357). These two spin components correspond to the classical concepts of [right-handed and left-handed circularly polarized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_polarization "Circular polarization") light. However, the transient [virtual photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtual_photon "Virtual photon") of [quantum electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics "Quantum electrodynamics") may also adopt unphysical polarization states.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Ryder-102)

In the prevailing [Standard Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model "Standard Model") of physics, the photon is one of four gauge bosons in the [electroweak interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electroweak_interaction "Electroweak interaction"); the [other three](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W_and_Z_bosons "W and Z bosons") are denoted W+, W− and Z 0 and are responsible for the [weak interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_interaction "Weak interaction"). Unlike the photon, these gauge bosons have [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_mass "Invariant mass"), owing to a [mechanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higgs_mechanism "Higgs mechanism") that breaks their [SU(2) gauge symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_unitary_group "Special unitary group"). The unification of the photon with W and Z gauge bosons in the electroweak interaction was accomplished by [Sheldon Glashow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheldon_Glashow "Sheldon Glashow"), [Abdus Salam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdus_Salam "Abdus Salam") and [Steven Weinberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steven_Weinberg "Steven Weinberg"), for which they were awarded the 1979 [Nobel Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize "Nobel Prize") in physics.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Glashow-103)[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Salam-104)[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-Weinberg-105) Physicists continue to hypothesize [grand unified theories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_unification_theory "Grand unification theory") that connect these four gauge bosons with the eight [gluon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gluon "Gluon") gauge bosons of [quantum chromodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_chromodynamics "Quantum chromodynamics"); however, key predictions of these theories, such as [proton decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton_decay "Proton decay"), have not been observed experimentally.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-106)

### Hadronic properties

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photon&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Hadronic properties")]

Measurements of the interaction between energetic photons and [hadrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadron "Hadron") show that the interaction is much more intense than expected by the interaction of merely photons with the hadron's electric charge. Furthermore, the interaction of energetic photons with protons is similar to the interaction of photons with neutrons[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-107) in spite of the fact that the electrical charge structures of protons and neutrons are substantially different. A theory called [vector meson dominance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_meson_dominance "Vector meson dominance") (VMD) was developed to explain this effect. According to VMD, the photon is a superposition of the pure electromagnetic photon, which interacts only with electric charges, and vector mesons, which mediate the residual [nuclear force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_force "Nuclear force").[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-108) However, if experimentally probed at very short distances, the intrinsic structure of the photon appears to have as components a charge-neutral flux of quarks and gluons, quasi-free according to asymptotic freedom in [QCD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_chromodynamics "Quantum chromodynamics"). That flux is described by the [_photon structure function_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon_Structure_Function "Photon Structure Function").[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-109)[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-110) A review by [Nisius (2000)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#CITEREFNisius2000) presented a comprehensive comparison of data with theoretical predictions.[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-111)

### Contributions to the mass of a system

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photon&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Contributions to the mass of a system")]

The energy of a system that emits a photon is _decreased_ by the energy ![Image 78: {\displaystyle E}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4232c9de2ee3eec0a9c0a19b15ab92daa6223f9b) of the photon as measured in the rest frame of the emitting system, which may result in a reduction in mass in the amount ![Image 79: {\displaystyle {E}/{c^{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61f55812ff776dde9279b91f06e7993ddff94932). Similarly, the mass of a system that absorbs a photon is _increased_ by a corresponding amount. As an application, the energy balance of nuclear reactions involving photons is commonly written in terms of the masses of the nuclei involved, and terms of the form ![Image 80: {\displaystyle {E}/{c^{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61f55812ff776dde9279b91f06e7993ddff94932) for the gamma photons (and for other relevant energies, such as the recoil energy of nuclei).[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-112)

This concept is applied in key predictions of [quantum electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_electrodynamics "Quantum electrodynamics") (QED, see above). In that theory, the mass of electrons (or, more generally, leptons) is modified by including the mass contributions of virtual photons, in a technique known as [renormalization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renormalization "Renormalization"). Such "[radiative corrections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiative_correction "Radiative correction")" contribute to a number of predictions of QED, such as the [magnetic dipole moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anomalous_magnetic_dipole_moment "Anomalous magnetic dipole moment") of [leptons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lepton "Lepton"), the [Lamb shift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lamb_shift "Lamb shift"), and the [hyperfine structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperfine_structure "Hyperfine structure") of bound lepton pairs, such as [muonium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muonium "Muonium") and [positronium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positronium "Positronium").[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-113)

Since photons contribute to the [stress–energy tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stress%E2%80%93energy_tensor "Stress–energy tensor"), they exert a [gravitational attraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity") on other objects, according to the theory of [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"). Conversely, photons are themselves affected by gravity; their normally straight trajectories may be bent by warped [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime"), as in [gravitational lensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_lens "Gravitational lens"), and [their frequencies may be lowered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_redshift "Gravitational redshift") by moving to a higher [gravitational potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potential_energy "Potential energy"), as in the [Pound–Rebka experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pound%E2%80%93Rebka_experiment "Pound–Rebka experiment"). However, these effects are not specific to photons; exactly the same effects would be predicted for classical [electromagnetic waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation").[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-114)

Light that travels through transparent matter does so at a lower speed than _c_, the speed of light in vacuum. The factor by which the speed is decreased is called the [refractive index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refractive_index "Refractive index") of the material. In a classical wave picture, the slowing can be explained by the light inducing [electric polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_polarization "Electric polarization") in the matter, the polarized matter radiating new light, and that new light interfering with the original light wave to form a delayed wave. In a particle picture, the slowing can instead be described as a blending of the photon with quantum excitations of the matter to produce [quasi-particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-particle "Quasi-particle") known as [polaritons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polariton "Polariton"). Polaritons have a nonzero [effective mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effective_mass_(solid-state_physics) "Effective mass (solid-state physics)"), which means that they cannot travel at _c_. Light of different frequencies may travel through matter at [different speeds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_speed_of_light "Variable speed of light"); this is called [dispersion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dispersion_(optics) "Dispersion (optics)") (not to be confused with scattering). In some cases, it can result in [extremely slow speeds of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slow_light "Slow light") in matter. The effects of photon interactions with other quasi-particles may be observed directly in [Raman scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raman_scattering "Raman scattering") and [Brillouin scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brillouin_scattering "Brillouin scattering").[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-115)

Photons can be scattered by matter. For example, photons scatter so many times in the solar [radiative zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiative_zone "Radiative zone") after leaving the [core of the Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_core "Solar core") that [radiant energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiant_energy "Radiant energy") takes about a million years to reach the [convection zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convection_zone "Convection zone").[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-116) However, photons emitted from the sun's [photosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosphere "Photosphere") take only 8.3 minutes to reach Earth.[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-117)

Photons can also be [absorbed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation) "Absorption (electromagnetic radiation)") by nuclei, atoms or molecules, provoking transitions between their [energy levels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_level "Energy level"). A classic example is the molecular transition of [retinal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retinal "Retinal") (C 20 H 28 O), which is responsible for [vision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visual_perception "Visual perception"), as discovered in 1958 by Nobel laureate [biochemist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biochemist "Biochemist")[George Wald](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Wald "George Wald") and co-workers. The absorption provokes a [cis–trans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cis%E2%80%93trans "Cis–trans")[isomerization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomerization "Isomerization") that, in combination with other such transitions, is transduced into nerve impulses. The absorption of photons can even break chemical bonds, as in the [photodissociation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photodissociation "Photodissociation") of [chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine"); this is the subject of [photochemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photochemistry "Photochemistry").[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-118)[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-119)

Technological applications
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photon&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Technological applications")]

Photons have many applications in technology. These examples are chosen to illustrate applications of photons _per se_, rather than general optical devices such as lenses, etc. that could operate under a classical theory of light. The laser is an important application and is discussed above under [stimulated emission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stimulated_emission "Stimulated emission").

Individual photons can be detected by several methods. The classic [photomultiplier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photomultiplier "Photomultiplier") tube exploits the [photoelectric effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect "Photoelectric effect"): a photon of sufficient energy strikes a metal plate and knocks free an electron, initiating an ever-amplifying avalanche of electrons. [Semiconductor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiconductor "Semiconductor")[charge-coupled device](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charge-coupled_device "Charge-coupled device") chips use a similar effect: an incident photon generates a charge on a microscopic [capacitor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capacitor "Capacitor") that can be detected. Other detectors such as [Geiger counters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geiger_counter "Geiger counter") use the ability of photons to [ionize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionize "Ionize") gas molecules contained in the device, causing a detectable change of [conductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_conductivity "Electrical conductivity") of the gas.[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-120)

Planck's energy formula ![Image 81: {\displaystyle E=h\nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6c0386dc6d9530519404f95570fcc8548ed2326) is often used by engineers and chemists in design, both to compute the change in energy resulting from a photon absorption and to determine the frequency of the light emitted from a given photon emission. For example, the [emission spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emission_spectrum "Emission spectrum") of a [gas-discharge lamp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas-discharge_lamp "Gas-discharge lamp") can be altered by filling it with (mixtures of) gases with different electronic [energy level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_level "Energy level") configurations.[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-121)

Under some conditions, an energy transition can be excited by "two" photons that individually would be insufficient. This allows for higher resolution microscopy, because the sample absorbs energy only in the spectrum where two beams of different colors overlap significantly, which can be made much smaller than the excitation volume of a single beam (see [two-photon excitation microscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-photon_excitation_microscopy "Two-photon excitation microscopy")). Moreover, these photons cause less damage to the sample, since they are of lower energy.[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-122)

In some cases, two energy transitions can be coupled so that, as one system absorbs a photon, another nearby system "steals" its energy and re-emits a photon of a different frequency. This is the basis of [fluorescence resonance energy transfer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorescence_resonance_energy_transfer "Fluorescence resonance energy transfer"), a technique that is used in [molecular biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_biology "Molecular biology") to study the interaction of suitable [proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein "Protein").[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-123)

Several different kinds of [hardware random number generators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hardware_random_number_generator "Hardware random number generator") involve the detection of single photons. In one example, for each bit in the random sequence that is to be produced, a photon is sent to a [beam-splitter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beam-splitter "Beam-splitter"). In such a situation, there are two possible outcomes of equal probability. The actual outcome is used to determine whether the next bit in the sequence is 0 or 1.[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-124)[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-125)

Quantum optics and computation
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photon&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Quantum optics and computation")]

Much research has been devoted to applications of photons in the field of [quantum optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_optics "Quantum optics"). Photons seem well-suited to be elements of an extremely fast [quantum computer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_computer "Quantum computer"), and the [quantum entanglement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_entanglement "Quantum entanglement") of photons is a focus of research. [Nonlinear optical processes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlinear_optics "Nonlinear optics") are another active research area, with topics such as [two-photon absorption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-photon_absorption "Two-photon absorption"), [self-phase modulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-phase_modulation "Self-phase modulation"), [modulational instability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modulational_instability "Modulational instability") and [optical parametric oscillators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_parametric_oscillator "Optical parametric oscillator"). However, such processes generally do not require the assumption of photons _per se_; they may often be modeled by treating atoms as nonlinear oscillators. The nonlinear process of [spontaneous parametric down conversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spontaneous_parametric_down_conversion "Spontaneous parametric down conversion") is often used to produce single-photon states. Finally, photons are essential in some aspects of [optical communication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_communication "Optical communication"), especially for [quantum cryptography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_cryptography "Quantum cryptography").[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-126)

[Two-photon physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-photon_physics "Two-photon physics") studies interactions between photons, which are rare. In 2018, Massachusetts Institute of Technology researchers announced the discovery of bound photon triplets, which may involve [polaritons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polariton "Polariton").[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-NW-20180216-127)[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon#cite_note-SCI-20180216-128)
