The illustration of movement of objects from the _Principles_

_**Principles of Philosophy**_ ([Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_language "Latin language"): _Principia Philosophiae_) is a book by [René Descartes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes "René Descartes"). In essence, it is a synthesis of the _[Discourse on Method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discourse\_on\_Method "Discourse on Method")_ and _[Meditations on First Philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meditations\_on\_First\_Philosophy "Meditations on First Philosophy")_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principles_of_Philosophy#cite_note-Durandin-1) It was written in [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin"), published in 1644 and dedicated to [Elisabeth of Bohemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elisabeth_of_the_Palatinate "Elisabeth of the Palatinate"), with whom Descartes had a long-standing friendship. A French version (_Les Principes de la Philosophie_) followed in 1647.

The book sets forth the principles of nature—the [laws of physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laws_of_physics "Laws of physics")—as Descartes viewed them. Most notably, it set forth the principle that in the absence of external forces, an object's motion will be uniform and in a straight line. [Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton") borrowed this principle from Descartes and included it in his own _[Principia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophiae\_Naturalis\_Principia\_Mathematica "Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica")_; to this day, it is still generally referred to as [Newton's first law of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_first_law_of_motion "Newton's first law of motion").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principles_of_Philosophy#cite_note-2) The book was primarily intended to replace the [Aristotelian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotelianism "Aristotelianism") curriculum then used in French and British universities. The work provides a systematic statement of his [metaphysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metaphysics "Metaphysics") and [natural philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_philosophy "Natural philosophy"), and represents the first truly comprehensive, [mechanistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics "Mechanics") account of the universe.

### Preface to the French edition

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Principles_of_Philosophy&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Preface to the French edition")]

Descartes asked Abbot Claude Picot to translate his Latin _Principia Philosophiae_ into French. For this edition, he wrote a preface disguised as a letter to the translator, whose title is "_Letter of the author to the translator of the book, that may be used as a preface_." This was published in 1647, when he was 51 years old and in the mature, final period of his life. In this writing, Descartes provides some reflections on his ideas of wisdom and philosophy. Its content may be summarized as follows:[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principles_of_Philosophy#cite_note-3)

Concept of philosophy
Philosophy is the study of _wisdom_, understood as the ability to conduct the human activities; and also as the perfect knowledge of all the things that a man can know for the direction of his life, maintenance of his health, and knowledge of the arts. Only God is perfectly wise, and the man is more or less wise, in proportion to the knowledge he has of the most important truths.

The degrees of knowledge
Descartes identifies four degrees of knowledge which he names _common_, and a fifth degree he designates _higher_. The first degree consists of clear and evident notions that can be acquired without the need for any meditation. The second degree is all that is learned by means of the senses. The third comprises what we learn when talking with others. The fourth consists of what we can learn from the writings of those capable of giving good instructions.

Higher wisdom
There have been great people throughout history who have pursued a better and more secure wisdom, a _fifth degree_ of knowledge. This has consisted of the search for the _first causes_, and those that have followed this pursuit have been named _philosophers_, but he thinks that none have yet been successful.

Doubt and certainty
Since [Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato") and [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle"), there has been discussion on doubt and certainty. Those that have favoured doubt have arrived at extremes of doubting even the most evident things, and those that have sought certainty have relied excessively on the senses. Though it has been accepted that the senses may mislead us, according to Descartes, nobody had yet expressed that the truth can not be based on the senses, but in the _understanding_, when it is founded on _evident perceptions_.

Meditations on first philosophy
The search for the first causes, or basic truths, as undertaken by Descartes is contained in this work. It explains the [metaphysical principles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metaphysics "Metaphysics") on which to build the rest of knowledge.

The tree of philosophy
Descartes describes philosophy as like a tree, whose roots are _metaphysics_, its trunk _physics_, and the branches are the rest of the sciences, mainly _medicine_, _mechanics_, and _morals_ that is the last level of wisdom. In the same way that trees have fruits in their outer parts, the usefulness of philosophy is also contained in the areas that stem from its foundation.

René Descartes' _Principia Philosophiae_. This copy was printed in 1656, and is owned by Southwest Research Institute rare book collection at the Tom Slick Memorial Library in San Antonio, Texas.

A copy of Descartes' _Principia philosophiae_ dated 1656 is owned by the [Tom Slick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tom_Slick "Tom Slick") rare book collection at the [Southwest Research Institute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southwest_Research_Institute "Southwest Research Institute") in Texas. The book was translated into Italian in 1722 by [Giuseppa Eleonora Barbapiccola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giuseppa_Eleonora_Barbapiccola "Giuseppa Eleonora Barbapiccola")[[it](https://it.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giuseppa_Eleonora_Barbapiccola "it:Giuseppa Eleonora Barbapiccola")]. (Cf. C. Landolfi, _Giuseppa Eleonora Barbapiccola: Opere scelte_, Mirista-moderna, Salerno 2024)

[D. Reidel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D._Reidel "D. Reidel"), a Dutch publisher, released an English edition of _Principia philosophiae_ in 1983 ([ISBN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISBN_(identifier) "ISBN (identifier)")[90-277-1451-7](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:BookSources/90-277-1451-7 "Special:BookSources/90-277-1451-7")), translated by Valentine Rodger and Reese P. Miller with explanatory notes. Though a translation of the original 1644 Latin work, this edition by Rodger and Miller includes additional material from the 1647 French translation.
