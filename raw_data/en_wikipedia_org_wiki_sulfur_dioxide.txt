**Sulfur dioxide** ([IUPAC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUPAC "IUPAC")-recommended spelling) or **sulphur dioxide** (traditional [Commonwealth English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commonwealth_English "Commonwealth English")) is the [chemical compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_compound "Chemical compound") with the formula [S](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur "Sulfur")[O](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen")

2. It is a colorless gas with a pungent smell that is responsible for the odor of burnt matches. It is released naturally by [volcanic activity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcanic_activity "Volcanic activity") and is produced as a by-product of metals refining and the burning of [sulfur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sour_gas "Sour gas")-[bearing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sour_crude_oil "Sour crude oil") fossil fuels.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Greenwood-9)

Sulfur dioxide is somewhat toxic to humans, although only when inhaled in relatively large quantities for a period of several minutes or more. It was known to medieval [alchemists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alchemy "Alchemy") as "volatile spirit of sulfur".[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Wothers-2019-10)

Structure and bonding
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_dioxide&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Structure and bonding")]

SO 2 is a bent molecule with _C_ 2v[symmetry point group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_groups_in_three_dimensions "Point groups in three dimensions"). A [valence bond theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valence_bond_theory "Valence bond theory") approach considering just _s_ and _p_ orbitals would describe the bonding in terms of [resonance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resonance_(chemistry) "Resonance (chemistry)") between two resonance structures.

[![Image 22](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/75/Sulfur-dioxide-resonance-2D.svg/330px-Sulfur-dioxide-resonance-2D.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Sulfur-dioxide-resonance-2D.svg)

Two resonance structures of sulfur dioxide

The sulfur–oxygen bond has a [bond order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_order "Bond order") of 1.5. There is support for this simple approach that does not invoke _d_ orbital participation.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-11) In terms of [electron-counting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_counting "Electron counting") formalism, the sulfur atom has an [oxidation state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidation_state "Oxidation state") of +4 and a [formal charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formal_charge "Formal charge") of +1.

[![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Io_Aurorae_color.jpg/250px-Io_Aurorae_color.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Io_Aurorae_color.jpg)

The blue auroral glows of Io's upper atmosphere are caused by volcanic sulfur dioxide.

Sulfur dioxide is found on Earth and exists in very small concentrations in the atmosphere at about 15 [ppb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts-per_notation "Parts-per notation").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-12)

On other planets, sulfur dioxide can be found in various concentrations, the most significant being the [atmosphere of Venus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Venus "Atmosphere of Venus"), where it is the third-most abundant atmospheric gas at 150 ppm. There, it reacts with water to form clouds of sulfurous acid (SO 2 + H 2 O ⇌ HSO−3 + H+), and is a key component of the planet's global atmospheric [sulfur cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_cycle "Sulfur cycle"). It has been implicated as a key agent in the warming of early [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars"), with estimates of concentrations in the lower atmosphere as high as 100 ppm,[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-HalevyZuber2007-13) though it only exists in trace amounts. On both Venus and Mars, as on Earth, its primary source is thought to be volcanic. The [atmosphere of Io](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Io "Atmosphere of Io"), a natural satellite of [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter"), is 90% sulfur dioxide[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-IobookChap10-14) and trace amounts are thought to also exist in the [atmosphere of Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Jupiter "Atmosphere of Jupiter"). The [James Webb Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope "James Webb Space Telescope") has observed the presence of sulfur dioxide on the [exoplanet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exoplanet "Exoplanet")[WASP-39b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WASP-39b "WASP-39b"), where it is formed through [photochemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photochemistry "Photochemistry") in the planet's atmosphere.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-15)

As an ice, it is thought to exist in abundance on the [Galilean moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilean_moons "Galilean moons")—as subliming ice or frost on the trailing hemisphere of [Io](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Io_(moon) "Io (moon)"),[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-CruikshankHowell1985-16) and in the crust and mantle of [Europa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europa_(moon) "Europa (moon)"), [Ganymede](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ganymede_(moon) "Ganymede (moon)"), and [Callisto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Callisto_(moon) "Callisto (moon)"), possibly also in liquid form and readily reacting with water.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-17)

Sulfur dioxide is primarily produced for [sulfuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid") manufacture (see [contact process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contact_process "Contact process"), but other processes predated that at least since 16th century[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Wothers-2019-10)). In the United States in 1979, 23.6 million metric tons (26 million U.S. short tons) of sulfur dioxide were used in this way, compared with 150,000 metric tons (165,347 U.S. short tons) used for other purposes. Most sulfur dioxide is produced by the combustion of elemental [sulfur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur "Sulfur"). Some sulfur dioxide is also produced by roasting [pyrite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrite "Pyrite") and other [sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfide "Sulfide") ores in air.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Ullmann-18)

An experiment showing burning of sulfur in [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen"). A flow-chamber joined to a gas washing bottle (filled with a solution of [methyl orange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_orange "Methyl orange")) is being used. The product is sulfur dioxide (SO 2) with some traces of [sulfur trioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_trioxide "Sulfur trioxide") (SO 3). The "smoke" that exits the gas washing bottle is, in fact, a sulfuric acid fog generated in the reaction.

Sulfur dioxide is the product of the burning of [sulfur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur "Sulfur") or of burning materials that contain sulfur:

S 8 + 8 O 2 → 8 SO 2, ΔH = −297 kJ/mol
To aid combustion, liquified sulfur (140–150°C (284–302°F) is sprayed through an atomizing nozzle to generate fine drops of sulfur with a large surface area. The reaction is [exothermic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exothermic "Exothermic"), and the combustion produces temperatures of 1,000–1,600°C (1,830–2,910°F). The significant amount of heat produced is recovered by steam generation that can subsequently be converted to electricity.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Ullmann-18)

The combustion of [hydrogen sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide "Hydrogen sulfide") and organosulfur compounds proceeds similarly. For example:

2 H 2 S + 3 O 2 → 2 SO 2 + 2 H 2 O
The [roasting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roasting_(metallurgy) "Roasting (metallurgy)") of sulfide ores such as [pyrite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrite "Pyrite"), [sphalerite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphalerite "Sphalerite"), and [cinnabar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cinnabar "Cinnabar") (mercury sulfide) also releases SO 2:[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-19)

4 FeS 2 + 11 O 2 → 2 Fe 2 O 3 + 8 SO 2 2 ZnS + 3 O 2 → 2 ZnO + 2 SO 2 HgS + O 2 → Hg + SO 2 4 FeS + 7 O 2 → 2 Fe 2 O 3 + 4 SO 2
A combination of these reactions is responsible for the largest source of sulfur dioxide, volcanic eruptions. These events can release millions of tons of SO 2.

### Reduction of higher oxides

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_dioxide&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Reduction of higher oxides")]

Sulfur dioxide can also be a byproduct in the manufacture of [calcium silicate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_silicate "Calcium silicate") cement; [CaSO 4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_sulfate "Calcium sulfate") is heated with [coke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coke_(fuel) "Coke (fuel)") and sand in this process:

2 CaSO 4 + 2 SiO 2 + C → 2 CaSiO 3 + 2 SO 2 + CO 2
Until the 1970s commercial quantities of sulfuric acid and cement were produced by this process in [Whitehaven](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitehaven "Whitehaven"), England. Upon being mixed with [shale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shale "Shale") or [marl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marl "Marl"), and roasted, the sulfate liberated sulfur dioxide gas, used in sulfuric acid production, the reaction also produced calcium silicate, a precursor in cement production.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-20)

On a laboratory scale, the action of hot concentrated sulfuric acid on copper [turnings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swarf "Swarf") produces sulfur dioxide.

Cu + 2 H 2 SO 4 → CuSO 4 + SO 2 + 2 H 2 O
Tin also reacts with concentrated sulfuric acid but it produces tin(II) sulfate which can later be pyrolyzed at 360°C into tin dioxide and dry sulfur dioxide.

Sn + H 2 SO 4 → SnSO 4 + H 2 SnSO 4 → SnO 2 + SO 2
The reverse reaction occurs upon acidification:

H+ + HSO−3 → SO 2 + H 2 O
Sulfites result by the action of aqueous base on sulfur dioxide:

SO 2 + 2 NaOH → Na 2 SO 3 + H 2 O
Sulfur dioxide is a mild but useful [reducing agent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reducing_agent "Reducing agent"). It is oxidized by [halogens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halogen "Halogen") to give the sulfuryl halides, such as [sulfuryl chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuryl_chloride "Sulfuryl chloride"):

SO 2 + Cl 2 → SO 2 Cl 2
Sulfur dioxide is the [oxidising agent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidising_agent "Oxidising agent") in the [Claus process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claus_process "Claus process"), which is conducted on a large scale in [oil refineries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_refineries "Oil refineries"). Here, sulfur dioxide is reduced by hydrogen sulfide to give elemental sulfur:

SO 2 + 2 H 2 S → 3 S + 2 H 2 O
The sequential oxidation of sulfur dioxide followed by its hydration is used in the production of sulfuric acid.

SO 2 + H 2 O + 1⁄2 O 2 → H 2 SO 4
Sulfur dioxide dissolves in water to give "[sulfurous acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfurous_acid "Sulfurous acid")", which cannot be isolated and is instead an acidic solution of [bisulfite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bisulfite "Bisulfite"), and possibly [sulfite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfite "Sulfite"), ions.

SO 2 + H 2 O ⇌ HSO−3 + H+_K_ a = 1.54×10−2; p _K_ a = 1.81

### Laboratory reactions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_dioxide&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Laboratory reactions")]

Sulfur dioxide is one of the few common acidic yet reducing gases. It turns moist litmus pink (being acidic), then white (due to its bleaching effect). It may be identified by bubbling it through a [dichromate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dichromate "Dichromate") solution, turning the solution from orange to green (Cr 3+ (aq)). It can also reduce ferric ions to ferrous.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Lucas-21)

Sulfur dioxide can react with certain 1,3-[dienes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diene "Diene") in a [cheletropic reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cheletropic_reaction "Cheletropic reaction") to form cyclic [sulfones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfone "Sulfone"). This reaction is exploited on an industrial scale for the synthesis of [sulfolane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfolane "Sulfolane"), which is an important solvent in the [petrochemical industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petrochemical_industry "Petrochemical industry").

[![Image 24](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Cheletropic_reaction_of_butadiene_with_SO2.svg/250px-Cheletropic_reaction_of_butadiene_with_SO2.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cheletropic_reaction_of_butadiene_with_SO2.svg)
Sulfur dioxide can bind to metal ions as a [ligand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ligand "Ligand") to form [metal sulfur dioxide complexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal_sulfur_dioxide_complex "Metal sulfur dioxide complex"), typically where the transition metal is in oxidation state 0 or +1. Many different bonding modes (geometries) are recognized, but in most cases, the ligand is monodentate, attached to the metal through sulfur, which can be either planar and pyramidal [η](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hapticity "Hapticity")1.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Greenwood-9) As a η 1-SO 2 (S-bonded planar) ligand sulfur dioxide functions as a Lewis base using the lone pair on S. SO 2 functions as a [Lewis acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lewis_acids "Lewis acids") in its η 1-SO 2 (S-bonded pyramidal) bonding mode with metals and in its 1:1 [adducts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adducts "Adducts") with Lewis bases such as [dimethylacetamide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimethylacetamide "Dimethylacetamide") and [trimethyl amine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trimethyl_amine "Trimethyl amine"). When bonding to Lewis bases the [acid parameters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ECW_model "ECW model") of SO 2 are E A = 0.51 and E A = 1.56.

The overarching, dominant use of sulfur dioxide is in the production of [sulfuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Ullmann-18)

### Precursor to sulfuric acid

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_dioxide&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Precursor to sulfuric acid")]

Sulfur dioxide is an intermediate in the production of sulfuric acid, being converted to [sulfur trioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_trioxide "Sulfur trioxide"), and then to [oleum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oleum "Oleum"), which is made into sulfuric acid. Sulfur dioxide for this purpose is made when sulfur combines with oxygen. The method of converting sulfur dioxide to sulfuric acid is called the [contact process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contact_process "Contact process"). Several million tons are produced annually for this purpose.

Sulfur dioxide is sometimes used as a preservative for dried apricots, dried figs, and other dried fruits, owing to its [antimicrobial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antimicrobial "Antimicrobial") properties and ability to prevent [oxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidation "Oxidation"),[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-22) and is called [E](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E_number "E number")220[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-23) when used in this way in Europe. As a preservative, it maintains the colorful appearance of the fruit and prevents [rotting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decomposition "Decomposition"). Historically, [molasses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molasses "Molasses") was "sulfured" as a preservative and also to lighten its color. Treatment of dried fruit was usually done outdoors, by igniting sublimed sulfur and burning in an enclosed space with the fruits.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-University_of_Georgia-24) Fruits may be sulfured by dipping them into [sodium bisulfite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_bisulfite "Sodium bisulfite"), [sodium sulfite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_sulfite "Sodium sulfite") or [sodium metabisulfite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_metabisulfite "Sodium metabisulfite").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-University_of_Georgia-24)

Sulfur dioxide was first used in [winemaking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winemaking "Winemaking") by the Romans, when they discovered that burning sulfur candles inside empty wine vessels keeps them fresh and free from vinegar smell.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-25)

It is still an important compound in winemaking, and is measured in [parts per million](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts_per_million "Parts per million") (_ppm_) in wine. It is present even in so-called unsulfurated wine at concentrations of up to 10 mg/L.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-26) It serves as an [antibiotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antibiotic "Antibiotic") and [antioxidant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antioxidant "Antioxidant"), protecting wine from spoilage by bacteria and oxidation – a phenomenon that leads to the browning of the wine and a loss of cultivar specific flavors.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Jackson-27)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Guerrero-28) Its antimicrobial action also helps minimize volatile acidity. Wines containing sulfur dioxide are typically labeled with "containing [sulfites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfite "Sulfite")".

Sulfur dioxide exists in wine in free and bound forms, and the combinations are referred to as total SO 2. Binding, for instance to the carbonyl group of [acetaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde "Acetaldehyde"), varies with the wine in question. The free form exists in equilibrium between molecular SO 2 (as a dissolved gas) and bisulfite ion, which is in turn in equilibrium with sulfite ion. These equilibria depend on the pH of the wine. Lower pH shifts the equilibrium towards molecular (gaseous) SO 2, which is the active form, while at higher pH more SO 2 is found in the inactive sulfite and bisulfite forms. The molecular SO 2 is active as an antimicrobial and antioxidant, and this is also the form which may be perceived as a pungent odor at high levels. Wines with total SO 2 concentrations below 10 ppm do not require "contains sulfites" on the label by US and EU laws. The upper limit of total SO 2 allowed in wine in the US is 350 ppm; in the EU it is 160 ppm for red wines and 210 ppm for white and rosé wines. In low concentrations, SO 2 is mostly undetectable in wine, but at free SO 2 concentrations over 50 ppm, SO 2 becomes evident in the smell and taste of wine.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

SO 2 is also a very important compound in winery sanitation. Wineries and equipment must be kept clean, and because bleach cannot be used in a winery due to the risk of [cork taint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cork_taint "Cork taint"),[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-29) a mixture of SO 2, water, and citric acid is commonly used to clean and sanitize equipment. [Ozone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone "Ozone") (O 3) is now used extensively for sanitizing in wineries due to its efficacy, and because it does not affect the wine or most equipment.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-30)

### As a reducing agent

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_dioxide&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: As a reducing agent")]

Sulfur dioxide is also a good [reductant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reducing_agent "Reducing agent"). In the presence of water, sulfur dioxide is able to decolorize substances. Specifically, it is a useful reducing [bleach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bleach "Bleach") for papers and delicate materials such as clothes. This bleaching effect normally does not last very long. Oxygen in the atmosphere reoxidizes the reduced dyes, restoring the color. In municipal wastewater treatment, sulfur dioxide is used to treat chlorinated wastewater prior to release. Sulfur dioxide reduces free and combined chlorine to [chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloride "Chloride").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-31)

Sulfur dioxide is fairly soluble in water, and by both IR and Raman spectroscopy; the hypothetical [sulfurous acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfurous_acid "Sulfurous acid"), H 2 SO 3, is not present to any extent. However, such solutions do show spectra of the hydrogen sulfite ion, HSO 3−, by reaction with water, and it is in fact the actual reducing agent present:

SO 2 + H 2 O ⇌ HSO 3− + H+
In the beginning of the 20th century sulfur dioxide was used in [Buenos Aires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buenos_Aires "Buenos Aires") as a fumigant to kill rats that carried the _[Yersinia pestis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yersinia\_pestis "Yersinia pestis")_ bacterium, which causes bubonic plague. The application was successful, and the application of this method was extended to other areas in South America. In Buenos Aires, where these apparatuses were known as [Sulfurozador](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfurozador "Sulfurozador"), but later also in Rio de Janeiro, New Orleans and San Francisco, the sulfur dioxide treatment machines were brought into the streets to enable extensive disinfection campaigns, with effective results.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-32)

### Biochemical and biomedical roles

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_dioxide&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Biochemical and biomedical roles")]

Sulfur dioxide or its conjugate base bisulfite is produced biologically as an intermediate in both sulfate-reducing organisms and in sulfur-oxidizing bacteria, as well. The role of sulfur dioxide in mammalian biology is not yet well understood.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-33) Sulfur dioxide blocks nerve signals from the [pulmonary stretch receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_stretch_receptors "Pulmonary stretch receptors") and abolishes the [Hering–Breuer inflation reflex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hering%E2%80%93Breuer_reflex "Hering–Breuer reflex").

It is considered that endogenous sulfur dioxide plays a significant physiological role in regulating [cardiac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardiac "Cardiac") and [blood vessel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_vessel "Blood vessel") function, and aberrant or deficient sulfur dioxide metabolism can contribute to several different cardiovascular diseases, such as [arterial hypertension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arterial_hypertension "Arterial hypertension"), [atherosclerosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atherosclerosis "Atherosclerosis"), [pulmonary arterial hypertension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_arterial_hypertension "Pulmonary arterial hypertension"), and [stenocardia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stenocardia "Stenocardia").[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-34)

It was shown that in children with pulmonary arterial hypertension due to congenital heart diseases the level of [homocysteine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homocysteine "Homocysteine") is higher and the level of endogenous sulfur dioxide is lower than in normal control children. Moreover, these biochemical parameters strongly correlated to the severity of pulmonary arterial hypertension. Authors considered homocysteine to be one of useful biochemical markers of disease severity and sulfur dioxide metabolism to be one of potential therapeutic targets in those patients.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-35)

Endogenous sulfur dioxide also has been shown to lower the [proliferation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_proliferation "Cell proliferation") rate of endothelial [smooth muscle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_muscle "Smooth muscle") cells in blood vessels, via lowering the [MAPK](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MAPK "MAPK") activity and activating [adenylyl cyclase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adenylyl_cyclase "Adenylyl cyclase") and [protein kinase A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_kinase_A "Protein kinase A").[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-36) Smooth muscle cell proliferation is one of important mechanisms of hypertensive remodeling of blood vessels and their [stenosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stenosis "Stenosis"), so it is an important pathogenetic mechanism in arterial hypertension and atherosclerosis.

Endogenous sulfur dioxide in low concentrations causes endothelium-dependent [vasodilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vasodilation "Vasodilation"). In higher concentrations it causes endothelium-independent vasodilation and has a negative inotropic effect on cardiac output function, thus effectively lowering blood pressure and myocardial oxygen consumption. The vasodilating and bronchodilating effects of sulfur dioxide are mediated via ATP-dependent [calcium channels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_channel "Calcium channel") and L-type ("dihydropyridine") calcium channels. Endogenous sulfur dioxide is also a potent antiinflammatory, antioxidant and cytoprotective agent. It lowers blood pressure and slows hypertensive remodeling of blood vessels, especially thickening of their intima. It also regulates lipid metabolism.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-37)

Endogenous sulfur dioxide also diminishes myocardial damage, caused by [isoproterenol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isoproterenol "Isoproterenol")[adrenergic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adrenergic "Adrenergic") hyperstimulation, and strengthens the myocardial antioxidant defense reserve.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-38)

### As a reagent and solvent in the laboratory

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_dioxide&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: As a reagent and solvent in the laboratory")]

Sulfur dioxide is a versatile inert solvent widely used for dissolving highly oxidizing salts. It is also used occasionally as a source of the sulfonyl group in [organic synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_synthesis "Organic synthesis"). Treatment of aryl [diazonium salts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diazonium_salt "Diazonium salt") with sulfur dioxide and [cuprous chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuprous_chloride "Cuprous chloride") yields the corresponding aryl sulfonyl chloride, for example:[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-39)

[![Image 25](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Preparation_of_m-trifluoromethylbenzenesulfonyl_chloride.svg/500px-Preparation_of_m-trifluoromethylbenzenesulfonyl_chloride.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Preparation_of_m-trifluoromethylbenzenesulfonyl_chloride.svg)
As a result of its very low [Lewis basicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lewis_basicity "Lewis basicity"), it is often used as a low-temperature solvent/diluent for superacids like [magic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magic_acid "Magic acid") (FSO 3 H/SbF 5), allowing for highly reactive species like _tert_-butyl cation to be observed spectroscopically at low temperature (though tertiary carbocations do react with SO 2 above about −30°C, and even less reactive solvents like [SO 2 ClF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuryl_chloride_fluoride "Sulfuryl chloride fluoride") must be used at these higher temperatures).[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-40)

Sulfur dioxide was one of the earliest [refrigerants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refrigerant "Refrigerant") adopted for mechanical refrigeration owing to its ease of liquefaction and high latent [heat of vaporization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_of_vaporization "Heat of vaporization"). In 1784, [Jean-François Clouet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Fran%C3%A7ois_Clouet "Jean-François Clouet") and [Gaspard Monge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaspard_Monge "Gaspard Monge") first demonstrated that sulfur dioxide gas could be liquefied at low temperatures. In the mid-1870s, [Raoul Pictet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raoul_Pictet "Raoul Pictet") successfully employed sulfur dioxide in a prototype refrigeration system. Beginning in 1920, it saw widespread use in the "Rollator" rotary-compressor [home refrigerators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refrigeration#Home_and_consumer_use "Refrigeration") produced by [Norge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norge_(appliance_manufacturer) "Norge (appliance manufacturer)"). Following the introduction of less toxic, non-flammable [chlorofluorocarbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon "Chlorofluorocarbon") (CFC) refrigerants, the use of sulfur dioxide in refrigeration systems gradually declined.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-ASHRAE-41)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Ayman_2020-42)

### As an indicator of volcanic activity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_dioxide&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: As an indicator of volcanic activity")]

Sulfur dioxide content in naturally-released geothermal gasses is measured by the [Icelandic Meteorological Office](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icelandic_Meteorological_Office "Icelandic Meteorological Office") as an indicator of possible volcanic activity.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-43)

[![Image 26](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/20180519_USGS_Leilani_Estates_Hawaii_Volcanic_EruptionDSC_0411_medium.jpg/250px-20180519_USGS_Leilani_Estates_Hawaii_Volcanic_EruptionDSC_0411_medium.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:20180519_USGS_Leilani_Estates_Hawaii_Volcanic_EruptionDSC_0411_medium.jpg)

[US Geological Survey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Geological_Survey "United States Geological Survey") volunteer tests for sulfur dioxide after the [2018 lower Puna eruption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2018_lower_Puna_eruption "2018 lower Puna eruption").

In the United States, the [Center for Science in the Public Interest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_for_Science_in_the_Public_Interest "Center for Science in the Public Interest") lists the two food preservatives, sulfur dioxide and [sodium bisulfite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_bisulfite "Sodium bisulfite"), as being safe for human consumption except for certain asthmatic individuals who may be sensitive to them, especially in large amounts.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-44) Symptoms of sensitivity to [sulfiting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfite#Health_effects "Sulfite") agents, including sulfur dioxide, manifest as potentially life-threatening trouble breathing within minutes of ingestion.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-45) Sulphites may also cause symptoms in non-asthmatic individuals, namely [dermatitis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dermatitis "Dermatitis"), [urticaria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hives "Hives"), [flushing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flushing_(physiology) "Flushing (physiology)"), [hypotension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypotension "Hypotension"), [abdominal pain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abdominal_pain "Abdominal pain") and diarrhea, and even life-threatening [anaphylaxis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaphylaxis "Anaphylaxis").[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-pmid24834193-46)

Incidental exposure to sulfur dioxide is routine, e.g. the smoke from matches, coal, and sulfur-containing fuels like [bunker fuel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bunker_fuel "Bunker fuel"). Relative to other chemicals, it is only mildly toxic and requires high concentrations to be actively hazardous.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-47) However, its ubiquity makes it a major air pollutant with significant impacts on human health.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-EPA-48)

In 2008, the [American Conference of Governmental Industrial Hygienists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Conference_of_Governmental_Industrial_Hygienists "American Conference of Governmental Industrial Hygienists") reduced the [short-term exposure limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Short-term_exposure_limit "Short-term exposure limit") to 0.25 parts per million (ppm). In the US, the [OSHA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration "Occupational Safety and Health Administration") set the [PEL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit "Permissible exposure limit") at 5 ppm (13 mg/m 3) time-weighted average. Also in the US, [NIOSH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NIOSH "NIOSH") set the [IDLH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IDLH "IDLH") at 100 ppm.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-NIOSH-49) In 2010, the [EPA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EPA "EPA") "revised the primary SO 2[NAAQS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NAAQS "NAAQS") by establishing a new one-hour standard at a level of 75 [parts per billion (ppb)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts_per_billion "Parts per billion"). EPA revoked the two existing primary standards because they would not provide additional public health protection given a one-hour standard at 75 ppb."[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-EPA-48)

[![Image 27](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Volcanic_injection.svg/500px-Volcanic_injection.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Volcanic_injection.svg)

The effect of major [volcanic eruptions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcanic_eruption "Volcanic eruption") on sulfate aerosol concentrations and chemical reactions in the atmosphere

Major [volcanic eruptions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcanic_eruption "Volcanic eruption") have an overwhelming effect on sulfate [aerosol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerosol "Aerosol") concentrations in the years when they occur: eruptions ranking 4 or greater on the [Volcanic Explosivity Index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcanic_Explosivity_Index "Volcanic Explosivity Index") inject SO 2 and water vapor directly into the [stratosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stratosphere "Stratosphere"), where they react to create sulfate aerosol plumes.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-nasa-aerosols-50) Volcanic emissions vary significantly in composition, and have complex chemistry due to the presence of ash particulates and a wide variety of other elements in the plume. Only [stratovolcanoes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stratovolcanoes "Stratovolcanoes") containing primarily [felsic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felsic "Felsic") magmas are responsible for these fluxes, as [mafic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mafic "Mafic") magma erupted in [shield volcanoes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shield_volcanoes "Shield volcanoes") doesn't result in plumes which reach the stratosphere.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-51) However, before the [Industrial Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_Revolution "Industrial Revolution"), dimethyl sulfide pathway was the largest contributor to sulfate aerosol concentrations in a more average year with no major volcanic activity. According to the [IPCC First Assessment Report](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IPCC_First_Assessment_Report "IPCC First Assessment Report"), published in 1990, volcanic emissions usually amounted to around 10 million tons in 1980s, while dimethyl sulfide amounted to 40 million tons. Yet, by that point, the global human-caused emissions of sulfur into the atmosphere became "at least as large" as _all_ natural emissions of sulfur-containing compounds _combined_: they were at less than 3 million tons per year in 1860, and then they increased to 15 million tons in 1900, 40 million tons in 1940 and about 80 millions in 1980. The same report noted that "in the industrialized regions of Europe and North America, anthropogenic emissions dominate over natural emissions by about a factor of ten or even more".[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-IPCC_FAR-52) In the eastern United States in the early 2000s, sulfate particles were estimated to account for 25% or more of all [air pollution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_pollution "Air pollution").[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-EPAHealth-53) Exposure to sulfur dioxide emissions by coal power plants (coal PM 2.5) in the US was associated with 2.1 times greater mortality risk than exposure to PM 2.5 from all sources.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-science2023mortality-54) Meanwhile, the [Southern Hemisphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Southern_Hemisphere "Southern Hemisphere") had much lower concentrations due to being much less densely populated, with an estimated 90% of the human population in the north. In the early 1990s, anthropogenic sulfur dominated in the [Northern Hemisphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northern_Hemisphere "Northern Hemisphere"), where only 16% of annual sulfur emissions were natural, yet amounted for less than half of the emissions in the Southern Hemisphere.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-55)

[![Image 28](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0c/Acid_rain_woods1.JPG/250px-Acid_rain_woods1.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Acid_rain_woods1.JPG)

Acid rain-damaged forest in Europe's [Black Triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Triangle_(region) "Black Triangle (region)")

Such an increase in sulfate aerosol emissions had a variety of effects. At the time, the most visible one was [acid rain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid_rain "Acid rain"), caused by [precipitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precipitation "Precipitation") from clouds carrying high concentrations of sulfate aerosols in the [troposphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Troposphere "Troposphere").[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-56) At its peak, acid rain has eliminated [brook trout](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brook_trout "Brook trout") and some other fish species and insect life from lakes and streams in geographically sensitive areas, such as [Adirondack Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adirondack_Mountains "Adirondack Mountains") in the United States.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-EPASurface-57) Acid rain worsens [soil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil "Soil") function as some of its [microbiota](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbiota "Microbiota") is lost and heavy metals like aluminium are mobilized (spread more easily) while essential nutrients and minerals such as [magnesium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnesium "Magnesium") can leach away because of the same. Ultimately, plants unable to tolerate lowered [pH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PH "PH") are killed, with montane forests being some of the worst-affected [ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem "Ecosystem") due to their regular exposure to sulfate-carrying fog at high altitudes.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-58)[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-EPA:_Forests-59)[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-60)[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-61)[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-62) While acid rain was too dilute to affect human health directly, breathing smog or even any air with elevated sulfate concentrations is known to contribute to [heart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heart "Heart") and [lung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung "Lung") conditions, including [asthma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asthma "Asthma") and [bronchitis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bronchitis "Bronchitis").[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-EPAHealth-53) Further, this form of pollution is linked to [preterm birth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preterm_birth "Preterm birth") and [low birth weight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_birth_weight "Low birth weight"), with a study of 74,671 pregnant women in Beijing finding that every additional 100 μg/m 3 of SO 2 in the air reduced infants' weight by 7.3 g, making it and other forms of air pollution the largest attributable risk factor for low birth weight ever observed.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-63)

[![Image 29](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Estimates_of_past_and_future_SO2_global_anthropogenic_emissions.png/330px-Estimates_of_past_and_future_SO2_global_anthropogenic_emissions.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Estimates_of_past_and_future_SO2_global_anthropogenic_emissions.png)

Early 2010s estimates of past and future anthropogenic global sulfur dioxide emissions, including the [Representative Concentration Pathways](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representative_Concentration_Pathway "Representative Concentration Pathway"). While no [climate change scenario](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_scenario "Climate change scenario") may reach Maximum Feasible Reductions (MFRs), all assume steep declines from today's levels. By 2019, sulfate emission reductions were confirmed to proceed at a very fast rate.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-XuRamanathanVictor-64)

Due largely to the US EPA's [Acid Rain Program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid_Rain_Program "Acid Rain Program"), the U.S. has had a 33% decrease in emissions between 1983 and 2002 (see table). This improvement resulted in part from [flue-gas desulfurization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flue-gas_desulfurization "Flue-gas desulfurization"), a technology that enables SO 2 to be chemically bound in [power plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Power_plant "Power plant") burning sulfur-containing coal or petroleum.

In particular, [calcium oxide (lime)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_oxide "Calcium oxide") reacts with sulfur dioxide to form [calcium sulfite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_sulfite "Calcium sulfite"):

CaO + SO 2 → CaSO 3
Aerobic oxidation of the CaSO 3 gives CaSO 4, [anhydrite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anhydrite "Anhydrite"). Most gypsum sold in Europe comes from flue-gas desulfurization.

To control sulfur emissions, dozens of methods with relatively high efficiencies have been developed for fitting of coal-fired power plants.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-65) Sulfur can be removed from coal during burning by using limestone as a bed material in [fluidized bed combustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluidized_bed_combustion "Fluidized bed combustion").[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-66)

Sulfur can also be removed from fuels before burning, preventing formation of SO 2 when the fuel is burnt. The [Claus process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claus_process "Claus process") is used in refineries to produce sulfur as a byproduct. The [Stretford process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stretford_process "Stretford process") has also been used to remove sulfur from fuel. [Redox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redox "Redox") processes using iron oxides can also be used, for example, Lo-Cat[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-67) or Sulferox.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-68)

Fuel additives such as [calcium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium "Calcium") additives and magnesium carboxylate may be used in marine engines to lower the emission of sulfur dioxide gases into the atmosphere.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-69)

### Effects on ozone layer

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_dioxide&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: Effects on ozone layer")]

Sulfur dioxide aerosols in the stratosphere can contribute to [ozone depletion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone_depletion "Ozone depletion") in the presence of chlorofluorocarbons and other halogenated ozone-depleting substances.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-klobas2017-70) The effects of volcanic eruptions containing sulfur dioxide aerosols on the ozone layer are complex, however. In the absence of anthropogenic or biogenic halogenated compounds in the lower stratosphere, depletion of [dinitrogen pentoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dinitrogen_pentoxide "Dinitrogen pentoxide") in the middle stratosphere associated with its reactivity to the aerosols can promote ozone formation.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-klobas2017-70) Injection of sulfur dioxide and large amounts of water vapor into the stratosphere following the [2022 eruption of Hunga Tonga-Hunga Haʻapai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2022_Hunga_Tonga%E2%80%93Hunga_Ha%CA%BBapai_eruption_and_tsunami "2022 Hunga Tonga–Hunga Haʻapai eruption and tsunami") resulted in altered atmospheric circulation that promoted a decrease in ozone in the southern latitudes but an increase in the tropics.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-71)[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-72) The additional presence of hydrochloric acid in eruptions can result in net ozone depletion.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-klobas2017-70)

### Impact on climate change

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_dioxide&action=edit&section=26 "Edit section: Impact on climate change")]

[![Image 30](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Julsrud_2022_dimming.jpg/330px-Julsrud_2022_dimming.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Julsrud_2022_dimming.jpg)

The observed trends of global dimming and brightening in four major geographic regions. The dimming was greater on the average cloud-free days (red line) than on the average of all days (purple line), strongly suggesting that sulfate aerosols were the cause.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Global_dimming_Julsrud2022-73)

Subsequent research estimated an average reduction in sunlight striking the terrestrial surface of around 4–5% per decade over the late 1950s–1980s, and 2–3% per decade when 1990s were included.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Global_dimming_Stanhill1992-74)[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Global_dimming_Gilgen1998-75)[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Global_dimming_Stanhill2001-76)[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Global_dimming_Liepert2002-77) Notably, solar radiation at the top of the atmosphere did not vary by more than 0.1-0.3% in all that time, strongly suggesting that the reasons for the dimming were on Earth.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Global_dimming_Eddy1982-78)[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Global_dimming_AGU2021-79) Additionally, only visible light and [infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared") radiation were dimmed, rather than the [ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet") part of the spectrum.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-80) Further, the dimming had occurred even when the skies were clear, and it was in fact stronger than during the cloudy days, proving that it was not caused by changes in cloud cover alone.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-81)[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Global_dimming_AGU2021-79)[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Global_dimming_Julsrud2022-73)

[![Image 31](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Physical_Drivers_of_climate_change.svg/500px-Physical_Drivers_of_climate_change.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Physical_Drivers_of_climate_change.svg)

The extent to which physical factors in the atmosphere or on land affect [climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change "Climate change"), including the cooling provided by sulfate aerosols and the dimming they cause. The large [error bar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Error_bar "Error bar") shows that there are still substantial unresolved uncertainties.

Since changes in aerosol concentrations already have an impact on the global climate, they would necessarily influence future projections as well. In fact, it is impossible to fully estimate the warming impact of all [greenhouse gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas "Greenhouse gas") without accounting for the counteracting cooling from aerosols.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Global_dimming_XuRamanathanVictor2018-82)[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Global_dimming_Bellouin2019-83)

Regardless of the current strength of aerosol cooling, all future [climate change scenarios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change_scenario "Climate change scenario") project decreases in particulates and this includes the scenarios where 1.5°C (2.7°F) and 2°C (3.6°F) targets are met: their specific emission reduction targets assume the need to make up for lower dimming.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Global_dimming_IPCC_WGI_SPM-84) Since models estimate that the cooling caused by sulfates is largely equivalent to the warming caused by [atmospheric methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_methane "Atmospheric methane") (and since methane is a relatively short-lived greenhouse gas), it is believed that simultaneous reductions in both would effectively cancel each other out.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Global_dimming_CB2021-85)

[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Global_dimming_Hassan2022-86) Yet, in the recent years, methane concentrations had been increasing at rates exceeding their previous period of peak growth in the 1980s,[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-87)[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-88) with [wetland methane emissions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wetland_methane_emissions "Wetland methane emissions") driving much of the recent growth,[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-89)[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-90) while air pollution is getting cleaned up aggressively.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Global_dimming_Quaas2022-91) These trends are some of the main reasons why 1.5°C (2.7°F) warming is now expected around 2030, as opposed to the mid-2010s estimates where it would not occur until 2040.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Global_dimming_XuRamanathanVictor2018-82)

#### Solar geoengineering

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfur_dioxide&action=edit&section=28 "Edit section: Solar geoengineering")]

[![Image 32: refer to caption and image description](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f7/SPICE_SRM_overview.jpg/500px-SPICE_SRM_overview.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:SPICE_SRM_overview.jpg)

Proposed tethered balloon to inject [aerosols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerosols "Aerosols") into the stratosphere

As the real world had shown the importance of sulfate aerosol concentrations to the global climate, research into the subject accelerated. Formation of the aerosols and their effects on the atmosphere can be studied in the lab, with methods like [ion-chromatography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion_chromatography "Ion chromatography") and [mass spectrometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_spectrometry "Mass spectrometry")[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-92) Samples of actual particles can be recovered from the [stratosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stratosphere "Stratosphere") using balloons or aircraft,[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-93) and remote [satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite "Satellite") were also used for observation.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-:32-94) This data is fed into the [climate models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_model "Climate model"),[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-95) as the necessity of accounting for aerosol cooling to truly understand the rate and evolution of warming had long been apparent, with the [IPCC Second Assessment Report](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IPCC_Second_Assessment_Report "IPCC Second Assessment Report") being the first to include an estimate of their impact on climate, and every major model able to simulate them by the time [IPCC Fourth Assessment Report](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IPCC_Fourth_Assessment_Report "IPCC Fourth Assessment Report") was published in 2007.[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-96) Many scientists also see the other side of this research, which is learning how to cause the same effect artificially.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-97) While discussed around the 1990s, if not earlier,[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-98) stratospheric aerosol injection as a [solar geoengineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_geoengineering "Solar geoengineering") method is best associated with [Paul Crutzen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Crutzen "Paul Crutzen")'s detailed 2006 proposal.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Crutzen062-99) Deploying in the stratosphere ensures that the aerosols are at their most effective, and that the progress of clean air measures would not be reversed: more recent research estimated that even under the highest-emission scenario [RCP 8.5](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representative_Concentration_Pathway "Representative Concentration Pathway"), the addition of stratospheric sulfur required to avoid 4°C (7.2°F) relative to now (and 5°C (9.0°F) relative to the preindustrial) would be effectively offset by the future controls on tropospheric sulfate pollution, and the amount required would be even less for less drastic warming scenarios.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-Visioni2020-100) This spurred a detailed look at its costs and benefits,[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-101) but even with hundreds of studies into the subject completed by the early 2020s, some notable uncertainties remain.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide#cite_note-IPCC_WGI_SRM-102)
