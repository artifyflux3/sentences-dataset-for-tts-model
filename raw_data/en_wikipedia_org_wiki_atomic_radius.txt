Diagram of a helium atom, showing the electron probability density as shades of gray.

The **atomic radius** of a [chemical element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element") is a measure of the size of its [atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom"), usually the mean or typical distance from the center of the [nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus") to the outermost isolated [electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron"). Since the boundary is not a well-defined physical entity, there are various non-equivalent definitions of atomic radius. Four widely used definitions of atomic radius are: [Van der Waals radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_der_Waals_radius "Van der Waals radius"), [ionic radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionic_radius "Ionic radius"), [metallic radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metallic_radius "Metallic radius") and [covalent radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covalent_radius "Covalent radius"). Typically, because of the difficulty to isolate atoms in order to measure their radii separately, atomic radius is measured in a chemically bonded state; however theoretical calculations are simpler when considering atoms in isolation. The dependencies on environment, probe, and state lead to a multiplicity of definitions.

Depending on the definition, the term may apply to atoms in [condensed matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter "Condensed matter"), [covalently bonding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covalent_bonding "Covalent bonding") in [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule"), or in [ionized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionization "Ionization") and [excited states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excited_state "Excited state"); and its value may be obtained through experimental measurements, or computed from theoretical models. The value of the radius may depend on the atom's state and context.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_radius#cite_note-1)

Electrons do not have definite orbits nor sharply defined ranges. Rather, their positions must be described as [probability distributions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_distribution "Probability distribution") that taper off gradually as one moves away from the nucleus, without a sharp cutoff; these are referred to as [atomic orbitals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_orbital "Atomic orbital") or electron clouds. Moreover, in condensed matter and molecules, the electron clouds of the atoms usually overlap to some extent, and some of the electrons may roam over a large region encompassing two or more atoms.

Under most definitions the radii of isolated neutral atoms range between 30 and 300 [pm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Picometre "Picometre") ([trillionths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude_(numbers)#10.E2.88.9212 "Orders of magnitude (numbers)") of a meter), or between 0.3 and 3 [ångströms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m "Ångström"). Therefore, the radius of an atom is more than 10,000 times the [radius of its nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_radius "Nuclear radius") (1–10 [fm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Femtometre "Femtometre")),[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_radius#cite_note-Basdevant-2) and less than 1/1000 of the [wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength "Wavelength") of visible [light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light "Light") (400–700 [nm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanometre "Nanometre")).

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Ethanol-3D-vdW.png/250px-Ethanol-3D-vdW.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ethanol-3D-vdW.png)

The approximate shape of a molecule of [ethanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol "Ethanol"), CH 3 CH 2 OH. Each atom is modeled by a sphere with the element's [Van der Waals radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_der_Waals_radius "Van der Waals radius").

For many purposes, atoms can be modeled as spheres. This is only a crude approximation, but it can provide quantitative explanations and predictions for many phenomena, such as the [density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density "Density") of liquids and solids, the [diffusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffusion "Diffusion") of fluids through [molecular sieves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_sieves "Molecular sieves"), the arrangement of atoms and ions in [crystals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal "Crystal"), and the [size and shape of molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-filling_model "Space-filling model").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The first to estimate the radius of an atom was [Johann Chrysostom Magnenus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Chrysostom_Magnenus "Johann Chrysostom Magnenus") in 1646. He was at [Mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_(liturgy) "Mass (liturgy)") and noticed the smell of incense permeating the church. He knew the size of the incense and estimated the size of the church. He presumed that he could detect the incense if one atom was in each nostril. He also presumed that the incense was distributed homogenously throughout the church. With these assumptions he was able to estimate the size of an atom to be about 10 to the power of −24 cubic metres. (The units he used have been converted to metric to make comparisons with later estimates easier.) Taking the cube root this gives an estimate of the atomic radius to be about 10 to the power of −8 metres. This is somewhat larger than current estimates but given the assumptions made in the calculation is very good. These calculations were published in his work _Democritus reviviscens sive de atomis_.

The concept of atomic radius was preceded in the 19th century by the concept of atomic volume, a relative measure of how much space would on average an atom occupy in a given solid or liquid material.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_radius#cite_note-3) By the end of the century this term was also used in an absolute sense, as a [molar volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molar_volume "Molar volume") divided by [Avogadro constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avogadro_constant "Avogadro constant").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_radius#cite_note-4) Such a volume is different for different crystalline forms even of the same compound,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_radius#cite_note-5) but physicists used it for rough, order-of-magnitude estimates of the atomic size, getting 10−8–10−7 cm for copper.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_radius#cite_note-6)

The earliest estimates of the atomic size was made by opticians in the 1830s, particularly [Cauchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augustin-Louis_Cauchy "Augustin-Louis Cauchy"),[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_radius#cite_note-:0-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_radius#cite_note-8) who developed models of light [dispersion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dispersion_(optics) "Dispersion (optics)") assuming a lattice of connected "molecules".[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_radius#cite_note-9) In 1857 [Clausius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolf_Clausius "Rudolf Clausius") developed a [gas-kinetic model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases "Kinetic theory of gases") which included the equation for [mean free path](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_free_path "Mean free path"). In the 1870s it was used to estimate gas molecule sizes, as well as an aforementioned comparison with [visible light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visible_light "Visible light")[wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength "Wavelength") and an estimate from the thickness of [soap bubble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soap_bubble "Soap bubble") film at which its contractile force rapidly diminishes.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_radius#cite_note-10) By 1900, various estimates of mercury atom diameter averaged around 275±20 pm[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_radius#cite_note-:0-7) (modern estimates give 300±10 pm, see below).

In 1920, shortly after it had become possible to determine the sizes of atoms using [X-ray crystallography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_crystallography "X-ray crystallography"), it was suggested that all atoms of the same element have the same radii.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_radius#cite_note-11) However, in 1923, when more crystal data had become available, it was found that the approximation of an atom as a sphere does not necessarily hold when comparing the same atom in different crystal structures.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_radius#cite_note-12)

Widely used definitions of atomic radius include:

*   [Van der Waals radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_der_Waals_radius "Van der Waals radius"): In the simplest definition, half the minimum distance between the nuclei of two atoms of the element that are not otherwise bound by covalent or metallic interactions.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_radius#cite_note-Pauling1945-13) The Van der Waals radius may be defined even for elements (such as metals) in which Van der Waals forces are dominated by other interactions. Because [Van der Waals interactions arise through quantum fluctuations of the atomic polarisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_der_Waals_force "Van der Waals force"), the polarisability (which can usually be measured or calculated more easily) may be used to define the Van der Waals radius indirectly.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_radius#cite_note-Federov2018-14)

*   [Ionic radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionic_radius "Ionic radius"): the nominal radius of the ions of an element in a specific ionization state, deduced from the spacing of atomic nuclei in crystalline salts that include that ion. In principle, the spacing between two adjacent oppositely charged ions (the [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_length "Bond length") of the [ionic bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionic_bond "Ionic bond") between them) should equal the sum of their ionic radii.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_radius#cite_note-Pauling1945-13)
*   [Covalent radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covalent_radius "Covalent radius"): the nominal radius of the atoms of an element when [covalently bound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covalent_bond "Covalent bond") to other atoms, as deduced from the separation between the atomic nuclei in molecules. In principle, the distance between two atoms that are bound to each other in a molecule (the length of that covalent bond) should equal the sum of their covalent radii.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_radius#cite_note-Pauling1945-13)
*   [Metallic radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metallic_radius "Metallic radius"): the nominal radius of atoms of an element when joined to other atoms by [metallic bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metallic_bond "Metallic bond").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]
*   [Bohr radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohr_radius "Bohr radius"): the radius of the lowest-energy electron orbit predicted by [Bohr model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohr_model "Bohr model") of the atom (1913).[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_radius#cite_note-bohrI-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_radius#cite_note-bohrII-16) It is only applicable to [atoms and ions with a single electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen-like_atom "Hydrogen-like atom"), such as [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen"), singly ionized [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium"), and [positronium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positronium "Positronium"). Although the model itself is now obsolete, the Bohr radius for the hydrogen atom is still regarded as an important physical constant, because it is equivalent to the quantum-mechanical most probable distance of the electron from the nucleus.

Explanation of the general trends
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Atomic_radius&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Explanation of the general trends")]

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4e/Atomic_number_to_radius_graph.png/250px-Atomic_number_to_radius_graph.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Atomic_number_to_radius_graph.png)

A graph comparing the atomic radius of elements with atomic numbers 1–100. Accuracy of ±5 pm.

Electrons in atoms fill [electron shells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_shell "Electron shell") from the lowest available energy level. As a consequence of the [Aufbau principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aufbau_principle "Aufbau principle"), each new [period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Period_(periodic_table) "Period (periodic table)") begins with the first two elements filling the next unoccupied [s-orbital](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_orbital "Atomic orbital"). Because an atom's s-orbital electrons are typically farthest from the nucleus, this results in a significant increase in atomic radius with the first elements of each period.

The atomic radius of each element generally decreases across each period due to an increasing number of protons, since an increase in the number of protons increases the attractive force acting on the atom's electrons. The greater attraction draws the electrons closer to the protons, decreasing the size of the atom. Down each group, the atomic radius of each element typically increases because there are more occupied electron [energy levels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_shell "Electron shell") and therefore a greater distance between protons and electrons.

The increasing nuclear charge is partly counterbalanced by the increasing number of electrons—a phenomenon that is known as [shielding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shielding_effect "Shielding effect")—which explains why the size of atoms usually increases down each column despite an increase in attractive force from the nucleus. Electron shielding causes the attraction of an atom's nucleus on its electrons to decrease, so electrons occupying higher energy states farther from the nucleus experience reduced attractive force, increasing the size of the atom. However, elements in the 5d-block ([lutetium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lutetium "Lutetium") to [mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(element) "Mercury (element)")) are much smaller than this trend predicts due to the weak shielding of the 4f-subshell. This phenomenon is known as the [lanthanide contraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lanthanide_contraction "Lanthanide contraction"). A similar phenomenon exists for [actinides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Actinoid_contraction "Actinoid contraction"); however, the general instability of [transuranic elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transuranic_element "Transuranic element") makes measurements for the remainder of the 5f-block difficult and for transactinides nearly impossible. Finally, for sufficiently heavy elements, the atomic radius may be decreased by [relativistic effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_chemistry "Relativistic quantum chemistry").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_radius#cite_note-18) This is a consequence of electrons near the strongly charged nucleus traveling at a sufficient fraction of the speed of light to gain a nontrivial amount of mass.

The following table summarizes the main phenomena that influence the atomic radius of an element:

| Factor | Principle | increase in... | _tend to_ | effect on radius |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| electron shells | quantum mechanics | principal and azimuthal [quantum numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_numbers "Quantum numbers") | increase down each column | increases the atomic radius |
| nuclear charge | attractive force acting on electrons by protons in nucleus | atomic number | increase along each period (left to right) | decreases the atomic radius |
| shielding | repulsive force acting on outermost shell electrons by inner electrons | number of electrons in inner shells | reduce the effect of nuclear charge | increases the atomic radius |

### Lanthanide contraction

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Atomic_radius&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Lanthanide contraction")]

The electrons in the 4f-[subshell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_shell "Electron shell"), which is progressively filled from [lanthanum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lanthanum "Lanthanum") (_[Z](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic\_number "Atomic number")_= 57) to [ytterbium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ytterbium "Ytterbium") (_Z_= 70), are not particularly effective at shielding the increasing nuclear charge from the sub-shells further out. The elements immediately following the [lanthanides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lanthanide "Lanthanide") have atomic radii which are smaller than would be expected and which are almost identical to the atomic radii of the elements immediately above them.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_radius#cite_note-Jolly_contract-19) Hence [lutetium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lutetium "Lutetium") is in fact slightly smaller than [yttrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yttrium "Yttrium"), [hafnium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hafnium "Hafnium") has virtually the same atomic radius (and chemistry) as [zirconium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zirconium "Zirconium"), and [tantalum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tantalum "Tantalum") has an atomic radius similar to [niobium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niobium "Niobium"), and so forth. The effect of the lanthanide contraction is noticeable up to [platinum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platinum "Platinum") (_Z_= 78), after which it is masked by a [relativistic effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_effect "Relativistic effect") known as the [inert-pair effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inert-pair_effect "Inert-pair effect").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Due to lanthanide contraction, the 5 following observations can be drawn:

1.   The size of Ln 3+ ions regularly decreases with atomic number. According to [Fajans' rules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fajans%27_rules "Fajans' rules"), decrease in size of Ln 3+ ions increases the covalent character and decreases the basic character between Ln 3+ and OH− ions in Ln(OH)3, to the point that Yb(OH)3 and Lu(OH)3 can dissolve with difficulty in hot concentrated NaOH. Hence the order of size of Ln 3+ is given: 

 La 3+> Ce 3+> ..., ... > Lu 3+.
2.   There is a regular decrease in their ionic radii.
3.   There is a regular decrease in their tendency to act as a reducing agent, with an increase in atomic number.
4.   The second and third rows of d-block transition elements are quite close in properties.
5.   Consequently, these elements occur together in natural minerals and are difficult to separate.

### d-block contraction

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Atomic_radius&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: d-block contraction")]

The d-block contraction is less pronounced than the lanthanide contraction but arises from a similar cause. In this case, it is the poor shielding capacity of the 3d-electrons which affects the atomic radii and chemistries of the elements immediately following the first row of the [transition metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transition_metal "Transition metal"), from [gallium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gallium "Gallium") (_Z_= 31) to [bromine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bromine "Bromine") (_Z_= 35).[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_radius#cite_note-Jolly_contract-19)
