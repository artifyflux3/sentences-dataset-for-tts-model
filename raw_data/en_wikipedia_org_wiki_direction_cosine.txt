In [analytic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_geometry "Analytic geometry"), the **direction cosines** (or **directional cosines**) of a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") are the [cosines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosine "Cosine") of the angles between the vector and the three positive coordinate axes. Equivalently, they are the contributions of each component of the [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)") to a [unit vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector") in that [direction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direction_(geometry) "Direction (geometry)").

Vector **v** in ⁠![Image 2: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5)⁠

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Direction_cosine_unit_vector.svg/250px-Direction_cosine_unit_vector.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Direction_cosine_unit_vector.svg)

Direction cosines and direction angles for the unit vector ![Image 4: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {\mathbf {v} }{|\mathbf {v} |}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c36429b6b1b46996c4f449ec15d801927954673f)

If **v** is a [Euclidean vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") in [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space")[Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), ⁠![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/deb17c1074c77de2cf88d45bcd6d7a795b0f5d44)⁠

![Image 6: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} =v_{x}\mathbf {e} _{x}+v_{y}\mathbf {e} _{y}+v_{z}\mathbf {e} _{z},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4ee63b95240fa17f06788a3ad928110d27eb7287)

where **e**_x_, **e**_y_, **e**_z_ are the [standard basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_basis "Standard basis") in Cartesian notation, then the direction cosines are

![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\begin{alignedat}{2}\alpha &{}=\cos a={\frac {\mathbf {v} \cdot \mathbf {e} _{x}}{\Vert \mathbf {v} \Vert }}&&{}={\frac {v_{x}}{\sqrt {v_{x}^{2}+v_{y}^{2}+v_{z}^{2}}}},\\\beta &{}=\cos b={\frac {\mathbf {v} \cdot \mathbf {e} _{y}}{\Vert \mathbf {v} \Vert }}&&{}={\frac {v_{y}}{\sqrt {v_{x}^{2}+v_{y}^{2}+v_{z}^{2}}}},\\\gamma &{}=\cos c={\frac {\mathbf {v} \cdot \mathbf {e} _{z}}{\Vert \mathbf {v} \Vert }}&&{}={\frac {v_{z}}{\sqrt {v_{x}^{2}+v_{y}^{2}+v_{z}^{2}}}}.\end{alignedat}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3c73274f76b023bb94737dc4df4a67afecb0d2b7)

It follows that by squaring each equation and adding the results

![Image 8: {\displaystyle \cos ^{2}a+\cos ^{2}b+\cos ^{2}c=\alpha ^{2}+\beta ^{2}+\gamma ^{2}=1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/890d231814bd1b5a20189e15005a114a7e611879)

Here _α_, _β_, _γ_ are the direction cosines and the Cartesian coordinates of the [unit vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector")![Image 9: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {\mathbf {v} }{|\mathbf {v} |}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f49ace27d533f5e9eda893d8c1d59ade54fb606) and _a_, _b_, _c_ are the direction angles of the vector **v**.

The direction angles _a_, _b_, _c_ are [acute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acute_angle "Acute angle") or [obtuse angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obtuse_angle "Obtuse angle"), i.e., 0 ≤ _a_ ≤ π, 0 ≤ _b_ ≤ _π_ and 0 ≤ _c_ ≤ _π_, and they denote the angles formed between **v** and the unit basis vectors **e**_x_, **e**_y_, **e**_z_.

More generally, **direction cosine** refers to the cosine of the angle between any two [vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector"). They are useful for forming [direction cosine matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector#Conversion_between_multiple_Cartesian_bases "Euclidean vector") that express one set of [orthonormal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal "Orthonormal")[basis vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_vectors "Basis vectors") in terms of another set, or for expressing a known [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") in a different basis. Simply put, direction cosines provide an easy method of representing the direction of a vector in a Cartesian coordinate system.

### Determining angles between two vectors

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Direction_cosine&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Determining angles between two vectors")]

If vectors **u** and **v** have direction cosines (_α u_, _β u_, _γ u_) and (_α v_, _β v_, _γ v_) respectively, with an angle θ between them, their units vectors are

![Image 10: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {\hat {u}} &={\frac {1}{\sqrt {u_{x}^{2}+u_{y}^{2}+u_{z}^{2}}}}\left(u_{x}\mathbf {e} _{x}+u_{y}\mathbf {e} _{y}+u_{z}\mathbf {e} _{z}\right)=\alpha _{u}\mathbf {e} _{x}+\beta _{u}\mathbf {e} _{y}+\gamma _{u}\mathbf {e} _{z}\\\mathbf {\hat {v}} &={\frac {1}{\sqrt {v_{x}^{2}+v_{y}^{2}+v_{z}^{2}}}}\left(v_{x}\mathbf {e} _{x}+v_{y}\mathbf {e} _{y}+v_{z}\mathbf {e} _{z}\right)=\alpha _{v}\mathbf {e} _{x}+\beta _{v}\mathbf {e} _{y}+\gamma _{v}\mathbf {e} _{z}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/890d9bfc87985ce37447ba19386d0de3510d6148) Taking the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") of these two unit vectors yield, ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathbf {{\hat {u}}\cdot {\hat {v}}} =\alpha _{u}\alpha _{v}+\beta _{u}\beta _{v}+\gamma _{u}\gamma _{v}=\cos \theta ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e369b0452d5e20e5b839d6f79e4b3e7cf73b2bb) where θ is the angle between the two unit vectors, and is also the angle between **u** and **v**.

Since θ is a geometric angle, and is never negative. Therefore only the positive value of the dot product is taken, yielding us the final result,

![Image 12: {\displaystyle \theta =\arccos \left(\alpha _{u}\alpha _{v}+\beta _{u}\beta _{v}+\gamma _{u}\gamma _{v}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d8ac1ede3010a08e023daf12fd69a2cc103e171)
