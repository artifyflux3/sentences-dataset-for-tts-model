For the conditions used in thermodynamic evaluations, see [Standard state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_state "Standard state").

**Standard temperature and pressure** (**STP**) or **standard conditions for temperature and pressure** are various [standard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technical_standard "Technical standard") sets of conditions for experimental measurements used to allow comparisons to be made between different sets of data. The most used standards are those of the [International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Chemistry "International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry") (IUPAC) and the [National Institute of Standards and Technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology "National Institute of Standards and Technology") (NIST), although these are not universally accepted. Other organizations have established a variety of other definitions.

In [industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industry_(manufacturing) "Industry (manufacturing)") and [commerce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commerce "Commerce"), the standard conditions for temperature and pressure are often necessary for expressing the volumes of gases and liquids and related quantities such as the rate of [volumetric flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volumetric_flow "Volumetric flow") (the volumes of gases vary significantly with temperature and pressure): [standard cubic meters per second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_cubic_meters_per_second "Standard cubic meters per second") (Sm 3/s), and normal cubic meters per second (Nm 3/s).

Many technical publications (books, journals, advertisements for equipment and machinery) simply state "standard conditions" without specifying them; often substituting the term with older "normal conditions", or "NC". In special cases this can lead to confusion and errors. Good practice always incorporates the reference conditions of temperature and pressure. If not stated, some room environment conditions are supposed, close to 1 atm pressure, 273.15 K (0°C), and 0% humidity.

Definitions
-----------

In chemistry, IUPAC changed its definition of standard temperature and pressure in 1982:[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure#cite_note-IUPAC_print-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure#cite_note-IUPAC_web-2)

*   Until 1982, STP was defined as a [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature") of 273.15[K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin") (0°C, 32°F) and an [absolute pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_pressure "Absolute pressure") of exactly 1[atm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_atmosphere_(unit) "Standard atmosphere (unit)") (101.325[kPa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit) "Pascal (unit)")).
*   Since 1982, STP has been defined as a [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature") of 273.15[K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin") (0°C, 32°F) and an [absolute pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_pressure "Absolute pressure") of exactly 1[bar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bar_(unit) "Bar (unit)") (100 kPa, 10 5[Pa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit) "Pascal (unit)")).

[NIST](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology "National Institute of Standards and Technology") uses a temperature of 20°C (293.15 K, 68°F) and an absolute pressure of 1 atm (14.696 psi, 101.325 kPa).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure#cite_note-3) This standard is also called **normal temperature and pressure** (abbreviated as **NTP**). However, a common temperature and pressure in use by NIST for thermodynamic experiments is 298.15 K (25°C, 77°F) and 1 [bar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bar_(unit) "Bar (unit)") (14.5038 [psi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pounds_per_square_inch "Pounds per square inch"), 100 [kPa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit) "Pascal (unit)")).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure#cite_note-5) NIST also uses 15°C (288.15 K, 59°F) for the temperature compensation of refined petroleum products, despite noting that these two values are not exactly consistent with each other.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure#cite_note-6)

The **ISO 13443** standard reference conditions for natural gas and similar fluids are 288.15 K (15.00°C; 59.00°F) and 101.325 kPa;[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure#cite_note-ISO13443-7) by contrast, the [American Petroleum Institute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Petroleum_Institute "American Petroleum Institute") adopts 60°F (15.56°C; 288.71 K).[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure#cite_note-8)

### Past uses

Before 1918, many professionals and scientists using the metric system of units defined the standard reference conditions of temperature and pressure for expressing gas volumes as being 15°C (288.15 K; 59.00°F) and 101.325[kPa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit) "Pascal (unit)") (1.00[atm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit) "Atmosphere (unit)"); 760[Torr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torr "Torr")). During those same years, the most commonly used standard reference conditions for people using the [imperial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_units "Imperial units") or [U.S. customary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_customary_units "United States customary units") systems was 60°F (15.56°C; 288.71 K) and 14.696[psi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pound-force_per_square_inch "Pound-force per square inch") (1 atm) because it was almost universally used by the oil and gas industries worldwide. The above definitions are no longer the most commonly used in either system of units.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure#cite_note-9)

### Current use

Many different definitions of standard reference conditions are currently being used by organizations all over the world. The table below lists a few of them, but there are more. Some of these organizations used other standards in the past. For example, IUPAC has, since 1982, defined standard reference conditions as being 0°C and 100 kPa (1 bar), in contrast to its old standard of 0°C and 101.325 kPa (1 atm).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure#cite_note-IUPAC_web-2) The new value is the mean atmospheric pressure at an altitude of about 112 metres, which is closer to the worldwide median altitude of human habitation (194 m).[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure#cite_note-10)

Natural gas companies in Europe, Australia, and South America have adopted 15°C (59°F) and 101.325 kPa (14.696 psi) as their standard gas volume reference conditions, used as the base values for defining the [standard cubic meter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_cubic_meter "Standard cubic meter").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure#cite_note-13) Also, the [International Organization for Standardization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization "International Organization for Standardization") (ISO), the [United States Environmental Protection Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency "United States Environmental Protection Agency") (EPA) and [National Institute of Standards and Technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology "National Institute of Standards and Technology") (NIST) each have more than one definition of standard reference conditions in their various standards and regulations.

International Standard Atmosphere
---------------------------------

In [aeronautics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aeronautics "Aeronautics") and [fluid dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_dynamics "Fluid dynamics") the "[International Standard Atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Standard_Atmosphere "International Standard Atmosphere")" (ISA) is a specification of pressure, temperature, density, and speed of sound at each altitude. At [standard mean sea level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_mean_sea_level "Standard mean sea level") it specifies a temperature of 15°C (59°F), pressure of 101,325 pascals (14.6959 psi) (1 [atm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit) "Atmosphere (unit)")), and a density of 1.2250 kilograms per cubic meter (0.07647 lb/cu ft). It also specifies a temperature [lapse rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lapse_rate "Lapse rate") of −6.5°C (−11.7°F) per km (approximately −2°C (−3.6°F) per 1,000 ft).[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure#cite_note-aeromech.usyd.edu.au-47)[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure#cite_note-Batchelor_1967-48)

The International Standard Atmosphere is representative of atmospheric conditions at mid latitudes. In the US this information is specified the [U.S. Standard Atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Standard_Atmosphere "U.S. Standard Atmosphere") which is identical to the "International Standard Atmosphere" at all altitudes up to 65,000 feet above sea level.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Standard laboratory conditions
------------------------------

Because many definitions of standard temperature and pressure differ in temperature significantly from standard laboratory temperatures (e.g. 0°C vs. ~28°C), reference is often made to "standard laboratory conditions" (a term deliberately chosen to be different from the term "standard conditions for temperature and pressure", despite its semantic near identity when interpreted literally). However, what is a "standard" [laboratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laboratory "Laboratory") temperature and pressure is inevitably geography-bound, given that different parts of the world differ in climate, altitude and the degree of use of heat/cooling in the workplace. For example, schools in [New South Wales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_South_Wales "New South Wales"), [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia "Australia") use 25°C at 100 kPa for standard laboratory conditions.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure#cite_note-49)[ASTM International](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASTM_International "ASTM International") has published [Standard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Technical_standard "Technical standard") ASTM E41- Terminology Relating to Conditioning and hundreds of special conditions for particular materials and [test methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Test_method "Test method"). Other [standards organizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standards_organization "Standards organization") also have specialized standard test conditions.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Molar volume of a gas
---------------------

It is as important to indicate the applicable reference conditions of temperature and pressure when stating the [molar volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molar_volume "Molar volume") of a gas[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure#cite_note-50) as it is when expressing a gas volume or volumetric flow rate. Stating the molar volume of a gas without indicating the reference conditions of temperature and pressure has very little meaning and can cause confusion.

The molar volume of gases around STP and at atmospheric pressure can be calculated with an accuracy that is usually sufficient by using the [ideal gas law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas_law "Ideal gas law"). The molar volume of any ideal gas may be calculated at various standard reference conditions as shown below:

*   _V_ m = 8.3145 × 273.15 / 101.325 = 22.414[dm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimetre "Decimetre")3/mol at 0°C and 101.325 kPa
*   _V_ m = 8.3145 × 273.15 / 100.000 = 22.711 dm 3/mol at 0°C and 100 kPa
*   _V_ m = 8.3145 × 288.15 / 101.325 = 23.645 dm 3/mol at 15°C and 101.325 kPa
*   _V_ m = 8.3145 × 298.15 / 101.325 = 24.466 dm 3/mol at 25°C and 101.325 kPa
*   _V_ m = 8.3145 × 298.15 / 100.000 = 24.790 dm 3/mol at 25°C and 100 kPa
*   _V_ m = 10.7316 × 519.67 / 14.696 = 379.48 ft 3/lbmol at 60°F and 14.696 psi (or about 0.8366 ft 3/gram mole)
*   _V_ m = 10.7316 × 519.67 / 14.730 = 378.61 ft 3/lbmol at 60°F and 14.73 psi

Technical literature can be confusing because many authors fail to explain whether they are using the [ideal gas constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas_constant "Ideal gas constant")_R_, or the specific gas constant _R_ s. The relationship between the two constants is _R_ s = _R_ / _m_, where _m_ is the [molecular mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_mass "Molecular mass") of the gas.

The [US Standard Atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._Standard_Atmosphere "U.S. Standard Atmosphere") (USSA) uses 8.31432 m 3·Pa/(mol·K) as the value of _R_. However, the USSA in 1976 does recognize that this value is not consistent with the values of the [Avogadro constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avogadro_constant "Avogadro constant") and the [Boltzmann constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_constant "Boltzmann constant").[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure#cite_note-USSA1976-51)
