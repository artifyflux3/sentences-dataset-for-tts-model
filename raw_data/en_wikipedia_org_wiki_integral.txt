[![Image 1: Definite integral example](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Integral_example.svg/330px-Integral_example.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Integral_example.svg)

A definite integral of a function can be represented as the [signed area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signed_area "Signed area") of the region bounded by its graph and the horizontal axis; in the above graph as an example, the integral of ![Image 2: {\displaystyle f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/202945cce41ecebb6f643f31d119c514bec7a074) is the yellow (−) area subtracted from the blue (+) area

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), an **integral** is the continuous analog of a [sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Summation "Summation"), which is used to calculate [areas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area"), [volumes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume"), and their generalizations. Integration, the process of computing an integral, is one of the two fundamental operations of [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"),[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-1) the other being [differentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative"). Integration was initially used to solve problems in mathematics and [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), such as finding the **area under a curve**, or determining displacement from velocity. Usage of integration expanded to a wide variety of scientific fields thereafter.

A **definite integral** computes the [signed area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signed_area "Signed area") of the region in the plane that is bounded by the [graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_function "Graph of a function") of a given [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") between two points in the [real line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_line "Real line"). Conventionally, areas above the horizontal [axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_axis "Coordinate axis") of the plane are positive while areas below are negative. Integrals also refer to the concept of an _[antiderivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiderivative "Antiderivative")_, a function whose [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") is the given function; in this case, they are also called _indefinite integrals_. The [fundamental theorem of calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_calculus "Fundamental theorem of calculus") relates definite integration to differentiation and provides a method to compute the definite integral of a function when its antiderivative is known; differentiation and integration are [inverse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_function "Inverse function") operations.

Although methods of calculating areas and volumes dated from [ancient Greek mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_mathematics "Ancient Greek mathematics"), the principles of integration were formulated independently by [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton") and [Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz "Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz") in the late 17th century, who thought of the area under a curve as an infinite sum of rectangles of [infinitesimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal "Infinitesimal") width. [Bernhard Riemann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernhard_Riemann "Bernhard Riemann") later gave a rigorous definition of integrals, which is based on a limiting procedure that approximates the area of a [curvilinear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates "Curvilinear coordinates") region by breaking the region into infinitesimally thin vertical slabs. In the early 20th century, [Henri Lebesgue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lebesgue "Henri Lebesgue") generalized Riemann's formulation by introducing what is now referred to as the [Lebesgue integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_integration "Lebesgue integration"); it is more general than Riemann's in the sense that a wider class of functions are Lebesgue-integrable.

Integrals may be generalized depending on the type of the function as well as the [domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_(mathematical_analysis) "Domain (mathematical analysis)") over which the integration is performed. For example, a [line integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_integral "Line integral") is defined for functions of two or more variables, and the [interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)") of integration is replaced by a curve connecting two points in space. In a [surface integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_integral "Surface integral"), the curve is replaced by a piece of a [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(mathematics) "Surface (mathematics)") in [three-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space").

### Pre-calculus integration

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Integral&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Pre-calculus integration")]

The first documented systematic technique capable of determining integrals is the [method of exhaustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Method_of_exhaustion "Method of exhaustion") of the [ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece") astronomer [Eudoxus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eudoxus_of_Cnidus "Eudoxus of Cnidus") and philosopher [Democritus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democritus "Democritus") (_ca._ 370 BC), which sought to find areas and volumes by breaking them up into an infinite number of divisions for which the area or volume was known.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-2) This method was further developed and employed by [Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes") in the 3rd century BC and used to calculate the [area of a circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area_of_a_circle "Area of a circle"), the [surface area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_area "Surface area") and [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume") of a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere"), area of an [ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse"), the area under a [parabola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabola "Parabola"), the volume of a segment of a [paraboloid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paraboloid "Paraboloid") of revolution, the volume of a segment of a [hyperboloid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperboloid "Hyperboloid") of revolution, and the area of a [spiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spiral "Spiral").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-3)

A similar method was independently developed in [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") around the 3rd century AD by [Liu Hui](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liu_Hui "Liu Hui"), who used it to find the area of the circle. This method was later used in the 5th century by Chinese father-and-son mathematicians [Zu Chongzhi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zu_Chongzhi "Zu Chongzhi") and [Zu Geng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zu_Geng_(mathematician) "Zu Geng (mathematician)") to find the volume of a sphere.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-4)

In the Middle East, Hasan Ibn al-Haytham, Latinized as [Alhazen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alhazen "Alhazen") (c. 965– c. 1040 AD) derived a formula for the sum of [fourth powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_power "Fourth power").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-5) Alhazen determined the equations to calculate the area enclosed by the curve represented by ![Image 3: {\displaystyle y=x^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7671c58f963c43ee6c236f3e3ed6c54a9c4bfdae) (which translates to the integral ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \int x^{k}\,dx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c636c2d61e234c5306b1682165ab4930b76da88) in contemporary notation), for any given non-negative integer value of ![Image 5: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-6) He used the results to carry out what would now be called an integration of this function, where the formulae for the sums of integral squares and fourth powers allowed him to calculate the volume of a [paraboloid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paraboloid "Paraboloid").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-7)

The next significant advances in integral calculus did not begin to appear until the 17th century. At this time, the work of [Cavalieri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bonaventura_Cavalieri "Bonaventura Cavalieri") with his [method of indivisibles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Method_of_indivisibles "Method of indivisibles"), and work by [Fermat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_de_Fermat "Pierre de Fermat"), began to lay the foundations of modern calculus,[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-8) with Cavalieri computing the integrals of _x_ _n_ up to degree _n_ = 9 in [Cavalieri's quadrature formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cavalieri%27s_quadrature_formula "Cavalieri's quadrature formula").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-9) The case _n_ = −1 required the invention of a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)"), the [hyperbolic logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_logarithm "Hyperbolic logarithm"), achieved by [quadrature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrature_(mathematics) "Quadrature (mathematics)") of the [hyperbola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbola "Hyperbola") in 1647.

Further steps were made in the early 17th century by [Barrow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Barrow "Isaac Barrow") and [Torricelli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evangelista_Torricelli "Evangelista Torricelli"), who provided the first hints of a connection between integration and [differentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_calculus "Differential calculus"). Barrow provided the first proof of the [fundamental theorem of calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_calculus "Fundamental theorem of calculus").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-10)[Wallis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Wallis "John Wallis") generalized Cavalieri's method, computing integrals of x to a general power, including negative powers and fractional powers.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-11)

The major advance in integration came in the 17th century with the independent discovery of the [fundamental theorem of calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_calculus "Fundamental theorem of calculus") by [Leibniz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz "Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz") and [Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-12) The theorem demonstrates a connection between integration and differentiation. This connection, combined with the comparative ease of differentiation, can be exploited to calculate integrals. In particular, the fundamental theorem of calculus allows one to solve a much broader class of problems. Equal in importance is the comprehensive mathematical framework that both Leibniz and Newton developed. Given the name infinitesimal calculus, it allowed for precise analysis of functions with continuous domains. This framework eventually became modern [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"), whose notation for integrals is drawn directly from the work of Leibniz.

While Newton and Leibniz provided a systematic approach to integration, their work lacked a degree of [rigour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigor#Mathematical_rigour "Rigor"). [Bishop Berkeley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Berkeley "George Berkeley") memorably attacked the vanishing increments used by Newton, calling them "[ghosts of departed quantities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Analyst#Content "The Analyst")".[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-13) Calculus acquired a firmer footing with the development of [limits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_(mathematics) "Limit (mathematics)"). Integration was first rigorously formalized, using limits, by [Riemann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernhard_Riemann "Bernhard Riemann").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-14) Although all bounded [piecewise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piecewise "Piecewise") continuous functions are Riemann-integrable on a bounded interval, subsequently more general functions were considered—particularly in the context of [Fourier analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_analysis "Fourier analysis")—to which Riemann's definition does not apply, and [Lebesgue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lebesgue "Henri Lebesgue") formulated a [different definition of integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#Lebesgue_integral), founded in [measure theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measure_(mathematics) "Measure (mathematics)") (a subfield of [real analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_analysis "Real analysis")). Other definitions of integral, extending Riemann's and Lebesgue's approaches, were proposed. These approaches based on the real number system are the ones most common today, but alternative approaches exist, such as a definition of integral as the [standard part](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_part "Standard part") of an infinite Riemann sum, based on the [hyperreal number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperreal_number "Hyperreal number") system.

### Historical notation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Integral&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Historical notation")]

The notation for the indefinite integral was introduced by [Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz "Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz") in 1675.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-15) He adapted the [integral symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_symbol "Integral symbol"), **∫**, from the letter _ſ_ ([long s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long_s "Long s")), standing for _summa_ (written as _ſumma_; Latin for "sum" or "total"). The modern notation for the definite integral, with limits above and below the integral sign, was first used by [Joseph Fourier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Fourier "Joseph Fourier") in _Mémoires_ of the French Academy around 1819–1820, reprinted in his book of 1822.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-16)

[Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton") used a small vertical bar above a variable to indicate integration, or placed the variable inside a box. The vertical bar was easily confused with **.**_x_ or _x_′, which are used to indicate differentiation, and the box notation was difficult for printers to reproduce, so these notations were not widely adopted.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-17)

### First use of the term

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Integral&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: First use of the term")]

The term was first printed in Latin by [Jacob Bernoulli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacob_Bernoulli "Jacob Bernoulli") in 1690: "Ergo et horum Integralia aequantur".[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-18)

Terminology and notation
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Integral&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Terminology and notation")]

In general, the integral of a [real-valued function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real-valued_function "Real-valued function")_f_(_x_) with respect to a real variable _x_ on an interval [_a_, _b_] is written as

![Image 6: {\displaystyle \int _{a}^{b}f(x)\,dx.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/249ee3fdbced31dfc328ff357f67bb134ca66b8d)
The integral sign ∫ represents integration. The symbol _dx_, called the [differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_(infinitesimal) "Differential (infinitesimal)") of the variable _x_, indicates that the variable of integration is _x_. The function _f_(_x_) is called the _integrand_, the points _a_ and _b_ are called the limits (or bounds) of integration, and the integral is said to be over the interval [_a_, _b_], called the interval of integration.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-:1-19) A function is said to be _integrable_ if its integral over its domain is finite. If limits are specified, the integral is called a definite integral.

When the limits are omitted, as in

![Image 7: {\displaystyle \int f(x)\,dx,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1bbd50daec1c6f3767a42b842a9b203d840e9934)
the integral is called an indefinite integral, which represents a class of functions (the [antiderivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiderivative "Antiderivative")) whose derivative is the integrand.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-20) The [fundamental theorem of calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_calculus "Fundamental theorem of calculus") relates the evaluation of definite integrals to indefinite integrals. There are several extensions of the notation for integrals to encompass integration on unbounded domains and/or in multiple dimensions (see later sections of this article).

In advanced settings, it is not uncommon to leave out _dx_ when only the simple [Riemann integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_integral "Riemann integral") is being used, or the exact type of integral is immaterial. For instance, one might write ![Image 8: {\textstyle \int _{a}^{b}(c_{1}f+c_{2}g)=c_{1}\int _{a}^{b}f+c_{2}\int _{a}^{b}g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73dcac73f0c30b213d7df13d5a0416d6069a75b0) to express the linearity of the integral, a property shared by the Riemann integral and all generalizations thereof.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-21)

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4a/Integral_approximations_J.svg/250px-Integral_approximations_J.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Integral_approximations_J.svg)

Approximations to integral of √_x_ from 0 to 1, with 5 yellow right endpoint partitions and 10 green left endpoint partitions

Integrals appear in many practical situations. For instance, from the length, width and depth of a swimming pool which is rectangular with a flat bottom, one can determine the volume of water it can contain, the area of its surface, and the length of its edge. But if it is oval with a rounded bottom, integrals are required to find exact and rigorous values for these quantities. In each case, one may divide the sought quantity into infinitely many [infinitesimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal "Infinitesimal") pieces, then sum the pieces to achieve an accurate approximation.

As another example, to find the area of the region bounded by the graph of the function _f_(_x_) =![Image 10: {\textstyle {\sqrt {x}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02f01419d50f8331ed8f948d3b0dce7d5bd75950) between _x_ = 0 and _x_ = 1, one can divide the interval into five pieces (0, 1/5, 2/5, ..., 1), then construct rectangles using the right end height of each piece (thus √0, √1/5, √2/5, ..., √1) and sum their areas to get the approximation

![Image 11: {\displaystyle \textstyle {\sqrt {\frac {1}{5}}}\left({\frac {1}{5}}-0\right)+{\sqrt {\frac {2}{5}}}\left({\frac {2}{5}}-{\frac {1}{5}}\right)+\cdots +{\sqrt {\frac {5}{5}}}\left({\frac {5}{5}}-{\frac {4}{5}}\right)\approx 0.7497,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07cee59815b00288f73cbdd9a9d9b4a3ba331c89)
which is larger than the exact value. Alternatively, when replacing these subintervals by ones with the left end height of each piece, the approximation one gets is too low: with twelve such subintervals the approximated area is only 0.6203. However, when the number of pieces increases to infinity, it will reach a limit which is the exact value of the area sought (in this case, 2/3). One writes

![Image 12: {\displaystyle \int _{0}^{1}{\sqrt {x}}\,dx={\frac {2}{3}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66f03313f6b5693e7d6f14663fca9efa4472e9ae)
which means 2/3 is the result of a weighted sum of function values, √_x_, multiplied by the infinitesimal step widths, denoted by _dx_, on the interval [0, 1].

Riemann sums converging

There are many ways of formally defining an integral, not all of which are equivalent. The differences exist mostly to deal with differing special cases which may not be integrable under other definitions, but are also occasionally for pedagogical reasons. The most commonly used definitions are Riemann integrals and Lebesgue integrals.

The Riemann integral is defined in terms of [Riemann sums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_sum "Riemann sum") of functions with respect to _tagged partitions_ of an interval.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-22) A tagged partition of a [closed interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_interval "Closed interval")[_a_, _b_] on the real line is a finite sequence

![Image 16: {\displaystyle a=x_{0}\leq t_{1}\leq x_{1}\leq t_{2}\leq x_{2}\leq \cdots \leq x_{n-1}\leq t_{n}\leq x_{n}=b.\,\!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7a95dc34e72c3da728c59d5f83e148f3e57f100b)
This partitions the interval [_a_, _b_] into n sub-intervals [_x_ _i_−1, _x_ _i_] indexed by i, each of which is "tagged" with a specific point _t_ _i_ ∈ [_x_ _i_−1, _x_ _i_]. A _Riemann sum_ of a function f with respect to such a tagged partition is defined as

![Image 17: {\displaystyle \sum _{i=1}^{n}f(t_{i})\,\Delta _{i};}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/76e9ff892e308950da2bbe3f2ac7b0a51356c6ce)
thus each term of the sum is the area of a rectangle with height equal to the function value at the chosen point of the given sub-interval, and width the same as the width of sub-interval, Δ _i_ = _x_ _i_−_x_ _i_−1. The _mesh_ of such a tagged partition is the width of the largest sub-interval formed by the partition, max _i_=1..._n_ Δ _i_. The _Riemann integral_ of a function f over the interval [_a_, _b_] is equal to S if:[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-23)

For all ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \varepsilon >0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e04ec3670b50384a3ce48aca42e7cc5131a06b12) there exists ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \delta >0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/595d5cea06fdcaf2642caf549eda2cfc537958a9) such that, for any tagged partition ![Image 20: {\displaystyle [a,b]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c4b788fc5c637e26ee98b45f89a5c08c85f7935) with mesh less than ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \delta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5321cfa797202b3e1f8620663ff43c4660ea03a),![Image 22: {\displaystyle \left|S-\sum _{i=1}^{n}f(t_{i})\,\Delta _{i}\right|<\varepsilon .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f42275c8f3dabbf0001fa4fa9d59df0300804af)
When the chosen tags are the maximum (respectively, minimum) value of the function in each interval, the Riemann sum becomes an upper (respectively, lower) [Darboux sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darboux_integral "Darboux integral"), suggesting the close connection between the Riemann integral and the [Darboux integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darboux_integral "Darboux integral").

[![Image 23: Comparison of Riemann and Lebesgue integrals](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Lebesgueintegralsimplefunctions_finer-dotted.svg/250px-Lebesgueintegralsimplefunctions_finer-dotted.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Lebesgueintegralsimplefunctions_finer-dotted.svg)

Lebesgue integration

It is often of interest, both in theory and applications, to be able to pass to the limit under the integral. For instance, a sequence of functions can frequently be constructed that approximate, in a suitable sense, the solution to a problem. Then the integral of the solution function should be the limit of the integrals of the approximations. However, many functions that can be obtained as limits are not Riemann-integrable, and so such limit theorems do not hold with the Riemann integral. Therefore, it is of great importance to have a definition of the integral that allows a wider class of functions to be integrated.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-24)

Such an integral is the Lebesgue integral, that exploits the following fact to enlarge the class of integrable functions: if the values of a function are rearranged over the domain, the integral of a function should remain the same. Thus [Henri Lebesgue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lebesgue "Henri Lebesgue") introduced the integral bearing his name, explaining this integral thus in a letter to [Paul Montel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Montel "Paul Montel"):[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-25)

> I have to pay a certain sum, which I have collected in my pocket. I take the bills and coins out of my pocket and give them to the creditor in the order I find them until I have reached the total sum. This is the Riemann integral. But I can proceed differently. After I have taken all the money out of my pocket I order the bills and coins according to identical values and then I pay the several heaps one after the other to the creditor. This is my integral.

As Folland puts it, "To compute the Riemann integral of f, one partitions the domain [_a_, _b_] into subintervals", while in the Lebesgue integral, "one is in effect partitioning the range of f ".[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-26) The definition of the Lebesgue integral thus begins with a [measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measure_(mathematics) "Measure (mathematics)"), μ. In the simplest case, the [Lebesgue measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_measure "Lebesgue measure")_μ_(_A_) of an interval _A_ = [_a_, _b_] is its width, _b_ − _a_, so that the Lebesgue integral agrees with the (proper) Riemann integral when both exist.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-27) In more complicated cases, the sets being measured can be highly fragmented, with no continuity and no resemblance to intervals.

Using the "partitioning the range of f " philosophy, the integral of a non-negative function _f_: **R** → **R** should be the sum over t of the areas between a thin horizontal strip between _y_ = _t_ and _y_ = _t_ + _dt_. This area is just _μ_{ _x_: _f_(_x_) >_t_} _dt_. Let _f_∗(_t_) = _μ_{ _x_: _f_(_x_) >_t_ }. The Lebesgue integral of f is then defined by

![Image 24: {\displaystyle \int f=\int _{0}^{\infty }f^{*}(t)\,dt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/67ab0b364de19ff1c40853e153ff423fd7c47154)
where the integral on the right is an ordinary improper Riemann integral (_f_∗ is a strictly decreasing positive function, and therefore has a [well-defined](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Well-defined "Well-defined") improper Riemann integral).[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-28) For a suitable class of functions (the [measurable functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measurable_function "Measurable function")) this defines the Lebesgue integral.

A general measurable function f is Lebesgue-integrable if the sum of the absolute values of the areas of the regions between the graph of f and the x-axis is finite:[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-29)

![Image 25: {\displaystyle \int _{E}|f|\,d\mu <+\infty .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f768a56292de5a02009fe6e4060bd6c6f12ed61)
In that case, the integral is, as in the Riemannian case, the difference between the area above the x-axis and the area below the x-axis:[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-:3-30)

![Image 26: {\displaystyle \int _{E}f\,d\mu =\int _{E}f^{+}\,d\mu -\int _{E}f^{-}\,d\mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a1950569fcd75f4d509ab0d3659010b05d63e014)
where

![Image 27: {\displaystyle {\begin{alignedat}{3}&f^{+}(x)&&{}={}\max\{f(x),0\}&&{}={}{\begin{cases}f(x),&{\text{if }}f(x)>0,\\0,&{\text{otherwise,}}\end{cases}}\\&f^{-}(x)&&{}={}\max\{-f(x),0\}&&{}={}{\begin{cases}-f(x),&{\text{if }}f(x)<0,\\0,&{\text{otherwise.}}\end{cases}}\end{alignedat}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02e8d66ef704a22d5abbc090506e442b7587fc23)
Although the Riemann and Lebesgue integrals are the most widely used definitions of the integral, a number of others exist, including:

*   The [Darboux integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darboux_integral "Darboux integral"), which is defined by Darboux sums (restricted Riemann sums) yet is equivalent to the [Riemann integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_integral "Riemann integral"). A function is Darboux-integrable if and only if it is Riemann-integrable. Darboux integrals have the advantage of being easier to define than Riemann integrals.
*   The [Riemann–Stieltjes integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann%E2%80%93Stieltjes_integral "Riemann–Stieltjes integral"), an extension of the Riemann integral which integrates with respect to a function as opposed to a variable.
*   The [Lebesgue–Stieltjes integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue%E2%80%93Stieltjes_integration "Lebesgue–Stieltjes integration"), further developed by [Johann Radon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Radon "Johann Radon"), which generalizes both the Riemann–Stieltjes and Lebesgue integrals.
*   The [Daniell integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daniell_integral "Daniell integral"), which subsumes the Lebesgue integral and [Lebesgue–Stieltjes integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue%E2%80%93Stieltjes_integration "Lebesgue–Stieltjes integration") without depending on [measures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measure_(mathematics) "Measure (mathematics)").
*   The [Haar integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haar_integral "Haar integral"), used for integration on locally compact topological groups, introduced by [Alfréd Haar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfr%C3%A9d_Haar "Alfréd Haar") in 1933.
*   The [Henstock–Kurzweil integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henstock%E2%80%93Kurzweil_integral "Henstock–Kurzweil integral"), variously defined by [Arnaud Denjoy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arnaud_Denjoy "Arnaud Denjoy"), [Oskar Perron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oskar_Perron "Oskar Perron"), and (most elegantly, as the gauge integral) [Jaroslav Kurzweil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jaroslav_Kurzweil "Jaroslav Kurzweil"), and developed by [Ralph Henstock](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ralph_Henstock "Ralph Henstock").
*   The [Khinchin integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Khinchin_integral "Khinchin integral"), named after [Aleksandr Khinchin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aleksandr_Khinchin "Aleksandr Khinchin").
*   The [Itô integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/It%C3%B4_integral "Itô integral") and [Stratonovich integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stratonovich_integral "Stratonovich integral"), which define integration with respect to [semimartingales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semimartingale "Semimartingale") such as [Brownian motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wiener_process "Wiener process").
*   The [Young integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Young_integral "Young integral"), which is a kind of Riemann–Stieltjes integral with respect to certain functions of [unbounded variation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounded_variation "Bounded variation").
*   The [rough path](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rough_path "Rough path") integral, which is defined for functions equipped with some additional "rough path" structure and generalizes stochastic integration against both [semimartingales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semimartingale "Semimartingale") and processes such as the [fractional Brownian motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractional_Brownian_motion "Fractional Brownian motion").
*   The [Choquet integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Choquet_integral "Choquet integral"), a subadditive or superadditive integral created by the French mathematician Gustave Choquet in 1953.
*   The [Bochner integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bochner_integral "Bochner integral"), a generalization of the Lebesgue integral to functions that take values in a [Banach space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banach_space "Banach space").

The collection of Riemann-integrable functions on a closed interval [_a_, _b_] forms a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") under the operations of [pointwise addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pointwise_addition "Pointwise addition") and multiplication by a scalar, and the operation of integration

![Image 28: {\displaystyle f\mapsto \int _{a}^{b}f(x)\;dx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e75c74f0d1966e467b8dee4b36b9a14d00248bda)
is a [linear functional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_functional "Linear functional") on this vector space. Thus, the collection of integrable functions is closed under taking [linear combinations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_combination "Linear combination"), and the integral of a linear combination is the linear combination of the integrals:[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-:0-31)

![Image 29: {\displaystyle \int _{a}^{b}(\alpha f+\beta g)(x)\,dx=\alpha \int _{a}^{b}f(x)\,dx+\beta \int _{a}^{b}g(x)\,dx.\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/26dd4e7559e6708844575862a2770422b4fc0ebb)
Similarly, the set of [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number")-valued Lebesgue-integrable functions on a given [measure space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measure_(mathematics) "Measure (mathematics)")E with measure μ is closed under taking linear combinations and hence form a vector space, and the Lebesgue integral

![Image 30: {\displaystyle f\mapsto \int _{E}f\,d\mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9acad9829c6f1b994440d268dcf7fb3da77ee22d)
is a linear functional on this vector space, so that:[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-:3-30)

![Image 31: {\displaystyle \int _{E}(\alpha f+\beta g)\,d\mu =\alpha \int _{E}f\,d\mu +\beta \int _{E}g\,d\mu .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/857560e35dc5c14a076966bfda1e5ae972a6c746)
More generally, consider the vector space of all [measurable functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measurable_function "Measurable function") on a measure space (_E_,_μ_), taking values in a [locally compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Locally_compact_space "Locally compact space")[complete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_metric_space "Complete metric space")[topological vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_vector_space "Topological vector space")V over a locally compact [topological field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_ring "Topological ring")_K_, _f_: _E_ → _V_. Then one may define an abstract integration map assigning to each function f an element of V or the symbol _∞_,

![Image 32: {\displaystyle f\mapsto \int _{E}f\,d\mu ,\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/689c556fb9428a1728bc2c3519c60d2d00671cc4)
that is compatible with linear combinations.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-32) In this situation, the linearity holds for the subspace of functions whose integral is an element of V (i.e. "finite"). The most important special cases arise when K is **R**, **C**, or a finite extension of the field **Q**_p_ of [p-adic numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P-adic_number "P-adic number"), and V is a finite-dimensional vector space over K, and when _K_ = **C** and V is a complex [Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space").

Linearity, together with some natural continuity properties and normalization for a certain class of "simple" functions, may be used to give an alternative definition of the integral. This is the approach of [Daniell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daniell_integral "Daniell integral") for the case of real-valued functions on a set X, generalized by [Nicolas Bourbaki](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolas_Bourbaki "Nicolas Bourbaki") to functions with values in a locally compact topological vector space. See [Hildebrandt 1953](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#CITEREFHildebrandt1953) for an axiomatic characterization of the integral.

A number of general inequalities hold for Riemann-integrable [functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") defined on a [closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_set "Closed set") and [bounded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounded_set "Bounded set")[interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)")[_a_, _b_] and can be generalized to other notions of integral (Lebesgue and Daniell).

*   _Upper and lower bounds._ An integrable function f on [_a_, _b_], is necessarily [bounded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounded_function "Bounded function") on that interval. Thus there are [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number")m and M so that _m_ ≤ _f_ (_x_) ≤ _M_ for all x in [_a_, _b_]. Since the lower and upper sums of f over [_a_, _b_] are therefore bounded by, respectively, _m_(_b_ − _a_) and _M_(_b_ − _a_), it follows that ![Image 33: {\displaystyle m(b-a)\leq \int _{a}^{b}f(x)\,dx\leq M(b-a).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/782f800b43b9db1bdfa991f143322d67185f991e)
*   _Inequalities between functions._[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-33) If _f_(_x_) ≤ _g_(_x_) for each x in [_a_, _b_] then each of the upper and lower sums of f is bounded above by the upper and lower sums, respectively, of g. Thus ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \int _{a}^{b}f(x)\,dx\leq \int _{a}^{b}g(x)\,dx.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0a4c6eb7a2f260ba880a015d890ba94e243c164) This is a generalization of the above inequalities, as _M_(_b_ − _a_) is the integral of the constant function with value M over [_a_, _b_]. In addition, if the inequality between functions is strict, then the inequality between integrals is also strict. That is, if _f_(_x_) <_g_(_x_) for each x in [_a_, _b_], then ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \int _{a}^{b}f(x)\,dx<\int _{a}^{b}g(x)\,dx.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29b6c34b1e601a3388ccd4988d4e5d3014dc62c0)
*   _Subintervals._ If [_c_, _d_] is a subinterval of [_a_, _b_] and _f_ (_x_) is non-negative for all x, then ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \int _{c}^{d}f(x)\,dx\leq \int _{a}^{b}f(x)\,dx.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7b525b803be2260364819821bc30394eb10795d2)
*   _Products and absolute values of functions._ If f and g are two functions, then we may consider their [pointwise products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pointwise_product "Pointwise product") and powers, and [absolute values](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value"): ![Image 37: {\displaystyle (fg)(x)=f(x)g(x),\;f^{2}(x)=(f(x))^{2},\;|f|(x)=|f(x)|.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bef28f306593eb8eaaba0e365ff005befc8eb5d8) If f is Riemann-integrable on [_a_, _b_] then the same is true for |_f_|, and ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \left|\int _{a}^{b}f(x)\,dx\right|\leq \int _{a}^{b}|f(x)|\,dx.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4d273e267ac96790ce130211f3135e6cad0a3c31) Moreover, if f and g are both Riemann-integrable then _fg_ is also Riemann-integrable, and ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \left(\int _{a}^{b}(fg)(x)\,dx\right)^{2}\leq \left(\int _{a}^{b}f(x)^{2}\,dx\right)\left(\int _{a}^{b}g(x)^{2}\,dx\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc9e4809b17198083e1940bd41feed64957d2147) This inequality, known as the [Cauchy–Schwarz inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy%E2%80%93Schwarz_inequality "Cauchy–Schwarz inequality"), plays a prominent role in [Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space") theory, where the left hand side is interpreted as the [inner product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product_space "Inner product space") of two [square-integrable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square-integrable_function "Square-integrable function") functions f and g on the interval [_a_, _b_].
*   _Hölder's inequality_.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-:4-34) Suppose that p and q are two real numbers, 1 ≤ _p_, _q_ ≤ ∞ with ⁠1/_p_⁠ + ⁠1/_q_⁠ = 1, and f and g are two Riemann-integrable functions. Then the functions |_f_|_p_ and |_g_|_q_ are also integrable and the following [Hölder's inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H%C3%B6lder%27s_inequality "Hölder's inequality") holds: ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \left|\int f(x)g(x)\,dx\right|\leq \left(\int \left|f(x)\right|^{p}\,dx\right)^{1/p}\left(\int \left|g(x)\right|^{q}\,dx\right)^{1/q}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd7fff3624f6c19b3d82b11455ee2f22be0e1f7c) For _p_ = _q_ = 2, Hölder's inequality becomes the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality.
*   _Minkowski inequality_.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-:4-34) Suppose that _p_ ≥ 1 is a real number and f and g are Riemann-integrable functions. Then |_f_|_p_, |_g_|_p_ and |_f_ + _g_|_p_ are also Riemann-integrable and the following [Minkowski inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_inequality "Minkowski inequality") holds: ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \left(\int \left|f(x)+g(x)\right|^{p}\,dx\right)^{1/p}\leq \left(\int \left|f(x)\right|^{p}\,dx\right)^{1/p}+\left(\int \left|g(x)\right|^{p}\,dx\right)^{1/p}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/912a0477da36636adc618fecc73946dcf0ee6c8e) An analogue of this inequality for Lebesgue integral is used in construction of [L p spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lp_space "Lp space").

In this section, f is a [real-valued](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") Riemann-integrable [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)"). The integral

![Image 42: {\displaystyle \int _{a}^{b}f(x)\,dx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ac02adeed584466d53dee65f3228ad66939eb58b)
over an interval [_a_, _b_] is defined if _a_<_b_. This means that the upper and lower sums of the function f are evaluated on a partition _a_ = _x_ 0 ≤ _x_ 1 ≤ . . . ≤ _x_ _n_ = _b_ whose values _x_ _i_ are increasing. Geometrically, this signifies that integration takes place "left to right", evaluating f within intervals [_x_ _i_ , _x_ _i_ +1] where an interval with a higher index lies to the right of one with a lower index. The values a and b, the end-points of the [interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)"), are called the [limits of integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limits_of_integration "Limits of integration") of f. Integrals can also be defined if _a_>_b_:_[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite\_note-:1-19)_

![Image 43: {\displaystyle \int _{a}^{b}f(x)\,dx=-\int _{b}^{a}f(x)\,dx.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f1f0e0394818f47c41957ff9281eaf3c305495a6)
With _a_ = _b_, this implies:

![Image 44: {\displaystyle \int _{a}^{a}f(x)\,dx=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f606503473aef1bb7d3ef5ca4e702a7484c5638c)
The first convention is necessary in consideration of taking integrals over subintervals of [_a_, _b_]; the second says that an integral taken over a degenerate interval, or a [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)"), should be [zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/0_(number) "0 (number)"). One reason for the first convention is that the integrability of f on an interval [_a_, _b_] implies that f is integrable on any subinterval [_c_, _d_], but in particular integrals have the property that if c is any [element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Element_(mathematics) "Element (mathematics)") of [_a_, _b_], then:_[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite\_note-:0-31)_

![Image 45: {\displaystyle \int _{a}^{b}f(x)\,dx=\int _{a}^{c}f(x)\,dx+\int _{c}^{b}f(x)\,dx.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/55d91ecf0b6d156886defe70f12327d351e385cc)
With the first convention, the resulting relation

![Image 46: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\int _{a}^{c}f(x)\,dx&{}=\int _{a}^{b}f(x)\,dx-\int _{c}^{b}f(x)\,dx\\&{}=\int _{a}^{b}f(x)\,dx+\int _{b}^{c}f(x)\,dx\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d655ef7683fe3dceda35d9566e748c395fc6571d)
is then well-defined for any cyclic permutation of a, b, and c.

Fundamental theorem of calculus
-------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Integral&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Fundamental theorem of calculus")]

The _fundamental theorem of calculus_ is the statement that [differentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") and integration are inverse operations: if a [continuous function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function") is first integrated and then differentiated, the original function is retrieved.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-35) An important consequence, sometimes called the _second fundamental theorem of calculus_, allows one to compute integrals by using an antiderivative of the function to be integrated.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-36)

Let f be a continuous real-valued function defined on a [closed interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics)#Definitions "Interval (mathematics)")[_a_, _b_]. Let F be the function defined, for all x in [_a_, _b_], by[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMontesinosZizlerZizler2015355-37)

![Image 47: {\displaystyle F(x)=\int _{a}^{x}f(t)\,dt.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/563036276f93ee00c4afaf3ee6cf668792d07589)
Then, F is continuous on [_a_, _b_], differentiable on the open interval (_a_, _b_), and

![Image 48: {\displaystyle F'(x)=f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5457591f5410f4bfe3b9c9fa2e50ae665fa2822c)
for all x in (_a_, _b_).

Let f be a real-valued function defined on a [closed interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_interval "Closed interval") [_a_, _b_] that admits an [antiderivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiderivative "Antiderivative")F on [_a_, _b_]. That is, f and F are functions such that for all x in [_a_, _b_],

![Image 49: {\displaystyle f(x)=F'(x).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90e48248b0c2565a6f9ba6b20cb4cde93598d5bf)
If f is integrable on [_a_, _b_] then

![Image 50: {\displaystyle \int _{a}^{b}f(x)\,dx=F(b)-F(a).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f470e7743fda04c3d353a4dee2f441ae454f528)[![Image 51](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/94/Improper_integral.svg/250px-Improper_integral.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Improper_integral.svg)

The [improper integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Improper_integral "Improper integral")![Image 52: {\displaystyle \int _{0}^{\infty }{\frac {dx}{(x+1){\sqrt {x}}}}=\pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b714edc05d14092854ef3e449cf315ba6e989f3d) has unbounded intervals for both domain and range.

A "proper" Riemann integral assumes the integrand is defined and finite on a closed and bounded interval, bracketed by the limits of integration. An improper integral occurs when one or more of these conditions is not satisfied. In some cases such integrals may be defined by considering the [limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_(mathematics) "Limit (mathematics)") of a [sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence "Sequence") of proper [Riemann integrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_integral "Riemann integral") on progressively larger intervals.

If the interval is unbounded, for instance at its upper end, then the improper integral is the limit as that endpoint goes to infinity:[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-38)

![Image 53: {\displaystyle \int _{a}^{\infty }f(x)\,dx=\lim _{b\to \infty }\int _{a}^{b}f(x)\,dx.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9089a514f9e1c1d1dfb26951f80935a63ccce9dc)
If the integrand is only defined or finite on a half-open interval, for instance (_a_, _b_], then again a limit may provide a finite result:[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-39)

![Image 54: {\displaystyle \int _{a}^{b}f(x)\,dx=\lim _{\varepsilon \to 0}\int _{a+\epsilon }^{b}f(x)\,dx.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82a30070a59eaeb9d9464aa4e2d085202f4b889)
That is, the improper integral is the [limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_(mathematics) "Limit (mathematics)") of proper integrals as one endpoint of the interval of integration approaches either a specified [real number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number"), or ∞, or −∞. In more complicated cases, limits are required at both endpoints, or at interior points.

### Multiple integration

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Integral&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Multiple integration")]

[![Image 55](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b0/Volume_under_surface.png/250px-Volume_under_surface.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Volume_under_surface.png)

Just as the definite integral of a positive function of one variable represents the [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area") of the region between the graph of the function and the _x_-axis, the _double integral_ of a positive function of two variables represents the [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume") of the region between the surface defined by the function and the plane that contains its domain.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-40) For example, a function in two dimensions depends on two real variables, _x_ and _y_, and the integral of a function _f_ over the rectangle _R_ given as the [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product") of two intervals ![Image 57: {\displaystyle R=[a,b]\times [c,d]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3493a3bdcd7bd76960bb3d7b766bd6c6b1c4b3ee) can be written

![Image 58: {\displaystyle \int _{R}f(x,y)\,dA}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a522eea362f7f00960feb1b7ae608fd6182eb1ce)
where the differential _dA_ indicates that integration is taken with respect to area. This [double integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_integral "Double integral") can be defined using [Riemann sums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_sum "Riemann sum"), and represents the (signed) volume under the graph of _z_ = _f_(_x_,_y_) over the domain _R_.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-:2-41) Under suitable conditions (e.g., if _f_ is continuous), [Fubini's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fubini%27s_theorem "Fubini's theorem") states that this integral can be expressed as an equivalent iterated integral[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-42)

This reduces the problem of computing a double integral to computing one-dimensional integrals. Because of this, another notation for the integral over _R_ uses a double integral sign:[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-:2-41)

### Line integrals and surface integrals

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Integral&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Line integrals and surface integrals")]

[![Image 63](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Line-Integral.gif/250px-Line-Integral.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Line-Integral.gif)

A line integral sums together elements along a curve.

The concept of an integral can be extended to more general domains of integration, such as curved lines and surfaces inside higher-dimensional spaces. Such integrals are known as line integrals and surface integrals respectively. These have important applications in physics, as when dealing with [vector fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field").

A _line integral_ (sometimes called a _path integral_) is an integral where the [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") to be integrated is evaluated along a [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve").[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-43) Various different line integrals are in use. In the case of a closed curve it is also called a _contour integral_.

The function to be integrated may be a [scalar field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field "Scalar field") or a [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field"). The value of the line integral is the sum of values of the field at all points on the curve, weighted by some scalar function on the curve (commonly [arc length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_length "Arc length") or, for a vector field, the [scalar product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product_space "Inner product space") of the vector field with a [differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_(infinitesimal) "Differential (infinitesimal)") vector in the curve).[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-44) This weighting distinguishes the line integral from simpler integrals defined on [intervals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)"). Many simple formulas in physics have natural continuous analogs in terms of line integrals; for example, the fact that [work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_work "Mechanical work") is equal to [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force"), **F**, multiplied by displacement, **s**, may be expressed (in terms of vector quantities) as:[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-45)

![Image 64: {\displaystyle W=\mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {s} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2102143a354b5c4cfe19a50340a1b568219a49ea)
For an object moving along a path _C_ in a [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field")**F** such as an [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field") or [gravitational field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_field "Gravitational field"), the total work done by the field on the object is obtained by summing up the differential work done in moving from **s** to **s** + _d_**s**. This gives the line integral[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-46)

![Image 65: {\displaystyle W=\int _{C}\mathbf {F} \cdot d\mathbf {s} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d831488a8fe259c861dc13cb61643918fd70f56)[![Image 66](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Surface_integral_illustration.svg/250px-Surface_integral_illustration.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Surface_integral_illustration.svg)

The definition of surface integral relies on splitting the surface into small surface elements.

A _surface integral_ generalizes double integrals to integration over a [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(mathematics) "Surface (mathematics)") (which may be a curved set in [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space "Space")); it can be thought of as the [double integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_integral "Multiple integral") analog of the [line integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_integral "Line integral"). The function to be integrated may be a [scalar field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field "Scalar field") or a [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field"). The value of the surface integral is the sum of the field at all points on the surface. This can be achieved by splitting the surface into surface elements, which provide the partitioning for Riemann sums.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-47)

For an example of applications of surface integrals, consider a vector field **v** on a surface _S_; that is, for each point _x_ in _S_, **v**(_x_) is a vector. Imagine that a fluid flows through _S_, such that **v**(_x_) determines the velocity of the fluid at x. The [flux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flux "Flux") is defined as the quantity of fluid flowing through _S_ in unit amount of time. To find the flux, one need to take the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") of **v** with the unit [surface normal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_(geometry) "Normal (geometry)") to _S_ at each point, which will give a scalar field, which is integrated over the surface:[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-48)

![Image 67: {\displaystyle \int _{S}{\mathbf {v} }\cdot \,d{\mathbf {S} }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27a969275a87437b804ded78fb9b02d57ce1dbbf)
The fluid flux in this example may be from a physical fluid such as water or air, or from electrical or magnetic flux. Thus surface integrals have applications in physics, particularly with the [classical theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_theory "Classical theory") of [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism").

In [complex analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis "Complex analysis"), the integrand is a [complex-valued function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex-valued_function "Complex-valued function") of a complex variable z instead of a real function of a real variable x. When a complex function is integrated along a curve ![Image 68: {\displaystyle \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a223c880b0ce3da8f64ee33c4f0010beee400b1a) in the complex plane, the integral is denoted as follows

![Image 69: {\displaystyle \int _{\gamma }f(z)\,dz.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/adf4ad465dcdd2fddac974923eaa3ed1913186e8)
This is known as a [contour integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contour_integral "Contour integral").

### Integrals of differential forms

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Integral&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: Integrals of differential forms")]

A [differential form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form") is a mathematical concept in the fields of [multivariable calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivariable_calculus "Multivariable calculus"), [differential topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_topology "Differential topology"), and [tensors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor"). Differential forms are organized by degree. For example, a one-form is a weighted sum of the differentials of the coordinates, such as:

![Image 70: {\displaystyle E(x,y,z)\,dx+F(x,y,z)\,dy+G(x,y,z)\,dz}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40dad737a1822d5c7e986c7c240c9d1027a863ae)
where _E_, _F_, _G_ are functions in three dimensions. A differential one-form can be integrated over an oriented path, and the resulting integral is just another way of writing a line integral. Here the basic differentials _dx_, _dy_, _dz_ measure infinitesimal oriented lengths parallel to the three coordinate axes.

A differential two-form is a sum of the form

![Image 71: {\displaystyle G(x,y,z)\,dx\wedge dy+E(x,y,z)\,dy\wedge dz+F(x,y,z)\,dz\wedge dx.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/190aa2cc6a89d697faf1e75090a09c632a166607)
Here the basic two-forms ![Image 72: {\displaystyle dx\wedge dy,dz\wedge dx,dy\wedge dz}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c285fb7d96a1408455e9e5510b394a79d50e0cd2) measure oriented areas parallel to the coordinate two-planes. The symbol ![Image 73: {\displaystyle \wedge }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1caa4004cb216ef2930bb12fe805a76870caed94) denotes the [wedge product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wedge_product "Wedge product"), which is similar to the [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product") in the sense that the wedge product of two forms representing oriented lengths represents an oriented area. A two-form can be integrated over an oriented surface, and the resulting integral is equivalent to the surface integral giving the flux of ![Image 74: {\displaystyle E\mathbf {i} +F\mathbf {j} +G\mathbf {k} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/94ab78cf97890001a9a5bf1671669da76dab26f0).

Unlike the cross product, and the three-dimensional vector calculus, the wedge product and the calculus of differential forms makes sense in arbitrary dimension and on more general manifolds (curves, surfaces, and their higher-dimensional analogs). The [exterior derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_derivative "Exterior derivative") plays the role of the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient") and [curl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curl_(mathematics) "Curl (mathematics)") of vector calculus, and [Stokes' theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_Stokes_theorem "Generalized Stokes theorem") simultaneously generalizes the three theorems of vector calculus: the [divergence theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem "Divergence theorem"), [Green's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green%27s_theorem "Green's theorem"), and the [Kelvin-Stokes theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin-Stokes_theorem "Kelvin-Stokes theorem").

The discrete equivalent of integration is [summation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Summation "Summation"). Summations and integrals can be put on the same foundations using the theory of [Lebesgue integrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_integral "Lebesgue integral") or [time-scale calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time-scale_calculus "Time-scale calculus").

### Functional integrals

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Integral&action=edit&section=27 "Edit section: Functional integrals")]

An integration that is performed not over a variable (or, in physics, over a space or time dimension), but over a [space of functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_space "Function space"), is referred to as a [functional integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_integral "Functional integral").

Integrals are used extensively in many areas. For example, in [probability theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_theory "Probability theory"), integrals are used to determine the probability of some [random variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_variable "Random variable") falling within a certain range.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-49) Moreover, the integral under an entire [probability density function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_density_function "Probability density function") must equal 1, which provides a test of whether a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") with no negative values could be a density function or not.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-50)

Integrals can be used for computing the [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area") of a two-dimensional region that has a curved boundary, as well as [computing the volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_integral "Volume integral") of a three-dimensional object that has a curved boundary. The area of a two-dimensional region can be calculated using the aforementioned definite integral.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-51) The volume of a three-dimensional object such as a disc or washer can be computed by [disc integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disc_integration "Disc integration") using the equation for the volume of a cylinder, ![Image 75: {\displaystyle \pi r^{2}h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f0350a9c71cfb1388ec50689af098b6be292acd), where ![Image 76: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) is the radius. In the case of a simple disc created by rotating a curve about the _x_-axis, the radius is given by _f_(_x_), and its height is the differential _dx_. Using an integral with bounds _a_ and _b_, the volume of the disc is equal to:[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-52)![Image 77: {\displaystyle \pi \int _{a}^{b}f^{2}(x)\,dx.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/408abd7f1c46b3061c00228796685a6be44558ef)Integrals are also used in physics, in areas like [kinematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics "Kinematics") to find quantities like [displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Displacement_(vector) "Displacement (vector)"), [time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time "Time"), and [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity"). For example, in [rectilinear motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectilinear_motion "Rectilinear motion"), the displacement of an object over the time interval ![Image 78: {\displaystyle [a,b]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c4b788fc5c637e26ee98b45f89a5c08c85f7935) is given by

where ![Image 80: {\displaystyle v(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/243a0bf98a12f48552ba6a70302122d81b237b3d) is the velocity expressed as a function of time.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-53) The work done by a force ![Image 81: {\displaystyle F(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71a82805d469cdfa7856c11d6ee756acd1dc7174) (given as a function of position) from an initial position ![Image 82: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) to a final position ![Image 83: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) is:[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-54)

![Image 84: {\displaystyle W_{A\rightarrow B}=\int _{A}^{B}F(x)\,dx.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cef9f21422022e97b692fe1c1d245fc67803d367)
Integrals are also used in [thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamics "Thermodynamics"), where [thermodynamic integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_integration "Thermodynamic integration") is used to calculate the difference in free energy between two given states.

The most basic technique for computing definite integrals of one real variable is based on the [fundamental theorem of calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_calculus "Fundamental theorem of calculus"). Let _f_(_x_) be the function of x to be integrated over a given interval [_a_, _b_]. Then, find an antiderivative of f; that is, a function F such that _F_′ = _f_ on the interval. Provided the integrand and integral have no [singularities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_singularity "Mathematical singularity") on the path of integration, by the fundamental theorem of calculus,

![Image 85: {\displaystyle \int _{a}^{b}f(x)\,dx=F(b)-F(a).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f470e7743fda04c3d353a4dee2f441ae454f528)
Sometimes it is necessary to use one of the many techniques that have been developed to evaluate integrals. Most of these techniques rewrite one integral as a different one which is hopefully more tractable. Techniques include [integration by substitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integration_by_substitution "Integration by substitution"), [integration by parts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integration_by_parts "Integration by parts"), [integration by trigonometric substitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_substitution "Trigonometric substitution"), and [integration by partial fractions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_fractions_in_integration "Partial fractions in integration").

Alternative methods exist to compute more complex integrals. Many [nonelementary integrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonelementary_integral "Nonelementary integral") can be expanded in a [Taylor series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor_series "Taylor series") and integrated term by term. Occasionally, the resulting infinite series can be summed analytically. The method of convolution using [Meijer G-functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meijer_G-function "Meijer G-function") can also be used, assuming that the integrand can be written as a product of Meijer G-functions. There are also many less common ways of calculating definite integrals; for instance, [Parseval's identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parseval%27s_identity "Parseval's identity") can be used to transform an integral over a rectangular region into an infinite sum. Occasionally, an integral can be evaluated by a trick; for an example of this, see [Gaussian integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_integral "Gaussian integral").

Computations of volumes of [solids of revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_of_revolution "Solid of revolution") can usually be done with [disk integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disk_integration "Disk integration") or [shell integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shell_integration "Shell integration").

Specific results which have been worked out by various techniques are collected in the [list of integrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lists_of_integrals "Lists of integrals").

Many problems in mathematics, physics, and engineering involve integration where an explicit formula for the integral is desired. Extensive [tables of integrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lists_of_integrals "Lists of integrals") have been compiled and published over the years for this purpose. With the spread of computers, many professionals, educators, and students have turned to [computer algebra systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_algebra_system "Computer algebra system") that are specifically designed to perform difficult or tedious tasks, including integration. Symbolic integration has been one of the motivations for the development of the first such systems, like [Macsyma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macsyma "Macsyma") and [Maple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maple_(software) "Maple (software)").

A major mathematical difficulty in symbolic integration is that in many cases, a relatively simple function does not have integrals that can be expressed in [closed form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed-form_expression "Closed-form expression") involving only [elementary functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_function "Elementary function"), include [rational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_function "Rational function") and [exponential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_function "Exponential function") functions, [logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logarithm "Logarithm"), [trigonometric functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometric_functions "Trigonometric functions") and [inverse trigonometric functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_trigonometric_functions "Inverse trigonometric functions"), and the operations of multiplication and composition. The [Risch algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Risch_algorithm "Risch algorithm") provides a general criterion to determine whether the antiderivative of an elementary function is elementary and to compute the integral if is elementary. However, functions with closed expressions of antiderivatives are the exception, and consequently, computerized algebra systems have no hope of being able to find an antiderivative for a randomly constructed elementary function. On the positive side, if the 'building blocks' for antiderivatives are fixed in advance, it may still be possible to decide whether the antiderivative of a given function can be expressed using these blocks and operations of multiplication and composition and to find the symbolic answer whenever it exists. The Risch algorithm, implemented in [Mathematica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematica "Mathematica"), [Maple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maple_(software) "Maple (software)") and other [computer algebra systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_algebra_system "Computer algebra system"), does just that for functions and antiderivatives built from rational functions, [radicals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nth_root "Nth root"), logarithm, and exponential functions.

Some special integrands occur often enough to warrant special study. In particular, it may be useful to have, in the set of antiderivatives, the [special functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_functions "Special functions") (like the [Legendre functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legendre_function "Legendre function"), the [hypergeometric function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypergeometric_function "Hypergeometric function"), the [gamma function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_function "Gamma function"), the [incomplete gamma function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incomplete_gamma_function "Incomplete gamma function") and so on). Extending Risch's algorithm to include such functions is possible but challenging and has been an active research subject.

More recently a new approach has emerged, using [_D_-finite functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D-finite_function "D-finite function"), which are the solutions of [linear differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_differential_equation "Linear differential equation") with polynomial coefficients. Most of the elementary and special functions are _D_-finite, and the integral of a _D_-finite function is also a _D_-finite function. This provides an algorithm to express the antiderivative of a _D_-finite function as the solution of a differential equation. This theory also allows one to compute the definite integral of a _D_-function as the sum of a series given by the first coefficients and provides an algorithm to compute any coefficient.

Rule-based integration systems facilitate integration. Rubi, a computer algebra system rule-based integrator, pattern matches an extensive system of symbolic integration rules to integrate a wide variety of integrands. This system uses over 6600 integration rules to compute integrals.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-FOOTNOTERichScheibeAbbasi2018-55) The [method of brackets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ramanujan%27s_master_theorem#Bracket_integration_method "Ramanujan's master theorem") is a generalization of Ramanujan's master theorem that can be applied to a wide range of univariate and multivariate integrals. A set of rules are applied to the coefficients and exponential terms of the integrand's power series expansion to determine the integral. The method is closely related to the [Mellin transform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mellin_transform "Mellin transform").[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGonzalezJiuMoll2020-56)

[![Image 86](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Numerical_quadrature_4up.png/250px-Numerical_quadrature_4up.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Numerical_quadrature_4up.png)

Numerical quadrature methods: rectangle method, trapezoidal rule, Romberg's method, Gaussian quadrature

Definite integrals may be approximated using several methods of [numerical integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerical_integration "Numerical integration"). The [rectangle method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle_method "Rectangle method") relies on dividing the region under the function into a series of rectangles corresponding to function values and multiplies by the step width to find the sum. A better approach, the [trapezoidal rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoidal_rule "Trapezoidal rule"), replaces the rectangles used in a Riemann sum with trapezoids. The trapezoidal rule weights the first and last values by one half, then multiplies by the step width to obtain a better approximation.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-57) The idea behind the trapezoidal rule, that more accurate approximations to the function yield better approximations to the integral, can be carried further: [Simpson's rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simpson%27s_rule "Simpson's rule") approximates the integrand by a piecewise quadratic function.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-58)

Riemann sums, the trapezoidal rule, and Simpson's rule are examples of a family of quadrature rules called the [Newton–Cotes formulas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%E2%80%93Cotes_formulas "Newton–Cotes formulas"). The degree n Newton–Cotes quadrature rule approximates the polynomial on each subinterval by a degree _n_ polynomial. This polynomial is chosen to interpolate the values of the function on the interval.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-59) Higher degree Newton–Cotes approximations can be more accurate, but they require more function evaluations, and they can suffer from numerical inaccuracy due to [Runge's phenomenon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Runge%27s_phenomenon "Runge's phenomenon"). One solution to this problem is [Clenshaw–Curtis quadrature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clenshaw%E2%80%93Curtis_quadrature "Clenshaw–Curtis quadrature"), in which the integrand is approximated by expanding it in terms of [Chebyshev polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chebyshev_polynomials "Chebyshev polynomials").

[Romberg's method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romberg%27s_method "Romberg's method") halves the step widths incrementally, giving trapezoid approximations denoted by _T_(_h_ 0), _T_(_h_ 1), and so on, where _h_ _k_+1 is half of _h_ _k_. For each new step size, only half the new function values need to be computed; the others carry over from the previous size. It then [interpolate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interpolation "Interpolation") a polynomial through the approximations, and extrapolate to _T_(0). [Gaussian quadrature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_quadrature "Gaussian quadrature") evaluates the function at the roots of a set of [orthogonal polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_polynomials "Orthogonal polynomials").[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-60) An n-point Gaussian method is exact for polynomials of degree up to 2 _n_ − 1.

The computation of higher-dimensional integrals (for example, volume calculations) makes important use of such alternatives as [Monte Carlo integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monte_Carlo_integration "Monte Carlo integration").[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-61)

The area of an arbitrary two-dimensional shape can be determined using a measuring instrument called [planimeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planimeter "Planimeter"). The volume of irregular objects can be measured with precision by the fluid [displaced](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Displacement_(fluid) "Displacement (fluid)") as the object is submerged.

Area can sometimes be found via [geometrical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrical "Geometrical")[compass-and-straightedge constructions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compass-and-straightedge_construction "Compass-and-straightedge construction") of an equivalent [square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square "Square").

### Integration by differentiation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Integral&action=edit&section=35 "Edit section: Integration by differentiation")]

Kempf, Jackson and Morales demonstrated mathematical relations that allow an integral to be calculated by means of [differentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative"). Their calculus involves the [Dirac delta function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_delta_function "Dirac delta function") and the [partial derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative "Partial derivative") operator ![Image 87: {\displaystyle \partial _{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/57563dabdda89ee216b5a897bf9f78f5c7030eb6). This can also be applied to [functional integrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_integral "Functional integral"), allowing them to be computed by [functional differentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_derivative "Functional derivative").[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral#cite_note-62)
