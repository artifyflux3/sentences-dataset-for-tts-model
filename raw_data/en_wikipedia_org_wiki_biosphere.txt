The **biosphere** (from [Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language")[βίος](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B2%CE%AF%CE%BF%CF%82#Ancient_Greek "wikt:βίος")_(\_bíos\_)_'life' and [σφαῖρα](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%83%CF%86%CE%B1%E1%BF%96%CF%81%CE%B1#Ancient_Greek "wikt:σφαῖρα")_(\_sphaîra\_)_'sphere'), also called the **ecosphere** (from [Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language")[οἶκος](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BF%E1%BC%B6%CE%BA%CE%BF%CF%82#Ancient_Greek "wikt:οἶκος")_(\_oîkos\_)_'settlement, house' and [σφαῖρα](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%83%CF%86%CE%B1%E1%BF%96%CF%81%CE%B1#Ancient_Greek "wikt:σφαῖρα")_(\_sphaîra\_)_'sphere'), is the worldwide sum of all [ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem "Ecosystem"). It can also be termed the zone of [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life "Life") on the [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth"). The biosphere (which is technically a [spherical shell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_shell "Spherical shell")) is virtually a [closed system with regard to matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_ecological_system "Closed ecological system"),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-ColumbiaEncyc-1) with minimal inputs and outputs. Regarding [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy"), it is an open system, with [photosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis "Photosynthesis") capturing [solar energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_energy "Solar energy") at a rate of around 100 [terawatts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Watt "Watt").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-2) By the most general [biophysiological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geophysiology "Geophysiology") definition, the biosphere is the global [ecological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecology "Ecology") system integrating all [living beings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Living_beings "Living beings") and their relationships, including their interaction with the elements of the [lithosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithosphere "Lithosphere"), [cryosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryosphere "Cryosphere"), [hydrosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrosphere "Hydrosphere"), and [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere "Earth's atmosphere"). The biosphere is postulated to have [evolved](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolved "Evolved"), beginning with a process of [biopoiesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_of_life "Origin of life") (life created naturally from non-living matter, such as simple organic compounds) or [biogenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biogenesis "Biogenesis") (life created from living matter), at least some 3.5 billion years ago.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-Campbell_2006-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-NYT-20131003-4)

In a general sense, biospheres are any closed, self-regulating systems containing ecosystems. This includes artificial biospheres such as [Biosphere 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere_2 "Biosphere 2") and [BIOS-3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BIOS-3 "BIOS-3"), and potentially [ones on other planets or moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrobiology "Astrobiology").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-webdictionary.co.uk-5)

Origin and use of the term
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biosphere&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Origin and use of the term")]

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/90_mile_beach.jpg/250px-90_mile_beach.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:90_mile_beach.jpg)

A [beach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beach "Beach") scene on Earth, simultaneously showing the lithosphere (ground), hydrosphere (ocean) and atmosphere (air)

The term "biosphere" was coined in 1875 by geologist [Eduard Suess](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eduard_Suess "Eduard Suess"), who defined it as the place on [Earth's surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_surface "Earth's surface") where [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life "Life") dwells.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-6)

While the concept has a [geological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geological "Geological") origin, it is an indication of the effect of both [Charles Darwin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Darwin "Charles Darwin") and [Matthew F. Maury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matthew_F._Maury "Matthew F. Maury") on the [Earth sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_sciences "Earth sciences"). The biosphere's ecological context comes from the 1920s (see [Vladimir I. Vernadsky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vladimir_I._Vernadsky "Vladimir I. Vernadsky")), preceding the 1935 introduction of the term "[ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem "Ecosystem")" by Sir [Arthur Tansley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Tansley "Arthur Tansley") (see [ecology history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecology_(history) "Ecology (history)")). Vernadsky defined [ecology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecology "Ecology") as the science of the biosphere. It is an [interdisciplinary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interdisciplinary "Interdisciplinary") concept for integrating [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy"), [geophysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geophysics "Geophysics"), [meteorology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorology "Meteorology"), [biogeography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biogeography "Biogeography"), [evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution "Evolution"), [geology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geology "Geology"), [geochemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geochemistry "Geochemistry"), [hydrology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrology "Hydrology") and, generally speaking, all life and Earth sciences.

Geochemists define the biosphere as being the total sum of living organisms (the "[biomass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomass_(ecology) "Biomass (ecology)")" or "[biota](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biota_(ecology) "Biota (ecology)")" as referred to by biologists and ecologists). In this sense, the biosphere is but one of four separate components of the geochemical model, the other three being _[geosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geosphere "Geosphere")_, _[hydrosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrosphere "Hydrosphere")_, and _[atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s\_atmosphere "Earth's atmosphere")_. When these four component spheres are combined into one system, it is known as the [ecosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosphere_(planetary) "Ecosphere (planetary)"). This term was coined during the 1960s and encompasses both biological and physical components of the planet.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-7)

The Second International Conference on Closed Life Systems defined _biospherics_ as the science and technology of analogs and [models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Model_(abstract) "Model (abstract)") of Earth's biosphere; i.e., artificial Earth-like biospheres.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-8) Others may include the creation of artificial non-Earth biospheres—for example, human-centered biospheres or a native [Martian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars") biosphere—as part of the topic of biospherics.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-9)

Currently, the total number of [living cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(biology) "Cell (biology)") on the Earth is estimated to be 10 30; the total number since the beginning of Earth, as 10 40, and the total number for the entire time of a [habitable planet Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_habitability "Planetary habitability") as 10 41.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-NYT-20231201-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-CB-20231106-11) This is much larger than the total number of estimated stars (and Earth-like planets) in the observable universe as 10 24, a number which is more than all the grains of beach sand on planet Earth;[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-ESA-2020-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-SWIN-20020201-13)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-CNET-20150319-14)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-MNRAS-20150313-15) but less than the total number of atoms estimated in the observable universe as 10 82;[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-LS-20210711-16) and the estimated total number of stars in an [inflationary universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflation_(cosmology) "Inflation (cosmology)") (observed and unobserved), as 10 100.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-SR-20200203-17)

The [earliest evidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_known_life_forms "Earliest known life forms") for life on Earth includes [biogenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biogenic_substance "Biogenic substance")[graphite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphite "Graphite") found in 3.7 billion-year-old [metasedimentary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metasediment "Metasediment") rocks from [Western Greenland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Greenland "Western Greenland")[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-NG-20131208-18) and [microbial mat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbial_mat "Microbial mat")[fossils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossils "Fossils") found in 3.48 billion-year-old [sandstone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sandstone "Sandstone") from [Western Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_Australia "Western Australia").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-AP-20131113-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-AST-20131108-20) More recently, in 2015, "remains of [biotic life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biotic_material "Biotic material")" were found in 4.1 billion-year-old rocks in Western Australia.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-AP-20151019-21)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-PNAS-20151014-pdf-22) In 2017, putative fossilized [microorganisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism "Microorganism") (or [microfossils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micropaleontology#Microfossils "Micropaleontology")) were announced to have been discovered in [hydrothermal vent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent "Hydrothermal vent") precipitates in the [Nuvvuagittuq suggesting "an almost instantaneous emergence of life" after [ocean formation 4.4 billion years ago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_of_water_on_Earth#History_of_water_on_Earth "Origin of water on Earth"), and not long after the [formation of the Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_the_Earth "Age of the Earth") 4.54 billion years ago.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-NAT-20170301-23)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-NYT-20170301-24)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-BBC-20170301-25)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-4.3b_oldest-26) According to biologist [Stephen Blair Hedges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephen_Blair_Hedges "Stephen Blair Hedges"), "If life arose relatively quickly on Earth ... then it could be common in the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe")."[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-AP-20151019-21)

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/XenophyophoreNOAA.jpg/250px-XenophyophoreNOAA.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:XenophyophoreNOAA.jpg)

[Xenophyophore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xenophyophore "Xenophyophore"), a barophilic organism, from the [Galapagos Rift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gal%C3%A1pagos_hotspot "Galápagos hotspot")

Every part of the planet, from the [polar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_region "Polar region") ice caps to the [equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator "Equator"), features life of some kind. Recent advances in [microbiology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbiology "Microbiology") have demonstrated that microbes live deep beneath the Earth's terrestrial surface and that the total mass of [microbial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbial "Microbial") life in so-called "uninhabitable zones" may, in [biomass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomass_(ecology) "Biomass (ecology)"), exceed all animal and plant life on the surface. The actual thickness of the biosphere on Earth is difficult to measure. Birds typically fly at altitudes as high as 1,800 m (5,900 ft; 1.1 mi) and fish live as much as 8,372 m (27,467 ft; 5.202 mi) underwater in the [Puerto Rico Trench](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puerto_Rico_Trench "Puerto Rico Trench").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-Campbell_2006-3)

There are more extreme examples for life on the planet: [Rüppell's vulture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R%C3%BCppell%27s_vulture "Rüppell's vulture") has been found at [altitudes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altitude "Altitude") of 11,300 metres (37,100 feet; 7.0 miles); [bar-headed geese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bar-headed_goose "Bar-headed goose") migrate at altitudes of at least 8,300 m (27,200 ft; 5.2 mi); [yaks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yak "Yak") live at elevations as high as 5,400 m (17,700 ft; 3.4 mi) above sea level; [mountain goats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mountain_goat "Mountain goat") live up to 3,050 m (10,010 ft; 1.90 mi). Herbivorous animals at these elevations depend on lichens, grasses, and herbs.

Life forms live in every part of the Earth's biosphere, including [soil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil "Soil"), [hot springs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hot_spring "Hot spring"), [inside rocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endolith "Endolith") at least 19 km (12 mi) deep underground, and at least 64 km (40 mi) high in the atmosphere.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-SD-19980625-UG-27)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-ABM-20150112-28)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-BBC-20151124-29) Marine life under many forms has been found in the deepest reaches of the [world ocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_ocean "World ocean") while much of the [deep sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_sea "Deep sea") remains to be explored.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-30)

Under certain test conditions, microorganisms have been observed to [survive the vacuum of outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panspermia#Research_in_outer_space "Panspermia").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-Dose-31)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-Horneck-32) The total amount of soil and subsurface bacterial [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") is estimated as 5×10 17 g.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-SD-19980625-UG-27) The mass of [prokaryote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prokaryote "Prokaryote") microorganisms—which includes bacteria and archaea, but not the nucleated [eukaryote microorganisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism#Eukaryotes "Microorganism")—may be as much as 0.8 trillion tons of carbon (of the total biosphere [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomass_(ecology) "Biomass (ecology)"), estimated at between 1 and 4 trillion tons).[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-AGCI-2014-33)[Barophilic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piezophile "Piezophile") marine microbes have been found at more than a depth of 10,000 m (33,000 ft; 6.2 mi) in the [Mariana Trench](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mariana_Trench "Mariana Trench"), the deepest spot in the Earth's oceans.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-34) In fact, single-celled life forms have been found in the deepest part of the Mariana Trench, by the [Challenger Deep](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Challenger_Deep "Challenger Deep"), at depths of 11,034 m (36,201 ft; 6.856 mi).[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-35)[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-LS-20130317-36)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-NG-20130317-37) Other researchers reported related studies that microorganisms thrive inside rocks up to 580 m (1,900 ft; 0.36 mi) below the sea floor under 2,590 m (8,500 ft; 1.61 mi) of ocean off the coast of the [northwestern United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northwestern_United_States "Northwestern United States"),[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-LS-20130317-36)[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-LS-20130314-38) as well as 2,400 m (7,900 ft; 1.5 mi) beneath the seabed off Japan.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-BBC-20141215-RM-39) Culturable thermophilic microbes have been extracted from cores drilled more than 5,000 m (16,000 ft; 3.1 mi) into the [Earth's crust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_crust "Earth's crust") in [Sweden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sweden "Sweden"),[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-Szewzyketal1994-40) from rocks between 65–75°C (149–167°F). Temperature [increases with increasing depth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geothermal_gradient "Geothermal gradient") into the Earth's crust. The rate at which the temperature increases depends on many factors, including the type of crust (continental vs. oceanic), rock type, geographic location, etc. The greatest known temperature at which microbial life can exist is 122°C (252°F) (_[Methanopyrus kandleri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanopyrus\_kandleri "Methanopyrus kandleri")_ Strain 116). It is likely that the limit of life in the "[deep biosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_biosphere "Deep biosphere")" is defined by temperature rather than absolute depth.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] On 20 August 2014, scientists confirmed the existence of microorganisms living 800 m (2,600 ft; 0.50 mi) below the ice of [Antarctica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antarctica "Antarctica").[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-NAT-20140820-41)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-FRB-20140820-42)

Earth's biosphere is divided into several [biomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biome "Biome"), inhabited by fairly similar [flora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flora_(plants) "Flora (plants)") and [fauna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fauna_(animals) "Fauna (animals)"). On land, biomes are separated primarily by [latitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude "Latitude"). Terrestrial biomes lying within the [Arctic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arctic_Circle "Arctic Circle") and [Antarctic Circles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antarctic_Circle "Antarctic Circle") are relatively barren of [plant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant "Plant") and [animal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal "Animal") life. In contrast, most of the more populous biomes lie near the [equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator "Equator").

Artificial biospheres
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Biosphere&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Artificial biospheres")]

[![Image 7: Biosphere 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Biosphere_2_4888964549.jpg/250px-Biosphere_2_4888964549.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Biosphere_2_4888964549.jpg)

Biosphere 2 in Arizona

Experimental biospheres, also called [closed ecological systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_ecological_system "Closed ecological system"), have been created to study ecosystems and the potential for supporting life outside the Earth. These include spacecraft and the following terrestrial laboratories:

*   [Biosphere 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere_2 "Biosphere 2") in [Arizona](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arizona "Arizona"), United States, 3.15 acres (13,000 m 2).
*   BIOS-1, BIOS-2 and [BIOS-3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BIOS-3 "BIOS-3") at the Institute of Biophysics in [Krasnoyarsk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krasnoyarsk "Krasnoyarsk"), [Siberia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siberia "Siberia"), in what was then the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-43)
*   Biosphere J (CEEF, Closed Ecology Experiment Facilities), an experiment in [Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan "Japan").[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-44)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-45)
*   Micro-Ecological Life Support System Alternative ([MELiSSA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MELiSSA "MELiSSA")) at [Autonomous University of Barcelona](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autonomous_University_of_Barcelona "Autonomous University of Barcelona")

No biospheres have been detected beyond the Earth; therefore, the existence of extraterrestrial biospheres remains hypothetical. The [rare Earth hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rare_Earth_hypothesis "Rare Earth hypothesis") suggests they should be very rare, save ones composed of [microbial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbe "Microbe") life only.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-46) On the other hand, [Earth analogs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_analog "Earth analog") may be quite numerous, at least in the [Milky Way galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way_galaxy "Milky Way galaxy"), given the large number of planets.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-47) Three of the planets discovered orbiting [TRAPPIST-1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TRAPPIST-1 "TRAPPIST-1") could possibly contain biospheres.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-48) Given limited understanding of [abiogenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenesis "Abiogenesis"), it is currently unknown what percentage of these planets actually develop biospheres.

Based on observations by the [Kepler Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler_Space_Telescope "Kepler Space Telescope") team, it has been calculated that provided the probability of abiogenesis is higher than 1 to 1000, the closest alien biosphere should be within 100 light-years from the Earth.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-49)

It is also possible that artificial biospheres will be created in the future, for example with the [terraforming of Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terraforming_of_Mars "Terraforming of Mars").[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere#cite_note-50)
