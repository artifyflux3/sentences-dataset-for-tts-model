The **Boomerang Nebula** ([canonical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical "Canonical") name [[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-WakelyHoran-8)) is a bipolar reflection[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-101093mnras1932321-9)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-Bolles''Scattered''-2) young planetary [nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nebula "Nebula")[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-10) located approximately 5,000 [light-years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year "Light-year") from [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth") in the constellation [Centaurus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centaurus "Centaurus").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-Bolles''Scattered''-2)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-11)

Holmberg & Lauberts ([Uppsala Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uppsala_Observatory "Uppsala Observatory")) and Schuster & West ([European Southern Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Southern_Observatory "European Southern Observatory") (ESO)) [[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-12) in their survey of 1976 or earlier discovered the existence of an object at the location.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-101093mnras1932321-9) Before or during 1978 I.S. Glass [[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-1979MNRAS-13) discovered the object as a nebula [[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-SAL%C3%89R-RAMBERG-14) with G. Wegner,[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-SAL%C3%89R-RAMBERG-14)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-1979MNRAS-13) both of [South African Astronomical Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_African_Astronomical_Observatory "South African Astronomical Observatory"), from data of the [ESO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ESO "ESO") Quick Blue Survey.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-1979MNRAS-13) Wegner and Glass in their paper of 1979 mentioned a "butterfly" or "bow-tie" like shape.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-1979MNRAS-13) K. N. R. Taylor ([University of New South Wales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_New_South_Wales "University of New South Wales")) and S. M. Scarrott ([Durham University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Durham_University "Durham University")) made observations July 17, 1979 and named it after the [boomerang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang "Boomerang").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-101093mnras1932321-9) Modelling of measurements of outflow of the nebula published 1997 by Sahai ([Jet Propulsion Laboratory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jet_Propulsion_Laboratory "Jet Propulsion Laboratory")) and Nyman (ESO &[Onsala Space Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Onsala_Space_Observatory "Onsala Space Observatory")) indicate [kelvin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin") (K) less than [cosmic microwave background radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave_background "Microwave background") (cmbr), so the most [cold natural place currently known](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude_(temperature) "Orders of magnitude (temperature)")[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-Sahai_&_Nyman,_1997-15) in the [observed Universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observed_universe "Observed universe").

The central star is an old star. The [max-diametrical temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star#Temperature "Star") of the central star is estimated to be 6000 K (by Wegner and Glass [[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-1991A&A242247B-16) 1978 or earlier) [[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-1979MNRAS-13) or 7000 K (Bujarrabal & Bachiller before July 1990).[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-1991A&A242247B-16)

The Boomerang Nebula is believed to be a [star system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system "Star system") evolving toward the [planetary nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula "Planetary nebula") phase. It continues to form and develop due to the outflow of gas from its core where a star in its late stage life sheds mass and emits starlight, illuminating dust in the nebula. [Millimeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millimeter "Millimeter") scale dust grains obscure portions of the nebula's center, so most escaping visible [light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light "Light") is in two opposing lobes forming a distinctive hourglass shape as viewed by [space telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_telescope "Space telescope") data on Earth. The outflowing gas at about 164[km/s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Km/s "Km/s") expands rapidly into space; this gas expansion results in the nebula's unusual K.

Using observations from 1994 and 1995 with the 15-metre [Swedish-ESO Submillimetre Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swedish-ESO_Submillimetre_Telescope "Swedish-ESO Submillimetre Telescope") in [Chile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chile "Chile"), the astronomers Sahai & Nyman concluded [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide") (CO) molecules produced after stellar co-absorption in a [binary system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_system "Binary system") of the nebula which outflow as a [gas wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_wind "Cosmic wind") were less kinetically excited than the local [outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space "Outer space") ([cmbr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation "Cosmic microwave background radiation")).[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-18)[Radiation transfer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_process "Thermodynamic process") of cmbr into the CO parts [[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-19) of the nebula wind indicated those parts only[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-20) must have a kelvin temperature state which is uniquely the least of any observed location in nature.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-Sahai_&_Nyman,_1997-15)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-21)

The kinetic energy (KE) of the CO outflow is theorized [[d]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-24) as the product of [common-envelope evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_envelope "Common envelope"),[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-Sahai6040102-25) which was a change in the outer environment (an envelope) of the dual orbital system of the binary system.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-Ivanovaetal0159-013-0059-2-22) The KE within the outflow is theorized as an environment forced out from the area of the orbital system of the larger star by the absorption of the lesser sized star into the [core](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_core "Stellar core") of the larger by terminal gravitational attraction.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-Sahai6040102-25) Cooling to sub cmbr temperature is by [adiabatic expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adiabatic_expansion "Adiabatic expansion").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-Sahaietal101088-26)

A succession of periodic observations from November 2011 ([Atacama Large Millimeter Array](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atacama_Large_Millimeter_Array "Atacama Large Millimeter Array")) ending June 2012 ([Australia Telescope Compact Array](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia_Telescope_Compact_Array "Australia Telescope Compact Array")) with archived observations from [Hubble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble "Hubble") (HST) (1998 & 2005) [[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-Sahaietal101088-26) revealed other features.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-27) The nebula's visible double lobe was observed to be surrounded by a larger spherical region of cold gas seen only in sub-millimeter radio wavelengths. The nebula's outer fringes appear to be gradually warming.

As of mid-2017, it is believed that the star at the center of the nebula is a dying [red giant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_giant "Red giant").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-rgb-28)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boomerang_Nebula#cite_note-29)
