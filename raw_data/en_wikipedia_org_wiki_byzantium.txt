**Byzantium** ([/b ɪ ˈ z æ n t i ə m,-ʃ ə m/](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English "Help:IPA/English")) or **Byzantion** ([Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language"): Βυζάντιον) was an [ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece")[city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polis "Polis") in [classical antiquity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_antiquity "Classical antiquity") that became known as [Constantinople](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantinople "Constantinople") in [late antiquity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_antiquity "Late antiquity") and modern [Istanbul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Istanbul "Istanbul"). The Greek name _Byzantion_ and its [Latinization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanization_of_Greek "Romanization of Greek")_Byzantium_ continued to be used as a [name of Constantinople](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Names_of_Constantinople "Names of Constantinople") sporadically and to varying degrees during the thousand-year existence of the [Eastern Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_Empire "Byzantine Empire"), which also became known by the former name of the city as the Byzantine Empire.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-2) Byzantium was colonized by [Greeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greeks "Greeks") from [Megara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megara "Megara") in the 7th century BCE and remained primarily Greek-speaking until [its conquest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fall_of_Constantinople "Fall of Constantinople") by the [Ottoman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire") in 1453 CE.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-3)

Etymology
---------

The etymology of _Byzantium_ is unknown. It has been suggested that the name is of [Thracian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thracian_language "Thracian language") origin.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-janin-4) It may be derived from the Thracian personal name Byzas which means "he-goat".[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-Georgacas1947-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-6) Ancient Greek legend refers to the Greek king [Byzas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzas "Byzas"), the leader of the Megarian colonists and founder ([Oikistes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oikistes "Oikistes")) of the city.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-7) The name _[Lygos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lygos "Lygos")_ for the city, which likely corresponds to an earlier [Thracian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thracian "Thracian") settlement,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-janin-4) is mentioned by [Pliny the Elder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder "Pliny the Elder") in his _[Natural History](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural\_History\_(Pliny) "Natural History (Pliny)")_.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-8)

_Byzántios,_ plural _Byzántioi_ ([Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language"): Βυζάντιος, Βυζάντιοι, [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_language "Latin language"): _Byzantius_; adjective the same) referred to Byzantion's inhabitants, also used as an [ethnonym](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnonym "Ethnonym") for the people of the city and as a family name.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-Georgacas1947-5) In the [Middle Ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Ages "Middle Ages"), _Byzántion_ was also a [synecdoche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synecdoche "Synecdoche") for the [eastern Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Roman_Empire "Eastern Roman Empire"). (An [ellipsis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsis_(linguistics) "Ellipsis (linguistics)") of [Medieval Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_Greek_language "Medieval Greek language"): Βυζάντιον κράτος, [romanized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanization_of_Medieval_Greek "Romanization of Medieval Greek"):_Byzántion krátos_).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-Georgacas1947-5)_Byzantinós_ ([Medieval Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_Greek_language "Medieval Greek language"): Βυζαντινός, [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_language "Latin language"): _Byzantinus_) denoted an inhabitant of the empire.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-Georgacas1947-5) The [Anglicization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anglicisation_of_names "Anglicisation of names") of Latin _Byzantinus_ yielded "Byzantine", with 15th and 16th century forms including _Byzantin_, _Bizantin(e)_, _Bezantin(e)_, and _Bysantin_ as well as _Byzantian_ and _Bizantian_.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-9)

The name _Byzantius_ and _Byzantinus_ were applied from the 9th century to gold [Byzantine coinage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_coinage "Byzantine coinage"), reflected in the French _besant_ (_d'or_), Italian _bisante_, and English _besant_, _byzant_, or _[bezant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bezant "Bezant")_.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-Georgacas1947-5) The English usage, derived from Old French _besan_ (pl. _besanz_), and relating to the coin, dates from the 12th century.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-10)

Later, the name _Byzantium_ became common in the West to refer to the [Eastern Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_Roman_Empire "Eastern Roman Empire"), whose capital was Constantinople. As a term for the east Roman state as a whole, _Byzantium_ was introduced by the historian [Hieronymus Wolf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hieronymus_Wolf "Hieronymus Wolf") only in 1555, a century after the last remnants of the empire, whose inhabitants continued to refer to their polity as the Roman Empire ([Medieval Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_Greek_language "Medieval Greek language"): Βασιλεία τῶν Ῥωμαίων, [romanized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanization_of_Medieval_Greek "Romanization of Medieval Greek"):_Basileía tōn Rhōmaíōn_, [lit.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literal_translation "Literal translation")'empire of the Romans'), had ceased to exist.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-Kazhdan2005-11)

Other places were historically known as _Byzántion_ (Βυζάντιον) – a city in [Libya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Libya "Ancient Libya") mentioned by [Stephanus of Byzantium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephanus_of_Byzantium "Stephanus of Byzantium") and another on the western coast of India referred to by the [Periplus of the Erythraean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periplus_of_the_Erythraean_Sea "Periplus of the Erythraean Sea"); in both cases the names were probably adaptations of names in local languages.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-Georgacas1947-5)[Faustus of Byzantium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faustus_of_Byzantium "Faustus of Byzantium") was from a city of that name in [Cilicia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cilicia "Cilicia").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-Georgacas1947-5)

History
-------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Byzantium&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: History")]

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Lysimachos.jpg/500px-Lysimachos.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Lysimachos.jpg)
**[O:](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obverse_and_reverse "Obverse and reverse")** Head of [Alexander the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_the_Great "Alexander the Great") with [Amun's horns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horns_of_Ammon "Horns of Ammon")**[R:](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obverse_and_reverse "Obverse and reverse")** Seated [Athena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Athena "Athena") holding [Nike](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nike_(mythology) "Nike (mythology)") with [wreath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wreath "Wreath"), [ΒΑΣΙΛΕΩΣ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basileus "Basileus") / [ΛΥΣΙΜΑΧΟΥ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lysimachus "Lysimachus"); monogram (ΠΩΛΥΒ) to left; ΒΥ below [throne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Throne "Throne"); [trident](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trident "Trident") in [exergue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exergue "Exergue")
[Silver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silver "Silver")[tetradrachm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dram_(unit) "Dram (unit)") struck in Byzantion c. 150–100 BCE. Byzantion struck coins in the name of [Lysimachus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lysimachus "Lysimachus") nearly 200 years after his death.

The origins of Byzantium are shrouded in legend. Tradition says that [Byzas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzas "Byzas") of [Megara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megara "Megara") (a city-state near [Athens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Athens "Athens")) founded the city when he sailed northeast across the [Aegean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aegean_Sea "Aegean Sea"). The date is usually given as 667 BCE on the authority of [Herodotus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herodotus "Herodotus"), who states the city was founded 17 years after [Chalcedon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chalcedon "Chalcedon"). [Eusebius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eusebius "Eusebius"), who wrote almost 800 years later, dates the founding of Chalcedon to 685/4 BCE, but he also dates the founding of Byzantium to 656 BCE (or a few years earlier depending on the edition). Herodotus' dating was later favored by [Constantine the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantine_the_Great "Constantine the Great"), who celebrated Byzantium's 1,000th anniversary between the years 333 and 334.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-12)

Byzantium was mainly a trading city due to its location at the [Black Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Sea "Black Sea")'s only entrance. Byzantium later conquered Chalcedon, across the Bosphorus on the Asiatic side.

The city was taken by the [Persian Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire "Achaemenid Empire") at the time of the [Scythian campaign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Scythian_campaign_of_Darius_I "European Scythian campaign of Darius I") (513 BCE) of Emperor [Darius I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Darius_I "Darius I") (r. 522–486 BCE), and was added to the administrative province of [Skudra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skudra "Skudra").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalcer1990599%E2%80%93600-13) Though [Achaemenid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Achaemenid_Empire "Achaemenid Empire") control of the city was never as stable as compared to other cities in [Thrace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thrace "Thrace"), it was considered, alongside [Sestos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sestos "Sestos"), to be one of the foremost Achaemenid ports on the European coast of the Bosphorus and the [Hellespont](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hellespont "Hellespont").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBalcer1990599%E2%80%93600-13)

Byzantium was besieged by Greek forces during the [Peloponnesian War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peloponnesian_War "Peloponnesian War"). As part of [Sparta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sparta "Sparta")'s strategy for cutting off grain supplies to Athens during their siege of Athens, Sparta took control of the city in 411 BCE, to bring the Athenians into submission. The [Athenian military](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Athenian_military "Athenian military") later retook the city in 408 BCE, when the Spartans had withdrawn following their settlement.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-14)

After siding with [Pescennius Niger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pescennius_Niger "Pescennius Niger") against the victorious [Septimius Severus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Septimius_Severus "Septimius Severus"), the city was besieged by Roman forces and suffered extensive damage in AD 196.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-15) Byzantium was rebuilt by Septimius Severus, now emperor, and quickly regained its previous prosperity. It was bound to [Perinthus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perinthus "Perinthus") during the period of Septimius Severus.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-16) After the war, Byzantium lost its city status and free city privileges, but [Caracalla](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caracalla "Caracalla") persuaded Severus to restore these rights. In appreciation, the Byzantines named Caracalla an archon of their city.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-:2-17) The strategic and highly defensible (due to being surrounded by water on almost all sides) location of Byzantium attracted [Roman Emperor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_emperor "Roman emperor")[Constantine I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantine_I_(emperor) "Constantine I (emperor)") who, in AD 330, refounded it as an imperial residence inspired by Rome itself, known as [Nova Roma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Rome "New Rome"). Later the city was called [Constantinople](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantinople "Constantinople") (Greek Κωνσταντινούπολις, _Konstantinoupolis_, "city of Constantine").

This combination of imperialism and location would affect Constantinople's role as the nexus between the continents of Europe and Asia. It was a commercial, cultural, and diplomatic centre and for centuries formed the capital of the [Byzantine Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_Empire "Byzantine Empire"), which decorated the city with numerous monuments, some still standing today. With its strategic position, Constantinople controlled the major trade routes between Asia and Europe, as well as the passage from the [Mediterranean Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea "Mediterranean Sea") to the [Black Sea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_Sea "Black Sea"). On May 29, 1453, the city was conquered by the [Ottoman Turks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Turks "Ottoman Turks"), and again became the capital of a powerful state, the [Ottoman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ottoman_Empire "Ottoman Empire"). The Turks called the city "Istanbul" (although it was not officially renamed until 1930); the name derives from the Greek phrase "στην πόλη", which means "to the city". To this day it remains the largest and most populous city in [Turkey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turkey "Turkey"), although [Ankara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ankara "Ankara") is now the national capital.

Emblem
------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Byzantium&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Emblem")]

Main article: [Star and crescent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_and_crescent "Star and crescent")

By the late [Hellenistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hellenistic_civilization "Hellenistic civilization") or early Roman period (1st century BCE), the [star and crescent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_and_crescent "Star and crescent") motif was associated to some degree with Byzantium; even though it became more widely used as the royal emblem of [Mithradates VI Eupator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mithradates_VI_Eupator "Mithradates VI Eupator") (who for a time incorporated the city into [his empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Pontus "Kingdom of Pontus")).[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-18)

Some Byzantine coins of the 1st century BCE and later show the head of [Artemis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis "Artemis") with bow and quiver, and feature a crescent with what appears to be an eight-rayed star on the reverse. According to accounts which vary in some of the details, in 340 BCE the Byzantines and their allies the [Athenians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Athens "Classical Athens") were under siege by the troops of [Philip of Macedon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philip_II_of_Macedon "Philip II of Macedon"). On a particularly dark and wet night Philip attempted a surprise attack but was thwarted by the appearance of a bright light in the sky. This light is occasionally described by subsequent interpreters as a [meteor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor "Meteor"), sometimes as the moon, and some accounts also mention the barking of dogs. However, the original accounts mention only a bright light in the sky, without specifying the moon.[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-20)[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-22) To commemorate the event the Byzantines erected a statue of [Hecate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hecate "Hecate")_lampadephoros_ (light-bearer or bringer). This story survived in the works of [Hesychius of Miletus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hesychius_of_Miletus "Hesychius of Miletus"), who in all probability lived in the time of [Justinian I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Justinian_I "Justinian I"). His works survive only in fragments preserved in [Photius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photius "Photius") and the tenth century lexicographer [Suidas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suda "Suda"). The tale is also related by [Stephanus of Byzantium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephanus_of_Byzantium "Stephanus of Byzantium"), and [Eustathius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eustathius_of_Thessalonica "Eustathius of Thessalonica").

Devotion to [Hecate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hecate "Hecate") was especially favored by the Byzantines for her aid in having protected them from the incursions of Philip of Macedon. Her symbols were the crescent and star, and the walls of her city were her provenance.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-FOOTNOTELimberis199415-23) This contradicts claims that only the symbol of the crescent was meant to symbolize Hecate, whereas the star was only added later in order to symbolize the Virgin Mary, as Constantine I is said to have rededicated the city to her in the year 330.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-24)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-25)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-26)

It is unclear precisely how the symbol Hecate/Artemis, one of many goddesses[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-28) would have been transferred to the city itself, but it seems likely to have been an effect of being credited with the intervention against Philip and the subsequent honors. This was a common process in ancient Greece, as in _Athens_ where the city was named after _Athena_ in honor of such an intervention in time of war.

Cities in the [Roman Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Empire "Roman Empire") often continued to issue their own coinage. "Of the many themes that were used on local coinage, celestial and astral symbols often appeared, mostly stars or crescent moons."[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantium#cite_note-29) The wide variety of these issues, and the varying explanations for the significance of the star and crescent on Roman coinage precludes their discussion here. It is, however, apparent that by the time of the Romans, coins featuring a star or crescent in some combination were not at all rare.
