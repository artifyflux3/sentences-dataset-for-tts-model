The **International System of Quantities** (**ISQ**) is a standard system of [quantities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantity "Quantity") used in [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") and in modern science in general. It includes basic quantities such as length and mass and the relationships between those quantities.[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Quantities#cite_note-2) This system underlies the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") (SI)[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Quantities#cite_note-4) but does not itself determine the units of measurement used for the quantities.

The system is formally described in a multi-part [ISO standard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_standard "ISO standard")[ISO/IEC 80000](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO/IEC_80000 "ISO/IEC 80000") (which also defines many other quantities used in science and technology), first completed in 2009 and subsequently revised and expanded.

The **base quantities** of a given system of [physical quantities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_quantity "Physical quantity") is a subset of those quantities, where no base quantity can be expressed in terms of the others, but where every quantity in the system can be expressed in terms of the base quantities. Within this constraint, the set of base quantities is chosen by convention. There are seven **ISQ base quantities**. The symbols for them, as for other quantities, are written in italics.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Quantities#cite_note-ISO_80000-1:2009-1)

The dimension of a physical quantity does not include magnitude or units. The conventional symbolic representation of the dimension of a base quantity is a single upper-case letter in [roman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_(typeface) "Roman (typeface)") (upright) [sans-serif](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sans-serif "Sans-serif")[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Quantities#cite_note-5) type.

A **derived quantity** is a quantity in a system of quantities that is defined in terms of only the base quantities of that system. The ISQ defines many derived quantities and corresponding [derived units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derived_unit "Derived unit").

### Dimensional expression of derived quantities

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=International_System_of_Quantities&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Dimensional expression of derived quantities")]

The conventional symbolic representation of the dimension of a derived quantity is the product of powers of the dimensions of the base quantities according to the definition of the derived quantity. #### Dimensionless quantities

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=International_System_of_Quantities&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Dimensionless quantities")]

A _quantity of [dimension one](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension\_one "Dimension one")_ is historically known as a _[dimensionless quantity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensionless\_quantity "Dimensionless quantity")_ (a term that is still commonly used); all its dimensional exponents are zero and its dimension symbol is ![Image 25: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf). Such a quantity can be regarded as a derived quantity in the form of the ratio of two quantities of the same dimension. The named dimensionless units "[radian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian")" (rad) and "[steradian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steradian "Steradian")" (sr) are acceptable for distinguishing dimensionless quantities of different kind, respectively [plane angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_angle "Plane angle") and [solid angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_angle "Solid angle").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Quantities#cite_note-80000-3:2019-7)

### Logarithmic quantities

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=International_System_of_Quantities&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Logarithmic quantities")]

The _level_ of a quantity is defined as the [logarithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logarithm "Logarithm") of the ratio of the quantity with a stated reference value of that quantity. Within the ISQ it is differently defined for a root-power quantity (also known by the deprecated term _field quantity_) and for a power quantity. It is not defined for ratios of quantities of other kinds. Within the ISQ, all levels are treated as derived quantities of dimension 1.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] Several units for levels are defined by the SI and classified as "non-SI units accepted for use with the SI units".[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Quantities#cite_note-8) An example of level is [sound pressure level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sound_pressure_level "Sound pressure level"), with the unit of [decibel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decibel "Decibel").

#### Other logarithmic quantities

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=International_System_of_Quantities&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Other logarithmic quantities")]

Units of [logarithmic frequency ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logarithmic_frequency_ratio "Logarithmic frequency ratio") include the [octave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octave "Octave"), corresponding to a factor of 2 in frequency (precisely) and the [decade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decade_(log_scale) "Decade (log scale)"), corresponding to a factor 10.

The ISQ recognizes another logarithmic quantity, [information entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_entropy "Information entropy"), for which the coherent unit is the [natural unit of information](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_unit_of_information "Natural unit of information") (symbol nat).[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The system is formally described in a multi-part [ISO standard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_standard "ISO standard")[ISO/IEC 80000](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO/IEC_80000 "ISO/IEC 80000"), first completed in 2009 but subsequently revised and expanded, which replaced standards published in 1992, [ISO 31](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_31 "ISO 31") and [ISO 1000](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO_1000 "ISO 1000"). Working jointly, ISO and IEC have formalized parts of the ISQ by giving information and definitions concerning quantities, systems of quantities, units, quantity and unit symbols, and coherent unit systems, with particular reference to the ISQ. ISO/IEC 80000 defines physical [quantities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantity "Quantity") that are measured with the [SI units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units")[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Quantities#cite_note-9) and also includes many other quantities in modern science and technology.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Quantities#cite_note-ISO_80000-1:2009-1) The name "International System of Quantities" is used by the [General Conference on Weights and Measures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Conference_on_Weights_and_Measures "General Conference on Weights and Measures") (CGPM) to describe the system of quantities that underlie the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units").
