This article is about associating a scalar value with every point in a space. For the set whose members are [scalars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(mathematics) "Scalar (mathematics)"), see [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)").

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a8/Scalar_field.png/250px-Scalar_field.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Scalar_field.png)

A scalar field such as temperature or pressure, where intensity of the field is represented by different hues of colors.

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") and [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), a **scalar field** is a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") associating a single[_[dubious](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy\_dispute#Disputed\_statement "Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute") – [discuss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Talk:Scalar\_field#Dubious "Talk:Scalar field")_][number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number "Number") to each [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)") in a [region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Region_(mathematics) "Region (mathematics)") of [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_(mathematics) "Space (mathematics)") – possibly [physical space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_space "Physical space"). The scalar may either be a pure [mathematical number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(mathematics) "Scalar (mathematics)") ([dimensionless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensionless "Dimensionless")) or a [scalar physical quantity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(physics) "Scalar (physics)") (with [units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_measurement "Unit of measurement")).

In a physical context, scalar fields are required to be independent of the choice of reference frame. That is, any two observers using the same units will agree on the value of the scalar field at the same absolute point in space (or [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime")) regardless of their respective points of origin. Examples used in physics include the [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature") distribution throughout space, the [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure") distribution in a fluid, and [spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)")-zero quantum fields, such as the [Higgs field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higgs_field "Higgs field"). These fields are the subject of [scalar field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field_theory "Scalar field theory").

Mathematically, a scalar field on a [region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Region_(mathematical_analysis) "Region (mathematical analysis)")_U_ is a [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real-valued_function "Real-valued function") or [complex-valued function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex-valued_function "Complex-valued function") or [distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distribution_(mathematics) "Distribution (mathematics)") on _U_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field#cite_note-2) The region _U_ may be a set in some [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), [Minkowski space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_space "Minkowski space"), or more generally a subset of a [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold"), and it is typical in mathematics to impose further conditions on the field, such that it be [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function") or often [continuously differentiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuously_differentiable "Continuously differentiable") to some order. A scalar field is a [tensor field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field "Tensor field") of order zero,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field#cite_note-3) and the term "scalar field" may be used to distinguish a function of this kind with a more general tensor field, [density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_bundle "Density bundle"), or [differential form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form").

Physically, a scalar field is additionally distinguished by having [units of measurement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Units_of_measurement "Units of measurement") associated with it. In this context, a scalar field should also be independent of the coordinate system used to describe the physical system—that is, any two [observers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observer_(special_relativity) "Observer (special relativity)") using the same units must agree on the numerical value of a scalar field at any given point of physical space. Scalar fields are contrasted with other physical quantities such as [vector fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field"), which associate a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") to every point of a region, as well as [tensor fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field "Tensor field") and [spinor fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinor "Spinor").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] More subtly, scalar fields are often contrasted with [pseudoscalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudoscalar "Pseudoscalar") fields.

In physics, scalar fields often describe the [potential energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potential_energy "Potential energy") associated with a particular [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force"). The force is a [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field"), which can be obtained as a factor of the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient") of the potential energy scalar field. Examples include:

*   Potential fields, such as the Newtonian [gravitational potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_potential "Gravitational potential"), or the [electric potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_potential "Electric potential") in [electrostatics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrostatics "Electrostatics"), are scalar fields which describe the more familiar forces.
*   A [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature"), [humidity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humidity "Humidity"), or [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure") field, such as those used in [meteorology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorology "Meteorology").

### Examples in quantum theory and relativity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Scalar_field&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Examples in quantum theory and relativity")]

*   In [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory"), a [scalar field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bosonic_field "Bosonic field") is associated with spin-0 particles. The scalar field may be real or complex valued. Complex scalar fields represent charged particles. These include the [Higgs field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higgs_field "Higgs field") of the [Standard Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model "Standard Model"), as well as the charged [pions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pions "Pions") mediating the [strong nuclear interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strong_nuclear_interaction "Strong nuclear interaction").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field#cite_note-4)
*   In the [Standard Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model "Standard Model") of elementary particles, a scalar [Higgs field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higgs_field "Higgs field") is used to give the [leptons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lepton "Lepton") and [massive vector bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W_and_Z_bosons "W and Z bosons") their mass, via a combination of the [Yukawa interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yukawa_interaction "Yukawa interaction") and the [spontaneous symmetry breaking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spontaneous_symmetry_breaking "Spontaneous symmetry breaking"). This mechanism is known as the [Higgs mechanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higgs_mechanism "Higgs mechanism").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field#cite_note-5) A candidate for the [Higgs boson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higgs_boson "Higgs boson") was first detected at CERN in 2012.
*   In [scalar theories of gravitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_theories_of_gravitation "Scalar theories of gravitation") scalar fields are used to describe the gravitational field.
*   [Scalar–tensor theories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar%E2%80%93tensor_theory "Scalar–tensor theory") represent the gravitational interaction through both a tensor and a scalar. Such attempts are for example the [Jordan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascual_Jordan "Pascual Jordan") theory[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field#cite_note-6) as a generalization of the [Kaluza–Klein theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory "Kaluza–Klein theory") and the [Brans–Dicke theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brans%E2%80%93Dicke_theory "Brans–Dicke theory").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field#cite_note-7)
    *   Scalar fields like the Higgs field can be found within scalar–tensor theories, using as scalar field the Higgs field of the [Standard Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model "Standard Model").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field#cite_note-9) This field interacts gravitationally and [Yukawa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yukawa_interaction "Yukawa interaction")-like (short-ranged) with the particles that get mass through it.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field#cite_note-10)

*   Scalar fields are found within superstring theories as [dilaton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dilaton "Dilaton") fields, breaking the conformal symmetry of the string, though balancing the quantum anomalies of this tensor.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field#cite_note-11)
*   Scalar fields are hypothesized to have caused the high accelerated expansion of the early universe ([inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflation_(cosmology) "Inflation (cosmology)")),[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field#cite_note-12) helping to solve the [horizon problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizon_problem "Horizon problem") and giving a hypothetical reason for the non-vanishing [cosmological constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_constant "Cosmological constant") of cosmology. Massless (i.e. long-ranged) scalar fields in this context are known as [inflatons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflaton "Inflaton"). Massive (i.e. short-ranged) scalar fields are proposed, too, using for example Higgs-like fields.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field#cite_note-13)

Other kinds of fields
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Scalar_field&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Other kinds of fields")]

*   [Vector fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field"), which associate a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(geometry) "Vector (geometry)") to every point in space. Some examples of [vector fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field") include the air flow ([wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wind "Wind")) in meteorology.
*   [Tensor fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field "Tensor field"), which associate a [tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") to every point in space. For example, in [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") gravitation is associated with the tensor field called [Einstein tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_tensor "Einstein tensor"). In [Kaluza–Klein theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory "Kaluza–Klein theory"), spacetime is extended to five dimensions and its [Riemann curvature tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_curvature_tensor "Riemann curvature tensor") can be separated out into ordinary [four-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension") gravitation plus an extra set, which is equivalent to [Maxwell's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations "Maxwell's equations") for the [electromagnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_field "Electromagnetic field"), plus an extra scalar field known as the "[dilaton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dilaton "Dilaton")".[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] (The [dilaton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dilaton "Dilaton") scalar is also found among the massless bosonic fields in [string theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_theory "String theory").)

*   [Scalar field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field_theory "Scalar field theory")
*   [Vector boson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_boson "Vector boson")
*   [Vector-valued function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector-valued_function "Vector-valued function")
