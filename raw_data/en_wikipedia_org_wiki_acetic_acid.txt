**Acetic acid** , systematically named **ethanoic acid** , is an acidic, colourless liquid and [organic compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound") with the [chemical formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")CH 3 COOH (also written as CH 3 CO 2 H, C 2 H 4 O 2, or HC 2 H 3 O 2). [Vinegar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinegar "Vinegar") is at least 4% acetic acid by volume, making acetic acid the main component of vinegar apart from water. Historically, vinegar was produced from the third century BC and was likely the first acid to be produced in large quantities.

Acetic acid is the second simplest [carboxylic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboxylic_acid "Carboxylic acid") (after [formic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid "Formic acid")). It is an important [chemical reagent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reagent "Reagent") and industrial chemical across various fields, used primarily in the production of [cellulose acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellulose_acetate "Cellulose acetate") for [photographic film](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photographic_film "Photographic film"), [polyvinyl acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyvinyl_acetate "Polyvinyl acetate") for wood [glue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adhesive "Adhesive"), and [synthetic fibres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthetic_fibres "Synthetic fibres") and fabrics. In households, diluted acetic acid is often used in [descaling agents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Descaling_agent "Descaling agent"). In the [food industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_industry "Food industry"), acetic acid is controlled by the [food additive code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E_number "E number") E260 as an [acidity regulator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acidity_regulator "Acidity regulator") and as a condiment. In [biochemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biochemistry "Biochemistry"), the [acetyl group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetyl_group "Acetyl group"), derived from acetic acid, is fundamental to all forms of life. When bound to [coenzyme A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coenzyme_A "Coenzyme A"), it is central to the [metabolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism "Metabolism") of [carbohydrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbohydrate "Carbohydrate") and [fats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fat "Fat").

The global demand for acetic acid as of 2023 is about 17.88 million [metric tonnes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tonne "Tonne") per year (t/a). Most of the world's acetic acid is produced via the [carbonylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonylation "Carbonylation") of [methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol"). Its production and subsequent industrial use poses health hazards to workers, including incidental skin damage and chronic respiratory injuries from inhalation.

The [trivial name](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trivial_name "Trivial name") "acetic acid" is the most commonly used and [preferred IUPAC name](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preferred_IUPAC_name "Preferred IUPAC name"). The systematic name "ethanoic acid", a valid [IUPAC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUPAC "IUPAC") name, is constructed according to the substitutive nomenclature.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-BB-prs310305-8) The name "acetic acid" derives from the [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin") word for [vinegar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinegar "Vinegar"), "_[acetum](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/acetum#Latin "wikt:acetum")_", which means "to sour" in relation to the bitter taste of fermented fruits.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Myers-9)

"Glacial acetic acid" is a name for water-free ([anhydrous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anhydrous "Anhydrous")) acetic acid. Similar to the [German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_language "German language") name "Eisessig" ("ice vinegar"), the name comes from the solid ice-like crystals that form with agitation, slightly below room temperature at 16.6°C (61.9°F). Acetic acid can never be truly water-free in an atmosphere that contains water, so the presence of 0.1% water in glacial acetic acid lowers its melting point by 0.2°C.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Purification_of_Laboratory_Chemicals-10)

A common [symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbol_(chemistry) "Symbol (chemistry)") for acetic acid is AcOH (or HOAc), where Ac is the [pseudoelement symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudoelement_symbol "Pseudoelement symbol") representing the [acetyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetyl "Acetyl")[group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_group "Functional group")CH 3−C(=O)−; the [conjugate base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjugate_acid "Conjugate acid"), [acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetate "Acetate") (CH 3 COO−), is thus represented as AcO−.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Cooper-11) Acetate is the [ion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion "Ion") resulting from loss of [H+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") from acetic acid. The name "acetate" can also refer to a [salt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt_(chemistry) "Salt (chemistry)") containing this anion, or an [ester](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ester "Ester") of acetic acid.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-12) (The symbol Ac for the acetyl functional group is not to be confused with the symbol Ac for the element [actinium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Actinium "Actinium"); context prevents confusion among organic chemists). To better reflect its structure, acetic acid is often written as CH 3−C(O)OH, CH 3−C(=O)−OH, CH 3 COOH, and CH 3 CO 2 H. In the context of [acid–base reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction "Acid–base reaction"), the abbreviation HAc is sometimes used,[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-13) where Ac in this case is a symbol for acetate (rather than acetyl).

The carboxymethyl functional group derived from removing one hydrogen from the [methyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl "Methyl") group of acetic acid has the [chemical formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")−CH 2−C(=O)−OH.

[Vinegar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinegar "Vinegar") is a diluted solution of acetic acid and was known early in civilization as the natural result of exposure of [beer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beer "Beer") and [wine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wine "Wine") to air because acetic acid-producing bacteria are present globally. It is likely the first acid to be produced in large quantities in history.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Myers-9) The use of acetic acid in [alchemy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alchemy "Alchemy") extends into the third century BC, when the Greek philosopher [Theophrastus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theophrastus "Theophrastus") described how vinegar acted on metals to produce [pigments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pigment "Pigment") useful in art, including _white lead_ ([lead carbonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lead_carbonate "Lead carbonate")) and _[verdigris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Verdigris "Verdigris")_, a green mixture of [copper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper "Copper") salts including [copper(II) acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper(II)_acetate "Copper(II) acetate"). Hippocrates used vinegar as an antiseptic and as a remedy for numerous conditions including fever, constipation, ulcers, and pleurisy.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Myers-9) Ancient [Romans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rome "Rome") boiled soured wine to produce a highly sweet syrup called _sapa_. [Sapa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defrutum "Defrutum") that was produced in lead pots was rich in [lead acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lead(II)_acetate "Lead(II) acetate"), a sweet substance also called _sugar of lead_ or _sugar of [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn\_(mythology) "Saturn (mythology)")_, which contributed to [lead poisoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lead_poisoning "Lead poisoning") among the Roman aristocracy.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-martin-14)

In the 16th-century [German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany "Germany") alchemist [Andreas Libavius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andreas_Libavius "Andreas Libavius") described the production of [acetone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone "Acetone") from the [dry distillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dry_distillation "Dry distillation") of lead acetate, [ketonic decarboxylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketonic_decarboxylation "Ketonic decarboxylation"). The presence of water in vinegar has such a profound effect on acetic acid's properties that for centuries chemists believed that glacial acetic acid and the acid found in vinegar were two different substances. French chemist [Pierre Adet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Adet "Pierre Adet") proved them identical.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-martin-14)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-15)

[![Image 26: glass beaker of crystallized acetic acid](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/AceticAcid012.jpg/250px-AceticAcid012.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:AceticAcid012.jpg)

Crystallized acetic acid

In 1845 German chemist [Hermann Kolbe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Kolbe "Hermann Kolbe")[synthesized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_synthesis "Chemical synthesis") acetic acid from [inorganic compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inorganic_compound "Inorganic compound") for the first time. This reaction sequence consisted of [chlorination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorination_reaction "Chlorination reaction") of [carbon disulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_disulfide "Carbon disulfide") to [carbon tetrachloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_tetrachloride "Carbon tetrachloride"), followed by [pyrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrolysis "Pyrolysis") to [tetrachloroethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrachloroethylene "Tetrachloroethylene") and aqueous chlorination to [trichloroacetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trichloroacetic_acid "Trichloroacetic acid"), and concluded with [electrolytic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrolysis "Electrolysis")[reduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_redox_reaction "Organic redox reaction") to acetic acid.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-goldwhite-16)

By 1910, most glacial acetic acid was obtained from the [pyroligneous liquor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyroligneous_acid "Pyroligneous acid"), a product of the distillation of wood. The acetic acid was isolated by treatment with [milk of lime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limewater "Limewater"), and the resulting [calcium acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_acetate "Calcium acetate") was then acidified with [sulfuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid") to recover acetic acid. At that time, Germany was producing 10,000 [tons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ton "Ton") of glacial acetic acid, around 30% of which was used for the manufacture of [indigo dye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indigo_dye "Indigo dye").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-martin-14)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-schweppe-17)

Because both [methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol") and [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide") are commodity raw materials, methanol carbonylation long appeared to be attractive precursors to acetic acid. [Henri Dreyfus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Dreyfus "Henri Dreyfus") at [British Celanese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Celanese "British Celanese") developed a methanol carbonylation pilot plant as early as 1925.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-wagner-18) However, a lack of practical materials that could contain the corrosive reaction mixture at the high [pressures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure") needed (200 [atm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit) "Atmosphere (unit)") or more) discouraged commercialization of these routes. The first commercial methanol carbonylation process, which used a [cobalt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cobalt "Cobalt") catalyst, was developed by German chemical company [BASF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BASF "BASF") in 1963. In 1968, a [rhodium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhodium "Rhodium")-based catalyst (_cis_−[Rh(CO)2 I 2]−) was discovered that could operate efficiently at lower pressure with almost no by-products. US chemical company [Monsanto Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monsanto_Company "Monsanto Company") built the first plant using this catalyst in 1970, and rhodium-catalyzed methanol carbonylation became the dominant method of acetic acid production (see [Monsanto process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monsanto_process "Monsanto process")). In the late 1990s, [BP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BP "BP") Chemicals commercialized the Cativa catalyst ([Ir(CO)2 I 2]−), which is promoted by [iridium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iridium "Iridium") for greater efficiency.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-19) Known as the [Cativa process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cativa_process "Cativa process"), the [iridium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iridium "Iridium")-catalyzed production of glacial acetic acid is [greener](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_chemistry "Green chemistry"), and has largely supplanted the Monsanto process, often in the same production plants.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-lancaster-20)

### Interstellar medium

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetic_acid&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Interstellar medium")]

[Interstellar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium") acetic acid was discovered in 1996 by a team led by David Mehringer[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Meh-21) using the former [Berkeley-Illinois-Maryland Association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berkeley-Illinois-Maryland_Association "Berkeley-Illinois-Maryland Association") array at the [Hat Creek Radio Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hat_Creek_Radio_Observatory "Hat Creek Radio Observatory") and the former [Millimeter Array](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millimeter_Array "Millimeter Array") located at the [Owens Valley Radio Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Owens_Valley_Radio_Observatory "Owens Valley Radio Observatory"). It was first detected in the [Sagittarius B2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sagittarius_B2 "Sagittarius B2") North molecular cloud (also known as the Sgr B2 [Large Molecule Heimat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_Molecule_Heimat "Large Molecule Heimat") source). Acetic acid has the distinction of being the first molecule discovered in the interstellar medium using solely [radio interferometers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_telescope#Radio_interferometry "Radio telescope"); in all previous ISM molecular discoveries made in the millimetre and centimetre wavelength regimes, single dish radio telescopes were at least partly responsible for the detections.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Meh-21)

[![Image 27](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/19/AceticAcid010.png/190px-AceticAcid010.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:AceticAcid010.png)

Acetic acid crystals

The hydrogen centre in the [carboxyl group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboxyl_group "Carboxyl group") (−COOH) in carboxylic acids such as acetic acid can separate from the molecule by ionization:

CH 3 COOH ⇌ CH 3 CO−2 + H+
Because of this release of the [proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") (H+), acetic acid has acidic character. Acetic acid is a weak [monoprotic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monoprotic_acid "Monoprotic acid"). In aqueous solution, it has a [pK a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid_dissociation_constant "Acid dissociation constant") value of 4.76.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Goldmine-22) Its [conjugate base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjugate_acid "Conjugate acid") is [acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetate "Acetate") (CH 3 COO−). A 1.0[M](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molarity "Molarity") solution (about the concentration of domestic vinegar) has a [pH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PH "PH") of 2.4, indicating that merely 0.4% of the acetic acid molecules are dissociated.[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-23)

[![Image 28: Deprotonation equilibrium of acetic acid in water](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Acetic_acid_deprotonation.svg/500px-Acetic_acid_deprotonation.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Acetic_acid_deprotonation.svg "Deprotonation equilibrium of acetic acid in water")

[![Image 29](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/be/Acetic_Acid_Hydrogenbridge_V.1.svg/250px-Acetic_Acid_Hydrogenbridge_V.1.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Acetic_Acid_Hydrogenbridge_V.1.svg)

Cyclic dimer of acetic acid; dashed **green** lines represent hydrogen bonds

In solid acetic acid, the molecules form chains of individual molecules interconnected by [hydrogen bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_bond "Hydrogen bond").[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-jones-24) In the vapour phase at 120°C (248°F), [dimers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimer_(chemistry) "Dimer (chemistry)") can be detected. Dimers also occur in the liquid phase in dilute solutions with non-hydrogen-bonding solvents, and to a certain extent in pure acetic acid,[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-briggs-25) but are disrupted by hydrogen-bonding solvents. The dissociation [enthalpy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enthalpy "Enthalpy") of the dimer is estimated at 65.0–66.0 kJ/mol, and the dissociation entropy at 154–157 J mol−1 K−1.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-togeas-26) Other carboxylic acids engage in similar intermolecular hydrogen bonding interactions.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-27)

[Liquid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid "Liquid") acetic acid is a [hydrophilic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrophile "Hydrophile") ([polar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_molecule "Polar molecule")) [protic solvent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protic_solvent "Protic solvent"), similar to [ethanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol "Ethanol") and [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water "Water"). With a [relative static permittivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relative_static_permittivity "Relative static permittivity") (dielectric constant) of 6.2, it dissolves not only polar compounds such as inorganic salts and [sugars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sugar "Sugar"), but also non-polar compounds such as oils as well as polar solutes. It is miscible with polar and non-polar [solvents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvent "Solvent") such as water, [chloroform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloroform "Chloroform"), and [hexane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexane "Hexane"). With higher alkanes (starting with [octane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octane "Octane")), acetic acid is not [miscible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miscible "Miscible") at all compositions, and solubility of acetic acid in alkanes declines with longer n-alkanes.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Zieborak-28) The solvent and [miscibility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miscibility "Miscibility") properties of acetic acid make it a useful industrial chemical, for example, as a solvent in the production of [dimethyl terephthalate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimethyl_terephthalate "Dimethyl terephthalate").[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Ullmann-29)

At physiological pHs, acetic acid is usually fully ionized to [acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetate "Acetate") in aqueous solution.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-30)

The [acetyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetyl "Acetyl")[group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_group "Functional group"), formally derived from acetic acid, is fundamental to all forms of life. Typically, it is bound to [coenzyme A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coenzyme_A "Coenzyme A") by [acetyl-CoA synthetase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetyl-CoA_synthetase "Acetyl-CoA synthetase") enzymes,[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-31) where it is central to the [metabolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism "Metabolism") of [carbohydrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbohydrate "Carbohydrate") and [fats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fat "Fat"). Unlike longer-chain carboxylic acids (the [fatty acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fatty_acids "Fatty acids")), acetic acid does not occur in natural [triglycerides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triglyceride "Triglyceride"). Most of the acetate generated in cells for use in [acetyl-CoA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetyl-CoA "Acetyl-CoA") is synthesized directly from [ethanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol "Ethanol") or [pyruvate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyruvic_acid "Pyruvic acid").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-32) However, the artificial triglyceride [triacetin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triacetin "Triacetin") (glycerine triacetate) is a common food additive and is found in cosmetics and topical medicines; this additive is metabolized to [glycerol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycerol "Glycerol") and acetic acid in the body.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-33)

Acetic acid is produced and [excreted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excretion "Excretion") by [acetic acid bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid_bacteria "Acetic acid bacteria"), notably the genus _[Acetobacter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetobacter "Acetobacter")_ and _[Clostridium acetobutylicum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clostridium\_acetobutylicum "Clostridium acetobutylicum")_. These bacteria are found universally in [foodstuffs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food "Food"), [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water "Water"), and [soil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil "Soil"), and acetic acid is produced naturally as fruits and other foods spoil. Acetic acid is also a component of the [vaginal lubrication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vaginal_lubrication "Vaginal lubrication") of [humans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human "Human") and other [primates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primate "Primate"), where it appears to serve as a mild [antibacterial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antibacterial "Antibacterial") agent.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-dict-34)

[![Image 30](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/Acetic_acid_1884_plant.jpg/250px-Acetic_acid_1884_plant.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Acetic_acid_1884_plant.jpg)

Purification and concentration plant for acetic acid in 1884

Acetic acid is produced industrially both naturally via bacterial [fermentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermentation_(biochemistry) "Fermentation (biochemistry)") and synthetically.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Myers-9) About 75% of acetic acid made for use in the chemical industry is made by the [carbonylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonylation "Carbonylation") of [methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol"), explained below.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Ullmann-29) The biological route accounts for only about 10% of world production, but it remains important for the production of vinegar because many food purity laws require vinegar used in foods to be of biological origin. Other processes are [methyl formate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_formate "Methyl formate") isomerization, conversion of [syngas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syngas "Syngas") to acetic acid, and gas phase oxidation of [ethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene "Ethylene") and [ethanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol "Ethanol").[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Yoneda_2001-35)

Acetic acid can be purified via [fractional freezing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractional_freezing "Fractional freezing") using an ice bath. The water and other [impurities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impurity "Impurity") will remain liquid while the acetic acid will [precipitate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precipitation_(chemistry) "Precipitation (chemistry)") out. As of 2003–2005, total worldwide production of virgin acetic acid[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-36) was estimated at 5 Mt/a (million tonnes per year), approximately half of which was produced in the United States. European production was approximately 1 Mt/a and declining, while Japanese production was 0.7 Mt/a. Another 1.5 Mt were recycled each year, bringing the total world market to 6.5 Mt/a.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-suresh-37)[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-38) Since then, the global production has increased from 10.7 Mt/a in 2010[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-39) to 17.88 Mt/a in 2023.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-40) The two biggest producers of virgin acetic acid are [Celanese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celanese "Celanese") and [BP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BP "BP") Chemicals. Other major producers include [Millennium Chemicals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millennium_Chemicals "Millennium Chemicals"), [Sterling Chemicals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sterling_Chemicals "Sterling Chemicals"), [Samsung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samsung "Samsung"), [Eastman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastman_Chemical_Company "Eastman Chemical Company"), and [Svensk Etanolkemi](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Svensk_Etanolkemi&action=edit&redlink=1 "Svensk Etanolkemi (page does not exist)")[[sv](https://sv.wikipedia.org/wiki/Svensk_Etanolkemi "sv:Svensk Etanolkemi")].[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-41)

### Methanol carbonylation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetic_acid&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Methanol carbonylation")]

Most acetic acid is produced by methanol [carbonylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonylation "Carbonylation"). In this process, [methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol") and [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide") react to produce acetic acid according to the equation:

[![Image 31](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Methanol_formylation.png/250px-Methanol_formylation.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Methanol_formylation.png)
The process involves [iodomethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iodomethane "Iodomethane") as an intermediate, and occurs in three steps. A [metal carbonyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal_carbonyl "Metal carbonyl")[catalyst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalysis "Catalysis") is needed for the carbonylation (step 2).[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Yoneda_2001-35)

1.   CH 3 OH + HI → CH 3 I + H 2 O
2.   CH 3 I + CO → CH 3 COI
3.   CH 3 COI + H 2 O → CH 3 COOH + HI

Two related processes exist for the carbonylation of methanol: the rhodium-catalyzed [Monsanto process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monsanto_process "Monsanto process"), and the iridium-catalyzed [Cativa process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cativa_process "Cativa process"). The latter process is [greener](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_chemistry "Green chemistry") and more efficient and has largely supplanted the former process.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-lancaster-20) Catalytic amounts of water are used in both processes, but the Cativa process requires less, so the [water-gas shift reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water-gas_shift_reaction "Water-gas shift reaction") is suppressed, and fewer by-products are formed.

By altering the process conditions, [acetic anhydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_anhydride "Acetic anhydride") may also be produced in plants using rhodium catalysis.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-42)

### Acetaldehyde oxidation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetic_acid&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Acetaldehyde oxidation")]

Prior to the commercialization of the Monsanto process, most acetic acid was produced by oxidation of [acetaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde "Acetaldehyde"). This remains the second-most-important manufacturing method, although it is usually not competitive with the carbonylation of methanol. The acetaldehyde can be produced by [hydration of acetylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkyne_hydration "Alkyne hydration"). This was the dominant technology in the early 1900s.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-43)

Light [naphtha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphtha "Naphtha") components are readily oxidized by oxygen or even air to give [peroxides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_peroxide "Organic peroxide"), which decompose to produce acetic acid according to the [chemical equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_equation "Chemical equation"), illustrated with [butane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butane "Butane"):

2 C 4 H 10 + 5 O 2 → 4 CH 3 CO 2 H + 2 H 2 O
Such oxidations require metal catalyst, such as the [naphthenate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthenic_acid "Naphthenic acid")[salts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt_(chemistry) "Salt (chemistry)") of [manganese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manganese "Manganese"), [cobalt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cobalt "Cobalt"), and [chromium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromium "Chromium").

The typical reaction is conducted at [temperatures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature") and pressures designed to be as hot as possible while still keeping the butane a liquid. Typical reaction conditions are 150°C (302°F) and 55 atm.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-44) Side-products may also form, including [butanone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butanone "Butanone"), [ethyl acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethyl_acetate "Ethyl acetate"), [formic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid "Formic acid"), and [propionic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propionic_acid "Propionic acid"). These side-products are also commercially valuable, and the reaction conditions may be altered to produce more of them where needed. However, the separation of acetic acid from these by-products adds to the cost of the process.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Sano_1999-45)

Similar conditions and [catalysts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalyst "Catalyst") are used for butane oxidation, the [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") in [air](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere "Earth's atmosphere") to produce acetic acid can oxidize [acetaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde "Acetaldehyde").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Sano_1999-45)

2 CH 3 CHO + O 2 → 2 CH 3 CO 2 H
Using modern catalysts, this reaction can have an acetic acid yield greater than 95%. The major side-products are [ethyl acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethyl_acetate "Ethyl acetate"), [formic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid "Formic acid"), and [formaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde "Formaldehyde"), all of which have lower [boiling points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boiling_point "Boiling point") than acetic acid and are readily separated by [distillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distillation "Distillation").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Sano_1999-45)

Acetaldehyde may be prepared from [ethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene "Ethylene") via the [Wacker process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wacker_process "Wacker process"), and then oxidized as above.

In more recent times, chemical company [Showa Denko](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Showa_Denko "Showa Denko"), which opened an ethylene oxidation plant in [Ōita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C5%8Cita_Prefecture "Ōita Prefecture"), Japan, in 1997, commercialized a cheaper single-stage conversion of ethylene to acetic acid.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Sano_1999-45) The process is catalyzed by a [palladium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palladium "Palladium") metal catalyst supported on a [heteropoly acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heteropoly_acid "Heteropoly acid") such as [silicotungstic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicotungstic_acid "Silicotungstic acid"). A similar process uses the same metal catalyst on silicotungstic acid and silica:[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Misono-46)

C 2 H 4 + O 2 → CH 3 CO 2 H
It is thought to be competitive with methanol carbonylation for smaller plants (100–250 kt/a), depending on the local price of ethylene.

### Oxidative fermentation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetic_acid&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Oxidative fermentation")]

For most of human history, acetic acid bacteria of the genus _[Acetobacter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetobacter "Acetobacter")_ have made acetic acid, in the form of vinegar. Given sufficient oxygen, these bacteria can produce vinegar from a variety of alcoholic foodstuffs. Commonly used feeds include [apple cider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cider "Cider"), [wine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wine "Wine"), and fermented [grain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cereal "Cereal"), [malt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malt "Malt"), [rice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rice "Rice"), or [potato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potato "Potato") mashes. The overall chemical reaction facilitated by these bacteria is:

C 2 H 5 OH + O 2 → CH 3 COOH + H 2 O
A dilute alcohol solution inoculated with _Acetobacter_ and kept in a warm, airy place will become vinegar over the course of a few months. Industrial vinegar-making methods accelerate this process by improving the supply of [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") to the bacteria.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-47)

The first batches of vinegar produced by fermentation probably followed errors in the [winemaking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winemaking "Winemaking") process. If [must](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Must "Must") is fermented at too high a temperature, acetobacter will overwhelm the [yeast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yeast_(wine) "Yeast (wine)") naturally occurring on the [grapes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grapes "Grapes"). As the demand for vinegar for culinary, medical, and sanitary purposes increased, [vintners](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vintners "Vintners") quickly learned to use other organic materials to produce vinegar in the hot summer months before the grapes were ripe and ready for processing into wine. This method was slow, however, and not always successful, as the vintners did not understand the process.[_[failed verification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability "Wikipedia:Verifiability")_][[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Hromatka-48)

One of the first modern commercial processes was the "fast method" or "German method", first practised in Germany in 1823. In this process, fermentation takes place in a tower packed with wood shavings or [charcoal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charcoal "Charcoal"). The alcohol-containing feed is trickled into the top of the tower, and fresh [air](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere "Earth's atmosphere") supplied from the bottom by either natural or forced [convection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convection "Convection"). The improved air supply in this process cut the time to prepare vinegar from months to weeks.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-49)

Nowadays, most vinegar is made in submerged tank [culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbiological_culture "Microbiological culture"), first described in 1949 by Otto Hromatka and Heinrich Ebner.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-50) In this method, alcohol is fermented to vinegar in a continuously stirred tank, and oxygen is supplied by bubbling air through the solution. Using modern applications of this method, vinegar of 15% acetic acid can be prepared in only 24 hours in batch process, even 20% in 60-hour fed-batch process.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Hromatka-48)

### Anaerobic fermentation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetic_acid&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Anaerobic fermentation")]

Species of [anaerobic bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaerobic_bacteria "Anaerobic bacteria"), including members of the genus _[Clostridium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clostridium "Clostridium")_ or _[Acetobacterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetobacterium "Acetobacterium")_, can convert sugars to acetic acid directly without creating [ethanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol "Ethanol") as an intermediate. The overall chemical reaction conducted by these bacteria may be represented as:

C 6 H 12 O 6 → 3 CH 3 COOH
These [acetogenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetogen "Acetogen") bacteria produce acetic acid from one-carbon compounds, including methanol, [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide"), or a mixture of [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") and [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen"):

2 CO 2 + 4 H 2 → CH 3 COOH + 2 H 2 O
This ability of _Clostridium_ to metabolize sugars directly, or to produce acetic acid from less costly inputs, suggests that these bacteria could produce acetic acid more efficiently than ethanol-oxidizers like _Acetobacter_. However, _Clostridium_ bacteria are less acid-tolerant than _Acetobacter_. Even the most acid-tolerant _Clostridium_ strains can produce vinegar in concentrations of only a few percent, compared to _Acetobacter_ strains that can produce vinegar in concentrations up to 20%. At present, it remains more cost-effective to produce vinegar using _Acetobacter_, rather than using _Clostridium_ and concentrating it. As a result, although acetogenic bacteria have been known since 1940, their industrial use is confined to a few niche applications.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-51)

Acetic acid is a chemical [reagent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reagent "Reagent") for the production of chemical compounds. The largest single use of acetic acid is in the production of vinyl acetate [monomer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monomer "Monomer"), closely followed by acetic anhydride and ester production. The volume of acetic acid used in vinegar is comparatively small.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Ullmann-29)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-suresh-37)

### Vinyl acetate monomer

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetic_acid&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Vinyl acetate monomer")]

The primary use of acetic acid is the production of [vinyl acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinyl_acetate "Vinyl acetate") monomer (VAM). In 2008, this application was estimated to consume a third of the world's production of acetic acid.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Ullmann-29) The reaction consists of [ethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene "Ethylene") and acetic acid with [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") over a [palladium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palladium "Palladium")[catalyst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalyst "Catalyst"), conducted in the gas phase.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-vinyl-esters-52)

2 H 3 C−COOH + 2 C 2 H 4 + O 2 → 2 H 3 C−CO−O−CH=CH 2 + 2 H 2 O
Vinyl acetate can be polymerized to [polyvinyl acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyvinyl_acetate "Polyvinyl acetate") or other [polymers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymers "Polymers"), which are components in [paints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paint "Paint") and [adhesives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adhesive "Adhesive").[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-vinyl-esters-52)

The major [esters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ester "Ester") of acetic acid are commonly used as solvents for [inks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ink "Ink"), [paints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paint "Paint") and [coatings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coating "Coating"). The esters include [ethyl acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethyl_acetate "Ethyl acetate"), _n_-[butyl acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butyl_acetate "Butyl acetate"), [isobutyl acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isobutyl_acetate "Isobutyl acetate"), and [propyl acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propyl_acetate "Propyl acetate"). They are typically produced by [catalyzed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalyst "Catalyst") reaction from acetic acid and the corresponding [alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohol_(chemistry) "Alcohol (chemistry)"):

CH 3 COO−H + HO−R → CH 3 COO−R + H 2 O, R = general [alkyl group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkyl_group "Alkyl group")
For example, acetic acid and [ethanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol "Ethanol") gives [ethyl acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethyl_acetate "Ethyl acetate") and [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water "Water").

CH 3 COO−H + HO−CH 2 CH 3 → CH 3 COO−CH 2 CH 3 + H 2 O
Most acetate [esters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Esters "Esters"), however, are produced from [acetaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde "Acetaldehyde") using the [Tishchenko reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tishchenko_reaction "Tishchenko reaction"). In addition, ether acetates are used as solvents for [nitrocellulose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrocellulose "Nitrocellulose"), [acrylic lacquers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrylic_lacquer "Acrylic lacquer"), [varnish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Varnish "Varnish") removers, and wood stains. First, glycol monoethers are produced from [ethylene oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_oxide "Ethylene oxide") or [propylene oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propylene_oxide "Propylene oxide") with alcohol, which are then esterified with acetic acid. The three major products are ethylene glycol monoethyl ether acetate (EEA), ethylene glycol monobutyl ether acetate (EBA), and propylene glycol monomethyl ether acetate (PMA, more commonly known as PGMEA in semiconductor manufacturing processes, where it is used as a resist solvent). This application consumes about 15% to 20% of worldwide acetic acid. Ether acetates, for example EEA, have been shown to be harmful to human reproduction.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-suresh-37)

The product of the [condensation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensation_reaction "Condensation reaction") of two molecules of acetic acid is [acetic anhydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_anhydride "Acetic anhydride"). The worldwide production of acetic anhydride is a major application, and uses approximately 25% to 30% of the global production of acetic acid. The main process involves dehydration of acetic acid to give [ketene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethenone "Ethenone") at 700–750°C. Ketene is thereafter reacted with acetic acid to obtain the anhydride:[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-acetic-anh-53)

CH 3 CO 2 H → CH 2=C=O + H 2 O CH 3 CO 2 H + CH 2=C=O → (CH 3 CO)2 O
Acetic anhydride is an [acetylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylation "Acetylation") agent. As such, its major application is for [cellulose acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellulose_acetate "Cellulose acetate"), a synthetic [textile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Textile "Textile") also used for [photographic film](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photographic_film "Photographic film"), which is produced by reacting cellulose with acetic acid and acetic anhydride in the presence of sulfuric acid.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Myers-9) Acetic anhydride is also a reagent for the production of [heroin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heroin "Heroin") and other compounds.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-acetic-anh-53)

As a polar [protic solvent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protic_solvent "Protic solvent"), acetic acid is frequently used for [recrystallization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recrystallization_(chemistry) "Recrystallization (chemistry)") to purify organic compounds. Acetic acid is used as a [solvent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvent "Solvent") in the production of [terephthalic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terephthalic_acid "Terephthalic acid") (TPA), the raw material for [polyethylene terephthalate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyethylene_terephthalate "Polyethylene terephthalate") (PET). In 2006, about 20% of acetic acid was used for TPA production.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-suresh-37)

Acetic acid is often used as a solvent for reactions involving [carbocations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbocation "Carbocation"), such as [Friedel-Crafts alkylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedel-Crafts#Friedel%E2%80%93Crafts_alkylation "Friedel-Crafts"). For example, one stage in the commercial manufacture of synthetic [camphor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camphor "Camphor") involves a [Wagner-Meerwein rearrangement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wagner-Meerwein_rearrangement "Wagner-Meerwein rearrangement") of [camphene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camphene "Camphene") to [isobornyl acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isobornyl_acetate "Isobornyl acetate"); here acetic acid acts both as a solvent and as a [nucleophile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleophile "Nucleophile") to trap the [rearranged](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rearrangement_reaction "Rearrangement reaction") carbocation.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-sell-54)

Glacial acetic acid is used in analytical chemistry for the estimation of weakly alkaline substances such as organic amides. Glacial acetic acid is a much weaker [base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_(chemistry) "Base (chemistry)") than water, so the amide behaves as a strong base in this medium. It then can be titrated using a solution in glacial acetic acid of a very strong acid, such as [perchloric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perchloric_acid "Perchloric acid").[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Felgner-55)

Acetic acid injection into a tumor has been used to treat cancer since the 1800s.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-56)[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-57)

Acetic acid is used as part of [cervical cancer screening](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cervical_cancer_screening "Cervical cancer screening") in many areas in the [developing world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developing_world "Developing world").[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Fok2015-58) The acid is applied to the [cervix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cervix "Cervix") and if an area of white appears after about a minute the test is positive.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Fok2015-58)

Acetic acid is an effective antiseptic when used as a 1% solution, with broad spectrum of activity against streptococci, staphylococci, pseudomonas, enterococci and others.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-59)[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-60)[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-61) It may be used to treat skin infections caused by pseudomonas strains resistant to typical antibiotics.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-62)

While diluted acetic acid is used in [iontophoresis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iontophoresis "Iontophoresis"), no high quality evidence supports this treatment for rotator cuff disease.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-63)[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-64)

As a treatment for [otitis externa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otitis_externa "Otitis externa"), it is on the [World Health Organization's List of Essential Medicines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WHO_Model_List_of_Essential_Medicines "WHO Model List of Essential Medicines").[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-WHO23rd-65)

Acetic acid has 349 kcal (1,460 kJ) per 100 g.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-FAOSouthgate-66) Vinegar is typically no less than 4% acetic acid by mass.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-67)[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-68)[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-69) Legal limits on acetic acid content vary by jurisdiction. Vinegar is used directly as a [condiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condiment "Condiment"), and in the [pickling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pickling "Pickling") of vegetables and other foods. Table vinegar tends to be more diluted (4% to 8% acetic acid), while commercial food pickling employs solutions that are more concentrated. The proportion of acetic acid used worldwide as vinegar is not as large as industrial uses, but it is by far the oldest and best-known application.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-70)

[![Image 36](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Ethanol_Skelett.svg/120px-Ethanol_Skelett.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ethanol_Skelett.svg)

Two typical organic reactions of acetic acid

Acetic acid undergoes the typical [chemical reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_reaction "Chemical reaction") of a carboxylic acid. Upon treatment with a standard base, it converts to metal [acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetate "Acetate") and [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water "Water"). With strong bases (e.g., organolithium reagents), it can be doubly deprotonated to give LiCH 2 COOLi. Reduction of acetic acid gives ethanol. The OH group is the main site of reaction, as illustrated by the conversion of acetic acid to [acetyl chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetyl_chloride "Acetyl chloride"). Other substitution derivatives include [acetic anhydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_anhydride "Acetic anhydride"); this [anhydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anhydride "Anhydride") is produced by [loss of water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensation_reaction "Condensation reaction") from two molecules of acetic acid. [Esters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ester "Ester") of acetic acid can likewise be formed via [Fischer esterification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fischer_esterification "Fischer esterification"), and [amides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amide "Amide") can be formed. When heated above 440°C (824°F), acetic acid decomposes to produce [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") and [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane"), or to produce [ketene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketene "Ketene") and water:[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-71)[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-72)[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Duan1995-73)

CH 3 COOH → CH 4 + CO 2 CH 3 COOH → CH 2=C=O + H 2 O

### Reactions with inorganic compounds

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acetic_acid&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Reactions with inorganic compounds")]

Acetic acid is mildly [corrosive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corrosion "Corrosion") to [metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal "Metal") including [iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron "Iron"), [magnesium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnesium "Magnesium"), and [zinc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zinc "Zinc"), forming [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") gas and salts called [acetates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetate "Acetate"):

Mg + 2 CH 3 COOH → (CH 3 COO)2 Mg + H 2
Because [aluminium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium "Aluminium") forms a [passivating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Passivation_(chemistry) "Passivation (chemistry)") acid-resistant film of [aluminium oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium_oxide "Aluminium oxide"), aluminium tanks are used to transport acetic acid.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-74) Containers lined with glass, [stainless steel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stainless_steel "Stainless steel") or [polyethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyethylene "Polyethylene") are also used for this purpose.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Ullmann-29) Metal acetates can also be prepared from acetic acid and an appropriate [base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_(chemistry) "Base (chemistry)"), as in the popular "[baking soda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_bicarbonate "Sodium bicarbonate") + vinegar" reaction giving off [sodium acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_acetate "Sodium acetate"):

NaHCO 3 + CH 3 COOH → CH 3 COONa + CO 2 + H 2 O
A [colour reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colour_reaction "Colour reaction") for salts of acetic acid is [iron(III) chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron(III)_chloride "Iron(III) chloride") solution, which results in a deeply red colour that disappears after acidification.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Charlot-75) A more sensitive test uses [lanthanum nitrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lanthanum_nitrate "Lanthanum nitrate") with iodine and ammonia to give a blue solution.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Neelakantam-76) Acetates when heated with [arsenic trioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arsenic_trioxide "Arsenic trioxide") form [cacodyl oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cacodyl_oxide "Cacodyl oxide"), which can be detected by its [malodorous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odour "Odour") vapours.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-Brantley1947-77)

Organic or inorganic salts are produced from acetic acid. Some commercially significant derivatives:

*   [Sodium acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_acetate "Sodium acetate"), used in the [textile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Textile "Textile") industry and as a food [preservative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preservative "Preservative") ([E262](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E_number "E number")).
*   [Copper(II) acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper(II)_acetate "Copper(II) acetate"), used as a [pigment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pigment "Pigment") and a [fungicide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungicide "Fungicide").
*   [Aluminium acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium_acetate "Aluminium acetate") and [iron(II) acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron(II)_acetate "Iron(II) acetate")—used as [mordants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mordant "Mordant") for [dyes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dye "Dye").
*   [Palladium(II) acetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palladium(II)_acetate "Palladium(II) acetate"), used as a catalyst for organic coupling reactions such as the [Heck reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heck_reaction "Heck reaction").

Halogenated acetic acids are produced from acetic acid. Some commercially significant derivatives:

*   [Chloroacetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloroacetic_acid "Chloroacetic acid") (monochloroacetic acid, MCA), dichloroacetic acid (considered a by-product), and [trichloroacetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trichloroacetic_acid "Trichloroacetic acid"). MCA is used in the manufacture of [indigo dye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indigo_dye "Indigo dye").
*   [Bromoacetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bromoacetic_acid "Bromoacetic acid"), which is esterified to produce the reagent [ethyl bromoacetate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethyl_bromoacetate "Ethyl bromoacetate").
*   [Trifluoroacetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trifluoroacetic_acid "Trifluoroacetic acid"), which is a common reagent in [organic synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_synthesis "Organic synthesis").

Amounts of acetic acid used in these other applications together account for another 5–10% of acetic acid use worldwide.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-suresh-37)

Prolonged inhalation exposure (eight hours) to acetic acid vapours at 10 ppm can produce some irritation of eyes, nose, and throat; at 100 ppm marked lung irritation and possible damage to lungs, eyes, and skin may result. Vapour concentrations of 1,000 ppm cause marked irritation of eyes, nose and upper respiratory tract and cannot be tolerated. These predictions were based on [animal experiments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_testing "Animal testing") and industrial exposure.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-CDC-78)

In 12 workers exposed for two or more years to an airborne average concentration of 51 ppm acetic acid (estimated), symptoms of conjunctive irritation, upper respiratory tract irritation, and hyperkeratotic dermatitis were produced. Exposure to 50 ppm or more is intolerable to most persons and results in intensive [lacrimation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tears "Tears") and irritation of the eyes, nose, and throat, with pharyngeal oedema and chronic bronchitis. Unacclimatized humans experience extreme eye and nasal irritation at concentrations in excess of 25 ppm, and conjunctivitis from concentrations below 10 ppm has been reported. In a study of five workers exposed for seven to 12 years to concentrations of 80 to 200 ppm at peaks, the principal findings were blackening and hyperkeratosis of the skin of the hands, conjunctivitis (but no corneal damage), bronchitis and pharyngitis, and erosion of the exposed teeth (incisors and canines).[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-:0-79)

Concentrated acetic acid (≥ 25%) is [corrosive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corrosion "Corrosion") to skin.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-IPCS_card-80) These burns or blisters may not appear until hours after exposure.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid#cite_note-81) The hazardous properties of acetic acid are dependent on the concentration of the (typically [aqueous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aqueous_solution "Aqueous solution")) solution, with the most significant increases in hazard levels at thresholds of 25% and 90% acetic acid concentration by weight. Concentrated acetic acid can be ignited only with difficulty at standard temperature and pressure, but becomes a flammable risk in temperatures greater than 39°C (102°F), and can form explosive mixtures with air at higher temperatures with [explosive limits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explosive_limit "Explosive limit") of 5.4–16% concentration.
