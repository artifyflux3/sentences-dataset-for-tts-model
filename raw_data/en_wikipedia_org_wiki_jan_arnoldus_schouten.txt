**Jan Arnoldus Schouten** (28 August 1883 – 20 January 1971) was a Dutch [mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician") and Professor at the [Delft University of Technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delft_University_of_Technology "Delft University of Technology"). He was an important contributor to the development of [tensor calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_calculus "Tensor calculus") and [Ricci calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_calculus "Ricci calculus"), and was one of the founders of the [Mathematisch Centrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centrum_Wiskunde_%26_Informatica "Centrum Wiskunde & Informatica") in [Amsterdam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amsterdam "Amsterdam").

Schouten was born in [Nieuwer-Amstel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nieuwer-Amstel "Nieuwer-Amstel") to a family of eminent shipping magnates. He attended a [Hogere Burger School](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hogere_Burger_School_(Haarlem) "Hogere Burger School (Haarlem)"), and later he took up studies in [electrical engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_engineering "Electrical engineering") at the [Delft Polytechnical School](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delft_University_of_Technology "Delft University of Technology"). After graduating in 1908, he worked for [Siemens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siemens "Siemens") in [Berlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin "Berlin") and for a public utility in [Rotterdam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotterdam "Rotterdam") before returning to study mathematics in Delft in 1912. During his study he had become fascinated by the power and subtleties of [vector analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus "Vector calculus"). After a short while in industry, he returned to Delft to study Mathematics, where he received his [Ph.D.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ph.D. "Ph.D.") degree in 1914 under supervision of Jacob Cardinaal with a thesis entitled _Grundlagen der Vektor- und Affinoranalysis_.

Schouten was an effective university administrator and leader of mathematical societies. During his tenure as professor and as institute head he was involved in various controversies with the topologist and [intuitionist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intuitionist "Intuitionist") mathematician [L. E. J. Brouwer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L._E._J._Brouwer "L. E. J. Brouwer"). He was a shrewd investor as well as mathematician and successfully managed the budget of the institute and Dutch mathematical society. He hosted the [International Congress of Mathematicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Congress_of_Mathematicians "International Congress of Mathematicians") in Amsterdam in early 1954, and gave the opening address. Schouten was one of the founders of the [Mathematisch Centrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centrum_Wiskunde_%26_Informatica "Centrum Wiskunde & Informatica") in [Amsterdam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amsterdam "Amsterdam").

Among his PhD candidates students were Johanna Manders (1919), [Dirk Struik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirk_Struik "Dirk Struik") (1922), Johannes Haantjes (1933), Wouter van der Kulk (1945), and [Albert Nijenhuis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Nijenhuis "Albert Nijenhuis") (1952).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_Arnoldus_Schouten#cite_note-1)

In 1933 Schouten became member of the [Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Netherlands_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences "Royal Netherlands Academy of Arts and Sciences").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_Arnoldus_Schouten#cite_note-2)

Schouten died in 1971 in [Epe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epe,_Netherlands "Epe, Netherlands"). His son [Jan Frederik Schouten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_Frederik_Schouten "Jan Frederik Schouten") (1910-1980) was Professor at the Eindhoven University of Technology from 1958 to 1978.

### _Grundlagen der Vektor- und Affinoranalysis_

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Jan_Arnoldus_Schouten&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Grundlagen der Vektor- und Affinoranalysis")]

Schouten's dissertation applied his "direct analysis", modeled on the vector analysis of [Josiah Willard Gibbs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Josiah_Willard_Gibbs "Josiah Willard Gibbs") and [Oliver Heaviside](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oliver_Heaviside "Oliver Heaviside"), to higher order tensor-like entities he called **affinors**. The symmetrical subset of affinors were tensors in the physicists' sense of [Woldemar Voigt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Woldemar_Voigt "Woldemar Voigt").

Entities such as **[axiators](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Axiators&action=edit&redlink=1 "Axiators (page does not exist)")**, **perversors**, and **deviators** appear in this analysis. Just as vector analysis has [dot products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") and [cross products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product"), so affinor analysis has different kinds of products for tensors of various levels. However, instead of two kinds of multiplication symbols, Schouten had at least twenty. This made the work a chore to read, although the conclusions were valid.

Schouten later said in conversation with [Hermann Weyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Weyl "Hermann Weyl") that he would "like to throttle the man who wrote this book." (Karin Reich, in her history of tensor analysis, misattributes this quote to Weyl.) Weyl did, however, say that Schouten's early book has "orgies of formalism that threaten the peace of even the technical scientist." (_Space, Time, Matter_, p.54). [Roland Weitzenböck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roland_Weitzenb%C3%B6ck "Roland Weitzenböck") wrote of "the terrible book he has committed."

### Levi-Civita connection

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Jan_Arnoldus_Schouten&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Levi-Civita connection")]

In 1906, [L. E. J. Brouwer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L._E._J._Brouwer "L. E. J. Brouwer") was the first [mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician") to consider the [parallel transport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_transport "Parallel transport") of a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(mathematics_and_physics) "Vector (mathematics and physics)") for the case of a space of [constant curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_curvature "Constant curvature").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_Arnoldus_Schouten#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_Arnoldus_Schouten#cite_note-4) In 1917, [Tullio Levi-Civita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita "Tullio Levi-Civita") pointed out its importance for the case of a [hypersurface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersurface "Hypersurface") immersed in a [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), i.e., for the case of a [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold") immersed in a "larger" ambient space.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_Arnoldus_Schouten#cite_note-5) In 1918, independently of Levi-Civita, Schouten obtained analogous results.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_Arnoldus_Schouten#cite_note-6) In the same year, [Hermann Weyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Weyl "Hermann Weyl") generalized Levi-Civita's results.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_Arnoldus_Schouten#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_Arnoldus_Schouten#cite_note-8) Schouten's derivation is generalized to many dimensions rather than just two, and Schouten's proofs are completely intrinsic rather than extrinsic, unlike [Tullio Levi-Civita](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tullio_Levi-Civita "Tullio Levi-Civita")'s. Despite this, since Schouten's article appeared almost a year after Levi-Civita's, the latter got the credit. Schouten was unaware of Levi-Civita's work because of poor journal distribution and communication during [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I"). Schouten engaged in a losing priority dispute with Levi-Civita. Schouten's colleague [L. E. J. Brouwer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luitzen_Egbertus_Jan_Brouwer "Luitzen Egbertus Jan Brouwer") took sides against Schouten. Once Schouten became aware of [Ricci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorio_Ricci-Curbastro "Gregorio Ricci-Curbastro")'s and Levi-Civita's work, he embraced their simpler and more widely accepted notation. With [David van Dantzig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_van_Dantzig "David van Dantzig"), Schouten also developed what is now known as a [Kähler manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%A4hler_manifold "Kähler manifold") two years before [Erich Kähler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erich_K%C3%A4hler "Erich Kähler").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_Arnoldus_Schouten#cite_note-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jan_Arnoldus_Schouten#cite_note-10) Again he did not receive full recognition for this discovery.

Schouten's name appears in various mathematical entities and theorems, such as the [Schouten tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schouten_tensor "Schouten tensor"), the [Schouten bracket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schouten_bracket "Schouten bracket") and the [Weyl–Schouten theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weyl%E2%80%93Schouten_theorem "Weyl–Schouten theorem").

He wrote _Der Ricci-Kalkül_ in 1922 surveying the field of tensor analysis.

In 1931 he wrote a treatise on [tensors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") and [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"). The second volume, on applications to differential geometry, was authored by his student [Dirk Jan Struik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirk_Jan_Struik "Dirk Jan Struik").

Schouten collaborated with [Élie Cartan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89lie_Cartan "Élie Cartan") on two articles as well as with many other eminent mathematicians such as [Kentaro Yano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kentaro_Yano_(mathematician) "Kentaro Yano (mathematician)") (with whom he co-authored three papers). Through his student and co-author Dirk Struik his work influenced many mathematicians in the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States").

In the 1950s Schouten completely rewrote and updated the German version of _Ricci-Kalkül_ and this was translated into English as _Ricci Calculus_. This covers everything that Schouten considered of value in tensor analysis. This included work on [Lie groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group") and other topics and that had been much developed since the first edition.

Later Schouten wrote _Tensor Analysis for Physicists_ attempting to present the subtleties of various aspects of tensor calculus for mathematically inclined physicists. It included [Paul Dirac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Dirac "Paul Dirac")'s matrix calculus. He still used part of his earlier affinor terminology.

Schouten, like Weyl and Cartan, was stimulated by [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein")'s theory of [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"). He co-authored a paper with [Alexander Aleksandrovich Friedmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Aleksandrovich_Friedmann "Alexander Aleksandrovich Friedmann") of Petersburg and another with [Václav Hlavatý](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V%C3%A1clav_Hlavat%C3%BD "Václav Hlavatý"). He interacted with [Oswald Veblen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oswald_Veblen "Oswald Veblen") of [Princeton University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Princeton_University "Princeton University"), and corresponded with [Wolfgang Pauli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Pauli "Wolfgang Pauli") on spin space. (See H. Goenner, Living Review link below.)
