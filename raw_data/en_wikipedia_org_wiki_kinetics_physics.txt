In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") and [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering"), **kinetics** is the branch of [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics") that is concerned with the relationship between the [motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion "Motion") and its causes, specifically, [forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force") and [torques](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetics_(physics)#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetics_(physics)#cite_note-Martin-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetics_(physics)#cite_note-3) Since the mid-20th century, the term "[dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamics_(mechanics) "Dynamics (mechanics)")" (or "[analytical dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_dynamics "Analytical dynamics")") has largely superseded "kinetics" in physics textbooks,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetics_(physics)#cite_note-4) though the term is still used in engineering.

In [plasma physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)"), kinetics refers to the study of [continua](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics "Continuum mechanics") in [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") space. This is usually in the context of non-thermal ([non-Maxwellian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%E2%80%93Boltzmann_distribution#Physical_applications "Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution")) velocity distributions, or processes that [perturb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perturbation_theory_(quantum_mechanics) "Perturbation theory (quantum mechanics)") thermal distributions. These "[kinetic plasmas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics)#Kinetic_model "Plasma (physics)")" cannot be adequately described with [fluid equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamics "Magnetohydrodynamics").

The term _kinetics_ is also used to refer to [chemical kinetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_kinetics "Chemical kinetics"), particularly in [chemical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_physics "Chemical physics") and [physical chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_chemistry "Physical chemistry").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetics_(physics)#cite_note-physical-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetics_(physics)#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetics_(physics)#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetics_(physics)#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetics_(physics)#cite_note-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetics_(physics)#cite_note-Alvarengahd-10) In such uses, a qualifier is often used or implied, for example: "physical kinetics", "crystal growth kinetics", and so on.
