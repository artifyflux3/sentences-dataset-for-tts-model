From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

**Linear motion**, also called **rectilinear motion**,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_motion#cite_note-1) is one-dimensional [motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion_(physics) "Motion (physics)") along a [straight line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_(mathematics) "Line (mathematics)"), and can therefore be described mathematically using only one spatial [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension"). The linear motion can be of two types: **uniform linear motion**, with constant [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") (zero [acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration")); and **non-uniform linear motion**, with variable velocity (non-zero acceleration). The motion of a [particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_particle "Point particle") (a point-like object) along a line can be described by its position ![Image 1: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4), which [varies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time-variant_system "Time-variant system") with ![Image 2: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560) (time). An example of linear motion is an athlete running a [100-meter dash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/100-meter_dash "100-meter dash") along a straight track.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_motion#cite_note-auto-2)

Linear motion is the most basic of all motion. According to [Newton's first law of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_first_law_of_motion "Newton's first law of motion"), objects that do not experience any [net force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_force "Net force") will continue to move in a straight line with a constant velocity until they are subjected to a net force. Under everyday circumstances, external forces such as [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity") and [friction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friction "Friction") can cause an object to change the direction of its motion, so that its motion cannot be described as linear.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_motion#cite_note-3)

One may compare linear motion to general motion. In general motion, a particle's position and velocity are described by [vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(geometric) "Vector (geometric)"), which have a magnitude and direction. In linear motion, the directions of all the vectors describing the system are equal and constant which means the objects move along the same axis and do not change direction. The analysis of such systems may therefore be simplified by neglecting the direction components of the vectors involved and dealing only with the [magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitude_(mathematics) "Magnitude (mathematics)").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_motion#cite_note-auto-2)

The motion in which all the particles of a body move through the same distance in the same time is called translatory motion. There are two types of translatory motions: rectilinear motion; [curvilinear motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_motion "Curvilinear motion"). Since linear motion is a motion in a single dimension, the [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance "Distance") traveled by an object in particular direction is the same as [displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Displacement_(vector) "Displacement (vector)").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_motion#cite_note-4) The [SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI "SI") unit of displacement is the [metre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre "Metre").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_motion#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_motion#cite_note-auto1-6) If ![Image 3: {\displaystyle x_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a8788bf85d532fa88d1fb25eff6ae382a601c308) is the initial position of an object and ![Image 4: {\displaystyle x_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d7af1b928f06e4c7e3e8ebfd60704656719bd766) is the final position, then mathematically the displacement is given by: ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \Delta x=x_{2}-x_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a73839bf6f41b9940a2e657f9cad8820f2adf61)

The equivalent of displacement in [rotational motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_motion "Rotational motion") is the [angular displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_displacement "Angular displacement")![Image 6: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) measured in [radians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian"). The displacement of an object cannot be greater than the distance because it is also a distance but the shortest one. Consider a person travelling to work daily. Overall displacement when he returns home is zero, since the person ends up back where he started, but the distance travelled is clearly not zero.

Velocity refers to a displacement in one direction with respect to an interval of time. It is defined as the rate of change of displacement over change in time.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_motion#cite_note-7) Velocity is a vector quantity, representing a direction and a magnitude of movement. The magnitude of a velocity is called speed. The SI unit of speed is ![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\text{m}}\cdot {\text{s}}^{-1},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f94e863aed1b458caa4f7c4c34d41354d69b5e56) that is [metre per second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre_per_second "Metre per second").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_motion#cite_note-auto1-6)

The [average velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Average_velocity "Average velocity") of a moving body is its total displacement divided by the total time needed to travel from the initial point to the final point. It is an estimated velocity for a distance to travel. Mathematically, it is given by:[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_motion#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_motion#cite_note-9)

![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{\text{avg}}={\frac {\Delta \mathbf {x} }{\Delta t}}={\frac {\mathbf {x} _{2}-\mathbf {x} _{1}}{t_{2}-t_{1}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e93d446ec2596e7cd204eab72e38bc98f5d13dc6)

where:

The magnitude of the average velocity ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \left|\mathbf {v} _{\text{avg}}\right|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bfa6631f10b4110d636a2e2e7b3df5bc2506e24f) is called an average speed.

#### Instantaneous velocity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Linear_motion&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Instantaneous velocity")]

In contrast to an average velocity, referring to the overall motion in a finite time interval, the [instantaneous velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instantaneous_velocity "Instantaneous velocity") of an object describes the state of motion at a specific point in time. It is defined by letting the length of the time interval ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \Delta t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c28867ecd34e2caed12cf38feadf6a81a7ee542) tend to zero, that is, the velocity is the time derivative of the displacement as a function of time.

![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} =\lim _{\Delta t\to 0}{\frac {\Delta \mathbf {x} }{\Delta t}}={\frac {d\mathbf {x} }{dt}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/63eee5e2a93bfc6a273d543a62e7e00d566ad092)

The magnitude of the instantaneous velocity ![Image 12: {\displaystyle |\mathbf {v} |}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16a779e65de92152d395f5576ce1001c8e56d7f6) is called the instantaneous speed. The instantaneous velocity equation comes from finding the limit as t approaches 0 of the average velocity. The instantaneous velocity shows the position function with respect to time. From the instantaneous velocity the instantaneous speed can be derived by getting the magnitude of the instantaneous velocity.

Acceleration is defined as the rate of change of velocity with respect to time. Acceleration is the second derivative of displacement i.e. acceleration can be found by differentiating position with respect to time twice or differentiating velocity with respect to time once.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_motion#cite_note-10) The SI unit of acceleration is ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \mathrm {m\cdot s^{-2}} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dfb24ef0fb8a75d7373cfc3961fb6504a4d56bca) or [metre per second squared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre_per_second_squared "Metre per second squared").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_motion#cite_note-auto1-6)

If ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} _{\text{avg}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87481d58f869cf3d9e060d44e4565aee78751a4f) is the average acceleration and ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \Delta \mathbf {v} =\mathbf {v} _{2}-\mathbf {v} _{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81207385df6bbca848da9e618cd51050c1566c1b) is the change in velocity over the time interval ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \Delta t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c28867ecd34e2caed12cf38feadf6a81a7ee542) then mathematically, ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} _{\text{avg}}={\frac {\Delta \mathbf {v} }{\Delta t}}={\frac {\mathbf {v} _{2}-\mathbf {v} _{1}}{t_{2}-t_{1}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fe6c2d559e8dbaede90b8cb137a700fe3731033)

The instantaneous acceleration is the limit, as ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \Delta t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c28867ecd34e2caed12cf38feadf6a81a7ee542) approaches zero, of the ratio ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \Delta \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ae2621f980eefffc0a3f3e806bfce57a5be867a) and ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \Delta t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c28867ecd34e2caed12cf38feadf6a81a7ee542), i.e., ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} =\lim _{\Delta t\to 0}{\frac {\Delta \mathbf {v} }{\Delta t}}={\frac {d\mathbf {v} }{dt}}={\frac {d^{2}\mathbf {x} }{dt^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5dfa60403586b31b8fbddb9c9e235a6a31b91431)

The rate of change of acceleration, the third derivative of displacement is known as jerk.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_motion#cite_note-auto2-11) The SI unit of jerk is ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathrm {m\cdot s^{-3}} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d27fe303b9b8e4893c120ae831f851371a94cf2). In the UK jerk is also referred to as jolt.

The rate of change of jerk, the fourth derivative of displacement is known as jounce.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_motion#cite_note-auto2-11) The SI unit of jounce is ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \mathrm {m\cdot s^{-4}} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4f9a349b6632f886b8f7320af01f993e7f17890) which can be pronounced as _metres per quartic second_.

In case of constant acceleration, the four [physical quantities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_quantities "Physical quantities") acceleration, velocity, time and displacement can be related by using the [equations of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equations_of_motion "Equations of motion").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_motion#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_motion#cite_note-13)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_motion#cite_note-14)

![Image 24: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{\text{f}}=\mathbf {v} _{\text{i}}+\mathbf {a} t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c51f2cb4f09000e4159ec694cefaff838adbb50a)![Image 25: {\displaystyle \mathbf {d} =\mathbf {v} _{\text{i}}t+{\frac {1}{2}}\mathbf {a} t^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/49e40eb6c5ea11cbad95b9c3394dbffc2806743b)![Image 26: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{\text{f}}^{2}=\mathbf {v} _{\text{i}}^{2}+2\mathbf {ad} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe6df5ce4ae669c8320e32a1d3fff45dbde4f0f8)![Image 27: {\displaystyle \mathbf {d} ={\frac {t}{2}}\left(\mathbf {v} _{\text{f}}+\mathbf {v} _{\text{i}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/003ac791f1f1b7c96b99fd113a1630c1ad6585be)
Here,

*   ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{\text{i}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68979aafacdb16a1bc146ed5051a809304a7d379) is the initial velocity
*   ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{\text{f}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/906af2d66ede429b258a88e9ab7ff9e74a49c135) is the final velocity
*   ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a957216653a9ee0d0133dcefd13fb75e36b8b9d) is acceleration
*   ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \mathbf {d} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8ec3b626fc045b6ff579316e29978fccfed884c2) is displacement
*   ![Image 32: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560) is time

These relationships can be demonstrated graphically. The [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slope "Slope") of a line on a displacement time graph represents the velocity. The gradient of the velocity time graph gives the acceleration while the area under the velocity time graph gives the displacement. The area under a graph of acceleration versus time is equal to the change in velocity.
