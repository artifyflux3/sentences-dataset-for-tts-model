[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Photoelectric_effect_in_a_solid_-_diagram.svg/250px-Photoelectric_effect_in_a_solid_-_diagram.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Photoelectric_effect_in_a_solid_-_diagram.svg)

Photoemission of [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") from a metal plate accompanied by the absorption of light quanta – [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon")

The **photoelectric effect** is the emission of [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") from a material caused by [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation") such as [ultraviolet light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet_light "Ultraviolet light"). Electrons emitted in this manner are called photoelectrons. The phenomenon is studied in [condensed matter physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics "Condensed matter physics"), [solid state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid-state_chemistry "Solid-state chemistry"), and [quantum chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_chemistry "Quantum chemistry") to draw inferences about the properties of atoms, molecules and solids. The effect has found use in [electronic devices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronics "Electronics") specialized for light detection and precisely timed electron emission.

The experimental results disagree with [classical electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_electromagnetism "Classical electromagnetism"), which predicts that continuous light waves transfer [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy") to electrons, which would then be emitted when they accumulate enough energy. An alteration in the [intensity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intensity_(physics) "Intensity (physics)") of light would theoretically change the [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy") of the emitted electrons, with sufficiently dim light resulting in a delayed emission. The experimental results instead show that electrons are dislodged only when the light exceeds a certain [frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency")—regardless of the light's intensity or duration of exposure. Because a low-frequency beam at a high intensity does not build up the energy required to produce photoelectrons, as would be the case if light's energy accumulated over time from a continuous wave, [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") proposed that a beam of light is not [a wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light#Wave_theory "Light") propagating through space, but discrete energy packets, which were later popularised as [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") by [Gilbert N. Lewis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gilbert_N._Lewis "Gilbert N. Lewis") since he coined the term 'photon' in his letter "The Conservation of Photons" to Nature published in 18 December 1926.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-2)

Emission of conduction electrons from typical [metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal "Metal") requires a few [electron-volt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt "Electronvolt") (eV) light quanta, corresponding to short-wavelength visible or ultraviolet light. In extreme cases, emissions are induced with photons approaching zero energy, like in systems with negative electron affinity and the emission from excited states, or a few hundred keV photons for [core electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Core_electron "Core electron") in [elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element") with a high [atomic number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_number "Atomic number").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-3) Study of the photoelectric effect led to important steps in understanding the quantum nature of light and electrons and influenced the formation of the concept of [wave–particle duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave%E2%80%93particle_duality "Wave–particle duality").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-serway_1-4) Other phenomena where light affects the movement of electric charges include the [photoconductive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoconductivity "Photoconductivity") effect, the [photovoltaic effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photovoltaic_effect "Photovoltaic effect"), and the [photoelectrochemical effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectrochemical_cell "Photoelectrochemical cell").

The photons of a light beam have a characteristic energy, called [photon energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon_energy "Photon energy"), which is proportional to the frequency of the light. In the photoemission process, when an electron within some material absorbs the energy of a photon and acquires more energy than its [binding energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_binding_energy "Electron binding energy"), it is likely to be ejected. If the photon energy is too low, the electron is unable to escape the material. Since an increase in the intensity of low-frequency light will only increase the number of low-energy photons, this change in intensity will not create any single photon with enough energy to dislodge an electron. Moreover, the energy of the emitted electrons will not depend on the intensity of the incoming light of a given frequency, but only on the energy of the individual photons.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-5)

While free electrons can absorb any energy when [irradiated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irradiated "Irradiated") as long as this is followed by an immediate re-emission, like in the [Compton effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compton_scattering "Compton scattering"), in quantum systems all of the energy from one photon is absorbed—if the process is allowed by [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Introduction_to_quantum_mechanics "Introduction to quantum mechanics")—or none at all. Part of the acquired energy is used to liberate the electron from its atomic binding, and the rest contributes to the electron's [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy") as a free particle.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_Lenard-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_Millikan-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-8) Because electrons in a material occupy many different quantum states with different binding energies, and because they can sustain energy losses on their way out of the material, the emitted electrons will have a range of kinetic energies. The electrons from the highest occupied states will have the highest kinetic energy. In metals, those electrons will be emitted from the [Fermi level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi_level "Fermi level").

When the photoelectron is emitted into a solid rather than into a vacuum, the term _internal photoemission_ is often used, and emission into a vacuum is distinguished as _external photoemission_.

### Experimental observation of photoelectric emission

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photoelectric_effect&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Experimental observation of photoelectric emission")]

Even though photoemission can occur from any material, it is most readily observed from metals and other conductors. This is because the process produces a charge imbalance which, if not neutralized by current flow, results in the increasing potential barrier until the emission completely ceases. The energy barrier to photoemission is usually increased by nonconductive oxide layers on metal surfaces, so most practical experiments and devices based on the photoelectric effect use clean metal surfaces in evacuated tubes. Vacuum also helps observing the electrons since it prevents gases from impeding their flow between the electrodes.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Sunlight is an inconsistent and variable source of ultraviolet light. Cloud cover, ozone concentration, altitude, and surface reflection all alter the amount of UV. Laboratory sources of UV are based on [xenon arc lamps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xenon_arc_lamps "Xenon arc lamps") or, for more uniform but weaker light, [fluorescent lamps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorescent_lamps "Fluorescent lamps").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-9) More specialized sources include [ultraviolet lasers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser "Laser")[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-10) and [synchrotron radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synchrotron_radiation "Synchrotron radiation").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-11)

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Photoelectric_effect_measurement_apparatus_-_microscopic_picture.svg/250px-Photoelectric_effect_measurement_apparatus_-_microscopic_picture.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Photoelectric_effect_measurement_apparatus_-_microscopic_picture.svg)

Schematic of the experiment to demonstrate the photoelectric effect. Filtered, monochromatic light of a certain wavelength strikes the emitting electrode (E) inside a vacuum tube. The collector electrode (C) is biased to a voltage V C that can be set to attract the emitted electrons, when positive, or prevent any of them from reaching the collector when negative.

The classical setup to observe the photoelectric effect includes a light source, a set of filters to [monochromatize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monochromator "Monochromator") the light, a [vacuum tube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_tube "Vacuum tube") transparent to ultraviolet light, an emitting electrode (E) exposed to the light, and a collector (C) whose voltage _V_ C can be externally controlled.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

A positive external voltage is used to direct the photoemitted electrons onto the collector. If the frequency and the intensity of the incident radiation are fixed, the photoelectric current _I_ increases with an increase in the positive voltage, as more and more electrons are directed onto the electrode. When no additional photoelectrons can be collected, the photoelectric current attains a saturation value. This current can only increase with the increase of the intensity of light.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

An increasing negative voltage prevents all but the highest-energy electrons from reaching the collector. When no current is observed through the tube, the negative voltage has reached the value that is high enough to slow down and stop the most energetic photoelectrons of kinetic energy _K_ max. This value of the retarding voltage is called the _stopping potential_ or _cut off_ potential _V_ o.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-12) Since the work done by the retarding potential in stopping the electron of charge _e_ is _eV_ o, the following must hold _eV_ o=_K_ max.

The current-voltage curve is sigmoidal, but its exact shape depends on the experimental geometry and the electrode material properties.

For a given metal surface, there exists a certain minimum frequency of incident [radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation "Radiation") below which no photoelectrons are emitted. This frequency is called the _threshold frequency_. Increasing the frequency of the incident beam increases the maximum kinetic energy of the emitted photoelectrons, and the stopping voltage has to increase. The number of emitted electrons may also change because the [probability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability "Probability") that each photon results in an emitted electron is a function of photon energy[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_].

An increase in the intensity of the same monochromatic light (so long as the intensity is not too high[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Zhang1996-13)), which is proportional to the number of photons impinging on the surface in a given time, increases the rate at which electrons are ejected—the photoelectric current _I—_ but the kinetic energy of the photoelectrons and the stopping voltage remain the same. For a given metal and frequency of incident radiation, the rate at which photoelectrons are ejected is directly proportional to the intensity of the incident light.

The time lag between the incidence of radiation and the emission of a photoelectron is very small, less than 10−9 second. Angular distribution of the photoelectrons is highly dependent on [polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polarization_(waves) "Polarization (waves)") (the direction of the electric field) of the incident light, as well as the emitting material's quantum properties such as atomic and molecular orbital symmetries and the [electronic band structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_band_structure "Electronic band structure") of crystalline solids. In materials without macroscopic order, the distribution of electrons tends to peak in the direction of polarization of linearly polarized light.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Bupp-14) The experimental technique that can measure these distributions to infer the material's properties is [angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle-resolved_photoemission_spectroscopy "Angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy").

### Theoretical explanation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photoelectric_effect&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Theoretical explanation")]

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/52/Photoelectric_effect_-_stopping_voltage_diagram_for_zinc_-_English.svg/250px-Photoelectric_effect_-_stopping_voltage_diagram_for_zinc_-_English.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Photoelectric_effect_-_stopping_voltage_diagram_for_zinc_-_English.svg)

Diagram of the maximum kinetic energy as a function of the frequency of light on zinc

In 1905, [Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") proposed a theory of the photoelectric effect using a concept that light consists of tiny packets of energy known as [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photons "Photons") or light quanta. Each packet carries energy ![Image 4: {\displaystyle h\nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3cc23768f43b26085f80e1882a94b31d24abd653) that is proportional to the frequency ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c15bbbb971240cf328aba572178f091684585468) of the corresponding electromagnetic wave. The proportionality constant ![Image 6: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a) has become known as the [Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant"). In the range of kinetic energies of the electrons that are removed from their varying atomic bindings by the absorption of a photon of energy ![Image 7: {\displaystyle h\nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3cc23768f43b26085f80e1882a94b31d24abd653), the highest kinetic energy ![Image 8: {\displaystyle K_{\max }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d91c6687104d1e85ee24d4f238df3700c95f8091) is ![Image 9: {\displaystyle K_{\max }=h\,\nu -W.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/63f3ac34ce004552a9bcefd8114d00c2b1210549) Here, ![Image 10: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7) is the minimum energy required to remove an electron from the surface of the material. It is called the [work function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work_function "Work function") of the surface and is sometimes denoted ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \Phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aed80a2011a3912b028ba32a52dfa57165455f24) or ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e).[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-15) If the work function is written as ![Image 13: {\displaystyle W=h\,\nu _{o},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba9c89fe5fd3116dc26602c3c517829347749f6a) the formula for the maximum kinetic energy of the ejected electrons becomes ![Image 14: {\displaystyle K_{\max }=h\left(\nu -\nu _{o}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a86bcbc02eb2b042bdc6adae9413e3bb569d1e1)

Kinetic energy is positive, and ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \nu >\nu _{o}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/168faa151e5e3401311409735cb95cdcce73a330) is required for the photoelectric effect to occur.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Fromhold1991-16) The frequency ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \nu _{o}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d8cf32371b42f618c233a9e60d15742518eaae34) is the threshold frequency for the given material. Above that frequency, the maximum kinetic energy of the photoelectrons as well as the stopping voltage in the experiment ![Image 17: {\textstyle V_{o}={\frac {h}{e}}\left(\nu -\nu _{o}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f21421a13faef5b34f1f839303179def5146b163) rise linearly with the frequency, and have no dependence on the number of photons and the intensity of the impinging monochromatic light. Einstein's formula, however simple, explained all the phenomenology of the photoelectric effect, and had far-reaching consequences in the [development of quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_quantum_mechanics "History of quantum mechanics").

### Photoemission from atoms, molecules and solids

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photoelectric_effect&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Photoemission from atoms, molecules and solids")]

Electrons that are bound in atoms, molecules and solids each occupy distinct states of well-defined [binding energies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binding_energy "Binding energy"). When light quanta deliver more than this amount of energy to an individual electron, the electron may be emitted into free space with excess (kinetic) energy that is ![Image 18: {\displaystyle h\nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3cc23768f43b26085f80e1882a94b31d24abd653) higher than the electron's binding energy. The distribution of kinetic energies thus reflects the distribution of the binding energies of the electrons in the atomic, molecular or crystalline system: an electron emitted from the state at binding energy ![Image 19: {\displaystyle E_{B}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b71d40b28612bb3f7578929558cccef2bb5798d) is found at kinetic energy ![Image 20: {\displaystyle E_{k}=h\nu -E_{B}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4157a0dd1ee1094cb71fda0e3d035ee0b8396176). This distribution is one of the main characteristics of the quantum system, and can be used for further studies in quantum chemistry and quantum physics.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

#### Models of photoemission from solids

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photoelectric_effect&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Models of photoemission from solids")]

The electronic properties of ordered, crystalline solids are determined by the distribution of the electronic states with respect to energy and momentum—the electronic band structure of the solid. Theoretical models of photoemission from solids show that this distribution is, for the most part, preserved in the photoelectric effect. The phenomenological _three-step model_[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-17) for ultraviolet and soft X-ray excitation decomposes the effect into these steps:[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Stefan2003-18)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-:0-20)

1.   Inner photoelectric effect in the bulk of the material that is a direct optical transition between an occupied and an unoccupied electronic state. This effect is subject to quantum-mechanical [selection rules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Selection_rule "Selection rule") for dipole transitions. The hole left behind the electron can give rise to secondary electron emission, or the so-called [Auger effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auger_effect "Auger effect"), which may be visible even when the primary photoelectron does not leave the material. In molecular solids [phonons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phonon "Phonon") are excited in this step and may be visible as satellite lines in the final electron energy.
2.   Electron propagation to the surface in which some electrons may be scattered because of interactions with other constituents of the solid. Electrons that originate deeper in the solid are much more likely to suffer collisions and emerge with altered energy and momentum. Their mean-free path is a [universal curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inelastic_mean_free_path "Inelastic mean free path") dependent on electron's energy.
3.   Electron escape through the surface barrier into free-electron-like states of the vacuum. In this step the electron loses energy in the amount of the _work function of the surface_, and suffers from the momentum loss in the direction perpendicular to the surface. Because the binding energy of electrons in solids is conveniently expressed with respect to the highest occupied state at the Fermi energy ![Image 21: {\displaystyle E_{F}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c11f54775db7f6468779534bdcc4ce0f9054905f), and the difference to the free-space (vacuum) energy is the work function of the surface, the kinetic energy of the electrons emitted from solids is usually written as ![Image 22: {\displaystyle E_{k}=h\nu -W-E_{B}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9047d0327d5263c0a54ee574183492515c5753f).

There are cases where the three-step model fails to explain peculiarities of the photoelectron intensity distributions. The more elaborate _one-step model_[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-21) treats the effect as a coherent process of photoexcitation into the final state of a finite crystal for which the wave function is free-electron-like outside of the crystal, but has a decaying envelope inside.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-:0-20)

In 1839, [Alexandre Edmond Becquerel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A._E._Becquerel "A. E. Becquerel") discovered the related [photovoltaic effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photovoltaic_effect "Photovoltaic effect") while studying the effect of light on [electrolytic cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrolytic_cell "Electrolytic cell").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Petrova-KochHezel2009-22) Though not equivalent to the photoelectric effect, his work on [photovoltaics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photovoltaics "Photovoltaics") was instrumental in showing a strong relationship between light and electronic properties of materials. In 1873, [Willoughby Smith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Willoughby_Smith "Willoughby Smith") discovered [photoconductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoconductivity "Photoconductivity") in [selenium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Selenium "Selenium") while testing the metal for its high resistance properties in conjunction with his work involving submarine telegraph cables.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Smith1873-23)

[Johann Elster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julius_Elster "Julius Elster") (1854–1920) and [Hans Geitel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Friedrich_Geitel "Hans Friedrich Geitel") (1855–1923), students in [Heidelberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heidelberg "Heidelberg"), investigated the effects produced by light on electrified bodies and developed the first practical photoelectric cells that could be used to measure the intensity of light.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_e-24)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-BudWarner1998-25): 458  They arranged metals with respect to their power of discharging negative electricity: [rubidium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubidium "Rubidium"), [potassium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium "Potassium"), [alloy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alloy "Alloy") of potassium and sodium, [sodium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium "Sodium"), [lithium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium "Lithium"), [magnesium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnesium "Magnesium"), [thallium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thallium "Thallium") and [zinc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zinc "Zinc"); for [copper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper "Copper"), [platinum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platinum "Platinum"), [lead](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lead "Lead"), [iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron "Iron"), [cadmium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cadmium "Cadmium"), [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon"), and [mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(element) "Mercury (element)") the effects with ordinary light were too small to be measurable. The order of the metals for this effect was the same as in Volta's series for contact-electricity, the most electropositive metals giving the largest photo-electric effect.

[![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b3/Gold_leaf_electroscope_1869.png/250px-Gold_leaf_electroscope_1869.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Gold_leaf_electroscope_1869.png)

Gold leaf [electroscope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electroscope "Electroscope") demonstrating the photoelectric effect. When the electroscope disk is negatively charged with excess electrons, the gold leaves mutually repel. If high-energy light (such as ultraviolet) is then shone on the disk, electrons are emitted by the photoelectric effect and the leaf repulsion ceases. But if the light used has insufficient energy to stimulate electron emission, the leaves stay separated regardless of duration.

In 1887, [Heinrich Hertz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinrich_Hertz "Heinrich Hertz") observed the photoelectric effect[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_f-26) and reported on the production and reception[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-27) of electromagnetic waves.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Smithsonian_report-28) The receiver in his apparatus consisted of a coil with a [spark gap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spark_gap "Spark gap"), where a spark would be seen upon detection of electromagnetic waves. He placed the apparatus in a darkened box to see the spark better. However, he noticed that the maximum spark length was reduced when inside the box. A glass panel placed between the source of electromagnetic waves and the receiver absorbed ultraviolet radiation that assisted the electrons in jumping across the gap. When removed, the spark length would increase. He observed no decrease in spark length when he replaced the glass with quartz, as [quartz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quartz "Quartz") does not absorb UV radiation.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The discoveries by Hertz led to a series of investigations by [Wilhelm Hallwachs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Hallwachs "Wilhelm Hallwachs"),[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-29)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_g-30) Hoor,[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_h-31)[Augusto Righi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Augusto_Righi "Augusto Righi")[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_i-32) and [Aleksander Stoletov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aleksandr_Stoletov "Aleksandr Stoletov")[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_j-33)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Stoletov-34) on the effect of light, and especially of ultraviolet light, on charged bodies. Hallwachs connected a zinc plate to an [electroscope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electroscope "Electroscope"). He allowed ultraviolet light to fall on a freshly cleaned zinc plate and observed that the zinc plate became uncharged if initially negatively charged, positively charged if initially uncharged, and more positively charged if initially positively charged. From these observations he concluded that some negatively charged particles were emitted by the zinc plate when exposed to ultraviolet light.

With regard to the _Hertz effect_, the researchers from the start showed the complexity of the phenomenon of photoelectric fatigue—the progressive diminution of the effect observed upon fresh metallic surfaces. According to Hallwachs, [ozone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone "Ozone") played an important part in the phenomenon,[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_m-35) and the emission was influenced by oxidation, humidity, and the degree of polishing of the surface. It was at the time unclear whether fatigue is absent in a vacuum.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

In the period from 1888 until 1891, a detailed analysis of the photoeffect was performed by [Aleksandr Stoletov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aleksandr_Stoletov "Aleksandr Stoletov") with results reported in six publications.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Stoletov-34) Stoletov invented a new experimental setup which was more suitable for a quantitative analysis of the photoeffect. He discovered a direct proportionality between the intensity of light and the induced photoelectric current (the first law of photoeffect or [Stoletov's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoletov%27s_law "Stoletov's law")). He measured the dependence of the intensity of the photo electric current on the gas pressure, where he found the existence of an optimal gas pressure corresponding to a maximum [photocurrent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photocurrent "Photocurrent"); this property was used for the creation of [solar cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_cell "Solar cell").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Many substances besides metals discharge negative electricity under the action of ultraviolet light. G. C. Schmidt[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_c-36) and O. Knoblauch[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-37) compiled a list of these substances.

In 1897, [J. J. Thomson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._J._Thomson "J. J. Thomson") investigated ultraviolet light in [Crookes tubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geissler_tube "Geissler tube").[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_o-38) Thomson deduced that the ejected particles, which he called corpuscles, were of the same nature as [cathode rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cathode_rays "Cathode rays"). These particles later became known as the electrons. Thomson enclosed a metal plate (a cathode) in a vacuum tube, and exposed it to high-frequency radiation.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-39) It was thought that the oscillating electromagnetic fields caused the atoms' field to resonate and, after reaching a certain amplitude, caused subatomic corpuscles to be emitted, and current to be detected. The amount of this current varied with the intensity and color of the radiation. Larger radiation intensity or frequency would produce more current.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

During the years 1886–1902, [Wilhelm Hallwachs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Hallwachs "Wilhelm Hallwachs") and [Philipp Lenard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philipp_Lenard "Philipp Lenard") investigated the phenomenon of photoelectric emission in detail. Lenard observed that a current flows through an evacuated glass tube enclosing two [electrodes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrode "Electrode") when ultraviolet radiation falls on one of them. As soon as ultraviolet radiation is stopped, the current also stops. This initiated the concept of photoelectric emission. The discovery of the ionization of gases by ultraviolet light was made by Philipp Lenard in 1900. As the effect was produced across several centimeters of air and yielded a greater number of positive ions than negative, it was natural to interpret the phenomenon, as J. J. Thomson did, as a _Hertz effect_ upon the particles present in the gas.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Smithsonian_report-28)

In 1902, Lenard observed that the [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy") of individual emitted electrons was independent of the applied light intensity.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_Lenard-6)[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-40) This appeared to be at odds with Maxwell's [wave theory of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_theory_of_light "Wave theory of light"), which predicted that the electron energy would be proportional to the [intensity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intensity_(physics) "Intensity (physics)") of the radiation.

Lenard observed the variation in electron energy with light frequency using a powerful electric arc lamp which enabled him to investigate large changes in intensity. However, Lenard's results were qualitative rather than quantitative because of the difficulty in performing the experiments: the experiments needed to be done on freshly cut metal so that the pure metal was observed, but it oxidized in a matter of minutes even in the partial vacuums he used. The current emitted by the surface was determined by the light's intensity, or brightness: doubling the intensity of the light doubled the number of electrons emitted from the surface.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Initial investigation of the photoelectric effect in gasses by Lenard[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_p-41) were followed up by J. J. Thomson[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_q-42) and then more decisively by Frederic Palmer Jr.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_r-43)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-44) The gas photoemission was studied and showed very different characteristics than those at first attributed to it by Lenard.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Smithsonian_report-28)

In 1900, while studying [black-body radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black-body_radiation "Black-body radiation"), the German physicist [Max Planck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Planck "Max Planck") suggested in his "On the Law of Distribution of Energy in the Normal Spectrum"[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-45) paper that the energy carried by electromagnetic waves could only be released in _packets_ of energy. In 1905, Albert Einstein published a paper advancing the hypothesis that light energy is carried in discrete quantized packets to explain experimental data from the photoelectric effect. Einstein theorized that the energy in each quantum of light was equal to the frequency of light multiplied by a constant, later called the [Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant"). A photon above a threshold frequency has the required energy to eject a single electron, creating the observed effect. This was a step in the development of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"). In 1914, [Robert A. Millikan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Andrews_Millikan "Robert Andrews Millikan")'s highly accurate measurements of the Planck constant from the photoelectric effect supported Einstein's model, even though a corpuscular theory of light was for Millikan, at the time, "quite unthinkable".[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-46) Einstein was awarded the 1921 [Nobel Prize in Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics "Nobel Prize in Physics") for "his discovery of the law of the photoelectric effect",[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_s-47) and Millikan was awarded the Nobel Prize in 1923 for "his work on the elementary charge of electricity and on the photoelectric effect".[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-48) In quantum perturbation theory of atoms and solids acted upon by electromagnetic radiation, the photoelectric effect is still commonly analyzed in terms of waves; the two approaches are equivalent because photon or wave absorption can only happen between quantized energy levels whose energy difference is that of the energy of photon.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Lamb1968-49)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Stefan2003-18)

Albert Einstein's mathematical description of how the photoelectric effect was caused by absorption of [quanta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum "Quantum") of light was in one of his [Annus Mirabilis papers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annus_Mirabilis_papers "Annus Mirabilis papers"), named "On a Heuristic Viewpoint Concerning the Production and Transformation of Light".[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-50) The paper proposed a simple description of _energy quanta_, and showed how they explained the blackbody radiation spectrum. His explanation in terms of absorption of discrete quanta of light agreed with experimental results. It explained why the energy of photoelectrons was not dependent on incident light _intensity_. This was a theoretical leap, but the concept was strongly resisted at first because it contradicted the wave theory of light that followed naturally from [James Clerk Maxwell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell "James Clerk Maxwell")'s equations of electromagnetism, and more generally, the assumption of [infinite divisibility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_divisibility "Infinite divisibility") of energy in physical systems.

Einstein's work predicted that the energy of individual ejected electrons increases linearly with the frequency of the light. The precise relationship had not at that time been tested. By 1905 it was known that the energy of photoelectrons increases with increasing _frequency_ of incident light and is independent of the _intensity_ of the light. However, the manner of the increase was not experimentally determined until 1914 when Millikan showed that Einstein's prediction was correct.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_Millikan-7)

The photoelectric effect helped to propel the then-emerging concept of wave–particle duality in the nature of light. Light simultaneously possesses the characteristics of both waves and particles, each being manifested according to the circumstances. The effect was impossible to understand in terms of the classical wave description of light,[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_u-51)[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_-52)[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_v-53) as the energy of the emitted electrons did not depend on the intensity of the incident radiation. Classical theory predicted that the electrons would 'gather up' energy over a period of time, and then be emitted.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_-52)[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_w-54)

Research in recent years on the photoelectric effect has been focused on measurements on emission time of photoelectrons. For long, it was believed that photoemission is an instantaneous process. However, a seminal role in this field was played by experimental techniques on attosecond generation of pulses of light for studies on electron dynamics, which was recognised through the 2023 Nobel Prize in physics to Pierre Agostini, Ferenc Krausz and Anne L’Huillier.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-NP-55) For example, in 2010, it was discovered that electron emission takes 20 attoseconds and that photoemission is associated with complex multielectron correlations and is not a single-electron process.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Schultze-56) In a more recent work in the context of [tungsten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tungsten "Tungsten"), measurements on photoelectron emission indicated that around 100 [attoseconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attosecond "Attosecond") are required to liberate an electron.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Cavalieri-57) In another research, the value was found to be 45 attoseconds.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ossiander-58) A broad consensus is emerging towards the fact that photoemission is not instantaneous and involves finite time.

The role of electric field in photoelectric effect has also been empirically studied and it was found that electromagnetic radiation with a specific orientation of electric field can excite electrons leading to enhanced emission in the Terahertz range.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ritchie-59)

[![Image 24](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Photomultiplier_schema_en.png/250px-Photomultiplier_schema_en.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Photomultiplier_schema_en.png)

Photomultiplier

These are extremely light-sensitive vacuum tubes with a coated [photocathode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photocathode "Photocathode") inside the envelope. The photo cathode contains combinations of materials such as cesium, rubidium, and antimony specially selected to provide a low work function, so when illuminated even by very low levels of light, the photocathode readily releases electrons. By means of a series of electrodes (dynodes) at ever-higher potentials, these electrons are accelerated and substantially increased in number through [secondary emission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secondary_emission "Secondary emission") to provide a readily detectable output current. Photomultipliers are still commonly used wherever low levels of light must be detected.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_Timothy-60)

[Video camera tubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Video_camera_tube "Video camera tube") in the early days of [television](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Television "Television") used the photoelectric effect. For example, [Philo Farnsworth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philo_Farnsworth "Philo Farnsworth")'s "[Image dissector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_dissector "Image dissector")" used a screen charged by the photoelectric effect to transform an optical image into a scanned electronic signal.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_x-61)

### Photoelectron spectroscopy

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photoelectric_effect&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Photoelectron spectroscopy")]

[![Image 25](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/24/ARPES_setup_-_ultraviolet_source_-_sample_holder_-_electron_analyzer.svg/250px-ARPES_setup_-_ultraviolet_source_-_sample_holder_-_electron_analyzer.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:ARPES_setup_-_ultraviolet_source_-_sample_holder_-_electron_analyzer.svg)

[Angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle-resolved_photoemission_spectroscopy "Angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy") ([ARPES](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle-resolved_photoemission_spectroscopy "Angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy")) experiment. Helium discharge lamp shines ultraviolet light onto the sample in ultra-high vacuum. Hemispherical electron analyzer measures the distribution of ejected electrons with respect to energy and momentum.

Because the kinetic energy of the emitted electrons is exactly the energy of the incident photon minus the energy of the electron's binding within an atom, molecule or solid, the binding energy can be determined by shining a [monochromatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monochromatic "Monochromatic")[X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray") or [UV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet") light of a known energy and measuring the kinetic energies of the photoelectrons.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Stefan2003-18) The distribution of electron energies is valuable for studying quantum properties of these systems. It can also be used to determine the elemental composition of the samples. For solids, the kinetic energy and emission angle distribution of the photoelectrons is measured for the complete determination of the [electronic band structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_band_structure "Electronic band structure") in terms of the allowed binding energies and momenta of the electrons. Modern instruments for angle-resolved photoemission spectroscopy are capable of measuring these quantities with a precision better than 1 meV and 0.1°.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

[Photoelectron spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectron_spectroscopy "Photoelectron spectroscopy") measurements are usually performed in a high-vacuum environment, because the electrons would be scattered by gas molecules if they were present. However, some companies are now selling products that allow photoemission in air. The light source can be a laser, a discharge tube, or a [synchrotron radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synchrotron_radiation "Synchrotron radiation") source.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_aa-62)

The [concentric hemispherical analyzer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hemispherical_electron_energy_analyzer "Hemispherical electron energy analyzer") is a typical electron energy analyzer. It uses an electric field between two hemispheres to change (disperse) the trajectories of incident electrons depending on their kinetic energies.

### Night vision devices

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photoelectric_effect&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Night vision devices")]

Photons hitting a thin film of alkali metal or [semiconductor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiconductor "Semiconductor") material such as [gallium arsenide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gallium_arsenide "Gallium arsenide") in an [image intensifier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_intensifier "Image intensifier") tube cause the ejection of photoelectrons due to the photoelectric effect. These are accelerated by an [electrostatic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrostatic_field "Electrostatic field") where they strike a [phosphor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphor "Phosphor") coated screen, converting the electrons back into photons. Intensification of the signal is achieved either through acceleration of the electrons or by increasing the number of electrons through secondary emissions, such as with a [micro-channel plate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micro-channel_plate "Micro-channel plate"). Sometimes a combination of both methods is used. Additional kinetic energy is required to move an electron out of the conduction band and into the vacuum level. This is known as the [electron affinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_affinity "Electron affinity") of the photocathode and is another barrier to photoemission other than the forbidden band, explained by the [band gap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Band_gap "Band gap") model. Some materials such as gallium arsenide have an effective electron affinity that is below the level of the conduction band. In these materials, electrons that move to the conduction band all have sufficient energy to be emitted from the material, so the film that absorbs photons can be quite thick. These materials are known as negative electron affinity materials.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The photoelectric effect will cause [spacecraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacecraft "Spacecraft") exposed to sunlight to develop a positive charge. This can be a major problem, as other parts of the spacecraft are in shadow which will result in the spacecraft developing a negative charge from nearby plasmas. The imbalance can discharge through delicate electrical components. The [static charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Static_electricity "Static electricity") created by the photoelectric effect is self-limiting, because a higher charged object does not give up its electrons as easily as a lower charged object does.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-63)[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_ab-64)

Light from the Sun hitting lunar dust causes it to become positively charged from the photoelectric effect. The charged dust then repels itself and lifts off the surface of the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon") by [electrostatic levitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrostatic_levitation "Electrostatic levitation").[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_ac-65)[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Ref_ad-66) This manifests itself almost like an "atmosphere of dust", visible as a thin haze and blurring of distant features, and visible as a dim glow after the sun has set. This was first photographed by the [Surveyor program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surveyor_program "Surveyor program") probes in the 1960s,[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-67) and most recently the [Chang'e 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chang%27e_3 "Chang'e 3") rover observed dust deposition on lunar rocks as high as about 28 cm.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-68) It is thought that the smallest particles are repelled kilometers from the surface and that the particles move in "fountains" as they charge and discharge.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-69)

Competing processes and photoemission cross section
---------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Photoelectric_effect&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Competing processes and photoemission cross section")]

[![Image 26](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Dominant_Photon-Matter_Interaction.svg/250px-Dominant_Photon-Matter_Interaction.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Dominant_Photon-Matter_Interaction.svg)

Plot of photon energies calculated for a given element (atomic number Z) at which the [cross section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_section_(physics) "Cross section (physics)") value for the process on the right becomes larger than the cross section for the process on the left. For calcium (Z=20), Compton scattering starts to dominate at _hυ_=0.08 MeV and ceases at 12 MeV.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-70)

When photon energies are as high as the electron rest energy of 511 keV, yet another process, [Compton scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compton_scattering "Compton scattering"), may occur. Above twice this energy, at 1.022 MeV, [pair production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pair_production "Pair production") is also more likely.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Evans1955-71) Compton scattering and pair production are examples of two other competing mechanisms.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] Even if the photoelectric effect is the favoured reaction for a particular interaction of a single photon with a bound electron, the result is also subject to quantum statistics and is not guaranteed. The probability of the photoelectric effect occurring is measured by the [cross section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_section_(physics) "Cross section (physics)") of the interaction, σ. This has been found to be a function of the atomic number of the target atom and photon energy. In a crude approximation, for photon energies above the highest atomic binding energy, the cross section is given by:[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Davisson1965-72)

![Image 27: {\displaystyle \sigma =\mathrm {constant} \cdot {\frac {Z^{n}}{E^{3}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a16d4366b0f1b2fbe0fa46b801e7c47a407af2a)
Here _Z_ is the [atomic number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_number "Atomic number") and _n_ is a number which varies between 4 and 5. The photoelectric effect rapidly decreases in significance in the gamma-ray region of the spectrum, with increasing photon energy. It is also more likely from elements with high atomic number. Consequently, high-_Z_ materials make good [gamma-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_ray "Gamma ray") shields, which is the principal reason why lead (_Z_=82) is preferred and most widely used.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoelectric_effect#cite_note-Knoll1999-73)
