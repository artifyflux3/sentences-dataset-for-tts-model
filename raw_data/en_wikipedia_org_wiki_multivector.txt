In [multilinear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilinear_algebra "Multilinear algebra"), a **multivector**, sometimes called **Clifford number** or **multor**,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector#cite_note-1) is an element of the [exterior algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_algebra "Exterior algebra")Λ(_V_) of a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space")V. This algebra is [graded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graded_algebra "Graded algebra"), [associative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_algebra "Associative algebra") and [alternating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternating_algebra "Alternating algebra"), and consists of [linear combinations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_combination "Linear combination") of **simple**_k_-vectors[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector#cite_note-Flanders-2) (also known as **decomposable**_k_-vectors[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector#cite_note-3) or [_k_-blades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blade_(geometry) "Blade (geometry)")) of the form

![Image 1: {\displaystyle v_{1}\wedge \cdots \wedge v_{k},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d3ae2790800e23a73bbb7612da6c1922f3385b6)
where ![Image 2: {\displaystyle v_{1},\ldots ,v_{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ac7feaa0a7904d6c6b883a6c3ca8d07fc71dad5a) are in V.

A **_k_-vector** is such a linear combination that is _homogeneous_ of degree k (all terms are _k_-blades for the same k). Depending on the authors, a "multivector" may be either a _k_-vector or any element of the exterior algebra (any linear combination of _k_-blades with potentially differing values of _k_).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector#cite_note-4)

In [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), a _k_-vector is usually a vector in the exterior algebra of the [tangent vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space "Tangent space") of a [smooth manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_manifold "Smooth manifold"); that is, it is an antisymmetric [tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") obtained by taking linear combinations of the [exterior product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_product "Exterior product") of _k_[tangent vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_vector "Tangent vector"), for some integer _k_ ≥ 0. A [differential _k_-form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form") is a _k_-vector in the exterior algebra of the [dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_space "Dual space") of the tangent space, which is also the dual of the exterior algebra of the tangent space.

For _k_ = 0, 1, 2 and 3, _k_-vectors are often called respectively _[scalars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar\_(mathematics) "Scalar (mathematics)")_, _[vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector\_(mathematics\_and\_physics) "Vector (mathematics and physics)")_, _[bivectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bivector "Bivector")_ and _trivectors_; they are respectively dual to [0-forms, 1-forms, 2-forms and 3-forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector#cite_note-%C5%81awrynowicz-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector#cite_note-Baylis-6)

The exterior product (also called the wedge product) used to construct multivectors is multilinear (linear in each input), associative and alternating. This means for vectors **u**, **v** and **w** in a vector space _V_ and for scalars _α_, _β_, the exterior product has the properties:

*   Linear in an input: ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} \wedge (\alpha \mathbf {v} +\beta \mathbf {w} )=\alpha \mathbf {u} \wedge \mathbf {v} +\beta \mathbf {u} \wedge \mathbf {w} ;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c03eb6dc848505aa2744d9a417a735673cc6da8b)
*   Associative: ![Image 4: {\displaystyle (\mathbf {u} \wedge \mathbf {v} )\wedge \mathbf {w} =\mathbf {u} \wedge (\mathbf {v} \wedge \mathbf {w} );}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68bcd6dc42968dac0c7e0bfd6c41eaffa1e2c787)
*   Alternating: ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} \wedge \mathbf {u} =0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/97bfb3c005f30ea684171890104e1683edbe788d)

The exterior product of _k_ vectors or a sum of such products (for a single _k_) is called a grade _k_ multivector, or a _k_-vector. The maximum grade of a multivector is the dimension of the vector space _V_.

Linearity in either input together with the alternating property implies linearity in the other input. The multilinearity of the exterior product allows a multivector to be expressed as a linear combination of exterior products of basis vectors of _V_. The exterior product of _k_ basis vectors of _V_ is the standard way of constructing each basis element for the space of _k_-vectors, which has dimension [(_n_ _k_)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N_choose_k "N choose k") in the exterior algebra of an _n_-dimensional vector space.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector#cite_note-Flanders-2)

The _k_-vector obtained from the exterior product of _k_ separate vectors in an _n_-dimensional space has components that define the projected (_k_ − 1)-volumes of the _k_-[parallelotope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped#Parallelotope "Parallelepiped") spanned by the vectors. The square root of the sum of the squares of these components defines the volume of the _k_-parallelotope.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector#cite_note-Flanders-2)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector#cite_note-7)

The following examples show that a bivector in two dimensions measures the area of a parallelogram, and the magnitude of a bivector in three dimensions also measures the area of a parallelogram. Similarly, a three-vector in three dimensions measures the volume of a parallelepiped.

It is easy to check that the magnitude of a three-vector in four dimensions measures the volume of the parallelepiped spanned by these vectors.

Properties of multivectors can be seen by considering the two-dimensional vector space _V_ = **R**2. Let the basis vectors be **e**1 and **e**2, so **u** and **v** are given by

![Image 6: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} =u_{1}\mathbf {e} _{1}+u_{2}\mathbf {e} _{2},\quad \mathbf {v} =v_{1}\mathbf {e} _{1}+v_{2}\mathbf {e} _{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d140784e4316a16c64a7632dcee1c058949367b3)
and the multivector **u** ∧ **v**, also called a bivector, is computed to be

![Image 7: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} \wedge \mathbf {v} \ =\ {\begin{vmatrix}u_{1}&v_{1}\\u_{2}&v_{2}\end{vmatrix}}\ (\mathbf {e} _{1}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{2}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee26e9eda90ead39a481f201276a2e896b104b16)
The vertical bars denote the determinant of the matrix, which is the area of the parallelogram spanned by the vectors **u** and **v**. The magnitude of **u** ∧ **v** is the area of this parallelogram. Notice that because _V_ has dimension two the basis bivector **e**1 ∧ **e**2 is the only multivector in Λ _V_.

The relationship between the magnitude of a multivector and the area or volume spanned by the vectors is an important feature in all dimensions. Furthermore, the linear functional version of a multivector that computes this volume is known as a differential form.

More features of multivectors can be seen by considering the three-dimensional vector space _V_ = **R**3. In this case, let the basis vectors be **e**1, **e**2, and **e**3, so **u**, **v** and **w** are given by

![Image 8: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {u} &=u_{1}\mathbf {e} _{1}+u_{2}\mathbf {e} _{2}+u_{3}\mathbf {e} _{3},&\mathbf {v} &=v_{1}\mathbf {e} _{1}+v_{2}\mathbf {e} _{2}+v_{3}\mathbf {e} _{3},&\mathbf {w} &=w_{1}\mathbf {e} _{1}+w_{2}\mathbf {e} _{2}+w_{3}\mathbf {e} _{3},\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66617201b8418b1466428d7b961ee14e6a79e2a9)
and the bivector **u** ∧ **v** is computed to be

![Image 9: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} \wedge \mathbf {v} \ =\ {\begin{vmatrix}u_{2}&v_{2}\\u_{3}&v_{3}\end{vmatrix}}\left(\mathbf {e} _{2}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{3}\right)+{\begin{vmatrix}u_{1}&v_{1}\\u_{3}&v_{3}\end{vmatrix}}\left(\mathbf {e} _{1}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{3}\right)+{\begin{vmatrix}u_{1}&v_{1}\\u_{2}&v_{2}\end{vmatrix}}\left(\mathbf {e} _{1}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{2}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3528047d42ab6c816b48bcfe721d8b809b583b1d)
The components of this bivector are the same as the components of the cross product. The magnitude of this bivector is the square root of the sum of the squares of its components.

This shows that the magnitude of the bivector **u** ∧ **v** is the area of the parallelogram spanned by the vectors **u** and **v** as it lies in the three-dimensional space _V_. The components of the bivector are the projected areas of the parallelogram on each of the three coordinate planes.

Notice that because _V_ has dimension three, there is one basis three-vector in Λ _V_. Compute the three-vector

This shows that the magnitude of the three-vector **u** ∧ **v** ∧ **w** is the volume of the parallelepiped spanned by the three vectors **u**, **v** and **w**.

In higher-dimensional spaces, the component three-vectors are projections of the volume of a parallelepiped onto the coordinate three-spaces, and the magnitude of the three-vector is the volume of the parallelepiped as it sits in the higher-dimensional space.

Grassmann coordinates
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Multivector&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Grassmann coordinates")]

In this section, we consider multivectors on a [projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_space "Projective space")_P_ _n_, which provide a convenient set of coordinates for lines, planes and hyperplanes that have properties similar to the homogeneous coordinates of points, called [Grassmann coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grassmann_coordinates "Grassmann coordinates").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector#cite_note-8)

Points in a real projective space _P_ _n_ are defined to be lines through the origin of the vector space **R**_n_+1. For example, the projective plane _P_ 2 is the set of lines through the origin of **R**3. Thus, multivectors defined on **R**_n_+1 can be viewed as multivectors on _P_ _n_.

A convenient way to view a multivector on _P_ _n_ is to examine it in an [affine component](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space "Affine space") of _P_ _n_, which is the intersection of the lines through the origin of **R**_n_+1 with a selected hyperplane, such as H: _x_ _n_+1 = 1. Lines through the origin of **R**3 intersect the plane E: _z_ = 1 to define an affine version of the projective plane that only lacks the points for which _z_ = 0, called the points at infinity.

### Multivectors on the projective plane _P_ 2

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Multivector&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Multivectors on the projective plane P2")]

Points in the affine component E: _z_ = 1 of the projective plane **P**2 have coordinates **x** = (_x_, _y_, 1). A linear combination of two points **p** = (_p_ 1, _p_ 2, 1) and **q** = (_q_ 1, _q_ 2, 1) defines a plane in **R**3 that intersects E in the line joining **p** and **q**. The multivector **p** ∧ **q** defines a parallelogram in **R**3 given by

![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} \wedge \mathbf {q} \ =\ (p_{2}-q_{2})(\mathbf {e} _{2}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{3})+(p_{1}-q_{1})(\mathbf {e} _{1}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{3})+(p_{1}q_{2}-q_{1}p_{2})(\mathbf {e} _{1}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{2}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee1ac6d534830d090162c0677c9980e17158153f)
Notice that substitution of _α_**p** + _β_**q** for **p** multiplies this multivector by a constant. Therefore, the components of **p** ∧ **q** are homogeneous coordinates for the plane through the origin of **R**3.

The set of points **x** = (_x_, _y_, 1) on the line through **p** and **q** is the intersection of the plane defined by **p** ∧ **q** with the plane E: _z_ = 1. These points satisfy **x** ∧ **p** ∧ **q** = 0, that is,

![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} \wedge \mathbf {p} \wedge \mathbf {q} \ =\ (x\mathbf {e} _{1}+y\mathbf {e} _{2}+\mathbf {e} _{3})\wedge {\big (}(p_{2}-q_{2})(\mathbf {e} _{2}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{3})+(p_{1}-q_{1})(\mathbf {e} _{1}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{3})+(p_{1}q_{2}-q_{1}p_{2})(\mathbf {e} _{1}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{2}){\big )}=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3868fd09617927ff7d3664c30fd5d9dce44a25e)
which simplifies to the equation of a line

![Image 13: {\displaystyle \lambda :x(p_{2}-q_{2})+y(p_{1}-q_{1})+(p_{1}q_{2}-q_{1}p_{2})=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1c4e9649f991765f3274e7a1f2d3497aa21224e2)
This equation is satisfied by points **x** = _α_**p** + _β_**q** for real values of α and β.

The three components of **p** ∧ **q** that define the line _λ_ are called the [Grassmann coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grassmann_coordinates "Grassmann coordinates") of the line. Because three homogeneous coordinates define both a point and a line, the geometry of points is said to be dual to the geometry of lines in the projective plane. This is called the [principle of duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duality_(projective_geometry) "Duality (projective geometry)").

### Multivectors on projective 3-space _P_ 3

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Multivector&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Multivectors on projective 3-space P3")]

Three-dimensional projective space _P_ 3 consists of all lines through the origin of **R**4. Let the three-dimensional hyperplane, H: _w_ = 1, be the affine component of projective space defined by the points **x** = (_x_, _y_, _z_, 1). The multivector **p** ∧ **q** ∧ **r** defines a parallelepiped in **R**4 given by

![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} \wedge \mathbf {q} \wedge \mathbf {r} ={\begin{vmatrix}p_{2}&q_{2}&r_{2}\\p_{3}&q_{3}&r_{3}\\1&1&1\end{vmatrix}}\mathbf {e} _{2}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{3}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{4}+{\begin{vmatrix}p_{1}&q_{1}&r_{1}\\p_{3}&q_{3}&r_{3}\\1&1&1\end{vmatrix}}\mathbf {e} _{1}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{3}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{4}+{\begin{vmatrix}p_{1}&q_{1}&r_{1}\\p_{2}&q_{2}&r_{2}\\1&1&1\end{vmatrix}}\mathbf {e} _{1}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{2}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{4}+{\begin{vmatrix}p_{1}&q_{1}&r_{1}\\p_{2}&q_{2}&r_{2}\\p_{3}&q_{3}&r_{3}\end{vmatrix}}\mathbf {e} _{1}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{2}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{3}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e64b574d5f0a7629fc4c17bc998121488e5cae0b)
Notice that substitution of α**p** + β**q** + γ**r** for **p** multiplies this multivector by a constant. Therefore, the components of **p** ∧ **q** ∧ **r** are homogeneous coordinates for the 3-space through the origin of **R**4.

A plane in the affine component H: _w_ = 1 is the set of points **x** = (_x_, _y_, _z_, 1) in the intersection of H with the 3-space defined by **p** ∧ **q** ∧ **r**. These points satisfy **x** ∧ **p** ∧ **q** ∧ **r** = 0, that is,

![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} \wedge \mathbf {p} \wedge \mathbf {q} \wedge \mathbf {r} =(x\mathbf {e} _{1}+y\mathbf {e} _{2}+z\mathbf {e} _{3}+\mathbf {e} _{4})\wedge \mathbf {p} \wedge \mathbf {q} \wedge \mathbf {r} =0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/809683a45368a04e9dda27580b1f676caad8a900)
which simplifies to the equation of a plane

![Image 16: {\displaystyle \lambda :x{\begin{vmatrix}p_{2}&q_{2}&r_{2}\\p_{3}&q_{3}&r_{3}\\1&1&1\end{vmatrix}}+y{\begin{vmatrix}p_{1}&q_{1}&r_{1}\\p_{3}&q_{3}&r_{3}\\1&1&1\end{vmatrix}}+z{\begin{vmatrix}p_{1}&q_{1}&r_{1}\\p_{2}&q_{2}&r_{2}\\1&1&1\end{vmatrix}}+{\begin{vmatrix}p_{1}&q_{1}&r_{1}\\p_{2}&q_{2}&r_{2}\\p_{3}&q_{3}&r_{3}\end{vmatrix}}=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/28286837753ddda263d882674818c30c59065265)
This equation is satisfied by points **x** = _α_**p** + _β_**q** + _γ_**r** for real values of _α_, _β_ and _γ_.

The four components of **p** ∧ **q** ∧ **r** that define the plane _λ_ are called the [Grassmann coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grassmann_coordinates "Grassmann coordinates") of the plane. Because four homogeneous coordinates define both a point and a plane in projective space, the geometry of points is dual to the geometry of planes.

**A line as the join of two points:** In projective space the line _λ_ through two points **p** and **q** can be viewed as the intersection of the affine space H: _w_ = 1 with the plane **x** = _α_**p** + _β_**q** in **R**4. The multivector **p** ∧ **q** provides homogeneous coordinates for the line

![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\lambda :\mathbf {p} \wedge \mathbf {q} &=(p_{1}\mathbf {e} _{1}+p_{2}\mathbf {e} _{2}+p_{3}\mathbf {e} _{3}+\mathbf {e} _{4})\wedge (q_{1}\mathbf {e} _{1}+q_{2}\mathbf {e} _{2}+q_{3}\mathbf {e} _{3}+\mathbf {e} _{4}),\\&={\begin{vmatrix}p_{1}&q_{1}\\1&1\end{vmatrix}}\mathbf {e} _{1}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{4}+{\begin{vmatrix}p_{2}&q_{2}\\1&1\end{vmatrix}}\mathbf {e} _{2}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{4}+{\begin{vmatrix}p_{3}&q_{3}\\1&1\end{vmatrix}}\mathbf {e} _{3}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{4}\\&+{\begin{vmatrix}p_{2}&q_{2}\\p_{3}&q_{3}\end{vmatrix}}\mathbf {e} _{2}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{3}+{\begin{vmatrix}p_{3}&q_{3}\\p_{1}&q_{1}\end{vmatrix}}\mathbf {e} _{3}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{1}+{\begin{vmatrix}p_{1}&q_{1}\\p_{2}&q_{2}\end{vmatrix}}\mathbf {e} _{1}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{2}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32f5b37e1ad352d536bf60d970fc2d0e370e5e9b)
These are known as the [Plücker coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pl%C3%BCcker_coordinates "Plücker coordinates") of the line, though they are also an example of Grassmann coordinates.

The left complement of the wedge product of right complements is called the antiwedge product, denoted by a downward pointing wedge, allowing us to write ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mu =\pi \vee \rho .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3cca059bfe9c3bb970823500060459027365a35d)

[W. K. Clifford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Kingdon_Clifford "William Kingdon Clifford") combined multivectors with the [inner product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product "Inner product") defined on the vector space, in order to obtain a general construction for hypercomplex numbers that includes the usual complex numbers and Hamilton's [quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion "Quaternion").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector#cite_note-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector#cite_note-11)

The Clifford product between two vectors **u** and **v** is bilinear and associative like the exterior product, and has the additional property that the multivector **uv** is coupled to the inner product **u** ⋅ **v** by Clifford's relation,

![Image 23: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} \mathbf {v} +\mathbf {v} \mathbf {u} =2\mathbf {u} \cdot \mathbf {v} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e35d707284e928754b38dfc5c9c23d6e5801e1d)
Clifford's relation retains the anticommuting property for vectors that are perpendicular. This can be seen from the mutually orthogonal unit vectors **e**_i_, _i_ = 1, ..., _n_ in **R**_n_: Clifford's relation yields

![Image 24: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{i}\mathbf {e} _{j}+\mathbf {e} _{j}\mathbf {e} _{i}=2\mathbf {e} _{i}\cdot \mathbf {e} _{j}=\delta _{i,j},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6181dc60316e706b92f68c3c674e3753043fa70)
which shows that the basis vectors mutually anticommute,

![Image 25: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{i}\mathbf {e} _{j}=-\mathbf {e} _{j}\mathbf {e} _{i},\quad i\neq j=1,\ldots ,n.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3d58a6fbf5045aaeeebae863427b742542d64ca5)
In contrast to the exterior product, the Clifford product of a vector with itself is not zero. To see this, compute the product

which yields

The set of multivectors constructed using Clifford's product yields an associative algebra known as a [Clifford algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_algebra "Clifford algebra"). Inner products with different properties can be used to construct different Clifford algebras.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector#cite_note-13)

The term _k-blade_ was used in _Clifford Algebra to Geometric Calculus_ (1984)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector#cite_note-14)

Multivectors play a central role in the mathematical formulation of physics known as geometric algebra. According to [David Hestenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Hestenes "David Hestenes"),

[Non-scalar] _k_-vectors are sometimes called _k-blades_ or, merely _blades_, to emphasize the fact that, in contrast to 0-vectors (scalars), they have "directional properties".[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector#cite_note-15)
In 2003 the term _blade_ for a multivector that can be written as the exterior product of [a scalar and] a set of vectors was used by C. Doran and A. Lasenby. Here, by the statement "Any multivector can be expressed as the sum of blades", scalars are implicitly defined as 0-blades.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector#cite_note-16)

In [geometric algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_algebra "Geometric algebra"), a multivector is defined to be the sum of different-grade [_k_-blades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blade_(geometry) "Blade (geometry)"), such as the summation of a [scalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(mathematics) "Scalar (mathematics)"), a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(geometric) "Vector (geometric)"), and a 2-vector.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector#cite_note-Rodrigues-17) A sum of only _k_-grade components is called a _k_-vector,[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector#cite_note-Sommer-18) or a _homogeneous_ multivector.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector#cite_note-Sanfeliu-19)

The highest grade element in a space is called a _[pseudoscalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudoscalar "Pseudoscalar")_.

If a given element is homogeneous of a grade _k_, then it is a _k_-vector, but not necessarily a _k_-blade. Such an element is a _k_-blade when it can be expressed as the exterior product of _k_ vectors. A geometric algebra generated by a four-dimensional vector space illustrates the point with an example: The sum of any two blades with one taken from the XY-plane and the other taken from the ZW-plane will form a 2-vector that is not a 2-blade. In a geometric algebra generated by a vector space of dimension 2 or 3, all sums of 2-blades may be written as a single 2-blade.

[![Image 28](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/N_vector_positive.svg/250px-N_vector_positive.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:N_vector_positive.svg)

Orientation defined by an ordered set of vectors.

[![Image 29](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5f/N_vector_negative.svg/250px-N_vector_negative.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:N_vector_negative.svg)

Reversed orientation corresponds to negating the exterior product.

Geometric interpretation of grade _n_ elements in a real exterior algebra for _n_ = 0 (signed point), 1 (directed line segment, or vector), 2 (oriented plane element), 3 (oriented volume). The exterior product of _n_ vectors can be visualized as any _n_-dimensional shape (e.g. _n_-[parallelotope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped#Parallelotope "Parallelepiped"), _n_-[ellipsoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid "Ellipsoid")); with magnitude ([hypervolume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypervolume "Hypervolume")), and [orientation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation_(vector_space) "Orientation (vector space)") defined by that on its (_n_ − 1)-dimensional boundary and on which side the interior is.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector#cite_note-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector#cite_note-21)

*   0-vectors are scalars;
*   1-vectors are vectors;
*   2-vectors are [bivectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bivector "Bivector");
*   (_n_ − 1)-vectors are [pseudovectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudovector "Pseudovector");
*   _n_-vectors are [pseudoscalars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudoscalar "Pseudoscalar").

In the presence of a [volume form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_form "Volume form") (such as given an [inner product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product "Inner product") and an orientation), pseudovectors and pseudoscalars can be identified with vectors and scalars, which is routine in [vector calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus "Vector calculus"), but without a volume form this cannot be done without making an arbitrary choice.

In the [algebra of physical space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra_of_physical_space "Algebra of physical space") (the geometric algebra of Euclidean 3-space, used as a model of (3+1)-spacetime), a sum of a scalar and a vector is called a [paravector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paravector "Paravector"), and represents a point in spacetime (the vector the space, the scalar the time).

A **bivector** is an element of the [antisymmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antisymmetric_tensor "Antisymmetric tensor")[tensor product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product "Tensor product") of a [tangent space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space "Tangent space") with itself.

In [geometric algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_algebra "Geometric algebra"), also, a **bivector** is a grade 2 element (a 2-vector) resulting from the [wedge product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wedge_product "Wedge product") of two vectors, and so it is geometrically an _oriented area_, in the same way a _vector_ is an oriented line segment. If **a** and **b** are two vectors, the bivector **a** ∧ **b** has

*   a [norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_(mathematics) "Norm (mathematics)") which is its area, given by ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \left\|\mathbf {a} \wedge \mathbf {b} \right\|=\left\|\mathbf {a} \right\|\,\left\|\mathbf {b} \right\|\,\sin(\phi _{a,b})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5987a96ec5784170f6fccc3431080b9104072637)
*   a direction: the plane where that area lies on, i.e., the plane determined by **a** and **b**, as long as they are linearly independent;
*   an orientation (out of two), determined by the order in which the originating vectors are multiplied.

Bivectors are connected to [pseudovectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudovector "Pseudovector"), and are used to represent rotations in geometric algebra.

As bivectors are elements of a vector space Λ 2 _V_ (where _V_ is a finite-dimensional vector space with dim _V_ = _n_), it makes sense to define an [inner product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product "Inner product") on this vector space as follows. First, write any element _F_ ∈ Λ 2 _V_ in terms of a basis (**e**_i_ ∧ **e**_j_)1 ≤ _i_<_j_ ≤ _n_ of Λ 2 _V_ as

![Image 31: {\displaystyle F=F^{ab}\mathbf {e} _{a}\wedge \mathbf {e} _{b}\quad (1\leq a<b\leq n),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/96c4490a49754e8eecb8ca86b8e0629380b0540c)
where the [Einstein summation convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_summation_convention "Einstein summation convention") is being used.

Now define a map G: Λ 2 _V_ × Λ 2 _V_ → **R** by insisting that

![Image 32: {\displaystyle G(F,H):=G_{abcd}F^{ab}H^{cd},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e55a9a0bb1eb3d649146263d4814f0dcbb4e2483)
where ![Image 33: {\displaystyle G_{abcd}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6763b5f508c9e3c7d5d35078d12cf42499599380) are a set of numbers.

Bivectors play many important roles in physics, for example, in the [classification of electromagnetic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classification_of_electromagnetic_fields "Classification of electromagnetic fields").
