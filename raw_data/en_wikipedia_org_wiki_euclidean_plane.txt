In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **Euclidean plane** is a [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") of [dimension two](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-dimensional_space "Two-dimensional space"), denoted ![Image 2: {\displaystyle {\textbf {E}}^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e8667dea0c8ec83f2ed680240ba70d6e1c1bcd8) or ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbb {E} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3c2c8e9a82f057e1a8f354057896167fe621734). It is a [geometric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_space "Geometric space") in which two [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") are required to determine the [position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_(geometry) "Position (geometry)") of each [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(mathematics) "Point (mathematics)"). It is an [affine space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space "Affine space"), which includes in particular the concept of [parallel lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_lines "Parallel lines"). It has also [metrical properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measurement "Measurement") induced by a [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance "Euclidean distance"), which allows to define [circles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle"), and [angle measurement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle").

A Euclidean plane with a chosen [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") is called a _[Cartesian plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian\_plane "Cartesian plane")_. The set ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd) of the ordered pairs of real numbers (the [real coordinate plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_coordinate_plane "Real coordinate plane")), equipped with the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product"), is often called _the_ Euclidean plane or _standard Euclidean plane_, since every Euclidean plane is [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphic "Isomorphic") to it.

Books I through IV and VI of [Euclid's Elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements "Euclid's Elements") dealt with two-dimensional geometry, developing such notions as similarity of shapes, the [Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem") (Proposition 47), equality of angles and [areas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area"), parallelism, the sum of the angles in a triangle, and the three cases in which triangles are "equal" (have the same area), among many other topics.

Later, the plane was described in a so-called _[Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian\_coordinate\_system "Cartesian coordinate system")_, a [coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") that specifies each [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)") uniquely in a [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(geometry) "Plane (geometry)") by a pair of [numerical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number "Number")_coordinates_, which are the [signed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive_and_negative_numbers "Positive and negative numbers") distances from the point to two fixed [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") directed lines, measured in the same [unit of length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_length "Unit length"). Each reference line is called a _coordinate axis_ or just _axis_ of the system, and the point where they meet is its _[origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin\_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)")_, usually at ordered pair (0,0). The coordinates can also be defined as the positions of the [perpendicular projections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_projection "Orthogonal projection") of the point onto the two axes, expressed as signed distances from the origin.

The idea of this system was developed in 1637 in writings by Descartes and independently by [Pierre de Fermat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_de_Fermat "Pierre de Fermat"), although Fermat also worked in three dimensions, and did not publish the discovery.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_plane#cite_note-1) Both authors used a single ([abscissa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abscissa "Abscissa")) axis in their treatments, with the lengths of [ordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinate "Ordinate") measured along lines not-necessarily-perpendicular to that axis.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_plane#cite_note-2) The concept of using a pair of fixed axes was introduced later, after Descartes' _[La Géométrie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/La\_G%C3%A9om%C3%A9trie "La Géométrie")_ was translated into Latin in 1649 by [Frans van Schooten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frans_van_Schooten "Frans van Schooten") and his students. These commentators introduced several concepts while trying to clarify the ideas contained in Descartes' work.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_plane#cite_note-3)

Later, the plane was thought of as a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)"), where any two points could be multiplied and, except for 0, divided. This was known as the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane"). The complex plane is sometimes called the Argand plane because it is used in Argand diagrams. These are named after [Jean-Robert Argand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Robert_Argand "Jean-Robert Argand") (1768–1822), although they were first described by Danish-Norwegian land surveyor and mathematician [Caspar Wessel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caspar_Wessel "Caspar Wessel") (1745–1818).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_plane#cite_note-4) Argand diagrams are frequently used to plot the positions of the [poles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pole_(complex_analysis) "Pole (complex analysis)") and [zeroes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_(complex_analysis) "Zero (complex analysis)") of a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_function "Mathematical function") in the complex plane.

"Plane coordinates" redirects here; not to be confused with [Coordinate plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_plane "Coordinate plane").

In mathematics, [analytic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_geometry "Analytic geometry") (also called Cartesian geometry) describes every point in two-dimensional space by means of two coordinates. Two perpendicular [coordinate axes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_axis "Coordinate axis") are given which cross each other at the [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)"). They are usually labeled _x_ and _y_. Relative to these axes, the position of any point in two-dimensional space is given by an ordered pair of real numbers, each number giving the distance of that point from the [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)") measured along the given axis, which is equal to the distance of that point from the other axis.

Another widely used coordinate system is the [polar coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinate_system "Polar coordinate system"), which specifies a point in terms of its distance from the origin and its angle relative to a rightward reference ray.

There are an infinitude of other curved shapes in two dimensions, notably including the [conic sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conic_section "Conic section"): the [ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse"), the [parabola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabola "Parabola"), and the [hyperbola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbola "Hyperbola").

Another mathematical way of viewing two-dimensional space is found in [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra"), where the idea of independence is crucial. The plane has two dimensions because the length of a [rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle "Rectangle") is independent of its width. In the technical language of linear algebra, the plane is two-dimensional because every point in the plane can be described by a linear combination of two independent [vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_vector "Coordinate vector").

### Line integrals and double integrals

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Euclidean_plane&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Line integrals and double integrals")]

For some [scalar field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field "Scalar field")_f_: _U_ ⊆ **R**_2_ → **R**, the line integral along a [piecewise smooth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piecewise_smooth "Piecewise smooth")[curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve")_C_ ⊂ _U_ is defined as

![Image 44: {\displaystyle \int \limits _{C}f\,ds=\int _{a}^{b}f(\mathbf {r} (t))|\mathbf {r} '(t)|\,dt,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/67195aa440cbabe3f4e94ae606ac48338aa43493)
where **r**: [a, b] → _C_ is an arbitrary [bijective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijective "Bijective")[parametrization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametric_equation "Parametric equation") of the curve _C_ such that **r**(_a_) and **r**(_b_) give the endpoints of _C_ and ![Image 45: {\displaystyle a<b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91a7698e4c7401bb321f97888b872b583a9e4642).

For a [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field")**F**: _U_ ⊆ **R**_2_ → **R**_2_, the line integral along a [piecewise smooth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piecewise_smooth "Piecewise smooth")[curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve")_C_ ⊂ _U_, in the direction of **r**, is defined as

### Fundamental theorem of line integrals

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Euclidean_plane&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Fundamental theorem of line integrals")]

The [fundamental theorem of line integrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_line_integrals "Fundamental theorem of line integrals") says that a [line integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_integral "Line integral") through a [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient") field can be evaluated by evaluating the original scalar field at the endpoints of the curve.

Let ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \varphi :U\subseteq \mathbb {R} ^{2}\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e1565d300a13e13812e9e9841d7e3ae6fee847db). Then

![Image 50: {\displaystyle \varphi \left(\mathbf {q} \right)-\varphi \left(\mathbf {p} \right)=\int _{\gamma [\mathbf {p} ,\mathbf {q} ]}\nabla \varphi (\mathbf {r} )\cdot d\mathbf {r} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a2a21102b51e23ffb1a4d827e19f973c2b9baa5a)
with **p**, **q** the endpoints of the curve γ.

Let _C_ be a positively [oriented](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve_orientation "Curve orientation"), [piecewise smooth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piecewise_smooth "Piecewise smooth"), [simple closed curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_closed_curve "Simple closed curve") in a [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(mathematics) "Plane (mathematics)"), and let _D_ be the region bounded by _C_. If _L_ and _M_ are functions of (_x_, _y_) defined on an [open region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set") containing _D_ and have [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function")[partial derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivatives "Partial derivatives") there, then[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_plane#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_plane#cite_note-8)

![Image 51: {\displaystyle \oint _{C}(L\,dx+M\,dy)=\iint _{D}\left({\frac {\partial M}{\partial x}}-{\frac {\partial L}{\partial y}}\right)\,dx\,dy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a510273c201e5680f7138f76b2c7c6aab6193ddf)
where the path of integration along C is [counterclockwise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Counterclockwise "Counterclockwise").

In [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology"), the plane is characterized as being the unique [contractible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contractible "Contractible")[2-manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2-manifold "2-manifold").

Its dimension is characterized by the fact that removing a point from the plane leaves a space that is connected, but not [simply connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simply_connected "Simply connected").

In [graph theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_theory "Graph theory"), a [planar graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planar_graph "Planar graph") is a [graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_(discrete_mathematics) "Graph (discrete mathematics)") that can be [embedded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_embedding "Graph embedding") in the plane, i.e., it can be drawn on the plane in such a way that its edges intersect only at their endpoints. In other words, it can be drawn in such a way that no edges cross each other.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_plane#cite_note-9) Such a drawing is called a _plane graph_ or _planar embedding of the graph_. A plane graph can be defined as a planar graph with a mapping from every node to a point on a plane, and from every edge to a [plane curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_curve "Plane curve") on that plane, such that the extreme points of each curve are the points mapped from its end nodes, and all curves are disjoint except on their extreme points.
