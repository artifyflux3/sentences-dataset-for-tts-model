Geometry where the axiom of Archimedes is negated

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), **non-Archimedean geometry**[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Archimedean_geometry#cite_note-1) is any of a number of forms of [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") in which the [axiom of Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom_of_Archimedes "Axiom of Archimedes") is negated. An example of such a geometry is the [Dehn plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dehn_plane "Dehn plane"). Non-Archimedean geometries may, as the example indicates, have properties significantly different from [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry").

There are two senses in which the term may be used, referring to geometries over [fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(algebra) "Field (algebra)") which violate one of the two senses of the [Archimedean property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedean_property "Archimedean property") (i.e. with respect to order or magnitude).

Geometry over a non-Archimedean ordered field
---------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Non-Archimedean_geometry&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Geometry over a non-Archimedean ordered field")]

The first sense of the term is the geometry over a [non-Archimedean ordered field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Archimedean_ordered_field "Non-Archimedean ordered field"), or a subset thereof. The aforementioned Dehn plane takes the self-product of the finite portion of a certain non-Archimedean ordered field based on the field of [rational functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_function "Rational function"). In this geometry, there are significant differences from Euclidean geometry; in particular, there are infinitely many parallels to a straight line through a point—so the [parallel postulate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_postulate "Parallel postulate") fails—but the sum of the angles of a triangle is still a straight angle.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Archimedean_geometry#cite_note-2)

Intuitively, in such a space, the points on a line cannot be described by the real numbers or a subset thereof, and there exist segments of "infinite" or "[infinitesimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal "Infinitesimal")" length.

Geometry over a non-Archimedean valued field
--------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Non-Archimedean_geometry&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Geometry over a non-Archimedean valued field")]

The second sense of the term is the [metric geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space") over a non-Archimedean [valued field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value_(algebra) "Absolute value (algebra)"),[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Archimedean_geometry#cite_note-3) or [ultrametric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultrametric_space "Ultrametric space"). In such a space, even more contradictions to Euclidean geometry result. For example, all triangles are isosceles, and overlapping [balls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ball_(mathematics) "Ball (mathematics)") nest. An example of such a space is the [p-adic numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P-adic_numbers "P-adic numbers").

Intuitively, in such a space, distances fail to "add up" or "accumulate".
