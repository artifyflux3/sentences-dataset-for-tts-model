Earth's atmosphere from space, showing a blue layer at the [stratosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stratosphere "Stratosphere"), above the clouds of the [troposphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Troposphere "Troposphere"). The [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon") is visible as a crescent in the background.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-1)

The **atmosphere of Earth** consists of a layer of mixed [gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas "Gas") that is retained by [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity_of_Earth "Gravity of Earth"), surrounding the [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth")'s [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_surface "Planetary surface"). It contains variable quantities of suspended [aerosols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerosol "Aerosol") and [particulates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particulate "Particulate") that create [weather](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weather "Weather") features such as [clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cloud "Cloud") and [hazes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haze "Haze"). The atmosphere serves as a protective buffer between the Earth's surface and [outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space "Outer space"). It shields the surface from most [meteoroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteoroid "Meteoroid") and ultraviolet [solar radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_irradiance "Solar irradiance"), reduces [diurnal temperature variation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diurnal_temperature_variation "Diurnal temperature variation") – the temperature extremes between [day](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daytime "Daytime") and [night](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Night "Night"), and keeps it warm through heat retention via the [greenhouse effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_effect "Greenhouse effect"). The atmosphere redistributes heat and moisture among different regions via [air currents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_current "Air current"), and provides the [chemical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_chemistry "Atmospheric chemistry") and [climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate "Climate") conditions that allow [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life "Life") to exist and [evolve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution "Evolution") on Earth.

By [mole fraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mole_fraction "Mole fraction") (i.e., by quantity of [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule")), dry air contains 78.08% [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen"), 20.95% [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen"), 0.93% [argon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argon "Argon"), 0.04% [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide_in_Earth%27s_atmosphere "Carbon dioxide in Earth's atmosphere"), and small amounts of other [trace gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trace_gas "Trace gas") (see [Composition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#Composition) below for more detail). Air also contains a variable amount of [water vapor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_vapor#Water_vapor_in_Earth's_atmosphere "Water vapor"), on average around 1% at sea level, and 0.4% over the entire atmosphere.

Earth's primordial atmosphere consisted of gases [accreted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics) "Accretion (astrophysics)") from the [solar nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_nebula "Solar nebula"), but the composition changed significantly over time, affected by many factors such as [volcanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcanism "Volcanism"), [outgassing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outgassing "Outgassing"), [impact events](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impact_event "Impact event"), [weathering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weathering "Weathering") and the [evolution of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution_of_life "Evolution of life") (particularly the [photoautotrophs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoautotroph "Photoautotroph")). In the present day, [human](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human "Human") activity has [contributed to atmospheric changes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment#Impacts_through_the_atmosphere "Human impact on the environment"), such as [climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthropogenic_climate_change "Anthropogenic climate change") (mainly through [deforestation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deforestation "Deforestation") and [fossil fuel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil_fuel "Fossil fuel")-related [global warming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_warming "Global warming")), [ozone depletion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone_depletion "Ozone depletion") and [acid deposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid_deposition "Acid deposition").

The atmosphere has a mass of about 5.15×10 18 kg,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-2) three quarters of which is within about 11 km (6.8 mi; 36,000 ft) of the surface. The atmosphere becomes thinner with increasing altitude, with no definite boundary between the atmosphere and [outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space "Outer space"). The [Kármán line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line "Kármán line") at 100 km (62 mi) is often used as a conventional definition of the [edge of space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_of_space "Edge of space"). Several [layers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#Stratification) can be distinguished in the atmosphere based on characteristics such as temperature and composition, namely the [troposphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Troposphere "Troposphere"), [stratosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stratosphere "Stratosphere"), [mesosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesosphere "Mesosphere"), [thermosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermosphere "Thermosphere") (formally the [ionosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionosphere "Ionosphere")) and [exosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exosphere "Exosphere"). Air composition, temperature and [atmospheric pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure "Atmospheric pressure") vary with [altitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altitude "Altitude"). Air suitable for use in [photosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis "Photosynthesis") by [terrestrial plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrestrial_plant "Terrestrial plant") and [respiration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiration_(physiology) "Respiration (physiology)") of [terrestrial animals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrestrial_animal "Terrestrial animal") is found within the troposphere.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-3)

The study of Earth's atmosphere and its processes is called [atmospheric science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_science "Atmospheric science") (aerology), and includes multiple subfields, such as [climatology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climatology "Climatology") and [atmospheric physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_physics "Atmospheric physics"). Early pioneers in the field include [Léon Teisserenc de Bort](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%A9on_Teisserenc_de_Bort "Léon Teisserenc de Bort") and [Richard Assmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Assmann "Richard Assmann").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-4) The study of the historic atmosphere is called [paleoclimatology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paleoclimatology "Paleoclimatology").

Composition
-----------

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Atmosphere_gas_proportions.svg/250px-Atmosphere_gas_proportions.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Atmosphere_gas_proportions.svg)

Composition of Earth's atmosphere by molecular count, excluding water vapor. Lower pie represents trace gases that together compose about 0.0434% of the atmosphere.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Allens-2002-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-CO2-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-methane-7)

The three major constituents of Earth's atmosphere are [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen"), [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen"), and [argon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argon "Argon"). Water vapor accounts for roughly 0.25% of the atmosphere by mass. In the lower atmosphere, the concentration of water vapor (a greenhouse gas) varies significantly from around 10 ppm by mole fraction in the coldest portions of the atmosphere to as much as 5% by mole fraction in hot, humid air masses, and concentrations of other atmospheric gases are typically quoted in terms of dry air (without water vapor).[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-WallaceHobbs-8): 8  The remaining gases are often referred to as trace gases,[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-9) among which are other [greenhouse gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas "Greenhouse gas"), principally carbon dioxide, methane, nitrous oxide, and ozone. Besides argon, other [noble gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noble_gas "Noble gas"), [neon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neon "Neon"), [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium"), [krypton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krypton "Krypton"), and [xenon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xenon "Xenon") are also present. Filtered air includes trace amounts of many other [chemical compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_compound "Chemical compound").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Graedel_et_al_2012-10)

Many substances of natural origin may be present in locally and seasonally variable small amounts as [aerosols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerosol "Aerosol") in an unfiltered air sample, including [dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dust "Dust") of mineral and organic composition, [pollen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pollen "Pollen") and [spores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spores "Spores"), [sea spray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_spray "Sea spray"), and [volcanic ash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcanic_ash "Volcanic ash").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Colbeck_Lazaridis_2010-11) Various industrial [pollutants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pollutant "Pollutant") also may be present as gases or aerosols, such as [chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine") (elemental or in compounds),[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Hao_Wang_et_al_2017-12)[fluorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorine "Fluorine") compounds,[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Faust_2023-13) and elemental [mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(element) "Mercury (element)") vapor.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Pacyna_et_al_2016-14) Sulfur compounds such as [hydrogen sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide "Hydrogen sulfide") and [sulfur dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide "Sulfur dioxide") (SO 2) may be derived from natural sources or from industrial air pollution.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Colbeck_Lazaridis_2010-11)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Kumar_Francisco_2017-15)

The average [molecular weight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_weight "Molecular weight") of dry air, which can be used to calculate densities or to convert between mole fraction and [mass fraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_fraction_(chemistry) "Mass fraction (chemistry)"), is about 28.946[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-M%C3%B6ller2003-17) or 28.964[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-18)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Allens-2002-5): 257 g/mol. This is decreased when the air is humid.

Up to an altitude of around 100 km (62 mi), atmospheric [turbulence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turbulence "Turbulence") mixes the component gases so that their relative concentrations remain the same. There exists a transition zone from roughly 80 to 120 km (50 to 75 mi) where this turbulent mixing gradually yields to [molecular diffusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_diffusion "Molecular diffusion"). The latter process forms the [heterosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heterosphere "Heterosphere") where the relative concentration of lighter gases increase with altitude.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Schlatter_2009-19)

Stratification
--------------

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Earth%27s_atmosphere.svg/250px-Earth%27s_atmosphere.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Earth%27s_atmosphere.svg)

A prism cross-section of the Earth's atmosphere. The strata are drawn to scale, but individual features are not.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-20): 322 

In general, air pressure and density decrease with altitude in the atmosphere. However, temperature has a more complicated profile with altitude and may remain relatively constant or even increase with altitude in some regions (see the [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#Temperature) section).[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Champion_et_al_1985-21) Because the general pattern of the temperature/altitude profile, or [lapse rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lapse_rate "Lapse rate"), is constant and measurable by means of instrumented [balloon soundings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weather_balloon "Weather balloon"), the temperature behavior provides a useful metric to distinguish atmospheric layers. This _atmospheric stratification_ divides the Earth's atmosphere into five main layers with these typical altitude ranges:[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Buis_2024-22)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-23)

*   Exosphere: 700–10,000 km (435–6,214 mi)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-UCAR-2011-24)
*   Thermosphere: 80–700 km (50–435 mi)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-thermosphere-25)
*   Mesosphere: 50–80 km (31–50 mi)
*   Stratosphere: 12–50 km (7–31 mi)
*   Troposphere: 0–12 km (0–7 mi)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-tropopauseheight-26)

### Exosphere

The exosphere is the outermost layer of Earth's atmosphere (though it is so tenuous that some scientists consider it to be part of interplanetary space rather than part of the atmosphere). It extends from the [thermopause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermopause "Thermopause") (also known as the "exobase") at the top of the [thermosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermosphere "Thermosphere") to a poorly defined boundary with the [solar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind "Solar wind") and [interplanetary medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium "Interplanetary medium"). The altitude of the exobase varies from about 500 kilometres (310 mi; 1,600,000 ft) to about 1,000 kilometres (620 mi) in times of higher incoming solar radiation.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-UCAR-27)

The upper limit varies depending on the definition. Various authorities consider it to end at about 10,000 kilometres (6,200 mi)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-28) or about 190,000 kilometres (120,000 mi)—about halfway to the moon, where the influence of Earth's gravity is about the same as [radiation pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation_pressure "Radiation pressure") from sunlight.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-UCAR-27) The [geocorona](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geocorona "Geocorona") visible in the far ultraviolet (caused by neutral hydrogen) extends to at least 100,000 kilometres (62,000 mi).[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-UCAR-27)

This layer is mainly composed of extremely low densities of hydrogen, with limited amounts of helium, carbon dioxide, and [nascent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nascent_state_(chemistry) "Nascent state (chemistry)") oxygen closer to the exobase.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Singh_2020-29) The atoms and molecules are so far apart that they can travel hundreds of kilometres without colliding with one another.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Champion_et_al_1985-21): 14–4  Thus, the exosphere no longer behaves like a gas, and the particles constantly [escape into space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_escape "Atmospheric escape"). These free-moving particles follow [ballistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ballistics "Ballistics")[trajectories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trajectory "Trajectory") and may migrate in and out of the [magnetosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetosphere "Magnetosphere") or the solar wind. Every second, the Earth loses about 3 kg of hydrogen, 50 g of helium, and much smaller amounts of other constituents.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Catling200922-30)

The exosphere is too far above Earth for [meteorological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorology "Meteorology") phenomena to be possible. The exosphere contains many of the [artificial satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite "Satellite") that [orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit "Orbit") Earth.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Liou_Johnson_2008-31)

### Thermosphere

The thermosphere is the second-highest layer of Earth's atmosphere. It extends from the mesopause (which separates it from the mesosphere) at an altitude of about 80 km (50 mi) up to the [thermopause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermopause "Thermopause") at an altitude range of 500–1000 km (310–620 mi). The height of the thermopause varies considerably due to changes in solar activity.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-thermosphere-25) The passage of the dusk and dawn [solar terminator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terminator_(solar) "Terminator (solar)") creates background density perturbations up to a factor of two through this layer, forming a dominant feature in this region.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Fitzpatrick_et_al_2025-32) Because the thermopause lies at the lower boundary of the exosphere, it is also referred to as the [exobase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exobase "Exobase"). Overlapping the thermosphere, from 50 to 600 kilometres (31 to 373 mi) above Earth's surface, is the [ionosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionosphere "Ionosphere") – a region of enhanced [plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)") density.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Blaunstein_Plohotniuc_2008-33)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-NOAA_layers-34)

The temperature of the thermosphere gradually increases with height and can rise as high as 1500°C (2700°F), though the gas molecules are so far apart that its [temperature in the usual sense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases "Kinetic theory of gases") is not very meaningful. This temperature increase is caused by absorption of [ionizing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion "Ion")[UV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet") and [X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray") emission from the Sun.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-NOAA_layers-34)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-NASA_1108-35) The air is so rarefied that an individual molecule (of [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen"), for example) travels an average of 1 kilometre (0.62 mi; 3300 ft) between collisions with other molecules.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-36) Although the thermosphere has a high proportion of molecules with high energy, it would not feel hot to a human in direct contact, because its density is too low to conduct a significant amount of energy to or from the skin.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-NOAA_layers-34)

This layer is completely cloudless and free of water vapor. However, non-hydrometeorological phenomena such as the [aurora borealis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aurora_borealis "Aurora borealis") and [aurora australis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aurora_australis "Aurora australis") are occasionally seen in the thermosphere at an altitude of around 100 km (62 mi).[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Lodders_Fegley_2015-37) The colors of the aurora are linked to the properties of the atmosphere at the altitude they occur. The most common is the green aurora, which comes from atomic oxygen in the 1 S state, and occurs at altitudes from 120 to 400 km (75 to 250 mi).[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-NOAA_Aurora-38) The [International Space Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Space_Station "International Space Station") orbits in the thermosphere, between 370 and 460 km (230 and 290 mi).[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-ISS-39) It is this layer where many of the satellites orbiting the Earth are present.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Liou_Johnson_2008-31)

### Mesosphere

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a5/ISS-46_Soyuz_TMA-17M_reentry.jpg/330px-ISS-46_Soyuz_TMA-17M_reentry.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:ISS-46_Soyuz_TMA-17M_reentry.jpg)

[Afterglow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Afterglow "Afterglow") of the [troposphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Troposphere "Troposphere") (orange), the [stratosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stratosphere "Stratosphere") (blue) and the mesosphere (dark) at which [atmospheric entry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_entry "Atmospheric entry") begins, leaving smoke trails, such as in this case of a [spacecraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacecraft "Spacecraft") reentry

The mesosphere is the third highest layer of Earth's atmosphere, occupying the region above the stratosphere and below the thermosphere. It extends from the stratopause at an altitude of about 50 km (31 mi) to the mesopause at 80–85 km (50–53 mi) above sea level.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-NOAA_layers-34) Temperatures drop with increasing altitude to the [mesopause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesopause "Mesopause") that marks the top of this middle layer of the atmosphere. It is the coldest place on Earth and has an average temperature around −85[°C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celsius "Celsius") (−120[°F](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fahrenheit "Fahrenheit"); 190[K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin")).[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-40)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-41) Because the atmosphere absorbs sound waves at a rate that is proportional to the square of the [frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency"), audible sounds from the ground do not reach the mesosphere. Infrasonic waves can reach this altitude, but they are difficult to emit at a high power level.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Yang_2016-42)

Just below the mesopause, the air is so cold that even the very scarce water vapor at this altitude can condense into polar-mesospheric [noctilucent clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noctilucent_cloud "Noctilucent cloud") of ice particles. These are the highest clouds in the atmosphere and may be visible to the naked eye if sunlight reflects off them about an hour or two after sunset or similarly before sunrise. They are most readily visible when the Sun is around 4 to 16 degrees below the horizon.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Gadsden_Parvianinen_2006-43)

Lightning-induced discharges known as [transient luminous events](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transient_luminous_event "Transient luminous event") (TLEs) occasionally form in the mesosphere above tropospheric [thunderclouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thunderclouds "Thunderclouds").[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Sato_2015-44) The mesosphere is also the layer where most [meteors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor "Meteor") and [satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite "Satellite") burn up upon [atmospheric entrance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_entry "Atmospheric entry").[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-NOAA_layers-34)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Karahan_et_al_2025-45) It is too high above Earth to be accessible to jet-powered aircraft and balloons, and too low to permit orbital spacecraft. The mesosphere is mainly accessed by [sounding rockets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sounding_rocket "Sounding rocket") and [rocket-powered aircraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket-powered_aircraft "Rocket-powered aircraft").[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Heatwole_2024-46)

### Stratosphere

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Kittinger-jump.jpg/250px-Kittinger-jump.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Kittinger-jump.jpg)

In 1960, [Joseph Kittinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Kittinger "Joseph Kittinger") set a record by parachuting from a gondola at 31.3 km (19.4 mi)

The stratosphere is the second-lowest layer of Earth's atmosphere. It lies above the troposphere and is separated from it by the [tropopause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropopause "Tropopause"). This layer extends from the top of the troposphere at roughly 12 km (7.5 mi) above Earth's surface to the [stratopause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stratopause "Stratopause") at an altitude of about 50 to 55 km (31 to 34 mi).[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Buis_2024-22) 99% of the total mass of the atmosphere lies below 30 km (19 mi),[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Holloway_Wayne_2015-47) and the atmospheric pressure at the top of the stratosphere is roughly 1/1000 the [pressure at sea level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure_at_sea_level "Pressure at sea level").[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Clouds_2025-48) It contains the [ozone layer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone_layer "Ozone layer"), which is the part of Earth's atmosphere that contains relatively high concentrations of that gas.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Saha_2012-49)

The stratosphere defines a layer in which temperatures rise with increasing altitude. This rise in temperature is caused by the absorption of [ultraviolet radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet_radiation "Ultraviolet radiation") (UV) from the Sun by the ozone layer, which restricts turbulence and mixing. Although the temperature may be −80°C (−110°F; 190 K) at the tropopause, the top of the stratosphere is much warmer, and may be just below 0°C.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-stratopause-50)[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Saha_2012-49)

The stratospheric temperature profile creates very stable atmospheric conditions, so the stratosphere lacks the weather-producing air turbulence that is so prevalent in the troposphere. Consequently, the stratosphere is almost completely free of clouds and other forms of weather.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Saha_2012-49) However, polar stratospheric or [nacreous clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nacreous_cloud "Nacreous cloud") are occasionally seen in the lower part of this layer of the atmosphere where the air is coldest.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Salby_1996-51) The stratosphere is the highest layer that can be accessed by [jet-powered aircraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jet-powered_aircraft "Jet-powered aircraft").[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Filippone_2012-52)

### Troposphere

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f6/ISS-47_Islands_In_The_Sky%2C_Indonesia.jpg/330px-ISS-47_Islands_In_The_Sky%2C_Indonesia.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:ISS-47_Islands_In_The_Sky,_Indonesia.jpg)

Earth from orbit, showing sunlight filtered into a reddish hue by the troposphere, while casting shadows from different [cloud types](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cloud_types "Cloud types"). The [stratosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stratosphere "Stratosphere") forms a thin band of [blue scattered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering "Rayleigh scattering") sunlight along the horizon.

The troposphere is the lowest layer of Earth's atmosphere. It extends from Earth's surface to an average height of about 12 km (7.5 mi), although this [altitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altitude "Altitude") varies from about 9 km (5.6 mi) at the [geographic poles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographic_pole "Geographic pole") to 17 km (11 mi) at the [Equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator "Equator"),[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-tropopauseheight-26) with some variation due to weather. The troposphere is bounded above by the [tropopause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropopause "Tropopause"), a boundary marked in most places by a [temperature inversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature_inversion "Temperature inversion") (i.e. a layer of relatively warm air above a colder one), and in others by a zone that is [isothermal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotherm_(contour_line) "Isotherm (contour line)") with height.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-53)[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-54)

Although variations do occur, the temperature usually declines with increasing altitude in the troposphere because the troposphere is mostly heated through energy transfer from the surface. Thus, the lowest part of the troposphere (i.e. Earth's surface) is typically the warmest section of the troposphere. This promotes vertical mixing (hence, the origin of its name in the Greek word τρόπος, _tropos_, meaning "turn").[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Frederick_2008-55) The troposphere contains roughly 80% of the [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") of Earth's atmosphere.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-56) The troposphere is denser than all its overlying layers because a larger atmospheric weight sits on top of the troposphere and causes it to be more severely compressed. Fifty percent of the total mass of the atmosphere is located in the lower 5.5 km (3.4 mi) of the troposphere.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Holloway_Wayne_2015-47)

Nearly all atmospheric water vapor or moisture is found in the troposphere, so it is the layer where most of Earth's weather takes place. The ability of the atmosphere to retain water decreases as the temperature declines, so 90% of the water vapor is held in the lower part of the troposphere.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Singh_2001-57) It has basically all the weather-associated cloud genus types generated by active wind circulation, although very tall cumulonimbus thunder clouds can penetrate the tropopause from below and rise into the lower part of the stratosphere.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Wang_et_al_2009-58) Most conventional [aviation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aviation "Aviation") activity takes place in the troposphere, and it is the only layer accessible by [propeller-driven aircraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propeller_(aeronautics) "Propeller (aeronautics)").[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Filippone_2012-52)[Contrails](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contrail "Contrail") are formed from jet engine water emission at altitudes where the atmospheric temperature is about −53°C (−63°F); typically around 7.7 km (4.8 mi) for modern engines.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Tiwary_Williams_2018-59)

### Other layers

Within the five principal layers above, which are largely determined by temperature, several secondary layers may be distinguished by other properties:

*   The [ozone layer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone_layer "Ozone layer") is contained within the stratosphere. In this layer [ozone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone "Ozone") reaches a peak concentration of 15 parts per million at an altitude of 32 km (20 mi), which is much higher than in the lower atmosphere but still very small compared to the main components of the atmosphere.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-60) It is mainly located in the lower portion of the stratosphere from about 15–35 km (9.3–21.7 mi),[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Allens-2002-5): 260  though the thickness varies seasonally and geographically. About 90% of the ozone in Earth's atmosphere is contained in the stratosphere.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-NOAA_Ozone-61)
*   The [ionosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionosphere "Ionosphere") is a region of the atmosphere that is ionized by solar radiation. It plays a significant role in [auroras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy) "Aurora (astronomy)"), [airglow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airglow "Airglow"), and [space weather](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_weather "Space weather") phenomenon.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Newell_et_al_2001-62)[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Basavaiah_2012-63) During daytime hours, it stretches from 50 to 1,000 km (31 to 621 mi) and includes the mesosphere, thermosphere, and parts of the exosphere. However, ionization in the mesosphere largely ceases during the night.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-UCAR_ION-64) The ionosphere forms the inner edge of the [plasmasphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasmasphere "Plasmasphere") – the inner [magnetosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetosphere "Magnetosphere").[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-plasmasphere-65) It has practical importance because it influences, for example, radio propagation on Earth.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Atiq_2018-66)
*   The [homosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homosphere "Homosphere") and [heterosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heterosphere "Heterosphere") are defined by whether the atmospheric gases are well mixed. The surface-based homosphere includes the troposphere, stratosphere, mesosphere, and the lowest part of the thermosphere, where the chemical composition of the atmosphere does not depend on molecular weight because the gases are mixed by turbulence.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-67) This relatively homogeneous layer ends at the _[turbopause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turbopause "Turbopause")_ found at about 100 km (62 mi; 330,000 ft),[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Schlatter_2009-19) the very [edge of space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line "Kármán line") itself as accepted by the [FAI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Federation_Aeronautique_Internationale "Federation Aeronautique Internationale"), which places it about 20 km (12 mi; 66,000 ft) above the mesopause.

Above this altitude lies the heterosphere, which includes the exosphere and most of the thermosphere. Here, the chemical composition varies with altitude. This is because the [distance that particles can move without colliding with one another](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_free_path "Mean free path") is large compared with the size of motions that cause mixing. This allows the gases to stratify by molecular weight,[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Schlatter_2009-19) with the heavier ones, such as oxygen and nitrogen, present only near the bottom of the heterosphere. The upper part of the heterosphere is composed almost completely of hydrogen, the lightest element.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-thought-68)
*   The [planetary boundary layer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_boundary_layer "Planetary boundary layer") is the part of the troposphere that is closest to Earth's surface and is directly affected by it, mainly through [turbulent diffusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turbulence "Turbulence"). During the day the planetary boundary layer usually is well-mixed, whereas at night it becomes stably stratified with weak or intermittent mixing. The depth of the planetary boundary layer ranges from as little as about 100 metres (330 ft) on clear, calm nights to 1,000–1,500 m (3,300–4,900 ft) or more during the afternoon.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-PBL-69)

The average temperature of the atmosphere at Earth's surface is 14°C (57°F; 287 K)[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-70) or 15°C (59°F; 288 K),[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-71) depending on the reference.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-72)[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-73)[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-74)

Physical properties
-------------------

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Comparison_US_standard_atmosphere_1962.svg/250px-Comparison_US_standard_atmosphere_1962.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Comparison_US_standard_atmosphere_1962.svg)

Comparison of the 1962 [US Standard Atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/US_Standard_Atmosphere "US Standard Atmosphere") graph of [geometric altitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_models#Geopotential_altitude "Atmospheric models") against [air density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_density "Air density"), [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure"), the [speed of sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_sound "Speed of sound") and [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature") with approximate altitudes of various objects.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-75)

### Pressure and thickness

The average atmospheric pressure at sea level is defined by the [International Standard Atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Standard_Atmosphere "International Standard Atmosphere") as 101325 [pascals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit) "Pascal (unit)") (760.00[Torr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torr "Torr"); 14.6959[psi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pound_per_square_inch "Pound per square inch"); 760.00[mmHg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millimetre_of_mercury "Millimetre of mercury")).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Allens-2002-5): 257  This is sometimes referred to as a unit of [standard atmospheres (atm)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit) "Atmosphere (unit)"). Total atmospheric mass is 5.1480×10 18 kg (1.13494×10 19 lb),[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-76) about 2.5% less than would be inferred from the average sea-level pressure and Earth's area of 51007.2 mega[hectares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hectare "Hectare"),[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Allens-2002-5): 240  this portion being displaced by Earth's mountainous terrain. Atmospheric pressure is the total weight of the air above unit area at the point where the pressure is measured. Thus air pressure varies with location and [weather](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low-pressure_area "Low-pressure area").

Air pressure [decreases exponentially](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_decay "Exponential decay") with altitude at a rate that depends on the air temperature. The rate of decrease is determined by a temperature-dependent parameter called the [scale height](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scale_height "Scale height"): for each increase in altitude by this height, the pressure decreases by a factor of _[e](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E\_(mathematical\_constant) "E (mathematical constant)")_ (the base of [natural logarithms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_logarithms "Natural logarithms"), approximately 2.718). For Earth, this value is typically 5.5 to 6 km for altitudes up to around 80 km (50 mi).[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Daniel_2002-77) However, the atmosphere is more accurately modeled with a customized equation for each layer that takes gradients of temperature, molecular composition, solar radiation and gravity into account. At heights over 100 km, the atmosphere is not well mixed, so each chemical species has its own scale height. At altitudes of 200 to 300 km, the combined scale height is 20 to 30 km.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Daniel_2002-77)

The mass of Earth's atmosphere is distributed approximately as follows:[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-78)

*   50% is below 5.6 km (18,000 ft)
*   90% is below 16 km (52,000 ft)
*   99.99997% is below 100 km (62 mi; 330,000 ft), the [Kármán line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%A1rm%C3%A1n_line "Kármán line"). By international convention, this marks the beginning of space where human travelers are considered [astronauts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronaut "Astronaut").

By comparison, the summit of [Mount Everest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Everest "Mount Everest") is at 8,848 m (29,029 ft); commercial [airliners](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airliners "Airliners") typically cruise between 9 and 12 km (30,000 and 38,000 ft),[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Sforza_2014-79) where the lower density and temperature of the air improve fuel economy; [weather balloons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weather_balloons "Weather balloons") reach about 35 km (115,000 ft);[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Kr%C3%A4uchi_et_al_2016-80) and the highest [X-15](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_X-15 "North American X-15") flight in 1963 reached 108.0 km (354,300 ft).

Even above the Kármán line, significant atmospheric effects such as [auroras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy) "Aurora (astronomy)") still occur.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Lodders_Fegley_2015-37)[Meteors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteors "Meteors") begin to glow in this region,[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-NOAA_layers-34) though the larger ones may not burn up until they penetrate more deeply. The various layers of Earth's [ionosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionosphere "Ionosphere"), important to [HF radio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HF_radio "HF radio") propagation, begin below 100 km and extend beyond 500 km. By comparison, the [International Space Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Space_Station "International Space Station") typically orbit at 370–460 km,[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-ISS-39) within the [F-layer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/F-layer "F-layer") of the ionosphere,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Allens-2002-5): 271  where they encounter enough [atmospheric drag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_drag "Atmospheric drag") to require reboosts every few months, otherwise [orbital decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_decay "Orbital decay") will occur, resulting in a return to Earth.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-ISS-39) Depending on solar activity, satellites can experience noticeable atmospheric drag at altitudes as high as 600–800 km.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Pisacane_2005-81)

### Temperature

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/02/Profil_temperature_atmosphere.png/250px-Profil_temperature_atmosphere.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Profil_temperature_atmosphere.png)

Temperature change by altitude

Starting at sea level, the temperature decreases with altitude until reaching the [stratosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stratosphere "Stratosphere") at around 11 km. Above, the temperature stabilizes over a large vertical distance. Starting above about 20 km, the temperature increases with height, due to heating within the ozone layer caused by the capture of significant [ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet") radiation from the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun") by the [molecular oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_oxygen "Molecular oxygen") and ozone gas in this region. A second region of increasing temperature with altitude occurs at very high altitudes, in the aptly-named [thermosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermosphere "Thermosphere") above 90 km.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-NOAA_layers-34)

During the night, the ground radiates more energy than it gains from the atmosphere. As energy is conducted from the nearby atmosphere to the cooler ground, it creates a [temperature inversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inversion_(meteorology) "Inversion (meteorology)") where the local temperature increases with altitude up to around 1,000 m.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-82)

#### Speed of sound

Because in an [ideal gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas "Ideal gas") of constant composition the [speed of sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_sound "Speed of sound") depends only on temperature and not on pressure or density, the speed of sound in the atmosphere with altitude takes on the form of the complicated temperature profile (see illustration to the right), and does not mirror altitudinal changes in density or pressure.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-SoS-83) For example, at sea level the speed of sound is 340 m/s. At the average temperature of the stratosphere, –60°C, the speed of sound decreases to 290 m/s.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Wang_2023-84)

### Density and mass

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Atmosphere_model.png/250px-Atmosphere_model.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Atmosphere_model.png)

Temperature and mass density against altitude from the [NRLMSISE-00](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NRLMSISE-00 "NRLMSISE-00")[standard atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Standard_Atmosphere "International Standard Atmosphere") model (the eight dotted lines in each "decade" are at the eight cubes 8, 27, 64, ..., 729)

The density of air at sea level is about 1.29 kg/m 3 (1.29 g/L, 0.00129 g/cm 3).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Allens-2002-5): 257  Density is not measured directly but is calculated from measurements of temperature, pressure and humidity using the equation of state for air (a form of the [ideal gas law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas_law "Ideal gas law")). Atmospheric density decreases as the altitude increases. This variation can be approximately modeled using the [barometric formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barometric_formula "Barometric formula").[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Hall_2021-85) More sophisticated models are used to predict the orbital decay of satellites.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Kumar_et_al_2022-86)

The average mass of the atmosphere is about 5 quadrillion (5×10 15) [tonnes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tonne "Tonne") or 1/1,200,000 the mass of Earth. According to the American [National Center for Atmospheric Research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Center_for_Atmospheric_Research "National Center for Atmospheric Research"), "The total mean mass of the atmosphere is 5.1480×10 18 kg with an annual range due to water vapor of 1.2 or 1.5×10 15 kg, depending on whether surface pressure or water vapor data are used; somewhat smaller than the previous estimate. The mean mass of water vapor is estimated as 1.27×10 16 kg and the dry air mass as 5.1352 ±0.0003×10 18 kg."[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Trenberth_Smith_2005-87)

Optical properties
------------------

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Earth_energy_budget.svg/330px-Earth_energy_budget.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Earth_energy_budget.svg)

The relative absorption, emission and reflection of solar radiation by the atmosphere, clouds and surface

Solar [radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation "Radiation") (or sunlight) is the energy Earth receives from the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun"). Earth also emits radiation back into space, but at longer wavelengths that humans cannot see. As energy propagates through the atmosphere, it is impacted by the process of [radiative transfer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiative_transfer "Radiative transfer"). That is, some of the incoming and emitted radiation is subject to [absorption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation) "Absorption (electromagnetic radiation)"), emission, and [scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_scattering "Atmospheric scattering") by the atmosphere. Another portion of the incident energy is reflected,[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-88)[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-89) with the two most important atmospheric reflectors being [dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fugitive_dust "Fugitive dust") and clouds. Depending on the properties of the [aerosol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerosol "Aerosol"), clouds can reflect up to 70% of the incident radiation. Globally, clouds reflect 20% of the incoming energy, contributing two thirds of the planet's total [albedo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albedo "Albedo").[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Sirvatka-90) In May 2017, glints of light, seen as twinkling from an orbiting satellite a million miles away, were found to be [reflected light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_(physics) "Reflection (physics)") from [ice crystals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_crystals "Ice crystals") in the troposphere.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-NYT-20170519-91)[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-GRL-201760515-92)

### Scattering

When light passes through Earth's atmosphere, [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") interact with it through _scattering_. If the light does not interact with the atmosphere, it is called _direct radiation_ and is what you see if you were to look directly at the Sun. _Indirect radiation_ is light that has been scattered in the atmosphere. For example, on an [overcast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overcast "Overcast") day when you cannot see your shadow, there is no direct radiation reaching you, it has all been scattered. As another example, due to a phenomenon called [Rayleigh scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering "Rayleigh scattering"), shorter (blue) wavelengths scatter more easily than longer (red) wavelengths. This is why the sky looks blue; you are seeing scattered blue light. This is also why sunsets are red. Because the Sun is close to the horizon, the Sun's rays pass through more atmosphere than normal before reaching your eye. Much of the blue light has been scattered out, leaving the red light in a sunset.[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Bloomfield_2007-93)

### Absorption

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Openstax_Astronomy_EM_spectrum_and_atmosphere.jpg/250px-Openstax_Astronomy_EM_spectrum_and_atmosphere.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Openstax_Astronomy_EM_spectrum_and_atmosphere.jpg)

Rough plot of Earth's atmospheric [transmittance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmittance "Transmittance") (or opacity) to various wavelengths of electromagnetic radiation, including [visible light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visible_light "Visible light")

Different molecules absorb different wavelengths of radiation. For example, O 2 and O 3 absorb almost all radiation with wavelengths shorter than 300 [nanometres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanometer "Nanometer").[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Ondoh_Marubashi_2001-94) Water (H 2 O) absorbs at many wavelengths above 700 nm.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Collins_et_al_2006-95) When a molecule absorbs a photon, it increases the energy of the molecule. This heats the atmosphere, but the atmosphere also cools by emitting radiation, as discussed below. In [astronomical spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_spectroscopy "Astronomical spectroscopy"), the absorption of specific frequencies by the atmosphere is referred to as [telluric contamination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telluric_contamination "Telluric contamination").[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Wang_2022-96)

The combined [absorption spectra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorption_spectra "Absorption spectra") of the gases in the atmosphere leave "windows" of low [opacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opacity_(optics) "Opacity (optics)"), allowing the transmission of only certain bands of light. The [optical window](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_window "Optical window") runs from around 300 nm ([ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet")-C) up into the range humans can see, the [visible spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visible_spectrum "Visible spectrum") (commonly called light), at roughly 400–700 nm and continues to the [infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared") to around 1100 nm. There are also [infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared_window "Infrared window") and [radio windows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_window "Radio window") that transmit some infrared and [radio waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_waves "Radio waves") at longer wavelengths. For example, the radio window runs from about one centimetre to about eleven-metre waves.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-McLean_2008-97)

### Emission

_Emission_ is the opposite of absorption, it is when an object emits radiation. Objects tend to emit amounts and wavelengths of radiation depending on their "[black body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_body "Black body")" emission curves, therefore hotter objects tend to emit more radiation, with shorter [wavelengths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength "Wavelength"). Colder objects emit less radiation, with longer wavelengths. For example, the Sun is approximately 6,000[K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin") (5,730[°C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celsius "Celsius"); 10,340[°F](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fahrenheit "Fahrenheit")), its radiation peaks near 500 nm, and is visible to the human eye. Earth is approximately 290 K (17°C; 62°F), so its radiation peaks near 10,000 nm, and is much too long to be visible to humans.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Shelton_2009-98)

Because of its temperature, the atmosphere emits infrared radiation. For example, on clear nights Earth's surface cools down faster than on cloudy nights. This is because clouds (H 2 O) are strong absorbers and emitters of infrared radiation.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Bohren_Clothiaux_2006-99) This is also why it becomes colder at night at higher elevations.

The [greenhouse effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_effect "Greenhouse effect") is directly related to this absorption and emission effect. Some gases in the atmosphere absorb and emit infrared radiation, but do not interact in this manner with sunlight in the visible spectrum. Common examples of these are CO 2 and H 2 O.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Wrigglesworth_1997-100) Without greenhouse gases in the atmosphere, the average temperature of [Earth's surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_surface "Earth's surface") would be a frozen −18°C (0°F), rather than the present comfortable average of 15°C (59°F).[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Ma_1998-101)

### Refractive index

[![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/SB_DouglasPreserve_SunAtmosphericEffects_2017_3_cropped.jpg/250px-SB_DouglasPreserve_SunAtmosphericEffects_2017_3_cropped.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:SB_DouglasPreserve_SunAtmosphericEffects_2017_3_cropped.jpg)

Distortive effect of [atmospheric refraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_refraction "Atmospheric refraction") upon the shape of the sun at the horizon

The [refractive index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refractive_index "Refractive index") of air is close to, but just greater than, 1.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-102) Systematic variations in the refractive index can lead to the bending of light rays over long optical paths. One example is that, under some circumstances, observers on board ships can see other vessels just over the [horizon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizon "Horizon") because light is refracted in the same direction as the [curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature "Curvature") of Earth's surface.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Basey_2019-103)

The refractive index of air depends on temperature,[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Edl%C3%A9n-104) giving rise to refraction effects when the temperature gradient is large. An example of such effects is the [mirage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirage "Mirage").[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-105)

Circulation
-----------

[![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4c/AtmosphCirc2.svg/250px-AtmosphCirc2.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:AtmosphCirc2.svg)

An idealised view of three pairs of large circulation cells

_Atmospheric circulation_ is the large-scale movement of air through the troposphere, and the means (with [ocean circulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocean_circulation "Ocean circulation")) by which heat is distributed around Earth. The large-scale structure of the atmospheric circulation varies from year to year, but the basic structure remains fairly constant because it is determined by Earth's rotation rate and the difference in solar radiation between the equator and poles. The [axial tilt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axial_tilt "Axial tilt") of the planet means the location of maximum heat is continually changing, resulting in [seasonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seasonality "Seasonality") variations. The uneven distribution of land and water further breaks up the flow of air.[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-GAC-106)

The flow of air around the planet is divided into three main [convection cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convection_cell "Convection cell") by latitude. Around the equator, the [Hadley cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadley_cell "Hadley cell") is driven by the rising flow of air along the [equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator "Equator"). In the upper atmosphere, this air flows toward the poles. At mid latitudes, this circulation is reversed, with ground air flowing toward the poles with the [Ferrel cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferrel_cell "Ferrel cell"). Finally, in the high latitudes is the [Polar cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_cells "Polar cells"), where air again rises and flows toward the poles.[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-GAC-106)

The interface between these cells is responsible for [jet streams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jet_stream "Jet stream"). These are narrow, fast moving bands that flow from west to east and typically form at an elevation of around 9,100 m (30,000 ft). Jet streams can shift around depending on conditions. They are strongest in winter, when the boundaries between hot and cold air are the most pronounced.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-NOAA_Jet_Stream-107) In the middle latitudes, it is [instabilities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baroclinic_instability "Baroclinic instability") in the jet streams that are responsible for moving [weather systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weather_system "Weather system").[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Moffatt_Shuckburgh_2011-108)

As with the [oceans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocean "Ocean"), the Earth's atmosphere is subject to waves and tidal forces. These are triggered by non-uniform heating by the Sun, and by the daily [solar cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_cycle "Solar cycle"), respectively. Wave-like behavior can occur on a variety of scales, from smaller [gravity waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity_wave "Gravity wave") that transfer [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") into the higher atmospheric layers, to much larger [planetary waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_wave "Planetary wave"), or [Rossby waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rossby_wave "Rossby wave"). [Atmospheric tides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_tide "Atmospheric tide") are periodic oscillations of the troposphere and stratosphere that transport energy to the upper atmosphere.[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Volland_2012-109)

Evolution of Earth's atmosphere
-------------------------------

### Earliest atmosphere

The first atmosphere, during the [Early Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Earth "Early Earth")'s [Hadean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadean "Hadean")[eon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geologic_eon "Geologic eon"), consisted of gases in the [solar nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_nebula#Formation_of_planets "Solar nebula"), primarily [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen"), and probably simple [hydrides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydride "Hydride") such as those now found in the [gas giants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_giant "Gas giant") ([Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter") and [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn")), notably [water vapor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_vapor "Water vapor"), [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane") and [ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia"). During this earliest era, the [Moon-forming collision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_impact_hypothesis "Giant impact hypothesis") and numerous [impacts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impact_event "Impact event") with large [meteorites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorite "Meteorite") heated the atmosphere, driving off the most volatile gases. The collision with [Theia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theia_(planet) "Theia (planet)"), in particular, melted and ejected large portions of [Earth's mantle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_mantle "Earth's mantle") and [crust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_crust "Earth's crust") and [outgassed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outgass "Outgass") significant amounts of [steam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steam "Steam") which eventually cooled and condensed to contribute to ocean water at the end of the Hadean.[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Zahnle-110): 10

### Second atmosphere

The increasing [solidification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solidification "Solidification") of [Earth's crust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_crust "Earth's crust") at the end of the Hadean closed off most of the [advective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advection "Advection")[heat transfer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_transfer "Heat transfer") to the surface, causing the atmosphere to cool, which [condensed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condense "Condense") most of the water vapor out of the air [precipitating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precipitation "Precipitation") into a [superocean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superocean "Superocean"). Further [outgassing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outgassing "Outgassing") from [volcanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcanism "Volcanism"), supplemented by gases introduced by huge [asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid "Asteroid") during the [Late Heavy Bombardment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_Heavy_Bombardment "Late Heavy Bombardment"), created the subsequent [Archean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archean "Archean") atmosphere, which consisted largely of [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") plus [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide"), [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane") and [inert gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inert_gas "Inert gas").[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Zahnle-110) A major part of carbon dioxide emissions [dissolved](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissolution_(chemistry) "Dissolution (chemistry)") in water and reacted with metals such as [calcium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium "Calcium") and [magnesium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnesium "Magnesium") during [weathering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weathering "Weathering") of [crustal rocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithosphere "Lithosphere") to form [carbonates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonate "Carbonate") that were deposited as [sediments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sediment "Sediment"). Water-related sediments have been found that date from as early as 3.8 billion years ago.[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-111)

About 3.4 billion years ago, nitrogen formed the major component of the then-stable "second atmosphere". The influence of the [evolution of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution_of_life "Evolution of life") has to be taken into account rather soon in the history of the atmosphere because hints of [earliest life forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earliest_life_forms "Earliest life forms") appeared as early as 3.5 billion years ago.[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-112) How Earth at that time maintained a climate warm enough for liquid water and life, if the early Sun put out 30% lower solar radiance than today, is a puzzle known as the "[faint young Sun paradox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faint_young_Sun_paradox "Faint young Sun paradox")".[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Feulner_2012-113)

The geological record however shows a continuous relatively warm surface during the complete early [temperature record](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature_record "Temperature record") of Earth – with the exception of one cold glacial phase about 2.4 billion years ago. In the late [Neoarchean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoarchean "Neoarchean"), an oxygen-containing atmosphere began to develop, apparently due to a billion years of [cyanobacterial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanobacteria "Cyanobacteria")[photosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis "Photosynthesis") (known as the [Great Oxygenation Event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Oxygenation_Event "Great Oxygenation Event")),[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Lyons_et_al_2014-114) which have been found as [stromatolite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stromatolite "Stromatolite")[fossils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil "Fossil") from 2.7 billion years ago. The early basic carbon isotopy ([isotope ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope_ratio "Isotope ratio") proportions) strongly suggests conditions similar to the current, and that the fundamental features of the [carbon cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_cycle "Carbon cycle") became established as early as 4 billion years ago.[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Hayes_Waldbauer_2006-115)

[Ancient sediments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxy_(climate)#Lake_and_ocean_sediments "Proxy (climate)") in the [Gabon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gabon "Gabon") dating from between about 2.15 and 2.08 billion years ago provide a record of Earth's dynamic oxygenation evolution. These fluctuations in oxygenation were likely driven by the [Lomagundi-Jatuli Carbon Isotope Excursion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lomagundi-Jatuli_Carbon_Isotope_Excursion "Lomagundi-Jatuli Carbon Isotope Excursion").[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-116)

### Third atmosphere

The constant re-arrangement of [continents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continent "Continent") by [plate tectonics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plate_tectonics "Plate tectonics") influences the long-term evolution of the atmosphere by transferring carbon dioxide to and from large continental [carbonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbonate "Carbonate") stores. Free oxygen did not exist in the atmosphere until about 2.4 billion years ago during the [Great Oxygenation Event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Oxygenation_Event "Great Oxygenation Event")[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Cordeiro_Tanaka_2020-117) and its appearance is indicated by the end of [banded iron formations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Banded_iron_formation "Banded iron formation") (which signals the depletion of [substrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Substrate_(chemistry) "Substrate (chemistry)") that can react with oxygen to produce [ferric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferric "Ferric") deposits) during the early [Proterozoic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proterozoic "Proterozoic") eon.[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-118)

Before this time, any oxygen produced by cyanobacterial photosynthesis would be readily removed by the [oxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidation "Oxidation") of [reducing substances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reducing_agent "Reducing agent") on the Earth's surface, notably [ferrous iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferrous_iron "Ferrous iron"), [sulfur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur "Sulfur") and [atmospheric methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_methane "Atmospheric methane"). Free oxygen molecules did not start to accumulate in the atmosphere until the rate of production of oxygen began to exceed the availability of reductant materials that removed oxygen. This point signifies a shift from a [reducing atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reducing_atmosphere "Reducing atmosphere") to an [oxidizing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidizing "Oxidizing") atmosphere.[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Laakso_Schrag_2017-119) O 2 showed major variations during the Proterozoic, including [a billion-year period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boring_Billion "Boring Billion") of [euxinia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euxinia "Euxinia"), until reaching a steady state of more than 15% by the end of the [Precambrian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precambrian "Precambrian").[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-120)

The rise of the more robust [eukaryotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryotic "Eukaryotic")[photoautotrophs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoautotroph "Photoautotroph") ([green](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_algae "Green algae") and [red algae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_algae "Red algae")) injected further oxygenation into the air, especially after the end of the [Cryogenian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryogenian "Cryogenian")[global glaciation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Snowball_Earth "Snowball Earth"), which was followed by an [evolutionary radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolutionary_radiation "Evolutionary radiation") event during the [Ediacaran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ediacaran "Ediacaran")[period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geologic_period "Geologic period") known as the [Avalon explosion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avalon_explosion "Avalon explosion"), where complex [metazoan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metazoa "Metazoa") life forms (including the earliest [cnidarians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cnidarian "Cnidarian"), [placozoans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Placozoa "Placozoa") and [bilaterians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilaterian "Bilaterian")) first proliferated. The following time span from 539 million years ago to the present day is the [Phanerozoic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phanerozoic "Phanerozoic") eon, during the earliest [period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geologic_period "Geologic period") of which, the [Cambrian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambrian "Cambrian"), more [actively moving](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motility "Motility") metazoan life began to appear and rapidly diversify in another radiation event called the [Cambrian explosion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambrian_explosion "Cambrian explosion"), whose [locomotive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal_locomotion "Animal locomotion")[metabolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism "Metabolism") was fuelled by the rising oxygen level.[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Towe_1970-121)

[![Image 15](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Sauerstoffgehalt-1000mj2.png/250px-Sauerstoffgehalt-1000mj2.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Sauerstoffgehalt-1000mj2.png)

Oxygen content of the atmosphere over the last billion years, showing the [Neoproterozoic oxygenation event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoproterozoic_oxygenation_event "Neoproterozoic oxygenation event")[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-122)[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-123)[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Berner_2001-124)

The amount of oxygen in the atmosphere has fluctuated over the last 600 million years, reaching a peak of about 35% around 280 million years ago during the [Carboniferous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboniferous "Carboniferous") period, significantly higher than today's 21%.[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Berner_2001-124) Two main processes govern changes in the atmosphere: the [evolution of](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolutionary_history_of_plants "Evolutionary history of plants")[plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant "Plant") and their increasing role in [carbon fixation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_fixation "Carbon fixation"), and the consumption of oxygen by rapidly diversifying [animal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal "Animal")[faunae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fauna "Fauna") and also by plants for [photorespiration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photorespiration "Photorespiration") and their own metabolic needs at night. Breakdown of [pyrite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrite "Pyrite") and [volcanic eruptions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcanic_eruption "Volcanic eruption") release sulfur into the atmosphere, which reacts and hence reduces oxygen in the atmosphere.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Calvo-Flores_2025-125) However, volcanic eruptions also release carbon dioxide,[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Gerlach_2011-126) which can fuel oxygenic photosynthesis by [terrestrial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrestrial_plant "Terrestrial plant") and [aquatic plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aquatic_plant "Aquatic plant"). The cause of the variation of the amount of oxygen in the atmosphere is not precisely understood. Periods with more oxygen in the atmosphere were often associated with more rapid development of animals.[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Cordeiro_Tanaka_2020-117)

Air pollution
-------------

Animation shows the buildup of tropospheric CO 2 in the Northern Hemisphere with a maximum around May. The maximum in the vegetation cycle follows in the late summer. Following the peak in vegetation, the drawdown of atmospheric CO 2 due to photosynthesis is apparent, particularly over the [boreal forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boreal_forests "Boreal forests").

_Air pollution_ is the introduction of airborne [chemicals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical "Chemical"), [particulate matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_particulate_matter "Atmospheric particulate matter") or [biological materials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_matter "Organic matter") that cause harm or discomfort to organisms.[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-127) The [population growth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Population_growth "Population growth"), [industrialization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrialization "Industrialization") and [motorization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motorization "Motorization") of [human](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human "Human")[societies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Societies "Societies") have significantly increased the amount of airborne [pollutants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pollutant "Pollutant") in the Earth's atmosphere, causing noticeable problems such as [smogs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smog "Smog"), [acid rains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid_rain "Acid rain") and [pollution-related diseases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pollution-related_diseases "Pollution-related diseases"). The [depletion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone_depletion "Ozone depletion") of the [stratospheric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stratospheric "Stratospheric")[ozone layer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone_layer "Ozone layer"), which shields the surface from harmful [ionizing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionizing_radiation "Ionizing radiation")[ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet") radiations, is also caused by air pollution, chiefly from [chlorofluorocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbons "Chlorofluorocarbons") and other ozone-depleting substances.[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-Harrop_2003-128)

Since 1750, human activity, especially after the [Industrial Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_Revolution "Industrial Revolution"), has increased the concentrations of various [greenhouse gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas "Greenhouse gas"), most importantly carbon dioxide, methane and [nitrous oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrous_oxide "Nitrous oxide"). [Greenhouse gas emissions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas_emissions "Greenhouse gas emissions"), coupled with [deforestation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deforestation "Deforestation") and [destruction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat_destruction "Habitat destruction") of [wetlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wetland "Wetland") via [logging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logging "Logging") and [land developments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Land_development "Land development"), have caused an observed [rise in global temperatures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_warming "Global warming"), with the global average surface temperatures being 1.1°C higher in the 2011–2020 decade than they were in 1850.[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-129) It has raised concerns of man-made [climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change "Climate change"), which can have significant [environmental impacts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change "Effects of climate change") such as [sea level rise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_level_rise "Sea level rise"), [ocean acidification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocean_acidification "Ocean acidification"), [glacial retreat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glacial_retreat "Glacial retreat") (which threatens [water security](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_security "Water security")), increasing [extreme weather](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extreme_weather "Extreme weather") events and [wildfires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wildfire "Wildfire"), [ecological collapse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecological_collapse "Ecological collapse") and [mass dying of wildlife](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holocene_extinction "Holocene extinction").[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth#cite_note-OECD-130)
