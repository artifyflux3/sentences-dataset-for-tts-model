A **bracket** is either of two tall fore- or back-facing [punctuation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punctuation "Punctuation") marks commonly used to isolate a segment of text or data from its surroundings.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcArthurMcArthur2005-1) They come in four main pairs of shapes, as given in the box to the right, which also gives their names, that vary between [British](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_English "British English") and [American English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_English "American English").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPointonClark2014406-2) "Brackets", without further qualification, are in British English the (...) marks and in American English the [...] marks.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPointonClark2014406-2)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcArthurMcArthur2005-1)

Other symbols are repurposed as brackets in specialist contexts, such as [those used by linguists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet#Brackets_and_transcription_delimiters "International Phonetic Alphabet").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeters2007101-3)

Brackets are typically deployed in symmetric pairs, and an individual bracket may be identified as a "left" or "right" bracket or, alternatively, an "opening bracket" or "closing bracket",[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-unicode-9-4) respectively, depending on the [directionality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Writing_system#Directionality "Writing system") of the context.

In casual writing and in technical fields such as computing or linguistic analysis of [grammar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammar "Grammar"), brackets [nest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nesting_(computing) "Nesting (computing)"), with segments of bracketed material containing embedded within them other further bracketed sub-segments.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcArthurMcArthur2005-1) The number of opening brackets matches the number of closing brackets in such cases.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcArthurMcArthur2005-1)

[Various forms of brackets are used in mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_mathematical_symbols#Brackets "Glossary of mathematical symbols"), with specific mathematical meanings, often for denoting specific [mathematical functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") and [subformulas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subformula "Subformula").

Angle brackets or [chevrons](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/chevron "wikt:chevron")**⟨⟩** were the earliest type of bracket to appear in [written English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Written_English "Written English"). [Erasmus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desiderius_Erasmus "Desiderius Erasmus") coined the term _lunula_ to refer to the round brackets or [parentheses](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/parenthesis "wikt:parenthesis")**()** recalling the shape of the [crescent moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crescent "Crescent") ([Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_language "Latin language"): _luna_).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-5)

Most typewriters only had the left and right parentheses. Square brackets appeared with some teleprinters.

Braces (curly brackets) first became part of a character set with the 8-bit code of the [IBM 7030 Stretch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IBM_7030_Stretch "IBM 7030 Stretch").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-6)

In 1961, [ASCII](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ASCII "ASCII") contained parentheses, square, and curly brackets, and also less-than and greater-than signs that could be used as angle brackets.

In English, [typographers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Typography "Typography") mostly prefer not to set brackets in [italics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italics "Italics"), even when the enclosed text is italic.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-7) However, in other languages like [German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_language "German language"), if brackets enclose text in italics, they are usually also set in italics.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-8)

The marks ( and ) are _parentheses_ (singular _parenthesis_ ) in American English, and either _round brackets_ or simply _brackets_ in British English.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPointonClark2014406-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPeters2007101-3) They are also known as "parens" , "circle brackets", or "smooth brackets".

In formal writing, "parentheses" is also used in British English.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Parentheses contain [adjunctive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adjunctive "Adjunctive") material that serves to clarify (in the manner of a [gloss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gloss_(annotation) "Gloss (annotation)")) or is aside from the main point.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-20)

A comma before or after the material can also be used, though if the sentence contains commas for other purposes, visual confusion may result. A [dash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dash "Dash") before and after the material is also sometimes used.

Parentheses may be used in formal writing to add supplementary information, such as "Senator John McCain ([R](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republican_Party_(United_States) "Republican Party (United States)")-Arizona) spoke at length". They can also indicate shorthand for "[either singular or plural](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncertain_plural "Uncertain plural")" for nouns, e.g. "the claim(s)". It can also be used for [gender-neutral language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gender-neutral_language "Gender-neutral language"), especially in languages with [grammatical gender](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammatical_gender "Grammatical gender"), e.g. "(s)he agreed with his/her physician" (the slash in the second instance, as one alternative is replacing the other, not adding to it).

Parenthetical phrases have been used extensively in informal writing and stream of consciousness literature. Examples include the southern American author [William Faulkner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Faulkner "William Faulkner") (see _[Absalom, Absalom!](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absalom,\_Absalom! "Absalom, Absalom!")_ and [the Quentin section of _The Sound and the Fury_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Sound_and_the_Fury#Part_2:_June_2,_1910 "The Sound and the Fury")) as well as poet [E. E. Cummings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E._E._Cummings "E. E. Cummings").

Parentheses have historically been used where the [em dash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Em_dash "Em dash") is currently used in alternatives, such as "parenthesis)(parentheses". Examples of this usage can be seen in editions of _[Fowler's Dictionary of Modern English Usage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A\_Dictionary\_of\_Modern\_English\_Usage "A Dictionary of Modern English Usage")_.

Parentheses may be nested (generally with one set (such as this) inside another set). This is not commonly used in formal writing (though sometimes other brackets [especially square brackets] will be used for one or more inner set of parentheses [in other words, secondary {or even tertiary} phrases can be found within the main parenthetical sentence]).

A [parenthesis in rhetoric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parenthesis_(rhetoric) "Parenthesis (rhetoric)") and [linguistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linguistics "Linguistics") refers to the entire bracketed text, not just to the enclosing marks used (so all the text in this set of round brackets may be described as "a parenthesis").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-21) Taking as an example the sentence "Mrs. Pennyfarthing (What? Yes, that was her name!) was my landlady.", the explanatory phrase between the parentheses is itself called a parenthesis. Again, the parenthesis implies that the meaning and flow of the bracketed phrase is supplemental to the rest of the text and the whole would be unchanged were the parenthesized sentences removed. The term refers to the syntax rather than the enclosure method: the same clause in the form "Mrs. Pennyfarthing– What? Yes, that was her name!– was my landlady" is also a parenthesis.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-22) (In non-specialist usage, the term "parenthetical phrase" is more widely understood.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-23))

In [phonetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phonetics "Phonetics"), parentheses are used for indistinguishable[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-IPA175-24) or unidentified utterances. They are also seen for silent articulation (mouthing),[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-IPA191-25) where the expected phonetic transcription is derived from lip-reading, and with periods to indicate silent pauses, for example (...) or (2 sec).

In some languages, such as the Nicodemus orthography for [Coeur d’Alene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coeur_d%27Alene_language#Orthography "Coeur d'Alene language"), parentheses are used as phonemic symbols instead of punctuation.

Traditionally in [accounting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accounting "Accounting"), contra amounts are placed in parentheses. A debit balance account in a series of credit balances will have parentheses and vice versa.

#### Parentheses in mathematics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bracket&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Parentheses in mathematics")]

Parentheses are used in [mathematical notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_notation "Mathematical notation") to indicate grouping, often inducing a different [order of operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_of_operations "Order of operations"). For example: in the usual order of algebraic operations, 4 × 3 + 2 equals 14, since the [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication "Multiplication") is done before the [addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition "Addition"). However, 4 × (3 + 2) equals 20, because the parentheses override normal precedence, causing the addition to be done first. Some authors follow the convention in mathematical equations that, when parentheses have one level of nesting, the inner pair are parentheses and the outer pair are square brackets. Example:

#### Parentheses in programming languages

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bracket&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Parentheses in programming languages")]

Parentheses are included in the syntaxes of many [programming languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Programming_language "Programming language"). Typically needed to denote an argument; to tell the compiler what data type the method/function needs to look for first in order to initialise. In some cases, such as in [LISP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LISP "LISP"), parentheses are a fundamental construct of the language. They are also often used for scoping functions and operators and for arrays. In [syntax diagrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syntax_diagram "Syntax diagram") they are used for grouping, such as in [extended Backus–Naur form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extended_Backus%E2%80%93Naur_form "Extended Backus–Naur form").

In Mathematica and the Wolfram language, parentheses are used to indicate grouping– for example, with pure anonymous functions.

If it is desired to include the [subgenus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subgenus "Subgenus") when giving the [scientific name](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_name "Scientific name") of an animal species or [subspecies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subspecies "Subspecies"), the subgenus's name is provided in parentheses between the [genus name](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generic_epithet "Generic epithet") and the [specific epithet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_epithet "Specific epithet").[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-26) For instance, _Polyphylla_ (_Xerasiobia_) _alba_ is a way to cite the species _Polyphylla alba_ while also mentioning that it is in the subgenus _Xerasiobia_.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-27) There is also a convention of citing a subgenus by enclosing it in parentheses after its genus, e.g., _Polyphylla_ (_Xerasiobia_) is a way to refer to the subgenus _Xerasiobia_ within the genus _Polyphylla_.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-28) Parentheses are similarly used to cite a subgenus with the name of a [prokaryotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prokaryotic "Prokaryotic") species, although the [International Code of Nomenclature of Prokaryotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Code_of_Nomenclature_of_Prokaryotes "International Code of Nomenclature of Prokaryotes") (ICNP) requires the use of the abbreviation "subgen". as well, e.g., _Acetobacter_ (subgen. _Gluconoacetobacter_) _liquefaciens_.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-29)

Parentheses are used in [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry") to denote a repeated substructure within a molecule, e.g. HC(CH 3)3 ([isobutane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isobutane "Isobutane")) or, similarly, to indicate the stoichiometry of ionic compounds with such substructures: e.g. Ca(NO 3)2 ([calcium nitrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_nitrate "Calcium nitrate")).

This is a notation that was pioneered by [Berzelius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berzelius "Berzelius"), who wanted chemical formulae to more resemble algebraic notation, with brackets enclosing groups that could be multiplied (e.g. in 3(AlO 2 + 2SO 3) the 3 multiplies everything within the parentheses).[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWatts1877140%E2%80%93141-30)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIhde1984115-31)

In [chemical nomenclature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_nomenclature "Chemical nomenclature"), parentheses are used to distinguish structural features and multipliers for clarity, for example in the polymer [poly(methyl methacrylate)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poly(methyl_methacrylate) "Poly(methyl methacrylate)").[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-32)

[ and ] are _square brackets_ in both British and American English, but are also more simply _brackets_ in the latter.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPointonClark2014406-2)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcArthurMcArthur2005-1) An older name for these brackets is "crotchets".[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-34)

Square brackets are often used to insert explanatory material or to mark where a [word or] passage was omitted from an original material by someone other than the original author, or to mark modifications in quotations.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-35) In transcribed interviews, sounds, responses and reactions that are not words but that can be described are set off in square brackets — "... [laughs] ...".

When quoted material is in any way altered, the alterations are enclosed in square brackets within the quotation to show that the quotation is not exactly as given, or to add an [annotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annotation "Annotation").[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-36) For example: _The Plaintiff asserted his cause is just, stating,_

> [m]y causes is [[_sic_]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sic "Sic") just.

In the original quoted sentence, the word "my" was capitalized: it has been modified in the quotation given and the change signalled with brackets. Similarly, where the quotation contained a grammatical error (is/are), the quoting author signalled that the error was in the original with "[_sic_]" (Latin for 'thus').

A bracketed [ellipsis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsis "Ellipsis"), [...], is often used to indicate omitted material: "I'd like to thank [several unimportant people] for their tolerance [...]"[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-37) Bracketed comments inserted into a quote indicate where the original has been modified for clarity: "I appreciate it [the honor], but I must refuse", and "the future of psionics [see definition] is in doubt". Or one can quote the original statement "I hate to do laundry" with a (sometimes grammatical) modification inserted: He "hate[s] to do laundry".

Additionally, a small letter can be replaced by a capital one, when the beginning of the original printed text is being quoted in another piece of text or when the original text has been omitted for succinctness— for example, when referring to a [verbose](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/verbose "wikt:verbose") original: "To the extent that policymakers and elite opinion in general have made use of economic analysis at all, they have, as the saying goes, done so the way a drunkard uses a lamppost: for support, not illumination", can be quoted succinctly as: "[P]olicymakers [...] have made use of economic analysis [...] the way a drunkard uses a lamppost: for support, not illumination." When nested parentheses are needed, brackets are sometimes used as a substitute for the inner pair of parentheses within the outer pair.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-38) When deeper levels of nesting are needed, convention is to alternate between parentheses and brackets at each level.

Alternatively, empty square brackets can also indicate omitted material, usually single letter only. The original, "Reading is also a process and it also changes you." can be rewritten in a quote as: It has been suggested that reading can "also change[] you".[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-39)

In translated works, brackets are used to signify the same word or phrase in the original language to avoid ambiguity.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-40) For example: _He is trained in the way of the open hand [karate]._

[Style and usage guides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Style_guide "Style guide") originating in the [news industry of the twentieth century](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/News_media#History "News media"), such as the _[AP Stylebook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AP\_Stylebook "AP Stylebook")_, recommend against the use of square brackets because "They cannot be transmitted over [news wires](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_wire_services "List of wire services")."[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-AP2014-41) However, this guidance has little relevance outside of the technological constraints of the industry and era.

In linguistics, [phonetic transcriptions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phonetic_transcription "Phonetic transcription") are generally enclosed within square brackets,[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-42) whereas [phonemic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phonemic "Phonemic") transcriptions typically use paired [slashes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slash_(punctuation) "Slash (punctuation)"), according to [International Phonetic Alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet#Brackets_and_transcription_delimiters "International Phonetic Alphabet") rules. Pipes (||) are often used to indicate a [morphophonemic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphophoneme "Morphophoneme") rather than phonemic representation. Other conventions are double slashes (⫽⫽), double pipes (‖‖) and curly brackets ({}).

In [lexicography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lexicography "Lexicography"), square brackets usually surround the section of a dictionary entry which contains the [etymology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Etymology "Etymology") of the word the entry defines.

Brackets (called _move-left symbols_ or _move right symbols_) are added to the sides of text in [proofreading](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proofreading "Proofreading") to indicate changes in indentation:

| Move left | [To Fate I sue, of other means bereft, the only refuge for the wretched left. |
| --- |
| Center | ]Paradise Lost[ |
| Move up | [![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c5/Quote_to_be_Moved_Up.svg/330px-Quote_to_be_Moved_Up.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Quote_to_be_Moved_Up.svg) |

Square brackets are used to denote parts of the text that need to be checked when preparing drafts prior to finalizing a document.

Square brackets are used in some countries in the citation of [law reports](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_report "Law report") to identify parallel citations to non-official reporters. For example:

> _Chronicle Pub. Co. v [Superior Court](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superior\_Court "Superior Court")_ (1998) 54 Cal.2d 548, [7 Cal.Rptr. 109]

In some other countries (such as [England and Wales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/England_and_Wales "England and Wales")), square brackets are used to indicate that the year is part of the citation and parentheses are used to indicate the year the judgment was given. For example:

> _National Coal Board v England_ [1954] AC 403

This case is in the 1954 volume of the Appeal Cases reports, although the decision may have been given in 1953 or earlier. Compare with:

> (1954) 98 Sol Jo 176

This citation reports a decision from 1954, in volume 98 of the _[Solicitors Journal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solicitors\_Journal "Solicitors Journal")_ which may be published in 1955 or later.

They often denote points that have not yet been agreed to in legal drafts and the year in which a report was made for certain [case law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Case_law "Case law") decisions.

#### Square brackets in mathematics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bracket&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Square brackets in mathematics")]

Brackets are used in [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") in a variety of notations, including standard notations for [commutators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutator "Commutator"), the [floor function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Floor_and_ceiling_functions "Floor and ceiling functions"), the [Lie bracket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_bracket_of_vector_fields "Lie bracket of vector fields"), [equivalence classes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_class#Notation_and_formal_definition "Equivalence class"), the [Iverson bracket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iverson_bracket "Iverson bracket"), and [matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)").

Square brackets may be used exclusively or in combination with parentheses to represent [intervals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)") as _interval notation_.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAchatzAnderson2005165%E2%80%93166-43) For example, [0,5] represents the set of real numbers from 0 to 5 inclusive. Both parentheses and brackets are used to denote a _half-open_ interval; [5, 12) would be the set of all real numbers between 5 and 12, including 5 but not 12. The numbers may come as close as they like to 12, including 11.999 and so forth, but 12.0 is not included. In some European countries, the notation [5, 12[ is also used.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-44)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-45) The endpoint adjoining the square bracket is known as _closed_, whereas the endpoint adjoining the parenthesis is known as _open_.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAchatzAnderson2005165%E2%80%93166-43)

In [group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_theory "Group theory") and [ring theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_theory "Ring theory"), brackets denote the [commutator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutator "Commutator"). In group theory, the commutator [g, h] is commonly defined as g −1 h −1 g h. In ring theory, the commutator [a, b] is defined as a b − b a.

Square brackets can also be used in [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry") to represent the [concentration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concentration "Concentration") of a [chemical substance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_substance "Chemical substance") in solution and to denote charge a Lewis structure of an ion (particularly distributed charge in a [complex ion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_(chemistry) "Complex (chemistry)")), repeating chemical units (particularly in polymers) and transition state structures, among other uses.

#### Square brackets in programming languages

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bracket&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Square brackets in programming languages")]

Brackets are used in many computer [programming languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Programming_language "Programming language"), primarily for [array](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Array_data_structure "Array data structure") indexing. But they are also used to denote general tuples, sets and other structures, just as in mathematics. There may be several other uses as well, depending on the language at hand. In [syntax diagrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syntax_diagram "Syntax diagram") they are used for optional portions, such as in [extended Backus–Naur form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extended_Backus%E2%80%93Naur_form "Extended Backus–Naur form").

### Double brackets ⟦ ⟧

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bracket&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Double brackets ⟦ ⟧")]

Double brackets (or white square brackets or [Scott](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dana_Scott "Dana Scott") brackets), ⟦⟧, are used to indicate the _semantic evaluation function_ in [formal semantics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formal_semantics_(linguistics) "Formal semantics (linguistics)") for natural language and [denotational semantics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denotational_semantics "Denotational semantics") for programming languages.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-46)[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-47) In the [Wolfram Language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfram_Language "Wolfram Language"), double brackets, either as iterated single brackets ([[) or ligatures (〚) are used for [list indexing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Array_index "Array index").[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-48)

The brackets stand for a function that maps a linguistic expression to its "denotation" or semantic value. In mathematics, double brackets may also be used to denote [intervals of integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics)#Integer_intervals "Interval (mathematics)") or, less often, the [floor function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Floor_and_ceiling_functions "Floor and ceiling functions"). In papyrology, following the [Leiden Conventions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leiden_Conventions "Leiden Conventions"), they are used to enclose text that has been deleted in antiquity.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-49)

### Lenticular brackets【】

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bracket&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Lenticular brackets【】")]

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/99/Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg/40px-Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Wiktionary-logo-en-v2.svg)

Look up _**[【】](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E3%80%90\_%E3%80%91 "wiktionary:【 】")**_ in Wiktionary, the free dictionary.

Some [East Asian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Asia "East Asia") languages use lenticular brackets 【】, a combination of square brackets and round brackets called [方頭括號](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E6%96%B9%E9%A0%AD%E6%8B%AC%E8%99%9F#Chinese "wikt:方頭括號") (_fāngtóu kuòhào_) in [Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language "Chinese language") and 隅付き括弧 (_sumitsuki kakko_) in [Japanese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_language "Japanese language"). They are used in titles and headings in both Chinese[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-50) and Japanese. On the Internet, they are used to emphasize a text. In Japanese, they are most frequently seen in dictionaries for quoting Chinese characters and Sino-Japanese loanwords.

### Quine corners ⌜⌝ and half brackets ⸤ ⸥ or ⸢ ⸣

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bracket&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Quine corners ⌜⌝ and half brackets ⸤ ⸥ or ⸢ ⸣")]

The Quine corners ⌜ and ⌝ have at least two uses in [mathematical logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_logic "Mathematical logic"): either as [quasi-quotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-quotation "Quasi-quotation"), a generalization of quotation marks, or to denote the [Gödel number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6del_number "Gödel number") of the enclosed [expression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expression_(mathematics) "Expression (mathematics)").

Half brackets are used in English to mark added text, such as in translations: "Bill saw ⸤her⸥".

In editions of [papyrological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Papyrology "Papyrology") texts, half brackets, ⸤ and ⸥ or ⸢ and ⸣, enclose text which is lacking in the papyrus due to damage, but can be restored by virtue of another source, such as an ancient quotation of the text transmitted by the papyrus.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-51) For example, [Callimachus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Callimachus "Callimachus")_Iambus_ 1.2 reads: ἐκ τῶν ὅκου βοῦν κολλύ⸤βου π⸥ιπρήσκουσιν. A hole in the papyrus has obliterated βου π, but these letters are supplied by an ancient commentary on the poem. Second intermittent sources can be between ⸢ and ⸣. Quine corners are sometimes used instead of half brackets.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-Unicode_Miscellaneous_Technical-14)

In music, they are known as "[accolades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accolade_(notation) "Accolade (notation)")" or "[braces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brace_(music) "Brace (music)")", and connect two or more lines (staves) of music that are played simultaneously.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-60)

#### Curly brackets in programming languages

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bracket&action=edit&section=28 "Edit section: Curly brackets in programming languages")]

In many programming languages, curly brackets enclose groups of [statements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statement_(programming) "Statement (programming)") and create a local [scope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scope_(computer_science) "Scope (computer science)"). Such languages ([C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C_(programming_language) "C (programming language)"), C#, C++ and many others) are therefore called [curly bracket languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curly_bracket_language "Curly bracket language").[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-61) They are also used to define structures and [enumerated type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enumerated_type "Enumerated type") in these languages.

In various [Unix shells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unix_shell "Unix shell"), they enclose a group of strings that are used in a process known as _brace expansion_, where each successive string in the group is interpolated at that point in the command line to generate the command-line's final form.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-FOOTNOTENewhamRosenblatt199814-62) The mechanism originated in the [C shell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C_shell "C shell") and the string generation mechanism is a simple interpolation that can occur anywhere in a command line and takes no account of existing filenames.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-FOOTNOTESobellSeebach2005323-63)

In [syntax diagrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syntax_diagram "Syntax diagram") they are used for repetition, such as in [extended Backus–Naur form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extended_Backus%E2%80%93Naur_form "Extended Backus–Naur form").

In the [Z](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Z_notation "Z notation")[formal specification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formal_specification "Formal specification") language, braces define a set.

#### Curly brackets in mathematics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bracket&action=edit&section=29 "Edit section: Curly brackets in mathematics")]

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") they delimit [sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)"), in what is called _set notation_.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBiggs2002-64) Braces enclose either a literal list of set elements, or a rule that defines the set elements.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBiggs2002-64) For example:

*   S = {a, b} defines a set S containing a and b.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBiggs2002-64)
*   S = {x | x> 0} defines a set S containing elements (implied to be numbers) x 0, x 1, and so on where every x n satisfies the rule that it is greater than zero.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBiggs2002-64)

They are often also used to denote the [Poisson bracket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poisson_bracket "Poisson bracket") between two quantities.

In [ring theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_theory "Ring theory"), braces denote the [anticommutator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticommutator "Anticommutator") where {a, b} is defined as a b + b a.

The symbols ⟨ and ⟩ are _angle brackets_ in both American and British English.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPointonClark2014406-2)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMcArthurMcArthur2005-1) In (largely archaic) [computer slang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_slang "Computer slang"), they were sometimes known as "brokets".[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-brocket-66)

The ASCII characters [less-than sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Less-than_sign "Less-than sign") and [greater-than sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greater-than_sign "Greater-than sign")< and > are widely substituted for angle brackets. In many cases, only these substituted characters are accepted by computer programs, and the Unicode angle brackets are not recognized (for instance, in [HTML tags](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HTML_tag "HTML tag")). The characters for "single" [guillemets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guillemet "Guillemet") (‹ and ›) (single european style quote marks) are also occasionally used to indicate angle brackets, and normal guillemets (« and ») (double european style quote marks) used when 'nested' (ie, double) angle brackets are needed.

The angle brackets U+27E8⟨MATHEMATICAL LEFT ANGLE BRACKET and U+27E9⟩MATHEMATICAL RIGHT ANGLE BRACKET are for mathematical use and Western languages, whereas U+3008〈LEFT ANGLE BRACKET and U+3009〉RIGHT ANGLE BRACKET are [double width forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halfwidth_and_fullwidth_forms "Halfwidth and fullwidth forms") for East Asian languages. The angle bracket symbols at U+2329 and U+232A are deprecated in favour of the U+3008 and U+3009 East Asian angle brackets. Unicode discourages their use for mathematics and in Western texts,[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-Unicode_Miscellaneous_Technical-14) because they are canonically equivalent to the CJK code points U+300n and thus likely to render as double-width symbols.

(The word _chevron_ is sometimes used for 'angle bracket', but chevrons are normally oriented horizontally rather than vertically.)

Angle brackets are larger than 'less-than' and 'greater-than' signs, which in turn are larger than guillemets.

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Angle_brackets_and_less%2Bgreater_signs_and_half_guillemets_in_different_fonts.svg/750px-Angle_brackets_and_less%2Bgreater_signs_and_half_guillemets_in_different_fonts.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Angle_brackets_and_less%2Bgreater_signs_and_half_guillemets_in_different_fonts.svg)

Angle brackets, less-than/greater-than signs and single [guillemets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guillemet "Guillemet") in fonts [Cambria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambria_(typeface) "Cambria (typeface)"), [DejaVu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DejaVu_fonts "DejaVu fonts") Serif, [Andron](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Andron_(typeface)&action=edit&redlink=1 "Andron (typeface) (page does not exist)") Mega Corpus, [Andika](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andika_(typeface) "Andika (typeface)") and [Everson Mono](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Everson_Mono "Everson Mono")

Angle brackets are infrequently used to denote [words that are thought](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrapersonal_communication "Intrapersonal communication") instead of spoken, such as:

⟨What an unusual flower!⟩
In [textual criticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Textual_criticism "Textual criticism"), and hence in many editions of pre-modern works, chevrons denote sections of the text which are illegible or otherwise lost; the editor will often insert their own reconstruction where possible within them.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-Trask-68)

In [linguistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linguistics "Linguistics"), angle brackets identify [graphemes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grapheme "Grapheme") (e.g., letters of an alphabet) or [orthography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthography "Orthography"), as in "The English word /kæt/ is spelled ⟨cat⟩."[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-69)[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-Sampson-70)[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-Trask-68)(See [IPA Brackets and transcription delimiters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Phonetic_Alphabet#Brackets_and_transcription_delimiters "International Phonetic Alphabet").)

In [epigraphy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epigraphy "Epigraphy"), they may be used for mechanical transliterations of a text into the Latin script.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-Sampson-70)

In [East Asian punctuation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotation_mark#Chinese,_Japanese_and_Korean_quotation_marks "Quotation mark"), angle brackets are used as [quotation marks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotation_mark "Quotation mark"). Angle bracket symbols are part of standard [Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_language "Chinese language"), [Japanese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_language "Japanese language") and– less frequently– [Korean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Korean_language "Korean language") punctuation, where they generally enclose the titles of books, as: 〈 ︙ 〉 or 《 ︙ 》 for traditional [vertical printing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tategaki "Tategaki") — written in vertical lines — and as 〈 ... 〉 or 《 ... 》 for [horizontal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yokogaki "Yokogaki") printing — in horizontal.

#### Angle brackets in mathematics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bracket&action=edit&section=33 "Edit section: Angle brackets in mathematics")]

Angle brackets (or 'chevrons') are used in [group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_theory "Group theory") to write [group presentations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_presentation "Group presentation"), and to denote the [subgroup generated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_generators "Group generators") by a collection of elements. In [set theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_theory "Set theory"), chevrons or parentheses are used to denote [ordered pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair "Ordered pair")[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bracket#cite_note-71) and other [tuples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple "Tuple"), whereas curly brackets are used for unordered sets.

#### Physics and mechanics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bracket&action=edit&section=34 "Edit section: Physics and mechanics")]

In physical sciences and statistical mechanics, angle brackets are used to denote an average (_[expected value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expected\_value#Notations "Expected value")_) over time or over another continuous parameter. For example:

![Image 8: {\displaystyle \left\langle V(t)^{2}\right\rangle =\lim _{T\to \infty }{\frac {1}{T}}\int _{-{\frac {T}{2}}}^{\frac {T}{2}}V(t)^{2}\,{\rm {d}}t.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27865e5436b89b0661120d76afa967c4c64f70e3)
In mathematical physics, especially [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), it is common to write the [inner product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product "Inner product") between elements as ⟨_a_|_b_⟩, as a short version of ⟨_a_|·|_b_⟩, or ⟨_a_|_Ô_|_b_⟩, where _Ô_ is an [operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operator_(physics) "Operator (physics)"). This is known as _Dirac notation_ or _[bra–ket notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bra%E2%80%93ket\_notation "Bra–ket notation")_, to note vectors from the [dual spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_space "Dual space") of the Bra⟨_A_| and the Ket|_B_⟩. But there are [other notations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product_space#Alternative_definitions,_notations_and_remarks "Inner product space") used.

In [continuum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics "Continuum mechanics"), chevrons may be used as [Macaulay brackets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macaulay_brackets "Macaulay brackets").

#### Angle brackets in programming languages

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bracket&action=edit&section=35 "Edit section: Angle brackets in programming languages")]

In [C++](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C%2B%2B "C++") angle brackets (actually less-than and greater-than) are used to surround arguments to [templates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Template_(C%2B%2B) "Template (C++)"). They are also used to surround the names of [header files](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Header_file#C/C++ "Header file"); this usage was inherited from and is also found in [C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C_(programming_language) "C (programming language)").

In the [Z](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Z_notation "Z notation")[formal specification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formal_specification "Formal specification") language, angle brackets define a sequence.

In [HTML](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HTML "HTML"), angle brackets (actually 'greater than' and 'less than' symbols) are used to bracket meta text. For example `<b>` denotes that the following text should be displayed as bold. Pairs of meta text tags are required – much as brackets themselves are usually in pairs. The end of the bold text segment would be indicated by `</b>`. This use is sometimes extended as an informal mechanism for communicating mood or tone in digital formats such as messaging, for example adding "<sighs>" at the end of a sentence.
