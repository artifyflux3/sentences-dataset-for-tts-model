An **ecosystem** (or **ecological system**) is a system formed by [organisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism "Organism") in interaction with their [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biophysical_environment "Biophysical environment").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011m-2): 458  The [biotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biotic_material "Biotic material") and [abiotic components](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiotic_component "Abiotic component") are linked together through [nutrient cycles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrient_cycle "Nutrient cycle") and [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy") flows.

Ecosystems are controlled by external and internal [factors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environmental_factor "Environmental factor"). External factors—including [climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate "Climate")—control the ecosystem's structure, but are not influenced by it. By contrast, internal factors control and are controlled by ecosystem processes; these include [decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decomposition "Decomposition"), the types of species present, root competition, shading, disturbance, and succession. While external factors generally determine which [resource](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resource_(biology) "Resource (biology)") inputs an ecosystem has, their availability within the ecosystem is controlled by internal factors. Ecosystems are [dynamic](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/dynamic "wikt:dynamic"), subject to periodic disturbances and always in the process of recovering from past disturbances. The tendency of an ecosystem to remain close to its equilibrium state, is termed its [resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resistance_(ecology) "Resistance (ecology)"). Its capacity to absorb disturbance and reorganize, while undergoing change so as to retain essentially the same function, structure, identity, is termed its [ecological resilience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecological_resilience "Ecological resilience").

Ecosystems can be studied through a variety of approaches—theoretical studies, studies monitoring specific ecosystems over long periods of time, those that look at differences between ecosystems to elucidate how they work and direct manipulative experimentation. [Biomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biome "Biome") are general classes or categories of ecosystems. However, there is no clear distinction between biomes and ecosystems. [Ecosystem classifications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecological_classification "Ecological classification") are specific kinds of ecological classifications that consider all four elements of the definition of ecosystems: a biotic component, an [abiotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiotic "Abiotic") complex, the interactions between and within them, and the physical space they occupy. Biotic factors are living things; such as plants, while abiotic are non-living components; such as soil. Plants allow energy to enter the system through [photosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis "Photosynthesis"), building up plant tissue. Animals play an important role in the movement of [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter") and energy through the system, by feeding on plants and one another. They also influence the quantity of plant and [microbial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbe "Microbe")[biomass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomass_(ecology) "Biomass (ecology)") present. By breaking down dead [organic matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_matter "Organic matter"), [decomposers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decomposer "Decomposer") release [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") back to the atmosphere and facilitate [nutrient cycling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrient_cycling "Nutrient cycling") by converting nutrients stored in dead biomass back to a form that can be readily used by plants and microbes.

Ecosystems provide a variety of goods and services upon which people depend, and may be part of. Ecosystem goods include the "tangible, material products" of ecosystem processes such as water, food, fuel, construction material, and [medicinal plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medicinal_plants "Medicinal plants"). [Ecosystem services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem_services "Ecosystem services"), on the other hand, are generally "improvements in the condition or location of things of value". These include things like the maintenance of [hydrological cycles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_cycle "Water cycle"), cleaning air and water, the maintenance of oxygen in the atmosphere, crop [pollination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pollination "Pollination") and even things like beauty, inspiration and opportunities for research. Many ecosystems become degraded through human impacts, such as [soil loss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erosion "Erosion"), [air](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_pollution "Air pollution") and [water pollution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_pollution "Water pollution"), [habitat fragmentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat_fragmentation "Habitat fragmentation"), [water diversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interbasin_transfer "Interbasin transfer"), [fire suppression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wildfire_suppression "Wildfire suppression"), and [introduced species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Introduced_species "Introduced species") and [invasive species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invasive_species "Invasive species"). These threats can lead to abrupt transformation of the ecosystem or to gradual disruption of biotic processes and degradation of [abiotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiotic_component "Abiotic component") conditions of the ecosystem. Once the original ecosystem has lost its defining features, it is considered ["collapsed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem_collapse "Ecosystem collapse")". [Ecosystem restoration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Restoration_ecology "Restoration ecology") can contribute to achieving the [Sustainable Development Goals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sustainable_Development_Goals "Sustainable Development Goals").

Definition
----------

An ecosystem (or ecological system) consists of all the organisms and the abiotic pools (or physical environment) with which they interact.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Tansley-1935-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011a-4): 5 [[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011m-2): 458  The biotic and [abiotic components](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiotic_component "Abiotic component") are linked together through nutrient cycles and energy flows.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Odum-1971-5)

"Ecosystem processes" are the transfers of energy and materials from one pool to another.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011m-2): 458  Ecosystem processes are known to "take place at a wide range of scales". Therefore, the correct scale of study depends on the question asked.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011a-4): 5

### Origin and development of the term

The term "ecosystem" was first used in 1935 in a publication by British ecologist [Arthur Tansley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Tansley "Arthur Tansley"). The term was coined by [Arthur Roy Clapham](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Roy_Clapham "Arthur Roy Clapham"), who came up with the word at Tansley's request.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Willis-1997-6) Tansley devised the concept to draw attention to the importance of transfers of materials between organisms and their environment.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011a-4): 9  He later refined the term, describing it as "The whole system, ... including not only the organism-complex, but also the whole complex of physical factors forming what we call the environment".[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Tansley-1935-3) Tansley regarded ecosystems not simply as natural units, but as "mental isolates".[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Tansley-1935-3) Tansley later defined the spatial extent of ecosystems using the term "[ecotope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecotope "Ecotope")".[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Tansley-1939-7)

[G. Evelyn Hutchinson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G._Evelyn_Hutchinson "G. Evelyn Hutchinson"), a [limnologist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limnologist "Limnologist") who was a contemporary of Tansley's, combined [Charles Elton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Sutherland_Elton "Charles Sutherland Elton")'s ideas about [trophic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trophic_level "Trophic level") ecology with those of Russian geochemist [Vladimir Vernadsky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vladimir_Vernadsky "Vladimir Vernadsky"). As a result, he suggested that mineral nutrient availability in a lake limited [algal production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algal_bloom "Algal bloom"). This would, in turn, limit the abundance of animals that feed on algae. [Raymond Lindeman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raymond_Lindeman "Raymond Lindeman") took these ideas further to suggest that the flow of energy through a lake was the primary driver of the ecosystem. Hutchinson's students, brothers [Howard T. Odum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Howard_T._Odum "Howard T. Odum") and [Eugene P. Odum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eugene_P._Odum "Eugene P. Odum"), further developed a "systems approach" to the study of ecosystems. This allowed them to study the flow of energy and material through ecological systems.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011a-4): 9

### External and internal factors

Ecosystems are controlled by both external and internal factors. External factors, also called state factors, control the overall structure of an ecosystem and the way things work within it, but are not themselves influenced by the ecosystem. On broad geographic scales, [climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate "Climate") is the factor that "most strongly determines ecosystem processes and structure".[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011a-4): 14  Climate determines the [biome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biome "Biome") in which the ecosystem is embedded. Rainfall patterns and seasonal temperatures influence photosynthesis and thereby determine the amount of energy available to the ecosystem.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011d-8): 145

[Parent material](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parent_material "Parent material") determines the nature of the soil in an ecosystem, and influences the supply of mineral nutrients. [Topography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topography "Topography") also controls ecosystem processes by affecting things like [microclimate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microclimate "Microclimate"), soil development and the movement of water through a system. For example, ecosystems can be quite different if situated in a small depression on the landscape, versus one present on an adjacent steep hillside.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011b-9): 39 [[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011c-10): 66

Other external factors that play an important role in ecosystem functioning include time and potential [biota](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biota_(ecology) "Biota (ecology)"), the organisms that are present in a region and could potentially occupy a particular site. Ecosystems in similar environments that are located in different parts of the world can end up doing things very differently simply because they have different pools of species present.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011j-11): 321  The [introduction of non-native species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Introduced_species "Introduced species") can cause substantial shifts in ecosystem function.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-12)

Unlike external factors, internal factors in ecosystems not only control ecosystem processes but are also controlled by them.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011a-4): 16  While the [resource](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resource_(biology) "Resource (biology)") inputs are generally controlled by external processes like climate and parent material, the availability of these resources within the ecosystem is controlled by internal factors like decomposition, root competition or shading.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-13) Other factors like disturbance, succession or the types of species present are also internal factors.

### Primary production

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/44/Seawifs_global_biosphere.jpg/500px-Seawifs_global_biosphere.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Seawifs_global_biosphere.jpg)

Global oceanic and terrestrial phototroph abundance, from September 1997 to August 2000. As an estimate of [autotroph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autotroph "Autotroph") biomass, it is only a rough indicator of primary production potential and not an actual estimate of it.

Primary production is the production of [organic matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_matter "Organic matter") from inorganic carbon sources. This mainly occurs through [photosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis "Photosynthesis"). The energy incorporated through this process supports life on earth, while the carbon makes up much of the organic matter in living and dead biomass, [soil carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil_carbon "Soil carbon") and [fossil fuels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil_fuel "Fossil fuel"). It also drives the [carbon cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_cycle "Carbon cycle"), which influences global [climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate "Climate") via the [greenhouse effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_effect "Greenhouse effect").

Through the process of photosynthesis, plants capture energy from light and use it to combine [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") and water to produce [carbohydrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbohydrate "Carbohydrate") and [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen"). The photosynthesis carried out by all the plants in an ecosystem is called the gross primary production (GPP).[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011d-8): 124  About half of the gross GPP is respired by plants in order to provide the energy that supports their growth and maintenance.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011e-14): 157  The remainder, that portion of GPP that is not used up by respiration, is known as the [net primary production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_primary_production "Net primary production") (NPP).[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011e-14): 157  Total photosynthesis is limited by a range of environmental factors. These include the amount of light available, the amount of [leaf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leaf "Leaf") area a plant has to capture light (shading by other plants is a major limitation of photosynthesis), the rate at which carbon dioxide can be supplied to the [chloroplasts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloroplast "Chloroplast") to support photosynthesis, the availability of water, and the availability of suitable temperatures for carrying out photosynthesis.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011d-8): 155

### Energy flow

[Energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy") and [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") enter ecosystems through photosynthesis, are incorporated into living tissue, transferred to other organisms that feed on the living and dead plant matter, and eventually released through respiration.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011e-14): 157  The carbon and energy incorporated into plant tissues (net primary production) is either consumed by animals while the plant is alive, or it remains uneaten when the plant tissue dies and becomes [detritus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detritus "Detritus"). In [terrestrial ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrestrial_ecosystem "Terrestrial ecosystem"), the vast majority of the net primary production ends up being broken down by [decomposers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decomposition "Decomposition"). The remainder is consumed by animals while still alive and enters the plant-based trophic system. After plants and animals die, the organic matter contained in them enters the detritus-based trophic system.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011i-15)

[Ecosystem respiration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem_respiration "Ecosystem respiration") is the sum of [respiration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellular_respiration "Cellular respiration") by all living organisms (plants, animals, and decomposers) in the ecosystem.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-16)[Net ecosystem production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_ecosystem_production "Net ecosystem production") is the difference between [gross primary production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_production "Primary production") (GPP) and ecosystem respiration.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Lovett-2006-17) In the absence of disturbance, net ecosystem production is equivalent to the net carbon accumulation in the ecosystem.

Energy can also be released from an ecosystem through disturbances such as [wildfire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wildfire "Wildfire") or transferred to other ecosystems (e.g., from a forest to a stream to a lake) by [erosion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erosion "Erosion").

In [aquatic systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aquatic_ecosystem "Aquatic ecosystem"), the proportion of plant biomass that gets consumed by [herbivores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbivore "Herbivore") is much higher than in terrestrial systems.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011i-15) In trophic systems, photosynthetic organisms are the primary producers. The organisms that consume their tissues are called primary consumers or [secondary producers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secondary_production "Secondary production")—[herbivores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbivores "Herbivores"). Organisms which feed on [microbes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbe "Microbe") ([bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria "Bacteria") and [fungi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungi "Fungi")) are termed [microbivores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbivore "Microbivore"). Animals that feed on primary consumers—[carnivores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carnivore "Carnivore")—are secondary consumers. Each of these constitutes a trophic level.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011i-15)

The sequence of consumption—from plant to herbivore, to carnivore—forms a [food chain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_chain "Food chain"). Real systems are much more complex than this—organisms will generally feed on more than one form of food, and may feed at more than one trophic level. Carnivores may capture some prey that is part of a plant-based trophic system and others that are part of a detritus-based trophic system (a bird that feeds both on herbivorous grasshoppers and earthworms, which consume detritus). Real systems, with all these complexities, form [food webs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_web "Food web") rather than food chains which present a number of common, non random properties in the topology of their network.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-18)

### Decomposition

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Decomposition_stages.jpg/750px-Decomposition_stages.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Decomposition_stages.jpg)

Sequence of a decomposing pig carcass over time

The carbon and nutrients in [dead organic matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil_organic_matter "Soil organic matter") are broken down by a group of processes known as decomposition. This releases nutrients that can then be re-used for plant and microbial production and returns carbon dioxide to the atmosphere (or water) where it can be used for photosynthesis. In the absence of decomposition, the dead organic matter would accumulate in an ecosystem, and nutrients and atmospheric carbon dioxide would be depleted.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011f-19): 183

Decomposition processes can be separated into three categories—[leaching](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leaching_(agriculture) "Leaching (agriculture)"), fragmentation and chemical alteration of dead material. As water moves through dead organic matter, it dissolves and carries with it the water-soluble components. These are then taken up by organisms in the soil, react with mineral soil, or are transported beyond the confines of the ecosystem (and are considered lost to it).[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011h-20): 271–280  Newly shed leaves and newly dead animals have high concentrations of water-soluble components and include [sugars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sugar "Sugar"), [amino acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid "Amino acid") and mineral nutrients. Leaching is more important in wet environments and less important in dry ones.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011c-10): 69–77

Fragmentation processes break organic material into smaller pieces, exposing new surfaces for colonization by microbes. Freshly shed [leaf litter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leaf_litter "Leaf litter") may be inaccessible due to an outer layer of [cuticle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant_cuticle "Plant cuticle") or [bark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bark_(botany) "Bark (botany)"), and [cell contents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protoplasm "Protoplasm") are protected by a [cell wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_wall "Cell wall"). Newly dead animals may be covered by an [exoskeleton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exoskeleton "Exoskeleton"). Fragmentation processes, which break through these protective layers, accelerate the rate of microbial decomposition.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011f-19): 184  Animals fragment detritus as they hunt for food, as does passage through the gut. [Freeze-thaw cycles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freeze-thaw_cycle "Freeze-thaw cycle") and cycles of wetting and drying also fragment dead material.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011f-19): 186

The chemical alteration of the dead organic matter is primarily achieved through bacterial and fungal action. Fungal [hyphae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypha "Hypha") produce enzymes that can break through the tough outer structures surrounding dead plant material. They also produce enzymes that break down [lignin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lignin "Lignin"), which allows them access to both cell contents and the nitrogen in the lignin. Fungi can transfer carbon and nitrogen through their hyphal networks and thus, unlike bacteria, are not dependent solely on locally available resources.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011f-19): 186

#### Decomposition rates

Decomposition rates vary among ecosystems.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Ochoa-Hueso-2019-21) The rate of decomposition is governed by three sets of factors—the physical environment (temperature, moisture, and soil properties), the quantity and quality of the dead material available to decomposers, and the nature of the microbial community itself.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011f-19): 194  Temperature controls the rate of microbial respiration; the higher the temperature, the faster the microbial decomposition occurs. Temperature also affects soil moisture, which affects decomposition. Freeze-thaw cycles also affect decomposition—freezing temperatures kill soil microorganisms, which allows leaching to play a more important role in moving nutrients around. This can be especially important as the soil thaws in the spring, creating a pulse of nutrients that become available.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011h-20): 280

Decomposition rates are low under very wet or very dry conditions. Decomposition rates are highest in wet, moist conditions with adequate levels of oxygen. Wet soils tend to become deficient in oxygen (this is especially true in [wetlands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wetland "Wetland")), which slows microbial growth. In dry soils, decomposition slows as well, but bacteria continue to grow (albeit at a slower rate) even after soils become too dry to support plant growth.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011f-19): 200

### Dynamics and resilience

Ecosystems are dynamic entities. They are subject to periodic disturbances and are always in the process of recovering from past disturbances.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011k-22): 347  When a [perturbation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perturbation_(biology) "Perturbation (biology)") occurs, an ecosystem responds by moving away from its initial state. The tendency of an ecosystem to remain close to its equilibrium state, despite that disturbance, is termed its [resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resistance_(ecology) "Resistance (ecology)"). The capacity of a system to absorb disturbance and reorganize while undergoing change so as to retain essentially the same function, structure, identity, and feedbacks is termed its [ecological resilience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecological_resilience "Ecological resilience").[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-23)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-24) Resilience thinking also includes humanity as an integral part of the [biosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere "Biosphere") where we are dependent on [ecosystem services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem_services "Ecosystem services") for our survival and must build and maintain their natural capacities to withstand shocks and disturbances.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-25) Time plays a central role over a wide range, for example, in the slow development of soil from bare rock and the faster [recovery of a community from disturbance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecological_succession "Ecological succession").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011e-14): 67

[Disturbance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disturbance_(ecology) "Disturbance (ecology)") also plays an important role in ecological processes. [F. Stuart Chapin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/F._Stuart_Chapin_III "F. Stuart Chapin III") and coauthors define disturbance as "a relatively discrete event in time that removes plant biomass".[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011k-22): 346  This can range from [herbivore](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbivore "Herbivore") outbreaks, treefalls, fires, hurricanes, floods, [glacial advances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glacial_motion "Glacial motion"), to [volcanic eruptions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Types_of_volcanic_eruptions "Types of volcanic eruptions"). Such disturbances can cause large changes in plant, animal and microbe populations, as well as soil organic matter content. Disturbance is followed by succession, a "directional change in ecosystem structure and functioning resulting from biotically driven changes in resource supply."[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011m-2): 470

The frequency and severity of disturbance determine the way it affects ecosystem function. A major disturbance like a volcanic eruption or [glacial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glacier "Glacier") advance and retreat leave behind soils that lack plants, animals or organic matter. Ecosystems that experience such disturbances undergo [primary succession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_succession "Primary succession"). A less severe disturbance like forest fires, hurricanes or cultivation result in [secondary succession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secondary_succession "Secondary succession") and a faster recovery.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011k-22): 348  More severe and more frequent disturbance result in longer recovery times.

From one year to another, ecosystems experience variation in their biotic and abiotic environments. A [drought](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drought "Drought"), a colder than usual winter, and a pest outbreak all are short-term variability in environmental conditions. Animal populations vary from year to year, building up during resource-rich periods and crashing as they overshoot their food supply. Longer-term changes also shape ecosystem processes. For example, the forests of eastern North America still show legacies of [cultivation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agriculture "Agriculture") which ceased in 1850 when large areas were reverted to forests.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011k-22): 340  Another example is the [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane") production in eastern [Siberian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siberia "Siberia") lakes that is controlled by [organic matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_matter "Organic matter") which accumulated during the [Pleistocene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pleistocene "Pleistocene").[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-26)

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/f/f8/Panorama_presa_las_ni%C3%B1as_mogan_gran_canaria.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Panorama_presa_las_ni%C3%B1as_mogan_gran_canaria.jpg)

### Nutrient cycling

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Nitrogen_Cycle.jpg/450px-Nitrogen_Cycle.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Nitrogen_Cycle.jpg)

Biological nitrogen cycling

Ecosystems continually exchange energy and carbon with the wider [environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environment_(systems) "Environment (systems)"). Mineral nutrients, on the other hand, are mostly cycled back and forth between plants, animals, microbes and the soil. Most nitrogen enters ecosystems through biological [nitrogen fixation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation "Nitrogen fixation"), is deposited through precipitation, dust, gases or is applied as [fertilizer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fertilizer "Fertilizer").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011h-20): 266  Most [terrestrial ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrestrial_ecosystems "Terrestrial ecosystems") are nitrogen-limited in the short term making [nitrogen cycling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle "Nitrogen cycle") an important control on ecosystem production.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011h-20): 289  Over the long term, phosphorus availability can also be critical.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-27)

Macronutrients which are required by all plants in large quantities include the primary nutrients (which are most limiting as they are used in largest amounts): Nitrogen, phosphorus, potassium.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011g-28): 231  Secondary major nutrients (less often limiting) include: Calcium, magnesium, sulfur. [Micronutrients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micronutrient "Micronutrient") required by all plants in small quantities include boron, chloride, copper, iron, manganese, molybdenum, zinc. Finally, there are also beneficial nutrients which may be required by certain plants or by plants under specific environmental conditions: aluminum, cobalt, iodine, nickel, selenium, silicon, sodium, vanadium.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011g-28): 231

Until modern times, nitrogen fixation was the major source of nitrogen for ecosystems. Nitrogen-fixing bacteria either live [symbiotically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbiosis "Symbiosis") with plants or live freely in the soil. The energetic cost is high for plants that support nitrogen-fixing symbionts—as much as 25% of gross primary production when measured in controlled conditions. Many members of the [legume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legume "Legume") plant family support nitrogen-fixing symbionts. Some [cyanobacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanobacteria "Cyanobacteria") are also capable of nitrogen fixation. These are [phototrophs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phototroph "Phototroph"), which carry out photosynthesis. Like other nitrogen-fixing bacteria, they can either be free-living or have symbiotic relationships with plants.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011k-22): 360  Other sources of nitrogen include [acid deposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid_deposition "Acid deposition") produced through the combustion of fossil fuels, [ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia") gas which evaporates from agricultural fields which have had fertilizers applied to them, and dust.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011h-20): 270  Anthropogenic nitrogen inputs account for about 80% of all nitrogen fluxes in ecosystems.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011h-20): 270

When plant tissues are shed or are eaten, the nitrogen in those tissues becomes available to animals and microbes. Microbial decomposition releases nitrogen compounds from dead organic matter in the soil, where plants, fungi, and bacteria compete for it. Some soil bacteria use organic nitrogen-containing compounds as a source of carbon, and release [ammonium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium "Ammonium") ions into the soil. This process is known as [nitrogen mineralization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonification "Ammonification"). Others convert ammonium to [nitrite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrite "Nitrite") and [nitrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrate "Nitrate") ions, a process known as [nitrification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrification "Nitrification"). [Nitric oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitric_oxide "Nitric oxide") and [nitrous oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrous_oxide "Nitrous oxide") are also produced during nitrification.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011h-20): 277  Under nitrogen-rich and oxygen-poor conditions, nitrates and nitrites are converted to [nitrogen gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen"), a process known as [denitrification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denitrification "Denitrification").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011h-20): 281

Mycorrhizal fungi which are symbiotic with plant roots, use carbohydrates supplied by the plants and in return transfer phosphorus and nitrogen compounds back to the plant roots.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-29)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Hestrin-2019-30) This is an important pathway of organic nitrogen transfer from dead organic matter to plants. This mechanism may contribute to more than 70 Tg of annually assimilated plant nitrogen, thereby playing a critical role in global nutrient cycling and ecosystem function.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Hestrin-2019-30)

Phosphorus enters ecosystems through [weathering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weathering "Weathering"). As ecosystems age this supply diminishes, making phosphorus-limitation more common in older landscapes (especially in the tropics).[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011h-20): 287–290  Calcium and sulfur are also produced by weathering, but acid deposition is an important source of sulfur in many ecosystems. Although magnesium and manganese are produced by weathering, exchanges between soil organic matter and living cells account for a significant portion of ecosystem fluxes. Potassium is primarily cycled between living cells and soil organic matter.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011h-20): 291

### Function and biodiversity

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/View_of_loch_lomond.JPG/250px-View_of_loch_lomond.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:View_of_loch_lomond.JPG)

[Loch Lomond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loch_Lomond "Loch Lomond") in [Scotland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scotland "Scotland") forms a relatively isolated ecosystem. The fish community of this lake has remained stable over a long period until a number of [introductions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Introduced_species "Introduced species") in the 1970s restructured its [food web](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_web "Food web").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-31)

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Spiny_Forest_Ifaty_Madagascar.jpg/250px-Spiny_Forest_Ifaty_Madagascar.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Spiny_Forest_Ifaty_Madagascar.jpg)

Spiny forest at Ifaty, [Madagascar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madagascar "Madagascar"), featuring various _[Adansonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adansonia "Adansonia")_ (baobab) species, _[Alluaudia procera](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alluaudia\_procera "Alluaudia procera")_ (Madagascar ocotillo) and other vegetation

[Biodiversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity "Biodiversity") plays an important role in ecosystem functioning.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Schulze-2005-32): 449–453  Ecosystem processes are driven by the species in an ecosystem, the nature of the individual species, and the relative abundance of organisms among these species. Ecosystem processes are the net effect of the actions of individual organisms as they interact with their environment. [Ecological theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoretical_ecology "Theoretical ecology") suggests that in order to coexist, species must have some level of [limiting similarity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limiting_similarity "Limiting similarity")—they must be different from one another in some fundamental way, otherwise, one species would [competitively exclude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Competitive_exclusion "Competitive exclusion") the other.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Schoener-2009-33) Despite this, the cumulative effect of additional species in an ecosystem is not linear: additional species may enhance nitrogen retention, for example. However, beyond some level of species richness,[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011j-11): 331  additional species may have little additive effect unless they differ substantially from species already present.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011j-11): 324  This is the case for example for [exotic species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Introduced_species "Introduced species").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011j-11): 321

The addition (or loss) of species that are ecologically similar to those already present in an ecosystem tends to only have a small effect on ecosystem function. Ecologically distinct species, on the other hand, have a much larger effect. Similarly, dominant species have a large effect on ecosystem function, while rare species tend to have a small effect. [Keystone species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keystone_species "Keystone species") tend to have an effect on ecosystem function that is disproportionate to their abundance in an ecosystem.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011j-11): 324

An [ecosystem engineer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem_engineer "Ecosystem engineer") is any [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism "Organism") that creates, significantly modifies, maintains or destroys a [habitat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat_(ecology) "Habitat (ecology)").[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-34)

Study approaches
----------------

### Ecosystem ecology

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/East_Scotia_Ridge_-_Plos_Biol_04.tif/lossy-page1-250px-East_Scotia_Ridge_-_Plos_Biol_04.tif.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:East_Scotia_Ridge_-_Plos_Biol_04.tif)

A [hydrothermal vent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrothermal_vent "Hydrothermal vent") is an ecosystem on the ocean floor. (The scale bar is 1 m.)

[Ecosystem ecology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem_ecology "Ecosystem ecology") is the "study of the interactions between organisms and their environment as an integrated system".[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011m-2): 458  The size of ecosystems can range up to ten [orders of magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_of_magnitude "Order of magnitude"), from the surface layers of rocks to the surface of the planet.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011a-4): 6

The [Hubbard Brook Ecosystem Study](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubbard_Brook_Ecosystem_Study "Hubbard Brook Ecosystem Study") started in 1963 to study the [White Mountains in New Hampshire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_Mountains_(New_Hampshire) "White Mountains (New Hampshire)"). It was the first successful attempt to study an entire [watershed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Watershed_management "Watershed management") as an ecosystem. The study used stream [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry") as a means of monitoring ecosystem properties, and developed a detailed [biogeochemical model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biogeochemistry "Biogeochemistry") of the ecosystem.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Lindenmayer-2010-35)[Long-term research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Long_Term_Ecological_Research_Network "Long Term Ecological Research Network") at the site led to the discovery of [acid rain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid_rain "Acid rain") in North America in 1972. Researchers documented the depletion of soil [cations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cations "Cations") (especially calcium) over the next several decades.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Likens-2004-36)

Ecosystems can be studied through a variety of approaches—theoretical studies, studies monitoring specific ecosystems over long periods of time, those that look at differences between ecosystems to elucidate how they work and direct manipulative experimentation.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Carpenter-1998-37) Studies can be carried out at a variety of scales, ranging from whole-ecosystem studies to studying [microcosms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microcosm:_Model_/_experimental_ecosystem "Microcosm: Model / experimental ecosystem") or [mesocosms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesocosm "Mesocosm") (simplified representations of ecosystems).[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Schindler-1998-38) American ecologist [Stephen R. Carpenter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stephen_R._Carpenter "Stephen R. Carpenter") has argued that microcosm experiments can be "irrelevant and diversionary" if they are not carried out in conjunction with field studies done at the ecosystem scale. In such cases, microcosm experiments may fail to accurately predict ecosystem-level dynamics.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Carpenter-1996-39)

### Classifications

[Biomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biome "Biome") are general classes or categories of ecosystems.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011a-4): 14  However, there is no clear distinction between biomes and ecosystems.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-40) Biomes are always defined at a very general level. Ecosystems can be described at levels that range from very general (in which case the names are sometimes the same as those of biomes) to very specific, such as "wet coastal needle-leafed forests".

Biomes vary due to global variations in [climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate "Climate"). Biomes are often defined by their structure: at a general level, for example, [tropical forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_forest "Tropical forest"), [temperate grasslands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperate_grasslands,_savannas,_and_shrublands "Temperate grasslands, savannas, and shrublands"), and arctic [tundra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tundra "Tundra").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011a-4): 14  There can be any degree of subcategories among ecosystem types that comprise a biome, e.g., needle-leafed [boreal forests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taiga "Taiga") or wet tropical forests. Although ecosystems are most commonly categorized by their structure and geography, there are also other ways to categorize and classify ecosystems such as by their level of human impact (see [anthropogenic biome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthropogenic_biome "Anthropogenic biome")), or by their integration with social processes or technological processes or their novelty (e.g. [novel ecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Novel_ecosystem "Novel ecosystem")). Each of these [taxonomies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxonomy "Taxonomy") of ecosystems tends to emphasize different structural or functional properties.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-IUCN-2020-41) None of these is the "best" classification.

[Ecosystem classifications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecological_classification "Ecological classification") are specific kinds of ecological classifications that consider all four elements of the definition of [ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystems "Ecosystems"): a biotic component, an [abiotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiotic "Abiotic") complex, the interactions between and within them, and the physical space they occupy.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-IUCN-2020-41) Different approaches to ecological classifications have been developed in terrestrial, freshwater and marine disciplines, and a function-based typology has been proposed to leverage the strengths of these different approaches into a unified system.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-42)

Human interactions with ecosystems
----------------------------------

Human activities are important in almost all ecosystems. Although humans exist and operate within ecosystems, their cumulative effects are large enough to influence external factors like climate.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011a-4): 14

### Ecosystem goods and services

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/78/Avalanche_Lake%2C_looking_south.jpg/250px-Avalanche_Lake%2C_looking_south.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Avalanche_Lake,_looking_south.jpg)

The [High Peaks Wilderness Area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_Peaks_Wilderness_Area "High Peaks Wilderness Area") in the 6,000,000-acre (2,400,000 ha) [Adirondack Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adirondack_Park "Adirondack Park") is an example of a diverse ecosystem.

Ecosystems provide a variety of goods and services upon which people depend.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Christensen-1996-43) Ecosystem goods include the "tangible, material products" of ecosystem processes such as water, food, fuel, construction material, and [medicinal plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medicinal_plant "Medicinal plant").[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-44)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Brown-2007-45) They also include less tangible items like [tourism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tourism "Tourism") and recreation, and genes from wild plants and animals that can be used to improve domestic species.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Christensen-1996-43)

[Ecosystem services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem_services "Ecosystem services"), on the other hand, are generally "improvements in the condition or location of things of value".[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Brown-2007-45) These include things like the maintenance of hydrological cycles, cleaning air and water, the maintenance of oxygen in the atmosphere, crop [pollination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pollination "Pollination") and even things like beauty, inspiration and opportunities for research.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Christensen-1996-43) While material from the ecosystem had traditionally been recognized as being the basis for things of economic value, ecosystem services tend to be taken for granted.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Brown-2007-45)

The _[Millennium Ecosystem Assessment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millennium\_Ecosystem\_Assessment "Millennium Ecosystem Assessment")_ is an international synthesis by over 1000 of the world's leading biological scientists that analyzes the state of the Earth's ecosystems and provides summaries and guidelines for decision-makers. The report identified four major categories of ecosystem services: provisioning, regulating, cultural and supporting services.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Millennium_Ecosystem_Assessment-2005-46) It concludes that human activity is having a significant and escalating impact on the biodiversity of the world ecosystems, reducing both their [resilience](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resilience_(ecology) "Resilience (ecology)") and [biocapacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biocapacity "Biocapacity"). The report refers to natural systems as humanity's "life-support system", providing essential ecosystem services. The assessment measures 24 ecosystem services and concludes that only four have shown improvement over the last 50 years, 15 are in serious decline, and five are in a precarious condition.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Millennium_Ecosystem_Assessment-2005-46): 6–19

The [Intergovernmental Science-Policy Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intergovernmental_Science-Policy_Platform_on_Biodiversity_and_Ecosystem_Services "Intergovernmental Science-Policy Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services") (IPBES) is an intergovernmental organization established to improve the interface between science and policy on issues of [biodiversity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity "Biodiversity") and ecosystem services.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-47)[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-48) It is intended to serve a similar role to the [Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intergovernmental_Panel_on_Climate_Change "Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change").[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-49)

Ecosystem services are limited and also threatened by human activities.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Ceccato-2014-50) To help inform decision-makers, many ecosystem services are being assigned economic values, often based on the cost of replacement with anthropogenic alternatives. The ongoing challenge of prescribing economic value to nature, for example through [biodiversity banking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity_banking "Biodiversity banking"), is prompting transdisciplinary shifts in how we recognize and manage the environment, [social responsibility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_responsibility "Social responsibility"), business opportunities, and our future as a species.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Ceccato-2014-50)

### Degradation and decline

As human population and per capita consumption grow, so do the resource demands imposed on ecosystems and the effects of the human [ecological footprint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecological_footprint "Ecological footprint"). Natural resources are vulnerable and limited. The environmental impacts of [anthropogenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_impact_on_the_environment "Human impact on the environment") actions are becoming more apparent. Problems for all ecosystems include: [environmental pollution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pollution "Pollution"), [climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Climate_change "Climate change") and [biodiversity loss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiversity_loss "Biodiversity loss"). For terrestrial ecosystems further threats include [air pollution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_pollution "Air pollution"), [soil degradation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil_retrogression_and_degradation "Soil retrogression and degradation"), and [deforestation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deforestation "Deforestation"). For [aquatic ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aquatic_ecosystems "Aquatic ecosystems") threats also include unsustainable exploitation of marine resources (for example [overfishing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Overfishing "Overfishing")), [marine pollution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_pollution "Marine pollution"), [microplastics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microplastics "Microplastics") pollution, the [effects of climate change on oceans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effects_of_climate_change_on_oceans "Effects of climate change on oceans") (e.g. warming and [acidification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ocean_acidification "Ocean acidification")), and building on coastal areas.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Alexander-1999-51)

Many ecosystems become degraded through human impacts, such as [soil loss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erosion "Erosion"), [air](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_pollution "Air pollution") and [water pollution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_pollution "Water pollution"), [habitat fragmentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitat_fragmentation "Habitat fragmentation"), [water diversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interbasin_transfer "Interbasin transfer"), [fire suppression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wildfire_suppression "Wildfire suppression"), and [introduced species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Introduced_species "Introduced species") and [invasive species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invasive_species "Invasive species").[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011l-52): 437

These threats can lead to abrupt transformation of the ecosystem or to gradual disruption of biotic processes and degradation of [abiotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiotic_component "Abiotic component") conditions of the ecosystem. Once the original ecosystem has lost its defining features, it is considered _[collapsed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem\_collapse "Ecosystem collapse")_ (see also [IUCN Red List of Ecosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUCN_Red_List_of_Ecosystems "IUCN Red List of Ecosystems")).[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Keith-2013-53) Ecosystem collapse could be reversible and in this way differs from [species extinction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Species_extinction "Species extinction").[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Boitani-2014-54) Quantitative assessments of the [risk of collapse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUCN_Red_List_of_Ecosystems "IUCN Red List of Ecosystems") are used as measures of conservation status and trends.

### Management

When [natural resource management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_resource_management "Natural resource management") is applied to whole ecosystems, rather than single species, it is termed [ecosystem management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem_management "Ecosystem management").[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Grumbine-1994-55) Although definitions of ecosystem management abound, there is a common set of principles which underlie these definitions: A fundamental principle is the long-term [sustainability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sustainability "Sustainability") of the production of goods and services by the ecosystem;[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011l-52) "intergenerational sustainability [is] a precondition for management, not an afterthought".[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Christensen-1996-43) While ecosystem management can be used as part of a plan for [wilderness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilderness "Wilderness") conservation, it can also be used in intensively managed ecosystems[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Christensen-1996-43) (see, for example, [agroecosystem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agroecosystem "Agroecosystem") and [close to nature forestry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Close_to_nature_forestry "Close to nature forestry")).

### Restoration and sustainable development

[Integrated conservation and development projects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrated_Conservation_and_Development_Project "Integrated Conservation and Development Project") (ICDPs) aim to address [conservation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_biology "Conservation biology") and human livelihood ([sustainable development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sustainable_development "Sustainable development")) concerns in [developing countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developing_country "Developing country") together, rather than separately as was often done in the past.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecosystem#cite_note-Chapin-2011l-52): 445
