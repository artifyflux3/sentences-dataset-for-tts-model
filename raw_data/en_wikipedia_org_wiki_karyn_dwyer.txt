**Karyn Elizabeth Dwyer** (22 March 1975 – 25 September 2018) was a Canadian actress, whose best known role was as Maggie in the 1999 film _[Better Than Chocolate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Better\_Than\_Chocolate "Better Than Chocolate")_.

Dwyer was the oldest of five children, three sons and two daughters, born into an [Irish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irish_people "Irish people")[Catholic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholicism "Catholicism") family in [Corner Brook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corner_Brook "Corner Brook"), [Newfoundland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newfoundland_and_Labrador "Newfoundland and Labrador"), Canada, on 22 March 1975.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karyn_Dwyer#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karyn_Dwyer#cite_note-2) Her father died at the age of 38. Her brother died at the age of 24. All her brothers were named after the [Boston Bruins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_Bruins "Boston Bruins"): Brad ([Park](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brad_Park "Brad Park")), Barry ([Beck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barry_Beck "Barry Beck")) and Paul ([Hurley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Hurley "Paul Hurley")).

Dwyer studied acting with the Youth Theatre. She made her stage debut at the Arts and Culture Center at the age of 10, playing the title role in Theatre Newfoundland and Labrador's production of _[Alice in Wonderland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alice%27s\_Adventures\_in\_Wonderland "Alice's Adventures in Wonderland")_ and went on to become an accomplished child stage actress performing in various theatres throughout Newfoundland and Labrador. She also performed in her school productions, won awards for acting, public speaking, singing and instrumental performance and wrote for the school newspaper. She moved to [Toronto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toronto "Toronto"), [Ontario](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ontario "Ontario"), to attend the [George Brown Theatre School](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Brown_Theatre_School "George Brown Theatre School"). Dwyer attended theatre school for one year and then began studying with [David Rotenberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Rotenberg "David Rotenberg")'s on-camera acting class also in Toronto and later John Riven's Meisner.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

After moving to Toronto, Dwyer landed her first film role acting opposite [David Cronenberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Cronenberg "David Cronenberg") in the independent Canadian film _Boozecan_. She wrote and starred in her one-woman show _Bad Girls_ at the Rivoli in Toronto. In 1994 she played Phoebe in _[As You Like It](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/As\_You\_Like\_It "As You Like It")_ opposite [Seana McKenna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seana_McKenna "Seana McKenna") and [Albert Schultz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Schultz "Albert Schultz") in the Du Maurier World Stage Theatre Festival.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

In 1999 Dwyer starred as the 19-year-old Maggie in the lesbian-themed film _[Better Than Chocolate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Better\_Than\_Chocolate "Better Than Chocolate")_, winning the role over hundreds of others who auditioned in a cross Canada search. The film won numerous audience choice awards at film festivals all around the world, was ranked 31st on _[The Hollywood Reporter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Hollywood\_Reporter "The Hollywood Reporter")'_ s Top 200 [independent films](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Independent_film "Independent film") list of 1999. It had one of Canada's highest international box office grosses and earned Dwyer a loyal cult following. _Better Than Chocolate_ opened to rave reviews at both the [Berlin Film Festival](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin_Film_Festival "Berlin Film Festival") and the [Vancouver International Film Festival](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vancouver_International_Film_Festival "Vancouver International Film Festival"). It was hailed by "[Variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variety_(magazine) "Variety (magazine)")" as _a terrifically entertaining romantic comedy_. "_[The Hollywood Reporter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Hollywood\_Reporter "The Hollywood Reporter")_" called Dwyer _the film's heart and soul_. _Better Than Chocolate_ ranked 31 on "The Hollywood Reporter"'s list of best independent films. The "[Chicago Tribune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chicago_Tribune "Chicago Tribune")" review declared that the _highlight of the movie is unquestionably Dwyer's performance as Maggie_.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Also that year, Dwyer played Summer Falls in the bigger budget [studio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Hollywood_movie_studios "List of Hollywood movie studios") film _[Superstar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superstar\_(1999\_film) "Superstar (1999 film)")_, with [Molly Shannon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molly_Shannon "Molly Shannon") and [Will Ferrell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Will_Ferrell "Will Ferrell") and produced by [Lorne Michaels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorne_Michaels "Lorne Michaels"). Dwyer returned to the stage playing the title role in [Native Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Native_Earth "Native Earth")'s _[Romeo and Juliet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romeo\_and\_Juliet "Romeo and Juliet")_, performance artist Sooze in [Eric Bogosian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eric_Bogosian "Eric Bogosian")'s _[Suburbia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suburbia\_(play) "Suburbia (play)")_ and originated the role of Carrie, a junkie prostitute in the experimental play _Exercises in Depravity_, which featured [R. H. Thomson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R._H._Thomson "R. H. Thomson").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karyn_Dwyer#cite_note-3)

She also starred in award-winning short films adapted from plays: _Pony_, adapted from _White Biting Dog_ by playwright [Judith Thompson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judith_Thompson "Judith Thompson"); _[Dying Like Ophelia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dying\_Like\_Ophelia "Dying Like Ophelia")_, adapted from _[Lion in the Streets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lion\_in\_the\_Streets "Lion in the Streets")_ also by playwright Judith Thompson; _Polished_, adapted from _Polished_ by playwright James Harkness. In 2005, Dwyer reunited with her _Better Than Chocolate_ director [Anne Wheeler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anne_Wheeler "Anne Wheeler"), guest starring in the awarding winning Canadian series _[This Is Wonderland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/This\_Is\_Wonderland "This Is Wonderland")_. True to her roots, Dwyer returned to Newfoundland and Labrador, where she studied filmmaking at NIFCO, guest starred on _[Republic of Doyle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic\_of\_Doyle "Republic of Doyle")_, and shared her talent at _For the Love of Learning, Inc_, a not-for-profit empowerment and creativity-based learning program, teaching theatre to Newfoundland and Labrador's youth to use their creativity to overcome social and / or economic obstacles. In 2006, Dwyer was voted one of the Top Ten Hottest Actresses and profiled as The Hottest Canadian on "The Hottest Canadian". She was also named a Newfoundland Entertainment Icon by "[The Newfoundland Herald](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Newfoundland_Herald "The Newfoundland Herald")".[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Dwyer died on 25 September 2018 at the age of 43 from suicide.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karyn_Dwyer#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karyn_Dwyer#cite_note-5)
