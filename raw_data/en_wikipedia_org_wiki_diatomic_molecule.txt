A [space-filling model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-filling_model "Space-filling model") of the diatomic molecule dinitrogen, N 2

**Diatomic molecules** (from [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language")_di-_'two') are [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule") composed of only two [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom"), of the same or different [chemical elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element"). If a diatomic molecule consists of two atoms of the same element, such as [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") (H 2) or [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") (O 2), then it is said to be [homonuclear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homonuclear_molecule "Homonuclear molecule"). Otherwise, if a diatomic molecule consists of two different atoms, such as [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide") (CO) or [nitric oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitric_oxide "Nitric oxide") (NO), the molecule is said to be [heteronuclear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heteronuclear_molecule "Heteronuclear molecule"). The bond in a homonuclear diatomic molecule is [non-polar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-polar "Non-polar").

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/90/Diatomic_molecules_periodic_table.svg/630px-Diatomic_molecules_periodic_table.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Diatomic_molecules_periodic_table.svg)

A [periodic table](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodic_table "Periodic table") showing the elements that exist as [homonuclear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homonuclear_molecule "Homonuclear molecule") diatomic molecules under typical laboratory conditions.

The only [chemical elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element") that form stable homonuclear diatomic molecules at [standard temperature and pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure "Standard temperature and pressure") (STP) (or at typical laboratory conditions of 1 [bar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bar_(pressure) "Bar (pressure)") and 25Â°C) are the [gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas "Gas") hydrogen (H 2), [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") (N 2), oxygen (O 2), [fluorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorine "Fluorine") (F 2), and [chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine") (Cl 2), and the liquid [bromine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bromine "Bromine") (Br 2).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_molecule#cite_note-1)

The [noble gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noble_gas "Noble gas") ([helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium"), [neon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neon "Neon"), [argon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argon "Argon"), [krypton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Krypton "Krypton"), [xenon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xenon "Xenon"), and [radon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radon "Radon")) are also gases at STP, but they are [monatomic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monatomic "Monatomic"). The homonuclear diatomic gases and noble gases together are called "elemental gases" or "molecular gases", to distinguish them from other gases that are [chemical compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_compound "Chemical compound").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_molecule#cite_note-2)

At slightly elevated temperatures, the halogens [bromine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bromine "Bromine") (Br 2) and [iodine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iodine "Iodine") (I 2) also form diatomic gases.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_molecule#cite_note-Chemistry-3) All halogens have been observed as diatomic molecules, except for [astatine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astatine "Astatine") and [tennessine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tennessine "Tennessine"), which are uncertain.

Other elements form diatomic molecules when evaporated, but these diatomic species repolymerize when cooled. Heating ("cracking") elemental phosphorus gives [diphosphorus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diphosphorus "Diphosphorus") (P 2). Sulfur vapor is mostly [disulfur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disulfur "Disulfur") (S 2). [Dilithium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dilithium "Dilithium") (Li 2) and [disodium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disodium "Disodium") (Na 2)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_molecule#cite_note-4) are known in the gas phase. Di[tungsten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tungsten "Tungsten") (W 2) and di[molybdenum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molybdenum "Molybdenum") (Mo 2) form with [sextuple bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sextuple_bond "Sextuple bond") in the gas phase. [Dirubidium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirubidium "Dirubidium") (Rb 2) is diatomic.

Heteronuclear molecules
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Diatomic_molecule&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Heteronuclear molecules")]

All other diatomic molecules are [chemical compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_compound "Chemical compound") of two different elements. Many elements can combine to form [heteronuclear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heteronuclear_molecule "Heteronuclear molecule") diatomic molecules, depending on temperature and pressure.

Examples are gases [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide") (CO), [nitric oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitric_oxide "Nitric oxide") (NO), and [hydrogen chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride "Hydrogen chloride") (HCl).

Many 1:1 [binary compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_compound "Binary compound") are not normally considered diatomic because they are [polymeric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymer "Polymer") at room temperature, but they form diatomic molecules when evaporated, for example gaseous MgO, SiO, and many others.

Hundreds of diatomic molecules have been identified[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_molecule#cite_note-5) in the environment of the Earth, in the laboratory, and in [interstellar space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_molecules_in_interstellar_space "List of molecules in interstellar space"). About 99% of the [Earth's atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere "Earth's atmosphere") is composed of two species of diatomic molecules: nitrogen (78%) and oxygen (21%). The natural abundance of [hydrogen (H 2)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") in the Earth's atmosphere is only of the order of parts per million, but H 2 is the most abundant diatomic molecule in the universe. The interstellar medium is dominated by hydrogen atoms.

All diatomic molecules are linear and characterized by a single parameter which is the [bond length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_length "Bond length") or distance between the two atoms. Diatomic nitrogen has a triple bond, diatomic oxygen has a double bond, and diatomic hydrogen, fluorine, chlorine, iodine, and bromine all have single bonds.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_molecule#cite_note-6)

Historical significance
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Diatomic_molecule&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Historical significance")]

Diatomic elements played an important role in the elucidation of the concepts of element, atom, and molecule in the 19th century, because some of the most common elements, such as hydrogen, oxygen, and nitrogen, occur as diatomic molecules. [John Dalton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Dalton "John Dalton")'s original atomic hypothesis assumed that all elements were monatomic and that the atoms in compounds would normally have the simplest atomic ratios with respect to one another. For example, Dalton assumed water's formula to be HO, giving the atomic weight of oxygen as eight times that of hydrogen,[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_molecule#cite_note-7) instead of the modern value of about 16. As a consequence, confusion existed regarding atomic weights and molecular formulas for about half a century.

As early as 1805, [Gay-Lussac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Louis_Gay-Lussac "Joseph Louis Gay-Lussac") and [von Humboldt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt "Alexander von Humboldt") showed that water is formed of two volumes of hydrogen and one volume of oxygen, and by 1811 [Amedeo Avogadro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amedeo_Avogadro "Amedeo Avogadro") had arrived at the correct interpretation of water's composition, based on what is now called [Avogadro's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avogadro%27s_law "Avogadro's law") and the assumption of diatomic elemental molecules. However, these results were mostly ignored until 1860, partly due to the belief that atoms of one element would have no [chemical affinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_affinity "Chemical affinity") toward atoms of the same element, and also partly due to apparent exceptions to Avogadro's law that were not explained until later in terms of dissociating molecules.

At the 1860 [Karlsruhe Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karlsruhe_Congress "Karlsruhe Congress") on atomic weights, [Cannizzaro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stanislao_Cannizzaro "Stanislao Cannizzaro") resurrected Avogadro's ideas and used them to produce a consistent table of atomic weights, which mostly agree with modern values. These weights were an important prerequisite for the discovery of the [periodic law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodic_law "Periodic law") by [Dmitri Mendeleev](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dmitri_Mendeleev "Dmitri Mendeleev") and [Lothar Meyer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lothar_Meyer "Lothar Meyer").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_molecule#cite_note-8)

Excited electronic states
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Diatomic_molecule&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Excited electronic states")]

Diatomic molecules are normally in their lowest or ground state, which conventionally is also known as the ![Image 3: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) state. When a gas of diatomic molecules is bombarded by energetic electrons, some of the molecules may be excited to higher electronic states, as occurs, for example, in the natural aurora; high-altitude nuclear explosions; and rocket-borne electron gun experiments.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_molecule#cite_note-gilmore1992-9) Such excitation can also occur when the gas absorbs light or other electromagnetic radiation. The excited states are unstable and naturally relax back to the ground state. Over various short time scales after the excitation (typically a fraction of a second, or sometimes longer than a second if the excited state is [metastable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metastability "Metastability")), transitions occur from higher to lower electronic states and ultimately to the ground state, and in each transition results a [photon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") is emitted. This emission is known as [fluorescence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorescence "Fluorescence"). Successively higher electronic states are conventionally named ![Image 4: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3), ![Image 5: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a), ![Image 6: {\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029), etc. (but this convention is not always followed, and sometimes lower case letters and alphabetically out-of-sequence letters are used, as in the example given below). The excitation energy must be greater than or equal to the energy of the electronic state in order for the excitation to occur.

The [molecular term symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_term_symbol "Molecular term symbol") is a shorthand expression of the angular momenta that characterize the electronic quantum states of a diatomic molecule, which are also [eigenstates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenstate "Eigenstate") of the electronic molecular [Hamiltonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_(quantum_mechanics) "Hamiltonian (quantum mechanics)"). It is also convenient, and common, to represent a diatomic molecule as two-point masses connected by a massless spring. The energies involved in the various motions of the molecule can then be broken down into three categories: the translational, rotational, and vibrational energies. While the study of vibrational energy level of the diatomic molecules can be described using the harmonic oscillator approximation or using the quantum vibrational interaction potentials.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_molecule#cite_note-Swati-13)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_molecule#cite_note-Al-Raeei-14) These potentials give more accurate energy levels because they take multiple vibrational effects into account.

### Comparison between rotational and vibrational energy spacings

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Diatomic_molecule&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Comparison between rotational and vibrational energy spacings")]

The spacing, and the energy of a typical spectroscopic transition, between vibrational energy levels is about 100 times greater than that of a typical transition between [rotational energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_energy "Rotational energy") levels.

The [good quantum numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Good_quantum_number "Good quantum number") for a diatomic molecule, as well as good approximations of rotational energy levels, can be obtained by modeling the molecule using [Hund's cases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hund%27s_cases "Hund's cases").

The mnemonics _BrINClHOF_, pronounced "Brinklehof",[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_molecule#cite_note-16)_HONClBrIF_, pronounced "Honkelbrif",[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_molecule#cite_note-sherman-17) âHOBrFINClâ, pronounced âHoberfinkelâ, and _HOFBrINCl_, pronounced "Hofbrinkle", have been coined to aid recall of the list of diatomic elements. Another method, for English-speakers, is the sentence: "_Never Have Fear of Ice Cold Beer_" as a representation of Nitrogen, Hydrogen, Fluorine, Oxygen, Iodine, Chlorine, Bromine.
