Traditionally, in two-dimensional [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **rhomboid** is a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") in which adjacent sides are of unequal lengths and angles are [non-right angled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle#Types_of_angles "Angle").

The terms "rhomboid" and "parallelogram" are often erroneously conflated with each other (i.e, when most people refer to a "parallelogram" they almost always mean a rhomboid, a specific subtype of parallelogram); however, while all rhomboids are parallelograms, not all parallelograms are rhomboids.

A parallelogram with sides of equal length ([equilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilateral "Equilateral")) is called a _[rhombus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombus "Rhombus")_ but not a rhomboid. A parallelogram with [right angled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle "Right angle") corners is a _[rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle "Rectangle")_ but not a rhomboid. A parallelogram is a rhomboid if it is neither a rhombus nor a rectangle.

History
-------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rhomboid&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: History")]

[Euclid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid "Euclid") introduced the term in his _[Elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s\_Elements "Euclid's Elements")_ in Book 1, Definition 22,

> _Of quadrilateral figures, a square is that which is both equilateral and right-angled; an oblong that which is right-angled but not equilateral; a rhombus that which is equilateral but not right-angled; and a rhomboid that which has its opposite sides and angles equal to one another but is neither equilateral nor right-angled. And let quadrilaterals other than these be called trapezia._

— Translation from the page of [D.E. Joyce](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_E._Joyce_(mathematician) "David E. Joyce (mathematician)"), Dept. Math. & Comp. Sci., Clark University [[1]](http://aleph0.clarku.edu/~djoyce/java/elements/bookI/defI22.html)

Euclid never used the definition of rhomboid again and introduced the word [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") in Proposition 34 of Book 1; _"In parallelogrammic areas the opposite sides and angles are equal to one another, and the diameter bisects the areas."_ Heath suggests that rhomboid was an older term already in use.

Symmetries
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rhomboid&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Symmetries")]

The rhomboid has no line of symmetry, but it has [rotational symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_symmetry "Rotational symmetry") of order 2.

Occurrence
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rhomboid&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Occurrence")]

### In biology

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rhomboid&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: In biology")]

In biology, rhomboid may describe a geometric rhomboid (e.g. the [rhomboid muscles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhomboid_muscles "Rhomboid muscles")) or a bilaterally-symmetrical [kite-shaped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kite_(geometry) "Kite (geometry)") or [diamond-shaped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombus "Rhombus") outline, as in [leaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_leaf_morphology#Leaf_and_leaflet_shapes "Glossary of leaf morphology") or [cephalopod fins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cephalopod_fin "Cephalopod fin").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhomboid#cite_note-1)

### In medicine

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rhomboid&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: In medicine")]

In a type of arthritis called [pseudogout](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudogout "Pseudogout"), crystals of [calcium pyrophosphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_pyrophosphate "Calcium pyrophosphate") dihydrate accumulate in the joint, causing inflammation. [Aspiration of the joint fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthrocentesis "Arthrocentesis") reveals rhomboid-shaped crystals under a microscope.

In anatomy, rhomboid-shaped muscles include the [rhomboid major muscle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhomboid_major_muscle "Rhomboid major muscle") and the [rhomboid minor muscle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhomboid_minor_muscle "Rhomboid minor muscle").
