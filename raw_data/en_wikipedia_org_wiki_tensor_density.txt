In [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), a **tensor density** or **relative tensor** is a generalization of the [tensor field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field "Tensor field") concept. A tensor density transforms as a tensor field when passing from one coordinate system to another (see [tensor field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field "Tensor field")), except that it is additionally multiplied or _weighted_ by a power ![Image 1: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7) of the [Jacobian determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_determinant "Jacobian determinant") of the coordinate transition function or its absolute value. A tensor density with a single index is called a **vector density**. A distinction is made among (authentic) tensor densities, pseudotensor densities, even tensor densities and odd tensor densities. Sometimes tensor densities with a negative weight ![Image 2: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7) are called **tensor capacity.**[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_density#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_density#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_density#cite_note-3) A tensor density can also be regarded as a [section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Section_(fiber_bundle) "Section (fiber bundle)") of the [tensor product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product "Tensor product") of a [tensor bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_bundle "Tensor bundle") with a [density bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_bundle "Density bundle").

In physics and related fields, it is often useful to work with the components of an algebraic object rather than the object itself. An example would be decomposing a vector into a sum of [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)") vectors weighted by some coefficients such as ![Image 3: {\displaystyle {\vec {v}}=c_{1}{\vec {e}}_{1}+c_{2}{\vec {e}}_{2}+c_{3}{\vec {e}}_{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0ada43413fa26851fb1802ec8e87666f919be11) where ![Image 4: {\displaystyle {\vec {v}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/85820588abd7333ef4d0c56539cb31c20e730753) is a vector in 3-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), ![Image 5: {\displaystyle c_{i}\in \mathbb {R} ^{1}{\text{ and }}{\vec {e}}_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb7fe2fae76df15a35aab12a30dd8080e68ee62a) are the usual standard basis vectors in Euclidean space. This is usually necessary for computational purposes, and can often be insightful when algebraic objects represent complex abstractions but their components have concrete interpretations. However, with this identification, one has to be careful to track changes of the underlying basis in which the quantity is expanded; it may in the course of a computation become expedient to [change the basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Change_of_basis "Change of basis") while the vector ![Image 6: {\displaystyle {\vec {v}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/85820588abd7333ef4d0c56539cb31c20e730753) remains fixed in physical space. More generally, if an algebraic object represents a geometric object, but is expressed in terms of a particular basis, then it is necessary to, when the basis is changed, also change the representation. Physicists will often call this representation of a geometric object a [tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") if it transforms under a sequence of [linear maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map") given a linear change of basis (although confusingly others call the underlying geometric object which hasn't changed under the coordinate transformation a "tensor", a convention this article strictly avoids). In general there are representations which transform in arbitrary ways depending on how the geometric invariant is reconstructed from the representation. In certain special cases it is convenient to use representations which transform almost like tensors, but with an additional, nonlinear factor in the transformation. A prototypical example is a matrix representing the cross product (area of spanned parallelogram) on ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/066b155c535a38739cc0c4b288324cbb7a4a227a) The representation is given by in the standard basis by ![Image 8: {\displaystyle {\vec {u}}\times {\vec {v}}={\begin{bmatrix}u_{1}&u_{2}\end{bmatrix}}{\begin{bmatrix}0&1\\-1&0\end{bmatrix}}{\begin{bmatrix}v_{1}\\v_{2}\end{bmatrix}}=u_{1}v_{2}-u_{2}v_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e8e85a185b9fef2b5396bc2dabd32df77ba0794b)

If we now try to express this same expression in a basis other than the standard basis, then the components of the vectors will change, say according to ![Image 9: {\textstyle {\begin{bmatrix}u'_{1}&u'_{2}\end{bmatrix}}^{\textsf {T}}=A{\begin{bmatrix}u_{1}&u_{2}\end{bmatrix}}^{\textsf {T}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d1c2c690bc924994a0f2e5305d6c80c96963adff) where ![Image 10: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) is some 2 by 2 matrix of real numbers. Given that the area of the spanned parallelogram is a geometric invariant, it cannot have changed under the change of basis, and so the new representation of this matrix must be: ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \left(A^{-1}\right)^{\textsf {T}}{\begin{bmatrix}0&1\\-1&0\end{bmatrix}}A^{-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a8aace51fc5b3973c2f521e8cea973b9b53f4b83) which, when expanded is just the original expression but multiplied by the determinant of ![Image 12: {\displaystyle A^{-1},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc5a7c51eddc55dce006ed7b1368fef589afb93c) which is also ![Image 13: {\textstyle {\frac {1}{\det A}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5cb8dee3e2bff3f32f6de3e4ea39f3460469c88) In fact this representation could be thought of as a two index tensor transformation, but instead, it is computationally easier to think of the tensor transformation rule as multiplication by ![Image 14: {\textstyle {\frac {1}{\det A}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79348b324d0b853145a60b706263dd65c1f95faf) rather than as 2 matrix multiplications (In fact in higher dimensions, the natural extension of this is ![Image 15: {\displaystyle n,n\times n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88ac4479b67aa40ac3ca191e42c214ec232aa820) matrix multiplications, which for large ![Image 16: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) is completely infeasible). Objects which transform in this way are called _tensor densities_ because they arise naturally when considering problems regarding areas and volumes, and so are frequently used in integration.

Some authors classify tensor densities into the two types called (authentic) tensor densities and pseudotensor densities in this article. Other authors classify them differently, into the types called even tensor densities and odd tensor densities. When a tensor density weight is an integer there is an equivalence between these approaches that depends upon whether the integer is even or odd.

Note that these classifications elucidate the different ways that tensor densities may transform somewhat pathologically under orientation-_reversing_ coordinate transformations. Regardless of their classifications into these types, there is only one way that tensor densities transform under orientation-_preserving_ coordinate transformations.

In this article we have chosen the convention that assigns a weight of +2 to ![Image 17: {\displaystyle g=\det \left(g_{\rho \sigma }\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/82a0f5e256c2dc4119a1afe9455aba950b90211a), the determinant of the [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor_(general_relativity) "Metric tensor (general relativity)") expressed with [covariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariance_and_contravariance_of_vectors "Covariance and contravariance of vectors") indices. With this choice, classical densities, like charge density, will be represented by tensor densities of weight +1. Some authors use a sign convention for weights that is the negation of that presented here.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_density#cite_note-4)

In contrast to the meaning used in this article, in general relativity "[pseudotensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudotensor "Pseudotensor")" sometimes means an object that does not transform like a tensor or relative tensor of any weight.

### Even and odd tensor densities

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_density&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Even and odd tensor densities")]

The transformations for even and odd tensor densities have the benefit of being well defined even when ![Image 30: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7) is not an integer. Thus one can speak of, say, an odd tensor density of weight +2 or an even tensor density of weight −1/2.

### Weights of zero and one

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_density&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Weights of zero and one")]

A tensor density of any type that has weight zero is also called an **absolute tensor**. An authentic tensor density of weight zero, which is also an even tensor density of weight zero, is also called an **ordinary tensor**.

If a weight is not specified but the word "relative" or "density" is used in a context where a specific weight is needed, it is usually assumed that the weight is +1.

### Algebraic properties

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_density&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Algebraic properties")]

1.   A [linear combination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_combination "Linear combination") (also known as a [weighted sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weighted_sum "Weighted sum")) of tensor densities of the same type and weight ![Image 35: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7) is again a tensor density of that type and weight.
2.   A product of two tensor densities of any types, and with weights ![Image 36: {\displaystyle W_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/74ab879909bd9762251f679bbb2fa738100baa45) and ![Image 37: {\displaystyle W_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/421467fcab2af38ddf3977b9adf66de8ef3abd57), is a tensor density of weight ![Image 38: {\displaystyle W_{1}+W_{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8fa59c463c663891059bd28aa36f1ce709bcbe8d) Furthermore, a product of authentic tensor densities and pseudotensor densities will be an authentic tensor density when an even number of the factors are pseudotensor densities; it will be a pseudotensor density when an odd number of the factors are pseudotensor densities. Similarly, a product of even tensor densities and odd tensor densities will be an even tensor density when an even number of the factors are odd tensor densities; it will be an odd tensor density when an odd number of the factors are odd tensor densities.
3.   The contraction of indices on a tensor density with weight ![Image 39: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7) again yields a tensor density of weight ![Image 40: {\displaystyle W.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/035a080f11445ba1e7745704a6031989a311a7d7)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_density#cite_note-7)
4.   Raising and lowering indices using the metric tensor (which is authentic, even, and of weight 0) leaves the weight unchanged,[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_density#cite_note-8) as can be proved by combining (2) and (3).

### Use of metric tensor to manipulate tensor densities

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_density&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Use of metric tensor to manipulate tensor densities")]

Consequently, an even tensor density, ![Image 67: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {T}}_{\nu \dots }^{\mu \dots },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a439bd3c3250dcbefc32ac5a5faeb1ba1532f43) of weight ![Image 68: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7), can be written in the form ![Image 69: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {T}}_{\nu \dots }^{\mu \dots }={\sqrt {-g}}\;^{W}T_{\nu \dots }^{\mu \dots }\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23930d60f5166a8bb1978b721e7111cc4e646e5f)

where ![Image 70: {\displaystyle T_{\nu \dots }^{\mu \dots }\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/887e3f7ed3687e9b7aac025a079cf7702fae280c) is an ordinary tensor. In a locally inertial coordinate system, where ![Image 71: {\displaystyle g_{\kappa \lambda }=\eta _{\kappa \lambda },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9e51e17fed1e498b67def6231fe8f970ad9a6fa) it will be the case that ![Image 72: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {T}}_{\nu \dots }^{\mu \dots }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/165c1e1f641e0cdfafa9fd8d609f52edac6af9b0) and ![Image 73: {\displaystyle T_{\nu \dots }^{\mu \dots }\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/887e3f7ed3687e9b7aac025a079cf7702fae280c) will be represented with the same numbers.

When using the metric connection ([Levi-Civita connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection "Levi-Civita connection")), the [covariant derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_derivative "Covariant derivative") of an even tensor density is defined as ![Image 74: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {T}}_{\nu \dots ;\alpha }^{\mu \dots }={\sqrt {-g}}\;^{W}T_{\nu \dots ;\alpha }^{\mu \dots }={\sqrt {-g}}\;^{W}\left({\sqrt {-g}}\;^{-W}{\mathfrak {T}}_{\nu \dots }^{\mu \dots }\right)_{;\alpha }\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/80dd144e4ae5d45d681871f9ee5b0e61e4fa9b2f)

For an arbitrary connection, the covariant derivative is defined by adding an extra term, namely ![Image 75: {\displaystyle -W\,\Gamma _{~\delta \alpha }^{\delta }\,{\mathfrak {T}}_{\nu \dots }^{\mu \dots }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c220edde585b718601d6828295708ac579d63ba1) to the expression that would be appropriate for the covariant derivative of an ordinary tensor.

Equivalently, the product rule is obeyed ![Image 76: {\displaystyle \left({\mathfrak {T}}_{\nu \dots }^{\mu \dots }{\mathfrak {S}}_{\tau \dots }^{\sigma \dots }\right)_{;\alpha }=\left({\mathfrak {T}}_{\nu \dots ;\alpha }^{\mu \dots }\right){\mathfrak {S}}_{\tau \dots }^{\sigma \dots }+{\mathfrak {T}}_{\nu \dots }^{\mu \dots }\left({\mathfrak {S}}_{\tau \dots ;\alpha }^{\sigma \dots }\right)\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88dce580f96b7284dbf371b7e3db8c1d14489ad6)

where, for the metric connection, the covariant derivative of any function of ![Image 77: {\displaystyle g_{\kappa \lambda }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29ca9aec92f4ab0e3c148c08aedc81bc5c8d6195) is always zero, ![Image 78: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}g_{\kappa \lambda ;\alpha }&=0\\\left({\sqrt {-g}}\;^{W}\right)_{;\alpha }&=\left({\sqrt {-g}}\;^{W}\right)_{,\alpha }-W\Gamma _{~\delta \alpha }^{\delta }{\sqrt {-g}}\;^{W}={\frac {W}{2}}g^{\kappa \lambda }g_{\kappa \lambda ,\alpha }{\sqrt {-g}}\;^{W}-W\Gamma _{~\delta \alpha }^{\delta }{\sqrt {-g}}\;^{W}=0\,.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee4cb4fb8349f92bde6ac59318e7b010b5879cd2)

The expression ![Image 79: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {-g}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8d5737dc40c4e185538cc69efb1487f5b33e6aca) is a scalar density. By the convention of this article it has a weight of +1.

The density of electric current ![Image 80: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {J}}^{\mu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8ca8078e727ffc93496301cc8dc39b031b8fc94b) (for example, ![Image 81: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {J}}^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5523151173340c02d7271661c4c8cdeffe2ae3bf) is the amount of electric charge crossing the 3-volume element ![Image 82: {\displaystyle dx^{3}\,dx^{4}\,dx^{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e3f6eb54a746c37bab8156c15c272fee9e25754d) divided by that element — do not use the metric in this calculation) is a contravariant vector density of weight +1. It is often written as ![Image 83: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {J}}^{\mu }=J^{\mu }{\sqrt {-g}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32bf76ad95e44508eeade9c741db3bffb871f44c) or ![Image 84: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {J}}^{\mu }=\varepsilon ^{\mu \alpha \beta \gamma }{\mathcal {J}}_{\alpha \beta \gamma }/3!,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/941a564b2dfba02944d18a760351b4769e1a6238) where ![Image 85: {\displaystyle J^{\mu }\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/26dc65d141aa11a8b3b0051f00f8d67a8e22b10c) and the [differential form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form")![Image 86: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {J}}_{\alpha \beta \gamma }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/97c4711c8db25393a4ab833cfb1b74c6dfebf888) are absolute tensors, and where ![Image 87: {\displaystyle \varepsilon ^{\mu \alpha \beta \gamma }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a262ff6f35fe034ead21dc8ab829f6caa000d6d) is the [Levi-Civita symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol "Levi-Civita symbol"); see below.

The density of [Lorentz force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_force "Lorentz force")![Image 88: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {f}}_{\mu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa8e4788e4953fc2f36791dc0ba18a8896410ef0) (that is, the linear momentum transferred from the electromagnetic field to matter within a 4-volume element ![Image 89: {\displaystyle dx^{1}\,dx^{2}\,dx^{3}\,dx^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/46f6f8c3f36b6254c7b250ea9aff682a3970cac4) divided by that element — do not use the metric in this calculation) is a covariant vector density of weight +1.
