Chemical compound

**Acrolein** (systematic name: **propenal**) is the simplest [unsaturated aldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha-beta_Unsaturated_carbonyl_compounds "Alpha-beta Unsaturated carbonyl compounds"). It is a colorless liquid with a foul and acrid aroma. The smell of burnt fat (as when [cooking oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cooking_oil "Cooking oil") is heated to its [smoke point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smoke_point "Smoke point")) is caused by [glycerol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycerol "Glycerol") in the burning fat breaking down into acrolein. It is produced industrially from [propene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene "Propene") and mainly used as a [biocide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biocide "Biocide") and a building block to other chemical compounds, such as the [amino acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid "Amino acid")[methionine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methionine "Methionine").

History
-------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acrolein&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: History")]

Acrolein was first named and characterized as an aldehyde by the Swedish chemist [Jöns Jacob Berzelius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J%C3%B6ns_Jacob_Berzelius "Jöns Jacob Berzelius") in 1839. He had been working with it as a thermal degradation product of [glycerol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycerol "Glycerol"), a material used in the manufacture of soap. The name is a contraction of 'acrid' (referring to its pungent smell) and 'oleum' (referring to its oil-like consistency). In the 20th century, acrolein became an important intermediate for the industrial production of [acrylic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrylic_acid "Acrylic acid") and acrylic plastics.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-4)

Production
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acrolein&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Production")]

Acrolein is prepared industrially by oxidation of [propene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propene "Propene"). The process uses air as the source of oxygen and requires [metal oxides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal_oxide "Metal oxide") as [heterogeneous catalysts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heterogeneous_catalyst "Heterogeneous catalyst"):[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-Ullmann-5)

[![Image 63](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5e/Acrolein_from_oxidation_of_propene.svg/500px-Acrolein_from_oxidation_of_propene.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Acrolein_from_oxidation_of_propene.svg)
About 500,000 tons of acrolein are produced in this way annually in North America, Europe, and Japan. Additionally, all [acrylic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrylic_acid "Acrylic acid") is produced via the transient formation of acrolein.

[Propane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propane "Propane") represents a promising but challenging feedstock for the synthesis of acrolein (and acrylic acid).The main challenge is in fact the overoxidation to this acid.

When [glycerol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycerol "Glycerol") (also called glycerin) is heated to 280°C, it decomposes into acrolein

[![Image 64](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Acrolein_from_glycerol_dehydration.svg/500px-Acrolein_from_glycerol_dehydration.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Acrolein_from_glycerol_dehydration.svg)
This route is attractive when glycerol is co-generated in the production of biodiesel from vegetable oils or animal fats. The dehydration of glycerol has been demonstrated but has not proven competitive with the route from [petrochemicals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petrochemical "Petrochemical").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-7)

### Niche or laboratory methods

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acrolein&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Niche or laboratory methods")]

The original industrial route to acrolein, developed by Degussa, involves [aldol condensation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aldol_condensation "Aldol condensation") of [formaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde "Formaldehyde") and [acetaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde "Acetaldehyde"):

[![Image 65](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Acrolein_from_formaldehyde_and_acetaldehyde.svg/500px-Acrolein_from_formaldehyde_and_acetaldehyde.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Acrolein_from_formaldehyde_and_acetaldehyde.svg)
Acrolein may also be produced on lab scale by the action of [potassium bisulfate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_bisulfate "Potassium bisulfate") on glycerol (glycerine). [[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-8)

[![Image 66](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Acrolein_from_glycerol_and_potassium_bisulfate.svg/500px-Acrolein_from_glycerol_and_potassium_bisulfate.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Acrolein_from_glycerol_and_potassium_bisulfate.svg)

Reactions
---------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acrolein&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Reactions")]

Acrolein is a relatively [electrophilic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrophile "Electrophile") compound and a reactive one, hence its high toxicity. It is a good [Michael acceptor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_acceptor "Michael acceptor"), hence its useful reaction with thiols. It forms [acetals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetal "Acetal") readily, a prominent one being the [spirocycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spirocycle "Spirocycle") derived from [pentaerythritol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentaerythritol "Pentaerythritol"), diallylidene pentaerythritol. Acrolein participates in many [Diels-Alder reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diels-Alder_reaction "Diels-Alder reaction"), even with itself. Via Diels-Alder reactions, it is a precursor to some commercial fragrances, including [myrac aldehyde ("lyral")](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myrac_aldehyde "Myrac aldehyde") and [norbornene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norbornene "Norbornene")-2-carboxaldehyde.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-Ullmann-5) The monomer [3,4-epoxycyclohexylmethyl-3',4'-epoxycyclohexane carboxylate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3,4-epoxycyclohexylmethyl-3%27,4%27-epoxycyclohexane_carboxylate "3,4-epoxycyclohexylmethyl-3',4'-epoxycyclohexane carboxylate") is also produced from acrolein via the intermediacy of [tetrahydrobenzaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahydrobenzaldehyde "Tetrahydrobenzaldehyde").

Uses
----

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acrolein&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Uses")]

### Military uses

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acrolein&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Military uses")]

Acrolein was used in warfare due to its irritant and blistering properties. The French used the chemical in their hand grenades and artillery shells[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-9) during [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I") under the name "Papite".[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-10)

### Biocide

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acrolein&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Biocide")]

Acrolein is mainly used as a [contact herbicide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contact_herbicide "Contact herbicide") to control submersed and floating weeds, as well as algae, in [irrigation canals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irrigation_canal "Irrigation canal"). It is used at a level of 10 ppm in irrigation and recirculating waters. In the [oil and gas industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oil_and_gas_industry "Oil and gas industry"), it is used as a [biocide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biocide "Biocide") in [drilling waters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drilling_water "Drilling water"), as well as a scavenger for [hydrogen sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide "Hydrogen sulfide") and [mercaptans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thiol "Thiol").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-Ullmann-5)

### Chemical precursor

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acrolein&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Chemical precursor")]

A number of useful compounds are made from acrolein, exploiting its bifunctionality. The amino acid [methionine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methionine "Methionine") is produced by addition of [methanethiol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanethiol "Methanethiol") followed by the [Strecker synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strecker_synthesis "Strecker synthesis"). Acrolein condenses with acetaldehyde and amines to give [methylpyridines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylpyridine "Methylpyridine").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-ul-11) It is also an intermediate in the [Skraup synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skraup_reaction "Skraup reaction") of [quinolines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quinoline "Quinoline").

Acrolein will polymerize in the presence of oxygen and in water at concentrations above 22%. The color and texture of the polymer depends on the conditions. The polymer is a clear, yellow solid. In water, it will form a hard, porous plastic.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Acrolein has been used as a fixative in preparation of biological specimens for [electron microscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_microscopy "Electron microscopy").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-Dykstra-12)

Health risks
------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acrolein&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Health risks")]

Acrolein is toxic and is a strong irritant for the skin, eyes, and nasal passages.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-Ullmann-5) The main metabolic pathway for acrolein is the [alkylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkylation "Alkylation") of [glutathione](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glutathione "Glutathione"). The [WHO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Health_Organization "World Health Organization") suggests a "tolerable oral acrolein intake" of 7.5 μg per day per kg of body weight. Although acrolein occurs in [French fries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_fries "French fries") (and other fried foods), the levels are only a few μg per kg.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-ToxRev-13) In response to occupational exposures to acrolein, the US [Occupational Safety and Health Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration "Occupational Safety and Health Administration") has set a [permissible exposure limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit "Permissible exposure limit") at 0.1 ppm (0.25 mg/m 3) at an eight-hour time-weighted average.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-cdc-14) Acrolein acts in an immunosuppressive manner and may promote regulatory cells,[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-15) thereby preventing the generation of allergies on the one hand, but also increasing the risk of cancer.

Acrolein was identified as one of the chemicals involved in the [2019 Kim Kim River toxic pollution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2019_Kim_Kim_River_toxic_pollution "2019 Kim Kim River toxic pollution") incident.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-16)

### Cigarette smoke

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acrolein&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Cigarette smoke")]

Connections exist between acrolein gas in the smoke from [tobacco cigarettes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tobacco_smoking "Tobacco smoking") and the risk of [lung cancer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung_cancer "Lung cancer").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-Feng-17) Acrolein is one of seven [toxicants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxicant "Toxicant") in [cigarette](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cigarette "Cigarette") smoke that are most associated with [respiratory tract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_tract "Respiratory tract")[carcinogenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carcinogenesis "Carcinogenesis").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-18) The mechanism of action of acrolein appears to involve induction of increased [reactive oxygen species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reactive_oxygen_species "Reactive oxygen species") and [DNA damage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA_damage_(naturally_occurring) "DNA damage (naturally occurring)") related to [oxidative stress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidative_stress "Oxidative stress").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-19)

In terms of the "noncarcinogenic health quotient"[_[jargon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual\_of\_Style#Technical\_language "Wikipedia:Manual of Style")_] for components in cigarette smoke, acrolein dominates, contributing 40 times more than the next component, [hydrogen cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide "Hydrogen cyanide").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-20) The acrolein content in cigarette smoke depends on the type of cigarette and added [glycerin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycerin "Glycerin"), making up to 220 μg acrolein per cigarette.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-21)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-22) Importantly, while the concentration of the constituents in mainstream smoke can be reduced by filters, this has no significant effect on the composition of the side-stream smoke where acrolein usually resides, and which is inhaled by [passive smoking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Passive_smoking "Passive smoking").[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-23)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-24)[E-cigarettes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E-cigarette "E-cigarette"), used normally, only generate "negligible" levels of acrolein (less than 10 μg "per puff").[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-McNeill2015-25)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-26)

### Chemotherapy metabolite

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acrolein&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Chemotherapy metabolite")]

[Cyclophosphamide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclophosphamide "Cyclophosphamide") and [ifosfamide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ifosfamide "Ifosfamide") treatment results in the production of acrolein.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-Paci-27) Acrolein produced during cyclophosphamide treatment collects in the urinary bladder and if untreated can cause hemorrhagic cystitis.

### Endogenous production

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acrolein&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Endogenous production")]

Acrolein is a component of [reuterin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reuterin "Reuterin").[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-28) Reuterin can be produced by gut microbes when glycerol is present. Microbe-produced reuterin is a potential resource of acrolein.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-29)

Analytical methods
------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Acrolein&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Analytical methods")]

The "acrolein test" is for the presence of [glycerin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glycerin "Glycerin") or [fats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fat "Fat"). A sample is heated with [potassium bisulfate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_bisulfate "Potassium bisulfate"), and acrolein is released if the test is positive. When a fat is heated strongly in the presence of a dehydrating agent such as potassium bisulfate (KHSO

4), the glycerol portion of the molecule is dehydrated to form the unsaturated [aldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aldehyde "Aldehyde"), acrolein (CH 2=CH–CHO), which has the odor peculiar to burnt cooking grease. More modern methods exist.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-ToxRev-13)

In the US, EPA methods 603 and 624.1 are designed to measure acrolein in industrial and municipal [wastewater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wastewater "Wastewater") streams.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-30)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrolein#cite_note-31)
