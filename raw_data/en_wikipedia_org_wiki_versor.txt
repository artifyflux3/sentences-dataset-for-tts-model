This article is about the quaternions of norm one. For other uses, see [Versor (disambiguation)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor_(disambiguation) "Versor (disambiguation)").

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **versor** is a [quaternion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion "Quaternion") of [norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion#Norm "Quaternion") one, also known as a **unit quaternion**. Each versor has the form

![Image 1: {\displaystyle u=\exp(a\mathbf {r} )=\cos a+\mathbf {r} \sin a,\quad \mathbf {r} ^{2}=-1,\quad a\in [0,\pi ],}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/befee51abcc1874a64aa7223e120c8d9a21fc707)
where the **r**2 = −1 condition means that **r** is an [imaginary unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit "Imaginary unit"). There is a [sphere of imaginary units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion#Square_roots_of_%E2%88%921 "Quaternion") in the quaternions. Note that the expression for a versor is just [Euler's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_formula "Euler's formula") for the imaginary unit **r**. In case _a_ = π/2 (a [right angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle "Right angle")), then ![Image 2: {\displaystyle u=\mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4e5ea42a6822b5a47256c531fc5270ff8cdbf926), and it is called a _[right versor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right\_versor "Right versor")_.

The mapping ![Image 3: {\displaystyle q\to u^{-1}qu}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/729aa42d072b8e3a2fa5e849f44ae3a5745c3a29) corresponds to [3-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space") rotation, and has the angle 2 _a_ about the axis **r** in [axis–angle representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axis%E2%80%93angle_representation "Axis–angle representation").

The collection of versors, with quaternion multiplication, forms a [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)"), and appears as a [3-sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3-sphere "3-sphere") in the 4-dimensional quaternion algebra.

Presentation on 3- and 2-spheres
--------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Versor&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Presentation on 3- and 2-spheres")]

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6a/Spherical_triangle.svg/250px-Spherical_triangle.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Spherical_triangle.svg)

arc _AB_ + arc _BC_ = arc _AC_

Hamilton denoted the **versor** of a quaternion _q_ by the symbol **U**_q_. He was then able to display the general quaternion in [polar coordinate form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_decomposition#Quaternion_polar_decomposition "Polar decomposition")

_q_ = **T**_q_**U**_q_,
where **T**_q_ is the norm of _q_. The norm of a versor is always equal to one; hence they occupy the unit [3-sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3-sphere "3-sphere") in ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \ \mathbb {H} \ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a769054089d056b14172d7c770260f441ee0023). Examples of versors include the eight elements of the [quaternion group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion_group "Quaternion group"). Of particular importance are the [right versors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Hamiltonian_quaternions#Right_versor "Classical Hamiltonian quaternions"), which have [angle π/2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle "Right angle"). These versors have zero scalar part, and so are [vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") of length one (unit vectors). The right versors form a [sphere of square roots of −1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion#Square_roots_of_%E2%88%921 "Quaternion") in the quaternion algebra. The generators _**i**_, _**j**_, and _**k**_ are examples of right versors, as well as their [additive inverses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Additive_inverse "Additive inverse"). Other versors include the twenty-four [Hurwitz quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurwitz_quaternion "Hurwitz quaternion") that have the norm 1 and form vertices of a [24-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/24-cell "24-cell") polychoron.

Hamilton defined a quaternion as the quotient of two vectors. A versor can be defined as the quotient of two unit vectors. For any fixed [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(geometry) "Plane (geometry)")Π the quotient of two unit vectors lying in Π depends only on the [angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle") (directed) between them, the same a as in the unit vector–angle representation of a versor explained above. That's why it may be natural to understand corresponding versors as directed [arcs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_(geometry) "Arc (geometry)") that connect pairs of unit vectors and lie on a [great circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_circle "Great circle") formed by intersection of Π with the [unit sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_sphere "Unit sphere"), where the plane Π passes through the origin. Arcs of the same direction and length (or, the same, [subtended angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_(geometry)#Length_of_an_arc_of_a_circle "Arc (geometry)") in [radians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian")) are [equipollent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equipollence_(geometry)#Extension "Equipollence (geometry)") and correspond to the same versor.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor#cite_note-1)

Such an arc, although lying in the [three-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space"), does not represent a path of a point rotating as described with the sandwiched product with the versor. Indeed, it represents the left multiplication action of the versor on quaternions that preserves the plane Π and the corresponding great circle of 3-vectors. The 3-dimensional rotation defined by the versor has the angle two times the arc's subtended angle, and preserves the same plane. It is a rotation about the corresponding vector **r**, that is [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") to Π.

On three unit vectors, Hamilton writes[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHamilton1899vol&nbsp;1,_p.&nbsp;146-2)

![Image 6: {\displaystyle q=\beta :\alpha =OB:OA\quad }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/482ac1fd1dbafce73716b02e3a58cbd2a4d8274e) and![Image 7: {\displaystyle q'=\gamma :\beta =OC:OB\quad }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ede0a16c24e57734b2f7da547ef1f9d0eeab1ad) imply![Image 8: {\displaystyle q'q=\gamma :\alpha =OC:OA~.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/032d9e09900930627029d76ac62d812934a6f69f)
Multiplication of quaternions of norm one corresponds to the (non-commutative) "addition" of great circle arcs on the unit sphere. Any pair of great circles either is the same circle or has two [intersection points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intersection_point "Intersection point"). Hence, one can always move the point B and the corresponding vector to one of these points such that the beginning of the second arc will be the same as the end of the first arc.

An equation

![Image 9: {\displaystyle \exp(c\ \mathbf {r} )\ \exp(a\ \mathbf {s} )=\exp(b\ \mathbf {t} )\ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50bec7681b25ba494696af329f77e52a5ab6c5e0)
implicitly specifies the unit vector–angle representation for the product of two versors. Its solution is an instance of the general [Campbell–Baker–Hausdorff formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Campbell%E2%80%93Baker%E2%80%93Hausdorff_formula "Campbell–Baker–Hausdorff formula") in [Lie group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group") theory. As the 3-sphere represented by versors in ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \ \mathbb {H} \ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a769054089d056b14172d7c770260f441ee0023) is a 3-parameter Lie group, practice with versor compositions is a step into [Lie theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_theory "Lie theory"). Evidently versors are the image of the [exponential map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_map_(Lie_theory) "Exponential map (Lie theory)") applied to a ball of radius π in the quaternion subspace of vectors.

Versors compose as aforementioned vector arcs, and Hamilton referred to this [group operation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") as "the sum of arcs", but as quaternions they simply multiply.

The geometry of [elliptic space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_space "Elliptic space") has been described as the space of versors.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor#cite_note-3)

### Representation of SO(3)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Versor&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Representation of SO(3)")]

The [orthogonal group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_group "Orthogonal group") in three dimensions, [rotation group SO(3)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_group_SO(3) "Rotation group SO(3)"), is frequently interpreted with versors via the [inner automorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_automorphism "Inner automorphism")![Image 11: {\displaystyle \ q\mapsto u^{-1}q\ u\ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/30fc5f98b1a45fd5a6cdb0eb5791dcacfac3ae2b) where u is a versor. Indeed, if

![Image 12: {\displaystyle \ u=\exp(a\ \mathbf {r} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/536ee8be15cc6fb305bbfd189ecec9047c0b4198) and vector **s** is perpendicular to **r**,
then

![Image 13: {\displaystyle u^{-1}\mathbf {s} \ u=\mathbf {s} \ \cos(2a)+\mathbf {s} \ \mathbf {r} \ \sin(2a)\ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2e30ee8c0bac589efb041e4592ae5492669fba2a)
by calculation.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor#cite_note-4) The plane ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \ \{\ x+y\ \mathbf {r} \ :\ (x,y)\in \mathbb {R} ^{2}\ \}\subset \mathbb {H} \ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5c3c4a25deaf82b1e6e58d8aa7f4cc4d4138ac2c) is isomorphic to ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \ \mathbb {C} \ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5557b4084d82d274d0a613148e21c20a657e9d11) and the inner automorphism, by commutativity, reduces to the identity mapping there. Since quaternions can be interpreted as an algebra of two complex dimensions, the rotation [action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_action_(mathematics) "Group action (mathematics)") can also be viewed through the [special unitary group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_unitary_group "Special unitary group")[SU(2)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SU(2) "SU(2)").

For a fixed **r**, versors of the form ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \ \exp(a\ \mathbf {r} )\ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/305b52f7e075251e36fc302f3b1427e782c36002) where ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \ a\in \left(-\pi ,\pi \ \right]\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45630a31d27f3bf01946d2ff044a7669dcbbd1fd) form a [subgroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subgroup "Subgroup") isomorphic to the [circle group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle_group "Circle group"). Orbits of the left multiplication action of this subgroup are fibers of a [fiber bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundle "Fiber bundle") over the 2-sphere, known as [Hopf fibration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hopf_fibration "Hopf fibration") in the case **r** = **i**; other vectors give isomorphic, but not identical fibrations. [Lyons (2003)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor#CITEREFLyons2003) gives an elementary introduction to quaternions to elucidate the Hopf fibration as a mapping on unit quaternions. He writes "the fibers of the Hopf map are circles in S 3".[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor#cite_note-5)

Versors have been used to represent rotations of the [Bloch sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bloch_sphere "Bloch sphere") with quaternion multiplication.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor#cite_note-6)

The facility of versors illustrate [elliptic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_geometry "Elliptic geometry"), in particular [elliptic space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_geometry#Elliptic_space "Elliptic geometry"), a three-dimensional realm of rotations. The versors are the points of this elliptic space, though they refer to [rotations in 4-dimensional Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotations_in_4-dimensional_Euclidean_space "Rotations in 4-dimensional Euclidean space"). Given two fixed versors u and v, the mapping ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \ q\mapsto u\ q\ v\ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9c2ab06adc76cbd955ea036daab1d1eb0c41225) is an _elliptic motion_. If one of the fixed versors is 1, then the motion is a _Clifford translation_ of the elliptic space, named after [William Kingdon Clifford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Kingdon_Clifford "William Kingdon Clifford") who was a proponent of the space. An elliptic line through versor u is ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \ \{\ u\ \exp(a\ \mathbf {r} )\ :\ 0\leq a<\pi \ \}~.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/444eedbc9d3405b0ccf9a18848cb8ae57d71f027) Parallelism in the space is expressed by [Clifford parallels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_parallel "Clifford parallel"). One of the methods of viewing elliptic space uses the [Cayley transform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cayley_transform "Cayley transform") to map the versors to ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \ \mathbb {R} ^{3}~.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/672dfaec4eabba61e77b117383b8f3dd716a97f4)

The set of all versors, with their multiplication as quaternions, forms a [continuous group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_group "Continuous group")_G_. For a fixed pair ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \ \{-\mathbf {r} ,+\mathbf {r} \ \}\ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/caf7ec94d8605f6999a2674070e7e8c849bec371) of right versors, ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \ G_{1}=\{\ \exp(a\ \mathbf {r} )\ :\ a\in \mathbb {R} \ \}\ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e1715ff91a43dab043ef60a36ffb78ce8ec74f9) is a [one-parameter subgroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-parameter_group "One-parameter group") that is isomorphic to the [circle group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle_group "Circle group").

Next consider the finite subgroups, beyond the [quaternion group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion_group "Quaternion group") Q 8:[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor#cite_note-8)

As noted by [Hurwitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adolf_Hurwitz "Adolf Hurwitz"), the 16 quaternions ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \ {\tfrac {\ 1\ }{2}}\left(\pm \mathbf {1} \pm \mathbf {i} \pm \mathbf {j} \pm \mathbf {k} \right)\ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e501d19d47aab36189e105766eae885877e9f5e0) all have norm one, so they are in _G_. Joined with Q 8, these unit [Hurwitz quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurwitz_quaternion "Hurwitz quaternion") form a group _G_ 2 of order 24 called the [binary tetrahedral group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_tetrahedral_group "Binary tetrahedral group"). The group elements, taken as points on S 3, form a [24-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/24-cell "24-cell").

By a process of [bitruncation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitruncation "Bitruncation") of the 24-cell, the [48-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/48-cell "48-cell") on _G_ is obtained, and these versors multiply as the [binary octahedral group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_octahedral_group "Binary octahedral group").

Another subgroup is formed by 120[icosians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosian "Icosian") which multiply in the manner of the [binary icosahedral group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_icosahedral_group "Binary icosahedral group").

A hyperbolic versor is a generalization of quaternionic versors to [indefinite orthogonal groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indefinite_orthogonal_group "Indefinite orthogonal group"), such as [Lorentz group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_group "Lorentz group"). It is defined as a quantity of the form

![Image 24: {\displaystyle \exp(a\mathbf {r} )=\cosh a+\mathbf {r} \sinh a~~}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/94241dd31ab325fe11ea2798a045b4f936c59d97) where ![Image 25: {\displaystyle ~~\mathbf {r} ^{2}=+1~.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/13b8c227b144b3e8d881b9bcc15aafdcb6b1386e)
Such elements arise in [split algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Split_algebra "Split algebra"), for example [split-complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Split-complex_number "Split-complex number") or [split-quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Split-quaternion "Split-quaternion"). It was the algebra of [tessarines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tessarines "Tessarines") discovered by [James Cockle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Cockle_(lawyer) "James Cockle (lawyer)") in 1848 that first provided hyperbolic versors. In fact, Cockle wrote the above equation (with **j** in place of **r**) when he found that the tessarines included the new type of imaginary element.

This versor was used by [Homersham Cox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homersham_Cox_(mathematician) "Homersham Cox (mathematician)") (1882/1883) in relation to quaternion multiplication.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor#cite_note-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor#cite_note-10) The primary exponent of hyperbolic versors was [Alexander Macfarlane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Macfarlane "Alexander Macfarlane"), as he worked to shape quaternion theory to serve physical science.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor#cite_note-11) He saw the modelling power of hyperbolic versors operating on the split-complex number plane, and in 1891 he introduced [hyperbolic quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_quaternion "Hyperbolic quaternion") to extend the concept to 4-space. Problems in that algebra led to use of [biquaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biquaternion "Biquaternion") after 1900. In a widely seen review, Macfarlane wrote:

... the root of a quadratic equation may be versor in nature or scalar in nature. If it is versor in nature, then the part affected by the radical involves the axis perpendicular to the plane of reference, and this is so, whether the radical involves the square root of minus one or not. In the former case the versor is circular, in the latter hyperbolic.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor#cite_note-12)
Today the concept of a [one-parameter group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-parameter_group "One-parameter group") subsumes the concepts of versor and hyperbolic versor as the terminology of [Sophus Lie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sophus_Lie "Sophus Lie") has replaced that of Hamilton and Macfarlane. In particular, for each **r** such that **r r** = +1 or **r r** = −1, the mapping ![Image 26: {\displaystyle a\mapsto \exp(a\,\mathbf {r} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/03463cae2759f76131e6b0c5a8ec6c0b71bfd1b2) takes the [real line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_line#In_real_algebras "Real line") to a group of hyperbolic or ordinary versors. In the ordinary case, when **r** and **−r** are [antipodes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antipodal_point "Antipodal point") on a sphere, the one-parameter groups have the same points but are oppositely directed. In physics, this aspect of [rotational symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_symmetry "Rotational symmetry") is termed a [doublet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doublet_(physics) "Doublet (physics)").

[Robb (1911)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor#CITEREFRobb1911) defined the parameter _[rapidity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rapidity "Rapidity")_, which specifies a change in [frame of reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_of_reference "Frame of reference"). This _rapidity_ parameter corresponds to the real variable in a one-parameter group of hyperbolic versors. With the further development of [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity") the action of a hyperbolic versor came to be called a [Lorentz boost](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_boost "Lorentz boost").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor#cite_note-13)

[Sophus Lie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sophus_Lie "Sophus Lie") was less than a year old when Hamilton first described quaternions, but Lie's name has become associated with all groups generated by exponentiation. The set of versors with their multiplication has been denoted Sl(1,q) by [Gilmore (1974)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor#CITEREFGilmore1974).[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor#cite_note-RG-14) Sl(1,q) is the [special linear group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_linear_group "Special linear group") of one dimension over quaternions, the "special" indicating that all elements are of norm one. The group is isomorphic to SU(2,c), a [special unitary group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_unitary_group "Special unitary group"), a frequently used designation since quaternions and versors are sometimes considered archaic for group theory. The [special orthogonal group SO(3,r) of rotations in three dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_group_SO(3) "Rotation group SO(3)") is closely related: it is a 2:1 homomorphic image of SU(2,c).

The subspace ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \ \{\ xi+yj+zk\ :\ x,y,z\in \mathbb {R} \ \}\subset \mathbb {H} \ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7c30b0e9be437146149d3f180773be5c2e58794) is called the [Lie algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra "Lie algebra") of the group of versors. The commutator product ![Image 28: {\displaystyle [u,v]=uv-vu\ ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/912c4417fb2a37c3a54d690f5adfec92a5c922e9) is just double the [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product") of two vectors, which forms the multiplication operation in the Lie algebra. The close relation to SU(1,c) and SO(3,r) is evident in the isomorphism of their Lie algebras.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor#cite_note-RG-14)

Lie groups that contain hyperbolic versors include the group on the [unit hyperbola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_hyperbola "Unit hyperbola") and the special unitary group [SU(1,1)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SU(1,1) "SU(1,1)").

The word is derived from Latin _versari_ = "to turn" with the suffix _-or_ forming a noun from the verb (i.e. _versor_ = "the turner"). It was introduced by [William Rowan Hamilton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Rowan_Hamilton "William Rowan Hamilton") in the 1840s in the context of his [quaternion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion "Quaternion") theory.

Versors in geometric algebra
----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Versor&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Versors in geometric algebra")]

The term "versor" is generalised in [geometric algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_algebra "Geometric algebra") to indicate a member ![Image 29: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) of the algebra that can be expressed as the product of invertible vectors, ![Image 30: {\displaystyle R=v_{1}v_{2}\cdots v_{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3650c39db2130e9c2ecd5325f0a98a226bbb0f37).[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHestenesSobczyk1984103-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDorstFontijneMann2007204-16)

Just as a quaternion versor ![Image 31: {\displaystyle u}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3e6bb763d22c20916ed4f0bb6bd49d7470cffd8) can be used to represent a rotation of a quaternion ![Image 32: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d) with mapping ![Image 33: {\displaystyle q\mapsto u^{-1}qu}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29c27fa23c9a1fec698f007eb24157585bb02244), so can a versor ![Image 34: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) in Geometric Algebra be used to represent the result of ![Image 35: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) reflections on a member ![Image 36: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) of the algebra with mapping ![Image 37: {\displaystyle A\mapsto (-1)^{k}RAR^{-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf076eb8945cc6343e1c650904b54ccda61986d5).

A rotation can be considered the result of two reflections, so it turns out a quaternion versor ![Image 38: {\displaystyle u}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3e6bb763d22c20916ed4f0bb6bd49d7470cffd8) can be identified as a 2-versor ![Image 39: {\displaystyle R=v_{1}v_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/527bf3548690b942ca7b73ae7dcdf68fb4fc73e6) in the geometric algebra of three real dimensions ![Image 40: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {G}}(3,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ab72edce2f9250739288e98ab783313534f9fbf).

In a departure from Hamilton's definition, multivector versors are not required to have unit norm, just to be invertible. Normalisation can still be useful however, so it is convenient to designate versors as _unit versors_ in a geometric algebra if ![Image 41: {\displaystyle R{\tilde {R}}=\pm 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b749d27da1afb7fa483fa9629f2b1d7da00a27f9), where the tilde denotes [reversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_algebra#Rotations "Geometric algebra") of the versor.
