Portion of the universe chosen for analysis

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Physical_systems-en.svg/250px-Physical_systems-en.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Physical_systems-en.svg)

[Weather map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weather_map "Weather map") as an example of a physical system

A **physical system** is a collection of [physical objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_object "Physical object") under study.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_system#cite_note-1) The collection differs from a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)"): all the objects must coexist and have some physical relationship.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_system#cite_note-2) In other words, it is a portion of the [physical universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_universe "Physical universe") chosen for analysis. Everything outside the system is known as the _[environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Environment\_(systems) "Environment (systems)")_, which is ignored except for its effects on the system.

The split between system and environment is the analyst's choice, generally made to simplify the analysis. For example, the water in a lake, the water in half of a lake, or an individual molecule of water in the lake can each be considered a physical system. An _[isolated system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isolated\_system "Isolated system")_ is one that has negligible interaction with its environment. Often a system in this sense is chosen to correspond to the more usual meaning of [system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/System "System"), such as a particular machine.

In the study of [quantum coherence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_coherence "Quantum coherence"), the "system" may refer to the microscopic properties of an object (e.g. the mean of a [pendulum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum "Pendulum") bob), while the relevant "environment" may be the internal [degrees of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(physics_and_chemistry) "Degrees of freedom (physics and chemistry)"), described classically by the pendulum's thermal vibrations. Because no quantum system is completely isolated from its surroundings,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_system#cite_note-3) it is important to develop a theoretical framework for treating these interactions in order to obtain an accurate understanding of [quantum systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_quantum_system "Open quantum system").

In [control theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Control_theory "Control theory"), a physical system being controlled (a "controlled system") is called a "[plant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant_(control_theory) "Plant (control theory)")".
