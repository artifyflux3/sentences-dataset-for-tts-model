In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **partial derivative** of a [function of several variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics)#MULTIVARIATE_FUNCTION "Function (mathematics)") is its [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") with respect to one of those variables, with the others held constant (as opposed to the [total derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_derivative "Total derivative"), in which all variables are allowed to vary). Partial derivatives are used in [vector calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus "Vector calculus") and [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry").

The partial derivative of a function ![Image 1: {\displaystyle f(x,y,\dots )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/76d0223679fa6d5d1f14842ff102708873ec6ed0) with respect to the variable ![Image 2: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) is variously denoted by

![Image 3: {\displaystyle f_{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb36605624ab2287dc5ec558513c625b88acfee3), ![Image 4: {\displaystyle f'_{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/569186b747ddd418d9ec413340f0d493c946c3b5), ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \partial _{x}f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61e205fae7044fcc8e7ee78d45bd60f51b77938f), ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \ D_{x}f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02037c5b6772a804bd370e88a1c24963e47c8511), ![Image 7: {\displaystyle D_{1}f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9b39ef4b9ce8af2ec07e986bf5ee4712504d7e4c), ![Image 8: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial }{\partial x}}f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0a86f79f3fbe9db71d0aa445112c5b0532709b4), or ![Image 9: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial f}{\partial x}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/253ee5c09b0115152cf10c41aa008c4c1e4e9601).

It can be thought of as the rate of change of the function in the ![Image 10: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4)-direction.

Sometimes, for ![Image 11: {\displaystyle z=f(x,y,\ldots )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fd49e9e0970da0d60e902324d5b855f92c62ecc), the partial derivative of ![Image 12: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98) with respect to ![Image 13: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) is denoted as ![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {\partial z}{\partial x}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f0f7701dcb1a54f56c1b1b6b920213e5f0631589) Since a partial derivative generally has the same arguments as the original function, its functional dependence is sometimes explicitly signified by the notation, such as in:

![Image 15: {\displaystyle f'_{x}(x,y,\ldots ),{\frac {\partial f}{\partial x}}(x,y,\ldots ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dbe2d13243c170c00c9d1b0533fd4ae0b83b69d0)

The symbol used to denote partial derivatives is [∂](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E2%88%82 "∂"). One of the first known uses of this symbol in mathematics is by [Marquis de Condorcet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marquis_de_Condorcet "Marquis de Condorcet") from 1770,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative#cite_note-Cajori_History_V2-1) who used it for [partial differences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_difference_equation "Partial difference equation"). The modern partial derivative notation was created by [Adrien-Marie Legendre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adrien-Marie_Legendre "Adrien-Marie Legendre") (1786), although he later abandoned it; [Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Gustav_Jacob_Jacobi "Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi") reintroduced the symbol in 1841.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative#cite_note-miller_earliest-2)

Like ordinary derivatives, the partial derivative is defined as a [limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_of_a_function "Limit of a function"). Let U be an [open subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set") of ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) and ![Image 17: {\displaystyle f:U\to \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/05f481901ff501baa824d1eab35eba6d9410ba57) a function. The partial derivative of f at the point ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} =(a_{1},\ldots ,a_{n})\in U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3f9c2d774cb3fa388b8e47e26370c6079f7ccff) with respect to the i-th variable _x_ _i_ is defined as

![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {\partial }{\partial x_{i}}}f(\mathbf {a} )&=\lim _{h\to 0}{\frac {f(a_{1},\ldots ,a_{i-1},a_{i}+h,a_{i+1}\,\ldots ,a_{n})\ -f(a_{1},\ldots ,a_{i},\dots ,a_{n})}{h}}\\&=\lim _{h\to 0}{\frac {f(\mathbf {a} +h\mathbf {e_{i}} )-f(\mathbf {a} )}{h}}\,.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3859e6a8799d3829ef293c25fde83fc539924a7f)

Where ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e_{i}} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2cafb0e24f75af7ddb0c594cf9ad4290d2a251ba) is the [unit vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector") of i-th variable _x_ _i_. Even if all partial derivatives ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \partial f/\partial x_{i}(a)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3c1917f58e98177cbeb24c95e4dfe97de6737a10) exist at a given point a, the function need not be [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function") there. However, if all partial derivatives exist in a [neighborhood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighborhood_(topology) "Neighborhood (topology)") of a and are continuous there, then f is [totally differentiable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_derivative "Total derivative") in that neighborhood and the total derivative is continuous. In this case, it is said that f is a _C_ 1 function. This can be used to generalize for vector valued functions, ![Image 22: {\displaystyle f:U\to \mathbb {R} ^{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8fa515d59788ce1d5710559e18f877be35871ee), by carefully using a componentwise argument.

The partial derivative ![Image 23: {\textstyle {\frac {\partial f}{\partial x}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/74ea04d099c68b829877f9405b8129b3900382d8) can be seen as another function defined on U and can again be partially differentiated. If the direction of derivative is _not_ repeated, it is called a _**mixed partial derivative**_. If all mixed second order partial derivatives are continuous at a point (or on a set), f is termed a _C_ 2 function at that point (or on that set); in this case, the partial derivatives can be exchanged by [Clairaut's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_of_second_derivatives#Schwarz's_theorem "Symmetry of second derivatives"):

![Image 24: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial ^{2}f}{\partial x_{i}\partial x_{j}}}={\frac {\partial ^{2}f}{\partial x_{j}\partial x_{i}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9ee1eff626708576007de868f76bf3a8186c1947)

Further information: [∂](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E2%88%82 "∂")

For the following examples, let f be a function in x, y, and z.

First-order partial derivatives:

![Image 25: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial f}{\partial x}}=f'_{x}=\partial _{x}f.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90b4ecc6ca905167ea37706ef93ab7dcf1f3cf8b)

Second-order partial derivatives:

![Image 26: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial ^{2}f}{\partial x^{2}}}=f''_{xx}=\partial _{xx}f=\partial _{x}^{2}f.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/771c789a569647095654e9322b7c216028fe83fb)

Second-order [mixed derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed_derivatives "Mixed derivatives"):

![Image 27: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial ^{2}f}{\partial y\,\partial x}}={\frac {\partial }{\partial y}}\left({\frac {\partial f}{\partial x}}\right)=(f'_{x})'_{y}=f''_{xy}=\partial _{yx}f=\partial _{y}\partial _{x}f.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2b6c23b5b37ca9685e60d1382db9cc28bbc529c)

Higher-order partial and mixed derivatives:

![Image 28: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial ^{i+j+k}f}{\partial x^{i}\partial y^{j}\partial z^{k}}}=f^{(i,j,k)}=\partial _{x}^{i}\partial _{y}^{j}\partial _{z}^{k}f.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ecf64bec791b6d31c9a968677b43d9a21c36dbf)

When dealing with functions of multiple variables, some of these variables may be related to each other, thus it may be necessary to specify explicitly which variables are being held constant to avoid ambiguity. In fields such as [statistical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_mechanics "Statistical mechanics"), the partial derivative of f with respect to x, holding y and z constant, is often expressed as

![Image 29: {\displaystyle \left({\frac {\partial f}{\partial x}}\right)_{y,z}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e9549cbe52dee0a103b4260fd64b8bca50547be)

Conventionally, for clarity and simplicity of notation, the partial derivative _function_ and the _value_ of the function at a specific point are [conflated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abuse_of_notation "Abuse of notation") by including the function arguments when the partial derivative symbol (Leibniz notation) is used. Thus, an expression like

![Image 30: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial f(x,y,z)}{\partial x}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e5417529a3e71b27a2f4cdd3a4786603a073d5f)

is used for the function, while

![Image 31: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial f(u,v,w)}{\partial u}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4ccd61aab2ed3ae545027d266dd36c906dd1bfec)

might be used for the value of the function at the point ![Image 32: {\displaystyle (x,y,z)=(u,v,w)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7dd7e6f4bc4d4f67c971409c1659dd2efe6b2099). However, this convention breaks down when we want to evaluate the partial derivative at a point like ![Image 33: {\displaystyle (x,y,z)=(17,u+v,v^{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8046e92ce2287f51964d8ef905fb59f57f3c755a). In such a case, evaluation of the function must be expressed in an unwieldy manner as

![Image 34: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial f(x,y,z)}{\partial x}}(17,u+v,v^{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89737667adc346c3a1449c535890c46c6ee1dcfd)

or

![Image 35: {\displaystyle \left.{\frac {\partial f(x,y,z)}{\partial x}}\right|_{(x,y,z)=(17,u+v,v^{2})}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eba45909fa23da4f59c087792cc6f2afd2966f8e)

in order to use the Leibniz notation. Thus, in these cases, it may be preferable to use the Euler differential operator notation with ![Image 36: {\displaystyle D_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9f07b53d3212e08ca316a536c8aac0bbefa79ee1) as the partial derivative symbol with respect to the i-th variable. For instance, one would write ![Image 37: {\displaystyle D_{1}f(17,u+v,v^{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/49eb31e5b25e35396206e1c36bda673bc3c13609) for the example described above, while the expression ![Image 38: {\displaystyle D_{1}f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9b39ef4b9ce8af2ec07e986bf5ee4712504d7e4c) represents the partial derivative _function_ with respect to the first variable.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative#cite_note-3)

For higher order partial derivatives, the partial derivative (function) of ![Image 39: {\displaystyle D_{i}f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6595a62c7d45801da5db9fac4c9588afee8051fe) with respect to the j-th variable is denoted ![Image 40: {\displaystyle D_{j}(D_{i}f)=D_{i,j}f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6c956e66f3a7482fc3aeae7a75d0eced077db234). That is, ![Image 41: {\displaystyle D_{j}\circ D_{i}=D_{i,j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed9b7fbc78f4313c02fe4ccae9413693816fda60), so that the variables are listed in the order in which the derivatives are taken, and thus, in reverse order of how the composition of operators is usually notated. Of course, [Clairaut's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clairaut%27s_theorem_on_equality_of_mixed_partials "Clairaut's theorem on equality of mixed partials") implies that ![Image 42: {\displaystyle D_{i,j}=D_{j,i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9108317cf4f94eadb0f5d01b623c95e3af13f5ba) as long as comparatively mild regularity conditions on f are satisfied.

An important example of a function of several variables is the case of a [scalar-valued function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar-valued_function "Scalar-valued function")![Image 43: {\displaystyle f(x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61d6311d8c66acc1a5755c4c7cb688d3b1fa0fcb) on a domain in Euclidean space ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) (e.g., on ![Image 45: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd) or ![Image 46: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5)). In this case f has a partial derivative ![Image 47: {\displaystyle \partial f/\partial x_{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21e6cef47d7d31e82d5445a261916e1feaa977da) with respect to each variable _x_ _j_. At the point a, these partial derivatives define the vector

![Image 48: {\displaystyle \nabla f(a)=\left({\frac {\partial f}{\partial x_{1}}}(a),\ldots ,{\frac {\partial f}{\partial x_{n}}}(a)\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5f7ccfa82b2a346312f4a0e89e0b19f930247a09)

This vector is called the _[gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient")_ of f at a. If f is differentiable at every point in some domain, then the gradient is a vector-valued function ∇_f_ which takes the point a to the vector ∇_f_(_a_). Consequently, the gradient produces a [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field").

A common [abuse of notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abuse_of_notation "Abuse of notation") is to define the [del operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Del_operator "Del operator") (∇) as follows in three-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space")![Image 49: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) with [unit vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vectors "Unit vectors")![Image 50: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {i} }},{\hat {\mathbf {j} }},{\hat {\mathbf {k} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99e7e9edf82e2cb750f3b94e90bb371e01e63e93):

![Image 51: {\displaystyle \nabla =\left[{\frac {\partial }{\partial x}}\right]{\hat {\mathbf {i} }}+\left[{\frac {\partial }{\partial y}}\right]{\hat {\mathbf {j} }}+\left[{\frac {\partial }{\partial z}}\right]{\hat {\mathbf {k} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7c70b5bce4676294a2be68361d333b3f44ce478)

Or, more generally, for n-dimensional Euclidean space ![Image 52: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) with coordinates ![Image 53: {\displaystyle x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/737e02a5fbf8bc31d443c91025339f9fd1de1065) and unit vectors ![Image 54: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {e} }}_{1},\ldots ,{\hat {\mathbf {e} }}_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb7becfd5b147ae60b1fe8232a6061ef694568f7):

![Image 55: {\displaystyle \nabla =\sum _{j=1}^{n}\left[{\frac {\partial }{\partial x_{j}}}\right]{\hat {\mathbf {e} }}_{j}=\left[{\frac {\partial }{\partial x_{1}}}\right]{\hat {\mathbf {e} }}_{1}+\left[{\frac {\partial }{\partial x_{2}}}\right]{\hat {\mathbf {e} }}_{2}+\dots +\left[{\frac {\partial }{\partial x_{n}}}\right]{\hat {\mathbf {e} }}_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4449e767ec489248b3a285415bf722bd550932c7)

Directional derivative
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Partial_derivative&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Directional derivative")]

Suppose that f is a function of more than one variable. For instance,

![Image 56: {\displaystyle z=f(x,y)=x^{2}+xy+y^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/039900ead89e94ecedee6c6d6242d349890c708d)

[![Image 57](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/Partial_func_eg.svg/250px-Partial_func_eg.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Partial_func_eg.svg)

A graph of _z_ = _x_ 2 + _xy_ + _y_ 2. For the partial derivative at (1, 1) that leaves y constant, the corresponding [tangent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent "Tangent") line is parallel to the xz-plane.

[![Image 58](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fe/X2%2BX%2B1.svg/250px-X2%2BX%2B1.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:X2%2BX%2B1.svg)

A slice of the graph above showing the function in the xz-plane at _y_ = 1. The two axes are shown here with different scales. The slope of the tangent line is 3.

The [graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_function "Graph of a function") of this function defines a [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(topology) "Surface (topology)") in [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"). To every point on this surface, there are an infinite number of [tangent lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_line "Tangent line"). Partial differentiation is the act of choosing one of these lines and finding its [slope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slope "Slope"). Usually, the lines of most interest are those that are parallel to the xz-plane, and those that are parallel to the yz-plane (which result from holding either y or x constant, respectively).

To find the slope of the line tangent to the function at _P_(1, 1) and parallel to the xz-plane, we treat y as a constant. The graph and this plane are shown on the right. Below, we see how the function looks on the plane _y_ = 1. By finding the [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") of the equation while assuming that y is a constant, we find that the slope of f at the point (_x_, _y_) is:

![Image 59: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial z}{\partial x}}=2x+y.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8600f313388154fc1128593fcc36dc882f5ce3bc)

So at (1, 1), by substitution, the slope is 3. Therefore,

![Image 60: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial z}{\partial x}}=3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea1cad13b43d949775b62faa5d8d03d291b79459)

at the point (1, 1). That is, the partial derivative of z with respect to x at (1, 1) is 3, as shown in the graph.

The function f can be reinterpreted as a family of functions of one variable indexed by the other variables:

![Image 61: {\displaystyle f(x,y)=f_{y}(x)=x^{2}+xy+y^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f0cab736120f8efe804488b4b3a31f36830ebec)

In other words, every value of y defines a function, denoted _f y_, which is a function of one variable x.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative#cite_note-6) That is,

![Image 62: {\displaystyle f_{y}(x)=x^{2}+xy+y^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f410c82c91c339d9ed707c82375df50504b019f5)

In this section the subscript notation _f y_ denotes a function contingent on a fixed value of y, and not a partial derivative.

Once a value of y is chosen, say a, then _f_(_x_,_y_) determines a function _f a_ which traces a curve _x_ 2 + _ax_ + _a_ 2 on the xz-plane:

![Image 63: {\displaystyle f_{a}(x)=x^{2}+ax+a^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88cc1fd6444f89d7f948a4e2e756fb6479b836e6)

In this expression, a is a _constant_, not a _variable_, so _f a_ is a function of only one real variable, that being x. Consequently, the definition of the derivative for a function of one variable applies:

![Image 64: {\displaystyle f_{a}'(x)=2x+a.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a95ee98bd41a86f4074e3af0694702b08fc5ad0)

The above procedure can be performed for any choice of a. Assembling the derivatives together into a function gives a function which describes the variation of f in the x direction:

![Image 65: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial f}{\partial x}}(x,y)=2x+y.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d17ea216fce04246df9e3b8bdbf3cd9d73812951)

This is the partial derivative of f with respect to x. Here '∂' is a rounded 'd' called the _[partial derivative symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial\_derivative\_symbol "Partial derivative symbol")_; to distinguish it from the letter 'd', '∂' is sometimes pronounced "partial".

Higher order partial derivatives
--------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Partial_derivative&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Higher order partial derivatives")]

Second and higher order partial derivatives are defined analogously to the higher order derivatives of univariate functions. For the function ![Image 66: {\displaystyle f(x,y,...)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff18ec63eaaa147697c6b430ef4408a341bb6603) the "own" second partial derivative with respect to x is simply the partial derivative of the partial derivative (both with respect to x):[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative#cite_note-7): 316–318

![Image 67: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial ^{2}f}{\partial x^{2}}}\equiv \partial {\frac {\partial f/\partial x}{\partial x}}\equiv {\frac {\partial f_{x}}{\partial x}}\equiv f_{xx}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8edc680c88cf269bb307c98f5a56383736282a04)

The cross partial derivative with respect to x and y is obtained by taking the partial derivative of f with respect to x, and then taking the partial derivative of the result with respect to y, to obtain

![Image 68: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial ^{2}f}{\partial y\,\partial x}}\equiv \partial {\frac {\partial f/\partial x}{\partial y}}\equiv {\frac {\partial f_{x}}{\partial y}}\equiv f_{xy}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abc9f9bca795707a16088b24b750334f3ddd3da7)

[Schwarz's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schwarz_theorem "Schwarz theorem") states that if the second derivatives are continuous, the expression for the cross partial derivative is unaffected by which variable the partial derivative is taken with respect to first and which is taken second. That is,

![Image 69: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial ^{2}f}{\partial x\,\partial y}}={\frac {\partial ^{2}f}{\partial y\,\partial x}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/728b5c13cea3fd9c4666aa9d4de545d2324dec00)

or equivalently ![Image 70: {\displaystyle f_{yx}=f_{xy}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dfbdfba920ee601787536b8c8264174f43fa47da)

Own and cross partial derivatives appear in the [Hessian matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hessian_matrix "Hessian matrix") which is used in the [second order conditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_order_condition "Second order condition") in [optimization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optimization "Optimization") problems. The higher order partial derivatives can be obtained by successive differentiation

Antiderivative analogue
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Partial_derivative&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Antiderivative analogue")]

There is a concept for partial derivatives that is analogous to [antiderivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiderivative "Antiderivative") for regular derivatives. Given a partial derivative, it allows for the partial recovery of the original function.

Consider the example of

![Image 71: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial z}{\partial x}}=2x+y.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8600f313388154fc1128593fcc36dc882f5ce3bc)

The so-called partial integral can be taken with respect to x (treating y as constant, in a similar manner to partial differentiation):

![Image 72: {\displaystyle z=\int {\frac {\partial z}{\partial x}}\,dx=x^{2}+xy+g(y).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72e3007cc85a754ab4693e8f1e94a13fa8633dbb)

Here, the [constant of integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_of_integration "Constant of integration") is no longer a constant, but instead a function of all the variables of the original function except x. The reason for this is that all the other variables are treated as constant when taking the partial derivative, so any function which does not involve x will disappear when taking the partial derivative, and we have to account for this when we take the antiderivative. The most general way to represent this is to have the constant represent an unknown function of all the other variables.

Thus the set of functions ![Image 73: {\displaystyle x^{2}+xy+g(y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1209f08a790580e88f203870e2c7c07d2323f4be), where g is any one-argument function, represents the entire set of functions in variables _x_, _y_ that could have produced the x-partial derivative ![Image 74: {\displaystyle 2x+y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59755d4f312f33cb403fc2472edb22d5c311603c).

If all the partial derivatives of a function are known (for example, with the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient")), then the antiderivatives can be matched via the above process to reconstruct the original function up to a constant. Unlike in the single-variable case, however, not every set of functions can be the set of all (first) partial derivatives of a single function. In other words, not every vector field is [conservative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservative_vector_field "Conservative vector field").

[![Image 75](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Cone_3d.png/250px-Cone_3d.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cone_3d.png)

The volume of a cone depends on height and radius

The [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume")V of a [cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone_(geometry) "Cone (geometry)") depends on the cone's [height](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Height "Height")h and its [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius")r according to the formula

![Image 76: {\displaystyle V(r,h)={\frac {\pi r^{2}h}{3}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a60da32de86d0941a385d184da972ea0506c92b9)

The partial derivative of V with respect to r is

![Image 77: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial V}{\partial r}}={\frac {2\pi rh}{3}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07a6b0ce101d23dd4528d62fb5892d4f70db197e)

which represents the rate with which a cone's volume changes if its radius is varied and its height is kept constant. The partial derivative with respect to h equals ![Image 78: {\textstyle {\frac {1}{3}}\pi r^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/016d551afe979c409ab050120204fdd53cdab507), which represents the rate with which the volume changes if its height is varied and its radius is kept constant.

By contrast, the [_total_ derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_derivative "Total derivative") of V with respect to r and h are respectively

![Image 79: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {dV}{dr}}&=\overbrace {\frac {2\pi rh}{3}} ^{\frac {\partial V}{\partial r}}+\overbrace {\frac {\pi r^{2}}{3}} ^{\frac {\partial V}{\partial h}}{\frac {dh}{dr}}\,,\\{\frac {dV}{dh}}&=\overbrace {\frac {\pi r^{2}}{3}} ^{\frac {\partial V}{\partial h}}+\overbrace {\frac {2\pi rh}{3}} ^{\frac {\partial V}{\partial r}}{\frac {dr}{dh}}\,.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d1c6f8c39a14608e79c6dc6d5d80c52dcd66169e)

The difference between the total and partial derivative is the elimination of indirect dependencies between variables in partial derivatives.

If (for some arbitrary reason) the cone's proportions have to stay the same, and the height and radius are in a fixed ratio k,

![Image 80: {\displaystyle k={\frac {h}{r}}={\frac {dh}{dr}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/74701cb8ddbadff074ab43473b8f00735c4b2bb7)

This gives the total derivative with respect to r,

![Image 81: {\displaystyle {\frac {dV}{dr}}={\frac {2\pi rh}{3}}+{\frac {\pi r^{2}}{3}}k\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b7a7ab4262833e3a57f3ad047df04d40b3d2c81)

which simplifies to

![Image 82: {\displaystyle {\frac {dV}{dr}}=k\pi r^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5befece4c393a59f9b5daae1a77d145ff18a05a)

Similarly, the total derivative with respect to h is

![Image 83: {\displaystyle {\frac {dV}{dh}}=\pi r^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/13e4601385c10ada0e1fff6fe47816d14836d79f)

The total derivative with respect to _both_ r and h of the volume intended as scalar function of these two variables is given by the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient") vector

![Image 84: {\displaystyle \nabla V=\left({\frac {\partial V}{\partial r}},{\frac {\partial V}{\partial h}}\right)=\left({\frac {2}{3}}\pi rh,{\frac {1}{3}}\pi r^{2}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f63bd42d6df8b23da4e62f3ac52c6bcc5ebab5f8)

Partial derivatives appear in any calculus-based [optimization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optimization "Optimization") problem with more than one choice variable. For example, in [economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economics "Economics") a firm may wish to maximize [profit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Profit_(economics) "Profit (economics)")π(_x_, _y_) with respect to the choice of the quantities x and y of two different types of output. The [first order conditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_order_condition "First order condition") for this optimization are π _x_ = 0 = π _y_. Since both partial derivatives π _x_ and π _y_ will generally themselves be functions of both arguments x and y, these two first order conditions form a [system of two equations in two unknowns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/System_of_equations "System of equations").

### Thermodynamics, quantum mechanics and mathematical physics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Partial_derivative&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Thermodynamics, quantum mechanics and mathematical physics")]

Partial derivatives appear in thermodynamic equations like [Gibbs-Duhem equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gibbs-Duhem_equation "Gibbs-Duhem equation"), in quantum mechanics as in [Schrödinger wave equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation "Schrödinger equation"), as well as in other equations from [mathematical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_physics "Mathematical physics"). The variables being held constant in partial derivatives here can be ratios of simple variables like [mole fractions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mole_fraction "Mole fraction")_x i_ in the following example involving the Gibbs energies in a ternary mixture system:

![Image 85: {\displaystyle {\bar {G_{2}}}=G+(1-x_{2})\left({\frac {\partial G}{\partial x_{2}}}\right)_{\frac {x_{1}}{x_{3}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4c176944fd9560c761bdba8d2b429e4b2f88398)

Express [mole fractions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mole_fraction "Mole fraction") of a component as functions of other components' mole fraction and binary mole ratios:

![Image 86: {\textstyle {\begin{aligned}x_{1}&={\frac {1-x_{2}}{1+{\frac {x_{3}}{x_{1}}}}}\\x_{3}&={\frac {1-x_{2}}{1+{\frac {x_{1}}{x_{3}}}}}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a07d9194fe0a4677c43c9fc3977f668dc89c8ba)

Differential quotients can be formed at constant ratios like those above:

![Image 87: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\left({\frac {\partial x_{1}}{\partial x_{2}}}\right)_{\frac {x_{1}}{x_{3}}}&=-{\frac {x_{1}}{1-x_{2}}}\\\left({\frac {\partial x_{3}}{\partial x_{2}}}\right)_{\frac {x_{1}}{x_{3}}}&=-{\frac {x_{3}}{1-x_{2}}}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d3a8544fe86d2dc33df0223ce1beeb1ace63360)

Ratios X, Y, Z of mole fractions can be written for ternary and multicomponent systems:

![Image 88: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}X&={\frac {x_{3}}{x_{1}+x_{3}}}\\Y&={\frac {x_{3}}{x_{2}+x_{3}}}\\Z&={\frac {x_{2}}{x_{1}+x_{2}}}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b14fb2244b84c13028b9b8a1eb034182dd7105bb)

which can be used for solving [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equation "Partial differential equation") like:

![Image 89: {\displaystyle \left({\frac {\partial \mu _{2}}{\partial n_{1}}}\right)_{n_{2},n_{3}}=\left({\frac {\partial \mu _{1}}{\partial n_{2}}}\right)_{n_{1},n_{3}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f265c67b2afae40abf726a25d07cfcd725c1747c)

This equality can be rearranged to have differential quotient of mole fractions on one side.

Partial derivatives are key to target-aware image resizing algorithms. Widely known as [seam carving](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seam_carving "Seam carving"), these algorithms require each [pixel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pixel "Pixel") in an image to be assigned a numerical 'energy' to describe their dissimilarity against orthogonal adjacent pixels. The [algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algorithm "Algorithm") then progressively removes rows or columns with the lowest energy. The formula established to determine a pixel's energy (magnitude of [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient") at a pixel) depends heavily on the constructs of partial derivatives.

Partial derivatives play a prominent role in [economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economics "Economics"), in which most functions describing economic behaviour posit that the behaviour depends on more than one variable. For example, a societal [consumption function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consumption_function "Consumption function") may describe the amount spent on consumer goods as depending on both income and wealth; the [marginal propensity to consume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marginal_propensity_to_consume "Marginal propensity to consume") is then the partial derivative of the consumption function with respect to income.
