**Ludwig Schläfli** (Swiss Standard German pronunciation:[[ˈluːdvɪɡ ˈʃlɛːfli]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German "Help:IPA/Standard German"); 15 January 1814 – 20 March 1895) was a Swiss mathematician, specialising in [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") and [complex analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis "Complex analysis") (at the time called function theory) who was one of the key figures in developing the notion of higher-[dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension") spaces.

Early life and education
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ludwig_Schl%C3%A4fli&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Early life and education")]

Schläfli spent most of his life in [Switzerland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Switzerland "Switzerland"). He was born in Grasswil (now part of [Seeberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seeberg "Seeberg")), his mother's hometown, and moved to nearby [Burgdorf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burgdorf,_Switzerland "Burgdorf, Switzerland") as a child. His clumsiness soon showed that he would not follow his father into tradework.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorie_der_vielfachen_Kontinuit%C3%A4t#cite_note-mt-3)

Instead, he entered the [gymnasium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gymnasium_(school) "Gymnasium (school)") in [Bern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bern "Bern") in 1829, at age 15, already deep into study of mathematics by way of a calculus text, [Abraham Gotthelf Kästner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abraham_Gotthelf_K%C3%A4stner "Abraham Gotthelf Kästner")'s _Mathematische Anfangsgründe der Analysis des Unendlichen_. In 1831 he entered the Akademie in Bern to study theology. By 1834 the Akademie had become the new [Universität Bern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universit%C3%A4t_Bern "Universität Bern"), and he continued there as a student. He graduated in 1836.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorie_der_vielfachen_Kontinuit%C3%A4t#cite_note-mt-3)

### Career and later life

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ludwig_Schl%C3%A4fli&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Career and later life")]

Schläfli became a schoolteacher in [Thun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thun "Thun"), where he worked from 1836 until 1847. He continued his studies in mathematics during this period, including weekly visits to the university. After meeting [Jakob Steiner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jakob_Steiner "Jakob Steiner") there in 1843, and impressing Steiner with his linguistic skills, he traveled with Steiner and [Peter Gustav Lejeune Dirichlet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Gustav_Lejeune_Dirichlet "Peter Gustav Lejeune Dirichlet") for a six-month visit to Italy, acting as the interpreter for the other two.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorie_der_vielfachen_Kontinuit%C3%A4t#cite_note-mt-3)

In 1847, Schläfli left his teaching position for lower-paid work as a [privatdozent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Privatdozent "Privatdozent") at the University of Bern. He was promoted to [extraordinary professor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extraordinary_professor "Extraordinary professor") in 1853, and to [ordinary professor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinary_professor "Ordinary professor") in 1868.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorie_der_vielfachen_Kontinuit%C3%A4t#cite_note-mt-3) After illness hampered his teaching, he retired in 1891,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorie_der_vielfachen_Kontinuit%C3%A4t#cite_note-en-4) and died on 20 March 1895.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorie_der_vielfachen_Kontinuit%C3%A4t#cite_note-mt-3)

Schläfli did pioneering research in the geometry of spaces of more than three dimensions, recorded in a treatise _Theorie der vielfachen Kontinuität_ that he wrote between 1850 and 1852. It was rejected by both the [Austrian Academy of Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austrian_Academy_of_Sciences "Austrian Academy of Sciences") and the [Berlin Academy of Science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin_Academy_of_Science "Berlin Academy of Science"), and published in full only in 1901, after Schläfli's death. Only then was its importance recognized, for instance by [Pieter Hendrik Schoute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pieter_Hendrik_Schoute "Pieter Hendrik Schoute"), who wrote that "This treatise surpasses in scientific value a good portion of everything that has been published up to the present day in the field of multidimensional geometry."[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorie_der_vielfachen_Kontinuit%C3%A4t#cite_note-mt-3) In this work, Schläfli identified and classified the [regular polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polytope "Regular polytope") of all higher dimensional Euclidean spaces, and classified them using a notation that is still widely used, the [Schläfli symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_symbol "Schläfli symbol"). At around the same time, he clarified the formulation of three-dimensional [spherical geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_geometry "Spherical geometry") by observing that it could be interpreted as the geometry of a [hypersphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersphere "Hypersphere") in four-dimensional space.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorie_der_vielfachen_Kontinuit%C3%A4t#cite_note-mt-3) The Schläfli functions, giving the volume of a spherical or Euclidean [simplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex "Simplex") in terms of its [dihedral angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihedral_angle "Dihedral angle"),[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorie_der_vielfachen_Kontinuit%C3%A4t#cite_note-kell-5) and the [Schläfli orthoscheme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_orthoscheme "Schläfli orthoscheme"), a special simplex with a path of right-angled dihedrals, come from Schläfli's work on higher dimensions.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorie_der_vielfachen_Kontinuit%C3%A4t#cite_note-cox-6)

Among the many topics of Schläfli other later works were the discovery of the [Schläfli double six](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_double_six "Schläfli double six") from Cayley's 27 lines on a [cubic surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubic_surface "Cubic surface"), a series of papers on [special functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_function "Special function"), work on the [modular group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modular_group "Modular group") prefiguring later discoveries of Dirichlet, and work on [Weber modular functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weber_modular_function "Weber modular function") and [class field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Class_field_theory "Class field theory") prefiguring later discoveries of [Heinrich Martin Weber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinrich_Martin_Weber "Heinrich Martin Weber").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorie_der_vielfachen_Kontinuit%C3%A4t#cite_note-mt-3)

Despite the lack of recognition of Schläfli within his own lifetime for his groundbreaking work on higher dimensions, he was noted for some of his other works. The University of Bern gave him an honorary doctorate in 1863. His work on the Schläfli double six won him the 1870 Steiner Prize of the Berlin Academy, and he was elected to the [Istituto Lombardo Accademia di Scienze e Lettere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Istituto_Lombardo_Accademia_di_Scienze_e_Lettere "Istituto Lombardo Accademia di Scienze e Lettere") in 1868, the [Göttingen Academy of Sciences and Humanities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6ttingen_Academy_of_Sciences_and_Humanities "Göttingen Academy of Sciences and Humanities") in 1871, and the [Accademia dei Lincei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accademia_dei_Lincei "Accademia dei Lincei") in 1883.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorie_der_vielfachen_Kontinuit%C3%A4t#cite_note-mt-3)
