In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **trapezoid** () in [North American English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_American_English "North American English"), or **trapezium** () in [British English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_English "British English"),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-2) is a [quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral "Quadrilateral") that has at least one pair of [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_(geometry) "Parallel (geometry)") sides.

The parallel sides are called the _bases_ of the trapezoid.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHopkins189133-3) The other two sides are called the _legs_[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHopkins189133-3) or _lateral sides_. If the trapezoid is a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram"), then the choice of bases and legs is arbitrary.

A trapezoid is usually considered to be a [convex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polygon "Convex polygon") quadrilateral in [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry"), but there are also [crossed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crossed_polygon "Crossed polygon") cases. If shape _ABCD_ is a convex trapezoid, then _ABDC_ is a crossed trapezoid. The metric formulas in this article apply in convex trapezoids.

_Trapezoid_ can be defined exclusively or inclusively. Under an exclusive definition a trapezoid is a quadrilateral having _exactly one_ pair of parallel sides, with the other pair of opposite sides non-parallel. Parallelograms including rhombi, rectangles, and squares are then not considered to be trapezoids.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUsiskinGriffin2008[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidff0nDwAAQBAJpgPA29_29]-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlsinaNelsen2020[httpbooksgooglecombooksidCGDSDwAAQBAJpgPA90_90]-5) Under an inclusive definition, a trapezoid is any quadrilateral with _at least_ one pair of parallel sides.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-ringenberg-6) In an inclusive classification scheme, definitions are hierarchical: a square is a type of rectangle and a type of rhombus, a rectangle or rhombus is a type of parallelogram, and every parallelogram is a type of trapezoid.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlsinaNelsen2020[httpbooksgooglecombooksidCGDSDwAAQBAJpgPA89_89]-7)

Professional mathematicians and [post-secondary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post-secondary_education "Post-secondary education") geometry textbooks nearly always prefer inclusive definitions and classifications, because they simplify statements and proofs of geometric theorems.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUsiskinGriffin2008[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidff0nDwAAQBAJpgPA32_32]-8) In primary and secondary education, definitions of _rectangle_ and _parallelogram_ are also nearly always inclusive, but an exclusive definition of _trapezoid_ is commonly found.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-10) This article uses the inclusive definition and considers parallelograms to be special kinds of trapezoids. (Cf. [Quadrilateral §Taxonomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#Taxonomy "Quadrilateral").)

To avoid confusion, some sources use the term _proper trapezoid_ to describe trapezoids with exactly one pair of parallel sides, analogous to uses of the word _proper_ in some other mathematical objects.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-12)

In the ancient Greek geometry of [Euclid's _Elements_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements "Euclid's Elements") (c. 300 BC), quadrilaterals were classified into exclusive categories: [square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square "Square"); oblong (non-square [rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle "Rectangle")); (non-square) [rhombus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombus "Rhombus"); rhomboid, meaning a non-rhombus non-rectangle [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram"); or trapezium (τραπέζιον, literally "table"), meaning any quadrilateral not already included in the previous categories.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-Murray-13)

The Neoplatonist philosopher [Proclus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proclus "Proclus") (mid 5th century AD) wrote an influential commentary on Euclid with a richer set of categories, which he attributed to [Posidonius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Posidonius "Posidonius") (c. 100 BC). In this scheme, a quadrilateral can be a parallelogram or a non-parallelogram. A parallelogram can itself be a square, an oblong (non-square rectangle), a rhombus, or a rhomboid (non-rhombus non-rectangle). A non-parallelogram can be a _trapezium_ with exactly one pair of parallel sides, which can be _isosceles_ (with equal legs) or _scalene_ (with unequal legs); or a _trapezoid_ (τραπεζοειδή, literally "table-like") with no parallel sides.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-Murray-13)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-14)

Hutton's definitions in 1795

All European languages except for English follow Proclus's meanings of _trapezium_ and _trapezoid_,[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEConwayBurgielGoodman-Strauss2016[httpbooksgooglecombooksidDrj1CwAAQBAJpgPA286_286]-15) as did English until the late 18th century, when an influential mathematical dictionary published by [Charles Hutton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Hutton "Charles Hutton") in 1795 transposed the two terms without explanation, leading to widespread inconsistency. Hutton's change was reversed in British English in about 1875, but it has been retained in American English to the present.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-Murray-13) Late 19th century American geometry textbooks define a trapezium as having _no_ parallel sides, a trapezoid as having _exactly one_ pair of parallel sides, and a parallelogram as having two sets of opposing parallel sides.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHopkins189133-3)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHobbs189966-16) To avoid confusion between contradictory British and American meanings of _trapezium_ and _trapezoid_, quadrilaterals with no parallel sides have sometimes been called _irregular quadrilaterals_.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-17)

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Trapezoid_special_cases.png/330px-Trapezoid_special_cases.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Trapezoid_special_cases.png)

Trapezoid special cases. The orange figures also qualify as parallelograms.

An [isosceles trapezoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isosceles_trapezoid "Isosceles trapezoid") is a trapezoid where the base angles have the same measure.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDodge201282-18)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-posamentier-bannister-19) As a consequence the two legs are also of equal length and it has [reflection symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_symmetry "Reflection symmetry").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHopkins189134-20) This is possible for acute trapezoids or right trapezoids as rectangles. An acute trapezoid is a trapezoid with two adjacent acute angles on its longer base, and the isosceles trapezoid is an example of an acute trapezoid. The isosceles trapezoid has a special case known as a three-sided trapezoid, meaning it is a trapezoid wherein two trapezoid's legs have equal lengths as the trapezoid's base at the top.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlsinaNelsen2020[httpbooksgooglecombooksidCGDSDwAAQBAJpgPA90_90%E2%80%9391]-21) The isosceles trapezoid is the [convex hull](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_hull "Convex hull") of an [antiparallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiparallelogram "Antiparallelogram"), a type of [crossed quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crossed_quadrilateral "Crossed quadrilateral"). Every antiparallelogram is formed with such a trapezoid by replacing two parallel sides by the two diagonals.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlsinaNelsen2020[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidCGDSDwAAQBAJpgPA212_212]-22)

An obtuse trapezoid, on the other hand, has one acute and one obtuse angle on each base. An example is [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") with equal acute angles.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlsinaNelsen2020[httpbooksgooglecombooksidCGDSDwAAQBAJpgPA90_90%E2%80%9391]-21)

A right trapezoid is a trapezoid with two adjacent [right angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle "Right angle"). One special type of right trapezoid is by forming three [right triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_triangle "Right triangle"),[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlsinaNelsen2020[httpbooksgooglecombooksidCGDSDwAAQBAJpgPA91_91]-23) which was used by [James Garfield](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_A._Garfield "James A. Garfield") to [prove](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Garfield%27s_proof_of_the_Pythagorean_theorem "Garfield's proof of the Pythagorean theorem") the [Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-24)

A **[tangential trapezoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangential_trapezoid "Tangential trapezoid")** is a trapezoid that has an [incircle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangential_quadrilateral "Tangential quadrilateral").

Given a convex quadrilateral, the following properties are equivalent, and each implies that the quadrilateral is a trapezoid:

*   It has two adjacent [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle") that are [supplementary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supplementary_angles "Supplementary angles"), that is, they add up to 180 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_(angle) "Degree (angle)").
*   The angle between a side and a [diagonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diagonal "Diagonal") is equal to the angle between the opposite side and the same diagonal.
*   The diagonals cut each other in mutually the same [ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ratio "Ratio") (this ratio is the same as that between the lengths of the parallel sides).
*   The diagonals cut the quadrilateral into four [triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle "Triangle") of which one opposite pair have equal areas.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJosefsson2013Prop._5-27)
*   The product of the areas of the two triangles formed by one diagonal equals the product of the areas of the two triangles formed by the other diagonal.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJosefsson2013Thm._6-28)
*   The areas _S_ and _T_ of some two opposite triangles of the four triangles formed by the diagonals satisfy the equation

![Image 18: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {K}}={\sqrt {S}}+{\sqrt {T}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0367ca89df3e78cb408b3237eaa88ceeb2b92c53)where _K_ is the area of the quadrilateral.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJosefsson2013Thm._8-29)
*   The midpoints of two opposite sides of the trapezoid and the intersection of the diagonals are [collinear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collinear "Collinear").[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJosefsson2013Thm._15-30)
*   The angles in the quadrilateral _ABCD_ satisfy ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \sin A\sin C=\sin B\sin D.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b40ec5bdb936df1b37368d8cb1c8f88afc96a52c)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJosefsson201325-31)
*   The cosines of two adjacent angles [sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition "Addition") to 0, as do the cosines of the other two angles.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJosefsson201325-31)
*   The cotangents of two adjacent angles sum to 0, as do the cotangents of the other two adjacent angles.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJosefsson201326-32)
*   One bimedian divides the quadrilateral into two quadrilaterals of equal areas.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJosefsson201326-32)
*   Twice the length of the bimedian connecting the midpoints of two opposite sides equals the sum of the lengths of the other sides.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJosefsson201331-33)

### Midsegment and height

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Trapezoid&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Midsegment and height")]

The _midsegment_ or _median_ of a trapezoid is the segment that joins the [midpoints](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Midpoint "Midpoint") of the legs. It is parallel to the bases. Its length _m_ is equal to the average of the lengths of the bases _a_ and _b_ of the trapezoid,[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHobbs189958-36)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-posamentier-bannister-19)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDodge2012[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidN1b_Unptpy8CpgPA117_117]-37)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-ringenberg-6)

![Image 22: {\displaystyle m={\frac {a+b}{2}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd41c4bddaa33acbbf99add0b0cf94cb317bd5af)
The midsegment of a trapezoid is one of the two [bimedians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral#Special_line_segments "Quadrilateral") (the other bimedian divides the trapezoid into equal areas).

The _height_ (or altitude) is the [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") distance between the bases.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHopkins189133-3) In the case that the two bases have different lengths (_a_ ≠ _b_), the height of a trapezoid _h_ can be determined by the length of its four sides using the formula[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-Mathworld-38)

where _c_ and _d_ are the lengths of the legs and ![Image 24: {\displaystyle p=a+b+c+d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a65171a2c73ba67252f46b1896eb312f8a5176cf).

When one of the parallel sides has shrunk to a point (say _a_ = 0), this formula reduces to [Heron's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heron%27s_formula "Heron's formula") for the area of a triangle.

Another equivalent formula for the area, which more closely resembles Heron's formula, is[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-Mathworld-38)

![Image 39: {\displaystyle K={\frac {a+b}{|b-a|}}{\sqrt {(s-b)(s-a)(s-b-c)(s-b-d)}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/105d7291d81a6f80e06b0b70354d3dcd07ec45f3)
where ![Image 40: {\displaystyle s={\tfrac {1}{2}}(a+b+c+d)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/801b31ad5295e801b48d56fc44c4c899e7dacff1) is the [semiperimeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiperimeter "Semiperimeter") of the trapezoid. (This formula is similar to [Brahmagupta's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahmagupta%27s_formula "Brahmagupta's formula"), but it differs from it, in that a trapezoid might not be [cyclic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_quadrilateral "Cyclic quadrilateral") (inscribed in a circle). The formula is also a special case of [Bretschneider's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bretschneider%27s_formula "Bretschneider's formula") for a general [quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral "Quadrilateral")).

The [bimedian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bimedian "Bimedian") connecting the parallel sides bisects the area. More generally, any line drawn through the midpoint of the median parallel to the bases, that intersects the bases, bisects the area. Any triangle connecting the two ends of one leg to the midpoint of the other leg is also half of the area.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHopkins189195-41)

In [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"), the [definite integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Definite_integral "Definite integral") of a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_of_a_real_variable "Function of a real variable")![Image 63: {\displaystyle f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/202945cce41ecebb6f643f31d119c514bec7a074) can be [numerically approximated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerical_integration "Numerical integration") as a discrete [sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Summation "Summation") by [partitioning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partition_of_an_interval "Partition of an interval") the interval of integration into small uniform intervals and approximating the function's value on each interval as the average of the values at its endpoints: ![Image 64: {\displaystyle \int _{a}^{b}f(x)\,dx\approx \sum _{k=1}^{N}{\tfrac {1}{2}}{\bigl (}f(x_{k-1})+f(x_{k}){\bigr )}\Delta x,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a4449cf6dcd1682401684b9b1bf5213717db1266) where ![Image 65: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) is the number of intervals, ![Image 66: {\displaystyle x_{0}=a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90490911c631400e3be106821aa5bcbc1b1b8133), ![Image 67: {\displaystyle x_{N}=b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21394f145dda2fbea342fcd65ff2bc6746a1028a), and ![Image 68: {\displaystyle \Delta x=(b-a)/N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/009d8d50c2e4ff9cabb22db8ceb866b7c5e67dab). Graphically, this amounts to approximating the region under the [graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_function "Graph of a function") of the function by a collection of trapezoids, so this method is called the _[trapezoidal rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoidal\_rule "Trapezoidal rule")_.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-47)

When any rectangle is viewed in [perspective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perspective_(graphical) "Perspective (graphical)") from a position which is centered on one axis but not the other, it appears to be an isosceles trapezoid, called the [keystone effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keystone_effect "Keystone effect") because arch [keystones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keystone_(architecture) "Keystone (architecture)") are commonly trapezoidal. For example, when a rectangular building [façade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fa%C3%A7ade "Façade") is photographed from the ground at a position directly in front using a [rectilinear lens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectilinear_lens "Rectilinear lens"), the image of the building is an isosceles trapezoid. Such photographs sometimes have a "keystone transformation" applied to them to recover rectangular shapes. [Video projectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Video_projector "Video projector") sometimes apply such a keystone transformation to the recorded image before projection, so that the image projected on a flat screen appears undistorted.

[![Image 69](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/14_campidoglio-altointro_%281%29.jpg/250px-14_campidoglio-altointro_%281%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:14_campidoglio-altointro_(1).jpg)

Piazza del Campidoglio viewed from directly above.

Trapezoidal doors and windows were the standard style for the [Inca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inca_architecture "Inca architecture"), although it can be found used by earlier cultures of the same region and did not necessarily originate with them.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-48)[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-49) An almena, a [battlement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battlement "Battlement") feature characteristic of [Moorish architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moorish_architecture "Moorish architecture"), is trapezoidal.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTECurl199919almena-50) Michaelangelo's redesign of the [Piazza del Campidoglio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piazza_del_Campidoglio "Piazza del Campidoglio") (see photograph at right) incorporated a trapezoid surrounding an ellipse, giving the effect of a square surrounding a circle when seen foreshortened at ground level.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTECurl1999486Michaelangelo_Buonarroti-51)[Cinematography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cinematography "Cinematography") takes advantage of trapezoids in the opposite way, to produce an excessive foreshortening effect from the camera viewpoint, giving the illusion of greater depth to a room in a movie studio than the set physically has.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTERam%C3%ADrez201284-52) Trapezoids were also used to produce the visual distortions of [Caligarism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caligarism "Caligarism").[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTERam%C3%ADrez201284-52)[Canals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canal "Canal") and drainage [ditches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ditch "Ditch") commonly have a trapezoidal cross-section.

In biology, especially [morphology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphology_(biology) "Morphology (biology)") and [taxonomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taxonomy_(biology) "Taxonomy (biology)"), terms such as _trapezoidal_ or _trapeziform_ commonly are useful in descriptions of particular organs or forms.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-Capinera2008-53)

Trapezoids are sometimes used as a graphical symbol. In [circuit diagrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circuit_diagram "Circuit diagram"), a trapezoid is the symbol for a [multiplexer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplexer "Multiplexer").[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-54) An isosceles trapezoid is used for the shape of road signs, for example, on secondary highways in [Ontario](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ontario "Ontario"), Canada.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlsinaNelsen2020[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidCGDSDwAAQBAJpgPA93_93]-55)

Non-Euclidean geometry
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Trapezoid&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Non-Euclidean geometry")]

In [spherical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_geometry "Spherical geometry") or [hyperbolic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry "Hyperbolic geometry"), the internal angles of a quadrilateral do not sum to 360°, but quadrilaterals analogous to trapezoids, parallelograms, and rectangles can still be defined, and additionally there are a few new types of quadrilaterals not distinguished in the Euclidean case.

A spherical or hyperbolic trapezoid is a quadrilateral with two opposite sides, the legs, each of whose two adjacent angles sum to the same quantity; the other two sides are the bases.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-56) As in Euclidean geometry, special cases include isosceles trapezoids whose legs are equal (as are the angles adjacent to each base), parallelograms with two pairs of opposite equal angles and two pairs of opposite equal sides, rhombuses with two pairs of opposite equal angles and four equal sides, rectangles with four equal (non-right) angles and two pairs of opposite equal sides, and squares with four equal (non-right) angles and four equal sides.

When a rectangle is cut in half along the line through the midpoints of two opposite sides, each of the resulting two pieces is an isosceles trapezoid with two right angles, called a [Saccheri quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saccheri_quadrilateral "Saccheri quadrilateral"). When a rectangle is cut into quarters by the two lines through pairs of opposite midpoints, each of the resulting four pieces is a quadrilateral with three right angles called a [Lambert quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambert_quadrilateral "Lambert quadrilateral"). In Euclidean geometry Saccheri and Lambert quadrilaterals are merely rectangles.

[![Image 70](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Young_456_French.svg/250px-Young_456_French.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Young_456_French.svg)

An example of trapezoidal number: 15 = 4 + 5 + 6

The [trapezoidal number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoidal_number "Trapezoidal number") is a set of [positive integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive_integer "Positive integer") obtained by summing consecutively two or more positive integers greater than one, forming a trapezoidal pattern.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-57)

The [crossed ladders problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crossed_ladders_problem "Crossed ladders problem") is the problem of finding the distance between the parallel sides of a right trapezoid, given the diagonal lengths and the distance from the perpendicular leg to the diagonal intersection.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlsinaNelsen2020[httpbooksgooglecombooksidCGDSDwAAQBAJpgPA102_102]-58)

*   [Frustum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frustum "Frustum"), a solid having trapezoidal faces
*   [Wedge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wedge_(geometry) "Wedge (geometry)"), a polyhedron defined by two triangles and three trapezoid faces.
