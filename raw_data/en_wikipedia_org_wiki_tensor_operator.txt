Tensor operators

In [pure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pure_mathematics "Pure mathematics") and [applied mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Applied_mathematics "Applied mathematics"), [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") and [computer graphics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_graphics "Computer graphics"), a **tensor operator** generalizes the notion of [operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operator_(mathematics) "Operator (mathematics)") which are [scalars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(mathematics) "Scalar (mathematics)") and [vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(mathematics_and_physics) "Vector (mathematics and physics)"). A special class of these are **spherical tensor operators** which apply the notion of the [spherical basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_basis "Spherical basis") and [spherical harmonics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_harmonics "Spherical harmonics"). The spherical basis closely relates to the description of [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum") in quantum mechanics and spherical harmonic functions. The [coordinate-free](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate-free "Coordinate-free") generalization of a tensor operator is known as a **representation operator**.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_operator#cite_note-1)

The general notion of scalar, vector, and tensor operators
----------------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_operator&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: The general notion of scalar, vector, and tensor operators")]

In quantum mechanics, physical observables that are scalars, vectors, and tensors, must be represented by scalar, vector, and tensor operators, respectively. Whether something is a scalar, vector, or tensor depends on how it is viewed by two observers whose coordinate frames are related to each other by a rotation. Alternatively, one may ask how, for a single observer, a physical quantity transforms if the state of the system is rotated. Consider, for example, a system consisting of a molecule of mass ![Image 2: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd), traveling with a definite center of mass momentum, ![Image 3: {\displaystyle p{\mathbf {\hat {z}} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/525305a7be8deb13ae0495ef56b5d1be09a677fd), in the ![Image 4: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98) direction. If we rotate the system by ![Image 5: {\displaystyle 90^{\circ }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c326d317eddef3ad3e6625e018a708e290a039f6) about the ![Image 6: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) axis, the momentum will change to ![Image 7: {\displaystyle p{\mathbf {\hat {x}} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6139c4d3056919dfb3cc78a33645de3392e038da), which is in the ![Image 8: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) direction. The center-of-mass kinetic energy of the molecule will, however, be unchanged at ![Image 9: {\displaystyle p^{2}/2M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ba5c9891c420926c53cccedb55a039107d64016). The kinetic energy is a scalar and the momentum is a vector, and these two quantities must be represented by a scalar and a vector operator, respectively. By the latter in particular, we mean an operator whose expected values in the initial and the rotated states are ![Image 10: {\displaystyle p{\mathbf {\hat {z}} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/525305a7be8deb13ae0495ef56b5d1be09a677fd) and ![Image 11: {\displaystyle p{\mathbf {\hat {x}} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6139c4d3056919dfb3cc78a33645de3392e038da). The kinetic energy on the other hand must be represented by a scalar operator, whose expected value must be the same in the initial and the rotated states.

In the same way, tensor quantities must be represented by tensor operators. An example of a tensor quantity (of rank two) is the electrical quadrupole moment of the above molecule. Likewise, the octupole and hexadecapole moments would be tensors of rank three and four, respectively.

Other examples of scalar operators are the total energy operator (more commonly called the [Hamiltonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_(quantum_mechanics) "Hamiltonian (quantum mechanics)")), the potential energy, and the dipole-dipole interaction energy of two atoms. Examples of vector operators are the momentum, the position, the orbital angular momentum, ![Image 12: {\displaystyle {\mathbf {L} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c43d95fa328fb814c3d7920979edc4375e93d68e), and the spin angular momentum, ![Image 13: {\displaystyle {\mathbf {S} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab623ba710ec8cf981b77b6f0681efa9d2e33ee7). (Fine print: Angular momentum is a vector as far as rotations are concerned, but unlike position or momentum it does not change sign under space inversion, and when one wishes to provide this information, it is said to be a pseudovector.)

Scalar, vector and tensor operators can also be formed by products of operators. For example, the scalar product ![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\mathbf {L} }\cdot {\mathbf {S} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88d652e32e56b691d1d6b49f30e8f02d6915f4c6) of the two vector operators, ![Image 15: {\displaystyle {\mathbf {L} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c43d95fa328fb814c3d7920979edc4375e93d68e) and ![Image 16: {\displaystyle {\mathbf {S} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab623ba710ec8cf981b77b6f0681efa9d2e33ee7), is a scalar operator, which figures prominently in discussions of the [spin–orbit interaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin%E2%80%93orbit_interaction "Spin–orbit interaction"). Similarly, the quadrupole moment tensor of our example molecule has the nine components

![Image 17: {\displaystyle Q_{ij}=\sum _{\alpha }q_{\alpha }\left(3r_{\alpha ,i}r_{\alpha ,j}-r_{\alpha }^{2}\delta _{ij}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4d787ecd56cd163a959eaf625cdd55c31ee929bf)Here, the indices ![Image 18: {\displaystyle i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/add78d8608ad86e54951b8c8bd6c8d8416533d20) and ![Image 19: {\displaystyle j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f461e54f5c093e92a55547b9764291390f0b5d0) can independently take on the values 1, 2, and 3 (or ![Image 20: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4), ![Image 21: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d), and ![Image 22: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98)) corresponding to the three Cartesian axes, the index ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) runs over all particles (electrons and nuclei) in the molecule, ![Image 24: {\displaystyle q_{\alpha }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed889eab78ea8c87191a6868306d90600164d00c) is the charge on particle ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3), and ![Image 26: {\displaystyle r_{\alpha ,i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65df10ea83864d8c2df5a65789d65761b73fa255) is the ![Image 27: {\displaystyle i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/add78d8608ad86e54951b8c8bd6c8d8416533d20)-th component of the position of this particle. Each term in the sum is a tensor operator. In particular, the nine products ![Image 28: {\displaystyle r_{\alpha ,i}r_{\alpha ,j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/265f399f51d01e7678c28f01ac1f77118c6f4a9f) together form a second rank tensor, formed by taking the outer product of the vector operator ![Image 29: {\displaystyle {\mathbf {r} }_{\alpha }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc96d7094bd4e31dc5868282f2325ad5d15eb5a9) with itself.

#### Tensor operator from vector operators

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_operator&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Tensor operator from vector operators")]

If ![Image 87: {\displaystyle {\vec {V}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae195b5427677e7fd3302b9bc400b2c9cbbe3082) and ![Image 88: {\displaystyle {\vec {W}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3bdcba4e8339910f96bfc9a94170678158c1c143) are two three dimensional vector operators, then a rank 2 Cartesian dyadic tensors can be formed from nine operators of form ![Image 89: {\displaystyle {\hat {T}}_{ij}={\hat {V_{i}}}{\hat {W_{j}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7266233e9189c6f27b79b6cc0ac20d3cad854616),![Image 90: {\displaystyle {U(R)}^{\dagger }{\hat {T}}_{ij}U(R)={U(R)}^{\dagger }({\hat {V_{i}}}{\hat {W_{j}}})U(R)=({U(R)}^{\dagger }{\hat {V}}_{i}U(R))({U(R)}^{\dagger }{\hat {W}}_{j}U(R))=\left(\sum _{l=1}^{3}R_{il}{\hat {V}}_{l}\cdot \sum _{k=1}^{3}R_{jk}{\hat {W}}_{k}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a83ea1375186ca0031b3c02a720510f31f827383)Rearranging terms, we get:![Image 91: {\displaystyle {U(R)}^{\dagger }{\hat {T}}_{ij}U(R)=\sum _{k=1}^{3}\sum _{l=1}^{3}\left(R_{il}R_{jk}{\hat {T}}_{lk}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/844fbb4b1cfe2360c5a4c0331600e912a5c66331)The RHS of the equation is change of basis equation for twice contravariant tensors where the basis are transformed by ![Image 92: {\displaystyle R^{-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91122db9e35b74f39c755e207749062fffa55e7e) or the vector components transform by ![Image 93: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) which matches transformation of vector operator components. Hence the operator tensor described forms a rank 2 tensor, in tensor representation,![Image 94: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {T} }}={\vec {V}}\otimes {\vec {W}}=({\hat {V}}_{i}{\hat {W}}_{j})(\mathbf {e} _{i}\otimes \mathbf {e} _{j})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/96bdfdc6b4ec45a76400fe140da9bf66829aece7)Similarly, an n-times contravariant tensor operator can be formed similarly by n vector operators.

We observe that the subspace spanned by linear combinations of the rank two tensor components form an invariant subspace, ie. the subspace does not change under rotation since the transformed components itself is a linear combination of the tensor components. However, this subspace is not irreducible ie. it can be further divided into invariant subspaces under rotation. Otherwise, the subspace is called reducible. In other words, there exists specific sets of different linear combinations of the components such that they transforms into a linear combination of the same set under rotation.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_operator#cite_note-:1-3) In the above example, we will show that the 9 independent tensor components can be divided into a set of 1, 3 and 5 combination of operators that each form irreducible invariant subspaces.

where:![Image 118: {\displaystyle {\widehat {T}}_{ij}^{(0)}={\frac {{\widehat {V}}_{k}{\widehat {W}}_{k}}{3}}\delta _{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4d8cb1a0389fdad2422346f8c70d485ba5a878ff)![Image 119: {\displaystyle {\widehat {T}}_{ij}^{(1)}={\frac {1}{2}}\left[{\widehat {V}}_{i}{\widehat {W}}_{j}-{\widehat {V}}_{j}{\widehat {W}}_{i}\right]={\widehat {V}}_{[i}{\widehat {W}}_{j]}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5da10a73e01c4f4c7b67ae2602bf545406f69ce)![Image 120: {\displaystyle {\widehat {T}}_{ij}^{(2)}={\tfrac {1}{2}}\left({\widehat {V}}_{i}{\widehat {W}}_{j}+{\widehat {V}}_{j}{\widehat {W}}_{i}\right)-{\tfrac {1}{3}}{\widehat {V}}_{k}{\widehat {W}}_{k}\delta _{ij}={\widehat {V}}_{(i}{\widehat {W}}_{j)}-T_{ij}^{(0)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a69648e09933bb6d3a6a71dfe664714a0ba2ea2a)

In general cartesian tensors of rank greater than 1 are reducible. In quantum mechanics, this particular example bears resemblance to the addition of two spin one particles where both are 3 dimensional, hence the total space being 9 dimensional, can be formed by spin 0, spin 1 and spin 2 systems each having 1 dimensional, 3 dimensional and 5 dimensional space respectively.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_operator#cite_note-:0-4) These three terms are irreducible, which means they cannot be decomposed further and still be tensors satisfying the defining transformation laws under which they must be invariant. Each of the irreducible representations **T**(0), **T**(1), **T**(2) ... transform like angular momentum eigenstates according to the number of independent components.

It is possible that a given tensor may have one or more of these components vanish. For example, the quadrupole moment tensor is already symmetric and traceless, and hence has only 5 independent components to begin with.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_operator#cite_note-:1-3)

For choices of ![Image 124: {\displaystyle {\hat {n}}={\hat {x}}\pm i{\hat {y}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c478ed02d07eaf4b3005d67877debfecd8d229b4) or ![Image 125: {\displaystyle {\hat {n}}={\hat {z}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3faa15546305b776075d7588ba1a700f156d1e60), we get:![Image 126: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\left[J_{\pm },{\widehat {T}}_{m}^{(j)}\right]&=\hbar {\sqrt {(j\mp m)(j\pm m+1)}}{\widehat {T}}_{m\pm 1}^{(j)}\\[1ex]\left[J_{z},{\widehat {T}}_{m}^{(j)}\right]&=\hbar m{\widehat {T}}_{m}^{(j)}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/face2c36ed427f5b0dacb0da9c32ba2a9c428657)Note the similarity of the above to:![Image 127: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}J_{\pm }|j,m\rangle &=\hbar {\sqrt {(j\mp m)(j\pm m+1)}}|j,m\pm 1\rangle \\[1ex]J_{z}|j,m\rangle &=\hbar m|j,m\rangle \end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e60b3d582e21b9ef4cd68f0419169ebeb4db3ff9)Since ![Image 128: {\displaystyle J_{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/573c4eac7701ac45330a1677abe1c3a7fc054ae1) and ![Image 129: {\displaystyle J_{y}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/782c237016b7230689619e010bf692a0adc79b38) are linear combinations of ![Image 130: {\displaystyle J_{\pm }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/93baf002b035232d7044b458c26346d66f813c44), they share the same similarity due to linearity.

If, only the commutation relations hold, using the following relation, ![Image 131: {\displaystyle |j,m\rangle \rightarrow U(R)|j,m\rangle =exp\left(-{i{\frac {\theta }{\hbar }}{\hat {n}}\cdot {\vec {J}}}\right)|j,m\rangle =\sum _{m'}D_{m'm}^{(j)}(R)|j,m'\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4742427dc18ecf19b9a33e9c6677f664d93dcd34)

we find due to similarity of actions of ![Image 132: {\displaystyle J}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/359e4f407b49910e02c27c2f52e87a36cd74c053) on wavefunction ![Image 133: {\displaystyle |j,m\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a622314da3619d737c01854be17f7a69103cc8f) and the commutation relations on ![Image 134: {\displaystyle {\widehat {T}}_{m}^{(j)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1543f957fc22176f548b26f98c5817a9bd56774f), that:

![Image 135: {\displaystyle {\widehat {T}}_{m}^{(j)}\rightarrow U(R)^{\dagger }{\widehat {T}}_{m}^{(j)}U(R)=exp\left({i{\frac {\theta }{\hbar }}{\hat {n}}\cdot ad_{\vec {J}}}\right){\widehat {T}}_{m}^{(j)}=\sum _{m'}D_{m'm}^{(j)}(R^{-1}){\widehat {T}}_{m'}^{(j)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6452893c8192f7a69c21c2aa27fd694d019beeb2)

where the exponential form is given by [Baker–Hausdorff lemma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baker%E2%80%93Campbell%E2%80%93Hausdorff_formula "Baker–Campbell–Hausdorff formula"). Hence, the above commutation relations and the transformation property are equivalent definitions of spherical tensor operators. It can also be shown that ![Image 136: {\displaystyle \{ad_{{\hat {J}}_{i}}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a939203aa044e0d7d052912c9dee8d880e66cb93) transform like a vector due to their commutation relation.

In the following section, construction of spherical tensors will be discussed. For example, since example of spherical vector operators is shown, it can be used to construct higher order spherical tensor operators. In general, spherical tensor operators can be constructed from two perspectives.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_operator#cite_note-Nielsen_Robinson-5) One way is to specify how spherical tensors transform under a physical rotation - a [group theoretical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_theory "Group theory") definition. A rotated angular momentum eigenstate can be decomposed into a linear combination of the initial eigenstates: the coefficients in the linear combination consist of Wigner rotation matrix entries. Or by continuing the previous example of the second order dyadic tensor **T** = **a** ⊗ **b**, casting each of **a** and **b** into the spherical basis and substituting into **T** gives the spherical tensor operators of the second order.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

#### Construction using Clebsch–Gordan coefficients

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_operator&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Construction using Clebsch–Gordan coefficients")]

Combination of two spherical tensors ![Image 137: {\displaystyle A_{q_{1}}^{(k_{1})}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/856374911a2ce681d8a2f13777501daf3acba910) and ![Image 138: {\displaystyle B_{q_{2}}^{(k_{2})}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e70456cc80ed183973c220e9f1cdc0579056bda4)in the following manner involving the [Clebsch–Gordan coefficients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clebsch%E2%80%93Gordan_coefficients "Clebsch–Gordan coefficients") can be proved to give another spherical tensor of the form:[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_operator#cite_note-:0-4)![Image 139: {\displaystyle T_{q}^{(k)}=\sum _{q_{1},q_{2}}\langle k_{1},k_{2};q_{1},q_{2}|k_{1},k_{2};k,q\rangle A_{q_{1}}^{(k_{1})}B_{q_{2}}^{(k_{2})}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/437b38f40e8becfdebfe85a07f889925fdf64e2f)

This equation can be used to construct higher order spherical tensor operators, for example, second order spherical tensor operators using two first order spherical tensor operators, say **A** and **B,** discussed previously:

![Image 140: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\widehat {T}}_{\pm 2}^{(2)}&={\widehat {a}}_{\pm 1}{\widehat {b}}_{\pm 1}\\[1ex]{\widehat {T}}_{\pm 1}^{(2)}&={\tfrac {1}{\sqrt {2}}}\left({\widehat {a}}_{\pm 1}{\widehat {b}}_{0}+{\widehat {a}}_{0}{\widehat {b}}_{\pm 1}\right)\\[1ex]{\widehat {T}}_{0}^{(2)}&={\tfrac {1}{\sqrt {6}}}\left({\widehat {a}}_{+1}{\widehat {b}}_{-1}+{\widehat {a}}_{-1}{\widehat {b}}_{+1}+2{\widehat {a}}_{0}{\widehat {b}}_{0}\right)\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec5e6846f3a41f46907440bb8f8abaed79f12e27)

Using the infinitesimal rotation operator and its Hermitian conjugate, one can derive the commutation relation in the spherical basis:![Image 141: {\displaystyle \left[J_{a},{\widehat {T}}_{q}^{(2)}\right]=\sum _{q'}{D(J_{a})}_{qq'}^{(2)}{\widehat {T}}_{q'}^{(2)}=\sum _{q'}\langle j{=}2,m{=}q|J_{a}|j{=}2,m{=}q'\rangle {\widehat {T}}_{q'}^{(2)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d12962647e7835dffd9869ddf19fd49ead0b75c4)and the finite rotation transformation in the spherical basis can be verified:![Image 142: {\displaystyle {U(R)}^{\dagger }{\widehat {T}}_{q}^{(2)}U(R)=\sum _{q'}{{D(R)}_{qq'}^{(2)}}^{*}{\widehat {T}}_{q'}^{(2)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae109b434a99e805b30fc03c0097425686e10a7c)

#### Using Spherical Harmonics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_operator&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Using Spherical Harmonics")]

Define an operator by its spectrum:![Image 143: {\displaystyle \Upsilon _{l}^{m}|r\rangle =r^{l}Y_{l}^{m}(\theta ,\phi )|r\rangle =\Upsilon _{l}^{m}({\vec {r}})|r\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/78da433ac839fb2d095b2454b792128adc96d210)Since for spherical harmonics under rotation:![Image 144: {\displaystyle Y_{\ell =k}^{m=q}(\mathbf {n} )=\langle \mathbf {n} |k,q\rangle \rightarrow U(R)^{\dagger }Y_{\ell =k}^{m=q}(\mathbf {n} )U(R)=Y_{\ell =k}^{m=q}(R\mathbf {n} )=\langle \mathbf {n} |D(R)^{\dagger }|k,q\rangle =\sum _{q'}D_{q',q}^{(k)}(R^{-1})Y_{\ell =k}^{m=q'}(\mathbf {n} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a9e3dba9f7549514e5785c8c7245117eaaabc76)It can also been shown that:![Image 145: {\displaystyle \Upsilon _{l}^{m}({\vec {r}})\rightarrow U(R)^{\dagger }\Upsilon _{l}^{m}({\vec {r}})U(R)=\sum _{m'}D_{m',m}^{(l)}(R^{-1})\Upsilon _{l}^{m'}({\vec {r}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b511fb557a0d9180f08aa4bd1e3cfa9034daef12)Then ![Image 146: {\displaystyle \Upsilon _{l}^{m}({\vec {V}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5125f74ba859e822420582272a8b2661586b82d5), where ![Image 147: {\displaystyle {\vec {V}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae195b5427677e7fd3302b9bc400b2c9cbbe3082) is a vector operator, also transforms in the same manner ie, is a spherical tensor operator. The process involves expressing ![Image 148: {\displaystyle \Upsilon _{l}^{m}({\vec {r}})=r^{l}Y_{l}^{m}(\theta ,\phi )=\Upsilon _{l}^{m}(x,y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cab7d6a358233cc10d51f1e2c85f1cc868c86903) in terms of x, y and z and replacing x, y and z with operators V x V y and V z which from vector operator. The resultant operator is hence a spherical tensor operator ![Image 149: {\displaystyle {\hat {T}}_{m}^{(l)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6ab05f4d874b48fe6db577218d7f6c5f5c4d6953).**[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_operator#ref_the_super_and_subscripts_switch_places_for_the_corresponding_labels_%E2%84%93_%E2%86%94_k_and_m_%E2%86%94_q_which_spherical_tensors_and_spherical_harmonics_use)** This may include constant due to normalization from spherical harmonics which is meaningless in context of operators.

The [Hermitian adjoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermitian_adjoint "Hermitian adjoint") of a spherical tensor may be defined as![Image 150: {\displaystyle (T^{\dagger })_{q}^{(k)}=(-1)^{k-q}(T_{-q}^{(k)})^{\dagger }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8d7942a63cda2ae33112ec412c07c9e34be68873)There is some arbitrariness in the choice of the phase factor: any factor containing (−1)±_q_ will satisfy the commutation relations.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_operator#cite_note-6) The above choice of phase has the advantages of being real and that the tensor product of two commuting [Hermitian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermitian "Hermitian") operators is still Hermitian.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_operator#cite_note-7) Some authors define it with a different sign on _q_, without the _k_, or use only the [floor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Floor_and_ceiling_functions "Floor and ceiling functions") of _k_.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_operator#cite_note-8)

### Orbital angular momentum and spherical harmonics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_operator&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Orbital angular momentum and spherical harmonics")]

Orbital angular momentum operators have the [ladder operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ladder_operator "Ladder operator"):

![Image 151: {\displaystyle L_{\pm }=L_{x}\pm iL_{y}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab18928cea07d6c5d937e9bbbab94b57ecf45b8d)

which raise or lower the orbital magnetic quantum number _m ℓ_ by one unit. This has almost exactly the same form as the spherical basis, aside from constant multiplicative factors.

### Spherical tensor operators and quantum spin

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_operator&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Spherical tensor operators and quantum spin")]

Spherical tensors can also be formed from algebraic combinations of the spin operators _S x_, _S y_, _S z_, as matrices, for a spin system with total quantum number _j_ = ℓ + _s_ (and ℓ = 0). Spin operators have the ladder operators:

![Image 152: {\displaystyle S_{\pm }=S_{x}\pm iS_{y}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d55ec863ac97e239b08d6933f2800543fed9c24)

which raise or lower the spin magnetic quantum number _m s_ by one unit.

Spherical bases have broad applications in pure and applied mathematics and physical sciences where spherical geometries occur.

### Dipole radiative transitions in a single-electron atom (alkali)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_operator&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Dipole radiative transitions in a single-electron atom (alkali)")]

The transition amplitude is proportional to matrix elements of the dipole operator between the initial and final states. We use an electrostatic, spinless model for the atom and we consider the transition from the initial energy level E nℓ to final level E n′ℓ′. These levels are degenerate, since the energy does not depend on the magnetic quantum number m or m′. The wave functions have the form,

![Image 153: {\displaystyle \psi _{n\ell m}(r,\theta ,\phi )=R_{n\ell }(r)Y_{\ell m}(\theta ,\phi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b078c6f67f975480deeb16675c1a4503213d3bc)

The dipole operator is proportional to the position operator of the electron, so we must evaluate matrix elements of the form,

![Image 154: {\displaystyle \langle n'\ell 'm'|\mathbf {r} |n\ell m\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dac659dec48d3e0655a000e92be8d3a0fd63f373)

where, the initial state is on the right and the final one on the left. The position operator **r** has three components, and the initial and final levels consist of 2ℓ + 1 and 2ℓ′ + 1 degenerate states, respectively. Therefore if we wish to evaluate the intensity of a spectral line as it would be observed, we really have to evaluate 3(2ℓ′+ 1)(2ℓ+ 1) matrix elements, for example, 3×3×5 = 45 in a 3d → 2p transition. This is actually an exaggeration, as we shall see, because many of the matrix elements vanish, but there are still many non-vanishing matrix elements to be calculated.

A great simplification can be achieved by expressing the components of r, not with respect to the Cartesian basis, but with respect to the spherical basis. First we define,

![Image 155: {\displaystyle r_{q}={\hat {\mathbf {e} }}_{q}\cdot \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f503e0d243e5563fa9590801391f1b8de081ace0)

Next, by inspecting a table of the _Y ℓm_′s, we find that for ℓ = 1 we have,

![Image 156: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}rY_{11}(\theta ,\phi )&=&&-r{\sqrt {\frac {3}{8\pi }}}\sin(\theta )e^{i\phi }&=&{\sqrt {\frac {3}{4\pi }}}\left(-{\frac {x+iy}{\sqrt {2}}}\right)\\rY_{10}(\theta ,\phi )&=&&r{\sqrt {\frac {3}{4\pi }}}\cos(\theta )&=&{\sqrt {\frac {3}{4\pi }}}z\\rY_{1-1}(\theta ,\phi )&=&&r{\sqrt {\frac {3}{8\pi }}}\sin(\theta )e^{-i\phi }&=&{\sqrt {\frac {3}{4\pi }}}\left({\frac {x-iy}{\sqrt {2}}}\right)\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f9c2609f4d9939dc4fa77573d04d29eb3101f6e)

where, we have multiplied each _Y_ 1 _m_ by the radius _r_. On the right hand side we see the spherical components _r q_ of the position vector **r**. The results can be summarized by,

![Image 157: {\displaystyle rY_{1q}(\theta ,\phi )={\sqrt {\frac {3}{4\pi }}}r_{q}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7dfc5997afc960389792b85b1238f6cf59318273)

for q = 1, 0, −1, where q appears explicitly as a magnetic quantum number. This equation reveals a relationship between vector operators and the angular momentum value ℓ = 1, something we will have more to say about presently. Now the matrix elements become a product of a radial integral times an angular integral, ![Image 158: {\displaystyle \langle n'\ell 'm'|r_{q}|n\ell m\rangle =\left(\int _{0}^{\infty }r^{2}drR_{n'\ell '}^{*}(r)rR_{n\ell }(r)\right)\left({\sqrt {\frac {4\pi }{3}}}\int \sin {(\theta )}d\Omega Y_{\ell 'm'}^{*}(\theta ,\phi )Y_{1q}(\theta ,\phi )Y_{\ell m}(\theta ,\phi )\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/69059f222c8a1274b051be1ab691ad036192f415)

We see that all the dependence on the three magnetic quantum numbers (m′,q,m) is contained in the angular part of the integral. Moreover, the angular integral can be evaluated by the three-_Y ℓm_ formula, whereupon it becomes proportional to the Clebsch-Gordan coefficient,

![Image 159: {\displaystyle \langle \ell 'm'|\ell 1mq\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a41748ac724fd35c005e0369af2da3fa1b5b988d)

The radial integral is independent of the three magnetic quantum numbers (_m_′, _q_, _m_), and the trick we have just used does not help us to evaluate it. But it is only one integral, and after it has been done, all the other integrals can be evaluated just by computing or looking up Clebsch–Gordan coefficients.

The selection rule _m_′ = _q_ + _m_ in the Clebsch–Gordan coefficient means that many of the integrals vanish, so we have exaggerated the total number of integrals that need to be done. But had we worked with the Cartesian components _r_ i of **r**, this selection rule might not have been obvious. In any case, even with the selection rule, there may still be many nonzero integrals to be done (nine, in the case 3d → 2p). The example we have just given of simplifying the calculation of matrix elements for a dipole transition is really an application of the Wigner–Eckart theorem, which we take up later in these notes.

The spherical tensor formalism provides a common platform for treating coherence and relaxation in [nuclear magnetic resonance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance "Nuclear magnetic resonance"). In [NMR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance "Nuclear magnetic resonance") and [EPR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_paramagnetic_resonance "Electron paramagnetic resonance"), spherical tensor operators are employed to express the quantum dynamics of [particle spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)"), by means of an equation of motion for the [density matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_matrix "Density matrix") entries, or to formulate dynamics in terms of an equation of motion in [Liouville space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liouville_space "Liouville space"). The Liouville space equation of motion governs the observable averages of spin variables. When relaxation is formulated using a spherical tensor basis in Liouville space, insight is gained because the relaxation matrix exhibits the cross-relaxation of spin observables directly.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_operator#cite_note-Nielsen_Robinson-5)
