The [twisted cubic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twisted_cubic "Twisted cubic") is a projective algebraic variety.

**Algebraic varieties** are the central objects of study in [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), a sub-field of [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"). Classically, an algebraic variety is defined as the [set of solutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solution_set "Solution set") of a [system of polynomial equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/System_of_polynomial_equations "System of polynomial equations") over the [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") or [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number"). Modern definitions generalize this concept in several different ways, while attempting to preserve the geometric intuition behind the original definition.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-Hartshorne-1): 58

Conventions regarding the definition of an algebraic variety differ slightly. For example, some definitions require an algebraic variety to be [irreducible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irreducible_component "Irreducible component"), which means that it is not the [union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_(set_theory) "Union (set theory)") of two smaller [sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") that are [closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_set "Closed set") in the [Zariski topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zariski_topology "Zariski topology"). Under this definition, non-irreducible algebraic varieties are called **algebraic sets**. Other conventions do not require irreducibility.

The [fundamental theorem of algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_algebra "Fundamental theorem of algebra") establishes a link between [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra "Algebra") and [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") by showing that a [monic polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monic_polynomial "Monic polynomial") (an algebraic object) in one variable with complex number coefficients is determined by the set of its [roots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_of_a_function "Zero of a function") (a geometric object) in the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane"). Generalizing this result, [Hilbert's Nullstellensatz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert%27s_Nullstellensatz "Hilbert's Nullstellensatz") provides a fundamental correspondence between [ideals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_(ring_theory) "Ideal (ring theory)") of [polynomial rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_ring "Polynomial ring") and algebraic sets. Using the _Nullstellensatz_ and related results, mathematicians have established a strong correspondence between questions on algebraic sets and questions of [ring theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_theory "Ring theory"). This correspondence is a defining feature of algebraic geometry.

Many algebraic varieties are [differentiable manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold"), but an algebraic variety may have [singular points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singular_point_of_an_algebraic_variety "Singular point of an algebraic variety") while a differentiable manifold cannot. Algebraic varieties can be characterized by their [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_of_an_algebraic_variety "Dimension of an algebraic variety"). Algebraic varieties of dimension one are called _[algebraic curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic\_curve "Algebraic curve")_ and algebraic varieties of dimension two are called _[algebraic surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic\_surface "Algebraic surface")_.

In the context of modern [scheme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scheme_(mathematics) "Scheme (mathematics)") theory, an algebraic variety over a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)") is an integral (irreducible and reduced) scheme over that field whose [structure morphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structure_morphism "Structure morphism") is separated and of finite type.

Overview and definitions
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_variety&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Overview and definitions")]

An _affine variety_ over an [algebraically closed field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraically_closed_field "Algebraically closed field") is conceptually the easiest type of variety to define, which will be done in this section. Next, one can define projective and quasi-projective varieties in a similar way. The most general definition of a variety is obtained by patching together smaller quasi-projective varieties. It is not obvious that one can construct genuinely new examples of varieties in this way, but [Nagata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Masayoshi_Nagata "Masayoshi Nagata") gave an example of such a new variety in the 1950s.

For an algebraically closed field K and a [natural number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number")n, let **A**_n_ be an [affine _n_-space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space "Affine space") over _K_, identified to ![Image 2: {\displaystyle K^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d63366b3d00300e06eee81786182062b98775c5) through the choice of an [affine coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_coordinate_system "Affine coordinate system"). The polynomials _f_ in the ring _K_[_x_ 1, ..., _x n_] can be viewed as _K_-valued functions on **A**_n_ by evaluating _f_ at the points in **A**_n_, i.e. by choosing values in _K_ for each _x i_. For each set _S_ of polynomials in _K_[_x_ 1, ..., _x n_], define the zero-locus _Z_(_S_) to be the set of points in **A**_n_ on which the functions in _S_ simultaneously vanish, that is to say

![Image 3: {\displaystyle Z(S)=\left\{x\in \mathbf {A} ^{n}\mid f(x)=0{\text{ for all }}f\in S\right\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7c75da95ce86ba1b26492033e94de7d872289c9)
A subset _V_ of **A**_n_ is called an **affine algebraic set** if _V_ = _Z_(_S_) for some _S_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-Hartshorne-1): 2  A nonempty affine algebraic set _V_ is called **irreducible** if it cannot be written as the union of two [proper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset") algebraic subsets.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-Hartshorne-1): 3  An irreducible affine algebraic set is also called an **affine variety**.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-Hartshorne-1): 3  (Some authors use the phrase _affine variety_ to refer to any affine algebraic set, irreducible or not.[[note 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-2))

Affine varieties can be given a [natural topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_topology "Natural topology") by declaring the [closed sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_set "Closed set") to be precisely the affine algebraic sets. This topology is called the Zariski topology.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-Hartshorne-1): 2

Given a subset _V_ of **A**_n_, we define _I_(_V_) to be the ideal of all polynomial functions vanishing on _V_:

![Image 4: {\displaystyle I(V)=\left\{f\in K[x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n}]\mid f(x)=0{\text{ for all }}x\in V\right\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e401fa9a6b3bc2616ed05a01fbd98adc8a388cf)
For any affine algebraic set _V_, the **coordinate ring** or **structure ring** of _V_ is the [quotient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_ring "Quotient ring") of the polynomial ring by this ideal.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-Hartshorne-1): 4

### Projective varieties and quasi-projective varieties

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_variety&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Projective varieties and quasi-projective varieties")]

Let k be an algebraically closed field and let **P**_n_ be the [projective _n_-space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry_of_projective_spaces "Algebraic geometry of projective spaces") over k. Let _f_ in _k_[_x_ 0, ..., _x n_] be a [homogeneous polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_polynomial "Homogeneous polynomial") of degree _d_. It is not well-defined to evaluate _f_ on points in **P**_n_ in [homogeneous coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_coordinates "Homogeneous coordinates"). However, because _f_ is homogeneous, meaning that _f_ (_λx_ 0, ..., _λx n_) = _λ d_ _f_ (_x_ 0, ..., _x n_), it _does_ make sense to ask whether _f_ vanishes at a point [_x_ 0: ...: _x n_]. For each set _S_ of homogeneous polynomials, define the zero-locus of _S_ to be the set of points in **P**_n_ on which the functions in _S_ vanish:

![Image 5: {\displaystyle Z(S)=\{x\in \mathbf {P} ^{n}\mid f(x)=0{\text{ for all }}f\in S\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0a785e9f06715d40f0c010733ecc48edbaa1db)
A subset _V_ of **P**_n_ is called a **projective algebraic set** if _V_ = _Z_(_S_) for some _S_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-Hartshorne-1): 9  An irreducible projective algebraic set is called a **projective variety**.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-Hartshorne-1): 10

Projective varieties are also equipped with the Zariski topology by declaring all algebraic sets to be closed.

Given a subset _V_ of **P**_n_, let _I_(_V_) be the ideal generated by all homogeneous polynomials vanishing on _V_. For any projective algebraic set _V_, the **[coordinate ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_coordinate_ring "Homogeneous coordinate ring")** of _V_ is the quotient of the polynomial ring by this ideal.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-Hartshorne-1): 10

A **[quasi-projective variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-projective_variety "Quasi-projective variety")** is a [Zariski open](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zariski_topology "Zariski topology") subset of a projective variety. Notice that every affine variety is quasi-projective.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-3) Notice also that the complement of an algebraic set in an affine variety is a quasi-projective variety; in the context of affine varieties, such a quasi-projective variety is usually not called a variety but a [constructible set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constructible_set_(topology) "Constructible set (topology)").

In classical algebraic geometry, all varieties were by definition [quasi-projective varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasiprojective_variety "Quasiprojective variety"), meaning that they were open subvarieties of closed subvarieties of a [projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_space "Projective space"). For example, in Chapter 1 of [Hartshorne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_Geometry_(book) "Algebraic Geometry (book)") a _variety_ over an algebraically closed field is defined to be a [quasi-projective variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-projective_variety "Quasi-projective variety"),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-Hartshorne-1): 15  but from Chapter 2 onwards, the term **variety** (also called an **abstract variety**) refers to a more general object, which locally is a quasi-projective variety, but when viewed as a whole is not necessarily quasi-projective; i.e. it might not have an embedding into [projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_space "Projective space").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-Hartshorne-1): 105  So classically the definition of an algebraic variety required an embedding into projective space, and this embedding was used to define the topology on the variety and the [regular functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_function "Regular function") on the variety. The disadvantage of such a definition is that not all varieties come with natural embeddings into projective space. For example, under this definition, the product **P**1 × **P**1 is not a variety until it is embedded into a larger projective space; this is usually done by the [Segre embedding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Segre_embedding "Segre embedding"). Furthermore, any variety that admits one embedding into projective space admits many others, for example by composing the embedding with the [Veronese embedding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Veronese_embedding "Veronese embedding"); thus many notions that should be intrinsic, such as that of a regular function, are not obviously so.

The earliest successful attempt to define an algebraic variety abstractly, without an embedding, was made by [André Weil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Weil "André Weil") in his _[Foundations of Algebraic Geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foundations\_of\_Algebraic\_Geometry "Foundations of Algebraic Geometry"),_ using [valuations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valuation_(algebra) "Valuation (algebra)"). [Claude Chevalley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claude_Chevalley "Claude Chevalley") made a definition of a [scheme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scheme_(mathematics) "Scheme (mathematics)"), which served a similar purpose, but was more general. However, [Alexander Grothendieck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Grothendieck "Alexander Grothendieck")'s definition of a scheme is more general still and has received the most widespread acceptance. In Grothendieck's language, an abstract algebraic variety is usually defined to be an [integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_scheme_theory#integral "Glossary of scheme theory"), [separated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_scheme_theory#separated "Glossary of scheme theory") scheme of [finite type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_algebraic_geometry#finite_type_(locally) "Glossary of algebraic geometry") over an algebraically closed field,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-Hartshorne-1): 104–105  although some authors drop the irreducibility or the reducedness or the separateness condition or allow the underlying field to be not algebraically closed.[[note 2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-4) Classical algebraic varieties are the quasiprojective integral separated finite type schemes over an algebraically closed field.

#### Existence of non-quasiprojective abstract algebraic varieties

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_variety&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Existence of non-quasiprojective abstract algebraic varieties")]

One of the earliest examples of a non-quasiprojective algebraic variety were given by Nagata.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-Nagata56-5) Nagata's example was not [complete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_variety "Complete variety") (the analog of compactness), but soon afterwards he found an algebraic surface that was complete and non-projective.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-Nagata57-6)[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-Hartshorne-1): Remark 4.10.2 p.105  Since then other examples have been found: for example, it is straightforward to construct [toric varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toric_variety "Toric variety") that are not quasi-projective but complete.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-7)

A **subvariety** is a subset of a variety that is itself a variety (with respect to the topological structure induced by the ambient variety). For example, every open subset of a variety is a variety. See also [closed immersion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_immersion "Closed immersion").

[Hilbert's Nullstellensatz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert%27s_Nullstellensatz "Hilbert's Nullstellensatz") says that closed subvarieties of an affine or projective variety are in one-to-one correspondence with the prime ideals or non-irrelevant homogeneous prime ideals of the coordinate ring of the variety.

Let _k_ = **C**, and **A**2 be the two-dimensional [affine space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space "Affine space") over **C**. Polynomials in the ring **C**[_x_, _y_] can be viewed as complex valued functions on **A**2 by evaluating at the points in **A**2. Let subset _S_ of **C**[_x_, _y_] contain a single element _f_ (_x_, _y_):

![Image 6: {\displaystyle f(x,y)=x+y-1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7a7a5556df91f0674d74f110a53073f09e7a6c70)
The zero-locus of _f_ (_x_, _y_) is the set of points in **A**2 on which this function vanishes: it is the set of all pairs of complex numbers (_x_, _y_) such that _y_ = 1 − _x_. This is called a [line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_(geometry) "Line (geometry)") in the affine plane. (In the **classical topology** coming from the topology on the complex numbers, a complex line is a real manifold of dimension two.) This is the set _Z_( _f_ ):

![Image 7: {\displaystyle Z(f)=\{(x,1-x)\in \mathbf {C} ^{2}\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d7ee8a81b95954c3d27280189ff9bcff5f7a5e3)
Thus the subset _V_ = _Z_( _f_ ) of **A**2 is an [algebraic set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#Affine_varieties). The set _V_ is not empty. It is irreducible, as it cannot be written as the union of two proper algebraic subsets. Thus it is an affine algebraic variety.

Let _k_ = **C**, and **A**2 be the two-dimensional affine space over **C**. Polynomials in the ring **C**[_x_, _y_] can be viewed as complex valued functions on **A**2 by evaluating at the points in **A**2. Let subset _S_ of **C**[_x_, _y_] contain a single element _g_(_x_, _y_):

![Image 8: {\displaystyle g(x,y)=x^{2}+y^{2}-1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb3f5fda37ab33ddf664630a45e2d5635e87da22)
The zero-locus of _g_(_x_, _y_) is the set of points in **A**2 on which this function vanishes, that is the set of points (_x_,_y_) such that _x_ 2 + _y_ 2 = 1. As _g_(_x_, _y_) is an [absolutely irreducible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolutely_irreducible "Absolutely irreducible") polynomial, this is an algebraic variety. The set of its real points (that is the points for which _x_ and _y_ are real numbers), is known as the [unit circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_circle "Unit circle"); this name is also often given to the whole variety.

The following example is neither a [hypersurface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersurface "Hypersurface"), nor a [linear space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space"), nor a single point. Let **A**3 be the three-dimensional affine space over **C**. The set of points (_x_, _x_ 2, _x_ 3) for _x_ in **C** is an algebraic variety, and more precisely an algebraic curve that is not contained in any plane.[[note 3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-8) It is the [twisted cubic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twisted_cubic "Twisted cubic") shown in the above figure. It may be defined by the equations

![Image 9: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}y-x^{2}&=0\\z-x^{3}&=0\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9ab06079479a1485abb2cc0ce507e4a37f592aca)
The irreducibility of this algebraic set needs a proof. One approach in this case is to check that the projection (_x_, _y_, _z_) → (_x_, _y_) is [injective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injective_function "Injective function") on the set of the solutions and that its image is an irreducible plane curve.

For more difficult examples, a similar proof may always be given, but may imply a difficult computation: first a [Gröbner basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gr%C3%B6bner_basis "Gröbner basis") computation to compute the dimension, followed by a random linear change of variables (not always needed); then a [Gröbner basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gr%C3%B6bner_basis "Gröbner basis") computation for another [monomial ordering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monomial_order "Monomial order") to compute the projection and to prove that it is [generically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generic_property "Generic property") injective and that its image is a [hypersurface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersurface "Hypersurface"), and finally a [polynomial factorization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_factorization "Polynomial factorization") to prove the irreducibility of the image.

#### General linear group

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_variety&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: General linear group")]

The set of _n_-by-_n_ matrices over the base field _k_ can be identified with the affine _n_ 2-space ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \mathbb {A} ^{n^{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71bf24d6e7a7b322a2b51c886aaac84b012ae73b) with coordinates ![Image 11: {\displaystyle x_{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a27e97949cb2cc8f2d4c2a9421477a65f839db11) such that ![Image 12: {\displaystyle x_{ij}(A)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e69d70d08afe55569bab0d1f0e6fa4670b949737) is the (_i_, _j_)-th entry of the matrix ![Image 13: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3). The [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant_of_a_matrix "Determinant of a matrix")![Image 14: {\displaystyle \det }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/742df4b24488fb61ad6fc7b4cf229a9168f08690) is then a polynomial in ![Image 15: {\displaystyle x_{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a27e97949cb2cc8f2d4c2a9421477a65f839db11) and thus defines the hypersurface ![Image 16: {\displaystyle H=V(\det )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4292be1ad9e18fbea8fe626a08840b2e80f0e9c6) in ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \mathbb {A} ^{n^{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71bf24d6e7a7b322a2b51c886aaac84b012ae73b). The complement of ![Image 18: {\displaystyle H}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75a9edddcca2f782014371f75dca39d7e13a9c1b) is then an open subset of ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \mathbb {A} ^{n^{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71bf24d6e7a7b322a2b51c886aaac84b012ae73b) that consists of all the invertible _n_-by-_n_ matrices, the [general linear group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_linear_group "General linear group")![Image 20: {\displaystyle \operatorname {GL} _{n}(k)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bc9e8d8cf24c036c3b17aba368840366cb313fc). It is an affine variety, since, in general, the complement of a hypersurface in an affine variety is affine. Explicitly, consider ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathbb {A} ^{n^{2}}\times \mathbb {A} ^{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a4867bbc96499a4eac4e5d1f5ab33a4430b481b7) where the affine line is given coordinate _t_. Then ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \operatorname {GL} _{n}(k)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bc9e8d8cf24c036c3b17aba368840366cb313fc) amounts to the zero-locus in ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \mathbb {A} ^{n^{2}}\times \mathbb {A} ^{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a4867bbc96499a4eac4e5d1f5ab33a4430b481b7) of the polynomial in ![Image 24: {\displaystyle x_{ij},t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ce20d241f4399f2d40cfac6d30cb9c9084c89aaa):

![Image 25: {\displaystyle t\cdot \det[x_{ij}]-1,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4eebf11e099dae629b56fdef0ddbe6a0190ada2c)
i.e., the set of matrices _A_ such that ![Image 26: {\displaystyle t\det(A)=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961287a186444b53213c85ae425518696f639be0) has a solution. This is best seen algebraically: the coordinate ring of ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \operatorname {GL} _{n}(k)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bc9e8d8cf24c036c3b17aba368840366cb313fc) is the [localization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Localization_(commutative_algebra) "Localization (commutative algebra)")![Image 28: {\displaystyle k[x_{ij}\mid 0\leq i,j\leq n][{\det }^{-1}]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/906251a2585beae9160026b614821edf0e9d3d6f), which can be identified with ![Image 29: {\displaystyle k[x_{ij},t\mid 0\leq i,j\leq n]/(t\det -1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e3d4682930efbb6a3665071a1a0df6a5b6e4cdf).

The multiplicative group k* of the base field _k_ is the same as ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \operatorname {GL} _{1}(k)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/efc2f13ab6ad99615859ba9c807c0cfbe575bc22) and thus is an affine variety. A finite product of it ![Image 31: {\displaystyle (k^{*})^{r}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/540225d48edac7e12e4dccbaae0f51ffa6e89eb0) is an [algebraic torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_torus "Algebraic torus"), which is again an affine variety.

A general linear group is an example of a [linear algebraic group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebraic_group "Linear algebraic group"), an affine variety that has a structure of a [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") in such a way the group operations are morphism of varieties.

#### Characteristic variety

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_variety&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Characteristic variety")]

Let _A_ be a not-necessarily-commutative algebra over a field _k_. Even if _A_ is not commutative, it can still happen that _A_ has a ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc)-filtration so that the [associated ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associated_graded_ring "Associated graded ring")![Image 33: {\displaystyle \operatorname {gr} A=\bigoplus _{i=-\infty }^{\infty }A_{i}/{A_{i-1}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7dccbea842f16b9d459de3c988f56f4a296c6ee4) is commutative, reduced and finitely generated as a _k_-algebra; i.e., ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \operatorname {gr} A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a7c1f11bc58808f27ef9a4f13f1defdd56da3a) is the coordinate ring of an affine (reducible) variety _X_. For example, if _A_ is the [universal enveloping algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_enveloping_algebra "Universal enveloping algebra") of a finite-dimensional [Lie algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra "Lie algebra")![Image 35: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {g}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40a913b1503ed9ec94361b99f7fd59ef60705c28), then ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \operatorname {gr} A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a7c1f11bc58808f27ef9a4f13f1defdd56da3a) is a polynomial ring (the [PBW theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PBW_theorem "PBW theorem")); more precisely, the coordinate ring of the dual vector space ![Image 37: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {g}}^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0acfc869f2ed2da044f5c582042afd3065e12bdb).

Let _M_ be a filtered module over _A_ (i.e., ![Image 38: {\displaystyle A_{i}M_{j}\subset M_{i+j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7a534fbe54d7d4b34975a4ecfa3e66f49f08e761)). If ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \operatorname {gr} M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d74600b40dbc52661865dfe9e25e9b7908f05f86) is fintiely generated as a ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \operatorname {gr} A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a7c1f11bc58808f27ef9a4f13f1defdd56da3a)-algebra, then the [support](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Support_of_a_module "Support of a module") of ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \operatorname {gr} M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d74600b40dbc52661865dfe9e25e9b7908f05f86) in _X_; i.e., the locus where ![Image 42: {\displaystyle \operatorname {gr} M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d74600b40dbc52661865dfe9e25e9b7908f05f86) does not vanish is called the [characteristic variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_variety "Characteristic variety") of _M_.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-9) The notion plays an important role in the theory of [_D_-modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D-module "D-module").

A [projective variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_variety "Projective variety") is a closed subvariety of a projective space. That is, it is the zero locus of a set of [homogeneous polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_polynomials "Homogeneous polynomials") that generate a [prime ideal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_ideal "Prime ideal").

[![Image 43](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/0/0c/Elliptic_curve2.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Elliptic_curve2.png)

The affine plane curve _y_ 2 = _x_ 3 − _x_. The corresponding projective curve is called an elliptic curve.

A plane projective curve is the zero locus of an irreducible homogeneous polynomial in three indeterminates. The [projective line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_line "Projective line")**P**1 is an example of a projective curve; it can be viewed as the curve in the projective plane **P**2 = {[_x_, _y_, _z_]} defined by _x_ = 0. For another example, first consider the affine cubic curve

![Image 44: {\displaystyle y^{2}=x^{3}-x.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c0627eabf66ffd684829247628b403bec9310c64)
in the 2-dimensional affine space (over a field of characteristic not two). It has the associated cubic homogeneous polynomial equation:

![Image 45: {\displaystyle y^{2}z=x^{3}-xz^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/70f4b749e0e903b2b5e672c37971cd48d74dd6cf)
which defines a curve in **P**2 called an [elliptic curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_curve "Elliptic curve"). The curve has genus one ([genus formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genus_formula "Genus formula")); in particular, it is not isomorphic to the projective line **P**1, which has genus zero. Using genus to distinguish curves is very basic: in fact, the genus is the first invariant one uses to classify curves (see also the construction of [moduli of algebraic curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moduli_of_algebraic_curves "Moduli of algebraic curves")).

#### Example 2: Grassmannian

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_variety&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Example 2: Grassmannian")]

Let _V_ be a finite-dimensional vector space. The [Grassmannian variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grassmannian_variety "Grassmannian variety")_G n_(_V_) is the set of all _n_-dimensional subspaces of _V_. It is a projective variety: it is embedded into a projective space via the [Plücker embedding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pl%C3%BCcker_embedding "Plücker embedding"):

![Image 46: {\displaystyle {\begin{cases}G_{n}(V)\hookrightarrow \mathbf {P} \left(\wedge ^{n}V\right)\\\langle b_{1},\ldots ,b_{n}\rangle \mapsto [b_{1}\wedge \cdots \wedge b_{n}]\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca49e905092e65ff3c107d6d5a880404396231d8)
where _b i_ are any set of linearly independent vectors in _V_, ![Image 47: {\displaystyle \wedge ^{n}V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7138ef9134549b40ecd503094a0e0a02d6018362) is the _n_-th [exterior power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_power "Exterior power") of _V_, and the bracket [_w_] means the line spanned by the nonzero vector _w_.

The Grassmannian variety comes with a natural [vector bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle") (or [locally free sheaf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Locally_free_sheaf "Locally free sheaf") in other terminology) called the [tautological bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tautological_bundle "Tautological bundle"), which is important in the study of [characteristic classes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_class "Characteristic class") such as [Chern classes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chern_class "Chern class").

#### Jacobian variety and abelian variety

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_variety&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Jacobian variety and abelian variety")]

Let _C_ be a smooth complete curve and ![Image 48: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Pic} (C)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/389b3854d40a72f523a426763c9af6e1b0406fbd) the [Picard group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Picard_group "Picard group") of it; i.e., the group of isomorphism classes of line bundles on _C_. Since _C_ is smooth, ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Pic} (C)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/389b3854d40a72f523a426763c9af6e1b0406fbd) can be identified as the [divisor class group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divisor_class_group "Divisor class group") of _C_ and thus there is the degree homomorphism ![Image 50: {\displaystyle \operatorname {deg} :\operatorname {Pic} (C)\to \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11a2d60027752a504fb594f39d22349e5f3ef925). The [Jacobian variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_variety "Jacobian variety")![Image 51: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Jac} (C)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/93b004a0342cf4acb68460b0ef0290bf615fcdb1) of _C_ is the kernel of this degree map; i.e., the group of the divisor classes on _C_ of degree zero. A Jacobian variety is an example of an [abelian variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_variety "Abelian variety"), a complete variety with a compatible [abelian group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group") structure on it (the name "abelian" is however not because it is an abelian group). An abelian variety turns out to be projective (in short, algebraic [theta functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theta_function "Theta function") give an embedding into a projective space. See [equations defining abelian varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equations_defining_abelian_varieties "Equations defining abelian varieties")); thus, ![Image 52: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Jac} (C)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/93b004a0342cf4acb68460b0ef0290bf615fcdb1) is a projective variety. The tangent space to ![Image 53: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Jac} (C)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/93b004a0342cf4acb68460b0ef0290bf615fcdb1) at the identity element is naturally isomorphic to ![Image 54: {\displaystyle \operatorname {H} ^{1}(C,{\mathcal {O}}_{C});}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8fbb3bbeb8368c79fe7b50c89ee5943f36cdc316)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-10) hence, the dimension of ![Image 55: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Jac} (C)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/93b004a0342cf4acb68460b0ef0290bf615fcdb1) is the genus of ![Image 56: {\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029).

Fix a point ![Image 57: {\displaystyle P_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/671bd891701e0d6cfa6da0114a5dd64233b58709) on ![Image 58: {\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029). For each integer ![Image 59: {\displaystyle n>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27a6a5d982d54202a14f111cb8a49210501b2c96), there is a natural morphism[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-11)

![Image 60: {\displaystyle C^{n}\to \operatorname {Jac} (C),\,(P_{1},\dots ,P_{r})\mapsto [P_{1}+\cdots +P_{n}-nP_{0}]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d95415e42a4d1ac4cb41a7199523cccd098ffa6)
where ![Image 61: {\displaystyle C^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50cc4309121472bbd901a7e54c365829cd150d82) is the product of _n_ copies of _C_. For ![Image 62: {\displaystyle g=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba0027652d8e5f694d4aa1c71ce16c9380ce1186) (i.e., _C_ is an elliptic curve), the above morphism for ![Image 63: {\displaystyle n=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9ec7e1edc2e6d98f5aec2a39ae5f1c99d1e1425) turns out to be an isomorphism;[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-Hartshorne-1): Ch. IV, Example 1.3.7.  in particular, an elliptic curve is an abelian variety.

Given an integer ![Image 64: {\displaystyle g\geq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef9beeeef79ee73a89cef1e1986fc88d7d248f4e), the set of isomorphism classes of smooth complete curves of genus ![Image 65: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) is called the [moduli of curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moduli_of_curves "Moduli of curves") of genus ![Image 66: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) and is denoted as ![Image 67: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {M}}_{g}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5b598f80fbad1b8e1cd191ff9df1700ba8c5c8c9). There are few ways to show this moduli has a structure of a possibly reducible algebraic variety; for example, one way is to use [geometric invariant theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_invariant_theory "Geometric invariant theory") which ensures a set of isomorphism classes has a (reducible) quasi-projective variety structure.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-12) Moduli such as the moduli of curves of fixed genus is typically not a projective variety; roughly the reason is that a degeneration (limit) of a smooth curve tends to be non-smooth or reducible. This leads to the notion of a [stable curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_curve "Stable curve") of genus ![Image 68: {\displaystyle g\geq 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b84b2394f64ae9927e09ee51b848be213edecafe), a not-necessarily-smooth complete curve with no terribly bad singularities and not-so-large automorphism group. The moduli of stable curves ![Image 69: {\displaystyle {\overline {\mathfrak {M}}}_{g}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4147e255ee32f5037a2c4c57f8a185abd0504155), the set of isomorphism classes of stable curves of genus ![Image 70: {\displaystyle g\geq 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b84b2394f64ae9927e09ee51b848be213edecafe), is then a projective variety which contains ![Image 71: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {M}}_{g}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5b598f80fbad1b8e1cd191ff9df1700ba8c5c8c9) as an open dense subset. Since ![Image 72: {\displaystyle {\overline {\mathfrak {M}}}_{g}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4147e255ee32f5037a2c4c57f8a185abd0504155) is obtained by adding boundary points to ![Image 73: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {M}}_{g}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5b598f80fbad1b8e1cd191ff9df1700ba8c5c8c9), ![Image 74: {\displaystyle {\overline {\mathfrak {M}}}_{g}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4147e255ee32f5037a2c4c57f8a185abd0504155) is colloquially said to be a [compactification](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Compactification_(algebraic_geometry)&action=edit&redlink=1 "Compactification (algebraic geometry) (page does not exist)") of ![Image 75: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {M}}_{g}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5b598f80fbad1b8e1cd191ff9df1700ba8c5c8c9). Historically a paper of Mumford and Deligne[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-13) introduced the notion of a stable curve to show ![Image 76: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {M}}_{g}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5b598f80fbad1b8e1cd191ff9df1700ba8c5c8c9) is irreducible when ![Image 77: {\displaystyle g\geq 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b84b2394f64ae9927e09ee51b848be213edecafe).

The moduli of curves exemplifies a typical situation: a moduli of nice objects tend not to be projective but only quasi-projective. Another case is a moduli of vector bundles on a curve. Here, there are the notions of [stable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_vector_bundle "Stable vector bundle") and semistable vector bundles on a smooth complete curve ![Image 78: {\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029). The moduli of semistable vector bundles of a given rank ![Image 79: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) and a given degree ![Image 80: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab) (degree of the determinant of the bundle) is then a projective variety denoted as ![Image 81: {\displaystyle SU_{C}(n,d)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/402d1496b171e2f860583696256d00fca96a52ee), which contains the set ![Image 82: {\displaystyle U_{C}(n,d)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ad4ace4170463b331fbf507aa7503312099daa2) of isomorphism classes of stable vector bundles of rank ![Image 83: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) and degree ![Image 84: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab) as an open subset.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-14) Since a line bundle is stable, such a moduli is a generalization of the Jacobian variety of ![Image 85: {\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029).

In general, in contrast to the case of moduli of curves, a compactification of a moduli need not be unique and, in some cases, different non-equivalent compactifications are constructed using different methods and by different authors. An example over ![Image 86: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7) is the problem of compactifying ![Image 87: {\displaystyle D/\Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31e36d6fca890fb2cf80bec0dcb1191a5535df42), the quotient of a bounded symmetric domain ![Image 88: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6) by an action of an arithmetic discrete group ![Image 89: {\displaystyle \Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cfde86a3f7ec967af9955d0988592f0693d2b19).[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-15) A basic example of ![Image 90: {\displaystyle D/\Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31e36d6fca890fb2cf80bec0dcb1191a5535df42) is when ![Image 91: {\displaystyle D={\mathfrak {H}}_{g}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/512447d1587bd55930bd37886f48ccefcc1a3237), [Siegel's upper half-space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siegel%27s_upper_half-space "Siegel's upper half-space") and ![Image 92: {\displaystyle \Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cfde86a3f7ec967af9955d0988592f0693d2b19)[commensurable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commensurability_(group_theory) "Commensurability (group theory)") with ![Image 93: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Sp} (2g,\mathbb {Z} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8d07869bdae034830dcf276ad7989c2080e86bbb); in that case, ![Image 94: {\displaystyle D/\Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31e36d6fca890fb2cf80bec0dcb1191a5535df42) has an interpretation as the moduli ![Image 95: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {A}}_{g}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/077d4daff49c6e91c17dd741ddc2323df981fe1e) of principally polarized complex abelian varieties of dimension ![Image 96: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) (a principal polarization identifies an abelian variety with its dual). The theory of toric varieties (or torus embeddings) gives a way to compactify ![Image 97: {\displaystyle D/\Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31e36d6fca890fb2cf80bec0dcb1191a5535df42), a [toroidal compactification](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Toroidal_compactification&action=edit&redlink=1 "Toroidal compactification (page does not exist)") of it.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-16)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-17) But there are other ways to compactify ![Image 98: {\displaystyle D/\Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31e36d6fca890fb2cf80bec0dcb1191a5535df42); for example, there is the [minimal compactification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimal_compactification "Minimal compactification") of ![Image 99: {\displaystyle D/\Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31e36d6fca890fb2cf80bec0dcb1191a5535df42) due to Baily and Borel: it is the [projective variety associated to the graded ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proj_construction "Proj construction") formed by [modular forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modular_form "Modular form") (in the Siegel case, [Siegel modular forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siegel_modular_form "Siegel modular form");[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-18) see also [Siegel modular variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siegel_modular_variety "Siegel modular variety")). The non-uniqueness of compactifications is due to the lack of moduli interpretations of those compactifications; i.e., they do not represent (in the category-theory sense) any natural moduli problem or, in the precise language, there is no natural [moduli stack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moduli_stack "Moduli stack") that would be an analog of moduli stack of stable curves.

### Non-affine and non-projective example

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_variety&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Non-affine and non-projective example")]

An algebraic variety can be neither affine nor projective. To give an example, let _X_ = **P**1 × **A**1 and _p_: _X_ → **A**1 the projection. Here _X_ is an algebraic variety since it is a product of varieties. It is not affine since **P**1 is a closed subvariety of _X_ (as the zero locus of _p_), but an affine variety cannot contain a projective variety of positive dimension as a closed subvariety. It is not projective either, since there is a nonconstant [regular function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_function "Regular function") on _X_; namely, _p_.

Another example of a non-affine non-projective variety is _X_ = **A**2 − (0, 0) (cf. _[Morphism of varieties §Examples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphism\_of\_varieties#Examples "Morphism of varieties")_.)

Consider the affine line ![Image 100: {\displaystyle \mathbb {A} ^{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/67530a5bd8c23c0be226ac63ddf5f6b2619e682d) over ![Image 101: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7). The complement of the circle ![Image 102: {\displaystyle \{z\in \mathbb {C} {\text{ with }}|z|^{2}=1\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff8d411bf776a44a0f8def619538f0a8a50af03f) in ![Image 103: {\displaystyle \mathbb {A} ^{1}=\mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8f62bdd01640ec554d3d7b70c9874f291475ff0c) is not an algebraic variety (nor even an algebraic set). Note that ![Image 104: {\displaystyle |z|^{2}-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f27bc4c8d06c68af94acd1c0de1e5b5d2533a62) is not a polynomial in ![Image 105: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98) (although it is a polynomial in the real coordinates ![Image 106: {\displaystyle x,y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ea0abffd33a692ded22accc104515a032851dff)). On the other hand, the complement of the origin in ![Image 107: {\displaystyle \mathbb {A} ^{1}=\mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8f62bdd01640ec554d3d7b70c9874f291475ff0c) is an algebraic (affine) variety, since the origin is the zero-locus of ![Image 108: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98). This may be explained as follows: the affine line has dimension one and so any subvariety of it other than itself must have strictly less dimension; namely, zero.

For similar reasons, a [unitary group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_group "Unitary group") (over the complex numbers) is not an algebraic variety, while the special linear group ![Image 109: {\displaystyle \operatorname {SL} _{n}(\mathbb {C} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/57faaf2eac1922e859d1a2047edb26f8fb2fbee1) is a closed subvariety of ![Image 110: {\displaystyle \operatorname {GL} _{n}(\mathbb {C} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1c81fb83c9a3c1639fe166eaa1c72180c64410b4), the zero-locus of ![Image 111: {\displaystyle \det -1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/604944a692a3584043dd1d5e06ec9941e4815307). (Over a different base field, a unitary group can however be given a structure of a variety.)

*   An affine algebraic set _V_ is a variety [if and only if](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If_and_only_if "If and only if")_I_(_V_) is a [prime ideal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_ideal "Prime ideal"); equivalently, _V_ is a variety if and only if its coordinate ring is an [integral domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_domain "Integral domain").[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-Harris-19): 52 [[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-Hartshorne-1): 4 
*   Every nonempty affine algebraic set may be written uniquely as a finite union of algebraic varieties (where none of the varieties in the decomposition is a subvariety of any other).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-Hartshorne-1): 5 
*   The **dimension** of a variety may be defined in various equivalent ways. See [Dimension of an algebraic variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_of_an_algebraic_variety "Dimension of an algebraic variety") for details.
*   A product of finitely many algebraic varieties (over an algebraically closed field) is an algebraic variety. A finite product of affine varieties is affine[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-20) and a finite product of projective varieties is projective.

Isomorphism of algebraic varieties
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_variety&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Isomorphism of algebraic varieties")]

Let _V_ 1, _V_ 2 be algebraic varieties. We say _V_ 1 and _V_ 2 are [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_isomorphism "Graph isomorphism"), and write _V_ 1 ≅ _V_ 2, if there are [regular maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_map_(algebraic_geometry) "Regular map (algebraic geometry)")_φ_: _V_ 1 → _V_ 2 and _ψ_: _V_ 2 → _V_ 1 such that the [compositions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition "Function composition")_ψ_ ∘ _φ_ and _φ_ ∘ _ψ_ are the [identity maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_function "Identity function") on _V_ 1 and _V_ 2 respectively.

Discussion and generalizations
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_variety&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Discussion and generalizations")]

The basic definitions and facts above enable one to do classical algebraic geometry. To be able to do more — for example, to deal with varieties over fields that are not [algebraically closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraically_closed_field "Algebraically closed field") — some foundational changes are required. The modern notion of a variety is considerably more abstract than the one above, though equivalent in the case of varieties over algebraically closed fields. An _abstract algebraic variety_ is a particular kind of scheme; the generalization to schemes on the geometric side enables an extension of the correspondence described above to a wider class of rings. A scheme is a [locally ringed space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Locally_ringed_space "Locally ringed space") such that every point has a neighbourhood that, as a locally ringed space, is isomorphic to a [spectrum of a ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectrum_of_a_ring "Spectrum of a ring"). Basically, a variety over k is a scheme whose [structure sheaf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structure_sheaf "Structure sheaf") is a [sheaf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheaf_(mathematics) "Sheaf (mathematics)") of k-algebras with the property that the rings _R_ that occur above are all [integral domains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_domain "Integral domain") and are all finitely generated k-algebras, that is to say, they are quotients of [polynomial algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_algebra "Polynomial algebra") by [prime ideals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_ideal "Prime ideal").

This definition works over any field k. It allows you to glue affine varieties (along common open sets) without worrying whether the resulting object can be put into some projective space. This also leads to difficulties since one can introduce somewhat pathological objects, e.g. an affine line with zero doubled. Such objects are usually not considered varieties, and are eliminated by requiring the schemes underlying a variety to be _separated_. (Strictly speaking, there is also a third condition, namely, that one needs only finitely many affine patches in the definition above.)

Some modern researchers also remove the restriction on a variety having [integral domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_domain "Integral domain") affine charts, and when speaking of a variety only require that the affine charts have trivial [nilradical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nilradical_of_a_ring "Nilradical of a ring").

A [complete variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_variety "Complete variety") is a variety such that any map from an open subset of a nonsingular [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_curve "Algebraic curve") into it can be extended uniquely to the whole curve. Every projective variety is complete, but not vice versa.

These varieties have been called "varieties in the sense of Serre", since [Serre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Serre "Jean-Pierre Serre")'s foundational paper [FAC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faisceaux_alg%C3%A9briques_coh%C3%A9rents "Faisceaux algébriques cohérents")[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_variety#cite_note-21) on [sheaf cohomology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheaf_cohomology "Sheaf cohomology") was written for them. They remain typical objects to start studying in algebraic geometry, even if more general objects are also used in an auxiliary way.

One way that leads to generalizations is to allow reducible algebraic sets (and fields k that aren't algebraically closed), so the rings _R_ may not be integral domains. A more significant modification is to allow [nilpotents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nilpotent "Nilpotent") in the sheaf of rings, that is, rings which are not **reduced**. This is one of several generalizations of classical algebraic geometry that are built into [Grothendieck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Grothendieck "Alexander Grothendieck")'s theory of schemes.

Allowing nilpotent elements in rings is related to keeping track of "multiplicities" in algebraic geometry. For example, the closed subscheme of the affine line defined by _x_ 2 = 0 is different from the subscheme defined by _x_ = 0 (the origin). More generally, the [fiber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_product_of_schemes "Fiber product of schemes") of a morphism of schemes _X_ → _Y_ at a point of _Y_ may be non-reduced, even if _X_ and _Y_ are reduced. Geometrically, this says that fibers of good mappings may have nontrivial "infinitesimal" structure.

There are further generalizations called [algebraic spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_space "Algebraic space") and [stacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_stack "Algebraic stack").

Algebraic manifolds
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Algebraic_variety&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Algebraic manifolds")]

An algebraic manifold is an algebraic variety that is also an _m_-dimensional manifold, and hence every sufficiently small local patch is isomorphic to _k m_. Equivalently, the variety is [smooth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_function "Smooth function") (free from singular points). When k is the real numbers, **R**, algebraic manifolds are called [Nash manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nash_manifold "Nash manifold"). Algebraic manifolds can be defined as the zero set of a finite collection of analytic algebraic functions. [Projective algebraic manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_algebraic_manifold "Projective algebraic manifold") are an equivalent definition for projective varieties. The [Riemann sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_sphere "Riemann sphere") is one example.
