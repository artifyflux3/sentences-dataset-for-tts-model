Signing of the Outer Space Treaty

The **Outer Space Treaty**, formally the **Treaty on Principles Governing the Activities of States in the Exploration and Use of Outer Space, including the Moon and Other Celestial Bodies**, is a [multilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilateralism "Multilateralism")[treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty "Treaty") that forms the basis of international [space law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_law "Space law"). Negotiated and drafted under the auspices of the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Security_Council "United Nations Security Council"), it was opened for signature in the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States"), the [United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom"), and the [Soviet Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_Union "Soviet Union") on 27 January 1967, entering into force on 10 October 1967. As of May 2025[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Outer_Space_Treaty&action=edit), 117 countries are parties to the treaty—including all major [spacefaring nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-faring_nations "Space-faring nations")—and another 22 are signatories.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-unoda-1)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-unodacn-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-6)

The Outer Space Treaty was spurred by the development of [intercontinental ballistic missiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intercontinental_ballistic_missile "Intercontinental ballistic missile") (ICBMs) in the 1950s, which could reach targets through [outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space "Outer space").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-US_DOS_OST-7) The Soviet Union's launch of [Sputnik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sputnik_1 "Sputnik 1"), the first artificial satellite, in October 1957, followed by a subsequent [arms race](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arms_race "Arms race") with the United States, hastened proposals to prohibit the use of outer space for military purposes. On 17 October 1963, the [U.N. General Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly "United Nations General Assembly") unanimously adopted a resolution prohibiting the introduction of [weapons of mass destruction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weapon_of_mass_destruction "Weapon of mass destruction") in outer space. Various proposals for an arms control treaty governing outer space were debated during a General Assembly session in December 1966, culminating in the drafting and adoption of the Outer Space Treaty the following January.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-US_DOS_OST-7)

Key provisions of the Outer Space Treaty include prohibiting nuclear weapons in space; limiting the use of the Moon and all other [celestial bodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_object "Astronomical object") to peaceful purposes; establishing that space shall be freely explored and used by all nations; and precluding any country from claiming sovereignty over outer space or any celestial body. Although it forbids establishing military bases, testing weapons and conducting military maneuvers on celestial bodies, the treaty does not expressly ban all military activities in space, nor the establishment of [military space forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_force "Space force") or the placement of conventional weapons in space.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-9) From 1968 to 1984, the OST gave birth to four additional agreements: rules for activities on the Moon; liability for damages caused by spacecraft; the safe return of fallen astronauts; and the registration of [space vehicles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_vehicle "Space vehicle").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-10)

OST provided many practical uses and was the most important link in the chain of international legal arrangements for space from the late 1950s to the mid-1980s. OST was at the heart of a 'network' of inter-state treaties and strategic power negotiations to achieve the best available conditions for [nuclear weapons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_weapon "Nuclear weapon") world security. The OST also declares that space is an area for free use and exploration by all and "shall be the province of all mankind". Drawing heavily from the [Antarctic Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antarctic_Treaty_System "Antarctic Treaty System") of 1961, the Outer Space Treaty likewise focuses on regulating certain activities and preventing unrestricted competition that could lead to conflict.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-US_DOS_OST-7) Consequently, it is largely silent or ambiguous on newly developed space activities such as [lunar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_resources "Lunar resources") and [asteroid mining](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid_mining "Asteroid mining").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-ORF_2019-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-IFL-13) Nevertheless, the Outer Space Treaty is the first and most foundational legal instrument of space law,[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-14) and its broader principles of promoting the civil and peaceful use of space continue to underpin multilateral initiatives in space, such as the [International Space Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Space_Station "International Space Station") and the [Artemis Program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_program "Artemis program").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-16)

Provisions
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Outer_Space_Treaty&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Provisions")]

The Outer Space Treaty represents the basic legal framework of international [space law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_law "Space law"). According to the [U.N. Office for Outer Space Affairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Office_for_Outer_Space_Affairs "United Nations Office for Outer Space Affairs") (UNOOSA), the core principles of the treaty are:[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-17)

*   the exploration and use of outer space shall be carried out for the benefit and in the interests of all countries and shall be the province of all mankind;
*   outer space shall be free for exploration and use by all states;
*   outer space is not subject to national appropriation by claim of sovereignty, by means of use or occupation, or by any other means;
*   states shall not place nuclear weapons or other weapons of mass destruction in orbit or on celestial bodies or station them in outer space in any other manner;
*   the Moon and other celestial bodies shall be used exclusively for peaceful purposes; prohibits their use for testing weapons of any kind, conducting military maneuvers, or establishing military bases, installations, and fortifications
*   astronauts shall be regarded as the envoys of mankind;
*   states shall be responsible for national space activities whether carried out by governmental or non-governmental entities;
*   states shall be liable for damage caused by their space objects; and
*   states shall avoid harmful contamination of space and celestial bodies.

Among its principles, it bars states party to the treaty from placing [weapons of mass destruction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weapon_of_mass_destruction "Weapon of mass destruction") in [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth") orbit, installing them on the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon") or any other [celestial body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_object "Astronomical object"), or otherwise stationing them in [outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space "Outer space"). It specifically limits the use of the Moon and other celestial bodies to peaceful purposes, and expressly prohibits their use for testing weapons of any kind, conducting military maneuvers, or establishing military bases, installations, and fortifications (Article IV). However, the treaty does not prohibit the placement of [conventional weapons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conventional_weapons "Conventional weapons") in orbit, and thus some highly destructive attack tactics, such as [kinetic bombardment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_bombardment "Kinetic bombardment"), are still potentially allowable.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-18) In addition, the treaty explicitly allows the use of military personnel and resources to support peaceful uses of space, mirroring a common practice permitted by the Antarctic Treaty regarding that continent. The treaty also states that the exploration of outer space shall be done to benefit all countries and that space shall be free for exploration and use by all the states.

Article II of the treaty explicitly forbids any government from "appropriating" a celestial body such as the Moon or a planet, whether by declaration, use, occupation, or "any other means".[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-19) However, the state that launches a space object, such as a satellite or space station, retains jurisdiction and control over that object;[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-20) by extension, a state is also liable for damages caused by its space object.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-21)

### Responsibility for activities in space

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Outer_Space_Treaty&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Responsibility for activities in space")]

Article VI of the Outer Space Treaty deals with international responsibility, stating that "the activities of non-governmental entities in outer space, including the Moon and other celestial bodies, shall require authorization and continuing supervision by the appropriate State Party to the Treaty" and that States Party shall bear international responsibility for national space activities whether carried out by governmental or non-governmental entities.

As a result of discussions arising from [Project West Ford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Project_West_Ford "Project West Ford") in 1963, a consultation clause was included in Article IX of the Outer Space Treaty: "A State Party to the Treaty which has reason to believe that an activity or experiment planned by another State Party in outer space, including the Moon and other celestial bodies, would cause potentially harmful interference with activities in the peaceful exploration and use of outer space, including the Moon and other celestial bodies, may request consultation concerning the activity or experiment."[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-22)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-23)

### Applicability in the 21st century

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Outer_Space_Treaty&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Applicability in the 21st century")]

Being primarily an arms control treaty for the peaceful use of outer space, the Outer Space Treaty offers limited and ambiguous regulations to newer space activities such as [lunar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_resources "Lunar resources") and [asteroid mining](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid_mining "Asteroid mining").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-ORF_2019-11)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-IFL-13)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-Davies-24) It is therefore debated whether the extraction of resources falls within the prohibitive language of appropriation, or whether the use of such resources encompasses the commercial use and exploitation.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-Koch_2018-25)

Seeking clearer guidelines, private U.S. companies lobbied the U.S. government, which in 2015 introduced the U.S. [Commercial Space Launch Competitiveness Act of 2015](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commercial_Space_Launch_Competitiveness_Act_of_2015 "Commercial Space Launch Competitiveness Act of 2015") legalizing space mining.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-26) Similar national legislation to legalize the appropriation of extraterrestrial resources are now being introduced by other countries, including Luxembourg, Japan, China, India, and Russia.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-ORF_2019-11)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-Davies-24)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-Library_Blog-27)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-28) In addition, the U.S. has led the creation of a series of bilateral agreements known as the [Artemis Accords](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords "Artemis Accords") that seek to clarify a number of issue related to the Outer Space Treaty, including the use of space resources.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-29) This has created some controversy regarding legal claims over the mining of celestial bodies for profit.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-Davies-24)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-Koch_2018-25)

### 1976 Bogota Declaration

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Outer_Space_Treaty&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: 1976 Bogota Declaration")]

The "[Declaration of the First Meeting of Equatorial Countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Declaration_of_the_First_Meeting_of_Equatorial_Countries "Declaration of the First Meeting of Equatorial Countries")", also known as the "Bogota Declaration", was one of the few attempts to challenge the Outer Space Treaty. It was promulgated in 1976 by eight [equatorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator "Equator") countries to assert [sovereignty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sovereignty "Sovereignty") over those portions of the [geostationary orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geostationary_orbit "Geostationary orbit") that continuously lie over the signatory nations' territory.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-30) These claims did not receive wider international support or recognition, and were subsequently abandoned.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-aasl31_20062-31)

Influence on space law
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Outer_Space_Treaty&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Influence on space law")]

As the first international legal instrument concerning space, the Outer Space Treaty is considered the "cornerstone" of space law.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-32)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-SLPandT-33) It was also the first major achievement of the United Nations in this area of law, following the adoption of the first U.N. General Assembly resolution on space in 1958,[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-34) and the first meeting of the U.N. [Committee on the Peaceful Uses of Outer Space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Committee_on_the_Peaceful_Uses_of_Outer_Space "United Nations Committee on the Peaceful Uses of Outer Space") (COPUOS) the subsequent year.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-UNISPACE-35)

Within roughly a decade of the treaty's entry into force, several other treaties were brokered by the U.N. to further develop the legal framework for activities in space:[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-SLPandT-33)

*   [Rescue Agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rescue_Agreement "Rescue Agreement") (1968)
*   [Space Liability Convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Liability_Convention "Space Liability Convention") (1972)
*   [Registration Convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Registration_Convention "Registration Convention") (1976)
*   [Moon Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon_Treaty "Moon Treaty") (1979)

With the exception of the Moon Treaty, to which only 18 nations are party, all other treaties on space law have been ratified by most major [space-faring nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space-faring_nations "Space-faring nations") (namely those capable of [orbital spaceflight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_first_orbital_launches_by_country "Timeline of first orbital launches by country")).[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-UNOOSA-36) COPUOS coordinates these treaties and other questions of [space jurisdiction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_jurisdiction "Space jurisdiction"), aided by the [U.N. Office for Outer Space Affairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_Office_for_Outer_Space_Affairs "United Nations Office for Outer Space Affairs").

List of parties
---------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Outer_Space_Treaty&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: List of parties")]

The Outer Space Treaty was opened for signature in the United States, the United Kingdom, and the Soviet Union on 27 January 1967, and entered into force on 10 October 1967. As of May 2025, 117 countries are parties to the treaty, while another 22 have signed the treaty but have not completed ratification.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-unoda-1)

Multiple dates indicate the different days in which states submitted their signature or deposition, which varied by location: (L) for [London](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/London "London"), (M) for [Moscow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moscow "Moscow"), and (W) for [Washington, D.C.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Washington,_D.C. "Washington, D.C.") Also indicated is whether the state became a party by way of signature and subsequent [ratification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ratification "Ratification"), by [accession](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/accession "wikt:accession") to the treaty after it had closed for signature, or by [succession of states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Succession_of_states "Succession of states") after separation from some other party to the treaty.

### Partially recognized state abiding by treaty

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Outer_Space_Treaty&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Partially recognized state abiding by treaty")]

The [Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Republic_of_China "Republic of China") (Taiwan), which is currently recognized by [11 UN member states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_recognition_of_the_Republic_of_China "International recognition of the Republic of China"), ratified the treaty prior to the [United Nations General Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly "United Nations General Assembly")'s [vote to transfer China's seat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations_General_Assembly_Resolution_2758 "United Nations General Assembly Resolution 2758") to the [People's Republic of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/People%27s_Republic_of_China "People's Republic of China") (PRC) in 1971. When the PRC subsequently ratified the treaty, they described the Republic of China's (ROC) ratification as "illegal". The ROC has committed itself to continue to adhere to the requirements of the treaty, and the United States has declared that it still considers the ROC to be "bound by its obligations".[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty#cite_note-unodacn-5)
