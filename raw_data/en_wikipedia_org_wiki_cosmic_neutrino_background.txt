From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

The **cosmic neutrino background** is a proposed background particle radiation composed of [neutrinos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino "Neutrino"). They are sometimes known as **relic neutrinos** or sometimes abbreviated **CNB** or **C ν B**, where the symbol ν is the Greek letter _[nu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nu\_(letter) "Nu (letter)")_, standard [particle physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_physics "Particle physics") symbol for a _[neutrino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino "Neutrino")_.

The C ν B is a relic of the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang"); while the [cosmic microwave background radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation "Cosmic microwave background radiation") (CMB) dates from when the universe was 379,000 years old, the [C ν B decoupled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino_decoupling "Neutrino decoupling") (separated) from matter when the universe was just one second old. It is estimated that today, the C ν B has a temperature of roughly 1.95[K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin").

As neutrinos rarely interact with matter, these neutrinos still exist today. They have a very low energy, around 10−4 to 10−6[eV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_volt "Electron volt").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background#cite_note-Follin-Knox-Millea-Pan-2015-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background#cite_note-Forbes-2016-09-09-cosmic-%CE%BD-2) Even high energy neutrinos are [notoriously difficult to detect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino_detector "Neutrino detector"), and the C ν B has energies around 10 10 times smaller, so the C ν B may not be directly observed in detail for many years, if at all.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background#cite_note-Follin-Knox-Millea-Pan-2015-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background#cite_note-Forbes-2016-09-09-cosmic-%CE%BD-2) However, [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang") cosmology makes many predictions about the C ν B, and there is very strong indirect evidence that the C ν B exists.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background#cite_note-Follin-Knox-Millea-Pan-2015-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background#cite_note-Forbes-2016-09-09-cosmic-%CE%BD-2)

The early universe consisted of a very hot dense plasma which was expanding and thus cooling. Particles in the plasma collided and reacted, maintaining an equilibrium according to the possible reactions. Around 1 s after the Big Bang, the equilibrium among electrons, positrons, and neutrinos was disrupted. Several annihilation reactions like ![Image 1: {\displaystyle {\text{e}}^{-}+{\text{e}}^{+}\longleftrightarrow \nu _{\text{e}}+{\bar {\nu }}_{\text{e}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89f8deab5c74debea13d64045290524642a99d59) stopped because expansion left the neutrinos too far apart on average to find each other.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background#cite_note-Longair-2006-3): 327  The neutrinos are said to be [decoupled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoupling_(cosmology) "Decoupling (cosmology)"). Rather than colliding and reacting, they kept going, a state known as [free streaming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_streaming "Free streaming"). Since the universe was almost perfectly homogeneous plasma, this decoupling happened throughout the universe at the same time. These neutrinos have been traveling in an ever expanding universe for over 13 billion years. Although vast in number their characteristic temperature is very low.

Temperature estimation
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_neutrino_background&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Temperature estimation")]

While the temperature of the cosmic neutrino background (C ν B) cannot be directly measured, it can be estimated from the measured temperature of the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background") (CMB) and physics of the early universe. The neutrinos and the photons were once at thermal equilibrium. The neutrinos decoupled first but before the photons decoupled, electrons and positrons in the hot plasma [annihilated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annihilation "Annihilation"), meaning they combined, producing more photons and raising the temperature. Conservation of [entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy "Entropy") allows the heating of the photons by the annihilation event to be estimated.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background#cite_note-Longair-2008-4): 340

The entropy of each particle in an equilibrium mixture is proportional to its [_effective number of degrees of freedom_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(physics_and_chemistry) "Degrees of freedom (physics and chemistry)"), g:

![Image 2: {\displaystyle s_{i}\propto g_{i}\,T^{3},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3b650f5424468cf00ad12977b66592215d4d2ba)
where and T is the plasma or photon temperature. The factor ![Image 3: {\displaystyle g_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ce36142a0a1c6660e82bdf3ef3f1551317efe0c) for the particle species engaged in the original equilibrium reaction:

+ 2 for each photon (or other massless [bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bosons "Bosons"), if any),[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background#cite_note-Weinberg-2008-Cosmology-5)+ 7/4 for each electron, positron, or other [fermion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermion "Fermion").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background#cite_note-Weinberg-2008-Cosmology-5)
The sum of all particle entropies, ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \Sigma _{i}g_{i}T^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8ad0fa116f14ee64e9e8ab6dc68302647ba039aa) is conserved. Annihilation converts the entropy of photons, electrons, and positrons into entropy of photons alone: ![Image 5: {\displaystyle {\frac {s_{\gamma }+s_{{\text{e}}^{-}}+s_{{\text{e}}^{+}}}{s_{\gamma }}}=\left({\frac {2+{\tfrac {7}{4}}+{\tfrac {7}{4}}}{2}}\right)=11/4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e3c0466207e899ef7aeb73c5dcd4c37d2ac42ff8) Consequently the photons will be hotter than the neutrinos by the cube root of this ratio:[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background#cite_note-Longair-2008-4): 340

![Image 6: {\displaystyle {\frac {T_{\gamma }}{T_{\nu }}}=\left({\frac {11}{4}}\right)^{1/3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3dd59102562dd3c0a368b0d17ae273e0074d3b0)
Since the cosmic photon background temperature at present has cooled to ![Image 7: {\displaystyle T_{\gamma }=2.725~{\text{K}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/694c16d5ef5a1ebded53c4e56514dda92451f7a2)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background#cite_note-Fixsen-Mather-2002-6) it follows that the neutrino background temperature is currently

![Image 8: {\displaystyle T_{\nu }\approx 1.95~{\text{K}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d4922f9308f3ab642333683a47a110703e469f6)
The above discussion is technically valid for massless neutrinos, which are always relativistic. For neutrinos with a non-zero rest mass, at low temperature where the neutrinos become non-relativistic, a description in terms of a temperature is not appropriate. In other words, when the neutrinos' thermal energy ![Image 9: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {3}{2}}kT_{\nu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27b34881c2617324a1f74fb5c28b6888ceb1cd56) (k is the [Boltzmann constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_constant "Boltzmann constant")) falls below the rest mass energy ![Image 10: {\displaystyle m_{\nu }c^{2};}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5cfae1695407eccee2462933708b56ef0636fe4) in a low-temperature case one should instead speak of the neutrinos' collective energy _density_, which remains both relevant and well-defined.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Relativistic neutrinos contribute to the radiation energy density of the universe ρ R, typically parameterized in terms of the effective number of neutrino species N ν:

![Image 11: {\displaystyle \rho _{\text{R}}={\frac {\pi ^{2}}{15}}T_{\gamma }^{4}(1+z)^{4}\left[1+{\frac {7}{8}}N_{\nu }\left({\frac {4}{11}}\right)^{4/3}\right],}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/391577e713cb4fecb8e0f9e4de2973c96bc1cfef)
where z denotes the [redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift"). The first term in the square brackets is due to the CMB, the second comes from the C ν B. The [Standard Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model "Standard Model") with its three neutrino species predicts a value of N ν ≃ 3.046,[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background#cite_note-Gianpiero-etal-2005-7) including a small correction caused by a non-thermal distortion of the spectra during [e+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positron "Positron")–[e−](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron")[annihilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annihilation "Annihilation"). The radiation density had a major impact on various physical processes in the early universe, leaving potentially detectable imprints on measurable quantities, thus allowing us to [infer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inference "Inference") the value of N ν from observations.

### Big Bang nucleosynthesis

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_neutrino_background&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Big Bang nucleosynthesis")]

Due to its effect on the [expansion rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_expansion_of_space "Metric expansion of space") of the universe during [Big Bang nucleosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_nucleosynthesis "Big Bang nucleosynthesis") (BBN), the theoretical expectations for the primordial abundances of light elements depend on N ν. Astrophysical measurements of the primordial [4 He](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium") and [2 D](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuterium "Deuterium") abundances lead to a value of N ν = 3.14+0.70

−0.65 at 68% [c.l.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confidence_interval "Confidence interval"),[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background#cite_note-Cyburt-etal-2005-8) in very good agreement with the Standard Model expectation.

### From the cosmic microwave background

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_neutrino_background&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: From the cosmic microwave background")]

#### Anisotropies and structure formation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_neutrino_background&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Anisotropies and structure formation")]

The presence of the C ν B affects the evolution of CMB anisotropies as well as the growth of matter perturbations in two ways: Due to its contribution to the radiation density of the universe (which determines for instance the time of matter–radiation equality), and due to the neutrinos' anisotropic stress which dampens the acoustic oscillations of the spectra. Additionally, [free-streaming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free-streaming "Free-streaming") massive neutrinos suppress the growth of structure on small scales. The [WMAP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe "Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe") spacecraft's five-year data combined with [type Ia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_Ia "Type Ia")[supernova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova "Supernova") data and information about the [baryon acoustic oscillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon_acoustic_oscillation "Baryon acoustic oscillation") scale yielded N ν = 4.34+0.88

−0.86 at 68% [c.l.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confidence_limit "Confidence limit"),[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background#cite_note-Komatsu-WMAP-etal-2011-9) providing an independent confirmation of the BBN constraints. The [Planck spacecraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft) "Planck (spacecraft)") collaboration has published the tightest bound to date on the effective number of neutrino species, at N ν = 3.15±0.23.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background#cite_note-Ade-Planck-etal-2016-10)

Big Bang cosmology makes many predictions about the C ν B, and there is very strong indirect evidence that the cosmic neutrino background exists, both from [Big Bang nucleosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_nucleosynthesis "Big Bang nucleosynthesis") predictions of the helium abundance, and from anisotropies in the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background"). One of these predictions is that neutrinos will have left a subtle imprint on the cosmic microwave background (CMB). It is well known that the CMB has irregularities. Some of the CMB fluctuations were roughly regularly spaced, because of the effect of [baryon acoustic oscillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon_acoustic_oscillation "Baryon acoustic oscillation"). In theory, the decoupled neutrinos should have had a very slight effect on the [phase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_(waves) "Phase (waves)") of the various CMB fluctuations.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background#cite_note-Follin-Knox-Millea-Pan-2015-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background#cite_note-Forbes-2016-09-09-cosmic-%CE%BD-2)

In 2015, it was reported that such shifts had been detected in the CMB. Moreover, the fluctuations corresponded to neutrinos of almost exactly the temperature predicted by [Big Bang theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_theory "Big Bang theory") (1.96 ± 0.02 K compared to a prediction of 1.95 K), and exactly three types of neutrino, the same number of neutrino flavours currently predicted by the [Standard Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model "Standard Model").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background#cite_note-Follin-Knox-Millea-Pan-2015-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background#cite_note-Forbes-2016-09-09-cosmic-%CE%BD-2)

Prospects for the direct detection
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_neutrino_background&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Prospects for the direct detection")]

Confirmation of the existence of these relic neutrinos may only be possible by directly detecting them using experiments on Earth. This will be difficult as the neutrinos which make up the C ν B are non-relativistic, in addition to interacting only weakly with normal matter, and so any effect they have in a detector will be hard to identify. The neutrino interactions that are measured in current particle detectors are all with neutrinos newly [created in the Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino#Solar "Neutrino"), [nuclear reactors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino#Reactor_neutrinos "Neutrino"), [weapons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino#Nuclear_weapons "Neutrino"), [particle accelerators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino#Accelerator_neutrinos "Neutrino"), [cosmic ray collisions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino#Atmospheric "Neutrino"), and [supernovas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SuperNova_Early_Warning_System "SuperNova Early Warning System"). Even among those, only the neutrinos with the highest kinetic energies are feasibly detectable. It is something of a "lose-lose" situation: The lower a neutrino's kinetic energy, the lower its probability of interacting with matter, and the even slighter, less noticeable, the matter's response will be even if some rare event were to occur.

One proposed method of direct detection of the C ν B is to use the capture of cosmic relic neutrinos on [tritium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tritium "Tritium") i.e. 3 H, leading to an induced form of [beta decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_decay "Beta decay").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background#cite_note-Long-Lunardn-Sabanclr-2014-11)

The neutrinos of the C ν B would lead to the production of electrons via the reaction

![Image 12: {\displaystyle \nu +{}^{3}{\text{H}}\rightarrow {}^{3}{\text{He}}+{\text{e}}^{-},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3666268f9c1ff96437fde2e545beffacde64517)
while the main background comes from electrons produced via natural beta decay

![Image 13: {\displaystyle {}^{3}{\text{H}}\rightarrow {}^{3}{\text{He}}+{\text{e}}^{-}+{\bar {\nu }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2c26aadd1ddc7f9a8213f8845ff497e55478e8a1)
These electrons would be detected by the experimental apparatus in order to measure the size of the C ν B. The latter source of electrons is far more numerous, however, their maximum energy is smaller than the average energy of the C ν B-electrons by twice the average neutrino mass. Since this mass is tiny, of the order of a few [electronvolts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt "Electronvolt") or less, such a detector must have an excellent energy resolution in order to separate the signal from the background. One such proposed experiment is called PTOLEMY, which will be made up of 100 g of tritium target.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background#cite_note-Betts-PTOLEMY-etal-2013-12) The detector demonstrator (with about 0.2 g of tritium) should be ready by 2025.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background#cite_note-Mangano-PTOLEMY-etal-2019-13)
