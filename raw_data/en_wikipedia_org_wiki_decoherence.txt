[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/50/DecoherenceQuantumClassical_en.svg/250px-DecoherenceQuantumClassical_en.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:DecoherenceQuantumClassical_en.svg)

In classical scattering of a target body by environmental [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon"), the motion of the target body will not be changed by the scattered photons on the average. In quantum scattering, the interaction between the scattered photons and the superposed target body will cause them to be [entangled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entangled_quantum_states "Entangled quantum states"), thereby delocalizing the phase coherence from the target body to the whole system, rendering the interference pattern unobservable.

**Quantum decoherence** is the loss of [quantum coherence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coherence_(physics)#Quantum_coherence "Coherence (physics)"). It involves generally a loss of information of a system to its environment. Quantum decoherence has been studied to understand how quantum systems convert to systems that can be explained by classical mechanics. Beginning out of attempts to extend the understanding of quantum mechanics, the theory has developed in several directions and experimental studies have confirmed some of the key issues. Quantum computing relies on quantum coherence and is one of the primary practical applications of the concept.

In [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), a physical system is described by a mathematical representation called a [quantum state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state "Quantum state"). Probabilities for the outcomes of experiments upon a system are calculated by applying the [Born rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Born_rule "Born rule") to the quantum state describing that system. Quantum states are either _pure_ or _mixed_; pure states are also known as _wavefunctions_. Assigning a pure state to a quantum system implies certainty about the outcome of some [measurement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measurement_in_quantum_mechanics "Measurement in quantum mechanics") on that system, i.e., that there exists a measurement for which one of the possible outcomes will occur with probability 1. In the absence of outside forces or interactions, a quantum state evolves [unitarily](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitarity_(physics) "Unitarity (physics)") over time. Consequently, a pure quantum state remains pure. However, if the system is not perfectly isolated, for example during a measurement, [coherence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_coherence "Quantum coherence") is shared with the environment and appears to be lost with time ─ a process called quantum decoherence or environmental decoherence. The quantum coherence is not lost but rather mixed with many more degrees of freedom in the environment, analogous to the way energy appears to be lost during friction in classical mechanics when it actually has produced heat in the environment.

[Decoherence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coherence_(physics) "Coherence (physics)") can be viewed as the loss of information from a system into the environment (often modeled as a [heat bath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_bath "Heat bath")),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-Bacon-1) since every system is loosely coupled with the energetic state of its surroundings. Viewed in isolation, the system's dynamics are non-unitary (although the combined system plus environment evolves in a unitary fashion).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-Lidar_and_Whaley-2) Thus the dynamics of the system alone are [irreversible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irreversibility "Irreversibility"). As with any coupling, [entanglements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_entanglement "Quantum entanglement") are generated between the system and environment. These have the effect of sharing [quantum information](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_information "Quantum information") with—or transferring it to—the surroundings.

History and interpretation
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_decoherence&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: History and interpretation")]

### Relation to interpretation of quantum mechanics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_decoherence&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Relation to interpretation of quantum mechanics")]

An [interpretation of quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interpretation_of_quantum_mechanics "Interpretation of quantum mechanics") is an attempt to explain how the mathematical theory of quantum physics might correspond to experienced [reality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reality "Reality").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-3) Decoherence calculations can be done in any interpretation of quantum mechanics, since those calculations are an application of the standard mathematical tools of quantum theory. However, the subject of decoherence has been closely related to the problem of interpretation throughout its history.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-camilleri2009-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-5)

Decoherence has been used to understand the possibility of the [collapse of the wave function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collapse_of_the_wave_function "Collapse of the wave function") in quantum mechanics. Decoherence does not generate _actual_ wave-function collapse. It only provides a framework for _apparent_ wave-function collapse, as the components of a quantum system entangle with other quantum systems within the same environment. That is, components of the wave function are decoupled from a [coherent system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coherence_(physics)#Quantum_coherence "Coherence (physics)") and acquire phases from their immediate surroundings. A total superposition of the global or [universal wavefunction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_wavefunction "Universal wavefunction") still exists (and remains coherent at the global level), but its ultimate fate remains an [interpretational issue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interpretations_of_quantum_mechanics "Interpretations of quantum mechanics").

With respect to the [measurement problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measurement_problem "Measurement problem"), decoherence provides an explanation for the transition of the system to a [mixture of states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed_state_(physics) "Mixed state (physics)") that seem to correspond to those states observers perceive. Moreover, observation indicates that this mixture looks like a proper [quantum ensemble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_ensemble "Quantum ensemble") in a measurement situation, as the measurements lead to the "realization" of precisely one state in the "ensemble".

The philosophical views of [Werner Heisenberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg "Werner Heisenberg") and [Niels Bohr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niels_Bohr "Niels Bohr") have often been grouped together as the "[Copenhagen interpretation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copenhagen_interpretation_of_quantum_mechanics "Copenhagen interpretation of quantum mechanics")", despite significant divergences between them on important points.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-Howard_2004-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-camilleri2015-7) In 1955, Heisenberg suggested that the interaction of a system with its surrounding environment would eliminate quantum interference effects. However, Heisenberg did not provide a detailed account of how this might transpire, nor did he make explicit the importance of entanglement in the process.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-camilleri2015-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-8)

### Origin of the concepts

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_decoherence&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Origin of the concepts")]

[Nevill Mott](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nevill_Mott "Nevill Mott")'s solution to the iconic [Mott problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mott_problem "Mott problem") in 1929 is considered in retrospect to be the first quantum decoherence work.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-FigariTeta-9) It was cited by the first modern theoretical treatment.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-10)

Although he did not use the term, the concept of quantum decoherence was first introduced in 1951 by the American physicist [David Bohm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Bohm "David Bohm"),[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-12) who called it the "destruction of interference in the process of measurement". Bohm later used decoherence to handle the measurement process in the [de Broglie-Bohm interpretation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Broglie%E2%80%93Bohm_theory "De Broglie–Bohm theory") of quantum theory.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-13)

The significance of decoherence was further highlighted in 1970 by the German physicist [H. Dieter Zeh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H._Dieter_Zeh "H. Dieter Zeh"),[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-Zeh-14) and it has been a subject of active research since the 1980s.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-Schlosshauer-15) Decoherence has been developed into a complete framework, but there is controversy as to whether it solves the [measurement problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measurement_problem "Measurement problem"), as the founders of decoherence theory admit in their seminal papers.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-Adler2001-16)

The study of decoherence as a proper subject began in 1970, with H. Dieter Zeh's paper "On the Interpretation of Measurement in Quantum Theory".[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-camilleri2009-4)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-Zeh-14) Zeh regarded the wavefunction as a physical entity, rather than a calculational device or a compendium of statistical information (as is typical for Copenhagen-type interpretations), and he proposed that it should evolve unitarily, in accord with the Schrödinger equation, at all times. Zeh was initially unaware of [Hugh Everett III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hugh_Everett_III "Hugh Everett III")'s earlier work,[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-everett57-17) which also proposed a universal wavefunction evolving unitarily; he revised his paper to reference Everett after learning of Everett's "relative-state interpretation" through an article by [Bryce DeWitt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bryce_DeWitt "Bryce DeWitt").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-camilleri2009-4) (DeWitt was the one who termed Everett's proposal the [many-worlds interpretation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Many-worlds_interpretation "Many-worlds interpretation"), by which name it is commonly known.) For Zeh, the question of how to interpret quantum mechanics was of key importance, and an interpretation along the lines of Everett's was the most natural. Partly because of a general disinterest among physicists for interpretational questions, Zeh's work remained comparatively neglected until the early 1980s, when two papers by [Wojciech Zurek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wojciech_Zurek "Wojciech Zurek")[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-zurek81-18)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-zurek82-19) invigorated the subject. Unlike Zeh's publications, Zurek's articles were fairly agnostic about interpretation, focusing instead on specific problems of density-matrix dynamics. Zurek's interest in decoherence stemmed from furthering Bohr's analysis of the double-slit experiment in his reply to the [Einstein–Podolsky–Rosen paradox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein%E2%80%93Podolsky%E2%80%93Rosen_paradox "Einstein–Podolsky–Rosen paradox"), work he had undertaken with [Bill Wootters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bill_Wootters "Bill Wootters"),[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-20) and he has since argued that decoherence brings a kind of rapprochement between Everettian and Copenhagen-type views.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-camilleri2009-4)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-21)

Decoherence does not claim to provide a mechanism for some actual wave-function collapse; rather it puts forth a reasonable framework for the appearance of wave-function collapse. The quantum nature of the system is simply entangled into the environment so that a total superposition of the wave function still exists, but exists—at least for all practical purposes—beyond the realm of measurement.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-22)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-23) By definition, the claim that a merged but unmeasurable wave function still exists cannot be proven experimentally. Decoherence is needed to understand why a quantum system begins to obey classical probability rules after interacting with its environment (due to the suppression of the interference terms when applying Born's probability rules to the system).

Criticism of the adequacy of decoherence theory to solve the measurement problem has been expressed by [Anthony Leggett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthony_Leggett "Anthony Leggett").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-24)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-25)

A more rigorous derivation in [Dirac notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_notation "Dirac notation") shows how decoherence destroys interference effects and the "quantum nature" of systems. ### Phase-space picture

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_decoherence&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Phase-space picture")]

An _N_-particle system can be represented in non-relativistic quantum mechanics by a [wave function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_function "Wave function")![Image 2: {\displaystyle \psi (x_{1},x_{2},\dots ,x_{N})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/12efaac58af3b2a6d6af248d6f7de6fab473c12f), where each _x i_ is a point in 3-dimensional space. This has analogies with the classical [phase space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_space "Phase space"). A classical phase space contains a real-valued function in 6 _N_ dimensions (each particle contributes 3 spatial coordinates and 3 momenta). In this case a "quantum" phase space, on the other hand, involves a complex-valued function on a 3 _N_-dimensional space. The position and momenta are represented by operators that do not [commute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutativity "Commutativity"), and ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45e5789e5d9c8f7c79744f43ecaaf8ba42a8553a) lives in the mathematical structure of a Hilbert space. Aside from these differences, however, the rough analogy holds.

Different previously isolated, non-interacting systems occupy different phase spaces. Alternatively we can say that they occupy different lower-dimensional [subspaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_subspace "Linear subspace") in the phase space of the joint system. The _effective_ dimensionality of a system's phase space is the number of _[degrees of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees\_of\_freedom\_(physics\_and\_chemistry) "Degrees of freedom (physics and chemistry)")_ present, which—in non-relativistic models—is 6 times the number of a system's _free_ particles. For a [macroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macroscopic "Macroscopic") system this will be a very large dimensionality. When two systems (the environment being one system) start to interact, though, their associated state vectors are no longer constrained to the subspaces. Instead the combined state vector time-evolves a path through the "larger volume", whose dimensionality is the product of the dimensions of the two subspaces. The extent to which two vectors interfere with each other is a measure of how "close" they are to each other (formally, their overlap or Hilbert space multiplies together) in the phase space. When a system couples to an external environment, the dimensionality of, and hence "volume" available to, the joint state vector increases enormously. Each environmental degree of freedom contributes an extra dimension.

The original system's wave function can be expanded in many different ways as a sum of elements in a quantum superposition. Each expansion corresponds to a projection of the wave vector onto a basis. The basis can be chosen at will. Choosing an expansion where the resulting basis elements interact with the environment in an element-specific way, such elements will—with overwhelming probability—be rapidly separated from each other by their natural unitary time evolution along their own independent paths. After a very short interaction, there is almost no chance of further interference. The process is effectively [irreversible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reversible_process_(thermodynamics) "Reversible process (thermodynamics)"). The different elements effectively become "lost" from each other in the expanded phase space created by coupling with the environment. In phase space, this decoupling is monitored through the [Wigner quasi-probability distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wigner_quasi-probability_distribution "Wigner quasi-probability distribution"). The original elements are said to have _decohered_. The environment has effectively selected out those expansions or decompositions of the original state vector that decohere (or lose phase coherence) with each other. This is called "environmentally-induced superselection", or [einselection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einselection "Einselection").[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-zurek03-26) The decohered elements of the system no longer exhibit [quantum interference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_interference "Quantum interference") between each other, as in a [double-slit experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double-slit_experiment "Double-slit experiment"). Any elements that decohere from each other via environmental interactions are said to be quantum-entangled with the environment. The converse is not true: not all entangled states are decohered from each other.

Any measuring device or apparatus acts as an environment, since at some stage along the measuring chain, it has to be large enough to be read by humans. It must possess a very large number of hidden degrees of freedom. In effect, the interactions may be considered to be quantum measurements. As a result of an interaction, the wave functions of the system and the measuring device become entangled with each other. Decoherence happens when different portions of the system's wave function become entangled in different ways with the measuring device. For two einselected elements of the entangled system's state to interfere, both the original system and the measuring in both elements device must significantly overlap, in the scalar product sense. If the measuring device has many degrees of freedom, it is _very_ unlikely for this to happen.

As a consequence, the system behaves as a classical [statistical ensemble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_ensemble "Statistical ensemble") of the different elements rather than as a single coherent [quantum superposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_superposition "Quantum superposition") of them. From the perspective of each ensemble member's measuring device, the system appears to have irreversibly collapsed onto a state with a precise value for the measured attributes, relative to that element. This provides one explanation of how the Born rule coefficients effectively act as probabilities as per the measurement postulate constituting a solution to the quantum measurement problem.

### Loss of interference and the transition from quantum to classical probabilities

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_decoherence&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Loss of interference and the transition from quantum to classical probabilities")]

The utility of decoherence lies in its application to the analysis of probabilities, before and after environmental interaction, and in particular to the vanishing of [quantum interference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_interference "Quantum interference") terms after decoherence has occurred. If we ask what is the probability of observing the system making a [transition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state "Quantum state") from ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45e5789e5d9c8f7c79744f43ecaaf8ba42a8553a) to ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4)_before_![Image 28: {\displaystyle \psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45e5789e5d9c8f7c79744f43ecaaf8ba42a8553a) has interacted with its environment, then application of the [Born probability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Born_probability "Born probability") rule states that the transition probability is the [squared modulus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squared_modulus "Squared modulus") of the scalar product of the two states:

![Image 29: {\displaystyle \operatorname {prob} _{\text{before}}(\psi \to \phi )=\left|\langle \psi |\phi \rangle \right|^{2}=\left|\sum _{i}\psi _{i}^{*}\phi _{i}\right|^{2}=\sum _{i}|\psi _{i}^{*}\phi _{i}|^{2}+\sum _{ij;i\neq j}\psi _{i}^{*}\psi _{j}\phi _{j}^{*}\phi _{i},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7b7096a41a946f108041bef68826e4435b584d57)
where ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \psi _{i}=\langle i|\psi \rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06b8fc110e591f37522342089d73558bb84dfcdf), ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \psi _{i}^{*}=\langle \psi |i\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ff7bcba3490e0aca68ec21c42372910053e37ad), and ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \phi _{i}=\langle i|\phi \rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/03e58a8115fee9c07cee8dfaf86a67c55dcdca73) etc.

The above expansion of the transition probability has terms that involve ![Image 33: {\displaystyle i\neq j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d95aeb406bb427ac96806bc00c30c91d31b858be); these can be thought of as representing _interference_ between the different basis elements or quantum alternatives. This is a purely quantum effect and represents the non-additivity of the probabilities of quantum alternatives.

To calculate the probability of observing the system making a quantum leap from ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45e5789e5d9c8f7c79744f43ecaaf8ba42a8553a) to ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4)_after_![Image 36: {\displaystyle \psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45e5789e5d9c8f7c79744f43ecaaf8ba42a8553a) has interacted with its environment, then application of the [Born probability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Born_probability "Born probability") rule states that we must sum over all the relevant possible states ![Image 37: {\displaystyle |\epsilon _{i}\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab969faf673fb6654a9b38d134364d5f75d91fb2) of the environment _before_ squaring the modulus:

![Image 38: {\displaystyle \operatorname {prob} _{\text{after}}(\psi \to \phi )=\sum _{j}\,\left|\langle {\text{after}}\right|\phi ,\epsilon _{j}\rangle |^{2}=\sum _{j}\,\left|\sum _{i}\psi _{i}^{*}\langle i,\epsilon _{i}|\phi ,\epsilon _{j}\rangle \right|^{2}=\sum _{j}\left|\sum _{i}\psi _{i}^{*}\phi _{i}\langle \epsilon _{i}|\epsilon _{j}\rangle \right|^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a9bd440ed7215c249a9f88831ecc673d4adfd333)
The internal summation vanishes when we apply the decoherence/einselection condition ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \langle \epsilon _{i}|\epsilon _{j}\rangle \approx \delta _{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2466e0dd2fd67efb157463fdb91fea80b1cbf7a3), and the formula simplifies to

![Image 40: {\displaystyle \operatorname {prob} _{\text{after}}(\psi \to \phi )\approx \sum _{j}|\psi _{j}^{*}\phi _{j}|^{2}=\sum _{i}|\psi _{i}^{*}\phi _{i}|^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2cd61dfeba7688a17aec024e080ab833a9481d27)
If we compare this with the formula we derived before the environment introduced decoherence, we can see that the effect of decoherence has been to move the summation sign ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \textstyle \sum _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eeaf2c66aede42aea0aac0aaf6b688af11c883c7) from inside of the modulus sign to outside. As a result, all the cross- or [quantum interference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_interference "Quantum interference")-terms

![Image 42: {\displaystyle \sum _{ij;i\neq j}\psi _{i}^{*}\psi _{j}\phi _{j}^{*}\phi _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e650e351b0b863847e5bb7985c59f7c7dd76439)
have vanished from the transition-probability calculation. The decoherence has [irreversibly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reversible_process_(thermodynamics) "Reversible process (thermodynamics)") converted quantum behaviour (additive [probability amplitudes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_amplitude "Probability amplitude")) to classical behaviour (additive probabilities).[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-zurek03-26)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-zurek91-27)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-Zurek02-28) However, Ballentine[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-29) shows that the significant impact of decoherence to reduce interference need not have significance for the transition of quantum systems to classical limits.

In terms of density matrices, the loss of interference effects corresponds to the diagonalization of the "environmentally traced-over" density matrix.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-zurek03-26)

### Density-matrix approach

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_decoherence&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Density-matrix approach")]

The effect of decoherence on [density matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_matrix "Density matrix") is essentially the decay or rapid vanishing of the [off-diagonal elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Off-diagonal_element "Off-diagonal element") of the [partial trace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_trace "Partial trace") of the joint system's density matrix, i.e. the [trace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trace_(linear_algebra) "Trace (linear algebra)"), with respect to _any_ environmental basis, of the density matrix of the combined system _and_ its environment. The decoherence irreversibly converts the "averaged" or "environmentally traced-over"[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-zurek03-26) density matrix from a pure state to a reduced mixture; it is this that gives the _appearance_ of [wave-function collapse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave-function_collapse "Wave-function collapse"). Again, this is called "environmentally induced superselection", or einselection.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-zurek03-26) The advantage of taking the partial trace is that this procedure is indifferent to the environmental basis chosen.

Initially, the density matrix of the combined system can be denoted as

![Image 43: {\displaystyle \rho =|{\text{before}}\rangle \langle {\text{before}}|=|\psi \rangle \langle \psi |\otimes |\epsilon \rangle \langle \epsilon |,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/17b92ed6cef3434a4790090497b07bbe3b87a711)
where ![Image 44: {\displaystyle |\epsilon \rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81706d6a98c537aa6f75f33b3e1a52e388967262) is the state of the environment. Then if the transition happens before any interaction takes place between the system and the environment, the environment subsystem has no part and can be [traced out](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_entanglement#Reduced_density_matrices "Quantum entanglement"), leaving the reduced density matrix for the system:

![Image 45: {\displaystyle \rho _{\text{sys}}=\operatorname {Tr} _{\textrm {env}}(\rho )=|\psi \rangle \langle \psi |\langle \epsilon |\epsilon \rangle =|\psi \rangle \langle \psi |.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59a30a853c59a07c84c922c198d444f50bf190a7)
Now the transition probability will be given as

![Image 46: {\displaystyle \operatorname {prob} _{\text{before}}(\psi \to \phi )=\langle \phi |\rho _{\text{sys}}|\phi \rangle =\langle \phi |\psi \rangle \langle \psi |\phi \rangle ={\big |}\langle \psi |\phi \rangle {\big |}^{2}=\sum _{i}|\psi _{i}^{*}\phi _{i}|^{2}+\sum _{ij;i\neq j}\psi _{i}^{*}\psi _{j}\phi _{j}^{*}\phi _{i},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0139590f8975fdde02a82e07906d410220773388)
where ![Image 47: {\displaystyle \psi _{i}=\langle i|\psi \rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06b8fc110e591f37522342089d73558bb84dfcdf), ![Image 48: {\displaystyle \psi _{i}^{*}=\langle \psi |i\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ff7bcba3490e0aca68ec21c42372910053e37ad), and ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \phi _{i}=\langle i|\phi \rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/03e58a8115fee9c07cee8dfaf86a67c55dcdca73) etc.

Now the case when transition takes place after the interaction of the system with the environment. The combined density matrix will be

![Image 50: {\displaystyle \rho =|{\text{after}}\rangle \langle {\text{after}}|=\sum _{i,j}\psi _{i}\psi _{j}^{*}|i,\epsilon _{i}\rangle \langle j,\epsilon _{j}|=\sum _{i,j}\psi _{i}\psi _{j}^{*}|i\rangle \langle j|\otimes |\epsilon _{i}\rangle \langle \epsilon _{j}|.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89cc6fada02dff031239d03cd89ce7c294721fac)
To get the reduced density matrix of the system, we trace out the environment and employ the decoherence/einselection condition and see that the off-diagonal terms vanish (a result obtained by Erich Joos and H. D. Zeh in 1985):[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-JZ-30)

![Image 51: {\displaystyle \rho _{\text{sys}}=\operatorname {Tr} _{\text{env}}{\Big (}\sum _{i,j}\psi _{i}\psi _{j}^{*}|i\rangle \langle j|\otimes |\epsilon _{i}\rangle \langle \epsilon _{j}|{\Big )}=\sum _{i,j}\psi _{i}\psi _{j}^{*}|i\rangle \langle j|\langle \epsilon _{j}|\epsilon _{i}\rangle =\sum _{i,j}\psi _{i}\psi _{j}^{*}|i\rangle \langle j|\delta _{ij}=\sum _{i}|\psi _{i}|^{2}|i\rangle \langle i|.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4d8467c7c5f2a58431f393ddc59197dd82cedde8)
Similarly, the final reduced density matrix after the transition will be

![Image 52: {\displaystyle \sum _{j}|\phi _{j}|^{2}|j\rangle \langle j|.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/30f862be6f9656e0fd8771e139382237075993a2)
The transition probability will then be given as

![Image 53: {\displaystyle \operatorname {prob} _{\text{after}}(\psi \to \phi )=\sum _{i,j}|\psi _{i}|^{2}|\phi _{j}|^{2}\langle j|i\rangle \langle i|j\rangle =\sum _{i}|\psi _{i}^{*}\phi _{i}|^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64856e134cf9284bd43e64bfd5bb15d87f3d6a4a)
which has no contribution from the interference terms

![Image 54: {\displaystyle \sum _{ij;i\neq j}\psi _{i}^{*}\psi _{j}\phi _{j}^{*}\phi _{i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b0ca92740a5c422e9a5fd48b9edc00378fdd3b9)
The density-matrix approach has been combined with the [Bohmian approach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Broglie%E2%80%93Bohm_theory "De Broglie–Bohm theory") to yield a _reduced-trajectory approach_, taking into account the system [reduced density matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reduced_density_matrix "Reduced density matrix") and the influence of the environment.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-31)

### Operator-sum representation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_decoherence&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Operator-sum representation")]

Consider a system _S_ and environment (bath) _B_, which are closed and can be treated quantum-mechanically. Let ![Image 55: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {H}}_{S}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/313e684309e1c49d4c9ed3716d7a06234c2e2a10) and ![Image 56: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {H}}_{B}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bfea1102a35aff8c6435147dc9dffc21b4cfe4ef) be the system's and bath's Hilbert spaces respectively. Then the Hamiltonian for the combined system is

![Image 57: {\displaystyle {\hat {H}}={\hat {H}}_{S}\otimes {\hat {I}}_{B}+{\hat {I}}_{S}\otimes {\hat {H}}_{B}+{\hat {H}}_{I},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/42cf30b4299d99b4e5123f269abb387675910d4c)
where ![Image 58: {\displaystyle {\hat {H}}_{S},{\hat {H}}_{B}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01ff886d1cbeda2ee681d7b2e6ee5bb0de94a0d1) are the system and bath Hamiltonians respectively, ![Image 59: {\displaystyle {\hat {H}}_{I}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/baa8c2b7a2e62020f8e3abd6c64bcb52b1b0a11f) is the interaction Hamiltonian between the system and bath, and ![Image 60: {\displaystyle {\hat {I}}_{S},{\hat {I}}_{B}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10c71ec2f53eeb035d9592a71d9dad99a6147671) are the identity operators on the system and bath Hilbert spaces respectively. The time-evolution of the [density operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_operator "Density operator") of this closed system is unitary and, as such, is given by

![Image 61: {\displaystyle \rho _{SB}(t)={\hat {U}}(t)\rho _{SB}(0){\hat {U}}^{\dagger }(t),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/62a70a4775706bde8e24b7ab2eda090976b0f3e6)
where the unitary operator is ![Image 62: {\displaystyle {\hat {U}}=e^{-i{\hat {H}}t/\hbar }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b8e04836d90ac21ae512dfd132632a76f3418f8). If the system and bath are not [entangled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_entanglement "Quantum entanglement") initially, then we can write ![Image 63: {\displaystyle \rho _{SB}=\rho _{S}\otimes \rho _{B}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3af257d0373ee62e5c222802f8c260ab8172c068). Therefore, the evolution of the system becomes

![Image 64: {\displaystyle \rho _{SB}(t)={\hat {U}}(t)[\rho _{S}(0)\otimes \rho _{B}(0)]{\hat {U}}^{\dagger }(t).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c836650756bfa412886604a3c49a67bd0802b10)
The system–bath interaction Hamiltonian can be written in a general form as

![Image 65: {\displaystyle {\hat {H}}_{I}=\sum _{i}{\hat {S}}_{i}\otimes {\hat {B}}_{i},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/76e497aed5897535758b5146f818a2a996104473)
where ![Image 66: {\displaystyle {\hat {S}}_{i}\otimes {\hat {B}}_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/58eda5947ab10cbfd8e98c60754683757be20dda) is the operator acting on the combined system–bath Hilbert space, and ![Image 67: {\displaystyle {\hat {S}}_{i},{\hat {B}}_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/28a8dac006b4f71cf2ae9f2a2dadc112fd6493f1) are the operators that act on the system and bath respectively. This coupling of the system and bath is the cause of decoherence in the system alone. To see this, a partial trace is performed over the bath to give a description of the system alone:

![Image 68: {\displaystyle \rho _{S}(t)=\operatorname {Tr} _{B}{\big [}{\hat {U}}(t)[\rho _{S}(0)\otimes \rho _{B}(0)]{\hat {U}}^{\dagger }(t){\big ]}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c4b62562fcef05e9dc4f661b5351d087cb46d42)
![Image 69: {\displaystyle \rho _{S}(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/339d2a92c7d76e74a1ee867964ec68805da4aae5) is called the _reduced density matrix_ and gives information about the system only. If the bath is written in terms of its set of orthogonal basis kets, that is, if it has been initially diagonalized, then ![Image 70: {\displaystyle \textstyle \rho _{B}(0)=\sum _{j}a_{j}|j\rangle \langle j|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d999a88a98530c403a52d0f0f1449f7ac1de93ed). Computing the partial trace with respect to this (computational) basis gives

![Image 71: {\displaystyle \rho _{S}(t)=\sum _{l}{\hat {A}}_{l}\rho _{S}(0){\hat {A}}_{l}^{\dagger },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2e5eb97b1190ecc232d925d57fd316c5842f8609)
where ![Image 72: {\displaystyle {\hat {A}}_{l},{\hat {A}}_{l}^{\dagger }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b371063babc55a4e3271c01ac70957bab2b292f7) are defined as the _Kraus operators_ and are represented as (the index ![Image 73: {\displaystyle l}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/829091f745070b9eb97a80244129025440a1cfac) combines indices ![Image 74: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) and ![Image 75: {\displaystyle j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f461e54f5c093e92a55547b9764291390f0b5d0)):

![Image 76: {\displaystyle {\hat {A}}_{l}={\sqrt {a_{j}}}\langle k|{\hat {U}}|j\rangle .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a3c5b6dfa435d8bae1700e1971026b7036530d0)
This is known as the _[operator-sum representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence-free\_subspaces#Operator-sum\_representation\_formulation "Decoherence-free subspaces")_ (OSR). A condition on the Kraus operators can be obtained by using the fact that ![Image 77: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Tr} [\rho _{S}(t)]=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5893f7722e29b0f4ee037cbec769adc0d8f4431); this then gives

![Image 78: {\displaystyle \sum _{l}{\hat {A}}_{l}^{\dagger }{\hat {A}}_{l}={\hat {I}}_{S}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4d251fc82f240f6c3cfdff51ec87b52d77f99ad9)
This restriction determines whether decoherence will occur or not in the OSR. In particular, when there is more than one term present in the sum for ![Image 79: {\displaystyle \rho _{S}(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/339d2a92c7d76e74a1ee867964ec68805da4aae5), then the dynamics of the system will be non-unitary, and hence decoherence will take place.

A more general consideration for the existence of decoherence in a quantum system is given by the _master equation_, which determines how the density matrix of the _system alone_ evolves in time (see also the [Belavkin equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belavkin_equation "Belavkin equation")[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-Belavkin89-32)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-Carmichael93-33)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-Bauer2012-34) for the evolution under continuous measurement). This uses the [Schrödinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_states#Schr%C3%B6dinger_picture_vs._Heisenberg_picture "Quantum states") picture, where evolution of the _state_ (represented by its density matrix) is considered. The master equation is

![Image 80: {\displaystyle \rho '_{S}(t)={\frac {-i}{\hbar }}{\big [}{\tilde {H}}_{S},\rho _{S}(t){\big ]}+L_{D}{\big [}\rho _{S}(t){\big ]},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59e25aa94db7a802148854d17986bb706abae68a)
where ![Image 81: {\displaystyle {\tilde {H}}_{S}=H_{S}+\Delta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c866e292ccfa8adc9155ed1d3a064aae074ae30e) is the system Hamiltonian ![Image 82: {\displaystyle H_{S}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99d62a9b4028878b7d67a1b7006d8db189350a20) along with a (possible) unitary contribution ![Image 83: {\displaystyle \Delta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32769037c408874e1890f77554c65f39c523ebe2) from the bath, and ![Image 84: {\displaystyle L_{D}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e064c9003e200ef4fb2b533436244aa9666c20d) is the _Lindblad decohering term_.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-Lidar_and_Whaley-2) The [Lindblad decohering term](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lindblad_equation "Lindblad equation") is represented as

![Image 85: {\displaystyle L_{D}{\big [}\rho _{S}(t){\big ]}={\frac {1}{2}}\sum _{\alpha ,\beta =1}^{M}b_{\alpha \beta }{\Big (}{\big [}\mathbf {F} _{\alpha },\rho _{S}(t)\mathbf {F} _{\beta }^{\dagger }{\big ]}+{\big [}\mathbf {F} _{\alpha }\rho _{S}(t),\mathbf {F} _{\beta }^{\dagger }{\big ]}{\Big )}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a789d5cc4655d6526f8875e94a4bccf974a10995)
The ![Image 86: {\displaystyle \{\mathbf {F} _{\alpha }\}_{\alpha =1}^{M}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a6ae2a09440282f2a69c62652bcc18d39f24740) are basis operators for the _M_-dimensional space of [bounded operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounded_operator "Bounded operator") that act on the system Hilbert space ![Image 87: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {H}}_{S}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/313e684309e1c49d4c9ed3716d7a06234c2e2a10) and are the _error generators_.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-Lidar,_Chuang,_and_Whaley-35) The matrix elements ![Image 88: {\displaystyle b_{\alpha \beta }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/70b7484be908f4c9a4d7b063c71b025125f52cb4) represent the elements of a [positive semi-definite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive_semidefinite_matrix "Positive semidefinite matrix")[Hermitian matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermitian_matrix "Hermitian matrix"); they characterize the decohering processes and, as such, are called the _noise parameters_.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-Lidar,_Chuang,_and_Whaley-35) The semigroup approach is particularly nice, because it distinguishes between the unitary and decohering (non-unitary) processes, which is not the case with the OSR. In particular, the non-unitary dynamics are represented by ![Image 89: {\displaystyle L_{D}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e064c9003e200ef4fb2b533436244aa9666c20d), whereas the unitary dynamics of the state are represented by the usual [Heisenberg commutator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heisenberg_commutator "Heisenberg commutator"). Note that when ![Image 90: {\displaystyle L_{D}{\big [}\rho _{S}(t){\big ]}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb2d71bb51b158393af6ed3f372d69d00383d8ca), the dynamical evolution of the system is unitary. The conditions for the evolution of the system density matrix to be described by the master equation are:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-Lidar_and_Whaley-2)

1.   the evolution of the system density matrix is determined by a one-parameter [semigroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semigroup "Semigroup")
2.   the evolution is "completely positive" (i.e. probabilities are preserved)
3.   the system and bath density matrices are _initially_ decoupled

Non-unitary modelling examples
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_decoherence&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Non-unitary modelling examples")]

Decoherence can be modelled as a non-[unitary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_operator "Unitary operator") process by which a system couples with its environment (although the combined system plus environment evolves in a unitary fashion).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-Lidar_and_Whaley-2) Thus the [dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamics_(physics) "Dynamics (physics)") of the system alone, treated in isolation, are non-unitary and, as such, are represented by irreversible transformations acting on the system's Hilbert space ![Image 91: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {H}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/19ef4c7b923a5125ac91aa491838a95ee15b804f). Since the system's dynamics are represented by irreversible representations, then any information present in the quantum system can be lost to the environment or [heat bath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_bath "Heat bath"). Alternatively, the decay of quantum information caused by the coupling of the system to the environment is referred to as decoherence.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-Bacon-1) Thus decoherence is the process by which information of a quantum system is altered by the system's interaction with its environment (which form a closed system), hence creating an entanglement between the system and heat bath (environment). As such, since the system is entangled with its environment in some unknown way, a description of the system by itself cannot be made without also referring to the environment (i.e. without also describing the state of the environment).

### Rotational decoherence

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_decoherence&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Rotational decoherence")]

Consider a system of _N_ qubits that is coupled to a bath symmetrically. Suppose this system of _N_ qubits undergoes a rotation around the ![Image 92: {\displaystyle |{\uparrow }\rangle \langle {\uparrow }|,|{\downarrow }\rangle \langle {\downarrow }|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cad406e1472c95da84af8449917df0e733f27128)![Image 93: {\displaystyle {\big (}|0\rangle \langle 0|,|1\rangle \langle 1|{\big )}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2531e0e5a7d6c1227fd4444e4cdc07f294163d4) eigenstates of ![Image 94: {\displaystyle {\hat {J_{z}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff6081609a8dec45a0d70b5928f46f6ff431f307). Then under such a rotation, a random [phase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_factor "Phase factor")![Image 95: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4) will be created between the eigenstates ![Image 96: {\displaystyle |0\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed066a3ad158da0ad6d6a421a606b1c8a35eb95b), ![Image 97: {\displaystyle |1\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f53021ca18e77477ee5bd3c1523e5830189ec5c) of ![Image 98: {\displaystyle {\hat {J_{z}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff6081609a8dec45a0d70b5928f46f6ff431f307). Thus these basis qubits ![Image 99: {\displaystyle |0\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed066a3ad158da0ad6d6a421a606b1c8a35eb95b) and ![Image 100: {\displaystyle |1\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f53021ca18e77477ee5bd3c1523e5830189ec5c) will transform in the following way:

![Image 101: {\displaystyle |0\rangle \to |0\rangle ,\quad |1\rangle \to e^{i\phi }|1\rangle .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/104d6831f064967c280542537dcd809bc87dbeaf)
This transformation is performed by the rotation operator

![Image 102: {\displaystyle R_{z}(\phi )={\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&e^{i\phi }\end{pmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fedbb80ea8f7cedb14e8e8c658eca5592eca71c2)
Since any qubit in this space can be expressed in terms of the basis qubits, then all such qubits will be transformed under this rotation. Consider the ![Image 103: {\displaystyle j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f461e54f5c093e92a55547b9764291390f0b5d0)th qubit in a pure state ![Image 104: {\displaystyle \vert \psi _{j}\rangle \langle \psi _{j}\vert }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/241eff0bab3cc074588f77d85ee5165df79407ae) where ![Image 105: {\displaystyle |\psi _{j}\rangle =a|0\rangle +b|1\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8283c8753c1d607625fc1cf7ebb4a6f1f36a5e96). Before application of the rotation this state is:

![Image 106: {\displaystyle \rho _{j,{\text{init}}}={\begin{pmatrix}|a|^{2}&ab^{\ast }\\a^{\ast }b&|b|^{2}\end{pmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/46c0db3f70fc39f2c1f859b80e8b009fd53cc5e5).
This state will decohere, since it is not ‘encoded’ with (dependent upon) the dephasing factor ![Image 107: {\displaystyle e^{i\phi }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/05c817052d719f5b21dd162c425e514232d14af6). This can be seen by examining the [density matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_matrix "Density matrix") averaged over the random phase ![Image 108: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4):

![Image 109: {\displaystyle \rho _{j}=\mathbb {E} [R_{z}(\phi )\vert \psi _{j}\rangle \langle \psi _{j}\vert R_{z}^{\dagger }(\phi )]=\int \limits _{-\infty }^{\infty }R_{z}(\phi )|\psi _{j}\rangle \langle \psi _{j}|R_{z}^{\dagger }(\phi )\;P({\text{d}}\phi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a42d1a5be32d5222031c9ac7f3e3f4c761cd2bb1),
where ![Image 110: {\displaystyle P(\cdot )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e50ee2585a75d92765235ba642e3c1a23f5ffd87) is a [probability measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_measure "Probability measure") of the random phase, ![Image 111: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4). Although not entirely necessary, let us assume for simplicity that this is given by the [Gaussian distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_distribution "Gaussian distribution"), _i.e._![Image 112: {\displaystyle P({\text{d}}\phi )={\frac {1}{{\sqrt {2\pi }}\sigma }}e^{-{\frac {1}{2}}({\frac {\phi }{\sigma }})^{2}}\,{\text{d}}\phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abeb2140ea23231806f37eb41fd000136ac91a21), where ![Image 113: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) represents the spread of the random phase. Then the density matrix computed as above is

![Image 114: {\displaystyle \rho _{j}={\begin{pmatrix}|a|^{2}&ab^{\ast }\,e^{-{\frac {1}{2}}\sigma ^{2}}\\a^{\ast }b\,e^{-{\frac {1}{2}}\sigma ^{2}}&|b|^{2}\end{pmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5865a90901b26d360efdf30500603fe83227e42f).
Observe that the off-diagonal elements—the coherence terms—decay as the spread of the random phase, ![Image 115: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36), increases over time (which is a realistic expectation). Thus the density matrices for each qubit of the system become indistinguishable over time. This means that no measurement can distinguish between the qubits, thus creating decoherence between the various qubit states. In particular, this dephasing process causes the qubits to collapse to one of the pure states in ![Image 116: {\displaystyle \{\vert 0\rangle \langle 0\vert ,\vert 1\rangle \langle 1\vert \}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/49eaf770b3458cb1bda3542a86f5706c43e6b69b). This is why this type of decoherence process is called **collective dephasing**, because the _mutual_ phases between _all_ qubits of the _N_-qubit system are destroyed.

**Depolarizing** is a non-unitary transformation on a quantum system which [maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map") pure states to mixed states. This is a non-unitary process because any transformation that reverses this process will map states out of their respective Hilbert space thus not preserving positivity (i.e. the original [probabilities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability "Probability") are mapped to negative probabilities, which is not allowed). The 2-dimensional case of such a transformation would consist of mapping pure states on the surface of the [Bloch sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bloch_sphere "Bloch sphere") to mixed states within the Bloch sphere. This would contract the Bloch sphere by some finite amount and the reverse process would expand the Bloch sphere, which cannot happen.

**Dissipation** is a decohering process by which the populations of quantum states are changed due to entanglement with a bath. An example of this would be a quantum system that can exchange its energy with a bath through the [interaction Hamiltonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence-free_subspaces#Operator-sum_representation_formulation "Decoherence-free subspaces"). If the system is not in its [ground state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_state "Ground state") and the bath is at a temperature lower than that of the system's, then the system will give off energy to the bath, and thus higher-energy eigenstates of the system Hamiltonian will decohere to the ground state after cooling and, as such, will all be non-[degenerate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degenerate_form "Degenerate form"). Since the states are no longer degenerate, they are not distinguishable, and thus this process is irreversible (non-unitary).

Decoherence represents an extremely fast process for macroscopic objects, since these are interacting with many microscopic objects, with an enormous number of degrees of freedom in their natural environment. The process is needed if we are to understand why we tend not to observe quantum behavior in everyday macroscopic objects and why we do see classical fields emerge from the properties of the interaction between matter and radiation for large amounts of matter. The time taken for off-diagonal components of the density matrix to effectively vanish is called the **decoherence time**. It is typically extremely short for everyday, macroscale processes.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-zurek03-26)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-zurek91-27)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-Zurek02-28) A modern basis-independent definition of the decoherence time relies on the short-time behavior of the fidelity between the initial and the time-dependent state[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-Beau2017-36) or, equivalently, the decay of the purity.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-Xu2019-37)

Experimental observations
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_decoherence&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Experimental observations")]

### Quantitative measurement

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_decoherence&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Quantitative measurement")]

The decoherence rate depends on a number of factors, including temperature or uncertainty in position, and many experiments have tried to measure it depending on the external environment.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-38)

The process of a quantum superposition gradually obliterated by decoherence was quantitatively measured for the first time by [Serge Haroche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serge_Haroche "Serge Haroche") and his co-workers at the [École Normale Supérieure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89cole_Normale_Sup%C3%A9rieure "École Normale Supérieure") in [Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris "Paris") in 1996.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-39) Their approach involved sending individual [rubidium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubidium "Rubidium") atoms, each in a superposition of two states, through a microwave-filled cavity. The two quantum states both cause shifts in the phase of the microwave field, but by different amounts, so that the field itself is also put into a superposition of two states. Due to photon scattering on cavity-mirror imperfection, the cavity field loses phase coherence to the environment. Haroche and his colleagues measured the resulting decoherence via correlations between the states of pairs of atoms sent through the cavity with various time delays between the atoms.

In July 2011, researchers from [University of British Columbia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_British_Columbia "University of British Columbia") and [University of California, Santa Barbara](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_California,_Santa_Barbara "University of California, Santa Barbara") showed that applying high magnetic fields to [single molecule magnets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single_molecule_magnets "Single molecule magnets") suppressed two of three known sources of decoherence.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-40)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-41)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-42) They were able to measure the dependence of decoherence on temperature and magnetic field strength.

[Decoherence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_computing#Decoherence "Quantum computing") causes the system to lose its quantumness, which invalidates the superposition principle and turns 'quantum' to 'classical'.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-43) It is a major challenge in quantum computing.

A real quantum system inevitably meets the surrounding environment, the interaction shows up as noise in physical process. It's extremely sensitive to environmental noise. Electromagnetic fields, temperature fluctuations, and other external perturbations, as well as measurement, lead to decoherence.

Decoherence is a challenge for the practical realization of [quantum computers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_computer "Quantum computer"), since such machines are expected to rely heavily on the undisturbed evolution of quantum coherences. They require that the coherence of states be preserved and that decoherence be managed, in order to actually perform quantum computation. Because of decoherence, it is necessary to finish the quantum process before the qubit state is decayed.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-44)

The physical quantity coherence time is defined as the time that the quantum state holds its superposition principle.

Preventing decoherence and thus extending the coherence time of quantum systems serves to improve the stability of the computation.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-45)

Researchers have developed many methods and tools to mitigate or eliminate the negative influences from decoherence. Several typical ways are listed below.

#### Isolation from Environment

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_decoherence&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: Isolation from Environment")]

The most basic and direct way to reduce decoherence is to prevent the quantum system from interacting with the environment by any type of isolation. Here are some typical examples of isolation methods.

*   [High Vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high_vacuum "Ultra-high vacuum"): Placing qubits in an ultra-high vacuum environment to minimize interaction with air molecules.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]
*   [Cryogenic Cooling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryogenic_cooling "Cryogenic cooling"): Operating quantum systems at extremely low temperatures to reduce thermal vibrations and noise.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]
*   [Electromagnetic Shielding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_shielding "Electromagnetic shielding"): Enclosing quantum systems in materials that block external electromagnetic fields - such as mu-metal or superconducting materials - reduces decoherence caused by unwanted electromagnetic interference.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]
*   Shielding [Cosmic Rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_ray "Cosmic ray"): In August 2020 scientists reported that ionizing radiation from environmental radioactive materials and cosmic rays may substantially limit the coherence times of [qubits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qubit "Qubit") if they aren't shielded adequately which may be critical for realizing fault-tolerant superconducting quantum computers in the future.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-46)[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-47)[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-48)
*   Better Materials: Fabricating qubits from special materials, like highly pure or isotopically enriched ones, to minimize intrinsic noise of the material, including noise from defects or nuclear spins.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]
*   Circuit Design: Optimizing the coherence ability when designing the construction of quantum circuits, similar to the concern in classical circuits.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]
*   Mechanical and Optical Isolation: Using equipment like vibration isolation tables and acoustic isolation materials, reducing sources of mechanical noise, and shielding against external light—common in physical experiments.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

#### Quantum Error Correction (QEC)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_decoherence&action=edit&section=26 "Edit section: Quantum Error Correction (QEC)")]

One of the most powerful tools for combating quantum decoherence is **[Quantum Error Correction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_error_correction "Quantum error correction")** (QEC). QEC schemes encode quantum information redundantly across multiple physical qubits, allowing for the detection and correction of errors without directly measuring the quantum state. These QEC protocols rely on the assumption that errors affect only a small fraction of qubits at any given time, enabling the detection and correction of errors through redundant encoding. Here are some representative QEC protocols.

*   [Shor Code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shor_code "Shor code"):[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-49) One of the first quantum error correction codes, it encodes a single qubit into nine physical qubits to protect against both bit-flip and phase-flip errors.

*   [Steane Code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steane_code "Steane code"):[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-50) A 7-qubit code that provides error correction for arbitrary errors.

*   Surface Codes:[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-51) A more scalable error correction code that uses a 2D lattice of qubits with high threshold for errors.

*   [Bosonic Codes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_error_correction#Bosonic_codes "Quantum error correction"): A type of quantum error-correcting code designed specifically to protect quantum information in continuous-variable systems.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

However, QEC comes at a significant cost: it requires a large number of physical qubits to encode a single logical qubit, and fault-tolerant error correction methods introduce additional computational overhead.

#### Dynamical Decoupling

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_decoherence&action=edit&section=27 "Edit section: Dynamical Decoupling")]

**[Dynamical Decoupling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamical_decoupling "Dynamical decoupling")** (DD) is another typical [quantum control](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_control "Quantum control") technique used against decoherence, especially for systems that are coupled to noisy environments. DD involves applying an external sequence of control pulses to the quantum system at strategically timed intervals to average out environmental interactions. This technique effectively manipulates the irreversible component of quantum systems interact with surrounding environment by the external controllable interactions.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-52) Dynamical decoupling has been experimentally demonstrated in various systems, including trapped ions[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-53) and superconducting qubits.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-54) Here are some examples of representative sequences.

*   [Spin Echo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_echo "Spin echo") (SE): SE is the consisting of a single π-pulse, which inverts the state of system.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

*   Periodic Dynamical Decoupling (PDD): Applying control pulse periodically, PDD averages out the influence of the environment and decoupling the qubit.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-55)

*   Carr-Purcell-Meiboom-Gill (CPMG) Sequence:[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decoherence#cite_note-56) CPMG is an extension of SE. It applies a series of π-pulses.
