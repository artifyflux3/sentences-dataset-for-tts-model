[Edward John Routh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_John_Routh "Edward John Routh"), 1831–1907

In classical mechanics, **Routh's procedure** or **Routhian mechanics** is a hybrid formulation of [Lagrangian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics") and [Hamiltonian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics "Hamiltonian mechanics") developed by [Edward John Routh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Routh "Edward Routh"). Correspondingly, the **Routhian** is the [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") which replaces both the [Lagrangian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics") and [Hamiltonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_function "Hamiltonian function") functions. Although Routhian mechanics is equivalent to Lagrangian mechanics and Hamiltonian mechanics, and introduces no new physics, it offers an alternative way to solve mechanical problems.

The Routhian, like the Hamiltonian, can be obtained from a [Legendre transform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legendre_transformation "Legendre transformation") of the Lagrangian, and has a similar mathematical form to the Hamiltonian, but is not exactly the same. The difference between the Lagrangian, Hamiltonian, and Routhian functions are their variables. For a given set of [generalized coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_coordinates "Generalized coordinates") representing the [degrees of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(physics_and_chemistry) "Degrees of freedom (physics and chemistry)") in the system, the Lagrangian is a function of the coordinates and velocities, while the Hamiltonian is a function of the coordinates and momenta.

The Routhian differs from these functions in that some coordinates are chosen to have corresponding generalized velocities, the rest to have corresponding generalized momenta. This choice is arbitrary, and can be done to simplify the problem. It also has the consequence that the **Routhian equations** are exactly the Hamiltonian equations for some coordinates and corresponding momenta, and the Lagrangian equations for the rest of the coordinates and their velocities. In each case the Lagrangian and Hamiltonian functions are replaced by a single function, the Routhian. The full set thus has the advantages of both sets of equations, with the convenience of splitting one set of coordinates to the Hamilton equations, and the rest to the Lagrangian equations.

In the case of Lagrangian mechanics, the [generalized coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_coordinates "Generalized coordinates")_q_ 1, _q_ 2, ... and the corresponding velocities _dq_ 1/_dt_, _dq_ 2/_dt_, ..., and possibly time[[nb 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Routhian_mechanics#cite_note-1)_t_, enter the Lagrangian,

![Image 2: {\displaystyle L(q_{1},q_{2},\ldots ,{\dot {q}}_{1},{\dot {q}}_{2},\ldots ,t)\,,\quad {\dot {q}}_{i}={\frac {dq_{i}}{dt}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/907541b88eb973b7e98bc7d37b3044397b9bdf00)
where the overdots denote [time derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_derivative "Time derivative").

In Hamiltonian mechanics, the generalized coordinates _q_ 1, _q_ 2, ... and the corresponding generalized momenta _p_ 1, _p_ 2, ..., and possibly time, enter the Hamiltonian,

![Image 3: {\displaystyle H(q_{1},q_{2},\ldots ,p_{1},p_{2},\ldots ,t)=\sum _{i}{\dot {q}}_{i}p_{i}-L(q_{1},q_{2},\ldots ,{\dot {q}}_{1}(p_{1}),{\dot {q}}_{2}(p_{2}),\ldots ,t)\,,\quad p_{i}={\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{i}}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04199f34016ff7b70ef68379e0285d47f7127a06)
where the second equation is the definition of the generalized momentum _p i_ corresponding to the coordinate _q i_ ([partial derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative "Partial derivative") are denoted using ∂). The velocities _dq i_/_dt_ are expressed as functions of their corresponding momenta by inverting their defining relation. In this context, _p i_ is said to be the momentum "canonically conjugate" to _q i_.

The Routhian is intermediate between _L_ and _H_; some coordinates _q_ 1, _q_ 2, ..., _q_ _n_ are chosen to have corresponding generalized momenta _p_ 1, _p_ 2, ..., _p_ _n_, the rest of the coordinates _ζ_ 1, _ζ_ 2, ..., _ζ s_ to have generalized velocities _dζ_ 1/_dt_, _dζ_ 2/_dt_, ..., _dζ s_/_dt_, and time may appear explicitly;[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Routhian_mechanics#cite_note-2)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Routhian_mechanics#cite_note-3)

**Routhian** (_n_ + _s_ degrees of freedom)

![Image 4: {\displaystyle R(q_{1},\ldots ,q_{n},\zeta _{1},\ldots ,\zeta _{s},p_{1},\ldots ,p_{n},{\dot {\zeta }}_{1},\ldots ,{\dot {\zeta }}_{s},t)=\sum _{i=1}^{n}p_{i}{\dot {q}}_{i}(p_{i})-L(q_{1},\ldots ,q_{n},\zeta _{1},\ldots ,\zeta _{s},{\dot {q}}_{1}(p_{1}),\ldots ,{\dot {q}}_{n}(p_{n}),{\dot {\zeta }}_{1},\ldots ,{\dot {\zeta }}_{s},t)\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/826cc6dc9e9cd9afa99437d1263cdf4379edc4ee)

where again the generalized velocity _dq_ _i_/_dt_ is to be expressed as a function of generalized momentum _p i_ via its defining relation. The choice of which _n_ coordinates are to have corresponding momenta, out of the _n_ + _s_ coordinates, is arbitrary.

The above is used by [Landau and Lifshitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Course_of_theoretical_physics "Course of theoretical physics"), and [Goldstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Mechanics_(Goldstein_book) "Classical Mechanics (Goldstein book)"). Some authors may define the Routhian to be the negative of the above definition.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Routhian_mechanics#cite_note-4)

Given the length of the general definition, a more compact notation is to use boldface for [tuples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple "Tuple") (or vectors) of the variables, thus **q** = (_q_ 1, _q_ 2, ..., _q n_), **ζ** = (_ζ_ 1, _ζ_ 2, ..., _ζ s_), **p** = (_p_ 1, _p_ 2, ..., _p n_), and _d_**ζ**/_dt_ = (_dζ_ 1/_dt_, _dζ_ 2/_dt_, ..., _dζ s_/_dt_), so that

![Image 5: {\displaystyle R(\mathbf {q} ,{\boldsymbol {\zeta }},\mathbf {p} ,{\dot {\boldsymbol {\zeta }}},t)=\mathbf {p} \cdot {\dot {\mathbf {q} }}-L(\mathbf {q} ,{\boldsymbol {\zeta }},{\dot {\mathbf {q} }},{\dot {\boldsymbol {\zeta }}},t)\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6706bdd9e602f20c24b4ebeda23b1b57f4a3117d)
where · is the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") defined on the tuples, for the specific example appearing here:

![Image 6: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} \cdot {\dot {\mathbf {q} }}=\sum _{i=1}^{n}p_{i}{\dot {q}}_{i}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ad6c381b961830780a2d9c5512de06fc242d795)

Equations of motion
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Routhian_mechanics&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Equations of motion")]

For reference, the [Euler-Lagrange equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler-Lagrange "Euler-Lagrange") for _s_ degrees of freedom are a set of _s_ coupled second order [ordinary differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinary_differential_equation "Ordinary differential equation") in the coordinates

![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{j}}}={\frac {\partial L}{\partial q_{j}}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5254612758f62cf3680780e74498214eb8a4abcb)
where _j_ = 1, 2, ..., _s_, and the [Hamiltonian equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics "Hamiltonian mechanics") for _n_ degrees of freedom are a set of 2 _n_ coupled first order ordinary differential equations in the coordinates and momenta

![Image 8: {\displaystyle {\dot {q}}_{i}={\frac {\partial H}{\partial p_{i}}}\,,\quad {\dot {p}}_{i}=-{\frac {\partial H}{\partial q_{i}}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8571e5bf501eed2cf9a11120cb91314c6c2e0497)
Below, the Routhian equations of motion are obtained in two ways, in the process other useful derivatives are found that can be used elsewhere.

### Two degrees of freedom

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Routhian_mechanics&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Two degrees of freedom")]

Consider the case of a system with two [degrees of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(physics_and_chemistry) "Degrees of freedom (physics and chemistry)"), _q_ and _ζ_, with generalized velocities _dq_/_dt_ and _dζ_/_dt_, and the Lagrangian is time-dependent. (The generalization to any number of degrees of freedom follows exactly the same procedure as with two).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Routhian_mechanics#cite_note-5) The Lagrangian of the system will have the form

![Image 9: {\displaystyle L(q,\zeta ,{\dot {q}},{\dot {\zeta }},t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16ca0e09849ad97034cce9c2d21605b51575bae4)
The [differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_of_a_function "Differential of a function") of _L_ is

![Image 10: {\displaystyle dL={\frac {\partial L}{\partial q}}dq+{\frac {\partial L}{\partial \zeta }}d\zeta +{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {q}}}}d{\dot {q}}+{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {\zeta }}}}d{\dot {\zeta }}+{\frac {\partial L}{\partial t}}dt\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/316b7e1e1c9076ae9ce153f47bb905e529de3527)
Now change variables, from the set (_q_, _ζ_, _dq_/_dt_, _dζ_/_dt_) to (_q_, _ζ_, _p_, _dζ_/_dt_), simply switching the velocity _dq_/_dt_ to the momentum _p_. This change of variables in the differentials is the [Legendre transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legendre_transformation "Legendre transformation"). The differential of the new function to replace _L_ will be a sum of differentials in _dq_, _dζ_, _dp_, _d_(_dζ_/_dt_), and _dt_. Using the definition of generalized momentum and Lagrange's equation for the coordinate _q_:

![Image 11: {\displaystyle p={\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {q}}}}\,,\quad {\dot {p}}={\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {q}}}}={\frac {\partial L}{\partial q}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4e186e2cfb7dac14d40ca6f59f85bb8fdcf289b3)
we have

![Image 12: {\displaystyle dL={\dot {p}}dq+{\frac {\partial L}{\partial \zeta }}d\zeta +pd{\dot {q}}+{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {\zeta }}}}d{\dot {\zeta }}+{\frac {\partial L}{\partial t}}dt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41389ce05991ae3341c051ed14128f4529dbf96f)
and to replace _pd_(_dq_/_dt_) by (_dq_/_dt_)_dp_, recall the [product rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_rule "Product rule") for differentials,[[nb 2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Routhian_mechanics#cite_note-6) and substitute

![Image 13: {\displaystyle pd{\dot {q}}=d({\dot {q}}p)-{\dot {q}}dp}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8646472c5bfd8b79210a48aa3fe2732e92d2091a)
to obtain the differential of a new function in terms of the new set of variables:

![Image 14: {\displaystyle d(L-p{\dot {q}})={\dot {p}}dq+{\frac {\partial L}{\partial \zeta }}d\zeta -{\dot {q}}dp+{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {\zeta }}}}d{\dot {\zeta }}+{\frac {\partial L}{\partial t}}dt\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e53422c682dac656a5217fe8a503a6d2bb58deef)
Introducing the Routhian

![Image 15: {\displaystyle R(q,\zeta ,p,{\dot {\zeta }},t)=p{\dot {q}}(p)-L}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0547ac2939dc76ab60ce1d3bf4329773b5423cbd)
where again the velocity _dq_/_dt_ is a function of the momentum _p_, we have

![Image 16: {\displaystyle dR=-{\dot {p}}dq-{\frac {\partial L}{\partial \zeta }}d\zeta +{\dot {q}}dp-{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {\zeta }}}}d{\dot {\zeta }}-{\frac {\partial L}{\partial t}}dt\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f320d45789c088871058ec37dc1d2c88c757d78e)
but from the above definition, the differential of the Routhian is

![Image 17: {\displaystyle dR={\frac {\partial R}{\partial q}}dq+{\frac {\partial R}{\partial \zeta }}d\zeta +{\frac {\partial R}{\partial p}}dp+{\frac {\partial R}{\partial {\dot {\zeta }}}}d{\dot {\zeta }}+{\frac {\partial R}{\partial t}}dt\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f30be167bbf8b0f75e2a6befba7ba031f713f87)
Comparing the coefficients of the differentials _dq_, _dζ_, _dp_, _d_(_dζ_/_dt_), and _dt_, the results are [Hamilton's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics "Hamiltonian mechanics") for the coordinate _q_,

![Image 18: {\displaystyle {\dot {q}}={\frac {\partial R}{\partial p}}\,,\quad {\dot {p}}=-{\frac {\partial R}{\partial q}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e9d56f67b9dd9db24d43025710bf6cc1ad19eaa)
and [Lagrange's equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange%27s_equation "Lagrange's equation") for the coordinate _ζ_

![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial R}{\partial {\dot {\zeta }}}}={\frac {\partial R}{\partial \zeta }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/feee4ebbd9a7279bb068af7d040c29a9dcab8630)
which follow from

![Image 20: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial L}{\partial \zeta }}=-{\frac {\partial R}{\partial \zeta }}\,,\quad {\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {\zeta }}}}=-{\frac {\partial R}{\partial {\dot {\zeta }}}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a2908e69bde5cfc1b91d4c9e182a1c11ebabc2ec)
and taking the total time derivative of the second equation and equating to the first. Notice the Routhian replaces the Hamiltonian and Lagrangian functions in all the equations of motion.

The remaining equation states the partial time derivatives of _L_ and _R_ are negatives

![Image 21: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial L}{\partial t}}=-{\frac {\partial R}{\partial t}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/30a957ac4e9fe803c6e43c838016a1630196cf56)

### Any number of degrees of freedom

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Routhian_mechanics&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Any number of degrees of freedom")]

For _n_ + _s_ coordinates as defined above, with Routhian

![Image 22: {\displaystyle R(q_{1},\ldots ,q_{n},\zeta _{1},\ldots ,\zeta _{s},p_{1},\ldots ,p_{n},{\dot {\zeta }}_{1},\ldots ,{\dot {\zeta }}_{s},t)=\sum _{i=1}^{n}p_{i}{\dot {q}}_{i}(p_{i})-L}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a4e6b3a0d7e6ccc1dccabf3befb8635c1c353778)
the equations of motion can be derived by a Legendre transformation of this Routhian as in the previous section, but another way is to simply take the partial derivatives of _R_ with respect to the coordinates _q i_ and _ζ j_, momenta _p i_, and velocities _dζ j_/_dt_, where _i_ = 1, 2, ..., _n_, and _j_ = 1, 2, ..., _s_. The derivatives are

![Image 23: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial R}{\partial q_{i}}}=-{\frac {\partial L}{\partial q_{i}}}=-{\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{i}}}=-{\dot {p}}_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75dc2e381a7d6b1bcf4d8e3e7f50e5d307288893)![Image 24: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial R}{\partial p_{i}}}={\dot {q}}_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99a54fc5f810ad62eb8dae9bf79fa08d5fb7dfdf)![Image 25: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial R}{\partial \zeta _{j}}}=-{\frac {\partial L}{\partial \zeta _{j}}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aaf36c791e500726afb7481a49e7b292838d82ab)![Image 26: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial R}{\partial {\dot {\zeta }}_{j}}}=-{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {\zeta }}_{j}}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b92c0c8bac33746ebf0d4b0b429502ef6ad10b18)![Image 27: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial R}{\partial t}}=-{\frac {\partial L}{\partial t}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dc2513c88a3501cf3e3fa36f9c6bfe258526fa1f)
The first two are identically the Hamiltonian equations. Equating the total time derivative of the fourth set of equations with the third (for each value of _j_) gives the Lagrangian equations. The fifth is just the same relation between time partial derivatives as before. To summarize[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Routhian_mechanics#cite_note-7)

**Routhian equations of motion** (_n_ + _s_ degrees of freedom)

![Image 28: {\displaystyle {\dot {q}}_{i}={\frac {\partial R}{\partial p_{i}}}\,,\quad {\dot {p}}_{i}=-{\frac {\partial R}{\partial q_{i}}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f22766de542c25caf14ebc205fbecd2a2cba841c)

![Image 29: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial R}{\partial {\dot {\zeta }}_{j}}}={\frac {\partial R}{\partial \zeta _{j}}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5dd454cb8eb137d14e358cc2db542d193fdb5ce9)

The total number of equations is 2 _n_ + _s_, there are 2 _n_ Hamiltonian equations plus _s_ Lagrange equations.

Since the Lagrangian has the same units as [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy"), the units of the Routhian are also energy. In [SI units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_units "SI units") this is the [Joule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joule "Joule").

Taking the total time derivative of the Lagrangian leads to the general result

![Image 30: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial L}{\partial t}}={\frac {d}{dt}}\left(\sum _{i=1}^{n}{\dot {q}}_{i}{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{i}}}+\sum _{j=1}^{s}{\dot {\zeta }}_{j}{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {\zeta }}_{j}}}-L\right)\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c37dd65dca697cd5c6f9d929e4b0521c2d6303c2)
If the Lagrangian is independent of time, the partial time derivative of the Lagrangian is zero, ∂_L_/∂_t_ = 0, so the quantity under the total time derivative in brackets must be a constant, it is the total energy of the system[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Routhian_mechanics#cite_note-8)

![Image 31: {\displaystyle E=\sum _{i=1}^{n}{\dot {q}}_{i}{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{i}}}+\sum _{j=1}^{s}{\dot {\zeta }}_{j}{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {\zeta }}_{j}}}-L\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/637958a53f9a7c2341ad7a8f832b7e6b04f47919)
(If there are external fields interacting with the constituents of the system, they can vary throughout space but not time). This expression requires the partial derivatives of _L_ with respect to _all_ the velocities _dq i_/_dt_ and _dζ j_/_dt_. Under the same condition of _R_ being time independent, the energy in terms of the Routhian is a little simpler, substituting the definition of _R_ and the partial derivatives of _R_ with respect to the velocities _dζ j_/_dt_,

![Image 32: {\displaystyle E=R-\sum _{j=1}^{s}{\dot {\zeta }}_{j}{\frac {\partial R}{\partial {\dot {\zeta }}_{j}}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1fc4c9ee3462238fa2f880ef10a7bd06720a8985)
Notice only the partial derivatives of _R_ with respect to the velocities _dζ j_/_dt_ are needed. In the case that _s_ = 0 and the Routhian is explicitly time-independent, then _E_ = _R_, that is, the Routhian equals the energy of the system. The same expression for _R_ in when _s_ = 0 is also the Hamiltonian, so in all _E_ = _R_ = _H_.

If the Routhian has explicit time dependence, the total energy of the system is not constant. The general result is

![Image 33: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial R}{\partial t}}={\dfrac {d}{dt}}\left(R-\sum _{j=1}^{s}{\dot {\zeta }}_{j}{\frac {\partial R}{\partial {\dot {\zeta }}_{j}}}\right)\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c26d788b660c8a1b36b8385f9689b45be5618b03)
which can be derived from the total time derivative of _R_ in the same way as for _L_.

Often the Routhian approach may offer no advantage, but one notable case where this is useful is when a system has [cyclic coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_coordinates "Cyclic coordinates") (also called "ignorable coordinates"), by definition those coordinates which do not appear in the original Lagrangian. The Lagrangian equations are powerful results, used frequently in theory and practice, since the equations of motion in the coordinates are easy to set up. However, if cyclic coordinates occur there will still be equations to solve for all the coordinates, including the cyclic coordinates despite their absence in the Lagrangian. The Hamiltonian equations are useful theoretical results, but less useful in practice because coordinates and momenta are related together in the solutions - after solving the equations the coordinates and momenta must be eliminated from each other. Nevertheless, the Hamiltonian equations are perfectly suited to cyclic coordinates because the equations in the cyclic coordinates trivially vanish, leaving only the equations in the non cyclic coordinates.

The Routhian approach has the best of both approaches, because cyclic coordinates can be split off to the Hamiltonian equations and eliminated, leaving behind the non cyclic coordinates to be solved from the Lagrangian equations. Overall fewer equations need to be solved compared to the Lagrangian approach.

The Routhian formulation is useful for systems with [cyclic coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_coordinates "Cyclic coordinates"), because by definition those coordinates do not enter _L_, and hence _R_. The corresponding partial derivatives of _L_ and _R_ with respect to those coordinates are zero, which equates to the corresponding generalized momenta reducing to constants. To make this concrete, if the _q i_ are all cyclic coordinates, and the _ζ j_ are all non cyclic, then

![Image 34: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial L}{\partial q_{i}}}={\dot {p}}_{i}=-{\frac {\partial R}{\partial q_{i}}}=0\quad \Rightarrow \quad p_{i}=\alpha _{i}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f2357bde232858234ccada83b51faa8b8bc0eed9)
where the _α i_ are constants. With these constants substituted into the Routhian, _R_ is a function of only the non cyclic coordinates and velocities (and in general time also)

![Image 35: {\displaystyle R(\zeta _{1},\ldots ,\zeta _{s},\alpha _{1},\ldots ,\alpha _{n},{\dot {\zeta }}_{1},\ldots ,{\dot {\zeta }}_{s},t)=\sum _{i=1}^{n}\alpha _{i}{\dot {q}}_{i}(\alpha _{i})-L(\zeta _{1},\ldots ,\zeta _{s},{\dot {q}}_{1}(\alpha _{1}),\ldots ,{\dot {q}}_{n}(\alpha _{n}),{\dot {\zeta }}_{1},\ldots ,{\dot {\zeta }}_{s},t)\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e9ee9677ab98020437d9c7fd16872588756f9214)
The 2 _n_ Hamiltonian equation in the cyclic coordinates automatically vanishes,

![Image 36: {\displaystyle {\dot {q}}_{i}={\frac {\partial R}{\partial \alpha _{i}}}=f_{i}(\zeta _{1}(t),\ldots ,\zeta _{s}(t),{\dot {\zeta }}_{1}(t),\ldots ,{\dot {\zeta }}_{s}(t),\alpha _{1},\ldots ,\alpha _{n},t)\,,\quad {\dot {p}}_{i}=-{\frac {\partial R}{\partial q_{i}}}=0\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27d808ab7a22d671e01d9155bab720e8d24a7da8)
and the _s_ Lagrangian equations are in the non cyclic coordinates

![Image 37: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial R}{\partial {\dot {\zeta }}_{j}}}={\frac {\partial R}{\partial \zeta _{j}}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5dd454cb8eb137d14e358cc2db542d193fdb5ce9)
Thus the problem has been reduced to solving the Lagrangian equations in the non cyclic coordinates, with the advantage of the Hamiltonian equations cleanly removing the cyclic coordinates. Using those solutions, the equations for ![Image 38: {\displaystyle {\dot {q}}_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa92ee5d3f74b0d1a40f53393bfec80758d3c2d3)can be integrated to compute ![Image 39: {\displaystyle q_{i}(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23a92e30dd438adbdca7b4774ef4b285b411dd64).

If we are interested in how the cyclic coordinates change with time, the equations for the generalized velocities corresponding to the cyclic coordinates can be integrated.

Routh's procedure does not guarantee the equations of motion will be simple, however it will lead to fewer equations.

### Central potential in spherical coordinates

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Routhian_mechanics&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Central potential in spherical coordinates")]

One general class of mechanical systems with cyclic coordinates are those with [central potentials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_potential "Central potential"), because potentials of this form only have dependence on radial separations and no dependence on angles.

Consider a particle of mass _m_ under the influence of a central potential _V_(_r_) in [spherical polar coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_polar_coordinates "Spherical polar coordinates")(_r_, _θ_, _φ_)

![Image 40: {\displaystyle L(r,{\dot {r}},\theta ,{\dot {\theta }},{\dot {\phi }})={\frac {m}{2}}({\dot {r}}^{2}+{r}^{2}{\dot {\theta }}^{2}+r^{2}\sin ^{2}\theta {\dot {\varphi }}^{2})-V(r)\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d46b53d9f1b05feb8124d22218dd00860bf07d64)
Notice _φ_ is cyclic, because it does not appear in the Lagrangian. The momentum conjugate to _φ_ is the constant

![Image 41: {\displaystyle p_{\phi }={\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {\phi }}}}=mr^{2}\sin ^{2}\theta {\dot {\phi }}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e1955a594b335488a7bc984b3baf5cbc1e109aef)
in which _r_ and _dφ_/_dt_ can vary with time, but the angular momentum _p φ_ is constant. The Routhian can be taken to be

![Image 42: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}R(r,{\dot {r}},\theta ,{\dot {\theta }})&=p_{\phi }{\dot {\phi }}-L\\&=p_{\phi }{\dot {\phi }}-{\frac {m}{2}}{\dot {r}}^{2}-{\frac {m}{2}}r^{2}{\dot {\theta }}^{2}-{\frac {p_{\phi }{\dot {\phi }}}{2}}+V(r)\\&={\frac {p_{\phi }{\dot {\phi }}}{2}}-{\frac {m}{2}}{\dot {r}}^{2}-{\frac {m}{2}}r^{2}{\dot {\theta }}^{2}+V(r)\\&={\frac {p_{\phi }^{2}}{2mr^{2}\sin ^{2}\theta }}-{\frac {m}{2}}{\dot {r}}^{2}-{\frac {m}{2}}r^{2}{\dot {\theta }}^{2}+V(r)\,.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bc5771b0cd4528a2fcc496fab25d0cb22a8e978a)
We can solve for _r_ and _θ_ using Lagrange's equations, and do not need to solve for _φ_ since it is eliminated by Hamiltonian's equations. The _r_ equation is

![Image 43: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial R}{\partial {\dot {r}}}}={\frac {\partial R}{\partial r}}\quad \Rightarrow \quad -m{\ddot {r}}=-{\frac {p_{\phi }^{2}}{mr^{3}\sin ^{2}\theta }}-mr{\dot {\theta }}^{2}+{\frac {\partial V}{\partial r}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7df309102290d0906afdcf9d1313a492fc3fbcf9)
and the _θ_ equation is

![Image 44: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial R}{\partial {\dot {\theta }}}}={\frac {\partial R}{\partial \theta }}\quad \Rightarrow \quad -m(2r{\dot {r}}{\dot {\theta }}+r^{2}{\ddot {\theta }})=-{\frac {p_{\phi }^{2}\cos \theta }{mr^{2}\sin ^{3}\theta }}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/647b9b50954a6e00f8ef4f7d9704e0eeed461dc7)
The Routhian approach has obtained two coupled nonlinear equations. By contrast the Lagrangian approach leads to _three_ nonlinear coupled equations, mixing in the first and second time derivatives of _φ_ in all of them, despite its absence from the Lagrangian.

The _r_ equation is

![Image 45: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {r}}}}={\frac {\partial L}{\partial r}}\quad \Rightarrow \quad m{\ddot {r}}=mr{\dot {\theta }}^{2}+mr\sin ^{2}\theta {\dot {\phi }}^{2}-{\frac {\partial V}{\partial r}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c4f270081cbd9359832778f67b543e54e60bee4)
the _θ_ equation is

![Image 46: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {\theta }}}}={\frac {\partial L}{\partial \theta }}\quad \Rightarrow \quad 2r{\dot {r}}{\dot {\theta }}+r^{2}{\ddot {\theta }}=r^{2}\sin \theta \cos \theta {\dot {\phi }}^{2}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9921f9a5dc8df737e196c5a48f450eb5d8da0ffa)
the _φ_ equation is

![Image 47: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {\phi }}}}={\frac {\partial L}{\partial \phi }}\quad \Rightarrow \quad 2r{\dot {r}}\sin ^{2}\theta {\dot {\phi }}+2r^{2}\sin \theta \cos \theta {\dot {\theta }}{\dot {\phi }}+r^{2}\sin ^{2}\theta {\ddot {\phi }}=0\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdaa9d85852babee44e236f57615c6f01c5c965f)

### Symmetric mechanical systems

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Routhian_mechanics&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Symmetric mechanical systems")]

[![Image 48](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Spherical_pendulum_Lagrangian_mechanics.svg/330px-Spherical_pendulum_Lagrangian_mechanics.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Spherical_pendulum_Lagrangian_mechanics.svg)

Spherical pendulum: angles and velocities

Consider the [spherical pendulum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_pendulum "Spherical pendulum"), a mass _m_ (known as a "pendulum bob") attached to a rigid rod of length _l_ of negligible mass, subject to a local gravitational field _g_. The system rotates with angular velocity _dφ_/_dt_ which is _not_ constant. The angle between the rod and vertical is _θ_ and is _not_ constant.

The Lagrangian is[[nb 3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Routhian_mechanics#cite_note-9)

![Image 49: {\displaystyle L(\theta ,{\dot {\theta }},{\dot {\phi }})={\frac {m\ell ^{2}}{2}}({\dot {\theta }}^{2}+\sin ^{2}\theta {\dot {\phi }}^{2})+mg\ell \cos \theta \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6c323bd516eef9f239e191ea38c0429085c4e4a)
and _φ_ is the cyclic coordinate for the system with constant momentum

![Image 50: {\displaystyle p_{\phi }={\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {\phi }}}}=m\ell ^{2}\sin ^{2}\theta {\dot {\phi }}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a3ae40f64afbfd0ff860efc118e4176a8b7f9f0)
which again is physically the angular momentum of the system about the vertical. The angle _θ_ and angular velocity _dφ_/_dt_ vary with time, but the angular momentum is constant. The Routhian is

![Image 51: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}R(\theta ,{\dot {\theta }})&=p_{\phi }{\dot {\phi }}-L\\&=p_{\phi }{\dot {\phi }}-{\frac {m\ell ^{2}}{2}}{\dot {\theta }}^{2}-{\frac {p_{\phi }{\dot {\phi }}}{2}}-mg\ell \cos \theta \\&={\frac {p_{\phi }{\dot {\phi }}}{2}}-{\frac {m\ell ^{2}}{2}}{\dot {\theta }}^{2}-mg\ell \cos \theta \\&={\frac {p_{\phi }^{2}}{2m\ell ^{2}\sin ^{2}\theta }}-{\frac {m\ell ^{2}}{2}}{\dot {\theta }}^{2}-mg\ell \cos \theta \end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e32da883cfb40ff11af4ae236c1f773e901b7aa7)
The _θ_ equation is found from the Lagrangian equations

![Image 52: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial R}{\partial {\dot {\theta }}}}={\frac {\partial R}{\partial \theta }}\quad \Rightarrow \quad -m\ell ^{2}{\ddot {\theta }}=-{\frac {p_{\phi }^{2}\cos \theta }{m\ell ^{2}\sin ^{3}\theta }}+mg\ell \sin \theta \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c674cd0394bfab60679382306191246d9eccf74)
or simplifying by introducing the constants

![Image 53: {\displaystyle a={\frac {p_{\phi }^{2}}{m^{2}\ell ^{4}}}\,,\quad b={\frac {g}{\ell }}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/adb14d03f4efa822a66934f220536f87a0d99834)
gives

![Image 54: {\displaystyle {\ddot {\theta }}=a{\frac {\cos \theta }{\sin ^{3}\theta }}-b\sin \theta \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b6b3f6b6435608ff9012f580f3c076747a60b9c)
This equation resembles the simple nonlinear [pendulum equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_equation "Pendulum equation"), because it can swing through the vertical axis, with an additional term to account for the rotation about the vertical axis (the constant _a_ is related to the angular momentum _p φ_).

Applying the Lagrangian approach there are two nonlinear coupled equations to solve.

The _θ_ equation is

![Image 55: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {\theta }}}}={\frac {\partial L}{\partial \theta }}\quad \Rightarrow \quad m\ell ^{2}{\ddot {\theta }}=m\ell ^{2}\sin \theta \cos \theta {\dot {\phi }}^{2}-mg\ell \sin \theta \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f1b02008cb8fef469c059bf0119e2f4604f6f6c)
and the _φ_ equation is

![Image 56: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {\phi }}}}={\frac {\partial L}{\partial \phi }}\quad \Rightarrow \quad 2\sin \theta \cos \theta {\dot {\theta }}{\dot {\phi }}+\sin ^{2}\theta {\ddot {\phi }}=0\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/15b20f1c958d7ac7d355a98721e655d3c7ba751a)

#### Heavy symmetrical top

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Routhian_mechanics&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Heavy symmetrical top")]

[![Image 57](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7d/Heavy_symmetric_top_euler_angles.svg/250px-Heavy_symmetric_top_euler_angles.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Heavy_symmetric_top_euler_angles.svg)

Heavy symmetric top in terms of the Euler angles

The heavy [symmetrical top](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetrical_top "Symmetrical top") of mass _M_ has Lagrangian[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Routhian_mechanics#cite_note-10)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Routhian_mechanics#cite_note-11)

![Image 58: {\displaystyle L(\theta ,{\dot {\theta }},{\dot {\psi }},{\dot {\phi }})={\frac {I_{1}}{2}}({\dot {\theta }}^{2}+{\dot {\phi }}^{2}\sin ^{2}\theta )+{\frac {I_{3}}{2}}({\dot {\psi }}^{2}+{\dot {\phi }}^{2}\cos ^{2}\theta )+I_{3}{\dot {\psi }}{\dot {\phi }}\cos \theta -Mg\ell \cos \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8ba607fdff0e711b7686e4906f4de1bc8604e1e5)
where _ψ_, _φ_, _θ_ are the [Euler angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_angles "Euler angles"), _θ_ is the angle between the vertical _z_-axis and the top's _z_′-axis, _ψ_ is the rotation of the top about its own _z_′-axis, and _φ_ the azimuthal of the top's _z_′-axis around the vertical _z_-axis. The principal [moments of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moments_of_inertia "Moments of inertia") are _I_ 1 about the top's own _x_′ axis, _I_ 2 about the top's own _y_′ axes, and _I_ 3 about the top's own _z_′-axis. Since the top is symmetric about its _z_′-axis, _I_ 1 = _I_ 2. Here the simple relation for local [gravitational potential energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_potential_energy "Gravitational potential energy")_V_ = _Mgl_ cos _θ_ is used where _g_ is the acceleration due to gravity, and the centre of mass of the top is a distance _l_ from its tip along its _z_′-axis.

The angles _ψ_, _φ_ are cyclic. The constant momenta are the angular momenta of the top about its axis and its precession about the vertical, respectively:

![Image 59: {\displaystyle p_{\psi }={\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {\psi }}}}=I_{3}{\dot {\psi }}+I_{3}{\dot {\phi }}\cos \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2aa546b2bd882aebf3ab50da29952efbbeb265de)![Image 60: {\displaystyle p_{\phi }={\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {\phi }}}}={\dot {\phi }}(I_{1}\sin ^{2}\theta +I_{3}\cos ^{2}\theta )+I_{3}{\dot {\psi }}\cos \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a52a6d22eed4f5db9d9b21f98c0d93fe9485cb7)
From these, eliminating _dψ_/_dt_:

![Image 61: {\displaystyle p_{\phi }-p_{\psi }\cos \theta =I_{1}{\dot {\phi }}\sin ^{2}\theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb8316a420b2a1d4878ca1efbdb5a17baad24f8f)
we have

![Image 62: {\displaystyle {\dot {\phi }}={\frac {p_{\phi }-p_{\psi }\cos \theta }{I_{1}\sin ^{2}\theta }}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3c86d79dbb8582022dd1ae7c1dfbf3e33aff193)
and to eliminate _dφ_/_dt_, substitute this result into _p ψ_ and solve for _dψ_/_dt_ to find

![Image 63: {\displaystyle {\dot {\psi }}={\frac {p_{\psi }}{I_{3}}}-\cos \theta \left({\frac {p_{\phi }-p_{\psi }\cos \theta }{I_{1}\sin ^{2}\theta }}\right)\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a401ce9fb9ee32706b3635f3545c184b164c2ec)
The Routhian can be taken to be

![Image 64: {\displaystyle R(\theta ,{\dot {\theta }})=p_{\psi }{\dot {\psi }}+p_{\phi }{\dot {\phi }}-L={\frac {1}{2}}(p_{\psi }{\dot {\psi }}+p_{\phi }{\dot {\phi }})-{\frac {I_{1}{\dot {\theta }}^{2}}{2}}+Mg\ell \cos \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b1dced310c375aaf5f14be656a084844aa0c33b)
and since

![Image 65: {\displaystyle {\frac {p_{\phi }{\dot {\phi }}}{2}}={\frac {p_{\phi }^{2}}{2I_{1}\sin ^{2}\theta }}-{\frac {p_{\psi }p_{\phi }\cos \theta }{2I_{1}\sin ^{2}\theta }}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ef254d3743be4dd561bbb918b2005740931f38f)![Image 66: {\displaystyle {\frac {p_{\psi }{\dot {\psi }}}{2}}={\frac {p_{\psi }^{2}}{2I_{3}}}-{\frac {p_{\psi }p_{\phi }\cos \theta }{2I_{1}\sin ^{2}\theta }}+{\frac {p_{\psi }^{2}\cos ^{2}\theta }{2I_{1}\sin ^{2}\theta }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7aa2ec8bf202d74dfc593c63c2c52556af958fbe)
we have

![Image 67: {\displaystyle R={\frac {p_{\psi }^{2}}{2I_{3}}}+{\frac {p_{\psi }^{2}\cos ^{2}\theta }{2I_{1}\sin ^{2}\theta }}+{\frac {p_{\phi }^{2}}{2I_{1}\sin ^{2}\theta }}-{\frac {p_{\psi }p_{\phi }\cos \theta }{I_{1}\sin ^{2}\theta }}-{\frac {I_{1}{\dot {\theta }}^{2}}{2}}+Mg\ell \cos \theta \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1aaad5ac6e739634b28c75b94f43d59b8700ea7b)
The first term is constant, and can be ignored since only the derivatives of _R_ will enter the equations of motion. The simplified Routhian, without loss of information, is thus

![Image 68: {\displaystyle R={\frac {1}{2I_{1}\sin ^{2}\theta }}\left[p_{\psi }^{2}\cos ^{2}\theta +p_{\phi }^{2}-2p_{\psi }p_{\phi }\cos \theta \right]-{\frac {I_{1}{\dot {\theta }}^{2}}{2}}+Mg\ell \cos \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24e20ce1ba84cb0d2c03b62b9d85dc8d2fd30fdf)
The equation of motion for _θ_ is, by direct calculation,

![Image 69: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial R}{\partial {\dot {\theta }}}}={\frac {\partial R}{\partial \theta }}\quad \Rightarrow \quad }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/120249f68a5447397c870f45f26326ab73ca2178)![Image 70: {\displaystyle -I_{1}{\ddot {\theta }}=-{\frac {\cos \theta }{I_{1}\sin ^{3}\theta }}\left[p_{\psi }^{2}\cos ^{2}\theta +p_{\phi }^{2}-{\frac {p_{\psi }p_{\phi }}{2}}\cos \theta \right]+{\frac {1}{2I_{1}\sin ^{2}\theta }}\left[-2p_{\psi }^{2}\cos \theta \sin \theta +{\frac {p_{\psi }p_{\phi }}{2}}\sin \theta \right]-Mg\ell \sin \theta \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ef7a28f94f643d30b72e14cef78d4fc632656b)
or by introducing the constants

![Image 71: {\displaystyle a={\frac {p_{\psi }^{2}}{I_{1}^{2}}}\,,\quad b={\frac {p_{\phi }^{2}}{I_{1}^{2}}}\,,\quad c={\frac {p_{\psi }p_{\phi }}{2I_{1}^{2}}}\,,\quad k={\frac {Mg\ell }{I_{1}}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/12e135ab1d5071eb1dcf253e3096939dccf60f2e)
a simpler form of the equation is obtained

![Image 72: {\displaystyle {\ddot {\theta }}={\frac {\cos \theta }{\sin ^{3}\theta }}(a\cos ^{2}\theta +b-c\cos \theta )+{\frac {1}{2\sin \theta }}(2a\cos \theta -c)+k\sin \theta \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0cb3f2b1b959a9f4141611fa73e88b722d4c4a60)
Although the equation is highly nonlinear, there is only one equation to solve for, it was obtained directly, and the cyclic coordinates are not involved.

By contrast, the Lagrangian approach leads to _three_ nonlinear coupled equations to solve, despite the absence of the coordinates _ψ_ and _φ_ in the Lagrangian.

The _θ_ equation is

![Image 73: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {\theta }}}}={\frac {\partial L}{\partial \theta }}\quad \Rightarrow \quad I_{1}{\ddot {\theta }}=(I_{1}-I_{3}){\dot {\phi }}^{2}\sin \theta \cos \theta -I_{3}{\dot {\psi }}{\dot {\phi }}\sin \theta +Mg\ell \sin \theta \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5f5e293687f775a80963d3eee91b49b58081b695)

### Velocity-dependent potentials

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Routhian_mechanics&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Velocity-dependent potentials")]

#### Classical charged particle in a uniform magnetic field

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Routhian_mechanics&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Classical charged particle in a uniform magnetic field")]

[![Image 76](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/Charged_particle_in_uniform_B_field.svg/330px-Charged_particle_in_uniform_B_field.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Charged_particle_in_uniform_B_field.svg)

Classical charged particle in uniform **B** field, using cylindrical coordinates. **Top:** If the radial coordinate _r_ and angular velocity _dθ_/_dt_ vary, the trajectory is a helicoid with varying radius but uniform motion in the _z_ direction. **Bottom:** Constant _r_ and _dθ_/_dt_ means a helicoid with constant radius.

Consider a classical [charged particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charged_particle "Charged particle") of mass _m_ and [electric charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge")_q_ in a static (time-independent) uniform (constant throughout space) [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field")**B**.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Routhian_mechanics#cite_note-12) The Lagrangian for a charged particle in a general [electromagnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_field "Electromagnetic field") given by the [magnetic potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_vector_potential "Magnetic vector potential")**A** and [electric potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_potential "Electric potential")![Image 77: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4) is

![Image 78: {\displaystyle L={\frac {m}{2}}{\dot {\mathbf {r} }}^{2}-q\phi +q{\dot {\mathbf {r} }}\cdot \mathbf {A} \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f727f9d5571dcb9b3175d05624bcb43698eba48)
It is convenient to use [cylindrical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylindrical_coordinates "Cylindrical coordinates")(_r_, _θ_, _z_), so that

![Image 79: {\displaystyle {\dot {\mathbf {r} }}=\mathbf {v} =(v_{r},v_{\theta },v_{z})=({\dot {r}},r{\dot {\theta }},{\dot {z}})\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d2b8b9d0d3d0e52c25a3a6af135f01fa6d6c9bc)![Image 80: {\displaystyle \mathbf {B} =(B_{r},B_{\theta },B_{z})=(0,0,B)\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72f68b170f10eed5b5514ba1ad2e72a96d423235)
In this case of no electric field, the electric potential is zero, ![Image 81: {\displaystyle \phi =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2c96cdab103e6c884877c86d6e5db6e471a167d5), and we can choose the axial gauge for the magnetic potential

![Image 82: {\displaystyle \mathbf {A} ={\frac {1}{2}}\mathbf {B} \times \mathbf {r} \quad \Rightarrow \quad \mathbf {A} =(A_{r},A_{\theta },A_{z})=(0,Br/2,0)\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/70e32699f9c9162c6181a661d97a1677801c903b)
and the Lagrangian is

![Image 83: {\displaystyle L(r,{\dot {r}},{\dot {\theta }},{\dot {z}})={\frac {m}{2}}({\dot {r}}^{2}+r^{2}{\dot {\theta }}^{2}+{\dot {z}}^{2})+{\frac {qBr^{2}{\dot {\theta }}}{2}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f017fae3c46ddf064f53c52456cc122447a7d4dd)
Notice this potential has an effectively cylindrical symmetry (although it also has angular velocity dependence), since the only spatial dependence is on the radial length from an imaginary cylinder axis.

There are two cyclic coordinates, _θ_ and _z_. The canonical momenta conjugate to _θ_ and _z_ are the constants

![Image 84: {\displaystyle p_{\theta }={\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {\theta }}}}=mr^{2}{\dot {\theta }}+{\frac {qBr^{2}}{2}}\,,\quad p_{z}={\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {z}}}}=m{\dot {z}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9549cc085ef2fe47b36814d25c28b6f36a5cd654)
so the velocities are

![Image 85: {\displaystyle {\dot {\theta }}={\frac {1}{mr^{2}}}\left(p_{\theta }-{\frac {qBr^{2}}{2}}\right)\,,\quad {\dot {z}}={\frac {p_{z}}{m}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5cdcbd4a0a01ed9b50fe157a0c3b4a1175bf044a)
The angular momentum about the _z_ axis is _not_ _p θ_, but the quantity _mr_ 2 _dθ_/_dt_, which is not conserved due to the contribution from the magnetic field. The canonical momentum _p θ_ is the conserved quantity. It is still the case that _p z_ is the linear or translational momentum along the _z_ axis, which is also conserved.

The radial component _r_ and angular velocity _dθ_/_dt_ can vary with time, but _p θ_ is constant, and since _p z_ is constant it follows _dz_/_dt_ is constant. The Routhian can take the form

![Image 86: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}R(r,{\dot {r}})&=p_{\theta }{\dot {\theta }}+p_{z}{\dot {z}}-L\\&=p_{\theta }{\dot {\theta }}+p_{z}{\dot {z}}-{\frac {m}{2}}{\dot {r}}^{2}-{\frac {p_{\theta }{\dot {\theta }}}{2}}-{\frac {p_{z}{\dot {z}}}{2}}-{\frac {1}{2}}qBr^{2}{\dot {\theta }}\\[6pt]&=(p_{\theta }-qBr^{2}){\frac {\dot {\theta }}{2}}-{\frac {m}{2}}{\dot {r}}^{2}+{\frac {p_{z}{\dot {z}}}{2}}\\[6pt]&={\frac {1}{2mr^{2}}}\left(p_{\theta }-qBr^{2}\right)\left(p_{\theta }-{\frac {qBr^{2}}{2}}\right)-{\frac {m}{2}}{\dot {r}}^{2}+{\frac {p_{z}^{2}}{2m}}\\[6pt]&={\frac {1}{2mr^{2}}}\left(p_{\theta }^{2}-{\frac {3}{2}}qBr^{2}+{\frac {(qB)^{2}r^{4}}{2}}\right)-{\frac {m}{2}}{\dot {r}}^{2}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51b9fda3f3085800e865df4ba152c3ca758ad2e2)
where in the last line, the _p z 2_/2 _m_ term is a constant and can be ignored without loss of continuity. The Hamiltonian equations for _θ_ and _z_ automatically vanish and do not need to be solved for. The Lagrangian equation in _r_

![Image 87: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial R}{\partial {\dot {r}}}}={\frac {\partial R}{\partial r}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f1644cadb7d55dd0643a2149a8aab48b39f56a28)
is by direct calculation

which after collecting terms is

the differential equation is

![Image 91: {\displaystyle {\ddot {r}}={\frac {a}{r^{3}}}+br}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef1bce73dd66826a4f615631b242486117f805ad)
To see how _z_ changes with time, integrate the momenta expression for _p z_ above

![Image 92: {\displaystyle z={\frac {p_{z}}{m}}t+c_{z}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2839510e0bbe5223be3fa502e1dd89acfe0c7487)
where _c z_ is an arbitrary constant, the initial value of _z_ to be specified in the [initial conditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Initial_conditions "Initial conditions").

The motion of the particle in this system is [helicoidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helicoid "Helicoid"), with the axial motion uniform (constant) but the radial and angular components varying in a spiral according to the equation of motion derived above. The initial conditions on _r_, _dr_/_dt_, _θ_, _dθ_/_dt_, will determine if the trajectory of the particle has a constant _r_ or varying _r_. If initially _r_ is nonzero but _dr_/_dt_ = 0, while _θ_ and _dθ_/_dt_ are arbitrary, then the initial velocity of the particle has no radial component, _r_ is constant, so the motion will be in a perfect helix. If _r_ is constant, the angular velocity is also constant according to the conserved _p θ_.

With the Lagrangian approach, the equation for _r_ would include _dθ_/_dt_ which has to be eliminated, and there would be equations for _θ_ and _z_ to solve for.

The _r_ equation is

![Image 93: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {r}}}}={\frac {\partial L}{\partial r}}\quad \Rightarrow \quad m{\ddot {r}}=mr{\dot {\theta }}^{2}+qBr{\dot {\theta }}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afddcc830d817b8d1f409576e375d3885e110d09)
the _θ_ equation is

![Image 94: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {\theta }}}}={\frac {\partial L}{\partial \theta }}\quad \Rightarrow \quad m(2r{\dot {r}}{\dot {\theta }}+r^{2}{\ddot {\theta }})+qBr{\dot {r}}=0\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f3eebbc621b12b9279af88e25db4d04a5f06c50f)
and the _z_ equation is

![Image 95: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {z}}}}={\frac {\partial L}{\partial z}}\quad \Rightarrow \quad m{\ddot {z}}=0\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/408a7a36b3faff8c9a0af10c32d5f89b618f840e)
The _z_ equation is trivial to integrate, but the _r_ and _θ_ equations are not, in any case the time derivatives are mixed in all the equations and must be eliminated.

1.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Routhian_mechanics#cite_ref-1)**The coordinates are functions of time, so the Lagrangian always has implicit time-dependence via the coordinates. If the Lagrangian changes with time irrespective of the coordinates, usually due to some time-dependent potential, then the Lagrangian is said to have "explicit" time-dependence. Similarly for the Hamiltonian and Routhian functions.
2.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Routhian_mechanics#cite_ref-6)**For two functions _u_ and _v_, the differential of the product is _d_(_uv_) = _udv_ + _vdu_.
3.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Routhian_mechanics#cite_ref-9)**The potential energy is actually ![Image 96: {\displaystyle V=mg\ell (1-\cos \theta )\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e39fd3118c994f46ee68762a88ebfdf13b9f3936) but since the first term is constant, it can be ignored in the Lagrangian (and Routhian) which only depend on derivatives of coordinates and velocities. Subtracting this from the kinetic energy means a plus sign in the Lagrangian, not minus.
