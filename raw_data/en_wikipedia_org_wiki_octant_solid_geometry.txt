From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

One of eight divisions of a Euclidean 3D coordinate system

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Cube_with_balanced_ternary_labels.svg/250px-Cube_with_balanced_ternary_labels.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cube_with_balanced_ternary_labels.svg)

Three axial planes (_x_=0, _y_=0, _z_=0) divide space into eight octants. The eight (±,±,±) coordinates of the cube vertices are used to denote them. The horizontal plane shows the four quadrants between _x_- and _y_-axis. (Vertex numbers are little-endian balanced ternary.)

An **octant** in [solid geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_geometry "Solid geometry") is one of the eight divisions of a [Euclidean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry")[three-dimensional coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") defined by the signs of the coordinates. It is analogous to the two-dimensional [_quadrant_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrant_(plane_geometry) "Quadrant (plane geometry)") and the one-dimensional [_ray_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_(geometry) "Ray (geometry)").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octant_(solid_geometry)#cite_note-1) The generalization of an octant is called _[orthant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthant "Orthant")_ or _hyperoctant_.

Naming and numbering
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Octant_(solid_geometry)&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Naming and numbering")]

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/Right-handed_coordinate_system_%28y_to_back%29.png/250px-Right-handed_coordinate_system_%28y_to_back%29.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Right-handed_coordinate_system_(y_to_back).png)

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Right-handed_coordinate_system_%28z_to_front%29.png/250px-Right-handed_coordinate_system_%28z_to_front%29.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Right-handed_coordinate_system_(z_to_front).png)

Two representations of a right-hand coordinate system. The first one corresponds to the cube image.

A convention for naming an octant is to give its list of signs, e.g. (+,−,−) or (−,+,−). Octant (+,+,+) is sometimes referred to as the _first octant_, although similar ordinal name descriptors are not defined for the other seven octants. The advantages of using the (±,±,±) notation are its unambiguousness, and extensibility for higher dimensions.

The following table shows the sign tuples together with likely ways to enumerate them. A binary enumeration with − as 1 can be easily generalized across dimensions. A binary enumeration with + as 1 defines the same order as [balanced ternary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balanced_ternary "Balanced ternary"). The [Roman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_numerals "Roman numerals") enumeration of the [quadrants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrant_(plane_geometry) "Quadrant (plane geometry)") is in [Gray code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gray_code "Gray code") order, so the corresponding Gray code is also shown for the octants.

Octants | Gray code | _x_ | _y_ | _z_ | Binary | Balanced ternary |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| − as 1 | + as 1 |
| < | > | < | > | < | > |
| 0 | + | + | + | 0 | 0 | 7 | 7 | 13 | 13 |
| 1 | − | + | + | 1 | 4 | 6 | 3 | 11 | −5 |
| 3 | + | − | + | 2 | 2 | 5 | 5 | 7 | 7 |
| 2 | − | − | + | 3 | 6 | 4 | 1 | 5 | −11 |
| 7 | + | + | − | 4 | 1 | 3 | 6 | −5 | 11 |
| 6 | − | + | − | 5 | 5 | 2 | 2 | −7 | −7 |
| 4 | + | − | − | 6 | 3 | 1 | 4 | −11 | 5 |
| 5 | − | − | − | 7 | 7 | 0 | 0 | −13 | −13 |[Quadrants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrant_(plane_geometry) "Quadrant (plane geometry)") for comparison | Roman | _x_ | _y_ | Binary | Balanced ternary |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| − as 1 | + as 1 |
| < | > | < | > | < | > |
| I | + | + | 0 | 0 | 3 | 3 | 4 | 4 |
| II | − | + | 1 | 2 | 2 | 1 | 2 | −2 |
| IV | + | − | 2 | 1 | 1 | 2 | −2 | 2 |
| III | − | − | 3 | 3 | 0 | 0 | −4 | −4 |

Little- and big-[endian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endianness "Endianness") are marked by "<" and ">", respectively.

Verbal descriptions are ambiguous, because they depend on the representation of the coordinate system. In the two depicted representations of a [right-hand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right-hand_rule "Right-hand rule") coordinate system, the first octant could be called _right-back-top_ or _right-top-front_ respectively.
