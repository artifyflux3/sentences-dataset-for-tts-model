[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/6/6d/Translational_motion.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Translational_motion.gif)

The [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature") of the [ideal gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas "Ideal gas") is proportional to the average [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy") of its particles. The [size](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohr_radius "Bohr radius") of [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium") atoms relative to their spacing is shown to scale under 1,950 [atmospheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit) "Atmosphere (unit)") of pressure. The atoms have an average speed relative to their size slowed down here two [trillion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1000000000000_(number) "1000000000000 (number)") fold from that at room temperature.

The **kinetic theory of gases** is a simple [classical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics") model of the [thermodynamic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamics "Thermodynamics") behavior of [gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas "Gas"). Its introduction allowed many principal concepts of thermodynamics to be established. It treats a gas as composed of numerous particles, too small to be seen with a microscope, in constant, random motion. These particles are now known to be the [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom") or [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule") of the gas. The kinetic theory of gases uses their collisions with each other and with the walls of their container to explain [the relationship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas_law#Combined_gas_law "Ideal gas law") between the [macroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macroscopic_scale "Macroscopic scale") properties of gases, such as [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume"), [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure"), and [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature"), as well as [transport properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transport_phenomena "Transport phenomena") such as [viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscosity "Viscosity"), [thermal conductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_conductivity "Thermal conductivity") and [mass diffusivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_diffusivity "Mass diffusivity").

The basic version of the model describes an [ideal gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas "Ideal gas"). It treats the collisions as [perfectly elastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elastic_collision "Elastic collision") and as the only interaction between the particles, which are additionally assumed to be much smaller than their average distance apart.

Due to the [time reversibility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_reversibility "Time reversibility") of microscopic dynamics ([microscopic reversibility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microscopic_reversibility "Microscopic reversibility")), the kinetic theory is also connected to the principle of [detailed balance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detailed_balance "Detailed balance"), in terms of the [fluctuation-dissipation theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluctuation-dissipation_theorem "Fluctuation-dissipation theorem") (for [Brownian motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brownian_motion "Brownian motion")) and the [Onsager reciprocal relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Onsager_reciprocal_relations "Onsager reciprocal relations").

The theory was historically significant as the first explicit exercise of the ideas of [statistical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_mechanics "Statistical mechanics").

### Kinetic theory of matter

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kinetic_theory_of_gases&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Kinetic theory of matter")]

In about 50 [BCE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Before_common_era "Before common era"), the Roman philosopher [Lucretius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lucretius "Lucretius") proposed that apparently static macroscopic bodies were composed on a small scale of rapidly moving atoms all bouncing off each other.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-1) This [Epicurean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epicureanism "Epicureanism") atomistic point of view was rarely considered in the subsequent centuries, when [Aristotlean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") ideas were dominant.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/Portrait_of_Francis_Bacon_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Portrait_of_Francis_Bacon_%28cropped%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Portrait_of_Francis_Bacon_(cropped).jpg)

Francis Bacon

One of the first and boldest statements on the relationship between motion of particles and [heat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat "Heat") was by the English philosopher [Francis Bacon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francis_Bacon "Francis Bacon") in 1620. "It must not be thought that heat generates motion, or motion heat (though in some respects this be true), but that the very essence of heat ... is motion and nothing else."[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-2) "not a ... motion of the whole, but of the small particles of the body."[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-3) In 1623, in _[The Assayer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Assayer "The Assayer")_, [Galileo Galilei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galileo_Galilei "Galileo Galilei"), in turn, argued that heat, pressure, smell and other phenomena perceived by our senses are apparent properties only, caused by the movement of particles, which is a real phenomenon.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGalilei1957273-4-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-5)

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/John_Locke._Portrait_by_Herman_Verelst_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-John_Locke._Portrait_by_Herman_Verelst_%28cropped%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:John_Locke._Portrait_by_Herman_Verelst_(cropped).jpg)

John Locke

In 1665, in _[Micrographia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrographia "Micrographia")_, the English polymath [Robert Hooke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Hooke "Robert Hooke") repeated Bacon's assertion,[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-7) and in 1675, his colleague, Anglo-Irish scientist [Robert Boyle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Boyle "Robert Boyle") noted that a hammer's "impulse" is transformed into the motion of a nail's constituent particles, and that this type of motion is what heat consists of.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-8) Boyle also believed that all macroscopic properties, including color, taste and elasticity, are caused by and ultimately consist of nothing but the arrangement and motion of indivisible particles of matter.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-9) In a lecture of 1681, Hooke asserted a direct relationship between the temperature of an object and the speed of its internal particles. "Heat ... is nothing but the internal Motion of the Particles of [a] Body; and the hotter a Body is, the more violently are the Particles moved."[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-10) In a manuscript published 1720, the English philosopher [John Locke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Locke "John Locke") made a very similar statement: "What in our sensation is _heat_, in the object is nothing but _motion_."[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-12) Locke too talked about the motion of the internal particles of the object, which he referred to as its "insensible parts".

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Catherine_II_visiting_Mikhail_Lomonosov_by_Ivan_Feodorov_1884.jpg/250px-Catherine_II_visiting_Mikhail_Lomonosov_by_Ivan_Feodorov_1884.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Catherine_II_visiting_Mikhail_Lomonosov_by_Ivan_Feodorov_1884.jpg)

[Catherine the Great](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catherine_the_Great "Catherine the Great") visiting Mikhail Lomonosov

In his 1744 paper _Meditations on the Cause of Heat and Cold_, Russian polymath [Mikhail Lomonosov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mikhail_Lomonosov "Mikhail Lomonosov") made a relatable appeal to everyday experience to gain acceptance of the microscopic and kinetic nature of matter and heat:[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-13)

> Movement should not be denied based on the fact it is not seen. Who would deny that the leaves of trees move when rustled by a wind, despite it being unobservable from large distances? Just as in this case motion remains hidden due to perspective, it remains hidden in warm bodies due to the extremely small sizes of the moving particles. In both cases, the viewing angle is so small that neither the object nor their movement can be seen.

Lomonosov also insisted that movement of particles is necessary for the processes of [dissolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvation "Solvation"), [extraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extraction_(chemistry) "Extraction (chemistry)") and [diffusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffusion "Diffusion"), providing as examples the dissolution and diffusion of salts by the action of water particles on the of the “molecules of salt”, the dissolution of metals in mercury, and the extraction of plant pigments by alcohol.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-14)

Also the [transfer of heat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_transfer "Heat transfer") was explained by the motion of particles. Around 1760, Scottish physicist and chemist [Joseph Black](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Black "Joseph Black") wrote: "Many have supposed that heat is a tremulous ... motion of the particles of matter, which ... motion they imagined to be communicated from one body to another."[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-15)

### Kinetic theory of gases

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kinetic_theory_of_gases&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Kinetic theory of gases")]

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Portr%C3%A4t_des_Daniel_Bernoulli_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Portr%C3%A4t_des_Daniel_Bernoulli_%28cropped%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Portr%C3%A4t_des_Daniel_Bernoulli_(cropped).jpg)

Daniel Bernoulli

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/HYDRODYNAMICA%2C_Danielis_Bernoulli.png/250px-HYDRODYNAMICA%2C_Danielis_Bernoulli.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:HYDRODYNAMICA,_Danielis_Bernoulli.png)

_Hydrodynamica_ front cover

In 1738 [Daniel Bernoulli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daniel_Bernoulli "Daniel Bernoulli") published _[Hydrodynamica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrodynamica "Hydrodynamica")_, which laid the basis for the [kinetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy") theory of [gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas "Gas"). In this work, Bernoulli posited the argument, that gases consist of great numbers of molecules moving in all directions, that their impact on a surface causes the pressure of the gas, and that their average [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy") determines the temperature of the gas. The theory was not immediately accepted, in part because [conservation of energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_energy "Conservation of energy") had not yet been established, and it was not obvious to [physicists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physicist "Physicist") how the collisions between molecules could be perfectly elastic.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-PonomarevKurchatov1993-16): 36–37

Pioneers of the kinetic theory, whose work was also largely neglected by their contemporaries, were Mikhail Lomonosov (1747),[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-17)[Georges-Louis Le Sage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georges-Louis_Le_Sage "Georges-Louis Le Sage") (ca. 1780, published 1818),[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-18)[John Herapath](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Herapath "John Herapath") (1816)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-19) and [John James Waterston](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_James_Waterston "John James Waterston") (1843),[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-20) which connected their research with the development of [mechanical explanations of gravitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_explanations_of_gravitation "Mechanical explanations of gravitation").

In 1856 [August Krönig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/August_Kr%C3%B6nig "August Krönig") created a simple gas-kinetic model, which only considered the [translational motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry) "Translation (geometry)") of the particles.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-21) In 1857 [Rudolf Clausius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolf_Clausius "Rudolf Clausius") developed a similar, but more sophisticated version of the theory, which included translational and, contrary to Krönig, also [rotational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation") and vibrational molecular motions. In this same work he introduced the concept of [mean free path](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_free_path "Mean free path") of a particle.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-22) In 1859, after reading a paper about the [diffusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffusion "Diffusion") of molecules by Clausius, Scottish physicist [James Clerk Maxwell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell "James Clerk Maxwell") formulated the [Maxwell distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell_distribution "Maxwell distribution") of molecular velocities, which gave the proportion of molecules having a certain velocity in a specific range.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-23) This was the first-ever statistical law in physics.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-24) Maxwell also gave the first mechanical argument that molecular collisions entail an equalization of temperatures and hence a tendency towards equilibrium.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-25) In his 1873 thirteen page article 'Molecules', Maxwell states: "we are told that an 'atom' is a material point, invested and surrounded by 'potential forces' and that when 'flying molecules' strike against a solid body in constant succession it causes what is called [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure") of air and other gases."[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-26) In 1871, [Ludwig Boltzmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Boltzmann "Ludwig Boltzmann") generalized Maxwell's achievement and formulated the [Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%E2%80%93Boltzmann_distribution "Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution"). The [logarithmic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logarithm "Logarithm") connection between [entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy "Entropy") and [probability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability "Probability") was also first stated by Boltzmann.

At the beginning of the 20th century, atoms were considered by many physicists to be purely hypothetical constructs, rather than real objects. An important turning point was [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein")'s (1905)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-27) and [Marian Smoluchowski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marian_Smoluchowski "Marian Smoluchowski")'s (1906)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-28) papers on [Brownian motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brownian_motion "Brownian motion"), which succeeded in making certain accurate quantitative predictions based on the kinetic theory.

Following the development of the [Boltzmann equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_equation "Boltzmann equation"), a framework for its use in developing transport equations was developed independently by [David Enskog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Enskog "David Enskog") and [Sydney Chapman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sydney_Chapman_(mathematician) "Sydney Chapman (mathematician)") in 1917 and 1916. The framework provided a route to prediction of the transport properties of dilute gases, and became known as [Chapman–Enskog theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chapman%E2%80%93Enskog_theory "Chapman–Enskog theory"). The framework was gradually expanded throughout the following century, eventually becoming a route to prediction of transport properties in real, dense gases.

The application of kinetic theory to ideal gases makes the following assumptions:

*   The gas consists of very small particles. This smallness of their size is such that the sum of the [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume") of the individual gas molecules is negligible compared to the volume of the container of the gas. This is equivalent to stating that the average distance separating the gas particles is large compared to their [size](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_radius "Atomic radius"), and that the elapsed time during a collision between particles and the container's wall is negligible when compared to the time between successive collisions.
*   The number of particles is so large that a statistical treatment of the problem is well justified. This assumption is sometimes referred to as the [thermodynamic limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_limit "Thermodynamic limit").
*   The rapidly moving particles constantly collide among themselves and with the walls of the container, and all these collisions are perfectly elastic.
*   Interactions (i.e. collisions) between particles are strictly binary and [uncorrelated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncorrelatedness_(probability_theory) "Uncorrelatedness (probability theory)"), meaning that there are no three-body (or higher) interactions, and the particles have no memory.
*   Except during collisions, the interactions among molecules are negligible. They exert no other [forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force") on one another.

Thus, the dynamics of particle motion can be treated classically, and the equations of motion are time-reversible.

As a simplifying assumption, the particles are usually assumed to have the same [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") as one another; however, the theory can be generalized to a mass distribution, with each mass type contributing to the gas properties independently of one another in agreement with [Dalton's law of partial pressures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dalton%27s_law "Dalton's law"). Many of the model's predictions are the same whether or not collisions between particles are included, so they are often neglected as a simplifying assumption in derivations (see below).[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-29)

More modern developments, such as the [revised Enskog theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revised_Enskog_theory "Revised Enskog theory") and the extended [Bhatnagar–Gross–Krook](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bhatnagar%E2%80%93Gross%E2%80%93Krook_operator "Bhatnagar–Gross–Krook operator") model,[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-30) relax one or more of the above assumptions. These can accurately describe the properties of dense gases, and gases with [internal degrees of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_vibration "Molecular vibration"), because they include the volume of the particles as well as contributions from intermolecular and intramolecular forces as well as quantized molecular rotations, quantum rotational-vibrational symmetry effects, and electronic excitation.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-31) While theories relaxing the assumptions that the gas particles occupy negligible volume and that collisions are strictly elastic have been successful, it has been shown that relaxing the requirement of interactions being binary and uncorrelated will eventually lead to divergent results.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-32)

### Pressure and kinetic energy

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kinetic_theory_of_gases&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Pressure and kinetic energy")]

In the kinetic theory of gases, the [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure") is assumed to be equal to the force (per unit area) exerted by the individual gas atoms or molecules hitting and rebounding from the gas container's surface.

Consider a gas particle traveling at velocity, ![Image 7: {\textstyle v_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6126ad3375b4bd895f169ef76fc2946672ec8d17), along the ![Image 8: {\displaystyle {\hat {i}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd413507d7fc526479705712bd81de4cf7953989)-direction in an enclosed volume with [characteristic length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_length "Characteristic length"), ![Image 9: {\displaystyle L_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fbbedf9f5f71ca52f8f24392c3cc18fca6c420dc), cross-sectional area, ![Image 10: {\displaystyle A_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1aed3b5def921afbe6cc48aaf8f9b11c6f1c1e2d), and volume, ![Image 11: {\displaystyle V=A_{i}L_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa7fb17e8d79d40564d3a848b2c8b2317835ea64). The gas particle encounters a boundary after characteristic time ![Image 12: {\displaystyle t=L_{i}/v_{i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b945e7e88627e9bf99c8c144cf8210f41a7217ce)

The [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") of the gas particle can then be described as ![Image 13: {\displaystyle p_{i}=mv_{i}=mL_{i}/t.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fd2ba9ca0fec55b65a4610654ee44386b124137)

We combine the above with [Newton's second law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_second_law "Newton's second law"), which states that the force experienced by a particle is related to the time rate of change of its momentum, such that ![Image 14: {\displaystyle F_{i}={\frac {\mathrm {d} p_{i}}{\mathrm {d} t}}={\frac {mL_{i}}{t^{2}}}={\frac {mv_{i}^{2}}{L_{i}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a36a70e5d603286d06e0bfec9f80e4de96de6327)

Now consider a large number, ![Image 15: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3), of gas particles with random orientation in a three-dimensional volume. Because the orientation is random, the average particle speed, ![Image 16: {\textstyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e970b082b4c08f1583d01d713815a0900fab6a8c), in every direction is identical ![Image 17: {\displaystyle v_{x}^{2}=v_{y}^{2}=v_{z}^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fee0e11ad152433a312c2554ac20dff27005a8b2)

Further, assume that the volume is symmetrical about its three dimensions, ![Image 18: {\displaystyle {\hat {i}},{\hat {j}},{\hat {k}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be6d2cdc181b754831cfe0aac50969d6d41a1941), such that ![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}V={}&V_{i}=V_{j}=V_{k},\\F={}&F_{i}=F_{j}=F_{k},\\&A_{i}=A_{j}=A_{k}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64ea12f0ef38a720b5d5f027b85ad32922849959) The total surface area on which the gas particles act is therefore ![Image 20: {\displaystyle A=3A_{i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3c6ac858a5d81cb573e2202108652d250c75981e)

The pressure exerted by the collisions of the ![Image 21: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) gas particles with the surface can then be found by adding the force contribution of every particle and dividing by the interior surface area of the volume, ![Image 22: {\displaystyle P={\frac {N{\overline {F}}}{A}}={\frac {NLF}{V}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07dd6cd42937e9c29936357f8451eb7f5522d2ad)![Image 23: {\displaystyle \Rightarrow PV=NLF={\frac {N}{3}}mv^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bcd5c7308abc99c7a0d0b21481628ac62a7bf433)

The total translational [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy")![Image 24: {\displaystyle K_{\text{t}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/716960268479ac029377dc89c33987380b8d970b) of the gas is defined as ![Image 25: {\displaystyle K_{\text{t}}={\frac {N}{2}}mv^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/470c78d49703699703cf8936475cb7aef46c334d) providing the result ![Image 26: {\displaystyle PV={\frac {2}{3}}K_{\text{t}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c1139421ef0eee4996a426880a73ad745db3b725)

This is an important, non-trivial result of the kinetic theory because it relates pressure, a [macroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macroscopic "Macroscopic") property, to the translational kinetic energy of the molecules, which is a [microscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microscopic "Microscopic") property.

The mass density of a gas ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \rho }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f7d439671d1289b6a816e6af7a304be40608d64) is expressed through the total mass of gas particles and through volume of this gas: ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \rho ={\frac {Nm}{V}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dff1befef9a83a8554777b350e91c7c07783596b). Taking this into account, the pressure is equal to ![Image 29: {\displaystyle P={\frac {\rho v^{2}}{3}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41a5a8f95917d485c55ac407535392bd40ab1312)

Relativistic expression for this formula is [[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-33)

![Image 30: {\displaystyle P={\frac {2\rho c^{2}}{3}}\left({\left(1-{\overline {v^{2}}}/c^{2}\right)}^{-1/2}-1\right),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/282bf527c60997efed84abaff73cc8a61dd38526) where ![Image 31: {\displaystyle c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a67b81c2de995bd608d5b2df50cd8cd7d92455) is [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light"). In the limit of small speeds, the expression becomes ![Image 32: {\displaystyle P\approx \rho {\overline {v^{2}}}/3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a5c85be1e91e01f00f3f11e8e2cb12931a03fb8).

### Temperature and kinetic energy

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kinetic_theory_of_gases&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Temperature and kinetic energy")]

Rewriting the above result for the pressure as ![Image 33: {\textstyle PV={\frac {1}{3}}Nmv^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a49cf4d67073e4040c35d5833dd048e4b5128b24), we may combine it with the [ideal gas law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas_law "Ideal gas law")

![Image 34: {\displaystyle PV=Nk_{\mathrm {B} }T,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a64eeb7d8dabe882a429ecbafd3e614d1c40618)1

where ![Image 35: {\displaystyle k_{\mathrm {B} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9b90d21a1c8fb907fddab1caded3b7f1eeffe3d) is the [Boltzmann constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_constant "Boltzmann constant") and ![Image 36: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0) is the [absolute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_temperature "Thermodynamic temperature")[temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature") defined by the ideal gas law, to obtain ![Image 37: {\displaystyle k_{\mathrm {B} }T={\frac {1}{3}}mv^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/13c401a7a04f15c667b293069772c6ae1e3384f7) which leads to a simplified expression of the average translational kinetic energy per molecule,[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-34)![Image 38: {\displaystyle {\frac {1}{2}}mv^{2}={\frac {3}{2}}k_{\mathrm {B} }T.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/945b0a6e1cebf11bf41fd5aadb4c48dfc297590f) The translational kinetic energy of the system is ![Image 39: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) times that of a molecule, namely ![Image 40: {\textstyle K_{\text{t}}={\frac {1}{2}}Nmv^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf893a390e969c333f25a0ad8609b7eceec290f6). The temperature, ![Image 41: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0) is related to the translational kinetic energy by the description above, resulting in

![Image 42: {\displaystyle T={\frac {1}{3}}{\frac {mv^{2}}{k_{\mathrm {B} }}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/74ebe650b4100fb09d52730e29348a1da06b26a6)2

which becomes

![Image 43: {\displaystyle T={\frac {2}{3}}{\frac {K_{\text{t}}}{Nk_{\mathrm {B} }}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9dd9fdf4ef0ebca0687c3ba05ca26117670de119)3

Equation (**[3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#math_3)**) is one important result of the kinetic theory: _The average molecular kinetic energy is proportional to the ideal gas law's absolute temperature_. From equations (**[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#math_1)**) and (**[3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#math_3)**), we have

![Image 44: {\displaystyle PV={\frac {2}{3}}K_{\text{t}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c1139421ef0eee4996a426880a73ad745db3b725)4

Thus, the product of pressure and volume per [mole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mole_(unit) "Mole (unit)") is proportional to the average translational molecular kinetic energy.

Equations (**[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#math_1)**) and (**[4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#math_4)**) are called the "classical results", which could also be derived from [statistical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_mechanics "Statistical mechanics"); for more details, see:[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-35)

The [equipartition theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equipartition_theorem "Equipartition theorem") requires that kinetic energy is partitioned equally between all kinetic [degrees of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom "Degrees of freedom"), _D_. A monatomic gas is axially symmetric about each spatial axis, so that _D_ = 3 comprising translational motion along each axis. A diatomic gas is axially symmetric about only one axis, so that _D_ = 5, comprising translational motion along three axes and rotational motion along two axes. A polyatomic gas, like [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water "Water"), is not radially symmetric about any axis, resulting in _D_ = 6, comprising 3 translational and 3 rotational degrees of freedom.

Because the [equipartition theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equipartition_theorem "Equipartition theorem") requires that kinetic energy is partitioned equally, the total kinetic energy is ![Image 45: {\displaystyle K=DK_{\text{t}}={\frac {D}{2}}Nmv^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91b0e330b8b5a8431a6345bde3b83f69a50425f8)

Thus, the energy added to the system per gas particle kinetic degree of freedom is ![Image 46: {\displaystyle {\frac {K}{ND}}={\frac {1}{2}}k_{\text{B}}T.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/304c906b3ff4e63cae66a32f6d410c651f97373a)

Therefore, the kinetic energy per kelvin of one mole of monatomic [ideal gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas "Ideal gas") (_D_ = 3) is ![Image 47: {\displaystyle K={\frac {D}{2}}k_{\text{B}}N_{\text{A}}={\frac {3}{2}}R,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a7abbf678c8cb21fc60fe1e8abb587d66749150) where ![Image 48: {\displaystyle N_{\text{A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/037f8d054f445451d8fee1b440cddba989e66ea1) is the [Avogadro constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avogadro_constant "Avogadro constant"), and _R_ is the [ideal gas constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas_constant "Ideal gas constant").

Thus, the ratio of the kinetic energy to the absolute temperature of an ideal monatomic gas can be calculated easily:

*   per mole: 12.47 J/K
*   per molecule: 20.7[yJ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yoctojoule "Yoctojoule")/K = 129 μeV/K

At [standard temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure "Standard temperature and pressure") (273.15 K), the kinetic energy can also be obtained:

*   per mole: 3406 J
*   per molecule: 5.65[zJ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeptojoule "Zeptojoule") = 35.2 meV.

At higher temperatures (typically thousands of kelvins), vibrational modes become active to provide additional degrees of freedom, creating a temperature-dependence on _D_ and the total molecular energy. Quantum [statistical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_mechanics "Statistical mechanics") is needed to accurately compute these contributions.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-36)

### Collisions with container wall

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kinetic_theory_of_gases&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Collisions with container wall")]

For an ideal gas in equilibrium, the rate of collisions with the container wall and velocity distribution of particles hitting the container wall can be calculated[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-OCW-37) based on naive kinetic theory, and the results can be used for analyzing [effusive flow rates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effusion#Physics_in_Effusion "Effusion"), which is useful in applications such as the [gaseous diffusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaseous_diffusion#Technology "Gaseous diffusion") method for [isotope separation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope_separation#Diffusion "Isotope separation").

Assume that in the container, the number density (number per unit volume) is ![Image 49: {\displaystyle n=N/V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b11227c3fcc30753882fbcfeed68ff3f8f82c279) and that the particles obey [Maxwell's velocity distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell-Boltzmann_distribution "Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution"): ![Image 50: {\displaystyle f_{\text{Maxwell}}(v_{x},v_{y},v_{z})\,dv_{x}\,dv_{y}\,dv_{z}=\left({\frac {m}{2\pi k_{\text{B}}T}}\right)^{3/2}e^{-{\frac {mv^{2}}{2k_{\text{B}}T}}}\,dv_{x}\,dv_{y}\,dv_{z}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eaa2af255ea13ebf69b3a809ef40b75c9507e6c8)

Then for a small area ![Image 51: {\displaystyle dA}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da1d35422f3d3b3cfcfd9b9aebe9176599fed24d) on the container wall, a particle with speed ![Image 52: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) at angle ![Image 53: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) from the normal of the area ![Image 54: {\displaystyle dA}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da1d35422f3d3b3cfcfd9b9aebe9176599fed24d), will collide with the area within time interval ![Image 55: {\displaystyle dt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ebee76a835701fd1f26047a09855f2ea36bb08fc), if it is within the distance ![Image 56: {\displaystyle v\,dt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cbf244b02794d0efed126d285a63f5940b15560b) from the area ![Image 57: {\displaystyle dA}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da1d35422f3d3b3cfcfd9b9aebe9176599fed24d). In kinetic theory of gases, the [mean free path](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_free_path#Kinetic_theory "Mean free path") is the average distance traveled by a molecule, or a number of molecules per volume, before they make their first collision. Let ![Image 107: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) be the collision [cross section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_section_(physics)#Collision_among_gas_particles "Cross section (physics)") of one molecule colliding with another. As in the previous section, the number density ![Image 108: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) is defined as the number of molecules per (extensive) volume, or ![Image 109: {\displaystyle n=N/V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b11227c3fcc30753882fbcfeed68ff3f8f82c279). The collision cross section per volume or collision cross section density is ![Image 110: {\displaystyle n\sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83a55c05fe78dbb89001a6ef41afef41dae8379b), and it is related to the mean free path ![Image 111: {\displaystyle \ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f066e981e530bacc07efc6a10fa82deee985929e) by![Image 112: {\displaystyle \ell ={\frac {1}{n\sigma {\sqrt {2}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf81f5b30c586f32533e3a4cece8a64cd33c7d8f)

Notice that the unit of the collision cross section per volume ![Image 113: {\displaystyle n\sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83a55c05fe78dbb89001a6ef41afef41dae8379b) is reciprocal of length.

Transport properties
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kinetic_theory_of_gases&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Transport properties")]

The kinetic theory of gases deals not only with gases in thermodynamic equilibrium, but also very importantly with gases not in thermodynamic equilibrium. This means using Kinetic Theory to consider what are known as "transport properties", such as [viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscosity "Viscosity"), [thermal conductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_conductivity "Thermal conductivity"), [mass diffusivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_diffusivity "Mass diffusivity") and [thermal diffusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermophoresis "Thermophoresis").

In its most basic form, Kinetic gas theory is only applicable to dilute gases. The extension of Kinetic gas theory to dense gas mixtures, [Revised Enskog Theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chapman%E2%80%93Enskog_theory#Revised_Enskog_Theory "Chapman–Enskog theory"), was developed in 1983-1987 by [E. G. D. Cohen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E._G._D._Cohen "E. G. D. Cohen"), [J. M. Kincaid](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=J._M._Kincaid&action=edit&redlink=1 "J. M. Kincaid (page does not exist)") and [M. Lòpez de Haro](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=M._L%C3%B2pez_de_Haro&action=edit&redlink=1 "M. Lòpez de Haro (page does not exist)"),[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-38)[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-39)[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-40)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-41) building on work by [H. van Beijeren](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=H._van_Beijeren&action=edit&redlink=1 "H. van Beijeren (page does not exist)") and [M. H. Ernst](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=M._H._Ernst&action=edit&redlink=1 "M. H. Ernst (page does not exist)").[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-42)

### Viscosity and kinetic momentum

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kinetic_theory_of_gases&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Viscosity and kinetic momentum")]

In books on elementary kinetic theory[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-Sears1975-43) one can find results for dilute gas modeling that are used in many fields. Derivation of the kinetic model for shear viscosity usually starts by considering a [Couette flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Couette_flow "Couette flow") where two parallel plates are separated by a gas layer. The upper plate is moving at a constant velocity to the right due to a force _F_. The lower plate is stationary, and an equal and opposite force must therefore be acting on it to keep it at rest. The molecules in the gas layer have a forward velocity component ![Image 114: {\displaystyle u}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3e6bb763d22c20916ed4f0bb6bd49d7470cffd8) which increase uniformly with distance ![Image 115: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) above the lower plate. The non-equilibrium flow is superimposed on a [Maxwell-Boltzmann equilibrium distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell-Boltzmann_distribution "Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution") of molecular motions.

Inside a dilute gas in a [Couette flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Couette_flow "Couette flow") setup, let ![Image 116: {\displaystyle u_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c7425f9c7ab645587060423c0af62f8a61fbc65) be the forward velocity of the gas at a horizontal flat layer (labeled as ![Image 117: {\displaystyle y=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/094f824655138f6b11d96a0da32e7f0716ba6959)); ![Image 118: {\displaystyle u_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c7425f9c7ab645587060423c0af62f8a61fbc65) is along the horizontal direction. The number of molecules arriving at the area ![Image 119: {\displaystyle dA}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da1d35422f3d3b3cfcfd9b9aebe9176599fed24d) on one side of the gas layer, with speed ![Image 120: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) at angle ![Image 121: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) from the normal, in time interval ![Image 122: {\displaystyle dt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ebee76a835701fd1f26047a09855f2ea36bb08fc) is ![Image 123: {\displaystyle nv\cos({\theta })\,dA\,dt\times \left({\frac {m}{2\pi k_{\mathrm {B} }T}}\right)^{3/2}\,e^{-{\frac {mv^{2}}{2k_{\mathrm {B} }T}}}(v^{2}\sin {\theta }\,dv\,d\theta \,d\phi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b33500438972de90f365d5bc1160aa8818cadf1c)

These molecules made their last collision at ![Image 124: {\displaystyle y=\pm \ell \cos \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/996da4a56a8cdda1021513559cb35a493e6adb9e), where ![Image 125: {\displaystyle \ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f066e981e530bacc07efc6a10fa82deee985929e) is the [mean free path](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_free_path#Kinetic_theory "Mean free path"). Each molecule will contribute a forward momentum of ![Image 126: {\displaystyle p_{x}^{\pm }=m\left(u_{0}\pm \ell \cos \theta {\frac {du}{dy}}\right),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/245e9c16e8b1fc583063efd66c5c4b1a8fe6f456) where plus sign applies to molecules from above, and minus sign below. Note that the forward velocity gradient ![Image 127: {\displaystyle du/dy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/128cb53bd4b702d840ae52abfe488684a8aa3d31) can be considered to be constant over a distance of mean free path.

Integrating over all appropriate velocities within the constraint ![Image 128: {\displaystyle v>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c314fc908a83c555d34968d25e86c5ae0b76ef6f), ![Image 129: {\textstyle 0<\theta <{\frac {\pi }{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef78a27752a1f9a51d3b66c7863740f477aa4756), ![Image 130: {\displaystyle 0<\phi <2\pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c394e57781f81a012c452065aa8b7a81367c489) yields the forward momentum transfer per unit time per unit area (also known as [shear stress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shear_stress "Shear stress")): ![Image 131: {\displaystyle \tau ^{\pm }={\frac {1}{4}}{\bar {v}}n\cdot m\left(u_{0}\pm {\frac {2}{3}}\ell {\frac {du}{dy}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8cd7e6d81ea4ca76dedd5ada042b3bda105544af)

The net rate of momentum per unit area that is transported across the imaginary surface is thus ![Image 132: {\displaystyle \tau =\tau ^{+}-\tau ^{-}={\frac {1}{3}}{\bar {v}}nm\cdot \ell {\frac {du}{dy}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5daed38cdfa773dfaf90769f0490e3536687d2fa)

Combining the above kinetic equation with [Newton's law of viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_law_of_viscosity "Newton's law of viscosity")![Image 133: {\displaystyle \tau =\eta {\frac {du}{dy}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47d996d2ba3d016df88a754a0db04b8caf71f8a8) gives the equation for shear viscosity, which is usually denoted ![Image 134: {\displaystyle \eta _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef3886c3f161b7f02d23221bfead21a18ffb82ff) when it is a dilute gas: ![Image 135: {\displaystyle \eta _{0}={\frac {1}{3}}{\bar {v}}nm\ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/073bb1c233a88f8fce5e5087391abea130cd8cba)

Combining this equation with the equation for mean free path gives ![Image 136: {\displaystyle \eta _{0}={\frac {1}{3{\sqrt {2}}}}{\frac {m{\bar {v}}}{\sigma }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/910e8a607919e02c73ecd3e0f39259a32b427fc1)

Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution gives the average (equilibrium) molecular speed as ![Image 137: {\displaystyle {\bar {v}}={\frac {2}{\sqrt {\pi }}}v_{p}=2{\sqrt {{\frac {2}{\pi }}{\frac {k_{\mathrm {B} }T}{m}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/690ce5f19dc22c4a15baa8b57722c45f7d47ac5e) where ![Image 138: {\displaystyle v_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/537e72b1a70774ae976de89f7919dc0e0a9bb86d) is the most probable speed. We note that ![Image 139: {\displaystyle k_{\text{B}}N_{\text{A}}=R\quad {\text{and}}\quad M=mN_{\text{A}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eab235109826357566a8b2358e8e46c78d3d42dd)

and insert the velocity in the viscosity equation above. This gives the well known equation [[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-Hildebrand1976-44) (with ![Image 140: {\displaystyle \sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/59f59b7c3e6fdb1d0365a494b81fb9a696138c36) subsequently estimated below) for [shear viscosity for dilute gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscosity_models_for_mixtures#Dilute_gas_limit_and_scaled_variables "Viscosity models for mixtures"): ![Image 141: {\displaystyle \eta _{0}={\frac {2}{3{\sqrt {\pi }}}}\cdot {\frac {\sqrt {mk_{\mathrm {B} }T}}{\sigma }}={\frac {2}{3{\sqrt {\pi }}}}\cdot {\frac {\sqrt {MRT}}{\sigma N_{\text{A}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b07b920d97041cff5c842edb9bf92926406e6b7c) and ![Image 142: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is the [molar mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molar_mass "Molar mass"). The equation above presupposes that the gas density is low (i.e. the pressure is low). This implies that the transport of momentum through the gas due to the translational motion of molecules is much larger than the transport due to momentum being transferred between molecules during collisions. The transfer of momentum between molecules is explicitly accounted for in [Revised Enskog theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revised_Enskog_theory "Revised Enskog theory"), which relaxes the requirement of a gas being dilute. The viscosity equation further presupposes that there is only one type of gas molecules, and that the gas molecules are perfect elastic and hard core particles of spherical shape. This assumption of elastic, hard core spherical molecules, like billiard balls, implies that the collision cross section of one molecule can be estimated by ![Image 143: {\displaystyle \sigma =\pi \left(2r\right)^{2}=\pi d^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fafbcc9ff591bf8451497ee853e6d7741373510d)

The radius ![Image 144: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) is called collision cross section radius or kinetic radius, and the diameter ![Image 145: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab) is called collision cross section diameter or [kinetic diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_diameter "Kinetic diameter") of a molecule in a monomolecular gas. There are no simple general relation between the collision [cross section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_section_(physics)#Collision_among_particles "Cross section (physics)") and the hard core size of the (fairly spherical) molecule. The relation depends on shape of the potential energy of the molecule. For a real spherical molecule (i.e. a noble gas atom or a reasonably spherical molecule) the interaction potential is more like the [Lennard-Jones potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lennard-Jones_potential "Lennard-Jones potential") or [Morse potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morse_potential "Morse potential") which have a negative part that attracts the other molecule from distances longer than the hard core radius. [The radius for zero Lennard-Jones potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_free_path#Mean_free_path_in_kinetic_theory "Mean free path") may then be used as a rough estimate for the kinetic radius. However, using this estimate will typically lead to an erroneous temperature dependency of the viscosity. For such interaction potentials, significantly more accurate results are obtained by numerical evaluation of the required [collision integrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_section_(physics) "Cross section (physics)").

The expression for viscosity obtained from [Revised Enskog Theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revised_Enskog_theory "Revised Enskog theory") reduces to the above expression in the limit of infinite dilution, and can be written as ![Image 146: {\displaystyle \eta =(1+\alpha _{\eta })\eta _{0}+\eta _{c}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bec1c059ce75874a6ee66a057e339981acff0a99) where ![Image 147: {\displaystyle \alpha _{\eta }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bed7a1d516001a7aa52282c98c953eb0cd308142) is a term that tends to zero in the limit of infinite dilution that accounts for excluded volume, and ![Image 148: {\displaystyle \eta _{c}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9cf6da182347dff3657dba35cc4add274134e352) is a term accounting for the transfer of momentum over a non-zero distance between particles during a collision.

### Thermal conductivity and heat flux

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kinetic_theory_of_gases&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Thermal conductivity and heat flux")]

Following a similar logic as above, one can derive the kinetic model for [thermal conductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_conductivity "Thermal conductivity")[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-Sears1975-43) of a dilute gas:

Consider two parallel plates separated by a gas layer. Both plates have uniform temperatures, and are so massive compared to the gas layer that they can be treated as [thermal reservoirs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_reservoir "Thermal reservoir"). The upper plate has a higher temperature than the lower plate. The molecules in the gas layer have a molecular kinetic energy ![Image 149: {\displaystyle \varepsilon }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a30c89172e5b88edbd45d3e2772c7f5e562e5173) which increases uniformly with distance ![Image 150: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) above the lower plate. The non-equilibrium energy flow is superimposed on a [Maxwell-Boltzmann equilibrium distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell-Boltzmann_distribution "Maxwell-Boltzmann distribution") of molecular motions.

Let ![Image 151: {\displaystyle \varepsilon _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/acb0a8377db20e42274444cb181d51b5532b5844) be the molecular kinetic energy of the gas at an imaginary horizontal surface inside the gas layer. The number of molecules arriving at an area ![Image 152: {\displaystyle dA}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da1d35422f3d3b3cfcfd9b9aebe9176599fed24d) on one side of the gas layer, with speed ![Image 153: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) at angle ![Image 154: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) from the normal, in time interval ![Image 155: {\displaystyle dt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ebee76a835701fd1f26047a09855f2ea36bb08fc) is ![Image 156: {\displaystyle nv\cos(\theta )\,dA\,dt\times \left({\frac {m}{2\pi k_{\mathrm {B} }T}}\right)^{3/2}e^{-{\frac {mv^{2}}{2k_{\text{B}}T}}}(v^{2}\sin(\theta )\,dv\,d\theta \,d\phi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f3ee8a7dd5a5ee94e25d42d9439e2a9832a1887)

These molecules made their last collision at a distance ![Image 157: {\displaystyle \ell \cos \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee992c97c70a011801ec6ed35aee1d94596f6b04) above and below the gas layer, and each will contribute a molecular kinetic energy of ![Image 158: {\displaystyle \varepsilon ^{\pm }=\left(\varepsilon _{0}\pm mc_{v}\ell \cos \theta \,{\frac {dT}{dy}}\right),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cca2bd20214d2767d50477346ceecf6b39cc257a) where ![Image 159: {\displaystyle c_{v}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1fd3e4dfd14610335b7e1fe0a498410dba4f0e6f) is the [specific heat capacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_heat_capacity "Specific heat capacity"). Again, plus sign applies to molecules from above, and minus sign below. Note that the temperature gradient ![Image 160: {\displaystyle dT/dy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/847e76cf562ad670573a7299c10acb7c65316a48) can be considered to be constant over a distance of mean free path.

Integrating over all appropriate velocities within the constraint ![Image 161: {\displaystyle v>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c314fc908a83c555d34968d25e86c5ae0b76ef6f), ![Image 162: {\textstyle 0<\theta <{\frac {\pi }{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef78a27752a1f9a51d3b66c7863740f477aa4756), ![Image 163: {\displaystyle 0<\phi <2\pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c394e57781f81a012c452065aa8b7a81367c489) yields the energy transfer per unit time per unit area (also known as [heat flux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_flux "Heat flux")): ![Image 164: {\displaystyle q_{y}^{\pm }=-{\frac {1}{4}}{\bar {v}}n\cdot \left(\varepsilon _{0}\pm {\frac {2}{3}}mc_{v}\ell {\frac {dT}{dy}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc35ef77f1515bc572abdf03105c668c6bb0e432)

Note that the energy transfer from above is in the ![Image 165: {\displaystyle -y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a22a3cf60e7d3ecd010a1011cb95f95ddcbb39c) direction, and therefore the overall minus sign in the equation. The net heat flux across the imaginary surface is thus ![Image 166: {\displaystyle q=q_{y}^{+}-q_{y}^{-}=-{\frac {1}{3}}{\bar {v}}nmc_{v}\ell \,{\frac {dT}{dy}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/327c2b751fe8d80bdb28106434960026a1c197ad)

Combining the above kinetic equation with [Fourier's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier%27s_law "Fourier's law")![Image 167: {\displaystyle q=-\kappa \,{\frac {dT}{dy}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5197b77b8e04ac4dfac5f054b47567bee17d907) gives the equation for thermal conductivity, which is usually denoted ![Image 168: {\displaystyle \kappa _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/118936f5b31aae8a7ffb08db99b00f70d4411907) when it is a dilute gas: ![Image 169: {\displaystyle \kappa _{0}={\frac {1}{3}}{\bar {v}}nmc_{v}\ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ad93a16a3af3267eeff6ec82b68b4a5b965d25e)

Similarly to viscosity, [Revised Enskog theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revised_Enskog_theory "Revised Enskog theory") yields an expression for thermal conductivity that reduces to the above expression in the limit of infinite dilution, and which can be written as ![Image 170: {\displaystyle \kappa =\alpha _{\kappa }\kappa _{0}+\kappa _{c}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3da9201204b41c2594c3bbd39946394d33c87cfc) where ![Image 171: {\displaystyle \alpha _{\kappa }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f57f3189a59a752d8e0253afa7db9138bc2556e0) is a term that tends to unity in the limit of infinite dilution, accounting for excluded volume, and ![Image 172: {\displaystyle \kappa _{c}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/693828339c432c8d867d6148d15a8903b9bd3b5c) is a term accounting for the transfer of energy across a non-zero distance between particles during a collision.

### Diffusion coefficient and diffusion flux

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kinetic_theory_of_gases&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Diffusion coefficient and diffusion flux")]

Following a similar logic as above, one can derive the kinetic model for [mass diffusivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_diffusivity "Mass diffusivity")[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-Sears1975-43) of a dilute gas:

Consider a [steady](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steady_state "Steady state") diffusion between two regions of the same gas with perfectly flat and parallel boundaries separated by a layer of the same gas. Both regions have uniform [number densities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_density "Number density"), but the upper region has a higher number density than the lower region. In the steady state, the number density at any point is constant (that is, independent of time). However, the number density ![Image 173: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) in the layer increases uniformly with distance ![Image 174: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) above the lower plate. The non-equilibrium molecular flow is superimposed on a [Maxwell–Boltzmann equilibrium distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%E2%80%93Boltzmann_distribution "Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution") of molecular motions.

Let ![Image 175: {\displaystyle n_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/63584d203ecb012a7bcb90f422408bbfe4018956) be the number density of the gas at an imaginary horizontal surface inside the layer. The number of molecules arriving at an area ![Image 176: {\displaystyle dA}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da1d35422f3d3b3cfcfd9b9aebe9176599fed24d) on one side of the gas layer, with speed ![Image 177: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) at angle ![Image 178: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) from the normal, in time interval ![Image 179: {\displaystyle dt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ebee76a835701fd1f26047a09855f2ea36bb08fc) is ![Image 180: {\displaystyle nv\cos(\theta )\,dA\,dt\times \left({\frac {m}{2\pi k_{\mathrm {B} }T}}\right)^{3/2}e^{-{\frac {mv^{2}}{2k_{\text{B}}T}}}(v^{2}\sin(\theta )\,dv\,d\theta \,d\phi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f3ee8a7dd5a5ee94e25d42d9439e2a9832a1887)

These molecules made their last collision at a distance ![Image 181: {\displaystyle \ell \cos \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee992c97c70a011801ec6ed35aee1d94596f6b04) above and below the gas layer, where the local number density is ![Image 182: {\displaystyle n^{\pm }=\left(n_{0}\pm \ell \cos \theta \,{\frac {dn}{dy}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1c225f849840a063f44dc22d9a55e8ca9af2e8ce)

Again, plus sign applies to molecules from above, and minus sign below. Note that the number density gradient ![Image 183: {\displaystyle dn/dy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31619df270fb46807aa2219668e6c781249330aa) can be considered to be constant over a distance of mean free path.

Integrating over all appropriate velocities within the constraint ![Image 184: {\displaystyle v>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c314fc908a83c555d34968d25e86c5ae0b76ef6f), ![Image 185: {\textstyle 0<\theta <{\frac {\pi }{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef78a27752a1f9a51d3b66c7863740f477aa4756), ![Image 186: {\displaystyle 0<\phi <2\pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c394e57781f81a012c452065aa8b7a81367c489) yields the molecular transfer per unit time per unit area (also known as [diffusion flux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffusion_flux "Diffusion flux")): ![Image 187: {\displaystyle J_{y}^{\pm }=-{\frac {1}{4}}{\bar {v}}\cdot \left(n_{0}\pm {\frac {2}{3}}\ell \,{\frac {dn}{dy}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/74220f7659c58e35e5ddbb32335419d9a1eb235d)

Note that the molecular transfer from above is in the ![Image 188: {\displaystyle -y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a22a3cf60e7d3ecd010a1011cb95f95ddcbb39c) direction, and therefore the overall minus sign in the equation. The net diffusion flux across the imaginary surface is thus ![Image 189: {\displaystyle J=J_{y}^{+}-J_{y}^{-}=-{\frac {1}{3}}{\bar {v}}\ell {\frac {dn}{dy}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a3b1fd5d6846eb4fd806c59049b87ba29154d4)

Combining the above kinetic equation with [Fick's first law of diffusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fick%27s_laws_of_diffusion#Fick's_first_law "Fick's laws of diffusion")![Image 190: {\displaystyle J=-D{\frac {dn}{dy}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3628cda1a33cacc2d50c53df76f870681a61189e) gives the equation for mass diffusivity, which is usually denoted ![Image 191: {\displaystyle D_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b943acf3ade50743482c4ee4570ad3de335d90d8) when it is a dilute gas: ![Image 192: {\displaystyle D_{0}={\frac {1}{3}}{\bar {v}}\ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae2114cccf40ee42fb29ec0e8cf4b1e69cb0db87)

The corresponding expression obtained from [Revised Enskog Theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revised_Enskog_theory "Revised Enskog theory") may be written as ![Image 193: {\displaystyle D=\alpha _{D}D_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b3861aba080112a4c11ba64542ba07d195599fe) where ![Image 194: {\displaystyle \alpha _{D}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6ff358ba1ce44034e9154949e297008ac02fa3da) is a factor that tends to unity in the limit of infinite dilution, which accounts for excluded volume and the variation [chemical potentials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_potential "Chemical potential") with density.

### Fluctuation and dissipation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kinetic_theory_of_gases&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Fluctuation and dissipation")]

The kinetic theory of gases entails that due to the [microscopic reversibility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microscopic_reversibility "Microscopic reversibility") of the gas particles' detailed dynamics, the system must obey the principle of [detailed balance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detailed_balance "Detailed balance"). Specifically, the [fluctuation-dissipation theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluctuation-dissipation_theorem "Fluctuation-dissipation theorem") applies to the [Brownian motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brownian_motion "Brownian motion") (or [diffusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffusion "Diffusion")) and the [drag force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drag_(physics) "Drag (physics)"), which leads to the [Einstein–Smoluchowski equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_relation_(kinetic_theory) "Einstein relation (kinetic theory)"):[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases#cite_note-45)![Image 195: {\displaystyle D=\mu \,k_{\text{B}}T,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4bc6c2727e74ee03d1375e6b798c779b1df765c6) where

*   D is the [mass diffusivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_diffusivity "Mass diffusivity");
*   μ is the "mobility", or the ratio of the particle's [terminal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terminal_velocity "Terminal velocity")[drift velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drift_velocity "Drift velocity") to an applied [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force"), _μ_ = _v_ d/_F_;
*   _k_ B is the [Boltzmann constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_constant "Boltzmann constant");
*   T is the [absolute temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_temperature "Absolute temperature").

Note that the mobility _μ_ = _v_ d/_F_ can be calculated based on the viscosity of the gas; Therefore, the Einstein–Smoluchowski equation also provides a relation between the mass diffusivity and the viscosity of the gas.

### Onsager reciprocal relations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kinetic_theory_of_gases&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Onsager reciprocal relations")]

The mathematical similarities between the expressions for shear viscocity, thermal conductivity and diffusion coefficient of the ideal (dilute) gas is not a coincidence; It is a direct result of the [Onsager reciprocal relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Onsager_reciprocal_relations "Onsager reciprocal relations") (i.e. the detailed balance of the [reversible dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microscopic_reversibility "Microscopic reversibility") of the particles), when applied to the [convection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convection "Convection") (matter flow due to temperature gradient, and heat flow due to pressure gradient) and [advection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advection#Distinction_between_advection_and_convection "Advection") (matter flow due to the velocity of particles, and momentum transfer due to pressure gradient) of the ideal (dilute) gas.
