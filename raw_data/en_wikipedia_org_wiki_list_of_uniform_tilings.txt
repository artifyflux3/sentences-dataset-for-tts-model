This table shows the 11 convex [uniform tilings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_tiling "Uniform tiling") (regular and semiregular) of the [Euclidean plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry"), and their dual tilings.

There are three regular and eight semiregular [tilings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tiling_by_regular_polygons "Tiling by regular polygons") in the plane. The semiregular tilings form new tilings from their duals, each made from one type of irregular face.

[John Conway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Horton_Conway "John Horton Conway") called these uniform duals _**Catalan tilings**_, in parallel to the [Catalan solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalan_solid "Catalan solid") polyhedra.

Uniform tilings are listed by their [vertex configuration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_configuration "Vertex configuration"), the sequence of faces that exist on each vertex. For example _4.8.8_ means one square and two octagons on a vertex.

These 11 uniform tilings have 32 different _[uniform colorings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform\_coloring "Uniform coloring")_. A uniform coloring allows identical sided polygons at a vertex to be colored differently, while still maintaining vertex-uniformity and transformational congruence between vertices. (Note: Some of the tiling images shown below are _not_ color-uniform.)

In addition to the 11 convex uniform tilings, there are also [14 known nonconvex tilings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_tiling#Expanded_lists_of_uniform_tilings "Uniform tiling"), using [star polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_polygon "Star polygon"), and reverse orientation vertex configurations. A further 28 uniform tilings are known using [apeirogons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apeirogon "Apeirogon"). If zigzags are also allowed, there are 23 more known uniform tilings and 10 more known families depending on a parameter: in 8 cases the parameter is continuous, and in the other 2 it is discrete. The set is not known to be complete.

In the 1987 book, _[Tilings and patterns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tilings\_and\_patterns "Tilings and patterns")_, [Branko Grünbaum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Branko_Gr%C3%BCnbaum "Branko Grünbaum") calls the vertex-uniform tilings _**Archimedean**_, in parallel to the [Archimedean solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedean_solid "Archimedean solid"). Their [dual tilings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_graph "Dual graph") are called _**Laves tilings**_ in honor of [crystallographer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography "Crystallography")[Fritz Laves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fritz_Laves "Fritz Laves").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_uniform_tilings#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_uniform_tilings#cite_note-2) They're also called **Shubnikov–Laves tilings** after [Aleksei Shubnikov](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexei_Vasilievich_Shubnikov "Alexei Vasilievich Shubnikov").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_uniform_tilings#cite_note-3)[John Conway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Horton_Conway "John Horton Conway") called the uniform duals _**Catalan tilings**_, in parallel to the [Catalan solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalan_solid "Catalan solid") polyhedra.

The Laves tilings have vertices at the centers of the regular polygons, and edges connecting centers of regular polygons that share an edge. The [tiles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prototile "Prototile") of the Laves tilings are called _[planigons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planigon "Planigon")_. This includes the 3 regular tiles (triangle, square and hexagon) and 8 irregular ones.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_uniform_tilings#cite_note-4) Each vertex has edges evenly spaced around it. Three dimensional analogues of the _planigons_ are called [stereohedrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stereohedron "Stereohedron").

These dual tilings are listed by their [face configuration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_configuration "Face configuration"), the number of faces at each vertex of a face. For example _V4.8.8_ means isosceles triangle tiles with one corner with four triangles, and two corners containing eight triangles. The orientations of the vertex planigons (up to [D 12](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihedral_group "Dihedral group")) are consistent with the vertex diagrams in the below sections.

Convex uniform tilings of the Euclidean plane
---------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=List_of_Euclidean_uniform_tilings&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Convex uniform tilings of the Euclidean plane")]

All reflectional forms can be made by [Wythoff constructions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wythoff_construction "Wythoff construction"), represented by [Wythoff symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wythoff_symbol "Wythoff symbol"), or [Coxeter-Dynkin diagrams](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter-Dynkin_diagram "Coxeter-Dynkin diagram"), each operating upon one of three [Schwarz triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schwarz_triangle#Triangles_for_the_Euclidean_plane "Schwarz triangle") (4,4,2), (6,3,2), or (3,3,3), with symmetry represented by [Coxeter groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group "Coxeter group"): [4,4], [6,3], or [3[3]]. [Alternated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternation_(geometry) "Alternation (geometry)") forms such as the snub can also be represented by special markups within each system. Only one uniform tiling can't be constructed by a Wythoff process, but can be made by an [elongation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elongation_(geometry) "Elongation (geometry)") of the triangular tiling. An orthogonal mirror construction [∞,2,∞] also exists, seen as two sets of parallel mirrors making a rectangular fundamental domain. If the domain is square, this symmetry can be doubled by a diagonal mirror into the [4,4] family.
