[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b8/Laser_Towards_Milky_Ways_Centre.jpg/500px-Laser_Towards_Milky_Ways_Centre.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Laser_Towards_Milky_Ways_Centre.jpg)

The [Paranal Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paranal_Observatory "Paranal Observatory") of [European Southern Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Southern_Observatory "European Southern Observatory") shooting a [laser guide star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser_guide_star "Laser guide star") to the [Galactic Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_Center "Galactic Center")

**Astronomy** is a [natural science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_science "Natural science") that studies [celestial objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_object "Astronomical object") and the [phenomena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenomena "Phenomena") that occur in the [cosmos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmos "Cosmos"). It uses [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), and [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry") to explain their origin and their overall [evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronology_of_the_Universe "Chronology of the Universe"). Objects of interest include [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planets "Planets"), [moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_satellite "Natural satellite"), [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star"), [nebulae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nebulae "Nebulae"), [galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy"), [meteoroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteoroid "Meteoroid"), [asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid "Asteroid"), and [comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet "Comet"). Relevant phenomena include [supernova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova "Supernova") explosions, [gamma ray bursts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_ray_burst "Gamma ray burst"), [quasars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasar "Quasar"), [blazars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blazar "Blazar"), [pulsars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulsar "Pulsar"), and [cosmic microwave background radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation "Cosmic microwave background radiation"). More generally, astronomy studies everything that originates beyond [Earth's atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth "Atmosphere of Earth"). [Cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmology "Cosmology") is the branch of astronomy that studies the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe") as a whole.

Astronomy is one of the oldest natural sciences. The early civilizations in [recorded history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recorded_history "Recorded history") made methodical observations of the [night sky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Night_sky "Night sky"). These include the [Egyptians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptian_astronomy "Egyptian astronomy"), [Babylonians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylonian_astronomy "Babylonian astronomy"), [Greeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_astronomy "Greek astronomy"), [Indians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_astronomy "Indian astronomy"), [Chinese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_astronomy "Chinese astronomy"), [Maya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maya_civilization "Maya civilization"), and many ancient [indigenous peoples of the Americas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas "Indigenous peoples of the Americas"). In the past, astronomy included disciplines as diverse as [astrometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrometry "Astrometry"), [celestial navigation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_navigation "Celestial navigation"), [observational astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observational_astronomy "Observational astronomy"), and the making of [calendars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calendar "Calendar").

Professional astronomy is split into [observational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observational_astronomy "Observational astronomy") and [theoretical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoretical_astronomy "Theoretical astronomy") branches. Observational astronomy is focused on acquiring data from observations of astronomical objects. This data is then analyzed using basic principles of physics. Theoretical astronomy is oriented toward the development of computer or analytical models to describe astronomical objects and phenomena. These two fields complement each other. Theoretical astronomy seeks to explain observational results and observations are used to confirm theoretical results.

Astronomy is one of the few sciences in which amateurs play an [active role](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citizen_science "Citizen science"). This is especially true for the discovery and observation of [transient events](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transient_astronomical_event "Transient astronomical event"). [Amateur astronomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amateur_astronomy "Amateur astronomy") have helped with many important discoveries, such as finding new comets.

Etymology
---------

_Astronomy_ (from the [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek")[ἀστρονομία](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%80%CF%83%CF%84%CF%81%CE%BF%CE%BD%CE%BF%CE%BC%CE%AF%CE%B1#Ancient_Greek "wikt:ἀστρονομία") from [ἄστρον](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BC%84%CF%83%CF%84%CF%81%CE%BF%CE%BD#Ancient_Greek "wikt:ἄστρον")_astron_, "star" and -νομία _[-nomia](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-nomy "wikt:-nomy")_ from [νόμος](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BD%CF%8C%CE%BC%CE%BF%CF%82#Ancient_Greek "wikt:νόμος")_nomos_, "law" or "rule") means study of celestial objects.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-1) Astronomy should not be confused with [astrology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrology "Astrology"), the belief system which claims that human affairs are correlated with the positions of celestial objects. The [two fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrology_and_astronomy "Astrology and astronomy") share a common origin but became distinct, astronomy being supported by [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") while astrology is not.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-2)

### Use of terms "astronomy" and "astrophysics"

"Astronomy" and "[astrophysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysics "Astrophysics")" are broadly synonymous in modern usage.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-scharrinhausen-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-odenwald-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-pennstateerie-5) In dictionary definitions, "astronomy" is "the study of objects and matter outside the Earth's atmosphere and of their physical and chemical properties",[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-mw-astronomy-6) while "astrophysics" is the branch of astronomy dealing with "the behavior, physical properties, and dynamic processes of celestial objects and phenomena".[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-mw-astrophysics-7) Sometimes, as in the introduction of the introductory textbook _The Physical Universe_ by [Frank Shu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_Shu "Frank Shu"), "astronomy" means the qualitative study of the subject, whereas "astrophysics" is the physics-oriented version of the subject.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-shu1982-8) Some fields, such as [astrometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrometry "Astrometry"), are in this sense purely astronomy rather than also astrophysics. Research departments may use "astronomy" and "astrophysics" according to whether the department is historically affiliated with a physics department,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-odenwald-4) and many professional [astronomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomer "Astronomer") have physics rather than astronomy degrees.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-pennstateerie-5) Thus, in modern use, the two terms are often used interchangeably.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-scharrinhausen-3)

History
-------

### Pre-historic

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1d/Nebra_solstice_2.jpg/250px-Nebra_solstice_2.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Nebra_solstice_2.jpg)

The [Nebra sky disc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nebra_sky_disc "Nebra sky disc") (c. 1800–1600 BCE), found near a possibly [astronomical complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_complex "Astronomical complex"), most likely depicting the Sun or full Moon, the Moon as a crescent, the [Pleiades](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pleiades "Pleiades") and the summer and winter solstices as strips of gold on the side of the disc,[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Meller_2021-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-10) with the top representing the [horizon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horizon "Horizon")[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-:03-11) and [north](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North "North").

In prehistoric times, people created artefacts such as the [Nebra sky disc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nebra_sky_disc "Nebra sky disc") which serves as an astronomical calendar, defining a year as twelve [lunar months](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_month "Lunar month"), 354 days, with intercalary months to make up the solar year. The disc is inlaid with symbols interpreted as a sun, moon, and stars including [a cluster of seven stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pleiades "Pleiades").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Meller_2021-9)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Halle-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-13)

### Classical

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Ct-33-planisphere.jpg/250px-Ct-33-planisphere.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ct-33-planisphere.jpg)

A Babylonian [planisphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planisphere "Planisphere") (7th century BCE). [Babylonian astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylonian_astronomy "Babylonian astronomy") was an early astronomical instrument. Its use of [sexagesimals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexagesimal "Sexagesimal") (e.g. 12, 24, 60, 360) is still being used today through having been broadly adopted for [timekeeping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timekeeping "Timekeeping") and [astrometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrometry "Astrometry").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-x754-14)

Civilizations such as [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptian_astronomy "Egyptian astronomy"), [Mesopotamia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylonian_astronomy "Babylonian astronomy"), [Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_astronomy "Greek astronomy"), [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_astronomy "Indian astronomy"), [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_astronomy "Chinese astronomy") together – with cross-cultural influences – created astronomical observatories and developed ideas on the nature of the Universe, along with calendars and astronomical instruments.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Sarma_2000-15) A key early development was the beginning of mathematical and scientific astronomy among the Babylonians, laying the foundations for astronomical traditions in other civilizations.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-16) The Babylonians discovered that [lunar eclipses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_eclipses "Lunar eclipses") recurred in the [saros cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saros_cycle "Saros cycle") of 223 [synodic months](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synodic_month "Synodic month").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-17)

Following the Babylonians, significant advances were made in [ancient Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece") and the [Hellenistic world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hellenistic_civilization "Hellenistic civilization"). Greek astronomy sought a rational, physical explanation for celestial phenomena.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-18) In the 3rd century BC, [Aristarchus of Samos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristarchus_of_Samos "Aristarchus of Samos") estimated the [size and distance of the Moon and Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristarchus_On_the_Sizes_and_Distances "Aristarchus On the Sizes and Distances"), and he proposed a model of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") where the Earth and planets rotated around the Sun, now called the [heliocentric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliocentrism "Heliocentrism") model.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-19) In the 2nd century BC, [Hipparchus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hipparchus "Hipparchus") discovered [precession](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precession "Precession"), calculated the size and distance of the Moon and invented the earliest known astronomical devices such as the [astrolabe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrolabe "Astrolabe").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-20) Hipparchus also created a catalog of 1020 stars, and most of the [constellations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constellation "Constellation") of the northern hemisphere derive from Greek astronomy.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-21) The [Antikythera mechanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antikythera_mechanism "Antikythera mechanism") (c. 150–80 BC) was an early [analog computer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analog_computer "Analog computer") designed to calculate the location of the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun"), [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon"), and [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planets "Planets") for a given date. Technological artifacts of similar complexity did not reappear until the 14th century, when mechanical [astronomical clocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_clock "Astronomical clock") appeared in Europe.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-insearchoflosttime-22)

After the classical Greek era, astronomy was dominated by the [geocentric model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geocentric_model "Geocentric model") of the Universe, or the [Ptolemaic system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemaic_system "Ptolemaic system"), named after [Ptolemy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemy "Ptolemy"). In this system, the Earth was believed to be the center of the Universe with the Sun, the Moon and the stars rotating around it.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-23)

### Post-classical

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Al-_Farg%C4%81n%C4%AB%2C_A%E1%B8%A5mad_ibn_Mu%E1%B8%A5ammad_%E2%80%93_Compilatio_astronomica%2C_1493_%E2%80%93_BEIC_13262685.jpg/250px-Al-_Farg%C4%81n%C4%AB%2C_A%E1%B8%A5mad_ibn_Mu%E1%B8%A5ammad_%E2%80%93_Compilatio_astronomica%2C_1493_%E2%80%93_BEIC_13262685.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Al-_Farg%C4%81n%C4%AB,_A%E1%B8%A5mad_ibn_Mu%E1%B8%A5ammad_%E2%80%93_Compilatio_astronomica,_1493_%E2%80%93_BEIC_13262685.jpg)

Portrait of [Alfraganus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfraganus "Alfraganus") in the _Compilatio astronomica_, 1493. [Islamic astronomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_astronomy "Islamic astronomy") collected and translated [Indian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_astronomy "Indian astronomy"), [Persian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Persian_astronomy "Persian astronomy") and [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_astronomy "Greek astronomy") texts, adding their own work.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-n063-24)

[Astronomy flourished in the medieval Islamic world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy_in_the_medieval_Islamic_world "Astronomy in the medieval Islamic world"). Astronomical [observatories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observatory "Observatory") were established there by the early 9th century.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Kennedy-1962-25)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Micheau-992-3-26)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-27) In 964, the [Andromeda Galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy "Andromeda Galaxy"), the largest [galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy") in the [Local Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_Group "Local Group"), was described by the Persian Muslim astronomer [Abd al-Rahman al-Sufi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abd_al-Rahman_al-Sufi "Abd al-Rahman al-Sufi") in his _[Book of Fixed Stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Book\_of\_Fixed\_Stars "Book of Fixed Stars")_.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-NSOG-28) The [SN 1006](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SN_1006 "SN 1006")[supernova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova "Supernova"), the brightest [apparent magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_magnitude "Apparent magnitude") stellar event in the last 1000 years, was observed by the Egyptian Arabic astronomer [Ali ibn Ridwan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ali_ibn_Ridwan "Ali ibn Ridwan") and [Chinese astronomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_astronomy "Chinese astronomy") in 1006.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-29) Iranian scholar [Al-Biruni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Biruni "Al-Biruni") observed that, contrary to [Ptolemy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptolemy "Ptolemy"), the Sun's [apogee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apogee "Apogee") (highest point in the heavens) was mobile, not fixed.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Goldstein1967-30)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-31) Arabic astronomers introduced many [Arabic names now used for individual stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Arabic_star_names "List of Arabic star names").[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-32)

The ruins at [Great Zimbabwe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Zimbabwe "Great Zimbabwe") and [Timbuktu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timbuktu "Timbuktu")[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-33) may have housed astronomical observatories.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-34) In Post-classical West Africa, astronomers studied the movement of stars and relation to seasons, crafting charts of the heavens and diagrams of orbits of the other planets based on complex mathematical calculations. [Songhai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Songhai_Empire "Songhai Empire") historian [Mahmud Kati](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mahmud_Kati "Mahmud Kati") documented a [meteor shower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor_shower "Meteor shower") in 1583.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-35)[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-36)

In medieval Europe, [Richard of Wallingford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_of_Wallingford "Richard of Wallingford") (1292–1336) invented the first astronomical clock, the [Rectangulus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangulus "Rectangulus") which allowed for the measurement of angles between planets and other astronomical bodies,[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Gimpel_1992-37) as well as an [equatorium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equatorium "Equatorium") called the _Albion_ which could be used for astronomical calculations such as [lunar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon"), [solar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun") and [planetary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet")[longitudes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longitude "Longitude").[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-38)[Nicole Oresme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicole_Oresme "Nicole Oresme") (1320–1382) discussed evidence for the rotation of the Earth.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-39)[Jean Buridan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Buridan "Jean Buridan") (1300–1361) developed the [theory of impetus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_impetus "Theory of impetus"), describing motions including of the celestial bodies.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-40)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-41) For over six centuries (from the recovery of ancient learning during the late Middle Ages into the Enlightenment), the [Roman Catholic Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Catholic_Church "Roman Catholic Church") gave more financial and social support to the study of astronomy than probably all other institutions. Among the Church's motives was finding the [date for Easter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Date_of_Easter "Date of Easter").[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-42)

### Early telescopic

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Galileo%27s_sketches_of_the_moon.png/250px-Galileo%27s_sketches_of_the_moon.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Galileo%27s_sketches_of_the_moon.png)

The first sketches of the Moon's topography, from [Galileo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galileo "Galileo")'s ground-breaking _[Sidereus Nuncius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sidereus\_Nuncius "Sidereus Nuncius")_ (1610)

During the [Renaissance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renaissance "Renaissance"), [Nicolaus Copernicus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolaus_Copernicus "Nicolaus Copernicus") proposed a heliocentric model of the solar system.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-43) His work was defended by [Galileo Galilei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galileo_Galilei "Galileo Galilei") and expanded upon by [Johannes Kepler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Kepler "Johannes Kepler").[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-44) Kepler was the first to devise a system that correctly described the details of the motion of the planets around the Sun. However, Kepler did not succeed in formulating a theory behind the laws he wrote down.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-45) It was [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton"), with his invention of [celestial dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_dynamics "Celestial dynamics") and his [law of gravitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity"), who finally explained the motions of the planets.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-f58-64-46) Newton also developed the [reflecting telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflecting_telescope "Reflecting telescope").[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-47)

The English astronomer [John Flamsteed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Flamsteed "John Flamsteed"), Britain's first [Astronomer Royal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomer_Royal "Astronomer Royal"), catalogued over 3000 stars.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-RMG_Flamsteed-48) More extensive star catalogues were produced by [Nicolas Louis de Lacaille](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolas_Louis_de_Lacaille "Nicolas Louis de Lacaille"). The astronomer [William Herschel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Herschel "William Herschel") made a detailed catalog of nebulosity and clusters, and in 1781 discovered the planet [Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus "Uranus"), the first new planet found.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-49)

During the 18–19th centuries, the study of the [three-body problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-body_problem "Three-body problem") by [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler"), [Alexis Claude Clairaut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexis_Claude_Clairaut "Alexis Claude Clairaut"), and [Jean le Rond d'Alembert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d%27Alembert "Jean le Rond d'Alembert") led to more accurate predictions about the motions of the Moon and planets. This work was further refined by [Joseph-Louis Lagrange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph-Louis_Lagrange "Joseph-Louis Lagrange") and [Pierre Simon Laplace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Simon_Laplace "Pierre Simon Laplace"), allowing the masses of the planets and moons to be estimated from their perturbations.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-50)

Significant advances in astronomy came about with the introduction of new technology, including the [spectroscope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscope "Spectroscope") and [astrophotography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophotography "Astrophotography"). In 1814–15, [Joseph von Fraunhofer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_von_Fraunhofer "Joseph von Fraunhofer") discovered some 574 [dark lines in the spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fraunhofer_lines "Fraunhofer lines") of the sun and of other stars.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Ferguson_Maciaszek_2014-51)[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-52) In 1859, [Gustav Kirchhoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustav_Kirchhoff "Gustav Kirchhoff") ascribed these lines to the presence of different elements.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-53)

### Deep space

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2f/Andromeda_Nebula_-_Isaac_Roberts%2C_29_December_1888_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Andromeda_Nebula_-_Isaac_Roberts%2C_29_December_1888_%28cropped%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Andromeda_Nebula_-_Isaac_Roberts,_29_December_1888_(cropped).jpg)

Photograph of the [Great Andromeda "Nebula"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy "Andromeda Galaxy") by [Isaac Roberts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Roberts "Isaac Roberts") in 1888. The 1923 calculation of its distance enabled calculation of the age and expansion of the [Universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe").

The existence of the Earth's galaxy, the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way"), as its own group of stars was only proven in the 20th century, along with the existence of other galaxies. The observed recession of those galaxies led to the discovery of the expansion of the [Universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe").[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Belkora2003-54) Using the Hooker Telescope, [Edwin Hubble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edwin_Hubble "Edwin Hubble") identified [Cepheid variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cepheid_variable "Cepheid variable") in several spiral nebulae and in 1922–1923 proved conclusively that [Andromeda Nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy "Andromeda Galaxy") and [Triangulum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangulum_Nebula "Triangulum Nebula") among others, were entire galaxies outside our own, thus proving that the universe consists of a multitude of galaxies.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-SharovNovikov1993-55) With this, Hubble formulated the [Hubble constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_constant "Hubble constant"), which enabled a calculation of the age of the Universe and the size of the Observable Universe. Such calculations became increasingly precise with better measurements, starting at 2 billion years and 280 million light-years, until 2006 when data of the [Hubble Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope "Hubble Space Telescope") supported very accurate calculations.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-p537-56)

Theoretical astronomy predicted the existence of objects such as [black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole "Black hole")[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Oppenheimer_Volkoff_1939-57) and [neutron stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_star "Neutron star").[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-58) These have been used to explain phenomena such as [quasars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasar "Quasar")[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Schmidt_1963-59) and [pulsars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulsar "Pulsar").[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Gold_1968-60) As for [physical cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_cosmology "Physical cosmology"), in the early 20th century the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang") theory of the universe was formulated, evidenced by [cosmic microwave background radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation "Cosmic microwave background radiation") and [Hubble's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble%27s_law "Hubble's law").[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-61)

[Space telescopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_telescope "Space telescope") have enabled measurements in parts of the electromagnetic spectrum normally blocked or blurred by the atmosphere.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-62) The [LIGO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LIGO "LIGO") project [detected evidence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves "First observation of gravitational waves") of [gravitational waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_waves "Gravitational waves") in 2015.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Discovery_2016-63)[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Abbot-64)

Observational astronomy
-----------------------

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Openstax_Astronomy_EM_spectrum_and_atmosphere.jpg/500px-Openstax_Astronomy_EM_spectrum_and_atmosphere.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Openstax_Astronomy_EM_spectrum_and_atmosphere.jpg)

Overview of types of [observational astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observational_astronomy "Observational astronomy"), relating wavelengths and their observability

The main source of information about [celestial bodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_body "Celestial body") and other objects is [visible light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visible_light "Visible light"), or more generally [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation").[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-65) Observational astronomy may be categorized according to the corresponding region of the [electromagnetic spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum "Electromagnetic spectrum") on which the observations are made. Specific information on these subfields is given below.

### Radio

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/USA.NM.VeryLargeArray.02.jpg/250px-USA.NM.VeryLargeArray.02.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:USA.NM.VeryLargeArray.02.jpg)

The [Very Large Array](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Very_Large_Array "Very Large Array") in [New Mexico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Mexico "New Mexico"), a [radio telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_telescope "Radio telescope")

Radio astronomy uses radiation with long [wavelengths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength "Wavelength"), mainly between 1 millimeter and 15 meters (frequencies from 20 MHz to 300 GHz), far outside the visible range.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-RAL-66)[Hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen"), otherwise an invisible gas, produces a [spectral line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line "Spectral line") at 21 cm (1420 MHz) which is observable at radio wavelengths.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-SKAO-67) Objects observable at radio wavelengths include interstellar gas,[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-SKAO-67)[pulsars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulsar "Pulsar"),[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-SKAO-67)[fast radio bursts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fast_radio_burst "Fast radio burst"),[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-SKAO-67)[supernovae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova "Supernova"),[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-68) and [active galactic nuclei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_galactic_nuclei "Active galactic nuclei").[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Radcliffe_Barthel_Garrett_2021-69)

### Infrared

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/71/The_Keck_Subaru_and_Infrared_obervatories.JPG/250px-The_Keck_Subaru_and_Infrared_obervatories.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:The_Keck_Subaru_and_Infrared_obervatories.JPG)

The [Subaru Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subaru_Telescope "Subaru Telescope") (left) and [Keck Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keck_Observatory "Keck Observatory") (center) on [Mauna Kea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mauna_Kea "Mauna Kea"), both observatories that operate at near-infrared and visible wavelengths. The [NASA Infrared Telescope Facility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA_Infrared_Telescope_Facility "NASA Infrared Telescope Facility") (right) is an example of a telescope that operates only at near-infrared wavelengths.

Infrared astronomy is founded on the detection and analysis of [infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared") radiation, wavelengths longer than red light and outside the range of our vision. The infrared spectrum is useful for studying objects that are too cold to radiate visible light, such as planets, [circumstellar disks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumstellar_disk "Circumstellar disk") or nebulae whose light is blocked by dust. The longer wavelengths of infrared can penetrate clouds of dust that block visible light, allowing the observation of young stars embedded in [molecular clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_cloud "Molecular cloud") and the cores of galaxies. Observations from the [Wide-field Infrared Survey Explorer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wide-field_Infrared_Survey_Explorer "Wide-field Infrared Survey Explorer") (WISE) have been particularly effective at unveiling numerous galactic [protostars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protostar "Protostar") and their host [star clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_clusters "Star clusters").[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-wright-70)[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-ma2013-71) With the exception of infrared [wavelengths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelengths "Wavelengths") close to visible light, such radiation is heavily absorbed by the atmosphere, or masked, as the atmosphere itself produces significant infrared emission. Consequently, infrared observatories have to be located in high, dry places on Earth or in space.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-72) Some molecules radiate strongly in the infrared. This allows the study of the chemistry of space.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-73)

### Optical

Historically, optical astronomy, which has been also called visible light astronomy, is the oldest form of astronomy.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-moore1997-74) Images of observations were originally drawn by hand. In the late 19th century and most of the 20th century, images were made using photographic equipment. Modern images are made using digital detectors, particularly using [charge-coupled devices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charge-coupled_device "Charge-coupled device") (CCDs) and recorded on modern medium. Although visible light itself extends from approximately 380 to 700 [nm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanometer "Nanometer")[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-75) that same equipment can be used to observe some [near-ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near-ultraviolet "Near-ultraviolet") and [near-infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near-infrared "Near-infrared") radiation.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-76)

### Ultraviolet

Ultraviolet astronomy employs [ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet") wavelengths which are absorbed by the Earth's atmosphere, requiring observations from the upper atmosphere or from space. Ultraviolet astronomy is best suited to the study of thermal radiation and spectral emission lines from hot blue [OB stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OB_star "OB star") that are very bright in this wave band.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-77)

### X-ray

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/B30727.jpg/250px-B30727.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:B30727.jpg)

X-ray jet made from a supermassive black hole found by NASA's Chandra X-ray Observatory, made visible by light from the early Universe

X-ray astronomy uses [X-radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-radiation "X-radiation"), produced by extremely hot and high-energy processes. Since X-rays are absorbed by the [Earth's atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere "Earth's atmosphere"), observations must be performed at high altitude, such as from [balloons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-altitude_balloon "High-altitude balloon"), [rockets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket "Rocket"), or [specialized satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_astronomy_satellite "X-ray astronomy satellite"). [X-ray sources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_X-ray_source "Astrophysical X-ray source") include [X-ray binaries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_binaries "X-ray binaries"), [supernova remnants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova_remnant "Supernova remnant"), [clusters of galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clusters_of_galaxies "Clusters of galaxies"), and [active galactic nuclei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_galactic_nuclei "Active galactic nuclei").[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Arnaud_2007-78)

### Gamma-ray

Gamma ray astronomy observes astronomical objects at the shortest wavelengths (highest energy) of the electromagnetic spectrum. [Gamma rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_ray "Gamma ray") may be observed directly by satellites such as the [Compton Gamma Ray Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compton_Gamma_Ray_Observatory "Compton Gamma Ray Observatory"),[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-79) or by specialized telescopes called [atmospheric Cherenkov telescopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_Cherenkov_telescope "Atmospheric Cherenkov telescope"). Cherenkov telescopes do not detect the gamma rays directly but instead detect the flashes of visible light produced when gamma rays are absorbed by the Earth's atmosphere.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-80)[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-spectrum-81)[Gamma-ray bursts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst "Gamma-ray burst"), which radiate transiently, are extremely energetic events, and are the brightest (most luminous) phenomena in the universe.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-82)

### Fields not based on the electromagnetic spectrum

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Antares_Neutrinoteleskop.jpg/250px-Antares_Neutrinoteleskop.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Antares_Neutrinoteleskop.jpg)

The underground [ANTARES neutrino telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ANTARES_(telescope) "ANTARES (telescope)")

Some events originating from great distances may be observed from the Earth using systems that do not rely on electromagnetic radiation.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Gaisser_1990-83)[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Abbott_2016-84)

In [neutrino astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino_astronomy "Neutrino astronomy"), astronomers use heavily shielded [underground facilities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino_observatory "Neutrino observatory") such as [SAGE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SAGE_(ruSsian_American_Gallium_Experiment) "SAGE (ruSsian American Gallium Experiment)"), [GALLEX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GALLEX "GALLEX"), and [Kamioka II/III](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kamioka_Observatory "Kamioka Observatory") for the detection of [neutrinos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino "Neutrino"). The vast majority of the neutrinos streaming through the Earth originate from the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun"), but 24 neutrinos were also detected from [supernova 1987A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova_1987A "Supernova 1987A"). [Cosmic rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_ray "Cosmic ray"), which consist of very high energy particles (atomic nuclei) that can decay or be absorbed when they enter the Earth's atmosphere, result in a cascade of secondary particles which can be detected by current observatories.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Gaisser_1990-83)

[Gravitational-wave astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational-wave_astronomy "Gravitational-wave astronomy") employs [gravitational-wave detectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational-wave_detector "Gravitational-wave detector") to collect observational data about distant massive objects. A few observatories have been constructed, such as the _Laser Interferometer Gravitational Observatory_[LIGO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LIGO "LIGO"). LIGO made its [first detection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_observation_of_gravitational_waves "First observation of gravitational waves") on 14 September 2015, observing gravitational waves from a [binary black hole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_black_hole "Binary black hole").[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Abbott_2016-84)[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-85) A second [gravitational wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_wave "Gravitational wave") was detected on 26 December 2015 and additional observations should continue but [gravitational waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_wave "Gravitational wave") require extremely sensitive instruments.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-86)[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-87)

The combination of observations made using electromagnetic radiation, neutrinos or gravitational waves and other complementary information, is known as [multi-messenger astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multi-messenger_astronomy "Multi-messenger astronomy").[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-88)[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-89)

### Astrometry and celestial mechanics

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Interferometric_astrometry.jpg/330px-Interferometric_astrometry.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Interferometric_astrometry.jpg)

Use of optical [interferometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interferometry "Interferometry") to determine precise positions of stars

One of the oldest fields in astronomy, and in all of science, is the measurement of the positions of celestial objects. Historically, accurate knowledge of the positions of the Sun, Moon, planets and stars has been essential in [celestial navigation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_navigation "Celestial navigation") (the use of celestial objects to guide navigation) and in the making of [calendars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calendar "Calendar").[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-:0-90) Careful measurement of the positions of the planets has led to a solid understanding of gravitational [perturbations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perturbation_theory "Perturbation theory"), and an ability to determine past and future positions of the planets with great accuracy, a field known as [celestial mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_mechanics "Celestial mechanics").[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-91) The measurement of [stellar parallax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_parallax "Stellar parallax") of nearby stars provides a fundamental baseline in the [cosmic distance ladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_distance_ladder "Cosmic distance ladder") that is used to measure the scale of the Universe. Parallax measurements of nearby stars provide an absolute baseline for the properties of more distant stars, as their properties can be compared.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-UWA_cosmic_distance_ladder-92) Measurements of the [radial velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radial_velocity "Radial velocity")[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Lindegren2003-93)[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-94) and [proper motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proper_motion "Proper motion") of stars allow astronomers to plot the movement of these systems through the Milky Way galaxy.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-95)

Theoretical astronomy
---------------------

Theoretical astronomers use several tools including [analytical models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_model "Mathematical model") and [computational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computation "Computation")[numerical simulations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerical_analysis "Numerical analysis"); each has its particular advantages. Analytical models of a process are better for giving broader insight into the heart of what is going on. Numerical models reveal the existence of phenomena and effects otherwise unobserved.[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-96)[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-97) Modern theoretical astronomy reflects dramatic advances in observation since the 1990s, including studies of the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background"), distant [supernovae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernovae "Supernovae") and [galaxy redshifts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift_survey "Redshift survey"), which have led to the development of a [standard model of cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model "Lambda-CDM model"). This model requires the universe to contain large amounts of [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter") and [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy") whose nature is currently not well understood, but the model gives detailed predictions that are in excellent agreement with many diverse observations.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-PDG-98)

Subfields by scale
------------------

### Physical cosmology

[![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Hubble_Extreme_Deep_Field_%28full_resolution%29.png/250px-Hubble_Extreme_Deep_Field_%28full_resolution%29.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hubble_Extreme_Deep_Field_(full_resolution).png)

[Hubble Extreme Deep Field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Extreme_Deep_Field "Hubble Extreme Deep Field")

[Physical cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_cosmology "Physical cosmology"), the study of [large-scale structure of the Universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large-scale_structure_of_the_Universe "Large-scale structure of the Universe"), seeks to understand the formation and evolution of the cosmos. Fundamental to modern cosmology is the well-accepted theory of the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang"), the concept that the universe begin extremely dense and hot, then [expanded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expansion_of_the_universe "Expansion of the universe") over the course of 13.8 billion years[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-99) to its present condition.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Dodelson2003-100) The concept of the Big Bang became widely accepted after the discovery of the [microwave background radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation "Cosmic microwave background radiation") in 1965.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Dodelson2003-100) Fundamental to the structure of the Universe is the existence of [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter") and [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy"). These are now thought to be its dominant components, forming 96% of the mass of the Universe. For this reason, much effort is expended in trying to understand the physics of these components.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-101)

[![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Grav.lens1.arp.750pix.jpg/250px-Grav.lens1.arp.750pix.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Grav.lens1.arp.750pix.jpg)

The blue, loop-shaped objects are multiple images of the same galaxy, duplicated by [gravitational lensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_lens "Gravitational lens"). The cluster's gravitational field bends light, magnifying and distorting the image of a more distant object.

The study of objects outside our galaxy is concerned with the [formation and evolution of galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_formation_and_evolution "Galaxy formation and evolution"), their morphology (description) and [classification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_morphological_classification "Galaxy morphological classification"), the observation of [active galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_galaxy "Active galaxy"), and at a larger scale, the [groups and clusters of galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_groups_and_clusters "Galaxy groups and clusters"). These assist the understanding of the [large-scale structure of the cosmos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large-scale_structure_of_the_cosmos "Large-scale structure of the cosmos").[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-:0-90)

### Galactic

Galactic astronomy studies galaxies including the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way"), a [barred spiral galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barred_spiral_galaxy "Barred spiral galaxy") that is a prominent member of the [Local Group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_Group "Local Group") of galaxies and contains the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System"). It is a rotating mass of gas, dust, stars and other objects, held together by mutual gravitational attraction. As the Earth is within the dusty outer arms, large portions of the Milky Way are obscured from view.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-:0-90): 837–842, 944

Kinematic studies of matter in the Milky Way and other galaxies show there is more mass than can be accounted for by visible matter. A [dark matter halo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter_halo "Dark matter halo") appears to dominate the mass, although the nature of this dark matter remains undetermined.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-102)

### Stellar

Further information: [Star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star")

The study of stars and [stellar evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_evolution "Stellar evolution") is fundamental to our understanding of the Universe. The astrophysics of stars has been determined through observation and theoretical understanding; and from computer simulations of the interior.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Amos7-103) Aspects studied include [star formation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_formation "Star formation") in [giant molecular clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_nebula "Dark nebula"); the formation of [protostars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protostar "Protostar"); and the transition to [nuclear fusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_fusion "Nuclear fusion") and [main-sequence stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Main-sequence_star "Main-sequence star"),[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Smith2004-104) carrying out [nucleosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleosynthesis "Nucleosynthesis").[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Amos7-103) Further processes studied include [stellar evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_evolution "Stellar evolution"),[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Amos-105) ending either with [supernovae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova "Supernova")[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-106) or [white dwarfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf "White dwarf"). The ejection of the outer layers forms a [planetary nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula "Planetary nebula").[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-107) The remnant of a supernova is a dense [neutron star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_star "Neutron star"), or, if the stellar mass was at least three times that of the Sun, a [black hole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole "Black hole").[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-108)

### Solar

[![Image 15](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Uvsun_trace_big.jpg/250px-Uvsun_trace_big.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Uvsun_trace_big.jpg)

An [ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet") image of the Sun's active [photosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosphere "Photosphere") as viewed by the NASA's [TRACE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TRACE "TRACE") space telescope.

Solar astronomy is the study of the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun"), a typical main-sequence [dwarf star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_star "Dwarf star") of [stellar class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_class "Stellar class") G2 V, and about 4.6 billion years (Gyr) old. Processes studied by the science include the [sunspot cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunspot_cycle "Sunspot cycle"),[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-solar_FAQ-109) the sun's changes in luminosity, both steady and periodic,[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Environmental_issues_:_essential_primary_sources.-110)[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-future-sun-111) and the behavior of the sun's various layers, namely its core with its [nuclear fusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_fusion "Nuclear fusion"), the [radiation zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation_zone "Radiation zone"), the [convection zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convection_zone "Convection zone"), the [photosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosphere "Photosphere"), the [chromosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromosphere "Chromosphere"), and the [corona](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_corona "Solar corona").[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-:0-90): 498–502

### Planetary science

[![Image 16](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Dust.devil.mars.arp.750pix.jpg/250px-Dust.devil.mars.arp.750pix.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Dust.devil.mars.arp.750pix.jpg)

The black spot at the top is a [dust devil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dust_devil "Dust devil") climbing a crater wall on [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars"). This moving, swirling column of [Martian atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Mars "Atmosphere of Mars") (comparable to a terrestrial [tornado](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tornado "Tornado")) created the long, dark streak.

Planetary science is the study of the assemblage of [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet"), [moons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_satellite "Natural satellite"), [dwarf planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_planet "Dwarf planet"), [comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet "Comet"), [asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid "Asteroid"), and other bodies orbiting the Sun, as well as extrasolar planets. The [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") has been relatively well-studied, initially through telescopes and then later by spacecraft.[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-geology-112)[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Montmerle2006-113)

Processes studied include [planetary differentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_differentiation "Planetary differentiation"); the generation of, and effects created by, a planetary [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field");[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-114) and the creation of heat within a planet, such as by collisions, radioactive decay, and [tidal heating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_acceleration "Tidal acceleration"). In turn, that heat can drive geologic processes such as [volcanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcanism "Volcanism"), tectonics, and surface [erosion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erosion "Erosion"), studied by branches of geology.[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-new_solar_system-115)

Interdisciplinary subfields
---------------------------

### Astrochemistry

Astrochemistry is an overlap of astronomy and [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry"). It studies the abundance and reactions of [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule") in the [Universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe"), and their interaction with [radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation "Radiation"). The word "astrochemistry" may be applied to both the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") and the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium"). Studies in this field contribute for example to the understanding of the [formation of the Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formation_of_the_Solar_System "Formation of the Solar System").[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-116)

### Astrobiology

Astrobiology (or exobiology[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-117)) studies the [origin of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenesis "Abiogenesis") and its development other than on earth. It considers whether [extraterrestrial life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life "Extraterrestrial life") exists, and how humans can detect it if it does.[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-about-118) It makes use of astronomy, [biochemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biochemistry "Biochemistry"), [geology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geology "Geology"), [microbiology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbiology "Microbiology"), physics, and planetary science to investigate the possibility of life on other worlds and help recognize [biospheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere "Biosphere") that might be different from that on Earth.[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-119)[The origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenesis "Abiogenesis") and early evolution of life is an inseparable part of the discipline of astrobiology.[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-120) That encompasses research on the origin of [planetary systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_system "Planetary system"), origins of [organic compounds in space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules "List of interstellar and circumstellar molecules"), rock-water-carbon interactions, [abiogenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenesis "Abiogenesis") on Earth, [planetary habitability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_habitability "Planetary habitability"), research on [biosignatures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosignature "Biosignature") for life detection, and studies on the potential for [life to adapt to challenges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extremophile "Extremophile") on Earth and in [outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space "Outer space").[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Goals2016-121)[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-NYT-20151218-jc-122)[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-123)

### Other

Astronomy and astrophysics have developed interdisciplinary links with other major scientific fields. [Archaeoastronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaeoastronomy "Archaeoastronomy") is the study of ancient or traditional astronomies in their cultural context, using [archaeological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archaeology "Archaeology") and [anthropological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthropology "Anthropology") evidence.[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-124)[Astrostatistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrostatistics "Astrostatistics") is the application of statistics to the analysis of large quantities of observational astrophysical data.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Hilbe_2017-125) As "[forensic astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forensic_astronomy "Forensic astronomy")", finally, methods from astronomy have been used to solve problems of art history[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-126)[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-127) and occasionally of law.[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-128)

Amateur
-------

[![Image 17](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a7/Telescope_trailer_22.jpg/250px-Telescope_trailer_22.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Telescope_trailer_22.jpg)

Amateur astronomers can build their own equipment, and hold star parties and gatherings, such as [Stellafane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellafane "Stellafane").

Astronomy is one of the sciences to which amateurs can contribute the most.[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-129) Collectively, amateur astronomers observe celestial objects and phenomena, sometimes with consumer-level equipment or [equipment that they build themselves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amateur_telescope_making "Amateur telescope making"). Common targets include the Sun, the Moon, planets, stars, comets, [meteor showers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteor_shower "Meteor shower"), and [deep-sky objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep-sky_object "Deep-sky object") such as star clusters, galaxies, and nebulae. Astronomy clubs throughout the world have programs to help their members set up and run observational programs such as to observe all the objects in the Messier (110 objects) or Herschel 400 catalogues.[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-130)[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-131) Most amateurs work at visible wavelengths, but some have experimented with wavelengths outside the visible spectrum. The pioneer of amateur radio astronomy, [Karl Jansky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Guthe_Jansky "Karl Guthe Jansky"), discovered a radio source at the centre of the Milky Way.[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-Imbriale_1998-132) Some amateur astronomers use homemade telescopes or radio telescopes originally built for astronomy research (_e.g._ the [One-Mile Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-Mile_Telescope "One-Mile Telescope")).[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-133)[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-134)

Amateurs can make occultation measurements to refine the orbits of minor planets. They can discover comets, and perform regular observations of variable stars. Improvements in digital technology have allowed amateurs to make advances in [astrophotography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophotography "Astrophotography").[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-135)[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-136)[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-137)

Unsolved problems
-----------------

In the 21st century, there remain important unanswered questions in astronomy. Some are cosmic in scope: for example, what are the [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter") and [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy") that dominate the evolution and fate of the cosmos?[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-physics_questions-138) What will be the [ultimate fate of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultimate_fate_of_the_universe "Ultimate fate of the universe")?[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-139) Why is the abundance of [lithium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium "Lithium") in the cosmos four times lower than predicted by the standard [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang") model?[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-140) Others pertain to more specific classes of phenomena. For example, is the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") normal or atypical?[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-141) What is the origin of the stellar mass spectrum, i.e. why do astronomers observe the same distribution of stellar masses—the [initial mass function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Initial_mass_function "Initial mass function")—regardless of initial conditions?[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-142) Likewise, questions remain about the formation of the [first galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protogalaxy "Protogalaxy"),[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-143) the origin of [supermassive black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole "Supermassive black hole"),[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-144) the source of [ultra-high-energy cosmic rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra-high-energy_cosmic_ray "Ultra-high-energy cosmic ray"),[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-145) and whether there is other [life in the Universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extraterrestrial_life "Extraterrestrial life"), especially [other intelligent life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extraterrestrial_intelligence "Extraterrestrial intelligence").[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-146)[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy#cite_note-147)
