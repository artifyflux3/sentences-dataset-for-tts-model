Life and work [ edit ] Only little is known of Seeber's origin and education. In 1810, he studied astronomy at the University of Göttingen with Carl Friedrich Gauss , a companion of this time was Christian Ludwig Gerling .

[ 1 ] [ a ] From 1819 to 1822 he was teacher at the cadet school at Karlsruhe. He got his doctor degree in 1824 from the University of Freiburg , [ 3 ] where he was professor ordinarius for physics until 1834 and three times Dean of the Philosophical Faculty in 1814, 1829, and 1834.

[ 4 ] From 1834 to 1840, he was professor of physics both at the Polytechnicum and the Lyceum in Karlsruhe.

[ 5 ] [ 6 ] Seeber applied twice in 1830 and 1838 for a professorship in Göttingen, but without success. In 1840, he took early retirement.

[ 1 ] Seeber is known for his mathematical studies with special regard to crystallography . He tried to find explanations for the changing properties of crystals, such as thermal expansion or elasticity , what was impossible with the current theory of the late 18th century created by René Just Haüy , that used a bricklike model of crystal structure.

[ 7 ] Seeber modernized Haüy's concept with introducing the idea of spherical particles (atoms or molecules) as basic components of the crystals, holding together in an equilibrium of attractive and repulsive forces.

[ 7 ] [ 8 ] Following Max von Laue , Seeber's "essentially modern" concept from 1824 "was the earliest application of the scientific atomic theory to a purely physical problem." [ 7 ] In his second work from 1831, Seeber continued the research on positive ternary quadratic forms Gauss had begun thirty years ago in his Disquisitiones Arithmeticae . Seeber derived criteria for equivalence or non-equivalence of the reduced forms for the determinant of ternary forms.

[ 9 ] He derived two lemmas for the relation of determinants with the coefficients of the reduced forms, but could only prove one of them, the second one remained as a conjecture.

[ 9 ] Gauss was able to prove this very shortly on three pages of his unusually detailed review of Seeber's work.

[ 1 ] Gauss claimed Seeber's work for its exemplary thoroughness and protected it against the possible reproach of "repulsive long-windedness". The reduction of ternary forms was later simplified by Gauss's successor Peter Gustav Lejeune Dirichlet (1847).
