From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

A **parametric surface** is a [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(mathematics) "Surface (mathematics)") in the [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space")![Image 1: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) which is defined by a [parametric equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametric_equation "Parametric equation") with two parameters ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} :\mathbb {R} ^{2}\to \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b6ce393a12dac65868a92d9fff2e8f6eef5f08aa). Parametric representation is a very general way to specify a surface, as well as [implicit representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Implicit_surface "Implicit surface"). Surfaces that occur in two of the main theorems of [vector calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus "Vector calculus"), [Stokes' theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes%27_theorem "Stokes' theorem") and the [divergence theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem "Divergence theorem"), are frequently given in a parametric form. The curvature and [arc length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_length "Arc length") of [curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve") on the surface, [surface area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_area "Surface area"), differential geometric invariants such as the [first](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_fundamental_form "First fundamental form") and [second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_fundamental_form "Second fundamental form") fundamental forms, [Gaussian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_curvature "Gaussian curvature"), [mean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_curvature "Mean curvature"), and [principal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_curvature "Principal curvature") curvatures can all be computed from a given parametrization.

The same surface admits many different parametrizations. For example, the coordinate _z_-plane can be parametrized as ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} (u,v)=(au+bv,cu+dv,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d30ee0b2a8fda677ac10781d298bde15fef90c97) for any constants _a_, _b_, _c_, _d_ such that _ad_ − _bc_ ≠ 0, i.e. the matrix ![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\begin{bmatrix}a&b\\c&d\end{bmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a93ef707e1cf36c080c48186963e6c72ddc4e086) is [invertible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invertible_matrix "Invertible matrix").

Local differential geometry
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parametric_surface&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Local differential geometry")]

The local shape of a parametric surface can be analyzed by considering the [Taylor expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor_expansion "Taylor expansion") of the function that parametrizes it. The arc length of a curve on the surface and the surface area can be found using [integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral").

Let the parametric surface be given by the equation ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} =\mathbf {r} (u,v),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6aeec617173611dac8fc72db0dff05aa533de232) where ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1) is a [vector-valued function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector-valued_function "Vector-valued function") of the parameters (_u_, _v_) and the parameters vary within a certain domain _D_ in the parametric _uv_-plane. The first partial derivatives with respect to the parameters are usually denoted ![Image 10: {\textstyle \mathbf {r} _{u}:={\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial u}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f2298bcc0cbad4476dff2ca3c1bd91e40ae2e81) and ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} _{v},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89e7a89c27c972cb7c85b9d8257d3c2b0a0d6e1c) and similarly for the higher derivatives, ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} _{uu},\mathbf {r} _{uv},\mathbf {r} _{vv}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11ee623cbca500d1a12ace1d847db30eedebb0b4)

In [vector calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus "Vector calculus"), the parameters are frequently denoted (_s_,_t_) and the partial derivatives are written out using the _∂_-notation: ![Image 13: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial s}},{\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial t}},{\frac {\partial ^{2}\mathbf {r} }{\partial s^{2}}},{\frac {\partial ^{2}\mathbf {r} }{\partial s\partial t}},{\frac {\partial ^{2}\mathbf {r} }{\partial t^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47ca3a7f476426b4c56e1f9af087b7d949b57ed3)

### Tangent plane and normal vector

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parametric_surface&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Tangent plane and normal vector")]

The parametrization is **regular** for the given values of the parameters if the vectors ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} _{u},\mathbf {r} _{v}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e24da07ef16a99ae9284c39d3c55d376567aa8c) are linearly independent. The **[tangent plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_plane "Tangent plane")** at a regular point is the affine plane in **R**3 spanned by these vectors and passing through the point **r**(_u_, _v_) on the surface determined by the parameters. Any tangent vector can be uniquely decomposed into a [linear combination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_combination "Linear combination") of ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} _{u}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e2a97cccf31e50f7607b90681f475b011ec0e65b) and ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} _{v}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/942d0bd94eb28034cf1c371d374c9fcfeb3b30c6) The [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product") of these vectors is a [normal vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_vector "Normal vector") to the [tangent plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_plane "Tangent plane"). Dividing this vector by its length yields a unit [normal vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_vector "Normal vector") to the parametrized surface at a regular point: ![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {n} }}={\frac {\mathbf {r} _{u}\times \mathbf {r} _{v}}{\left|\mathbf {r} _{u}\times \mathbf {r} _{v}\right|}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a1d327bcae01f88201432878a6ce5947f63d8ef)

In general, there are two choices of the unit [normal vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_vector "Normal vector") to a surface at a given point, but for a regular parametrized surface, the preceding formula consistently picks one of them, and thus determines an [orientation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientability "Orientability") of the surface. Some of the differential-geometric invariants of a surface in **R**3 are defined by the surface itself and are independent of the orientation, while others change the sign if the orientation is reversed.

The [surface area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_area "Surface area") can be calculated by integrating the length of the normal vector ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} _{u}\times \mathbf {r} _{v}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ff772630b2034e7a0f40bc94da52acd06e751e3) to the surface over the appropriate region _D_ in the parametric _uv_ plane: ![Image 19: {\displaystyle A(D)=\iint _{D}\left|\mathbf {r} _{u}\times \mathbf {r} _{v}\right|du\,dv.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a17d3af46948bcb306792b8f92a7df6aaa38e076)

Although this formula provides a closed expression for the surface area, for all but very special surfaces this results in a complicated [double integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_integral "Double integral"), which is typically evaluated using a [computer algebra system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_algebra_system "Computer algebra system") or approximated numerically. Fortunately, many common surfaces form exceptions, and their areas are explicitly known. This is true for a [circular cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry) "Cylinder (geometry)"), [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere"), [cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone_(geometry) "Cone (geometry)"), [torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torus "Torus"), and a few other [surfaces of revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_of_revolution "Surface of revolution").

This can also be expressed as a [surface integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_integral "Surface integral") over the [scalar field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field "Scalar field") 1: ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \int _{S}1\,dS.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db0d23fc40bcb7b042472d2b81e480bbfcc8a960)

### First fundamental form

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parametric_surface&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: First fundamental form")]

The **first fundamental form** is a [quadratic form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_form "Quadratic form")![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathrm {I} =E\,du^{2}+2\,F\,du\,dv+G\,dv^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c27aa75d58db93fb8cf73ee2fcd29b3f7157706b) on the [tangent plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_plane "Tangent plane") to the surface which is used to calculate distances and angles. For a parametrized surface ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} =\mathbf {r} (u,v),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6aeec617173611dac8fc72db0dff05aa533de232) its coefficients can be computed as follows: ![Image 23: {\displaystyle E=\mathbf {r} _{u}\cdot \mathbf {r} _{u},\quad F=\mathbf {r} _{u}\cdot \mathbf {r} _{v},\quad G=\mathbf {r} _{v}\cdot \mathbf {r} _{v}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d7bf6c1aa282f952db9d7577870825cdbc54e586)

[Arc length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_length "Arc length") of parametrized curves on the surface _S_, the angle between curves on _S_, and the surface area all admit expressions in terms of the first fundamental form.

If (_u_(_t_), _v_(_t_)), _a_ ≤ _t_ ≤ _b_ represents a parametrized curve on this surface then its arc length can be calculated as the integral: ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \int _{a}^{b}{\sqrt {E\,u'(t)^{2}+2F\,u'(t)v'(t)+G\,v'(t)^{2}}}\,dt.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/94013c6070b59195cf8b748b7f863d78fdece40b)

The first fundamental form may be viewed as a family of [positive definite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Definite_bilinear_form "Definite bilinear form")[symmetric bilinear forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_bilinear_form "Symmetric bilinear form") on the tangent plane at each point of the surface depending smoothly on the point. This perspective helps one calculate the angle between two curves on _S_ intersecting at a given point. This angle is equal to the angle between the tangent vectors to the curves. The first fundamental form evaluated on this pair of vectors is their [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product"), and the angle can be found from the standard formula ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \cos \theta ={\frac {\mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {b} }{\left|\mathbf {a} \right|\left|\mathbf {b} \right|}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d5605bdb14cdb4c5c26a4825677c88bf3e080b0) expressing the [cosine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosine "Cosine") of the angle via the dot product.

Surface area can be expressed in terms of the first fundamental form as follows: ![Image 26: {\displaystyle A(D)=\iint _{D}{\sqrt {EG-F^{2}}}\,du\,dv.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/acaf87b6da398e1bd1e10f81f04b2c5900567896)

By [Lagrange's identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange%27s_identity "Lagrange's identity"), the expression under the square root is precisely ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \left|\mathbf {r} _{u}\times \mathbf {r} _{v}\right|^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d4f151bc2993f3640ce84f3a8bd7dda7c31c165f), and so it is strictly positive at the regular points.

### Second fundamental form

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parametric_surface&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Second fundamental form")]

The second fundamental form ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \mathrm {I\!I} =L\,du^{2}+2M\,du\,dv+N\,dv^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4c64083d5f458fa6ef5a338c8f96201e3be7774) is a quadratic form on the tangent plane to the surface that, together with the first fundamental form, determines the curvatures of curves on the surface. In the special case when (_u_, _v_) = (_x_, _y_) and the tangent plane to the surface at the given point is horizontal, the second fundamental form is essentially the quadratic part of the [Taylor expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor_expansion "Taylor expansion") of _z_ as a function of _x_ and _y_.

For a general parametric surface, the definition is more complicated, but the second fundamental form depends only on the [partial derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative "Partial derivative") of order one and two. Its coefficients are defined to be the projections of the second partial derivatives of ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1) onto the unit normal vector ![Image 30: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {n} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aae87b164ba005e99b51066c46d1eacc7f56564a) defined by the parametrization: ![Image 31: {\displaystyle L=\mathbf {r} _{uu}\cdot {\hat {\mathbf {n} }},\quad M=\mathbf {r} _{uv}\cdot {\hat {\mathbf {n} }},\quad N=\mathbf {r} _{vv}\cdot {\hat {\mathbf {n} }}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32d72e3ae3f2cfd6a610c08d9ba9cdb566ceed67)

Like the first fundamental form, the second fundamental form may be viewed as a family of symmetric bilinear forms on the tangent plane at each point of the surface depending smoothly on the point.

The first and second fundamental forms of a surface determine its important differential-geometric [invariants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_(mathematics) "Invariant (mathematics)"): the [Gaussian curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_curvature "Gaussian curvature"), the [mean curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_curvature "Mean curvature"), and the [principal curvatures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal_curvature "Principal curvature").

The principal curvatures are the invariants of the pair consisting of the second and first fundamental forms. They are the roots _κ_ 1, _κ_ 2 of the quadratic equation ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \det(\mathrm {I\!I} -\kappa \mathrm {I} )=0,\quad \det {\begin{bmatrix}L-\kappa E&M-\kappa F\\M-\kappa F&N-\kappa G\end{bmatrix}}=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eed7b13a99f281f1decc3fb4209648ac2047a4e1)

The **Gaussian curvature**_K_ = _κ_ 1 _κ_ 2 and the **mean curvature**_H_ = (_κ_ 1 + _κ_ 2)/2 can be computed as follows: ![Image 33: {\displaystyle K={\frac {LN-M^{2}}{EG-F^{2}}},\quad H={\frac {EN-2FM+GL}{2(EG-F^{2})}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d0205507a28294291f8c6fc32365176d5ab539f)

Up to a sign, these quantities are independent of the parametrization used, and hence form important tools for analysing the geometry of the surface. More precisely, the principal curvatures and the mean curvature change the sign if the orientation of the surface is reversed, and the Gaussian curvature is entirely independent of the parametrization.

The sign of the Gaussian curvature at a point determines the shape of the surface near that point: for _K_> 0 the surface is locally [convex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_set "Convex set") and the point is called _elliptic_, while for _K_< 0 the surface is saddle shaped and the point is called _hyperbolic_. The points at which the Gaussian curvature is zero are called _parabolic_. In general, parabolic points form a curve on the surface called the _parabolic line_. The first fundamental form is [positive definite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive_definite_matrix "Positive definite matrix"), hence its determinant _EG_ − _F_ 2 is positive everywhere. Therefore, the sign of _K_ coincides with the sign of _LN_ − _M_ 2, the determinant of the second fundamental.

The coefficients of the [first fundamental form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametric_surface#First_fundamental_form) presented above may be organized in a [symmetric matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_matrix "Symmetric matrix"): ![Image 34: {\displaystyle F_{1}={\begin{bmatrix}E&F\\F&G\end{bmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c911fe92cd99d9ea2240efbd099df9f083a57899) And the same for the coefficients of the [second fundamental form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametric_surface#Second_fundamental_form), also presented above: ![Image 35: {\displaystyle F_{2}={\begin{bmatrix}L&M\\M&N\end{bmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/090f1cee508588356c1be4cfe0743034c1ba5ada)

Defining now matrix ![Image 36: {\displaystyle A=F_{1}^{-1}F_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/457eb26c1bc4dfc0b59ac82c6c762f80277622cb), the principal curvatures _κ_ 1 and _κ_ 2 are the [eigenvalues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvalue "Eigenvalue") of _A_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametric_surface#cite_note-1)

Now, if **v**1 = (_v_ 11, _v_ 12) is the [eigenvector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvector "Eigenvector") of _A_ corresponding to principal curvature _κ_ 1, the [unit vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector") in the direction of ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \mathbf {t} _{1}=v_{11}\mathbf {r} _{u}+v_{12}\mathbf {r} _{v}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0cc8e0b9ad353728e534970d25c89e53b01aa9e1) is called the principal vector corresponding to the principal curvature _κ_ 1.

Accordingly, if **v**2 = (_v_ 21,_v_ 22) is the [eigenvector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvector "Eigenvector") of _A_ corresponding to principal curvature _κ_ 2, the unit vector in the direction of ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \mathbf {t} _{2}=v_{21}\mathbf {r} _{u}+v_{22}\mathbf {r} _{v}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/071176de1d873916ecbafb9fef8fa322b818acba) is called the principal vector corresponding to the principal curvature _κ_ 2.
