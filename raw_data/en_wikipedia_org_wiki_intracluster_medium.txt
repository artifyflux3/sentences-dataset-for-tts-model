The overlaid blue cloud illustrates the intracluster medium around the [Spiderweb Galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spiderweb_Galaxy "Spiderweb Galaxy"), as seen when the universe was 3 billion years old

In [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy"), the **intracluster medium** (**ICM**) is the superheated [plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)") that permeates a [galaxy cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_cluster "Galaxy cluster"). The gas consists mainly of [ionized hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionized_hydrogen "Ionized hydrogen") and [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium") and accounts for most of the [baryonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon "Baryon") material in galaxy clusters. The ICM is heated to temperatures on the order of 10 to 100 [megakelvins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orders_of_magnitude_(temperature) "Orders of magnitude (temperature)"), emitting strong [X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray") radiation.

The ICM is composed primarily of ordinary [baryons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon "Baryon"), mainly ionised hydrogen and helium.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracluster_medium#cite_note-:0-1) This plasma is enriched with heavier elements, including [iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron "Iron"). The average amount of heavier elements relative to hydrogen, known as [metallicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metallicity "Metallicity") in astronomy, ranges from a third to a half of the value in the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracluster_medium#cite_note-:0-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracluster_medium#cite_note-:4-2) Studying the chemical composition of the ICMs as a function of radius has shown that cores of the galaxy clusters are more metal-rich than at larger radii.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracluster_medium#cite_note-:4-2) In some clusters (e.g. the [Centaurus Cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centaurus_Cluster "Centaurus Cluster")) the metallicity of the gas can rise to above that of the sun.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracluster_medium#cite_note-3) Due to the [gravitational field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_field "Gravitational field") of clusters, metal-enriched gas ejected from [supernova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova "Supernova") remains [gravitationally bound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_binding_energy "Gravitational binding energy") to the cluster as part of the ICM.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracluster_medium#cite_note-:4-2) By looking at varying [redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift"), which corresponds to looking at different epochs of the evolution of the Universe, the ICM can provide a history record of element production in a galaxy.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracluster_medium#cite_note-4)

Roughly 15% of a galaxy cluster's mass resides in the ICM. The stars and galaxies contribute only around 5% to the total mass. It is theorized that most of the mass in a galaxy cluster consists of [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter") and not baryonic matter. For the [Virgo Cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Cluster "Virgo Cluster"), the ICM contains roughly 3 × 10 14 M☉ while the total mass of the cluster is estimated to be 1.2 × 10 15 M☉.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracluster_medium#cite_note-:0-1)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracluster_medium#cite_note-5)

Although the ICM on the whole contains the bulk of a cluster's baryons, it is not very dense, with typical values of 10−3 particles per cubic centimeter. The [mean free path](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_free_path "Mean free path") of the particles is roughly 10 16 m, or about one lightyear. The density of the ICM rises towards the centre of the cluster with a relatively strong peak. In addition, the temperature of the ICM typically drops to 1/2 or 1/3 of the outer value in the central regions. Once the density of the plasma reaches a critical value, enough interactions between the ions ensures cooling via X-ray radiation.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracluster_medium#cite_note-6)

Observing the intracluster medium
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Intracluster_medium&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Observing the intracluster medium")]

As the ICM is at such high temperatures, it emits [X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray") radiation, mainly by the [bremsstrahlung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bremsstrahlung "Bremsstrahlung") process and X-ray [emission lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emission_lines "Emission lines") from the heavy elements.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracluster_medium#cite_note-:0-1) These X-rays can be observed using an [X-ray telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_telescope "X-ray telescope") and through analysis of this data, it is possible to determine the physical conditions, including the temperature, density, and metallicity of the plasma.

Measurements of the temperature and density profiles in galaxy clusters allow for a determination of the mass distribution profile of the ICM through [hydrostatic equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium "Hydrostatic equilibrium") modeling. The mass distributions determined from these methods reveal masses that far exceed the luminous mass seen and are thus a strong indication of dark matter in galaxy clusters.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracluster_medium#cite_note-7)

Inverse [Compton scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compton_scattering "Compton scattering") of low energy photons through interactions with the relativistic electrons in the ICM cause distortions in the spectrum of the [cosmic microwave background radiation (CMB)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background"), known as the [Sunyaev–Zel'dovich effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunyaev%E2%80%93Zel%27dovich_effect "Sunyaev–Zel'dovich effect"). These temperature distortions in the CMB can be used by telescopes such as the [South Pole Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Pole_Telescope "South Pole Telescope") to detect dense clusters of galaxies at high redshifts.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracluster_medium#cite_note-8)

In December 2022, the [James Webb Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope "James Webb Space Telescope") is reported to be studying the faint light emitted in the intracluster medium.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracluster_medium#cite_note-SPC-20221209-9) A 2018 study found this to be an "accurate luminous tracer of dark matter".[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracluster_medium#cite_note-10)

Plasma in regions of the cluster, with a cooling time shorter than the age of the system, should be cooling due to strong X-ray radiation where emission is proportional to the density squared. Since the density of the ICM is highest towards the center of the cluster, the [radiative cooling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiative_cooling "Radiative cooling") time drops a significant amount.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracluster_medium#cite_note-:3-11) The central cooled gas can no longer support the weight of the external hot gas and the [pressure gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure_gradient "Pressure gradient") drives what is known as a [cooling flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cooling_flow "Cooling flow") where the hot gas from the external regions flows slowly towards the center of the cluster. This inflow would result in regions of cold gas and thus regions of new [star formation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_formation "Star formation").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracluster_medium#cite_note-:2-12) Recently however, with the launch of new X-ray telescopes such as the [Chandra X-ray Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chandra_X-ray_Observatory "Chandra X-ray Observatory"), images of galaxy clusters with better spatial resolution have been taken. These new images do not indicate signs of new star formation on the order of what was historically predicted, motivating research into the mechanisms that would prevent the central ICM from cooling.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracluster_medium#cite_note-:3-11)

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/NASA-PerseusGalaxyCluster-ChandraXRayObservatory-20140624.jpg/250px-NASA-PerseusGalaxyCluster-ChandraXRayObservatory-20140624.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:NASA-PerseusGalaxyCluster-ChandraXRayObservatory-20140624.jpg)

[Chandra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chandra_X-ray_Observatory "Chandra X-ray Observatory") image of the [Perseus Cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perseus_Cluster "Perseus Cluster")'s radio lobes. These relativistic jets of plasma emit [radio waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_wave "Radio wave"), are X-ray "cold", and appear as dark patches in stark contrast to the rest of the ICM. 

There are two popular explanations of the mechanisms that prevent the central ICM from cooling: feedback from [active galactic nuclei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_galactic_nucleus "Active galactic nucleus") through injection of [relativistic jets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_jet "Astrophysical jet") of plasma[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracluster_medium#cite_note-:1-13) and sloshing of the ICM plasma during mergers with subclusters.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracluster_medium#cite_note-14)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracluster_medium#cite_note-15) The relativistic jets of material from active galactic nuclei can be seen in images taken by telescopes with high angular resolution such as the [Chandra X-ray Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chandra_X-ray_Observatory "Chandra X-ray Observatory").
