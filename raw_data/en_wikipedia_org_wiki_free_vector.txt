In the [natural sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_sciences "Natural sciences"), a **vector quantity** (also known as a **vector physical quantity**, **physical vector**, or simply **vector**) is a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(mathematics_and_physics) "Vector (mathematics and physics)")-valued [physical quantity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_quantity "Physical quantity").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_vector#cite_note-a306-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_vector#cite_note-o531-2) It is typically formulated as the product of a _[unit of measurement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit\_of\_measurement "Unit of measurement")_ and a _vector [numerical value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerical\_value "Numerical value")_ ([unitless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitless "Unitless")), often a [Euclidean vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") with [magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_norm "Vector norm") and [direction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direction_(geometry) "Direction (geometry)"). For example, a [position vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_vector "Position vector") in [physical space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_space "Physical space") may be expressed as [three](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three_dimensional "Three dimensional")[Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates") with [SI unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_unit "SI unit") of [meters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meters "Meters").

In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") and [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering"), particularly in [mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics "Mechanics"), a physical vector may be endowed with additional structure compared to a geometrical vector.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_vector#cite_note-m813-3) A **bound vector** is defined as the combination of an ordinary vector quantity and a _[point of application](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point\_of\_application "Point of application")_ or _point of action_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_vector#cite_note-a306-1)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_vector#cite_note-Teodorescu-4) Bound vector quantities are formulated as a _[directed line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directed\_line\_segment "Directed line segment")_, with a definite initial point besides the magnitude and direction of the main vector.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_vector#cite_note-a306-1)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_vector#cite_note-m813-3) For example, a [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force") on the [Euclidean plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_plane "Euclidean plane") has two Cartesian components in SI unit of [newtons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newtons "Newtons") and an accompanying two-dimensional position vector in meters, for a total of four numbers on the plane (and six in space).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_vector#cite_note-p822-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_vector#cite_note-z733-6)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_vector#cite_note-Teodorescu-4) A simpler example of a bound vector is the [translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry) "Translation (geometry)") vector from an initial point to an end point; in this case, the bound vector is an [ordered pair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair "Ordered pair") of points in the same position space, with all coordinates having the same [quantity dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantity_dimension "Quantity dimension") and unit (length an meters).[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_vector#cite_note-p422-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_vector#cite_note-w531-8) A **sliding vector** is the combination of an ordinary vector quantity and a _[line of application](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line\_of\_application "Line of application")_ or _line of action_, over which the vector quantity can be translated (without rotations). A **free vector** is a vector quantity having an undefined [support](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Support_(mathematics) "Support (mathematics)") or region of application; it can be freely translated with no consequences; a [displacement vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Displacement_vector "Displacement vector") is a prototypical example of free vector.

Aside from the notion of units and support, physical vector quantities may also differ from Euclidean vectors in terms of [metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_metric "Distance metric"). For example, an event in [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") may be represented as a [position four-vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_four-vector "Position four-vector"), with [coherent derived unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coherent_derived_unit "Coherent derived unit") of meters: it includes a position Euclidean vector and a [timelike](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timelike "Timelike") component, _t_ ⋅ _c_ 0 (involving the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light")). In that case, the [Minkowski metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_metric "Minkowski metric") is adopted instead of the [Euclidean metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_metric "Euclidean metric").

Vector quantities are a generalization of [scalar quantities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_quantity "Scalar quantity") and can be further generalized as [tensor quantities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_quantity "Tensor quantity").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_vector#cite_note-w531-8) Individual vectors may be ordered in a [sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence "Sequence") over time (a [time series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_series "Time series")), such as position vectors [discretizing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discretizing "Discretizing") a [trajectory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trajectory "Trajectory"). A vector may also result from the [evaluation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_evaluation "Function evaluation"), at a particular instant, of a continuous [vector-valued function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector-valued_function "Vector-valued function") (e.g., the [pendulum equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_equation "Pendulum equation")). In the natural sciences, the term "vector quantity" also encompasses _[vector fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector\_field "Vector field")_ defined over a [two-](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-dimensional "Two-dimensional") or three-dimensional [region](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Region_(mathematics) "Region (mathematics)") of space, such as [wind velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wind_velocity "Wind velocity") over Earth's surface. [Pseudo vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo_vector "Pseudo vector") and [bivectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bivector "Bivector") are also admitted as physical vector quantities.
