In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **parallelepiped** is a [three-dimensional figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_figure "Three-dimensional figure") formed by six [parallelograms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") (the term _[rhomboid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhomboid "Rhomboid")_ is also sometimes used with this meaning). By analogy, it relates to a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") just as a [cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube "Cube") relates to a [square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square "Square").[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped#cite_note-1)

Three equivalent definitions of _parallelepiped_ are

*   a [hexahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexahedron "Hexahedron") with three pairs of parallel faces,
*   a [polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron "Polyhedron") with six faces ([hexahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexahedron "Hexahedron")), each of which is a parallelogram, and
*   a [prism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_(geometry) "Prism (geometry)") of which the base is a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram").

The [rectangular cuboid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangular_cuboid "Rectangular cuboid") (six [rectangular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangular "Rectangular") faces), [cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube "Cube") (six [square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square "Square") faces), and the [rhombohedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombohedron "Rhombohedron") (six [rhombus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombus "Rhombus") faces) are all special cases of parallelepiped.

"Parallelepiped" is now usually pronounced or ;[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped#cite_note-2) traditionally it was [_PARR-ə-lel-EP-ih-ped_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key")[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped#cite_note-3) because of its etymology in [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek") παραλληλεπίπεδον _parallelepipedon_ (with short -i-), a body "having [parallel planes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_planes "Parallel planes")".

Parallelepipeds are a subclass of the [prismatoids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prismatoid "Prismatoid").

Any of the three pairs of parallel faces can be viewed as the base planes of the prism. A parallelepiped has three sets of four parallel edges; the edges within each set are of equal length.

Parallelepipeds result from [linear transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_transformation "Linear transformation") of a [cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube "Cube") (for the non-degenerate cases: the bijective linear transformations).

Since each face has [point symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_symmetry "Point symmetry"), a parallelepiped is a [zonohedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zonohedron "Zonohedron"). Also the whole parallelepiped has point symmetry _C i_ (see also [triclinic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triclinic "Triclinic")). Each face is, seen from the outside, the mirror image of the opposite face. The faces are in general [chiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chirality_(mathematics) "Chirality (mathematics)"), but the parallelepiped is not.

A [space-filling tessellation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honeycomb_(geometry) "Honeycomb (geometry)") is possible with [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_(geometry) "Congruence (geometry)") copies of any parallelepiped.

Corresponding tetrahedron
The volume of any [tetrahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron "Tetrahedron") that shares three converging edges of a parallelepiped is equal to one sixth of the volume of that parallelepiped (see [proof](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron#Volume "Tetrahedron")).

*   The parallelepiped with O h symmetry is known as a **[cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube "Cube")**, which has six congruent square faces.
*   The parallelepiped with D 4h symmetry is known as a **[square cuboid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_cuboid "Square cuboid")**, which has two square faces and four congruent rectangular faces.
*   The parallelepiped with D 3d symmetry is known as a **[trigonal trapezohedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonal_trapezohedron "Trigonal trapezohedron")**, which has six congruent [rhombic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombus "Rhombus") faces (also called an **isohedral rhombohedron**).
*   For parallelepipeds with D 2h symmetry, there are two cases: 
    *   **[Rectangular cuboid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangular_cuboid "Rectangular cuboid")**: it has six rectangular faces (also called a **rectangular parallelepiped**, or sometimes simply a _cuboid_).
    *   **Right rhombic prism**: it has two rhombic faces and four congruent rectangular faces. Note: the fully rhombic special case, with two rhombic faces and four congruent square faces ![Image 37: {\displaystyle (a=b=c)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ee21af2732f491a28d99d49dda0110a3e377329), has the same name, and the same symmetry group (D 2h , order 8).

*   For parallelepipeds with C 2h symmetry, there are two cases: 
    *   **Right parallelogrammic prism**: it has four rectangular faces and two parallelogrammic faces.
    *   **Oblique rhombic prism**: it has two rhombic faces, while of the other faces, two adjacent ones are equal and the other two also (the two pairs are each other's mirror image).

Perfect parallelepiped
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Parallelepiped&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Perfect parallelepiped")]

A _perfect parallelepiped_ is a parallelepiped with integer-length edges, face diagonals, and [space diagonals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_diagonal "Space diagonal"). In 2009, dozens of perfect parallelepipeds were shown to exist,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped#cite_note-4) answering an open question of [Richard Guy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_K._Guy "Richard K. Guy"). One example has edges 271, 106, and 103, minor face diagonals 101, 266, and 255, major face diagonals 183, 312, and 323, and space diagonals 374, 300, 278, and 272.

Some perfect parallelepipeds having two rectangular faces are known. But it is not known whether there exist any with all faces rectangular; such a case would be called a perfect [cuboid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuboid "Cuboid").

[Coxeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter "Coxeter") called the generalization of a parallelepiped in higher dimensions a **parallelotope**. In modern literature, the term parallelepiped is often used in higher (or arbitrary finite) dimensions as well.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped#cite_note-5)

Specifically in _n_-dimensional space it is called _n_-dimensional parallelotope, or simply n-parallelotope (or n-parallelepiped). Thus a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") is a 2-parallelotope and a parallelepiped is a 3-parallelotope.

The [diagonals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diagonals "Diagonals") of an _n_-parallelotope intersect at one point and are bisected by this point. [Inversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inversion_in_a_point "Inversion in a point") in this point leaves the _n_-parallelotope unchanged. See also _[Fixed points of isometry groups in Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixed\_points\_of\_isometry\_groups\_in\_Euclidean\_space "Fixed points of isometry groups in Euclidean space")_.

The edges radiating from one vertex of a _k_-parallelotope form a [_k_-frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K-frame "K-frame")![Image 38: {\displaystyle (v_{1},\ldots ,v_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8ab0b34f196f25857f60aa90852f6ebba17a21ba) of the vector space, and the parallelotope can be recovered from these vectors, by taking linear combinations of the vectors, with weights between 0 and 1.

The _n_-volume of an _n_-parallelotope embedded in ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a87a024931038d1858dc22e8a194e5978c3412e) where ![Image 40: {\displaystyle m\geq n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b02f25e62da7fe3162ac80446437cdc1c0fd341) can be computed by means of the [Gram determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gram_determinant "Gram determinant"). Alternatively, the volume is the norm of the [exterior product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exterior_product "Exterior product") of the vectors: ![Image 41: {\displaystyle V=\left\|v_{1}\wedge \cdots \wedge v_{n}\right\|.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/15f71d29cde188c5675c873fe4208817adaeafef)

If _m_ = _n_, this amounts to the absolute value of the determinant of [matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") formed by the components of the n vectors.

A formula to compute the volume of an n-parallelotope _P_ in ![Image 42: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d), whose _n_ + 1 vertices are ![Image 43: {\displaystyle V_{0},V_{1},\ldots ,V_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c8bfb0143f0191bd526384b60676a6643a789f2), is ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \mathrm {Vol} (P)=\left|\det \left(\left[V_{0}\ 1\right]^{\mathsf {T}},\left[V_{1}\ 1\right]^{\mathsf {T}},\ldots ,\left[V_{n}\ 1\right]^{\mathsf {T}}\right)\right|,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b6c1e6f94320ae11d0400b05f08f25e1a86e7f1f) where ![Image 45: {\displaystyle [V_{i}\ 1]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d2362bea0baa876bb7e76e2c43bbac55d2241798) is the row vector formed by the concatenation of the components of ![Image 46: {\displaystyle V_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f300b83673e961a9d48f3862216b167f94e5668c) and 1.

Similarly, the volume of any _n_-[simplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex "Simplex") that shares _n_ converging edges of a parallelotope has a volume equal to one 1/[_n_!](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Factorial "Factorial") of the volume of that parallelotope.

The term _parallelepiped_ stems from [Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek")[παραλληλεπίπεδον](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CF%80%CE%B1%CF%81%CE%B1%CE%BB%CE%BB%CE%B7%CE%BB%CE%B5%CF%80%CE%AF%CF%80%CE%B5%CE%B4%CE%BF%CE%BD#Ancient_Greek "wikt:παραλληλεπίπεδον") (_parallēlepípedon_, "body with parallel plane surfaces"), from _parallēl_ ("parallel") + _epípedon_ ("plane surface"), from _epí-_ ("on") + _pedon_ ("ground"). Thus the faces of a parallelepiped are planar, with opposite faces being parallel.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped#cite_note-oed-6)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped#cite_note-7)

In English, the term _parallelipipedon_ is attested in a 1570 translation of [Euclid's Elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements "Euclid's Elements") by [Henry Billingsley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Billingsley "Henry Billingsley"). The spelling _parallelepipedum_ is used in the 1644 edition of [Pierre Hérigone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_H%C3%A9rigone "Pierre Hérigone")'s _Cursus mathematicus_. In 1663, the present-day _parallelepiped_ is attested in [Walter Charleton's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Charleton "Walter Charleton")_Chorea gigantum_.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped#cite_note-oed-6)

[Charles Hutton's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Hutton "Charles Hutton") Dictionary (1795) shows _parallelopiped_ and _parallelopipedon_, showing the influence of the combining form _parallelo-_, as if the second element were _pipedon_ rather than _epipedon_. [Noah Webster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noah_Webster "Noah Webster") (1806) includes the spelling _parallelopiped_. The 1989 edition of the _Oxford English Dictionary_ describes _parallelopiped_ (and _parallelipiped_) explicitly as incorrect forms, but these are listed without comment in the 2004 edition, and only pronunciations with the emphasis on the fifth syllable _pi_ (/paɪ/) are given.
