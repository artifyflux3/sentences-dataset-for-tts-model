Chemical compound

**Phosphorus monoxide** is an unstable [radical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_(chemistry) "Radical (chemistry)")[inorganic compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inorganic_compound "Inorganic compound") with molecular formula [P](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus "Phosphorus")[O](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_monoxide#cite_note-EA-2019-2)

Phosphorus monoxide is notable as one of the few molecular compounds containing [phosphorus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus "Phosphorus") that has been detected outside of Earth. Other phosphorus containing molecules found in space include [PN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_mononitride "Phosphorus mononitride"), [PC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monophosphide "Carbon monophosphide"), PC 2, [HCP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylidynephosphane "Methylidynephosphane") and [PH 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine "Phosphine"). It was detected in the [circumstellar shell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumstellar_shell "Circumstellar shell") of [VY Canis Majoris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/VY_Canis_Majoris "VY Canis Majoris") and in the star forming region catalogued as [AFGL 5142](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=AFGL_5142&action=edit&redlink=1 "AFGL 5142 (page does not exist)"). The compound has been found to have been initially produced in [star-forming regions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_formation#Stellar_nurseries "Star formation"), and speculated to be carried by [interstellar comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_comet "Interstellar comet") throughout [outer space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_space "Outer space"), including to the [early Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_Earth "Early Earth").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_monoxide#cite_note-EA-2019-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_monoxide#cite_note-PHYS-20200115-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_monoxide#cite_note-ARX-2019-4)

Phosphorus monoxide plays a role in the phosphorescence of phosphorus.

Discovery
---------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Phosphorus_monoxide&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Discovery")]

In 1894 [W. N. Hartley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Noel_Hartley "Walter Noel Hartley") was the first to report an observation of ultraviolet emission from a phosphorus compound, that was later expanded on by Geuter. The source of the spectral lines and bands were known to be related to phosphorus, but the exact nature was unknown. In 1927 [H. J. Emeléus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harry_Julius_Emel%C3%A9us "Harry Julius Emeléus") and R. H. Purcell determined that the cause was a phosphorus oxide. But it was in 1921 that P. N. Ghosh and G. N. Ball determined that the oxide was phosphorus monoxide.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_monoxide#cite_note-mous-5)

Phosphorus monoxide is believed to be the most abundant phosphorus-containing molecule found in [interstellar clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_cloud "Interstellar cloud").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_monoxide#cite_note-Ligand-6) Phosphorus was identified as a cosmically abundant element in 1998 after researchers found a cosmic ratio of phosphorus to hydrogen (P/H) of about 3×10−7. Even with the prevalence of phosphorus in interstellar clouds, very few phosphorus bearing molecules had been identified and found in very few sources; phosphorus nitride, PN, and the free radical CP were found in a carbon rich envelope of [IRC +10215](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=IRC_%2B10215&action=edit&redlink=1 "IRC +10215 (page does not exist)") in 1987. This suggested that more phosphorus containing molecules had to be found in interstellar space. While examining the oxygen-rich shell of the supergiant star [VY Canis Majoris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/VY_Canis_Majoris "VY Canis Majoris") (VY CMa) the presence of PO was detected. VY CMa was studied using the [Submillimeter Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinrich_Hertz_Submillimeter_Telescope "Heinrich Hertz Submillimeter Telescope") (SMT) of the Arizona Radio Observatory (ARO). The telescope was able to observe the rotational frequencies of PO. ARO's 10 m SMT was able to measure the rotational transitions of PO showing J=5.5→4.5 at 240 GHz and J=6.5→5.5 at 284 GHz toward the evolved star, each consisting of well-defined lambda-doublets. Since the detection of PO towards the envelope of the VY CMa supergiant in 2001, PO has been found in many more interstellar clouds and is found in abundance around oxygen-rich shells.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_monoxide#cite_note-Space-7)

Formation
---------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Phosphorus_monoxide&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Formation")]

PO is formed when phosphorus is burnt in oxygen or ozone. It is a transient molecule observed in hot flames, or can be condensed into noble gas matrix.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_monoxide#cite_note-8) PO can be formed in an [inert gas matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inert_gas_matrix "Inert gas matrix") in the [photolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photodissociation "Photodissociation") of P 4 S 3 O, a [phosphorus oxysulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Phosphorus_oxysulfide&action=edit&redlink=1 "Phosphorus oxysulfide (page does not exist)").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_monoxide#cite_note-9)

On Earth, phosphorus monoxide can be prepared for study by spraying [phosphoric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphoric_acid "Phosphoric acid") into a flame. Because commercial [acetylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene "Acetylene") gas contains some [phosphine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine "Phosphine"), an [oxy-acetylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxy-acetylene "Oxy-acetylene") flame will have weak PO emission bands in its spectrum also. In the flame, PO oxidises back to P 4 O 10.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_monoxide#cite_note-hara-10)

Reactions
---------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Phosphorus_monoxide&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Reactions")]

### Phosphorescence

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Phosphorus_monoxide&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Phosphorescence")]

As white phosphorus oxidises it gives out a greenish-white glow. The glow happens as PO is oxidised by one of these reactions: PO + O• → PO 2; or PO + O 2 →PO 2 + O•.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_monoxide#cite_note-11) The possible ways that PO appears in this process is by breakup of the P 2 O molecule which in turn may come from P 4 O.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_monoxide#cite_note-12)

### Ligand

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Phosphorus_monoxide&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Ligand")]

Phosphorus monoxide can act as a [ligand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ligand "Ligand") on [transition elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transition_element "Transition element") such as [molybdenum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molybdenum "Molybdenum"), [ruthenium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruthenium "Ruthenium") and [osmium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osmium "Osmium"). The phosphorus forms a [triple bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple_bond "Triple bond") with the metal.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_monoxide#cite_note-13)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_monoxide#cite_note-14) The first to be discovered was on a [nickel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nickel "Nickel")-[tungsten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tungsten "Tungsten") cluster. The WNi 2 P 2 cluster was oxidised by a peroxide to yield a μ 3-coordination, where each phosphorus atom is bound to three metal atoms.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_monoxide#cite_note-15)

Properties
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Phosphorus_monoxide&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Properties")]

### Bond

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Phosphorus_monoxide&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Bond")]

Phosphorus monoxide is a [free radical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_radical "Free radical") with [phosphorus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus "Phosphorus") double bonded to [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") with phosphorus having an unpaired [valence electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valence_electron "Valence electron"). The [bond order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_order "Bond order") is about 1.8.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_monoxide#cite_note-mous-5) The P=O bond in PO has a dissociation energy of 6.4 eV.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_monoxide#cite_note-16) The bond length of the PO double bond is 1.476 Å, and free PO shows an infrared vibrational frequency of 1220 cm−1 due to the stretching of the bond.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_monoxide#cite_note-Structure-17) The free radical nature of PO makes it highly reactive and unstable compared to other phosphorus oxides that have been further oxidized.

### Spectrum

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Phosphorus_monoxide&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Spectrum")]

The visible to ultraviolet spectrum of phosphorus monoxide has three important bands. There is a continuum band near 540 nm. The β-system near 324 nm is due to the D 2 Σ→2 Π transition. The γ-system has bands near 246 nm due to a A 2 Σ→2 Π transition. Peaks in this band occur at 230, 238, 246, 253, and 260 nm in the ultraviolet. All these bands can be emission, absorption, or fluorescence depending on the method of illumination and temperature.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_monoxide#cite_note-hara-10) There is also a C'2 Δ state.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_monoxide#cite_note-18)

The γ-system band can be broken down into sub-bands based on the different vibrational transitions. (0,0), (0,1) and (1,0) are designations for the sub-bands produced by the transition between two vibration states, as the electronic transition occurs. Each of these contains eight series termed branches. These are o P 12, P 2, Q 2, R 2, P 1, Q 1, R 1 and s R 21.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_monoxide#cite_note-19)

### Molecule

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Phosphorus_monoxide&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Molecule")]

The ionisation potential of PO is 8.39 eV. When ionised, PO forms the cation PO+. The adiabatic [electron affinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_affinity "Electron affinity") of PO is 1.09 eV. On gaining an electron the PO− ion forms.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_monoxide#cite_note-mous-5)

r e in the ground state is 1.4763735 Å.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_monoxide#cite_note-mous-5)

The dipole moment of the molecule is 1.88 D. The phosphorus atom has a slight positive charge calculated as 0.35 of the electron.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_monoxide#cite_note-mous-5)
