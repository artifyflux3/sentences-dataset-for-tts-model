**Methanol** (also called **methyl alcohol** and **wood spirit**, amongst other names) is an organic [chemical compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_compound "Chemical compound") and the simplest [aliphatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aliphatic "Aliphatic")[alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohol_(chemistry) "Alcohol (chemistry)"), with the [chemical formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")[C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon")[H](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen")3[O](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen")H (a [methyl group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_group "Methyl group") linked to a [hydroxyl group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_group "Hydroxyl group"), often abbreviated as **MeOH**). It is a light, [volatile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volatility_(chemistry) "Volatility (chemistry)"), colorless and [flammable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flammable "Flammable") liquid with a distinctive alcoholic odor similar to that of [ethanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol "Ethanol") (potable alcohol), but is more acutely toxic than the latter.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-ershdb-17) Methanol acquired the name **wood alcohol** because it was once produced through [destructive distillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Destructive_distillation "Destructive distillation") of [wood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wood "Wood"). Today, methanol is mainly produced industrially by [hydrogenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogenation "Hydrogenation") of [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-Ullmann-18)

Methanol consists of a methyl group linked to a polar hydroxyl group. With more than 20 million tons produced annually, it is used as a [precursor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precursor_(chemistry) "Precursor (chemistry)") to other [commodity chemicals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commodity_chemicals "Commodity chemicals"), including [formaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde "Formaldehyde"), [acetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid "Acetic acid"), [methyl _tert_-butyl ether](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_tert-butyl_ether "Methyl tert-butyl ether"), [methyl benzoate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_benzoate "Methyl benzoate"), [anisole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anisole "Anisole"), [peroxyacids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peroxyacid "Peroxyacid"), as well as a host of more specialized chemicals.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-Ullmann-18)

Small amounts of methanol are present in normal, healthy human individuals. One study found a mean of 4.5[ppm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts_per_million "Parts per million") in the exhaled breath of test subjects.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-19) The mean endogenous methanol in humans of 0.45 g/d may be metabolized from [pectin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pectin "Pectin") found in fruit; one kilogram of apple produces up to 1.4 g of pectin (0.6 g of methanol.)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-20)

Methanol is produced by [anaerobic bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaerobic_organism "Anaerobic organism") and [phytoplankton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phytoplankton "Phytoplankton").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-21)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-22)

### Interstellar medium

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methanol&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Interstellar medium")]

Methanol is also found in abundant quantities in star-forming regions of space and is used in astronomy as a marker for such regions. It is detected through its spectral emission lines.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-23)

In 2006, astronomers using the [MERLIN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MERLIN "MERLIN") array of radio telescopes at [Jodrell Bank Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jodrell_Bank "Jodrell Bank") discovered a large cloud of methanol in space 0.463 [terametres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terametre "Terametre") (288 million miles) across.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-24)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-25) In 2016, astronomers detected methanol in a planet-forming disc around the young star [TW Hydrae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TW_Hydrae "TW Hydrae") using the [Atacama Large Millimeter Array](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atacama_Large_Millimeter_Array "Atacama Large Millimeter Array") radio telescope.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-26)

In their [embalming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embalming "Embalming") process, the [ancient Egyptians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egyptians "Ancient Egyptians") used a mixture of substances, including methanol, which they obtained from the [pyrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrolysis "Pyrolysis") of wood. Pure methanol, however, was first isolated in 1661 by [Robert Boyle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Boyle "Robert Boyle"), when he produced it via the distillation of [buxus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buxus "Buxus") (boxwood).[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-27) It later became known as "pyroxylic spirit". In 1834, the French chemists [Jean-Baptiste Dumas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Dumas "Jean-Baptiste Dumas") and [Eugene Peligot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eugene_Peligot "Eugene Peligot") determined its elemental composition.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-Dumas-28)

They also introduced the word "methylène" to organic chemistry, forming it from [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language")_[methy](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BC%CE%AD%CE%B8%CF%85#Ancient\_Greek "wiktionary:μέθυ")_ = "alcoholic liquid" + _[hȳlē](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%E1%BD%95%CE%BB%CE%B7#Ancient\_Greek "wiktionary:ὕλη")_ = "forest, wood, timber, material". "Methylène" designated a ["radical"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_theory "Radical theory") that was about 14% hydrogen by weight and contained one carbon atom. This would be CH 2, but at the time carbon was thought to have an [atomic weight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_weight "Atomic weight") only six times that of hydrogen, so they gave the formula as CH.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-Dumas-28) They then called wood alcohol (l'esprit de bois) "bihydrate de méthylène" (bihydrate because they thought the formula was C 4 H 8 O 4 or (CH)4(H 2 O)2). The term "methyl" was derived in about 1840 by [back-formation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back-formation "Back-formation") from "methylene", and was then applied to describe "methyl alcohol". This was shortened to "methanol" in 1892 by the [International Conference on Chemical Nomenclature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUPAC_nomenclature "IUPAC nomenclature").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-29) The [suffix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suffix_(linguistics) "Suffix (linguistics)")[-yl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/-yl "-yl"), which, in [organic chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_chemistry "Organic chemistry"), forms names of [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") groups, is from the word _methyl_.

French chemist [Paul Sabatier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Sabatier_(chemist) "Paul Sabatier (chemist)") presented the first [process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sabatier_reaction "Sabatier reaction") that could be used to produce methanol synthetically in 1905. This process suggested that carbon dioxide and hydrogen could be reacted to produce methanol.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-:1-9) German chemists [Alwin Mittasch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alwin_Mittasch "Alwin Mittasch") and Mathias Pier, working for [Badische-Anilin & Soda-Fabrik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BASF "BASF") (BASF), developed a means to convert [synthesis gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syngas "Syngas") (a mixture of [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide"), [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide"), and [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen")) into methanol and received a patent. According to Bozzano and Manenti, BASF's process was first utilized in [Leuna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leuna "Leuna"), Germany in 1923. Operating conditions consisted of "high" temperatures (between 300 and 400°C) and pressures (between 250 and 350 atm) with a [zinc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zinc_oxide "Zinc oxide")/[chromium oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromium(III)_oxide "Chromium(III) oxide") catalyst.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-:10-30)

US patent 1,569,775 was applied for on 4 September 1924 and issued on 12 January 1926 to BASF;[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-31) the process used a [chromium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromium(III)_oxide "Chromium(III) oxide") and [manganese oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manganese_oxide "Manganese oxide")[catalyst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalyst "Catalyst") with extremely vigorous conditions: pressures ranging from 50 to 220 [atm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_(unit) "Atmosphere (unit)"), and temperatures up to 450°C. Modern methanol production has been made more efficient through use of catalysts (commonly copper) capable of operating at lower pressures. The modern low pressure methanol (LPM) process was developed by [ICI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_Chemical_Industries "Imperial Chemical Industries") in the late 1960s with the technology patent long since expired.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-32)

During [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), methanol was used as a fuel in several German military rocket designs, under the name [M-Stoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_stoffs "List of stoffs"), and in a roughly 50/50 mixture with [hydrazine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrazine "Hydrazine"), known as [C-Stoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C-Stoff "C-Stoff").

The use of methanol as a motor fuel received attention during the [oil crises of the 1970s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1970s_energy_crisis "1970s energy crisis"). By the mid-1990s, over 20,000 methanol [flexible fuel vehicles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flexible_fuel_vehicle "Flexible fuel vehicle") (FFVs) capable of operating on methanol or gasoline were introduced in the US. In addition, low levels of methanol were blended in gasoline fuels sold in Europe during much of the 1980s and early-1990s. Automakers stopped building methanol FFVs by the late-1990s, switching their attention to ethanol-fueled vehicles. While the methanol FFV program was a technical success, rising methanol pricing in the mid- to late-1990s during a period of slumping gasoline pump prices diminished interest in [methanol fuels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol_fuel "Methanol fuel").[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-33)

In the early 1970s, a process was developed by [Mobil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mobil "Mobil") for [producing gasoline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol_to_gasoline "Methanol to gasoline") fuel from methanol.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-34)

Between the 1960s and 1980s methanol emerged as a precursor to the feedstock chemicals acetic acid and [acetic anhydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_anhydride "Acetic anhydride"). These processes include the [Monsanto acetic acid synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monsanto_acetic_acid_synthesis "Monsanto acetic acid synthesis"), [Cativa process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cativa_process "Cativa process"), and [Tennessee Eastman acetic anhydride process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monsanto_process#Tennessee_Eastman_acetic_anhydride_process "Monsanto process").

### Production of formaldehyde, acetic acid, methyl _tert_-butyl ether

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methanol&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Production of formaldehyde, acetic acid, methyl tert-butyl ether")]

Methanol is primarily converted to formaldehyde, which is widely used in many areas, especially [polymers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymer "Polymer"). The conversion entails oxidation:

2 CH 3 OH + O 2 → 2 CH 2 O + 2 H 2 O
[Acetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid "Acetic acid") can be produced from methanol.

[![Image 21](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3a/Cativa-process-catalytic-cycle.png/500px-Cativa-process-catalytic-cycle.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cativa-process-catalytic-cycle.png)

The [Cativa process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cativa_process "Cativa process") converts methanol into [acetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetic_acid "Acetic acid").[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-Cativa-35)

Methanol and [isobutene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isobutene "Isobutene") are combined to give [methyl _tert_-butyl ether](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_tert-butyl_ether "Methyl tert-butyl ether") (MTBE). MTBE is a major octane booster in gasoline.

### Methanol to hydrocarbons, olefins, gasoline

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methanol&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Methanol to hydrocarbons, olefins, gasoline")]

Condensation of methanol to produce [hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon") and even [aromatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aromatic_hydrocarbon "Aromatic hydrocarbon") systems is the basis of several technologies related to [gas to liquids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_to_liquids "Gas to liquids"). These include methanol-to-hydrocarbons (MtH), methanol to gasoline (MtG), methanol to olefins (MtO), and methanol to propylene (MtP). These conversions are catalyzed by [zeolites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeolite "Zeolite") as [heterogeneous catalysts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heterogeneous_catalyst "Heterogeneous catalyst"). The MtG process was once commercialized at [Motunui](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motunui "Motunui") in New Zealand.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-36)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-37)

The [European Fuel Quality Directive](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=European_Fuel_Quality_Directive&action=edit&redlink=1 "European Fuel Quality Directive (page does not exist)") allows fuel producers to blend up to 3% methanol, with an equal amount of cosolvent, with gasoline sold in Europe. In 2019, it is estimated that [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") used as much as 7 million tons of methanol as transportation fuels, representing over 5% of their fuel pool.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-38)

Methanol is the precursor to most simple [methylamines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylamine "Methylamine"), [methyl halides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_halide "Methyl halide"), and methyl ethers.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-Ullmann-18) Methyl esters are produced from methanol, including the transesterification of fats and production of [biodiesel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biodiesel "Biodiesel") via [transesterification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transesterification "Transesterification").[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-39)[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-40)

### Niche and potential uses

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methanol&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Niche and potential uses")]

Methanol is a promising [energy carrier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_carrier "Energy carrier") because, as a liquid, it is easier to store than hydrogen and natural gas. Its [energy density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density "Energy density") is, however, lower than [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane"), per kg. Its combustion energy density is 15.6 [MJ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joule "Joule")/[L](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liter "Liter") ([LHV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_of_combustion#Lower_heating_value "Heat of combustion")), whereas that of ethanol is 24 and gasoline is 33 MJ/L.

Further advantages for methanol is its ready biodegradability and low environmental toxicity. It does not persist in either aerobic (oxygen-present) or anaerobic (oxygen-absent) environments. The half-life for methanol in groundwater is just one to seven days, while many common gasoline components have half-lives in the hundreds of days (such as [benzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene "Benzene") at 10–730 days). Since methanol is [miscible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miscibility "Miscibility") with water and biodegradable, it is unlikely to accumulate in groundwater, surface water, air or soil.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-41)

Methanol is occasionally used to fuel [internal combustion engines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine "Internal combustion engine"). It burns forming carbon dioxide and water:

2 CH 3 OH + 3 O 2 → 2 CO 2 + 4 H 2 O
Methanol fuel has been proposed for ground transportation. The chief advantage of a methanol economy is that it could be adapted to gasoline internal combustion engines with minimum modification to the engines and to the infrastructure that delivers and stores liquid fuel. Its energy density, however, is less than gasoline, meaning more frequent fill ups would be required. However, it is equivalent to super high-octane gasoline in horsepower, and most modern computer-controlled fuel injection systems can already use it.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-42)

Methanol is an alternative fuel for ships that helps the shipping industry meet increasingly strict emissions regulations. It significantly reduces emissions of [sulfur oxides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_oxide "Sulfur oxide") (SO _x_), [nitrogen oxides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NOx "NOx") (NO _x_) and particulate matter. Methanol can be used with high efficiency in marine diesel engines after minor modifications using a small amount of pilot fuel (dual fuel).[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-43)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-44)

In China, methanol fuels industrial boilers, which are used extensively to generate heat and steam for various industrial applications and residential heating. Its use is displacing coal, which is under pressure from increasingly stringent environmental regulations.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-45)

[Direct-methanol fuel cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct-methanol_fuel_cell "Direct-methanol fuel cell") are unique in their low temperature, atmospheric pressure operation, which lets them be greatly miniaturized.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-46)[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-47) This, combined with the relatively easy and safe storage and handling of methanol, may open the possibility of fuel cell-powered [consumer electronics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consumer_electronics "Consumer electronics"), such as laptop computers and mobile phones.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-48)

Methanol is also a widely used fuel in camping and boating stoves. Methanol burns well in an unpressurized burner, so alcohol stoves are often very simple, sometimes little more than a cup to hold fuel. This lack of complexity makes them a favorite of hikers who spend extended time in the wilderness. Similarly, the alcohol can be gelled to reduce risk of leaking or spilling, as with the brand "[Sterno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sterno "Sterno")".

Methanol is mixed with water and injected into high performance diesel and gasoline engines for an increase of power and a decrease in intake air temperature in a process known as [water methanol injection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_injection_(engines) "Water injection (engines)").

Methanol is used as a [denaturant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denaturation_(food) "Denaturation (food)") for ethanol, the product being known as _denatured alcohol_ or _methylated spirit_. This was commonly used during the [US prohibition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/US_prohibition "US prohibition") to discourage consumption of [bootlegged](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moonshine "Moonshine") liquor, and ended up causing several deaths.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-49)

It is sometimes used as a fuel in alcohol lamps, portable fire pits and camping stoves.

Methanol is used as a [solvent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvent "Solvent") and as an [antifreeze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antifreeze_(coolant) "Antifreeze (coolant)") in [pipelines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pipeline_transport "Pipeline transport") and [windshield washer fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Windshield_washer_fluid "Windshield washer fluid"). Methanol was used as an automobile coolant antifreeze in the early 1900s.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-50) As of May 2018, methanol was banned in the EU for use in windscreen washing or defrosting due to its risk of human consumption[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-51)[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-52) as a result of [2012 Czech Republic methanol poisonings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2012_Czech_Republic_methanol_poisonings "2012 Czech Republic methanol poisonings").[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-53)

In some [wastewater treatment plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sewage_treatment "Sewage treatment"), a small amount of methanol is added to [wastewater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wastewater "Wastewater") to provide a carbon food source for the [denitrifying bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denitrifying_bacteria "Denitrifying bacteria"), which convert [nitrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrate "Nitrate") to [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") gas and reduce the nitrification of sensitive [aquifers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aquifer "Aquifer").

Methanol is used as a destaining agent in [polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyacrylamide_gel_electrophoresis "Polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis").

Carbon monoxide and hydrogen react over a catalyst to produce methanol. Today, the most widely used catalyst is a mixture of copper and [zinc oxides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zinc_oxide "Zinc oxide"), [supported](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalyst_support "Catalyst support") on [alumina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alumina "Alumina"), as first used by [ICI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_Chemical_Industries "Imperial Chemical Industries") in 1966. At 5–10 MPa (50–100 atm) and 250°C (482°F), the reaction

CO + 2 H 2 → CH 3 OH
is characterized by high selectivity (>99.8%). The production of [synthesis gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syngas "Syngas") from methane produces three [moles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mole_(unit) "Mole (unit)") of hydrogen for every mole of carbon monoxide, whereas the synthesis consumes only two moles of hydrogen gas per mole of carbon monoxide. One way of dealing with the excess hydrogen is to inject [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide") into the methanol synthesis reactor, where it, too, reacts to form methanol according to the equation

CO 2 + 3 H 2 → CH 3 OH + H 2 O
In terms of mechanism, the process occurs via initial conversion of CO into CO 2, which is then [hydrogenated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogenation "Hydrogenation"):[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-54)

CO 2 + 3 H 2 → CH 3 OH + H 2 O
where the H 2 O byproduct is recycled via the [water-gas shift reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_gas_shift_reaction "Water gas shift reaction")

CO + H 2 O → CO 2 + H 2
This gives an overall reaction

CO + 2 H 2 → CH 3 OH
which is the same as listed above. In a process closely related to methanol production from synthesis gas, a feed of hydrogen and CO 2 can be used directly.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-55) The main advantage of this process is that [captured CO 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage "Carbon capture and storage") and hydrogen sourced from [electrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water "Electrolysis of water") could be used, removing the dependence on fossil fuels.

The catalytic conversion of methane to methanol is effected by enzymes including [methane monooxygenases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane_monooxygenase "Methane monooxygenase"). These enzymes are mixed-function oxygenases, i.e. oxygenation is coupled with production of water[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-56) and [NAD+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicotinamide_adenine_dinucleotide "Nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide"):[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-:0-57)

CH 4 + O 2 + NADPH + H+ → CH 3 OH + H 2 O + NAD+
Both Fe- and Cu-dependent enzymes have been characterized.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-:0-57) Intense but largely fruitless efforts have been undertaken to emulate this reactivity.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-Alayon-58)[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-Catalytic_and_Mechanic_Insights-59) Methanol is more easily oxidized than is the feedstock methane, so the reactions tend not to be selective. Some strategies exist to circumvent this problem. Examples include [Shilov systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shilov_system "Shilov system") and Fe- and Cu-containing zeolites.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-60) These systems do not necessarily mimic the mechanisms employed by [metalloenzymes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metalloenzymes "Metalloenzymes"), but draw some inspiration from them. Active sites can vary substantially from those known in the enzymes. For example, a dinuclear active site is proposed in the [sMMO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane_monooxygenase "Methane monooxygenase") enzyme, whereas a mononuclear iron ([alpha-oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha-Oxygen "Alpha-Oxygen")) is proposed in the Fe-zeolite.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-61)

Global emissions of methanol by plants are estimated at between 180 and 250 million tons per year.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-62) This is between two and three times larger than man-made industrial production of methanol.

As of 2023, 0.2% of global methanol production is produced in ways that have relatively low greenhouse gas emissions; this is known as "green" methanol.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-:2-63) Most green methanol is produced from gasification of [biomass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomass_(energy) "Biomass (energy)").[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-:2-63)[Syngas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syngas "Syngas") is produced from biomass [gasification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gasification "Gasification") and further converted into green methanol.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-64)

Another method of producing green methanol involves combining hydrogen, carbon dioxide, and a catalyst under high heat and pressure.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-:2-63) To be classified as green methanol, the hydrogen must be [produced using renewable electricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_hydrogen "Green hydrogen"),[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-:2-63) and the carbon dioxide must be from [carbon capture and storage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage "Carbon capture and storage"), [direct air capture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_air_capture "Direct air capture") or biomass of recent origin.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-:2-63) Some definitions of green methanol specify that the carbon dioxide must be captured during the burning of [bioenergy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bioenergy "Bioenergy").[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-65)

Quality specifications and analysis
-----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methanol&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Quality specifications and analysis")]

Methanol is available commercially in various purity grades. Commercial methanol is generally classified according to ASTM purity grades A and AA. Both grade A and grade AA purity are 99.85% methanol by weight. Grade "AA" methanol contains trace amounts of ethanol as well.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-:10-30)

Methanol for chemical use normally corresponds to Grade AA. In addition to water, typical impurities include [acetone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone "Acetone") and ethanol (which are very difficult to separate by distillation). UV-vis spectroscopy is a convenient method for detecting aromatic impurities. Water content can be determined by the [Karl-Fischer titration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Fischer_titration "Karl Fischer titration").

Methanol is highly flammable. Its vapours are slightly denser than air and can travel to a distant ignition source and ignite. Methanol fires should be extinguished with [dry chemical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dry_chemical "Dry chemical"), [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide"), water spray or alcohol-resistant foam.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-CDC_card-12) Methanol flames are invisible in daylight.

Ingesting as little as 10 mL (0.34 US fl oz) of pure methanol can cause permanent blindness by destruction of the [optic nerve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optic_nerve "Optic nerve"). 30 mL (1.0 US fl oz) is potentially fatal.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-Vale-66) The median lethal dose is 100 mL (3.4 US fl oz), _i.e._, 1–2 mL/kg body weight of pure methanol.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-67) The [reference dose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reference_dose "Reference dose") for methanol is 0.5 mg/kg in a day.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-68)[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-69) Toxic effects begin hours after ingestion, and antidotes can often prevent permanent damage.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-Vale-66) Because of its similarities in both appearance and odor to [ethanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol "Ethanol") (the alcohol in beverages) or [isopropyl alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isopropyl_alcohol "Isopropyl alcohol"), it is difficult to differentiate between the three.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-70)

Methanol is [toxic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxicity "Toxicity") by two mechanisms. First, methanol can be fatal due to effects on the central nervous system, acting as a [central nervous system depressant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_nervous_system_depression "Central nervous system depression") in the same manner as [ethanol poisoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohol_intoxication#Acute_alcohol_poisoning "Alcohol intoxication"). Second, in a process of [toxication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxication "Toxication"), it is [metabolised](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism "Metabolism") to [formic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid "Formic acid") (which is present as the formate ion) via [formaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde "Formaldehyde") in a process initiated by the [enzyme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enzyme "Enzyme")[alcohol dehydrogenase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohol_dehydrogenase "Alcohol dehydrogenase") in the [liver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liver "Liver").[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-Schep-71) Methanol is converted to formaldehyde via alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) and formaldehyde is converted to formic acid (formate) via [aldehyde dehydrogenase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aldehyde_dehydrogenase "Aldehyde dehydrogenase") (ALDH). The conversion to formate via ALDH proceeds completely, with no detectable formaldehyde remaining.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-noCHOH-72) Formate is toxic because it inhibits mitochondrial [cytochrome c oxidase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytochrome_c_oxidase "Cytochrome c oxidase"), causing [hypoxia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypoxia_(medical) "Hypoxia (medical)") at the cellular level, and [metabolic acidosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolic_acidosis "Metabolic acidosis"), among a variety of other metabolic disturbances.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-73)

Outbreaks of methanol poisoning have occurred primarily due to contamination of [drinking alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanol "Ethanol"). It is also worth noting that ethanol is a natural antidote to methanol poisoning.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-74) Contaminated batches of alcohol contained a much higher concentration of methanol than ethanol, which did not provide a sufficient dose of the antidote and caused it to be ineffective. This is more common in the [developing world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developing_country "Developing country").[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-EM2016-75) In 2013 more than 1700 cases nonetheless occurred in the United States. Those affected are often adult men.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-Fer2016-76) Outcomes may be good with early treatment.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-Kr2012-77) Toxicity to methanol was described as early as 1856.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol#cite_note-78)

Because of its toxic properties, methanol is frequently used as a denaturant additive for ethanol manufactured for industrial uses. This addition of methanol exempts industrial ethanol (commonly known as "[denatured alcohol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denatured_alcohol "Denatured alcohol")" or "methylated spirit") from liquor [excise taxation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excise_tax_in_the_United_States "Excise tax in the United States") in the US and other countries.
