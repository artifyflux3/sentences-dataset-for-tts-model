Speed at which relativistic effects become significant

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/60px-Question_book-new.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg)This article **needs additional citations for [verification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability "Wikipedia:Verifiability")**. _Find sources:_["Relativistic speed"](https://www.google.com/search?as_eq=wikipedia&q=%22Relativistic+speed%22)–[news](https://www.google.com/search?tbm=nws&q=%22Relativistic+speed%22+-wikipedia&tbs=ar:1)**·**[newspapers](https://www.google.com/search?&q=%22Relativistic+speed%22&tbs=bkt:s&tbm=bks)**·**[books](https://www.google.com/search?tbs=bks:1&q=%22Relativistic+speed%22+-wikipedia)**·**[scholar](https://scholar.google.com/scholar?q=%22Relativistic+speed%22)**·**[JSTOR](https://www.jstor.org/action/doBasicSearch?Query=%22Relativistic+speed%22&acc=on&wc=on)_(September 2016)_ _([Learn how and when to remove this message](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Maintenance\_template\_removal "Help:Maintenance template removal"))_

**Relativistic speed** refers to speed at which [relativistic effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_chemistry "Relativistic quantum chemistry") become significant to the desired accuracy of measurement of the phenomenon being observed. Relativistic effects are those discrepancies between values calculated by models considering and not considering [relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_relativity "Theory of relativity").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_speed#cite_note-1) Related words are [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity"), [rapidity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rapidity "Rapidity"), and [celerity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proper_velocity "Proper velocity") which is [proper velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proper_velocity "Proper velocity"). [Speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed "Speed") is a [scalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(mathematics) "Scalar (mathematics)"), being the [magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitude_(vector) "Magnitude (vector)") of the velocity [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(mathematics_and_physics) "Vector (mathematics and physics)") which in relativity is the [four-velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-velocity "Four-velocity") and in three-dimension Euclidean space a three-velocity. Speed is [empirically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empirical_research "Empirical research") measured as average speed, although [current devices in common use](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lidar "Lidar") can estimate speed over very small [intervals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)") and closely approximate [instantaneous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instant "Instant") speed. Non-relativistic discrepancies include [cosine error](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosine_error_effect "Cosine error effect") which occurs in [speed detection devices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radar_gun "Radar gun") when only one scalar component of the three-velocity is measured and the [Doppler effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doppler_effect "Doppler effect") which may affect observations of wavelength and frequency.

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Lorentz_factor.svg/500px-Lorentz_factor.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Lorentz_factor.svg)

[Lorentz factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_factor "Lorentz factor"), _γ,_ as a function of speed, _v_. Its initial value is 1 when speed is zero and increases without bound as speed approaches light speed, _c_.

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Lorentz_factor_inverse.svg/500px-Lorentz_factor_inverse.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Lorentz_factor_inverse.svg)

Inverse of Lorentz factor as a function of speed, _v_, as a proportion of light speed, _c_ - a circular arc.

Relativistic effects are highly [non-linear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonlinear_system "Nonlinear system") and for everyday purposes are insignificant because the [Newtonian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion "Newton's laws of motion") model closely approximates the relativity model. In special relativity the [Lorentz factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_factor "Lorentz factor") is a measure of [time dilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_dilation "Time dilation"), [length contraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length_contraction "Length contraction") and the relativistic [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_Mass "Relativistic Mass") increase of a moving object.
