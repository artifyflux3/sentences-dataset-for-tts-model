**Ammonium** is a modified form of [ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia") that has an extra hydrogen atom. It is a positively charged ([cationic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cation "Cation")) [molecular ion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyatomic_ion "Polyatomic ion") with the [chemical formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")NH+4 or [NH 4]+. It is formed by the [addition of a proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonation "Protonation") (a hydrogen nucleus) to ammonia (NH 3). Ammonium is also a general name for positively charged (protonated) substituted [amines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amine "Amine") and [quaternary ammonium cations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternary_ammonium_cation "Quaternary ammonium cation") ([NR 4]+), where one or more [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen")[atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atoms "Atoms") are replaced by [organic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound") or other groups (indicated by R). Not only is ammonium a source of [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") and a key [metabolite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolite "Metabolite") for many living organisms, but it is an integral part of the global [nitrogen cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle "Nitrogen cycle").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium#cite_note-:02-2) As such, human impact in recent years could have an effect on the biological communities that depend on it.

Acid–base properties
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ammonium&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Acid–base properties")]

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a0/Hydrochloric_acid_ammonia.jpg/250px-Hydrochloric_acid_ammonia.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hydrochloric_acid_ammonia.jpg)

Fumes from [hydrochloric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid "Hydrochloric acid") and ammonia forming a white cloud of [ammonium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_chloride "Ammonium chloride")

The ammonium ion is generated when ammonia, a weak base, reacts with [Brønsted acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Br%C3%B8nsted%E2%80%93Lowry_acid%E2%80%93base_theory "Brønsted–Lowry acid–base theory") ([proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") donors):

H+ + NH 3 → [NH 4]+
The ammonium ion is mildly acidic, reacting with Brønsted bases to return to the uncharged ammonia molecule:

[NH 4]+ + B− → HB + NH 3
Thus, the treatment of concentrated solutions of ammonium salts with a strong base gives ammonia. When ammonia is dissolved in water, a tiny amount of it converts to ammonium ions:

H 2 O + NH 3 ⇌ OH− + [NH 4]+
The degree to which ammonia forms the ammonium ion depends on the [pH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PH "PH") of the solution. If the pH is low, the equilibrium shifts to the right: more ammonia molecules are converted into ammonium ions. If the pH is high (the concentration of [hydrogen ions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_ion "Hydrogen ion") is low and [hydroxide ions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxide_ion "Hydroxide ion") is high), the equilibrium shifts to the left: the [hydroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxide "Hydroxide") ion abstracts a proton from the ammonium ion, generating ammonia.

Formation of ammonium compounds can also occur in the [vapor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vapor "Vapor") phase; for example, when ammonia vapor comes in contact with hydrogen chloride vapor, a white cloud of ammonium chloride forms, which eventually settles out as a [solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid "Solid") in a thin white layer on surfaces.

Salts and characteristic reactions
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ammonium&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Salts and characteristic reactions")]

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e0/Bildung_Ammonium.svg/250px-Bildung_Ammonium.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bildung_Ammonium.svg)

Formation of ammonium

Ammonium cation is found in a variety of [salts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt_(chemistry) "Salt (chemistry)") such as [ammonium carbonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_carbonate "Ammonium carbonate"), [ammonium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_chloride "Ammonium chloride"), and [ammonium nitrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_nitrate "Ammonium nitrate"). Most simple ammonium salts are very [soluble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soluble "Soluble") in water. An exception is [ammonium hexachloroplatinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_hexachloroplatinate "Ammonium hexachloroplatinate"), the formation of which was once used as a test for ammonium. The ammonium salts of nitrate and especially [perchlorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perchlorate "Perchlorate") are highly explosive, in these cases, ammonium is the reducing agent.

In an unusual process, ammonium ions form an [amalgam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amalgam_(chemistry) "Amalgam (chemistry)"). Such species are prepared by the addition of sodium amalgam to a solution of ammonium chloride.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium#cite_note-3) This amalgam eventually decomposes to release ammonia and hydrogen.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium#cite_note-4)

To find whether the ammonium ion is present in the salt, first, the salt is heated in presence of [alkali hydroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkali_hydroxide "Alkali hydroxide") releasing a gas with a characteristic smell, which is [ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia").

[NH 4]+ + OH−_heat_→NH 3 + H 2 O
To further confirm ammonia, it is passed through a glass rod dipped in an HCl solution ([hydrochloric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid "Hydrochloric acid")), creating white dense fumes of [ammonium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_chloride "Ammonium chloride").

NH 3(g) + HCl(aq) → [NH 4]Cl(s)
Ammonia, when passed through CuSO 4 ([copper(II) sulfate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper(II)_sulfate "Copper(II) sulfate")) solution, changes its color from blue to deep blue, forming [Schweizer's reagent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schweizer%27s_reagent "Schweizer's reagent").

CuSO 4(aq) + 4 NH 3(aq) + 4 H 2 O → [Cu(NH 3)4(H 2 O)2](OH)2(aq) + H 2 SO 4(aq)
Ammonia or ammonium ion when added to [Nessler's reagent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nessler%27s_reagent "Nessler's reagent") gives a brown color precipitate known as the iodide of Million's base in basic medium.

Ammonium ion when added to [chloroplatinic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloroplatinic_acid "Chloroplatinic acid") gives a yellow precipitate of [ammonium hexachloroplatinate(IV)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_hexachloroplatinate "Ammonium hexachloroplatinate").

H 2[PtCl 6](aq) + [NH 4]+(aq) → [NH 4]2[PtCl 6](s) + 2 H+
Ammonium ion when added to [sodium cobaltinitrite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_cobaltinitrite "Sodium cobaltinitrite") gives a yellow precipitate of ammonium cobaltinitrite.

Na 3[Co(NO 2)6](aq) + 3 [NH 4]+(aq) → [NH 4]3[Co(NO 2)6](s) + 3 Na+(aq)
Ammonium ion gives a white precipitate of ammonium bitartrate when added to [potassium bitartrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_bitartrate "Potassium bitartrate").

KC 4 H 5 O 6(aq) + [NH 4]+(aq) → [NH 4]C 4 H 5 O 6(s) + K+(aq)

Structure and bonding
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ammonium&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Structure and bonding")]

The [lone electron pair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lone_pair "Lone pair") on the [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") atom (N) in ammonia, represented as a line above the N, forms a [coordinate bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_covalent_bond "Coordinate covalent bond") with a [proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") (H+). After that, all four N−H bonds are equivalent, being polar [covalent bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covalent_bond "Covalent bond"). The ion has a [tetrahedral structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedral_molecular_geometry "Tetrahedral molecular geometry") and is [isoelectronic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isoelectronicity "Isoelectronicity") with [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane") and the [borohydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borohydride "Borohydride") anion. In terms of size, the ammonium cation (_r_ ionic=175 pm)[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] resembles the [caesium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caesium "Caesium") cation (_r_ ionic=183 pm).[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

The hydrogen atoms in the ammonium ion can be substituted with an [alkyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkyl "Alkyl") group or some other organic group to form a **substituted ammonium ion** ([IUPAC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUPAC "IUPAC") nomenclature: **aminium ion**). Depending on the number of organic groups, the ammonium cation is called a [primary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_(chemistry) "Primary (chemistry)"), [secondary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secondary_(chemistry) "Secondary (chemistry)"), [tertiary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tertiary_(chemistry) "Tertiary (chemistry)"), or [quaternary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternary_(chemistry) "Quaternary (chemistry)"). Except the quaternary ammonium cations, the organic ammonium cations are weak acids.

An example of a reaction forming an ammonium ion is that between [dimethylamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimethylamine "Dimethylamine"), (CH 3)2 NH, and an acid to give the [dimethylammonium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimethylammonium "Dimethylammonium") cation, [(CH 3)2 NH 2]+:

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6b/Dimethylammonium-formation-2D.png/500px-Dimethylammonium-formation-2D.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Dimethylammonium-formation-2D.png)
Quaternary ammonium cations have four organic groups attached to the nitrogen atom, they lack a hydrogen atom bonded to the nitrogen atom. These cations, such as the [tetra-_n_-butylammonium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetra-n-butylammonium "Tetra-n-butylammonium") cation, are sometimes used to replace sodium or potassium ions to increase the [solubility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solubility "Solubility") of the associated anion in organic [solvents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvents "Solvents"). Primary, secondary, and tertiary ammonium salts serve the same function but are less [lipophilic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipophilic "Lipophilic"). They are also used as [phase-transfer catalysts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase-transfer_catalysts "Phase-transfer catalysts") and [surfactants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surfactant "Surfactant").

An unusual class of organic ammonium salts is derivatives of amine [radical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_(chemistry) "Radical (chemistry)") cations, [•NR 3]+ such as [tris(4-bromophenyl)ammoniumyl hexachloroantimonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tris(4-bromophenyl)ammoniumyl_hexachloroantimonate "Tris(4-bromophenyl)ammoniumyl hexachloroantimonate").

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Nitrogen_Cycle_2.svg/330px-Nitrogen_Cycle_2.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Nitrogen_Cycle_2.svg)

Ammonium exists as a result of ammonification and [decomposers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decomposer "Decomposer"). Ammonium is eventually nitrified, where it contributes to the flow of [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") through the ecosystem. Human impacts are not shown here, but can impact the global [nitrogen cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_cycle "Nitrogen cycle").

Because [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") often limits net [primary production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_production "Primary production") due to its use in [enzymes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enzyme "Enzyme") that mediate the biochemical reactions that are necessary for life, ammonium is utilized by some microbes and plants.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium#cite_note-:03-5) For example, energy is released by the [oxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redox "Redox") of ammonium in a process known as [nitrification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrification "Nitrification"), which produces [nitrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrate "Nitrate") and [nitrite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrite "Nitrite").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium#cite_note-:1-6) This process is a form of [autotrophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autotrophy "Autotrophy") that is common amongst _[Nitrosomonas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrosomonas "Nitrosomonas")_, _[Nitrobacter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrobacter "Nitrobacter")_, _[Nitrosolobus](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nitrosolobus&action=edit&redlink=1 "Nitrosolobus (page does not exist)")_, and _[Nitrosospira](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nitrosospira&action=edit&redlink=1 "Nitrosospira (page does not exist)")_, amongst others.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium#cite_note-:1-6)

The amount of ammonium in soil that is available for [nitrification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrification "Nitrification") by microbes varies depending on environmental conditions.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium#cite_note-8) For example, ammonium is deposited as a waste product from some animals, although it is converted into [urea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urea "Urea") in mammals, sharks, and amphibians, and into [uric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uric_acid "Uric acid") in birds, reptiles, and terrestrial snails.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium#cite_note-9) Its availability in soils is also influenced by [mineralization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mineralization_(soil_science) "Mineralization (soil science)"), which makes more ammonium available from organic [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") sources, and [immobilization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immobilization_(soil_science) "Immobilization (soil science)"), which sequesters ammonium into organic [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") sources, both of which are mitigated by biological factors.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium#cite_note-:1-6)

Conversely, [nitrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrate "Nitrate") and [nitrite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrite "Nitrite") can be reduced to ammonium as a way for living organisms to access [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") for growth in a process known as assimilatory nitrate reduction.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium#cite_note-:2-10) Once assimilated, it can be incorporated into [proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein "Protein") and [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA "DNA").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium#cite_note-11)

Ammonium can accumulate in soils where [nitrification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrification "Nitrification") is slow or inhibited, which is common in hypoxic soils.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium#cite_note-12) For example, ammonium mobilization is one of the key factors for the [symbiotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbiosis "Symbiosis") association between plants and fungi, called [mycorrhizae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mycorrhiza "Mycorrhiza").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium#cite_note-13) However, plants that consistently utilize ammonium as a [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") source often must invest into more extensive root systems due to ammonium's limited mobility in soils compared to other [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") sources.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium#cite_note-14)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium#cite_note-15)

Ammonium deposition from the atmosphere has increased in recent years due to volatilization from livestock waste and increased fertilizer use.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium#cite_note-16) Because net [primary production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_production "Primary production") is often limited by [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen"), increased ammonium levels could impact the biological communities that rely on it. For example, increasing [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") content has been shown to increase plant growth, but aggravate soil phosphorus levels, which can impact microbial communities.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium#cite_note-17)

The ammonium cation has very similar properties to the heavier [alkali metal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkali_metal "Alkali metal") cations and is often considered a close equivalent.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium#cite_note-Holleman&Wiberg-18)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium#cite_note-Stevenson-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium#cite_note-Bernal&Massey-20) Ammonium is expected to behave as a [metal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal "Metal") ([NH 4]+ ions in a sea of [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrons "Electrons")) at very high pressures, such as inside [giant planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_planet "Giant planet") such as [Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus "Uranus") and [Neptune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune "Neptune").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium#cite_note-Stevenson-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium#cite_note-Bernal&Massey-20)

Under normal conditions, ammonium does not exist as a pure metal but does as an [amalgam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amalgam_(chemistry) "Amalgam (chemistry)") (alloy with [mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(element) "Mercury (element)")).[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium#cite_note-21)
