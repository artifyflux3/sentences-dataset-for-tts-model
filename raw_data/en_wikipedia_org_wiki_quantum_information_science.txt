**Quantum information science** is a field that combines the principles of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") with [information theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_theory "Information theory") to study the processing, analysis, and transmission of information. It covers both theoretical and experimental aspects of quantum physics, including the limits of what can be achieved with [quantum information](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_information "Quantum information"). The term **quantum information theory** is sometimes used, but it refers to the theoretical aspects of information processing and does not include experimental research.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_information_science#cite_note-1)

At its core, quantum information science explores how [information](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information "Information") behaves when [stored](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_retrieval "Information retrieval") and manipulated using quantum systems. Unlike classical information, which is encoded in bits that can only be 0 or 1, quantum information uses quantum bits or [qubits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qubit "Qubit") that can exist simultaneously in multiple states because of [superposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_superposition "Quantum superposition"). Additionally, [entanglement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_entanglement "Quantum entanglement")—a uniquely quantum linkage between particles—enables correlations that have no classical counterpart.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_information_science#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_information_science#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_information_science#cite_note-4) This new way of handling information opens up transformative possibilities in computation, communication, and [sensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_sensor "Quantum sensor").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_information_science#cite_note-5)

Scientific and engineering studies
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_information_science&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Scientific and engineering studies")]

Quantum information science is inherently interdisciplinary, bringing together physics, computer science, mathematics, and engineering. It involves developing theoretical frameworks, designing quantum algorithms, constructing quantum hardware, and implementing quantum communication protocols.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_information_science#cite_note-6)

[Quantum teleportation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_teleportation "Quantum teleportation"), [entanglement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_entanglement "Quantum entanglement") and the manufacturing of [quantum computers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_computer "Quantum computer") depend on a comprehensive understanding of quantum physics and engineering. [Google](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Google "Google") and [IBM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IBM "IBM"), among others, have invested significantly in quantum computer hardware research, leading to significant progress in manufacturing quantum computers since the 2010s. Currently, it is possible to build a quantum computer with over 100 [qubits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qubit "Qubit"), but the error rates are high due to several factors including decoherence[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_information_science#cite_note-7), the lack of suitable hardware and materials for quantum computer manufacturing, which make it difficult to create a scalable quantum computer.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_information_science#cite_note-8)

[Quantum cryptography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_cryptography "Quantum cryptography") devices are now available for commercial use. The [one time pad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One_time_pad "One time pad"), a cipher used by spies during the [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"), uses a sequence of random keys for encryption. These keys can be securely exchanged using quantum entangled particle pairs, as the principles of the [no-cloning theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/No-cloning_theorem "No-cloning theorem") and [wave function collapse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_function_collapse "Wave function collapse") ensure the secure exchange of the random keys. The development of devices that can transmit quantum entangled particles is a significant scientific and engineering goal.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

[Qiskit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qiskit "Qiskit"), [Cirq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cirq "Cirq") and [Q Sharp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Q_Sharp "Q Sharp") are popular quantum programming languages. Additional [programming languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Programming_languages "Programming languages") for quantum computers are needed, as well as a larger community of competent quantum programmers. To this end, additional learning resources are needed, since there are many fundamental differences in quantum programming which limits the number of skills that can be carried over from traditional programming.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_information_science#cite_note-9)

Related mathematical subjects
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Quantum_information_science&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Related mathematical subjects")]

[Quantum algorithms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_algorithms "Quantum algorithms") and [quantum complexity theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_complexity_theory "Quantum complexity theory") are two of the subjects in [algorithms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algorithm "Algorithm") and [computational complexity theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_complexity_theory "Computational complexity theory"). In 1994, mathematician [Peter Shor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Shor "Peter Shor") introduced a quantum algorithm for [prime factorization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_factorization "Prime factorization")[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_information_science#cite_note-10) that, with a quantum computer containing 4,000 [logical qubits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_and_logical_qubits "Physical and logical qubits"), could potentially break widely used ciphers like [RSA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RSA_(cryptosystem) "RSA (cryptosystem)") and [ECC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic-curve_cryptography "Elliptic-curve cryptography"), posing a major security threat. This led to increased investment in [quantum computing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_computing "Quantum computing") research and the development of [post-quantum cryptography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post-quantum_cryptography "Post-quantum cryptography")[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_information_science#cite_note-11) to prepare for the fault-tolerant quantum computing (FTQC) era.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_information_science#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_information_science#cite_note-13)
