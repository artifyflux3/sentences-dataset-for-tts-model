In [applied sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Applied_science "Applied science"), the **equivalent radius** (or **mean radius**) is the radius of a circle or sphere with the same perimeter, area, or volume of a non-circular or non-spherical object. The **equivalent diameter** (or **mean diameter**) (![Image 1: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6)) is twice the equivalent radius.

Perimeter equivalent
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Equivalent_radius&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Perimeter equivalent")]

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ae/Using_a_DTApe.JPG/250px-Using_a_DTApe.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Using_a_DTApe.JPG)

Measurement of tree circumference, the tape calibrated to show diameter, at breast height. The tape assumes a circular shape.

The [perimeter of a circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perimeter_of_a_circle "Perimeter of a circle") of radius _R_ is ![Image 3: {\displaystyle 2\pi R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a56efcb39c0744370094e561e7630cd3069a5371). Given the perimeter of a non-circular object _P_, one can calculate its **perimeter-equivalent radius** by setting

![Image 4: {\displaystyle P=2\pi R_{\text{eq}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ad08f0fe933a4e2676f7fca23136681780a0b77)
or, alternatively:

![Image 5: {\displaystyle R_{\text{eq}}={\frac {P}{2\pi }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2c9b93e25452a9b437035e1cb8e743dfaa46834f)
For example, a square of side _L_ has a perimeter of ![Image 6: {\displaystyle 4L}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d38f8c37a8c5632ebe86f8c9ae32118c63b22a6e). Setting that perimeter to be equal to that of a circle imply that

![Image 7: {\displaystyle R_{\text{eq}}={\frac {2L}{\pi }}\approx 0.6366L}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a58de797475b8d69684986d6ff5d8e4f5534e22)
Applications:

*   [US hat size](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/US_hat_size "US hat size") is the circumference of the head, measured in inches, divided by [pi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi_(constant) "Pi (constant)"), rounded to the nearest 1/8 inch. This corresponds to the 1D mean diameter.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_radius#cite_note-1)
*   [Diameter at breast height](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diameter_at_breast_height "Diameter at breast height") is the circumference of [tree trunk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tree_trunk "Tree trunk"), measured at height of 4.5 feet, divided by pi. This corresponds to the 1D mean diameter. It can be measured directly by a [girthing tape](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Girthing_tape&action=edit&redlink=1 "Girthing tape (page does not exist)").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_radius#cite_note-2)

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/20/Area-equivalent_radius_and_diameter.svg/250px-Area-equivalent_radius_and_diameter.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Area-equivalent_radius_and_diameter.svg)

The area-equivalent radius of a 2D object is the radius of a circle with the same area as the object

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Wetted_Perimeter.svg/250px-Wetted_Perimeter.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Wetted_Perimeter.svg)

Cross sectional area of a trapezoidal open channel, red highlights the [wetted perimeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wetted_perimeter "Wetted perimeter"), where water is in contact with the channel. The [hydraulic diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydraulic_diameter "Hydraulic diameter") is the equivalent circular configuration with the same circumference as the wetted perimeter.

The [area of a circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area_of_a_circle "Area of a circle") of radius _R_ is ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \pi R^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db4bdad3187c4001ed7079d18509f3b52d18479c). Given the area of a non-circular object _A_, one can calculate its **area-equivalent radius** by setting

![Image 11: {\displaystyle A=\pi R_{\text{eq}}^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3bb5b4117bcd7870075b32a3cd103554542822e0)
or, alternatively:

![Image 12: {\displaystyle R_{\text{eq}}={\sqrt {\frac {A}{\pi }}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ded0cc28faccfaebe9999bc4c4f3a7af5b7667e)
Often the area considered is that of a [cross section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_section_(geometry) "Cross section (geometry)").

For example, a square of side length _L_ has an area of ![Image 13: {\displaystyle L^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba162c66ca85776c83557af5088cc6f8584d1912). Setting that area to be equal that of a circle imply that

![Image 14: {\displaystyle R_{\text{eq}}={\sqrt {\frac {1}{\pi }}}L\approx 0.3183L}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a6576b2960fedfe70b477b216a44b096a6dc40c6)
Similarly, an [ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse") with [semi-major axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-major_axis "Semi-major axis")![Image 15: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) and [semi-minor axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-minor_axis "Semi-minor axis")![Image 16: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3) has mean radius ![Image 17: {\displaystyle R_{\text{eq}}={\sqrt {a\cdot b}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fd2a1358b21419f17a6122f50efa29dfdc3f8014).

For a circle, where ![Image 18: {\displaystyle a=b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1956b03d1314c7071ac1f45ed7b1e29422dcfcc4), this simplifies to ![Image 19: {\displaystyle R_{\text{eq}}=a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87abb635946a5decca8a3a576ef6b5e892417ff0).

Applications:

*   The [hydraulic diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydraulic_diameter "Hydraulic diameter") is similarly defined as 4 times the [cross-sectional area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-sectional_area "Cross-sectional area") of a pipe _A_, divided by its ["wetted" perimeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wetted_perimeter "Wetted perimeter")_P_. For a circular pipe of radius _R_, at full flow, this is

![Image 20: {\displaystyle D_{\text{H}}={\frac {4\pi R^{2}}{2\pi R}}=2R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e431fc1a6a833e32d62b66a938cb4dd6d600a96c)as one would expect. This is equivalent to the above definition of the 2D mean diameter. However, for historical reasons, the [hydraulic radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydraulic_radius "Hydraulic radius") is defined as the cross-sectional area of a pipe _A_, divided by its wetted perimeter _P_, which leads to ![Image 21: {\displaystyle D_{\text{H}}=4R_{\text{H}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e175f8b0dd9819b5d5fc9c4b45ca982443ee3bfb), and the hydraulic radius is _half_ of the 2D mean radius.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_radius#cite_note-3)
*   In [aggregate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Construction_aggregate "Construction aggregate") classification, the equivalent diameter is the "diameter of a circle with an equal aggregate sectional area", which is calculated by ![Image 22: {\displaystyle D=2{\sqrt {\frac {A}{\pi }}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da19b494dad82c271011eb7ce5d4073a14d2d906). It is used in many digital image processing programs.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_radius#cite_note-4)

[![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/33/Ellipsoide.svg/330px-Ellipsoide.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ellipsoide.svg)

A sphere (top), rotational ellipsoid (left) and triaxial ellipsoid (right)

The [volume of a sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_of_a_sphere "Volume of a sphere") of radius _R_ is ![Image 24: {\displaystyle {\frac {4}{3}}\pi R^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba956339c766e51a61ed79699fafa56b8fe3b7e8). Given the volume of a non-spherical object _V_, one can calculate its **volume-equivalent radius** by setting

![Image 25: {\displaystyle V={\frac {4}{3}}\pi R_{\text{eq}}^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/daeefd93ffe4aa66e7bf70e5bddddfb69552c8ff)
or, alternatively:

![Image 26: {\displaystyle R_{\text{eq}}={\sqrt[{3}]{\frac {3V}{4\pi }}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/971b336cb40005d8de7260ab4e6468db9f5e7943)
For example, a cube of side length _L_ has a volume of ![Image 27: {\displaystyle L^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/230b1613b9f13becdd796c5e934ab4dcc4277dd8). Setting that volume to be equal that of a sphere imply that

![Image 28: {\displaystyle R_{\text{eq}}={\sqrt[{3}]{\frac {3}{4\pi }}}L\approx 0.6204L}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/85bb7558e3541d789b504896a85150076aeceef4)
Similarly, a [tri-axial ellipsoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tri-axial_ellipsoid "Tri-axial ellipsoid") with axes ![Image 29: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc), ![Image 30: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3) and ![Image 31: {\displaystyle c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a67b81c2de995bd608d5b2df50cd8cd7d92455) has mean radius ![Image 32: {\displaystyle R_{\text{eq}}={\sqrt[{3}]{a\cdot b\cdot c}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9f4634f961d3bf8cbe1dfb5237da94274448c441).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_radius#cite_note-Leconte-5) The formula for a rotational ellipsoid is the special case where ![Image 33: {\displaystyle a=b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1956b03d1314c7071ac1f45ed7b1e29422dcfcc4). Likewise, an [oblate spheroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblate_spheroid "Oblate spheroid") or [rotational ellipsoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_ellipsoid "Rotational ellipsoid") with axes ![Image 34: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) and ![Image 35: {\displaystyle c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a67b81c2de995bd608d5b2df50cd8cd7d92455) has a mean radius of ![Image 36: {\displaystyle R_{\text{eq}}={\sqrt[{3}]{a^{2}\cdot c}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c50910bdf543a7efe98b346f0d5ad4fc5f2dc9fb).[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_radius#cite_note-Chambat-6) For a sphere, where ![Image 37: {\displaystyle a=b=c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41dfe5f5f74cb7a2c79093352406fd1d19123882), this simplifies to ![Image 38: {\displaystyle R_{\text{eq}}=a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87abb635946a5decca8a3a576ef6b5e892417ff0).

Applications:

*   For planet [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth"), which can be approximated as an oblate spheroid with radii 6 378.1 km and 6 356.8 km, the 3D mean radius is ![Image 39: {\displaystyle R={\sqrt[{3}]{6378.1^{2}\cdot 6356.8}}=6371.0{\text{ km}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d1c9b558e1c6435b432e8536585072a51d0153c).[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_radius#cite_note-Chambat-6)

### Curvature-equivalent radius

[![Image 45](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/Osculating_circle.svg/250px-Osculating_circle.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Osculating_circle.svg)

An osculating circle

*   [Cubic mean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubic_mean "Cubic mean")
*   [Earth ellipsoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_ellipsoid "Earth ellipsoid")
*   [Earth radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_radius "Earth radius")
*   [Galaxy effective radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_effective_radius "Galaxy effective radius")
*   [Geoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geoid "Geoid")
*   [Geometric mean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_mean "Geometric mean")
*   [Semidiameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semidiameter "Semidiameter")
