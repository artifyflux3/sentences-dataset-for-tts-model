The **trihydrogen cation** or **protonated molecular hydrogen** ([IUPAC name](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUPAC_nomenclature_of_inorganic_chemistry "IUPAC nomenclature of inorganic chemistry"): **hydrogen[onium ion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Onium_ion "Onium ion")**) is a [cation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cation "Cation") (positive [ion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion "Ion")) with [formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")**H+3**, consisting of three [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") nuclei ([protons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton")) sharing two [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron").

The trihydrogen cation is one of the most abundant [ions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion "Ion") in the universe. It is stable in the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium") (ISM) due to the low temperature and low density of interstellar space. The role that H+3 plays in the gas-phase chemistry of the ISM is unparalleled by any other [polyatomic ion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyatomic_ion "Polyatomic ion").

The trihydrogen cation is the simplest [triatomic molecule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triatomic_molecule "Triatomic molecule"), because its two electrons are the only [valence electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valence_electron "Valence electron") in the system. It is also the simplest example of a [three-center two-electron bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-center_two-electron_bond "Three-center two-electron bond") system.

H+3 was first discovered by [J. J. Thomson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._J._Thomson "J. J. Thomson") in 1911.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-elecrays-1) While using an early form of [mass spectrometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_spectrometry "Mass spectrometry") to study the resultant species of [plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)") discharges, he discovered a large abundance of a [polyatomic ion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyatomic_ion "Polyatomic ion") with a [mass-to-charge ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass-to-charge_ratio "Mass-to-charge ratio") of 3. He stated that the only two possibilities were C 4+ or H+3. Since the signal grew stronger in pure [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") gas, he correctly assigned the species as H+3.

The formation pathway was discovered by Hogness & Lunn in 1925.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-2) They also used an early form of mass spectrometry to study hydrogen discharges. They found that as the pressure of hydrogen increased, the amount of H+3 increased linearly and the amount of H+2 decreased linearly. In addition, there was little H+ at any pressure. These data suggested the [proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") exchange formation pathway discussed below.

In 1961, Martin _et al._ first suggested that H+3 may be present in interstellar space given the large amount of hydrogen in interstellar space and its reaction pathway was [exothermic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exothermic "Exothermic") (~1.5[eV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt "Electronvolt")).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-occurrence-3) This led to the suggestion of Watson and Herbst & Klemperer in 1973 that H+3 is responsible for the formation of many observed molecular ions.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-Watson1973-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-5)

It was not until 1980 that the first spectrum of H+3 was discovered by Takeshi Oka,[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-Oka1980-6) which was of the ν 2 fundamental band (see [#Spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#Spectroscopy)) using a technique called [frequency modulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency_modulation "Frequency modulation") detection. This started the search for extraterrestrial H+3. [Emission lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emission_line "Emission line") were detected in the late 1980s and early 1990s in the [ionospheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionosphere "Ionosphere") of [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter"), [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn"), and [Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus "Uranus").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-Drossart1989-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-Geballe1993-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-Trafton1993-9) In the textbook by Bunker and Jensen[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-10) Figure 1.1 reproduces part of the ν 2 emission band from a region of auroral activity in the upper atmosphere of Jupiter, [[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-11) and its Table 12.3 lists the transition wavenumbers of the lines in the band observed by Oka[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-Oka1980-6) with their assignments.

In 1996, H+3 was finally detected in the interstellar medium (ISM) by Geballe & Oka in two molecular [interstellar clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_clouds "Interstellar clouds") in the sightlines GL2136 and W33A.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-Geballe1996-12) In 1998, H+3 was unexpectedly detected by McCall _et al._ in a diffuse interstellar cloud in the sightline [Cygnus OB2#12](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cygnus_OB2-12 "Cygnus OB2-12").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-McCall1998-13) In 2006 Oka announced that H+3 was ubiquitous in interstellar medium, and that the [Central Molecular Zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Molecular_Zone "Central Molecular Zone") contained a million times the concentration of ISM generally.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-14)

The structure of H+3

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/07/Trihydrogen-cation-MO-diagram.svg/500px-Trihydrogen-cation-MO-diagram.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Trihydrogen-cation-MO-diagram.svg)

The [MO diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MO_diagram "MO diagram") of the trihydrogen cation.

The three hydrogen atoms in the molecule form an [equilateral triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilateral_triangle "Equilateral triangle"), with a [bond length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_length "Bond length") of 0.90[Å](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m "Ångström") on each side. The bonding among the atoms is a [three-center two-electron bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-center_two-electron_bond "Three-center two-electron bond"), a [delocalized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delocalized "Delocalized") resonance hybrid type of structure. The strength of the bond has been calculated to be around 4.5[eV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt "Electronvolt") (104 kcal/mol).[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-15)

In theory, the cation has 10 [isotopologues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotopologue "Isotopologue"), resulting from the replacement of one or more protons by nuclei of the other hydrogen [isotopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope "Isotope"); namely, [deuterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuterium "Deuterium") nuclei ([deuterons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuteron "Deuteron"), 2 H+) or [tritium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tritium "Tritium") nuclei ([tritons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triton_(physics) "Triton (physics)"), 3 H+). Some of them have been detected in interstellar clouds.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-paga2009-16) They differ in the [atomic mass number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_mass_number "Atomic mass number")_A_ and the number of [neutrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron")_N_:

*   H+3 = 1 H+3 (_A_=3, _N_=0) (the common one).[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-robe2003-17)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-paga2009-16)
*   [DH 2]+ = [2 H 1 H 2]+ (_A_=4, _N_=1) (deuterium dihydrogen cation).[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-robe2003-17)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-paga2009-16)
*   [D 2 H]+ = [2 H 2 1 H]+ (_A_=5, _N_=2) (dideuterium hydrogen cation).[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-robe2003-17)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-paga2009-16)
*   D+3 = 2 H+3 (_A_=6, _N_=3) (trideuterium cation).[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-robe2003-17)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-paga2009-16)
*   [TH 2]+ = [3 H 1 H 2]+ (_A_=5, _N_=2) (tritium dihydrogen cation).
*   [TDH]+ = [3 H 2 H 1 H]+ (_A_=6, _N_=3) (tritium deuterium hydrogen cation).
*   [TD 2]+ = [3 H 2 H 2]+ (_A_=7, _N_=4) (tritium dideuterium cation).
*   [T 2 H]+ = [3 H 2 1 H]+ (_A_=7, _N_=4) (ditritium hydrogen cation).
*   [T 2 D]+ = [3 H 2 2 H]+ (_A_=8, _N_=5) (ditritium deuterium cation).
*   T+3 = 3 H+3 (_A_=9, _N_=6) (tritritium cation).

The deuterium isotopologues have been implicated in the fractionation of deuterium in dense interstellar cloud cores.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-robe2003-17)

The main pathway for the production of H+3 is by the reaction of [H+2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydrogen_cation "Dihydrogen cation") and H 2.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-eherbstastro-18)

H+2 + H 2 → H+3 + H
The concentration of H+2 is what limits the rate of this reaction in nature - the only known natural source of it is via ionization of H 2 by a [cosmic ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_ray "Cosmic ray") in interstellar space:

H 2 + cosmic ray → H+2 + e− + cosmic ray
The cosmic ray has so much energy, it is almost unaffected by the relatively small energy transferred to the hydrogen when ionizing an H 2 molecule. In interstellar clouds, cosmic rays leave behind a trail of H+2, and therefore H+3. In laboratories, H+3 is produced by the same mechanism in plasma discharge cells, with the discharge potential providing the energy to ionize the H 2.

There are many destruction reactions for H+3. The dominant destruction pathway in dense interstellar clouds is by proton transfer with a neutral collision partner. The most likely candidate for a destructive collision partner is the second most abundant molecule in space, [CO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide").

H+3 + CO → HCO+ + H 2
The significant product of this reaction is HCO+, an important molecule for interstellar chemistry. Its strong [dipole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dipole "Dipole") and high abundance make it easily detectable by [radioastronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioastronomy "Radioastronomy"). H+3 can also react with atomic [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") to form OH+ and H 2.

H+3 + O → OH+ + H 2
OH+ then usually reacts with more H 2 to create further [hydrogenated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogenate "Hydrogenate") molecules.

OH+ + H 2 → OH+2 + H OH+2 + H 2 → OH+3 + H
At this point, the reaction between OH+3 and H 2 is no longer exothermic in interstellar clouds. The most common destruction pathway for OH+3 is [dissociative recombination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissociative_recombination "Dissociative recombination"), yielding four possible sets of products: H 2 O + H, OH + H 2, OH + 2H, and O + H 2 + H. While [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water "Water") is a possible product of this reaction, it is not a very efficient product. Different experiments have suggested that water is created anywhere from 5–33% of the time. Water formation on [grains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_dust "Cosmic dust") is still considered the primary source of water in the interstellar medium.

The most common destruction pathway of H+3 in diffuse interstellar clouds is dissociative recombination. This reaction has multiple products. The major product is dissociation into three hydrogen atoms, which occurs roughly 75% of the time. The minor product is H 2 and H, which occurs roughly 25% of the time.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-eherbstastro-18)

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/15/H3%2B%26H2.svg/500px-H3%2B%26H2.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:H3%2B%26H2.svg)

A collision of _ortho_-H+3 and _para_-H 2.

The protons of [1 H 3]+ can be in two different [spin configurations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)"), called _ortho_ and _para_. _Ortho_-H+3 has all three proton spins parallel, yielding a total [nuclear spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_spin "Nuclear spin") of 3/2. _Para_-H+3 has two proton spins parallel while the other is anti-parallel, yielding a total nuclear spin of 1/2.

The most abundant molecule in dense interstellar clouds is 1 H 2 which also has [_ortho_ and _para_ states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_isomers_of_hydrogen "Spin isomers of hydrogen"), with total nuclear spins 1 and 0, respectively. When a H+3 molecule collides with a H 2 molecule, a proton transfer can take place. The transfer still yields a H+3 molecule and a H 2 molecule, but can potentially change the total nuclear spin of the two molecules depending on the nuclear spins of the protons. When an _ortho_-H+3 and a _para_-H 2 collide, the result may be a _para_-H+3 and an _ortho_-H 2.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-eherbstastro-18)

The [spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopy "Spectroscopy") of H+3 is challenging. The pure rotational spectrum is exceedingly weak.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-19) Ultraviolet light is too energetic and would dissociate the molecule. [Rovibronic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rovibronic_excitation "Rovibronic excitation") (infrared) spectroscopy provides the ability to observe H+3. Rovibronic spectroscopy is possible with H+3 because one of the [vibrational modes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_mode "Normal mode") of H+3, the ν 2 asymmetric bend mode (see [example of ν 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_absorption_by_water "Electromagnetic absorption by water")) has a weak transition dipole moment. Since Oka's initial spectrum,[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-Oka1980-6) over 900 [absorption lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorption_spectroscopy "Absorption spectroscopy") have been detected in the infrared region. H+3 emission lines have also been found by observing the atmospheres of the Jovian planets. H+3 emission lines are found by observing molecular hydrogen and finding a line that cannot be attributed to molecular hydrogen.

Astronomical detection
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Trihydrogen_cation&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Astronomical detection")]

H+3 has been detected in two types of the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe") environments: [jovian planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jovian_planets "Jovian planets") and [interstellar clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_cloud "Interstellar cloud"). In jovian planets, it has been detected in the planets' [ionospheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionosphere#Ionospheres_of_other_planets_and_natural_satellites "Ionosphere"), the region where the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun")'s [high energy radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind "Solar wind") ionizes the [particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle "Particle") in the planets' [atmospheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere "Atmosphere"). Since there is a high level of [H 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") in these atmospheres, this radiation can produce a significant amount of H+3. Also, with a [broadband source](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind "Solar wind") like the Sun, there is plenty of radiation to pump the H+3 to [higher energy states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_level "Energy level") from which it can relax by [spontaneous emission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spontaneous_emission "Spontaneous emission").

### Planetary atmospheres

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Trihydrogen_cation&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Planetary atmospheres")]

The detection of the first H+3[emission lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emission_lines "Emission lines") was reported in 1989 by Drossart _et al._,[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-Drossart1989-7) found in the ionosphere of Jupiter. Drossart found a total of 23 H+3 lines with a [column density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area_density#Astronomy "Area density") of 1.39×10 9/cm 2. Using these lines, they were able to assign a temperature to the H+3 of around 1,100 K (830°C), which is comparable to temperatures determined from emission lines of other species like H 2. In 1993, H+3 was found in [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn") by Geballe _et al._[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-Geballe1993-8) and in [Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus "Uranus") by Trafton _et al._[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-Trafton1993-9)

### Molecular interstellar clouds

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Trihydrogen_cation&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Molecular interstellar clouds")]

H+3 was not detected in the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium") until 1996, when Geballe & Oka reported the detection of H+3 in two [molecular cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_cloud "Molecular cloud") sightlines, GL 2136 and [W33A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W33A "W33A").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-Geballe1996-12) Both sources had temperatures of H+3 of about 35 K (−238°C) and column densities of about 10 14/cm 2. Since then, H+3 has been detected in numerous other molecular cloud sightlines, such as [AFGL 2136](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=AFGL_2136&action=edit&redlink=1 "AFGL 2136 (page does not exist)"),[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-McCall1999-20)[Mon R2 IRS 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mon_R2_IRS_3&action=edit&redlink=1 "Mon R2 IRS 3 (page does not exist)"),[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-McCall1999-20)[GCS 3–2](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=GCS_3%E2%80%932&action=edit&redlink=1 "GCS 3–2 (page does not exist)"),[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-mgoto-21)[GC IRS 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=GC_IRS_3&action=edit&redlink=1 "GC IRS 3 (page does not exist)"),[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-mgoto-21) and [LkHα 101](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NGC_1579 "NGC 1579").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-Brittain2004-22)

### Diffuse interstellar clouds

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Trihydrogen_cation&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Diffuse interstellar clouds")]

Unexpectedly, three H+3 lines were detected in 1998 by McCall _et al._ in the [diffuse interstellar cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Diffuse_interstellar_cloud&action=edit&redlink=1 "Diffuse interstellar cloud (page does not exist)") sightline of [Cyg OB2 No. 12](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cyg_OB2_No._12&action=edit&redlink=1 "Cyg OB2 No. 12 (page does not exist)").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-McCall1998-13) Before 1998, the density of H 2 was thought to be too low to produce a detectable amount of H+3. McCall detected a temperature of ~27 K (−246°C) and a column density of ~10 14/cm 2, the same column density as [Geballe & Oka](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Geballe_%26_Oka&action=edit&redlink=1 "Geballe & Oka (page does not exist)"). Since then, H+3 has been detected in many other diffuse cloud sightlines, such as GCS 3–2,[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-mgoto-21) GC IRS 3,[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-mgoto-21) and [ζ Persei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeta_Persei "Zeta Persei").[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-McCall2004-23)

### Steady-state model predictions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Trihydrogen_cation&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Steady-state model predictions")]

To approximate the path length of H+3 in these clouds, Oka[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation#cite_note-Oka2006-24) used the steady-state model to determine the predicted number densities in diffuse and dense clouds. As explained above, both diffuse and dense clouds have the same formation mechanism for H+3, but different dominating destruction mechanisms. In dense clouds, proton transfer with CO is the dominating destruction mechanism. This corresponds to a predicted number density of 10−4 cm−3 in dense clouds.

![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}n({\mathrm {H} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{3}}{\vphantom {A}}^{+}})&={\frac {\zeta }{k_{\mathrm {CO} }}}\left[{\frac {n({\mathrm {H} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{2}}})}{n({\mathrm {CO} })}}\right]\approx 10^{-4}/{\text{cm}}^{3}\\n({\mathrm {H} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{3}}{\vphantom {A}}^{+}})&={\frac {\zeta }{k_{\mathrm {e} }}}\left[{\frac {n({\mathrm {H} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{2}}})}{n({\mathrm {C} {\vphantom {A}}^{+}})}}\right]\approx 10^{-6}/{\text{cm}}^{3}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8ade7ed17df512bcbb6b6f934f9d06c941ef35e4)
In diffuse clouds, the dominating destruction mechanism is dissociative recombination. This corresponds to a predicted number density of 10−6/cm 3 in diffuse clouds. Therefore, since column densities for diffuse and dense clouds are roughly the same order of magnitude, diffuse clouds must have a path length 100 times greater than that for dense clouds. Therefore, by using H+3 as a probe of these clouds, their relative sizes can be determined.
