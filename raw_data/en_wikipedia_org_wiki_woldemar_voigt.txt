**Woldemar Voigt** (German:[[foːkt]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German "Help:IPA/Standard German")[](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/7/7c/De-Voigt2.ogg/De-Voigt2.ogg.mp3 "Play audio")[ⓘ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:De-Voigt2.ogg "File:De-Voigt2.ogg"); 2 September 1850 – 13 December 1919) was a German [mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician") and [physicist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physicist "Physicist").

Voigt was born in [Leipzig](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leipzig "Leipzig"), and died in [Göttingen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6ttingen "Göttingen"). He was a student of [Franz Ernst Neumann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franz_Ernst_Neumann "Franz Ernst Neumann").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Woldemar_Voigt#cite_note-olesko-2) Voigt taught at the [Georg August University of Göttingen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_August_University_of_G%C3%B6ttingen "Georg August University of Göttingen") and eventually went on to head the Mathematical Physics Department there. He was succeeded in 1914 by [Peter Debye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Debye "Peter Debye"), who took charge of the theoretical department of the Physical Institute.

Voigt worked on [crystal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal "Crystal")[physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), [thermodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamics "Thermodynamics") and [electro-optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electro-optics "Electro-optics"). His main work was the _Lehrbuch der Kristallphysik_ (_Textbook on crystal physics_), first published in 1910. He discovered what is now called the [Voigt effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voigt_effect "Voigt effect") in 1898. The word [tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") in its current meaning was introduced by him in 1898.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Woldemar_Voigt#cite_note-crystals-3)[Voigt profile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voigt_profile "Voigt profile") and [Voigt notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voigt_notation "Voigt notation") are named after him. He was also an amateur musician and became known as a Bach expert (see External links). He was the first to suggest, in 1886, that Bach's [Concerto for two harpsichords in C minor, BWV 1060](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Concerto_for_two_harpsichords_in_C_minor,_BWV_1060 "Concerto for two harpsichords in C minor, BWV 1060") was originally scored for violin and oboe.

In 1887 Voigt formulated a form of the [Lorentz transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_transformation "Lorentz transformation") between a rest frame of reference and a frame moving with speed ![Image 2: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) in the ![Image 3: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) direction. However, as Voigt himself said, the transformation was aimed at a specific problem and did not carry with it the idea of a general [coordinate transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system#Transformations "Coordinate system"), as is the case in [relativity theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativity_theory "Relativity theory").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Woldemar_Voigt#cite_note-doppler-4)

Voigt transformation
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Woldemar_Voigt&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Voigt transformation")]

In modern notation Voigt's transformation was

![Image 4: {\displaystyle x'=x-vt,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/265922e28224a041e55d6bac7dea14b79928cc39)![Image 5: {\displaystyle y'=y/\gamma ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/13852c89a86587cc8aab01858b84e4c9f1cc5840)![Image 6: {\displaystyle z'=z/\gamma ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb8ab461e9e12fca7acd31219391122af0936dc2)![Image 7: {\displaystyle t'=t-vx/c^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2eaddea2f5e4d1a013f98abc633c3580ca0f4c74)
where ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \gamma =1/{\sqrt {1-v^{2}/c^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0efffd10b8047241e9d9f4498b77760af9457bd). If the right-hand sides of his equations are multiplied by ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a223c880b0ce3da8f64ee33c4f0010beee400b1a), they become the modern [Lorentz transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_transformation "Lorentz transformation"). [Hermann Minkowski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Minkowski "Hermann Minkowski") said in 1908 that the transformations which play the main role in the principle of relativity were first examined by Voigt in 1887. Also [Hendrik Lorentz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hendrik_Lorentz "Hendrik Lorentz") (1909) is on record as saying that he could have taken these transformations into his theory of electrodynamics, if only he had known of them, rather than developing his own. It is interesting then to examine the consequences of these transformations from this point of view. Lorentz might then have seen that the transformation introduced [relativity of simultaneity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativity_of_simultaneity "Relativity of simultaneity"), and also [time dilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_dilation "Time dilation"). However, the magnitude of the dilation was greater than the now accepted value in the Lorentz transformations. Moving clocks, obeying Voigt's time transformation, indicate an elapsed time ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \Delta t_{\text{Voigt}}=\gamma ^{-2}\Delta t=\gamma ^{-1}\Delta t_{\text{Lorentz}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd63f1461f7a7b0ab2f6148a0058fef62c6003fe), while stationary clocks indicate an elapsed time ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \Delta t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c28867ecd34e2caed12cf38feadf6a81a7ee542).

Since Voigt's transformation preserves the speed of light in all frames, the [Michelson–Morley experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michelson%E2%80%93Morley_experiment "Michelson–Morley experiment") and the [Kennedy–Thorndike experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kennedy%E2%80%93Thorndike_experiment "Kennedy–Thorndike experiment") can not distinguish between the two transformations. The crucial question is the issue of time dilation. The experimental measurement of [time dilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_dilation "Time dilation") by [Ives and Stillwell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ives%E2%80%93Stilwell_experiment "Ives–Stilwell experiment") (1938) and others settled the issue in favor of the Lorentz transformation.
