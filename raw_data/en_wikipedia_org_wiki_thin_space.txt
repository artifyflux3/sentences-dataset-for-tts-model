From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Space character about 1/5 em wide

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/67/Example_of_thin_space.svg/350px-Example_of_thin_space.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Example_of_thin_space.svg)

Spacing examples. The top row is unspaced, the middle row has a thin space between the words, and the bottom has a regular space.

In [typography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Typography "Typography"), a **thin space** is a [space character](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_(punctuation) "Space (punctuation)") whose width is usually 1⁄5 or 1⁄6 of an [em](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Em_(typography) "Em (typography)"). It is used to add a narrow space, such as between nested [quotation marks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotation_mark "Quotation mark") or to separate [glyphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glyph "Glyph") that interfere with one another. It is not as narrow as the [hair space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hair_space "Hair space"). It is also used in the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") and in many countries as a [thousands separator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thousands_separator "Thousands separator") when writing numbers in groups of three digits, in order to facilitate reading.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thin_space#cite_note-1) It also avoids the ambiguity of the comma, used as a thousands separator in many countries but as a decimal point in Europe.

In [Unicode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode "Unicode"), thin space is encoded at [U+](https://en.wikibooks.org/wiki/Unicode/Character_reference/2000-2FFF "b:Unicode/Character reference/2000-2FFF")2009 THIN SPACE (&thinsp;, &ThinSpace;). Some text editors, such as IntelliJ IDEA and Android Studio, will display the character as its suggested abbreviation of "`THSP`".[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thin_space#cite_note-2) Unicode's [U+](https://en.wikibooks.org/wiki/Unicode/Character_reference/2000-2FFF "b:Unicode/Character reference/2000-2FFF")202F NARROW NO-BREAK SPACE is a [non-breaking space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-breaking_space "Non-breaking space") with a width similar to that of the thin space.

In [LaTeX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LaTeX "LaTeX") and [Plain TeX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plain_TeX "Plain TeX"), `\thinspace` produces a narrow, [non-breaking space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-breaking_space "Non-breaking space").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thin_space#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thin_space#cite_note-4) Inside and outside of math formulae in LaTeX, `\,` also produces a narrow, non-breaking space.

In all versions of [LibreOffice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LibreOffice "LibreOffice") and in some of [Microsoft Word](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microsoft_Word "Microsoft Word"), the special characters and symbols [dialog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialog_box "Dialog box") (often available via _Insert > Symbol_ or _Insert > Special Characters_), has both the thin space and the narrow no-break space available for point-and-click insertion. In LibreOffice's Symbol dialog, there is an easy-to-find box field to narrow the searching; in Word's Symbol dialog, under font = "(normal text)", the characters are found in subset = "General Punctuation", Unicode character 2009 and nearby. Other word processing programs and in many Linux configurations, have ways of producing a thin space using keyboard shortcuts.
