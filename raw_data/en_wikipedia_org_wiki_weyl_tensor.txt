In [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), the **Weyl curvature tensor**, named after [Hermann Weyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Weyl "Hermann Weyl"),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weyl_tensor#cite_note-1) is a measure of the [curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature "Curvature") of [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") or, more generally, a [pseudo-Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold"). Like the [Riemann curvature tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_curvature_tensor "Riemann curvature tensor"), the Weyl tensor expresses the [tidal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_force "Tidal force") that a body feels when moving along a [geodesic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic "Geodesic"). The Weyl tensor differs from the Riemann curvature tensor in that it does not convey information on how the volume of the body changes, but rather only how the shape of the body is distorted by the tidal force. The [Ricci curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_curvature "Ricci curvature"), or [trace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trace_(linear_algebra) "Trace (linear algebra)") component of the Riemann tensor contains precisely the information about how volumes change in the presence of tidal forces, so the Weyl tensor is the [traceless](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traceless "Traceless") component of the Riemann tensor. This [tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") has the same symmetries as the Riemann tensor, but satisfies the extra condition that it is trace-free: [metric contraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_contraction#Metric_contraction "Tensor contraction") on any pair of indices yields zero. It is obtained from the Riemann tensor by subtracting a tensor that is a linear expression in the Ricci tensor.

In [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), the Weyl curvature is the only part of the curvature that exists in free space—a solution of the [vacuum Einstein equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_field_equation "Einstein field equation")—and it governs the propagation of [gravitational waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_waves "Gravitational waves") through regions of space devoid of matter.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weyl_tensor#cite_note-Danehkar2009-2) More generally, the Weyl curvature is the only component of curvature for [Ricci-flat manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci-flat_manifold "Ricci-flat manifold") and always governs the [characteristics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Method_of_characteristics "Method of characteristics") of the field equations of an [Einstein manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_manifold "Einstein manifold").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weyl_tensor#cite_note-Danehkar2009-2)

In dimensions 2 and 3 the Weyl curvature tensor vanishes identically. In dimensions ≥ 4, the Weyl curvature is generally nonzero. If the Weyl tensor vanishes in dimension ≥ 4, then the metric is locally [conformally flat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformally_flat "Conformally flat"): there exists a [local coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Local_coordinate_system "Local coordinate system") in which the metric tensor is proportional to a constant tensor. This fact was a key component of [Nordström's theory of gravitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nordstr%C3%B6m%27s_theory_of_gravitation "Nordström's theory of gravitation"), which was a precursor of [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity").

The Weyl tensor can be obtained from the full curvature tensor by subtracting out various traces. This is most easily done by writing the Riemann tensor as a (0,4) valence tensor (by contracting with the metric). The (0,4) valence Weyl tensor is then ([Petersen 2006](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weyl_tensor#CITEREFPetersen2006), p.92)

where _n_ is the dimension of the manifold, _g_ is the metric, _R_ is the Riemann tensor, _Ric_ is the [Ricci tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_tensor "Ricci tensor"), _s_ is the [scalar curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_curvature "Scalar curvature"), and ![Image 2: {\displaystyle h{~\wedge \!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\;\bigcirc ~}k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3971e29210af939122f390c97753e1f7f8e1ea8) denotes the [Kulkarni–Nomizu product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kulkarni%E2%80%93Nomizu_product "Kulkarni–Nomizu product") of two symmetric (0,2) tensors:

In tensor component notation, this can be written as

![Image 4: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}C_{ik\ell m}=R_{ik\ell m}+{}&{\frac {1}{n-2}}\left(R_{im}g_{k\ell }-R_{i\ell }g_{km}+R_{k\ell }g_{im}-R_{km}g_{i\ell }\right)\\{}+{}&{\frac {1}{(n-1)(n-2)}}R\left(g_{i\ell }g_{km}-g_{im}g_{k\ell }\right).\ \end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4dd46717d72d85b6d34a3a2b3b2cdb9fd2a1dbc5)
The ordinary (1,3) valent Weyl tensor is then given by contracting the above with the inverse of the metric.

The decomposition (**[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weyl_tensor#math_1)**) expresses the Riemann tensor as an [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal "Orthogonal")[direct sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_sum_of_vector_bundles "Direct sum of vector bundles"), in the sense that

![Image 5: {\displaystyle |R|^{2}=|C|^{2}+\left|{\frac {1}{n-2}}\left(\mathrm {Ric} -{\frac {s}{n}}g\right){~\wedge \!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\;\bigcirc ~}g\right|^{2}+\left|{\frac {s}{2n(n-1)}}g{~\wedge \!\!\!\!\!\!\!\!\;\bigcirc ~}g\right|^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bad2034fb8a4f47953723891f68f22bdea3bc8ba)
This decomposition, known as the [Ricci decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_decomposition "Ricci decomposition"), expresses the Riemann curvature tensor into its [irreducible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irreducible_representation "Irreducible representation") components under the action of the [orthogonal group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_group "Orthogonal group").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weyl_tensor#cite_note-FOOTNOTESingerThorpe1969-3) In dimension 4, the Weyl tensor further decomposes into invariant factors for the action of the [special orthogonal group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_orthogonal_group "Special orthogonal group"), the self-dual and antiself-dual parts _C_+ and _C_−.

The Weyl tensor can also be expressed using the [Schouten tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schouten_tensor "Schouten tensor"), which is a trace-adjusted multiple of the Ricci tensor,

Then

In indices,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weyl_tensor#cite_note-4)

![Image 8: {\displaystyle C_{abcd}=R_{abcd}-{\frac {2}{n-2}}\left(g_{a[c}R_{d]b}-g_{b[c}R_{d]a}\right)+{\frac {2}{(n-1)(n-2)}}R~g_{a[c}g_{d]b}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b1fe6a1fc5ad6e2ad77b9fc603fdaae8c3bba1f4)
where ![Image 9: {\displaystyle R_{abcd}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/30d0d3245d30b6553b1e3fd857d6c4f473501807) is the Riemann tensor, ![Image 10: {\displaystyle R_{ab}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/95e2940a775e31cc7bb12718daeac703f3f74367) is the Ricci tensor, ![Image 11: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) is the Ricci scalar (the scalar curvature) and brackets around indices refers to the [antisymmetric part](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antisymmetric_tensor "Antisymmetric tensor"). Equivalently,

![Image 12: {\displaystyle {C_{ab}}^{cd}={R_{ab}}^{cd}-4S_{[a}^{[c}\delta _{b]}^{d]}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/179a28f7ac753ff166f6bb694dd0fbde056ebf4e)
where _S_ denotes the [Schouten tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schouten_tensor "Schouten tensor").

### Conformal rescaling

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Weyl_tensor&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Conformal rescaling")]

The Weyl tensor has the special property that it is invariant under [conformal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformal_map "Conformal map") changes to the [metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor"). That is, if ![Image 13: {\displaystyle g_{\mu \nu }\mapsto g'_{\mu \nu }=fg_{\mu \nu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/784ce7689ed8096ab7f1e29435259b3b075c2ce4) for some positive scalar function ![Image 14: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) then the (1,3) valent Weyl tensor satisfies ![Image 15: {\displaystyle {C'}_{\ \ bcd}^{a}=C_{\ \ bcd}^{a}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/307dc13a68bcbb0125a2b7a5c277ab616a2933ff). For this reason the Weyl tensor is also called the **conformal tensor**. It follows that a [necessary condition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Necessary_condition "Necessary condition") for a [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold") to be [conformally flat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformally_flat "Conformally flat") is that the Weyl tensor vanish. In dimensions ≥ 4 this condition is [sufficient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sufficient_condition "Sufficient condition") as well. In dimension 3 the vanishing of the [Cotton tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotton_tensor "Cotton tensor") is a necessary and sufficient condition for the Riemannian manifold being conformally flat. Any 2-dimensional (smooth) Riemannian manifold is conformally flat, a consequence of the existence of [isothermal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isothermal_coordinates "Isothermal coordinates").

Indeed, the existence of a conformally flat scale amounts to solving the overdetermined partial differential equation

![Image 16: {\displaystyle Ddf-df\otimes df+\left(|df|^{2}+{\frac {\Delta f}{n-2}}\right)g=\operatorname {Ric} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/872eb01576329713d0b24cbd349bc931ffd2a0f1)
In dimension ≥ 4, the vanishing of the Weyl tensor is the only [integrability condition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integrability_condition "Integrability condition") for this equation; in dimension 3, it is the [Cotton tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotton_tensor "Cotton tensor") instead.

The Weyl tensor has the same symmetries as the Riemann tensor. This includes:

![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}C(u,v)&=-C(v,u)\\\langle C(u,v)w,z\rangle &=-\langle C(u,v)z,w\rangle \\C(u,v)w+C(v,w)u+C(w,u)v&=0.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f608eda2cab70fd00723268c7168242cefe711a8)
In addition, of course, the Weyl tensor is trace free:

![Image 18: {\displaystyle \operatorname {tr} C(u,\cdot )v=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2e75729e51f30561a05331a8252cc1e181c06dea)
for all _u_, _v_. In indices these four conditions are

![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}C_{abcd}=-C_{bacd}&=-C_{abdc}\\C_{abcd}+C_{acdb}+C_{adbc}&=0\\{C^{a}}_{bac}&=0.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c09a672226b54ca8e579b9f2ccb1742cd12013b8)
Taking traces of the usual second Bianchi identity of the Riemann tensor eventually shows that

![Image 20: {\displaystyle \nabla _{a}{C^{a}}_{bcd}=2(n-3)\nabla _{[c}S_{d]b}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/95bc5946c57649b107d431fc644496b3176823cd)
where _S_ is the [Schouten tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schouten_tensor "Schouten tensor"). The valence (0,3) tensor on the right-hand side is the [Cotton tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cotton_tensor "Cotton tensor"), apart from the initial factor.
