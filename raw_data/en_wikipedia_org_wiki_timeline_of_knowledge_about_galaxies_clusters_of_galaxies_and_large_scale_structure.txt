From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

The following is a timeline of [galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy"), [clusters of galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_cluster "Galaxy cluster"), and [large-scale structure of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe#Large-scale_structure "Observable universe").

*   5th century BC– [Democritus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democritus "Democritus") proposes that the bright band in the night sky known as the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") might consist of [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star").
*   4th century BC– [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle") believes the Milky Way to be caused by "the ignition of the fiery exhalation of some stars which were large, numerous and close together" and that the "ignition takes place in the upper part of the [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere "Atmosphere"), in the [region of the world which is continuous with the heavenly motions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sublunary_sphere "Sublunary sphere")".[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-Montada-1)
*   964– [Abd al-Rahman al-Sufi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abd_al-Rahman_al-Sufi "Abd al-Rahman al-Sufi") (Azophi), a [Persian astronomer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy_in_medieval_Islam "Astronomy in medieval Islam"), makes the first recorded observations of the [Andromeda Galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy "Andromeda Galaxy")[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-NSOG-2) and the [Large Magellanic Cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_Magellanic_Cloud "Large Magellanic Cloud")[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-obspm-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-obspm2-4) in his _[Book of Fixed Stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Book\_of\_Fixed\_Stars "Book of Fixed Stars")_, and which are the first galaxies other than the Milky Way to be recorded.
*   11th century– [Al-Biruni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Biruni "Al-Biruni"), another Persian astronomer, describes the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") galaxy as a collection of fragments of numerous [nebulous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nebula "Nebula") stars.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-5)
*   11th century– [Alhazen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alhazen "Alhazen") (Ibn al-Haytham), an [Arabian astronomer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_astronomy "Islamic astronomy"), refutes Aristotle's theory on the Milky Way by making the first attempt at observing and measuring the Milky Way's [parallax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallax "Parallax"),[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-6) and he thus "determined that because the Milky Way had no parallax, it was very remote from the Earth and did not belong to the atmosphere".[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-7)
*   12th century– [Avempace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avempace "Avempace") (Ibn Bajjah) of [Islamic Spain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Andalus "Al-Andalus") proposes the Milky Way to be made up of many stars but that it appears to be a continuous image due to the effect of [refraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refraction "Refraction") in the [Earth's atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere "Earth's atmosphere").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-Montada-1)
*   14th century– [Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibn_Qayyim_al-Jawziyya "Ibn Qayyim al-Jawziyya") of [Syria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syria "Syria") proposes the Milky Way galaxy to be "a myriad of tiny stars packed together in the sphere of the fixed stars", and that these stars are larger than [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-Livingston-8)
*   1521– [Ferdinand Magellan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferdinand_Magellan "Ferdinand Magellan") observes the [Magellanic Clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magellanic_Clouds "Magellanic Clouds") during his circumnavigating expedition.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-9)
*   1610– [Galileo Galilei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galileo_Galilei "Galileo Galilei") uses a [telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telescope "Telescope") to determine that the bright band on the [sky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sky "Sky"), the "[Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way")", is composed of many faint stars.
*   1612– Simon Marius using a moderate telescope observes Andromeda and describes as a "flame seen through horn".[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-10)
*   1750– [Thomas Wright](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Wright_(astronomer) "Thomas Wright (astronomer)") discusses [galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy") and the flattened shape of the Milky Way and speculates nebulae as separate.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-11)
*   1755– [Immanuel Kant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immanuel_Kant "Immanuel Kant") drawing on Wright's work conjectures that our galaxy is a rotating disk of stars held together by [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity"), and that the [nebulae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nebula "Nebula") are separate such galaxies; he calls them _[Island Universes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Island\_universe "Island universe")_.
*   1774– [Charles Messier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Messier "Charles Messier") releases a preliminary list of 45 [Messier objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Messier_object "Messier object"), three of which turn out to be the galaxies including [Andromeda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy "Andromeda Galaxy") and [Triangulum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangulum_Galaxy "Triangulum Galaxy"). By 1781 the final published list grows to 103 objects, 34 of which turn out to be galaxies.
*   1785– [William Herschel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Herschel "William Herschel") carried the first attempt to describe the shape of the Milky Way and the position of the Sun in it by carefully counting the number of stars in different regions of the sky. He produced a diagram of the shape of the galaxy with the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") close to the center.
*   1845– [Lord Rosse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Parsons,_3rd_Earl_of_Rosse "William Parsons, 3rd Earl of Rosse") discovers a nebula with a distinct spiral shape.

*   1912 – [Henrietta Swan Leavitt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henrietta_Swan_Leavitt "Henrietta Swan Leavitt") publishes her study of [cepheid variable stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cepheid_variable "Cepheid variable") in the [Small Magellanic Cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_Magellanic_Cloud "Small Magellanic Cloud") and the period-luminosity curve. [The Leavitt Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Period-luminosity_relation "Period-luminosity relation") allows astronomers to accurately measure galactic distances.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-12)
*   1912 – [Vesto Slipher's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vesto_Slipher "Vesto Slipher") spectrographic studies of spiral nebulae find high [Doppler shifts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doppler_shift "Doppler shift") indicating recessional velocity.
*   1917 – [Heber Curtis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heber_Curtis "Heber Curtis") finds novae in [Andromeda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andromeda_(constellation) "Andromeda (constellation)") Nebula [M31](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy "Andromeda Galaxy") were ten [magnitudes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitude_(astronomy) "Magnitude (astronomy)") fainter than normal, giving a distance estimate of 150,000 [parsecs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec") supporting the "island universes" or independent galaxies hypothesis for spiral nebulae.
*   1918– [Harlow Shapley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harlow_Shapley "Harlow Shapley") demonstrates that [globular clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Globular_cluster "Globular cluster") are arranged in a spheroid or halo whose center is not the Earth, and hypothesizes, correctly, that its center is the [Galactic Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_Center "Galactic Center") of the galaxy,
*   26 April 1920– Harlow Shapley and [Heber Curtis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heber_Curtis "Heber Curtis")[debate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Debate_(astronomy) "Great Debate (astronomy)") whether Andromeda Nebula is within the Milky Way. Curtis notes dark lanes in Andromeda resembling the dust clouds in the Milky Way, as well as significant Doppler shift.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-13)
*   1922 – [Ernst Öpik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernst_%C3%96pik "Ernst Öpik") distance determination supports Andromeda as extra-galactic object.
*   1923– [Edwin Hubble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edwin_Hubble "Edwin Hubble") resolves the [Shapley–Curtis debate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Debate_(astronomy) "Great Debate (astronomy)") by finding [Cepheids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cepheid_variable "Cepheid variable") in the [Andromeda Galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andromeda_Galaxy "Andromeda Galaxy"), definitively proving that there are other galaxies beyond the Milky Way.
*   1930– [Robert Trumpler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Trumpler "Robert Trumpler") uses [open cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_cluster "Open cluster") observations to quantify the [absorption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorption_(electromagnetic_radiation) "Absorption (electromagnetic radiation)") of [light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light "Light") by [interstellar dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_dust "Interstellar dust") in the [galactic plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galactic_plane "Galactic plane"); this absorption had plagued earlier models of the Milky Way.
*   1932– [Karl Guthe Jansky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Guthe_Jansky "Karl Guthe Jansky") discovers [radio noise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_noise "Radio noise") from the center of the Milky Way.
*   1933– [Fritz Zwicky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fritz_Zwicky "Fritz Zwicky") applies the [virial theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virial_theorem "Virial theorem") to the [Coma Cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coma_Cluster "Coma Cluster") and obtains evidence for unseen [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass").
*   1936– Edwin Hubble introduces the spiral, barred spiral, elliptical, and irregular galaxy classifications.
*   1939– [Grote Reber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grote_Reber "Grote Reber") discovers the radio source [Cygnus A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cygnus_A "Cygnus A").
*   1943– [Carl Keenan Seyfert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Keenan_Seyfert "Carl Keenan Seyfert") identifies six spiral galaxies with unusually broad [emission lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emission_line "Emission line"), named [Seyfert galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seyfert_galaxy "Seyfert galaxy").
*   1949– J. G. Bolton, G. J. Stanley, and O. B. Slee identify [NGC 4486](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NGC_4486 "NGC 4486") ([M87](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_galaxy_M87 "Elliptical galaxy M87")) and [NGC 5128](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NGC_5128 "NGC 5128") as extragalactic radio sources.

*   1953– [Gérard de Vaucouleurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_de_Vaucouleurs "Gérard de Vaucouleurs") discovers that the galaxies within approximately 200 million [light-years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year "Light-year") of the [Virgo Cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Cluster "Virgo Cluster") are confined to a giant [supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercluster "Supercluster") disk.
*   1954– [Walter Baade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Baade "Walter Baade") and [Rudolph Minkowski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolph_Minkowski "Rudolph Minkowski") identify the extragalactic optical counterpart of the radio source Cygnus A.
*   1959– Hundreds of radio sources are detected by the [Cambridge Interferometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambridge_Interferometer "Cambridge Interferometer") which produces the [3C catalogue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third_Cambridge_Catalogue_of_Radio_Sources "Third Cambridge Catalogue of Radio Sources"). Many of these are later found to be distant quasars and radio galaxies.
*   1960– [Thomas Matthews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Matthews_(astronomer) "Thomas Matthews (astronomer)") determines the radio position of the _[3C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third\_Cambridge\_Catalogue\_of\_Radio\_Sources "Third Cambridge Catalogue of Radio Sources")_ source [3C 48](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3C_48 "3C 48") to within 5".
*   1960– [Allan Sandage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allan_Sandage "Allan Sandage") optically studies [3C 48](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3C_48 "3C 48") and observes an unusual blue quasistellar object.
*   1962– [Cyril Hazard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyril_Hazard "Cyril Hazard"), M. B. Mackey, and A. J. Shimmins use [lunar occultations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_occultation "Lunar occultation") to determine a precise position for the [quasar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasar "Quasar")[3C 273](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3C_273 "3C 273") and deduce that it is a double source.
*   1962– [Olin Eggen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olin_Eggen "Olin Eggen"), [Donald Lynden-Bell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Lynden-Bell "Donald Lynden-Bell"), and [Allan Sandage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allan_Sandage "Allan Sandage") theorize [galaxy formation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_formation "Galaxy formation") by a single (relatively) rapid monolithic collapse, with the halo forming first, followed by the disk.
*   1963– [Maarten Schmidt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maarten_Schmidt "Maarten Schmidt") identifies the redshifted Balmer lines from the quasar [3C 273](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3C_273 "3C 273").
*   1973– [Jeremiah Ostriker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jeremiah_Ostriker "Jeremiah Ostriker") and [James Peebles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jim_Peebles "Jim Peebles") discover that the amount of visible matter in the disks of typical spiral galaxies is not enough for Newtonian gravitation to keep the disks from flying apart or drastically changing shape.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-14)
*   1973– Donald Gudehus finds that the diameters of the brightest cluster galaxies have increased due to merging, the diameters of the faintest cluster galaxies have decreased due to tidal distention, and that the Virgo cluster has a substantial peculiar velocity.
*   1974– [B. L. Fanaroff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernie_Fanaroff "Bernie Fanaroff") and [J. M. Riley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julia_Riley "Julia Riley")[distinguish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fanaroff-Riley_classification "Fanaroff-Riley classification") between edge-darkened (FR I) and edge-brightened (FR II) radio sources.
*   1976– [Sandra Faber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sandra_Faber "Sandra Faber") and [Robert Jackson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Jackson_(scientist) "Robert Jackson (scientist)") discover the Faber-Jackson relation between the [luminosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luminosity "Luminosity") of an elliptical galaxy and the [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") dispersion in its center. In 1991 the relation is revised by Donald Gudehus.
*   1977– [R. Brent Tully](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R._Brent_Tully "R. Brent Tully") and [Richard Fisher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._Richard_Fisher "J. Richard Fisher") publish the [Tully–Fisher relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tully%E2%80%93Fisher_relation "Tully–Fisher relation") between the luminosity of an isolated spiral galaxy and the velocity of the flat part of its [rotation curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_curve "Rotation curve").
*   1978– [Steve Gregory](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Steve_Gregory_(astronomer)&action=edit&redlink=1 "Steve Gregory (astronomer) (page does not exist)") and [Laird Thompson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laird_A._Thompson "Laird A. Thompson") describe the Coma supercluster.
*   1978– [Donald Gudehus](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Donald_Gudehus&action=edit&redlink=1 "Donald Gudehus (page does not exist)") finds evidence that clusters of galaxies are moving at several hundred kilometers per second relative to the cosmic microwave background radiation.
*   1978– [Vera Rubin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vera_Rubin "Vera Rubin"), [Kent Ford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kent_Ford_(astronomer) "Kent Ford (astronomer)"), N. Thonnard, and [Albert Bosma](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Albert_Bosma&action=edit&redlink=1 "Albert Bosma (page does not exist)") measure the rotation curves of several spiral galaxies and find significant deviations from what is predicted by the Newtonian gravitation of visible stars.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-15)
*   1978– [Leonard Searle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonard_Searle "Leonard Searle") and [Robert Zinn](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Robert_Zinn&action=edit&redlink=1 "Robert Zinn (page does not exist)") theorize that [galaxy formation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_formation "Galaxy formation") occurs through the merger of smaller groups.

*   1981– [Robert Kirshner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Kirshner "Robert Kirshner"), [August Oemler](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=August_Oemler&action=edit&redlink=1 "August Oemler (page does not exist)"), [Paul Schechter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_L._Schechter "Paul L. Schechter"), and [Stephen Shectman](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Stephen_Shectman&action=edit&redlink=1 "Stephen Shectman (page does not exist)") find evidence for a [giant void](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bo%C3%B6tes_Void "Boötes Void") in [Boötes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bo%C3%B6tes "Boötes"), 250 to 330 million light years across.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-16)
*   1985– [Robert Antonucci](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Robert_Antonucci&action=edit&redlink=1 "Robert Antonucci (page does not exist)") and J. Miller discover that the Seyfert II galaxy [NGC 1068](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NGC_1068 "NGC 1068") has broad lines which can only be seen in [polarized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polarized_light "Polarized light") reflected light.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-17)
*   1986– [Amos Yahil](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Amos_Yahil&action=edit&redlink=1 "Amos Yahil (page does not exist)"), [David Walker](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=David_Walker_(astronomer)&action=edit&redlink=1 "David Walker (astronomer) (page does not exist)"), and [Michael Rowan-Robinson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Rowan-Robinson "Michael Rowan-Robinson") find that the direction of the [IRAS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IRAS "IRAS") galaxy density [dipole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dipole "Dipole") agrees with the direction of the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background") temperature dipole.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-18)
*   1987– [David Burstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=David_Burstein&action=edit&redlink=1 "David Burstein (page does not exist)"), [Roger Davies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roger_Davies_(astrophysicist) "Roger Davies (astrophysicist)"), [Alan Dressler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alan_Dressler "Alan Dressler"), [Sandra Faber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sandra_Faber "Sandra Faber"), [Donald Lynden-Bell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Lynden-Bell "Donald Lynden-Bell"), R. J. Terlevich, and [Gary Wegner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gary_A._Wegner "Gary A. Wegner") claim that a large group of galaxies within about 200 million light years of the Milky Way are moving together towards the "[Great Attractor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Attractor "Great Attractor")" in the direction of [Hydra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydra_(constellation) "Hydra (constellation)") and [Centaurus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centaurus "Centaurus").
*   1987– [R. Brent Tully](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R._Brent_Tully "R. Brent Tully") discovers the [Pisces–Cetus Supercluster Complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pisces%E2%80%93Cetus_Supercluster_Complex "Pisces–Cetus Supercluster Complex"), a structure one billion light years long and 150 million light years wide.
*   1989– [Margaret Geller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Margaret_Geller "Margaret Geller") and [John Huchra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Huchra "John Huchra") discover the "[Great Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Wall_(astronomy) "Great Wall (astronomy)")", a sheet of galaxies more than 500 million light years long and 200 million wide, but only 15 million light years thick.
*   1990– [Michael Rowan-Robinson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Rowan-Robinson "Michael Rowan-Robinson") and [Tom Broadhurst](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tom_Broadhurst&action=edit&redlink=1 "Tom Broadhurst (page does not exist)") discover that the IRAS galaxy [IRAS F10214+4724](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=IRAS_F10214%2B4724&action=edit&redlink=1 "IRAS F10214+4724 (page does not exist)") is the brightest known object in the Universe.
*   1991– [Donald Gudehus](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Donald_Gudehus&action=edit&redlink=1 "Donald Gudehus (page does not exist)") discovers a serious systematic bias in certain cluster galaxy data (surface brightness vs. radius parameter, and the ![Image 1: {\displaystyle D_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0fe03857347bf517e7fbda4085b0dafd6018cf18) method) which affect galaxy distances and evolutionary history; he devises a new distance indicator, the reduced galaxian radius parameter, ![Image 2: {\displaystyle r_{g}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee9e9d992552dd787862208ccb7d0ca36c738924), which is free of biases.
*   1992– First detection of large-scale structure in the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background") indicating the seeds of the first clusters of galaxies in the early Universe.
*   1995– First detection of small-scale structure in the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background").
*   1995– [Hubble Deep Field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Deep_Field "Hubble Deep Field") survey of galaxies in field 144 arc seconds across.
*   1998– The [2dF Galaxy Redshift Survey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2dF_Galaxy_Redshift_Survey "2dF Galaxy Redshift Survey") maps the large-scale structure in a section of the Universe close to the Milky Way.
*   1998– The [Hubble Deep Field South](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Deep_Field_South "Hubble Deep Field South") is compiled.
*   1998– Discovery of [accelerating universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_universe "Accelerating universe").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-19)
*   2000– Data from several [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background") experiments give strong evidence that the Universe is "flat" (space is not curved, although space-time is), with important implications for the formation of large-scale structure.

*   2001– First data release from the ongoing [Sloan Digital Sky Survey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sloan_Digital_Sky_Survey "Sloan Digital Sky Survey")
*   2004– The [European Southern Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Southern_Observatory "European Southern Observatory") discovers [Abell 1835 IR1916](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abell_1835_IR1916 "Abell 1835 IR1916"), the most distant galaxy yet seen from Earth.
*   2004– The [Arcminute Microkelvin Imager](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arcminute_Microkelvin_Imager "Arcminute Microkelvin Imager") begins to map the distribution of distant clusters of galaxies
*   2005– [Spitzer Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spitzer_Space_Telescope "Spitzer Space Telescope") data confirm what had been considered likely since the early 1990s from [radio telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_telescope "Radio telescope") data, i.e., that the [Milky Way Galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way_Galaxy "Milky Way Galaxy") is a [barred spiral galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barred_spiral_galaxy "Barred spiral galaxy").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-21)
*   2012– Astronomers report the discovery of the most distant [dwarf galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_galaxy "Dwarf galaxy") yet found, approximately 10 billion light-years away.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-22)
*   2012– The [Huge-LQG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huge-LQG "Huge-LQG"), a [large quasar group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_quasar_group "Large quasar group"), one of the largest known structures in the universe, is discovered.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-23)
*   2013– The galaxy [Z8 GND 5296](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Z8_GND_5296 "Z8 GND 5296") is confirmed by [spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopy "Spectroscopy") to be one of the most distant galaxies found up to this time. Formed just 700 million years after the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang"), expansion of the universe has carried it to its current location, about 13 billion light years away from Earth (30 billion light years [comoving distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comoving_distance "Comoving distance")).[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-24)
*   2013– The [Hercules–Corona Borealis Great Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hercules%E2%80%93Corona_Borealis_Great_Wall "Hercules–Corona Borealis Great Wall"), a massive [galaxy filament](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filament "Galaxy filament") and the largest known structure in the universe, was discovered through [gamma-ray burst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma-ray_burst "Gamma-ray burst") mapping.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-2014paper-25)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-original-26)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-conundrum-27)
*   2014– The [Laniakea Supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laniakea_Supercluster "Laniakea Supercluster"), the galaxy supercluster that is home to the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") is defined via a new way of defining superclusters according to the [relative velocities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radial_velocity "Radial velocity") of [galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy").[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-:0-28)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-29) The new definition of the local supercluster subsumes the prior defined local supercluster, the [Virgo Supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virgo_Supercluster "Virgo Supercluster"), as an appendage.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-NRAO-2014-09-03-30)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-Reuters-2014-09-03-31)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-Nature-2014-09-03-32)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-NYT-20140903-33)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-SandT-2014-09-03-34)
*   2020– Astronomers report the discovery of a large cavity in the [Ophiuchus Supercluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ophiuchus_Supercluster "Ophiuchus Supercluster"), first detected in 2016 and originating from a [supermassive black hole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supermassive_black_hole "Supermassive black hole") with the mass of 10 million solar masses. The cavity is a result of [the largest known explosion in the Universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ophiuchus_Supercluster_explosion "Ophiuchus Supercluster explosion"). The formerly [active galactic nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_galactic_nucleus "Active galactic nucleus") created it by emitting radiation and particle [jets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_jet "Relativistic jet"), possibly as a result of a spike in supply of gas to the black hole that could have occurred if a galaxy fell into the centre of the cavity.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-NYT-20200306-35)[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-36)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-37)
*   2020– Astronomers report to have discovered the [disk galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disk_galaxy "Disk galaxy")[Wolfe Disk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfe_Disk "Wolfe Disk"), dating back to when the universe was only 1.5 billion years old, possibly indicating the need to revise theories of [galaxy formation and evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_formation_and_evolution "Galaxy formation and evolution").[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-38)[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-39)[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-40)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-41)
*   2020– The [South Pole Wall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Pole_Wall "South Pole Wall") is a massive cosmic structure formed by a giant wall of galaxies (a galaxy filament) that extends across at least 1.37 billion light-years of space, and is located approximately a half billion light-years away.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-APJ-20200710-42)[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-HARV-202001-43)[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-HW-20200710-44)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-NYT-20200710-45)[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-LS-20200709-46)[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-SA-20200714-47)
*   2020– After a 20-year-long survey, astrophysicists of the [Sloan Digital Sky Survey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sloan_Digital_Sky_Survey "Sloan Digital Sky Survey") publish the [largest, most detailed 3D map of the universe so far](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmology "Cosmology"), fill a gap of 11 billion years in [its expansion history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronology_of_the_universe "Chronology of the universe"), and provide data which supports the theory of a flat [geometry of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape_of_the_universe "Shape of the universe") and confirms that different regions seem to be [expanding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expansion_of_the_universe "Expansion of the universe") at [different speeds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble%27s_law#Measured_values_of_the_Hubble_constant "Hubble's law").[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-48)[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-49)
*   2022– [James Webb Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope "James Webb Space Telescope") (JWST) releases the [Webb's First Deep Field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Webb%27s_First_Deep_Field "Webb's First Deep Field").
*   2022– JWST detects [CEERS-93316](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CEERS-93316 "CEERS-93316"), a candidate high-redshift [galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy"), with an estimated [redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift") of approximately z = 16.7, corresponding to 235.8 million years[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-EDIN-20220801-50) after the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang").[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-Planck_2018age-51) If confirmed, it is one of the earliest and most distant known galaxies observed.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timeline_of_knowledge_about_galaxies,_clusters_of_galaxies,_and_large-scale_structure#cite_note-ARX-20220725-52)
