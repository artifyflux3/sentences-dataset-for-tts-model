**Helium** (from [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language"): ἥλιος, [romanized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanization_of_Greek "Romanization of Greek"):_[helios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helios "Helios")_, [lit.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literal_translation "Literal translation")'sun') is a [chemical element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element"); it has [symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_symbol "Chemical symbol")**He** and [atomic number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_number "Atomic number") 2. It is a colorless, odorless, non-toxic, [inert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inert_gas "Inert gas"), [monatomic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monatomic "Monatomic")[gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas "Gas") and the first in the [noble gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noble_gas "Noble gas") group in the [periodic table](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodic_table "Periodic table").[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-14) Its [boiling point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boiling_point "Boiling point") is the lowest among all the [elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element"), and it does not have a [melting point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melting_point "Melting point") at standard pressures. It is the second-lightest and second-most [abundant element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements "Abundance of the chemical elements") in the observable [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe"), after [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen"). It is present at about 24% of the total elemental mass, which is more than 12 times the mass of all the heavier elements combined. Its abundance is similar to this in both the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun") and [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter"), because of the very high [nuclear binding energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_binding_energy "Nuclear binding energy") (per [nucleon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleon "Nucleon")) of [helium-4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium-4 "Helium-4") with respect to the next three elements after helium. This helium-4 binding energy also accounts for why it is a product of both [nuclear fusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_fusion "Nuclear fusion") and [radioactive decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioactive_decay "Radioactive decay"). The most common isotope of helium in the universe is helium-4, the vast majority of which was formed during the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang"). Large amounts of new helium are created by nuclear fusion of hydrogen in [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stars "Stars").

Helium was first detected as an unknown, yellow [spectral line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_line "Spectral line") signature in sunlight during a [solar eclipse in 1868](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_August_18,_1868 "Solar eclipse of August 18, 1868") by [Georges Rayet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georges_Rayet "Georges Rayet"),[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-15) Captain C. T. Haig,[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-16)[Norman R. Pogson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N._R._Pogson "N. R. Pogson"),[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-17) and Lieutenant John Herschel,[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-18) and was subsequently confirmed by French astronomer [Jules Janssen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Janssen "Pierre Janssen").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-19) Janssen is often jointly credited with detecting the element, along with [Norman Lockyer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norman_Lockyer "Norman Lockyer"). Janssen recorded the helium spectral line during the solar eclipse of 1868, while Lockyer observed it from Britain. However, only Lockyer proposed that the line was due to a new element, which he named after the Sun. The formal [discovery of the element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discovery_of_the_chemical_elements "Discovery of the chemical elements") was made in [1895](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1895 "1895") by chemists [Sir William Ramsay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sir_William_Ramsay "Sir William Ramsay"), [Per Teodor Cleve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Per_Teodor_Cleve "Per Teodor Cleve"), and [Nils Abraham Langlet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nils_Abraham_Langlet "Nils Abraham Langlet"), who found helium emanating from the [uranium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranium "Uranium") ore [cleveite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cleveite "Cleveite"), which is now not regarded as a separate mineral species, but as a variety of [uraninite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uraninite "Uraninite").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-mindat_cleveite-20)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-mindat_uraninite-21) In 1903, large reserves of helium were found in [natural gas fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_gas_field "Natural gas field") in parts of the United States, by far the largest supplier of the gas today.

Liquid helium is used in [cryogenics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_cryogenics "Helium cryogenics") (its largest single use, consuming about a quarter of production), and in the [cooling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryocooler "Cryocooler") of [superconducting magnets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superconducting_magnet "Superconducting magnet"), with its main commercial application in [MRI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MRI "MRI") scanners. Helium's other industrial uses—as a pressurizing and purge gas, as a protective atmosphere for [arc welding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_welding "Arc welding"), and in processes such as growing [crystals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal "Crystal") to make [silicon wafers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_wafer "Silicon wafer")—account for half of the gas produced. A small but well-known use is as a [lifting gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lifting_gas "Lifting gas") in [balloons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balloon "Balloon") and [airships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airship "Airship").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-22) As with any gas whose density differs from that of air, inhaling a small volume of helium temporarily changes the timbre and quality of the [human voice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_voice "Human voice"). In scientific research, the behavior of the two fluid phases of helium-4 (helium I and helium II) is important to researchers studying [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") (in particular the property of [superfluidity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity "Superfluidity")) and to those looking at the phenomena, such as [superconductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superconductivity "Superconductivity"), produced in [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter") near [absolute zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_zero "Absolute zero").

On Earth, it is relatively rare—5.2 [ppm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts_per_million "Parts per million") by volume in the [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere "Atmosphere"). Most terrestrial helium present today is created by the natural [radioactive decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioactive_decay "Radioactive decay") of heavy radioactive elements ([thorium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thorium "Thorium") and uranium, although there are other examples), as the [alpha particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_particle "Alpha particle") emitted by such decays consist of helium-4 [nuclei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus"). This [radiogenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiogenic "Radiogenic") helium is trapped with [natural gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_gas "Natural gas") in concentrations as great as 7% by volume, from which it is extracted commercially by a low-temperature separation process called [fractional distillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractional_distillation "Fractional distillation"). Terrestrial helium is a non-renewable resource because once released into the atmosphere, it promptly [escapes into space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_escape "Atmospheric escape"). Its supply is thought to be rapidly diminishing.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Connor-23)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-24) However, some studies suggest that helium produced deep in the Earth by radioactive decay can collect in natural gas reserves in larger-than-expected quantities,[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-25) in some cases having been released by volcanic activity.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Sample-26)

History
-------

### Scientific discoveries

The first evidence of helium was observed on August 18, 1868, as a bright yellow line with a [wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength "Wavelength") of 587.49 nanometers in the [spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emission_spectrum "Emission spectrum") of the [chromosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromosphere "Chromosphere") of the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun"). The line was detected by French astronomer [Jules Janssen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Janssen "Pierre Janssen") during [a total solar eclipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_eclipse_of_August_18,_1868 "Solar eclipse of August 18, 1868") in [Guntur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guntur "Guntur"), India.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-frnch-27)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-nbb-28) This line was initially assumed to be [sodium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium "Sodium"). On October 20 of the same year, English astronomer [Norman Lockyer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norman_Lockyer "Norman Lockyer") observed a yellow line in the solar spectrum, which he named the D 3 because it was near the known D 1 and D 2[Fraunhofer lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fraunhofer_line "Fraunhofer line") of sodium.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Lockyer_1868-29)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-enc-30) He concluded that it was caused by an element in the Sun unknown on Earth. Lockyer named the element with the Greek word for the Sun, ἥλιος (_[helios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helios "Helios")_).[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-31)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-32) It is sometimes said that English chemist [Edward Frankland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Frankland "Edward Frankland") was also involved in the naming, but this is unlikely as he doubted the existence of this new element. The ending "-ium" is unusual, as it normally applies only to metallic elements; probably Lockyer, being an astronomer, was unaware of the chemical conventions.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-jensen-33)

[![Image 5: Picture of visible spectrum with superimposed sharp yellow and blue and violet lines](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Helium_spectrum.jpg/250px-Helium_spectrum.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Helium_spectrum.jpg)

Spectral lines of helium

In 1881, Italian physicist [Luigi Palmieri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luigi_Palmieri "Luigi Palmieri") detected helium on Earth for the first time through its D 3 spectral line, when he analyzed a material that had been [sublimated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition) "Sublimation (phase transition)") during a recent eruption of [Mount Vesuvius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Vesuvius "Mount Vesuvius").[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Palmieri_1881-34)

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/William_Ramsay_working.jpg/250px-William_Ramsay_working.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:William_Ramsay_working.jpg)

Sir [William Ramsay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Ramsay "William Ramsay"), the discoverer of terrestrial helium

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a3/Clevite_sample_%2835321726345%29.jpg/250px-Clevite_sample_%2835321726345%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Clevite_sample_(35321726345).jpg)

The cleveite sample from which Ramsay first purified helium[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Kirk-35)

On March 26, 1895, Scottish chemist [Sir William Ramsay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Ramsay "William Ramsay") isolated helium on Earth by treating the mineral cleveite (a variety of uraninite with at least 10% [rare-earth elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rare-earth_elements "Rare-earth elements")) with mineral [acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid "Acid"). Ramsay was looking for [argon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argon "Argon") but, after separating [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") and [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") from the gas, liberated by [sulfuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid"), he noticed a bright yellow line that matched the D 3 line observed in the spectrum of the Sun.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-enc-30)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-36)[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-37)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-38) These samples were identified as helium by Lockyer and British physicist [William Crookes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Crookes "William Crookes").[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-39)[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-40) It was independently isolated from cleveite in the same year by chemists [Per Teodor Cleve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Per_Teodor_Cleve "Per Teodor Cleve") and [Abraham Langlet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abraham_Langlet "Abraham Langlet") in [Uppsala](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uppsala "Uppsala"), Sweden, who collected enough of the gas to accurately determine its [atomic weight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_weight "Atomic weight").[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-41)[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-42)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-nbb-28)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-43) Helium was also isolated by American geochemist [William Francis Hillebrand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Francis_Hillebrand "William Francis Hillebrand") prior to Ramsay's discovery, when he noticed unusual spectral lines while testing a sample of the mineral uraninite. Hillebrand, however, attributed the lines to [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen").[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-44) His letter of congratulations to Ramsay offers an interesting case of discovery, and near-discovery, in science.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-45)

In 1907, [Ernest Rutherford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernest_Rutherford "Ernest Rutherford") and [Thomas Royds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Royds "Thomas Royds") demonstrated that [alpha particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_particle "Alpha particle") are helium [nuclei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus") by allowing the particles to penetrate the thin glass wall of an [evacuated tube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evacuated_tube "Evacuated tube"), then creating a discharge in the tube, to study the spectrum of the new gas inside.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-46) In 1908, helium was first liquefied by Dutch physicist [Heike Kamerlingh Onnes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heike_Kamerlingh_Onnes "Heike Kamerlingh Onnes") by cooling the gas to less than 5 K (−268.15°C; −450.67°F).[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-47)[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-48) He tried to solidify it by further reducing the temperature but failed, because helium does not solidify at atmospheric pressure. Onnes' student [Willem Hendrik Keesom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Willem_Hendrik_Keesom "Willem Hendrik Keesom") was eventually able to solidify 1 cm 3 of helium in 1926 by applying additional external pressure.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-49)[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-50)

In 1913, [Niels Bohr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niels_Bohr "Niels Bohr") published his "trilogy"[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Hoyer-51)[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-52) on atomic structure that included a reconsideration of the [Pickering–Fowler series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pickering%E2%80%93Fowler_series "Pickering–Fowler series") as central evidence in support of his [model of the atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohr_model "Bohr model").[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-53)[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Robotti-54) This series is named for [Edward Charles Pickering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Charles_Pickering "Edward Charles Pickering"), who in 1896 published observations of previously unknown lines in the spectrum of the star [ζ Puppis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeta_Puppis "Zeta Puppis")[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-55) (these are now known to occur with [Wolf–Rayet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolf-Rayet_star "Wolf-Rayet star") and other hot stars).[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-56) Pickering attributed the observation (lines at 4551, 5411, and 10123[Å](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%85ngstr%C3%B6m "Ångström")) to a new form of hydrogen with half-integer transition levels.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-57)[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-58) In 1912, [Alfred Fowler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_Fowler "Alfred Fowler")[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-59) managed to produce similar lines from a hydrogen-helium mixture, and supported Pickering's conclusion as to their origin.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-60) Bohr's model does not allow for half-integer transitions (nor does quantum mechanics) and Bohr concluded that Pickering and Fowler were wrong, and instead assigned these spectral lines to ionised helium, He+.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-61) Fowler was initially skeptical[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-62) but was ultimately convinced[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-63) that Bohr was correct,[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Hoyer-51) and by 1915 "spectroscopists had transferred [the Pickering–Fowler series] definitively [from hydrogen] to helium."[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Robotti-54)[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-64) Bohr's theoretical work on the Pickering series had demonstrated the need for "a re-examination of problems that seemed already to have been solved within classical theories" and provided important confirmation for his atomic theory.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Robotti-54)

In 1938, Russian physicist [Pyotr Leonidovich Kapitsa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyotr_Leonidovich_Kapitsa "Pyotr Leonidovich Kapitsa") discovered that [helium-4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium-4 "Helium-4") has almost no [viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscosity "Viscosity") at temperatures near [absolute zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_zero "Absolute zero"), a phenomenon now called [superfluidity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluidity "Superfluidity").[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-65) This phenomenon is related to [Bose–Einstein condensation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_condensation "Bose–Einstein condensation"). In 1972, the same phenomenon was observed in [helium-3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium-3 "Helium-3"), but at temperatures much closer to absolute zero, by American physicists [Douglas D. Osheroff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Douglas_D._Osheroff "Douglas D. Osheroff"), [David M. Lee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_M._Lee "David M. Lee"), and [Robert C. Richardson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Coleman_Richardson "Robert Coleman Richardson"). The phenomenon in helium-3 is thought to be related to pairing of helium-3 [fermions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermion "Fermion") to make [bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boson "Boson"), in analogy to [Cooper pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cooper_pairs "Cooper pairs") of electrons producing [superconductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superconductivity "Superconductivity").[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-66)

In 1961, Vignos and Fairbank reported the existence of a different phase of solid helium-4, designated the gamma-phase. It exists for a narrow range of pressure between 1.45 and 1.78 K.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-67)

### Extraction and use

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Kansas_Helium_Marker.jpg/250px-Kansas_Helium_Marker.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Kansas_Helium_Marker.jpg)

Historical marker, denoting a massive helium find near [Dexter, Kansas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dexter,_Kansas "Dexter, Kansas")

After an oil drilling operation in 1903 in [Dexter, Kansas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dexter,_Kansas "Dexter, Kansas") produced a gas geyser that would not burn, Kansas state geologist [Erasmus Haworth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erasmus_Haworth "Erasmus Haworth") collected samples of the escaping gas and took them back to the [University of Kansas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Kansas "University of Kansas") at Lawrence where, with the help of chemists [Hamilton Cady](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamilton_Cady "Hamilton Cady") and David McFarland, he discovered that the gas consisted of, by volume, 72% nitrogen, 15% [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane") (a [combustible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combustible "Combustible") percentage only with sufficient oxygen), 1% hydrogen, and 12% an unidentifiable gas.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-nbb-28)[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-68) With further analysis, Cady and McFarland discovered that 1.84% of the gas sample was helium.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-69)[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-70) This showed that despite its overall rarity on Earth, helium was concentrated in large quantities under the [American Great Plains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Great_Plains "American Great Plains"), available for extraction as a byproduct of [natural gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_gas "Natural gas").[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-71)

Following a suggestion by Sir [Richard Threlfall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_Threlfall "Richard Threlfall"), the [United States Navy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Navy "United States Navy") sponsored three small experimental helium plants during World War I. The goal was to supply [barrage balloons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barrage_balloon "Barrage balloon") with the non-flammable, lighter-than-air gas. A total of 5,700 m 3 (200,000 cu ft) of 92% helium was produced in the program even though less than a cubic meter of the gas had previously been obtained.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-enc-30) Some of this gas was used in the world's first helium-filled airship, the U.S. Navy's [C-class blimp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C-class_blimp "C-class blimp") C-7, which flew its maiden voyage from [Hampton Roads, Virginia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hampton_Roads,_Virginia "Hampton Roads, Virginia"), to [Bolling Field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bolling_Field "Bolling Field") in Washington, D.C., on December 1, 1921,[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-72) nearly two years before the Navy's first _rigid_ helium-filled airship, the [Naval Aircraft Factory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naval_Aircraft_Factory "Naval Aircraft Factory")-built [USS _Shenandoah_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/USS_Shenandoah_(ZR-1) "USS Shenandoah (ZR-1)"), flew in September 1923.

Although the extraction process using low-temperature [gas liquefaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_liquefaction "Gas liquefaction") was not developed in time to be significant during World War I, production continued. Helium was primarily used as a [lifting gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lifting_gas "Lifting gas") in lighter-than-air craft. During World War II, the demand increased for helium for lifting gas and for shielded arc [welding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Welding "Welding"). The [helium mass spectrometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_mass_spectrometer "Helium mass spectrometer") was also vital in the atomic bomb [Manhattan Project](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manhattan_Project "Manhattan Project").[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-73)

The [government of the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Government_of_the_United_States "Government of the United States") set up the [National Helium Reserve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Helium_Reserve "National Helium Reserve") in 1925 at [Amarillo, Texas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amarillo,_Texas "Amarillo, Texas"), with the goal of supplying military [airships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airship "Airship") in time of war and commercial airships in peacetime.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-enc-30) Because of the [Helium Act of 1925](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_Act_of_1925 "Helium Act of 1925"), which banned the export of scarce helium on which the US then had a production monopoly, together with the prohibitive cost of the gas, German [Zeppelins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeppelin "Zeppelin") were forced to use hydrogen as lifting gas, which would gain infamy in the [Hindenburg disaster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindenburg_disaster "Hindenburg disaster"). The helium market after World War II was depressed but the reserve was expanded in the 1950s to ensure a supply of [liquid helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_helium "Liquid helium") as a coolant to create oxygen/hydrogen [rocket fuel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket_fuel "Rocket fuel") (among other uses) during the [Space Race](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Race "Space Race") and [Cold War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War "Cold War"). Helium use in the United States in 1965 was more than eight times the peak wartime consumption.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-74)

After the Helium Acts Amendments of 1960 (Public Law 86–777), the [U.S. Bureau of Mines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Bureau_of_Mines "United States Bureau of Mines") arranged for five private plants to recover helium from natural gas. For this helium conservation program, the Bureau built a 425-mile (684 km) pipeline from [Bushton, Kansas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bushton,_Kansas "Bushton, Kansas"), to connect those plants with the government's partially depleted Cliffside gas field near Amarillo, Texas. This helium-nitrogen mixture was injected and stored in the Cliffside gas field until needed, at which time it was further purified.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-75)

By 1995, a billion cubic meters of the gas had been collected and the reserve was US$1.4 billion in debt, prompting the [Congress of the United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congress_of_the_United_States "Congress of the United States") in 1996 to discontinue the reserve.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-nbb-28)[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-stwertka-76) The resulting [Helium Privatization Act of 1996](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_Privatization_Act_of_1996 "Helium Privatization Act of 1996")[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-77) (Public Law 104–273) directed the [United States Department of the Interior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Department_of_the_Interior "United States Department of the Interior") to empty the reserve, with sales starting by 2005.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-78)

Helium produced between 1930 and 1945 was about 98.3% pure (2% nitrogen), which was adequate for airships. In 1945, a small amount of 99.9% helium was produced for welding use. By 1949, commercial quantities of Grade A 99.95% helium were available.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-79)

For many years, the United States produced more than 90% of commercially usable helium in the world, while extraction plants in Canada, Poland, Russia, and other nations produced the remainder. In the mid-1990s, a new plant in [Arzew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arzew "Arzew"), Algeria, producing 17 million cubic metres (600 million cubic feet) began operation, with enough production to cover all of Europe's demand. Meanwhile, by 2000, the consumption of helium within the U.S. had risen to more than 15 million kg per year.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-80) In 2004–2006, additional plants in [Ras Laffan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ras_Laffan_Industrial_City "Ras Laffan Industrial City"), [Qatar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qatar "Qatar"), and [Skikda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skikda "Skikda"), Algeria were built. Algeria quickly became the second leading producer of helium.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-wwsupply-81) Through this time, both helium consumption and the costs of producing helium increased.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Kaplan2007-82) From 2002 to 2007 helium prices doubled.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Basu2007-83)

As of 2012, the [United States National Helium Reserve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Helium_Reserve "National Helium Reserve") accounted for 30 percent of the world's helium.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Newcomb-84) The reserve was expected to run out of helium in 2018.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Newcomb-84) Despite that, a proposed bill in the [United States Senate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Senate "United States Senate") would allow the reserve to continue to sell the gas. Other large reserves were in the [Hugoton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hugoton_Natural_Gas_Area "Hugoton Natural Gas Area") in [Kansas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kansas "Kansas"), United States, and nearby gas fields of Kansas and the [panhandles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Panhandles "Panhandles") of [Texas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Texas "Texas") and [Oklahoma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oklahoma "Oklahoma"). New helium plants were scheduled to open in 2012 in [Qatar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qatar "Qatar"), Russia, and the US state of [Wyoming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wyoming "Wyoming"), but they were not expected to ease the shortage.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Newcomb-84)

In 2013, Qatar started up the world's largest helium unit,[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-85) although the [2017 Qatar diplomatic crisis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2017_Qatar_diplomatic_crisis "2017 Qatar diplomatic crisis") severely affected helium production there.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-86) 2014 was widely acknowledged to be a year of over-supply in the helium business, following years of renowned shortages.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-87) Nasdaq reported (2015) that for [Air Products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_Products "Air Products"), an international corporation that sells gases for industrial use, helium volumes remain under economic pressure due to feedstock supply constraints.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-88)

Characteristics
---------------

### Atom

[![Image 9: Picture of a diffuse gray sphere with grayscale density decreasing from the center. Length scale about 1 Angstrom. An inset outlines the structure of the core, with two red and two blue atoms at the length scale of 1 femtometer.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/23/Helium_atom_QM.svg/250px-Helium_atom_QM.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Helium_atom_QM.svg)

**The helium atom.** Depicted are the [nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus") (pink) and the [electron cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_cloud "Electron cloud") distribution (black). The nucleus (upper right) in helium-4 is in reality spherically symmetric and closely resembles the electron cloud, although for more complicated nuclei this is not always the case.

#### In quantum mechanics

In the perspective of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), helium is the second simplest [atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom") to model, following the [hydrogen atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_atom "Hydrogen atom"). Helium is composed of two electrons in [atomic orbitals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_orbital "Atomic orbital") surrounding a nucleus containing two protons and (usually) two neutrons. As in Newtonian mechanics, no system that consists of more than two particles can be solved with an exact analytical mathematical approach (see [3-body problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3-body_problem "3-body problem")) and helium is no exception. Thus, numerical mathematical methods are required, even to solve the system of one nucleus and two electrons. Such [computational chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_chemistry "Computational chemistry") methods have been used to create a quantum mechanical picture of helium electron binding which is accurate to within < 2% of the correct value, in a few computational steps.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-89) Such models show that each electron in helium partly screens the nucleus from the other, so that the [effective nuclear charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effective_nuclear_charge "Effective nuclear charge")_Z_ eff which each electron sees is about 1.69 units, not the 2 charges of a classic "bare" helium nucleus.

#### Related stability of the helium-4 nucleus and electron shell

The nucleus of the helium-4 atom is identical with an [alpha particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_particle "Alpha particle"). High-energy electron-scattering experiments show its charge to decrease exponentially from a maximum at a central point, exactly as does the charge density of helium's own [electron cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_cloud "Electron cloud"). This symmetry reflects similar underlying physics: the pair of neutrons and the pair of protons in helium's nucleus obey the same quantum mechanical rules as do helium's pair of electrons (although the nuclear particles are subject to a different nuclear binding potential), so that all these [fermions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermion "Fermion") fully occupy 1s orbitals in pairs, none of them possessing orbital angular momentum, and each cancelling the other's intrinsic spin. This arrangement is thus energetically extremely stable for all these particles and has [astrophysical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_astrophysics "Nuclear astrophysics") implications.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Parker2022-90) Namely, adding another particle – proton, neutron, or alpha particle – would consume rather than release energy; all systems with [mass number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_number "Mass number") 5, as well as [beryllium-8](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beryllium-8 "Beryllium-8") (comprising two alpha particles), are unbound.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-8gap-91)

For example, the stability and low energy of the electron cloud state in helium accounts for the element's chemical inertness, and also the lack of interaction of helium atoms with each other, producing the lowest melting and boiling points of all the elements. In a similar way, the particular energetic stability of the helium-4 nucleus, produced by similar effects, accounts for the ease of helium-4 production in atomic reactions that involve either heavy-particle emission or fusion. Some stable helium-3 (two protons and one neutron) is produced in fusion reactions from hydrogen, though its estimated abundance in the universe is about 10−5 relative to helium-4.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Pitrou18-92)

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/53/Binding_energy_curve_-_common_isotopes.svg/250px-Binding_energy_curve_-_common_isotopes.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Binding_energy_curve_-_common_isotopes.svg)

Binding energy per nucleon of common isotopes. The binding energy per particle of helium-4 is significantly larger than all nearby nuclides.

The unusual stability of the helium-4 nucleus is also important [cosmologically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmology "Cosmology"): it explains the fact that in the first few minutes after the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang"), as the "soup" of free protons and neutrons which had initially been created in about 6:1 ratio cooled to the point that nuclear binding was possible, almost all first compound atomic nuclei to form were helium-4 nuclei. Owing to the relatively tight binding of helium-4 nuclei, its production consumed nearly all of the free neutrons in a few minutes, before they could beta-decay, and thus few neutrons were available to form heavier atoms such as lithium, beryllium, or boron. Helium-4 nuclear binding per nucleon is stronger than in any of these elements (see [nucleogenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleogenesis "Nucleogenesis") and [binding energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binding_energy "Binding energy")) and thus, once helium had been formed, no energetic drive was available to make elements 3, 4 and 5.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-bbn99-93) It is barely energetically favorable for helium to fuse into the next element with a lower energy per [nucleon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleon "Nucleon"), carbon. However, due to the short lifetime of the intermediate beryllium-8, this process requires three helium nuclei striking each other nearly simultaneously (see [triple-alpha process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple-alpha_process "Triple-alpha process")).[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-8gap-91) There was thus no time for significant carbon to be formed in the few minutes after the Big Bang, before the early expanding universe cooled to the temperature and pressure point where helium fusion to carbon was no longer possible. This left the early universe with a very similar ratio of hydrogen/helium as is observed today (3 parts hydrogen to 1 part helium-4 by mass), with nearly all the neutrons in the universe trapped in helium-4.

All heavier elements (including those necessary for rocky planets like the Earth, and for carbon-based or other life) have thus been created since the Big Bang in stars which were hot enough to fuse helium itself. All elements other than hydrogen and helium today account for only 2% of the mass of atomic matter in the universe. Helium-4, by contrast, comprises about 24% of the mass of the universe's ordinary matter—nearly all the ordinary matter that is not hydrogen.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Pitrou18-92)[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Hsyu20-94)

### Gas and plasma phases

[![Image 11: Illuminated light red gas discharge tubes shaped as letters H and e](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/HeTube.jpg/250px-HeTube.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:HeTube.jpg)

Helium discharge tube shaped into 'He', the element's symbol.

Helium is the second least reactive noble gas after [neon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neon "Neon"), and thus the second least reactive of all elements.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-95) It is [chemically inert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemically_inert "Chemically inert") and monatomic in all standard conditions. Because of helium's relatively low molar (atomic) mass, its [thermal conductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_conductivity "Thermal conductivity"), [specific heat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_heat "Specific heat"), and [sound speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_sound "Speed of sound") in the gas phase are all greater than any other gas except [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen"). For these reasons and the small size of helium monatomic molecules, helium [diffuses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffusion "Diffusion") through solids at a rate three times that of air and around 65% that of hydrogen.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-enc-30)

Helium is the least water-[soluble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solubility "Solubility") monatomic gas,[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-96) and one of the least water-soluble of any gas ([CF 4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrafluoromethane "Tetrafluoromethane"), [SF 6](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_hexafluoride "Sulfur hexafluoride"), and [C 4 F 8](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octafluorocyclobutane "Octafluorocyclobutane") have lower mole fraction solubilities: 0.3802, 0.4394, and 0.2372 x 2/10−5, respectively, versus helium's 0.70797 x 2/10−5),[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-97) and helium's [index of refraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Index_of_refraction "Index of refraction") is closer to unity than that of any other gas.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-98) Helium has a negative [Joule–Thomson coefficient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joule%E2%80%93Thomson_coefficient "Joule–Thomson coefficient") at normal ambient temperatures, meaning it heats up when allowed to freely expand. Only below its [Joule–Thomson inversion temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joule%E2%80%93Thomson_inversion_temperature "Joule–Thomson inversion temperature") (of about 32 to 50 K at 1 atmosphere) does it cool upon free expansion.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-enc-30) Once precooled below this temperature, helium can be liquefied through expansion cooling.

Most extraterrestrial helium is [plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)") in stars, with properties quite different from those of atomic helium. In a plasma, helium's electrons are not bound to its nucleus, resulting in very high electrical conductivity, even when the gas is only partially ionized. The charged particles are highly influenced by magnetic and electric fields. For example, in the [solar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind "Solar wind") together with ionized hydrogen, the particles interact with the Earth's [magnetosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetosphere "Magnetosphere"), giving rise to [Birkeland currents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birkeland_current "Birkeland current") and the [aurora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aurora "Aurora").[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-99)

### Liquid phase

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Phase_diagram_of_Helium-4-en.svg/330px-Phase_diagram_of_Helium-4-en.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Phase_diagram_of_Helium-4-en.svg)

Phase diagram of helium-4. (Atmospheric pressure is about 0.1 MPa)

[![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3f/2_Helium.png/250px-2_Helium.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:2_Helium.png)

Liquefied helium. This helium is not only liquid, but has been cooled to the point of [superfluidity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluid "Superfluid"). The drop of liquid at the bottom of the glass represents helium spontaneously escaping from the container over the side, to empty out of the container. The energy to drive this process is supplied by the potential energy of the falling helium.

Helium liquifies when cooled below 4.2 K at atmospheric pressure. Unlike any other element, however, helium remains liquid down to a temperature of [absolute zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_zero "Absolute zero"). This is a direct effect of quantum mechanics: specifically, the [zero point energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_point_energy "Zero point energy") of the system is too high to allow freezing. Pressures above about 25 atmospheres are required to freeze it. There are two liquid phases: Helium I is a conventional liquid, and Helium II, which occurs at a lower temperature, is a [superfluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluid "Superfluid").

#### Helium I

Below its [boiling point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boiling_point "Boiling point") of 4.22 K (−268.93°C; −452.07°F) and above the [lambda point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda_point "Lambda point") of 2.1768 K (−270.9732°C; −455.7518°F), the [isotope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope "Isotope") helium-4 exists in a normal colorless liquid state, called _helium I_.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-enc-30) Like other [cryogenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryogenic "Cryogenic") liquids, helium I boils when it is heated and contracts when its temperature is lowered. Below the lambda point, however, helium does not boil, and it expands as the temperature is lowered further.

Helium I has a gas-like [index of refraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Index_of_refraction "Index of refraction") of 1.026 which makes its surface so hard to see that floats of [Styrofoam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expanded_polystyrene "Expanded polystyrene") are often used to show where the surface is.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-enc-30) This colorless liquid has a very low [viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscosity "Viscosity") and a density of 0.145–0.125 g/mL (between about 0 and 4 K),[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-crc6120-100) which is only one-fourth the value expected from [classical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_physics "Classical physics").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-enc-30)[Quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") is needed to explain this property and thus both states of liquid helium (helium I and helium II) are called _quantum fluids_, meaning they display atomic properties on a macroscopic scale. This may be an effect of its boiling point being so close to absolute zero, preventing random molecular motion ([thermal energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_energy "Thermal energy")) from masking the atomic properties.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-enc-30)

#### Helium II

Liquid helium below its lambda point (called _helium II_) exhibits very unusual characteristics. Due to its high [thermal conductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_conductivity "Thermal conductivity"), when it boils, it does not bubble but rather evaporates directly from its surface. [Helium-3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium-3 "Helium-3") also has a [superfluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superfluid "Superfluid") phase, but only at much lower temperatures; as a result, less is known about the properties of the isotope.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-enc-30)

[![Image 14: A cross-sectional drawing showing one vessel inside another. There is a liquid in the outer vessel, and it tends to flow into the inner vessel over its walls.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Helium-II-creep.svg/250px-Helium-II-creep.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Helium-II-creep.svg)

Unlike ordinary liquids, helium II will creep along surfaces in order to reach an equal level; after a short while, the levels in the two containers will equalize. The [Rollin film](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rollin_film "Rollin film") also covers the interior of the larger container; if it were not sealed, the helium II would creep out and escape.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-enc-30)

Helium II is a superfluid, a [quantum mechanical state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macroscopic_quantum_phenomena "Macroscopic quantum phenomena") of matter with strange properties. For example, when it flows through capillaries as thin as 10 to 100 [nm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanometre "Nanometre") it has no measurable [viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscosity "Viscosity").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-nbb-28) However, when measurements were done between two moving discs, a viscosity comparable to that of gaseous helium was observed. Existing theory explains this using the _two-fluid model_ for helium II. In this model, liquid helium below the lambda point is viewed as containing a proportion of helium atoms in a [ground state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_state "Ground state"), which are superfluid and flow with exactly zero viscosity, and a proportion of helium atoms in an excited state, which behave more like an ordinary fluid.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-101)

In the _fountain effect_, a chamber is constructed which is connected to a reservoir of helium II by a [sintered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sintering "Sintering") disc through which superfluid helium leaks easily but through which non-superfluid helium cannot pass. If the interior of the container is heated, the superfluid helium changes to non-superfluid helium. In order to maintain the equilibrium fraction of superfluid helium, superfluid helium leaks through and increases the pressure, causing liquid to fountain out of the container.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-102)

The thermal conductivity of helium II is greater than that of any other known substance, a million times that of helium I and several hundred times that of [copper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper "Copper").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-enc-30) This is because heat conduction occurs by an exceptional quantum mechanism. Most materials that conduct heat well have a [valence band](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valence_band "Valence band") of free electrons which serve to transfer the heat. Helium II has no such valence band but nevertheless conducts heat well. The [flow of heat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_transfer "Heat transfer") is governed by equations that are similar to the [wave equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_equation "Wave equation") used to characterize sound propagation in air. When heat is introduced, it moves at 20 meters per second at 1.8 K through helium II as waves in a phenomenon known as _[second sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second\_sound "Second sound")_.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-enc-30)

Helium II also exhibits a creeping effect. When a surface extends past the level of helium II, the helium II moves along the surface, against the force of [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity"). Helium II will escape from a vessel that is not sealed by creeping along the sides until it reaches a warmer region where it evaporates. It moves in a 30 nm-thick film regardless of surface material. This film is called a [Rollin film](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rollin_film "Rollin film") and is named after the man who first characterized this trait, [Bernard V. Rollin](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bernard_V._Rollin&action=edit&redlink=1 "Bernard V. Rollin (page does not exist)").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-enc-30)[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-103)[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-104) As a result of this creeping behavior and helium II's ability to leak rapidly through tiny openings, it is very difficult to confine. Unless the container is carefully constructed, the helium II will creep along the surfaces and through valves until it reaches somewhere warmer, where it will evaporate. Waves propagating across a Rollin film are governed by the same equation as [gravity waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity_wave "Gravity wave") in shallow water, but rather than gravity, the restoring force is the [van der Waals force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_der_Waals_force "Van der Waals force").[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-105) These waves are known as _[third sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Third\_sound "Third sound")_.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-106)

### Solid phases

Helium remains liquid down to [absolute zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_zero "Absolute zero") at atmospheric pressure, but it freezes at high pressure. Solid helium requires a temperature of 1–1.5 K (about −272°C or −457°F) at about 25 bar (2.5 MPa) of pressure.[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-107) It is often hard to distinguish solid from liquid helium since the [refractive index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refractive_index "Refractive index") of the two phases are nearly the same. The solid has a sharp [melting point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melting_point "Melting point") and has a [crystalline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal "Crystal") structure, but it is highly [compressible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compressibility "Compressibility"); applying pressure in a laboratory can decrease its volume by more than 30%.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-LANL.gov-108) With a [bulk modulus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bulk_modulus "Bulk modulus") of about 27 [MPa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megapascal "Megapascal")[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-109) it is ~100 times more compressible than water. Solid helium has a density of 0.214±0.006 g/cm 3 at 1.15 K and 66 atm; the projected density at 0 K and 25 bar (2.5 MPa) is 0.187±0.009 g/cm 3.[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-110) At higher temperatures, helium will solidify with sufficient pressure. At room temperature, this requires about 114,000 atm.[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Pinceaux1979-111)

Helium-4 and helium-3 both form several crystalline solid phases, all requiring at least 25 bar. They both form an α phase, which has a [hexagonal close-packed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexagonal_crystal_family#Hexagonal_close_packed "Hexagonal crystal family") (hcp) crystal structure, a β phase, which is [face-centered cubic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system "Cubic crystal system") (fcc), and a γ phase, which is [body-centered cubic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system "Cubic crystal system") (bcc).[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Keller_1969-112)

### Isotopes

There are nine known [isotopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope "Isotope") of helium of which two, [helium-3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium-3 "Helium-3") and [helium-4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium-4 "Helium-4"), are [stable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stable_isotope "Stable isotope"). In the Earth's atmosphere, one atom is 3

He for every million that are 4

He.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-nbb-28) Unlike most elements, helium's isotopic abundance varies greatly by origin, due to the different formation processes. The most common isotope, helium-4, is produced on Earth by [alpha decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_decay "Alpha decay") of heavier radioactive elements; the alpha particles that emerge are fully ionized helium-4 nuclei. Helium-4 is an unusually stable nucleus because its [nucleons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleon "Nucleon") are arranged into [complete shells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_shell_model "Nuclear shell model"). It was also formed in enormous quantities during [Big Bang nucleosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_nucleosynthesis "Big Bang nucleosynthesis").[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-bigbang-113)

Helium-3 is present on Earth only in trace amounts. Most of it has been present since Earth's formation, though some falls to Earth trapped in [cosmic dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_dust "Cosmic dust").[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-heliumfundamentals-114) Trace amounts are also produced by the [beta decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_decay "Beta decay") of [tritium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tritium "Tritium").[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-115) Rocks from the Earth's crust have isotope ratios varying by as much as a factor of ten, and these ratios can be used to investigate the origin of rocks and the composition of the Earth's [mantle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mantle_(geology) "Mantle (geology)").[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-heliumfundamentals-114)3

He is much more abundant in stars as a product of nuclear fusion. Thus in the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium"), the proportion of 3

He to 4

He is about 100 times higher than on Earth.[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-116) Extraplanetary material, such as [lunar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon") and [asteroid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid "Asteroid")[regolith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regolith "Regolith"), have trace amounts of helium-3 from being bombarded by [solar winds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind "Solar wind"). The [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon")'s surface contains helium-3 at concentrations on the order of 10 [ppb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts_per_billion "Parts per billion"), much higher than the approximately 5 [ppt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts_per_trillion "Parts per trillion") found in the Earth's atmosphere.[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-117)[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-118) A number of people, starting with Gerald Kulcinski in 1986,[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-119) have proposed to explore the Moon, mine lunar regolith, and use the helium-3 for [fusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_fusion "Nuclear fusion").

Liquid helium-4 can be cooled to about 1 K (−272.15°C; −457.87°F) using [evaporative cooling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evaporative_cooling "Evaporative cooling") in a [1-K pot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1-K_pot "1-K pot"). Similar cooling of helium-3, which has a lower boiling point, can achieve about 0.2 kelvin in a [helium-3 refrigerator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium-3#Cryogenics "Helium-3"). Equal mixtures of liquid 3

He and 4

He below 0.8 K separate into two immiscible phases due to their dissimilarity (they follow different [quantum statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_statistics "Quantum statistics"): helium-4 atoms are [bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boson "Boson") while helium-3 atoms are [fermions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermion "Fermion")).[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-enc-30)[Dilution refrigerators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dilution_refrigerator "Dilution refrigerator") use this immiscibility to achieve temperatures of a few millikelvins.[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-120)

It is possible to produce [exotic helium isotopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exotic_helium_isotopes "Exotic helium isotopes"), which rapidly decay into other substances. The shortest-lived heavy helium isotope is the [unbound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_drip_line "Nuclear drip line") helium-10 with a [half-life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Half-life "Half-life") of 2.6(4)×10−22 s.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-NUBASE2020-6) Helium-6 decays by emitting a [beta particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_particle "Beta particle") and has a half-life of 0.8 second. Helium-7 and helium-8 are created in certain [nuclear reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_reaction "Nuclear reaction").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-enc-30) Helium-6 and helium-8 are known to exhibit a [nuclear halo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_halo "Nuclear halo").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-enc-30)

Helium has a [valence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valence_(chemistry) "Valence (chemistry)") of zero and is chemically unreactive under all normal conditions.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-LANL.gov-108) It is an electrical insulator unless [ionized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion "Ion"). As with the other noble gases, helium has metastable [energy levels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_level "Energy level") that allow it to remain ionized in an electrical discharge with a [voltage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voltage "Voltage") below its [ionization potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionization_potential "Ionization potential").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-enc-30) Helium can form unstable [compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_(chemistry) "Compound (chemistry)"), known as [excimers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excimer "Excimer"), with tungsten, iodine, fluorine, sulfur, and phosphorus when it is subjected to a [glow discharge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glow_discharge "Glow discharge"), to electron bombardment, or reduced to [plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_physics "Plasma physics") by other means. The molecular compounds HeNe, HgHe 10, and WHe 2, and the molecular ions He+

2, He 2+

2, [HeH+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion "Helium hydride ion"), and HeD+

 have been created this way.[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-123) HeH+ is also stable in its ground state but is extremely reactive—it is the strongest [Brønsted acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Br%C3%B8nsted%E2%80%93Lowry_acid%E2%80%93base_theory "Brønsted–Lowry acid–base theory") known, and therefore can exist only in isolation, as it will protonate any molecule or counteranion it contacts. This technique has also produced the neutral molecule He 2, which has a large number of [band systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectral_band "Spectral band"), and HgHe, which is apparently held together only by polarization forces.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-enc-30)

[Van der Waals compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_der_Waals_compound "Van der Waals compound") of helium can also be formed with cryogenic helium gas and atoms of some other substance, such as [LiHe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LiHe "LiHe") and [He 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihelium "Dihelium").[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-fr13-124)

Theoretically, other true compounds may be possible, such as helium fluorohydride (HHeF), which would be analogous to [HArF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argon_fluorohydride "Argon fluorohydride"), discovered in 2000.[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-125) Calculations show that two new compounds containing a helium-oxygen bond could be stable.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-126) Two new molecular species, predicted using theory, CsFHeO and N(CH 3)4 FHeO, are derivatives of a metastable FHeO− anion first theorized in 2005 by a group from Taiwan.[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-127)

Helium atoms have been inserted into the hollow carbon cage molecules (the [fullerenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fullerene "Fullerene")) by heating under high pressure. The [endohedral fullerene molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endohedral_fullerene "Endohedral fullerene") formed are stable at high temperatures. When chemical derivatives of these fullerenes are formed, the helium stays inside.[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-128) If [helium-3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium-3 "Helium-3") is used, it can be readily observed by helium [nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_magnetic_resonance_spectroscopy "Nuclear magnetic resonance spectroscopy").[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-129) Many fullerenes containing helium-3 have been reported. Although the helium atoms are not attached by covalent or ionic bonds, these substances have distinct properties and a definite composition, like all stoichiometric chemical compounds.

Under high pressures helium can form compounds with various other elements. Helium-nitrogen [clathrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clathrate "Clathrate") (He(N 2)11) crystals have been grown at room temperature at pressures ca. 10 GPa in a [diamond anvil cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diamond_anvil_cell "Diamond anvil cell").[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-130) The [insulating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insulator_(electricity) "Insulator (electricity)")[electride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electride "Electride")[Na 2 He](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disodium_helide "Disodium helide") has been shown to be thermodynamically stable at pressures above 113 GPa. It has a [fluorite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorite "Fluorite") structure.[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-DongOganov2017-131)

Occurrence and production
-------------------------

### Natural abundance

Although it is rare on Earth, helium is the second most abundant element in the known Universe, constituting 23% of its [baryonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon "Baryon") mass. Only hydrogen is more abundant.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-nbb-28) The vast majority of helium was formed by [Big Bang nucleosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_nucleosynthesis "Big Bang nucleosynthesis") one to three minutes after the Big Bang. As such, measurements of its abundance contribute to cosmological models. In [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star"), it is formed by the [nuclear fusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_fusion "Nuclear fusion") of hydrogen in [proton–proton chain reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton%E2%80%93proton_chain "Proton–proton chain") and the [CNO cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CNO_cycle "CNO cycle"), part of [stellar nucleosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_nucleosynthesis "Stellar nucleosynthesis").[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-bigbang-113)

In the [Earth's atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere "Earth's atmosphere"), the concentration of helium by volume is only 5.2 parts per million.[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-132)[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-133) The concentration is low and fairly constant despite the continuous production of new helium because most helium in the Earth's atmosphere [escapes into space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_escape "Atmospheric escape") by several processes.[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-134)[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-135)[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-TalkOriginsCreationism-136) In the Earth's [heterosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heterosphere "Heterosphere"), a part of the upper atmosphere, helium and hydrogen are the most abundant elements.

Most helium on Earth is a result of [radioactive decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioactive_decay "Radioactive decay"). Helium is found in large amounts in minerals of [uranium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranium "Uranium") and [thorium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thorium "Thorium"), including [uraninite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uraninite "Uraninite") and its varieties [cleveite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cleveite "Cleveite") and [pitchblende](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pitchblende "Pitchblende"),[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-mindat_cleveite-20)[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-mindat_pitchblende-137)[carnotite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carnotite "Carnotite") and [monazite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monazite "Monazite") (a group name; "monazite" usually refers to [monazite-(Ce)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monazite-(Ce) "Monazite-(Ce)")),[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-mindat_monazite-138)[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-mindat_monaziteCe-139) because they emit alpha particles (helium nuclei, He 2+) to which electrons immediately combine as soon as the particle is stopped by the rock. In this way an estimated 3000 metric tons of helium are generated per year throughout the [lithosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithosphere "Lithosphere").[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-cook-140)[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-141)[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-142) In the Earth's crust, the concentration of helium is 8 parts per billion. In seawater, the concentration is only 4 parts per trillion. There are also small amounts in mineral [springs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spring_(hydrosphere) "Spring (hydrosphere)"), volcanic gas, and [meteoric iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteoric_iron "Meteoric iron"). Because helium is trapped in the subsurface under conditions that also trap natural gas, the greatest natural concentrations of helium on the planet are found in natural gas, from which most commercial helium is extracted. The concentration varies in a broad range from a few ppm to more than 7% in a small gas field in [San Juan County, New Mexico](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/San_Juan_County,_New_Mexico "San Juan County, New Mexico").[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-143)[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-144)

As of 2021, the world's helium reserves were estimated at 31 billion cubic meters, with a third of that being in [Qatar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qatar "Qatar").[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-145) In 2015 and 2016 additional probable reserves were announced to be under the Rocky Mountains in North America[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-146) and in the [East African Rift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_African_Rift "East African Rift").[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Sample-26)

The [Bureau of Land Management](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bureau_of_Land_Management "Bureau of Land Management") (BLM) has proposed an October 2024 plan for managing natural resources in western Colorado. The plan involves closing 543,000 acres to oil and gas leasing while keeping 692,300 acres open. Among the open areas, 165,700 acres have been identified as suitable for helium recovery. The United States possesses an estimated 306 billion cubic feet of recoverable helium, sufficient to meet current consumption rates of 2.15 billion cubic feet per year for approximately 150 years.[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-147)

### Modern extraction and distribution

Extracting helium from air is not economical.[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-148) For large-scale use, helium is extracted by [fractional distillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractional_distillation "Fractional distillation") from natural gas, which can contain as much as 7% helium.[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-149) Since helium has a lower [boiling point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boiling_point "Boiling point") than any other element, low temperatures and high pressure are used to liquefy nearly all the other gases (mostly [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") and [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane")). The resulting crude helium gas is purified by successive exposures to lowering temperatures, in which almost all of the remaining nitrogen and other gases are precipitated out of the gaseous mixture. [Activated charcoal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Activated_charcoal "Activated charcoal") is used as a final purification step, usually resulting in 99.995% pure Grade-A helium.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-enc-30) The principal impurity in Grade-A helium is [neon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neon "Neon"). In a final production step, most of the helium that is produced is liquefied via a [cryogenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryogenic "Cryogenic") process. This is necessary for applications requiring liquid helium and also allows helium suppliers to reduce the cost of long-distance transportation, as the largest liquid helium containers have more than five times the capacity of the largest gaseous helium tube trailers.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-wwsupply-81)[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-150)

In 2008, approximately 169 million [standard cubic meters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_cubic_meter "Standard cubic meter") (SCM) of helium were extracted from natural gas or withdrawn from helium reserves, with approximately 78% from the United States, 10% from Algeria, and most of the remainder from Russia, Poland, and Qatar.[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-151) By 2013, increases in helium production in Qatar (under the company [Qatargas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qatargas "Qatargas") managed by [Air Liquide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_Liquide "Air Liquide")) had increased Qatar's fraction of world helium production to 25%, making it the second largest exporter after the United States.[[151]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-bloomberg.com-152) An estimated 54 billion cubic feet (1.5×10 9 m 3) deposit of helium was found in Tanzania in 2016.[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-153) A large-scale helium plant was opened in [Ningxia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ningxia "Ningxia"), [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China "China") in 2020.[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-154)

In the United States, most helium is extracted from the natural gas of the [Hugoton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hugoton_Natural_Gas_Area "Hugoton Natural Gas Area") and nearby gas fields in Kansas, Oklahoma, and the Panhandle Field in Texas.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-wwsupply-81)[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-155) Much of this gas was once sent by pipeline to the [National Helium Reserve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Helium_Reserve "National Helium Reserve"), but since 2005, this reserve has been depleted and sold off, and it is expected to be largely depleted by 2021[[151]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-bloomberg.com-152) under the October 2013 _Responsible Helium Administration and Stewardship Act_ (H.R. 527).[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-156) The helium fields of the western United States are emerging as an alternate source of helium supply, particularly those of the "[Four Corners](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four_Corners "Four Corners")" region (the states of Arizona, Colorado, New Mexico and Utah).[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-157)

Diffusion of crude natural gas through special [semipermeable membranes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semipermeable_membrane "Semipermeable membrane") and other barriers is another method to recover and purify helium.[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-158) In 1996, the U.S. had _proven_ helium reserves in such gas well complexes of about 147 billion [standard cubic feet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_cubic_feet "Standard cubic feet") (4.2 billion SCM).[[158]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-159) At rates of use at that time (72 million SCM per year in the U.S.; see pie chart below) this would have been enough helium for about 58 years of U.S. use, and less than this (perhaps 80% of the time) at world use rates, although factors in saving and processing impact effective reserve numbers.

Helium is commercially available in either liquid or gaseous form. As a liquid, it can be supplied in small insulated containers called [dewars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dewar_flask "Dewar flask") which hold as much as 1,000 liters of helium, or in large ISO containers, which have nominal capacities as large as 42 m 3 (around 11,000 U.S. [gallons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gallon "Gallon")). In gaseous form, small quantities of helium are supplied in high-pressure cylinders holding as much as 8 m 3 (approximately . 282 standard cubic feet), while large quantities of high-pressure gas are supplied in tube trailers, which have capacities of as much as 4,860 m 3 (approx. 172,000 standard cubic feet).

### Conservation advocates

According to helium conservationists like Nobel laureate physicist [Robert Coleman Richardson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Coleman_Richardson "Robert Coleman Richardson"), writing in 2010, the free market price of helium has contributed to "wasteful" usage (e.g. for [helium balloons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tethered_helium_balloon "Tethered helium balloon")). Prices in the 2000s had been lowered by the decision of the U.S. Congress to sell off the country's large helium stockpile by 2015.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Connor-23) According to Richardson, the price needed to be multiplied by 20 to eliminate the excessive wasting of helium. In the 2012 Nuttall et al. paper titled "Stop squandering helium", it was also proposed to create an International Helium Agency that would build a sustainable market for "this precious commodity".[[159]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-160)

Applications
------------

While balloons are perhaps the best-known use of helium, they are a minor part of all helium use.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-stwertka-76) Helium is used for many purposes that require some of its unique properties, such as its low [boiling point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boiling_point "Boiling point"), low [density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density "Density"), low [solubility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solubility "Solubility"), high [thermal conductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_conductivity "Thermal conductivity"), or [inertness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemically_inert "Chemically inert"). Of the 2014 world helium total production of about 32 million kg (180 million standard cubic meters) helium per year, the largest use (about 32% of the total in 2014) is in cryogenic applications, most of which involves cooling the superconducting magnets in medical [MRI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MRI "MRI") scanners and [NMR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NMR "NMR") spectrometers.[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-162) Other major uses were pressurizing and purging systems, welding, maintenance of controlled atmospheres, and leak detection. Other uses by category were relatively minor fractions.[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-usgs-helium-161)

### Controlled atmospheres

Helium is used as a protective gas in growing [silicon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon "Silicon") and [germanium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germanium "Germanium") crystals, in [titanium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titanium "Titanium") and [zirconium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zirconium "Zirconium") production, and in [gas chromatography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_chromatography "Gas chromatography"),[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-LANL.gov-108) because it is inert. Because of its inertness, [thermally and calorically perfect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas "Ideal gas") nature, high [speed of sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_sound "Speed of sound"), and high value of the [heat capacity ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_capacity_ratio "Heat capacity ratio"), it is also useful in [supersonic wind tunnels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supersonic_wind_tunnel "Supersonic wind tunnel")[[162]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-163) and [impulse facilities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impulse_facility "Impulse facility").[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-164)

### Gas tungsten arc welding

Helium is used as a [shielding gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shielding_gas "Shielding gas") in [arc welding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_welding "Arc welding") processes on materials that are contaminated and weakened by air or nitrogen at welding temperatures.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-nbb-28) A number of inert shielding gases are used in gas tungsten arc welding, but helium is used instead of cheaper [argon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argon "Argon") especially for welding materials that have higher [heat conductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_conductivity "Heat conductivity"), like [aluminium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium "Aluminium") or [copper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper "Copper").

### Minor uses

#### Industrial leak detection

[![Image 18: Photo of a large, metal-framed device (about 3×1×1.5 m) standing in a room.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Ac-system_2.jpg/250px-Ac-system_2.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ac-system_2.jpg)

A dual chamber helium leak detection machine

One industrial application for helium is [leak detection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leak_detection "Leak detection"). Because helium [diffuses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffusion "Diffusion") through solids three times faster than air, it is used as a tracer gas to detect [leaks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leak "Leak") in high-vacuum equipment (such as cryogenic tanks) and high-pressure containers.[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-nostrand-165) The tested object is placed in a chamber, which is then evacuated and filled with helium. The helium that escapes through the leaks is detected by a sensitive device ([helium mass spectrometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_mass_spectrometer "Helium mass spectrometer")), even at the leak rates as small as 10−9 mbar·L/s (10−10 Pa·m 3/s). The measurement procedure is normally automatic and is called helium integral test. A simpler procedure is to fill the tested object with helium and to manually search for leaks with a hand-held device.[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-166)

Helium leaks through cracks should not be confused with gas permeation through a bulk material. While helium has documented permeation constants (thus a calculable permeation rate) through glasses, ceramics, and synthetic materials, inert gases such as helium will not permeate most bulk metals.[[166]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-167)

#### Flight

[![Image 19: The Good Year Blimp](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Goodyear-blimp.jpg/250px-Goodyear-blimp.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Goodyear-blimp.jpg)

Because of its low density and incombustibility, helium is the gas of choice to fill airships such as the [Goodyear blimp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goodyear_blimp "Goodyear blimp").

Because it is [lighter than air](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lighter_than_air "Lighter than air"), [airships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airship "Airship") and balloons are inflated with helium for [lift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lifting_gas "Lifting gas"). While hydrogen gas is more buoyant and escapes permeating through a membrane at a lower rate, helium has the advantage of being non-flammable, and indeed [fire-retardant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fire-retardant "Fire-retardant"). Another minor use is in [rocketry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket "Rocket"), where helium is used as an [ullage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ullage "Ullage") medium to backfill rocket propellant tanks in flight and to condense hydrogen and oxygen to make [rocket fuel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket_fuel "Rocket fuel"). It is also used to purge fuel and oxidizer from ground support equipment prior to launch and to pre-cool liquid hydrogen in [space vehicles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_vehicle "Space vehicle"). For example, the [Saturn V](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn_V "Saturn V") rocket used in the [Apollo program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo_program "Apollo program") needed about 370,000 cubic metres (13 million cubic feet) of helium to launch.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-LANL.gov-108)

#### Minor commercial and recreational uses

Helium as a breathing gas has no [narcotic properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_narcosis "Nitrogen narcosis"), so helium mixtures such as [trimix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trimix_(breathing_gas) "Trimix (breathing gas)"), [heliox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliox "Heliox") and [heliair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trimix_(breathing_gas)#Heliair "Trimix (breathing gas)") are used for [deep diving](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_diving "Deep diving") to reduce the effects of narcosis, which worsen with increasing depth.[[167]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-168)[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-thomas-169) As pressure increases with depth, the density of the breathing gas also increases, and the low molecular weight of helium is found to considerably reduce the effort of breathing by lowering the density of the mixture. This reduces the [Reynolds number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reynolds_number "Reynolds number") of flow, leading to a reduction of [turbulent flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turbulent_flow "Turbulent flow") and an increase in [laminar flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laminar_flow "Laminar flow"), which requires less breathing.[[169]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-170)[[170]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-BOCheox21-171) At depths below 150 metres (490 ft) divers breathing helium-oxygen mixtures begin to experience tremors and a decrease in psychomotor function, symptoms of [high-pressure nervous syndrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-pressure_nervous_syndrome "High-pressure nervous syndrome").[[171]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-HungerBennett-172) This effect may be countered to some extent by adding an amount of narcotic gas such as hydrogen or nitrogen to a helium–oxygen mixture.[[172]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-173)

[Helium–neon lasers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium%E2%80%93neon_laser "Helium–neon laser"), a type of low-powered gas laser producing a red beam, had various practical applications which included [barcode readers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barcode_reader "Barcode reader") and [laser pointers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser_pointer "Laser pointer"), before they were almost universally replaced by cheaper [diode lasers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diode_laser "Diode laser").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-nbb-28)

For its inertness and high [thermal conductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_conductivity "Thermal conductivity"), neutron transparency, and because it does not form radioactive isotopes under reactor conditions, helium is used as a heat-transfer medium in some [gas-cooled nuclear reactors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas-cooled_reactor "Gas-cooled reactor").[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-nostrand-165)

Helium, mixed with a heavier gas such as xenon, is useful for [thermoacoustic refrigeration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermoacoustic_refrigeration "Thermoacoustic refrigeration") due to the resulting high [heat capacity ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_capacity_ratio "Heat capacity ratio") and low [Prandtl number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prandtl_number "Prandtl number").[[173]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-174) The inertness of helium has environmental advantages over conventional refrigeration systems which contribute to ozone depletion or global warming.[[174]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-175)

Helium is also used in some [hard disk drives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hard_disk_drive "Hard disk drive").[[175]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-176)

#### Scientific uses

The use of helium reduces the distorting effects of temperature variations in the space between [lenses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lens_(optics) "Lens (optics)") in some [telescopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telescope "Telescope") due to its extremely low [index of refraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Index_of_refraction "Index of refraction").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-enc-30) This method is especially used in solar telescopes where a vacuum tight telescope tube would be too heavy.[[176]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-177)[[177]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-178)

Helium is a commonly used carrier gas for [gas chromatography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_chromatography "Gas chromatography").

The age of rocks and minerals that contain uranium and thorium can be estimated by measuring the level of helium with a process known as [helium dating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_dating "Helium dating").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-nbb-28)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-enc-30)

Helium at low temperatures is used in [cryogenics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryogenics "Cryogenics") and in certain cryogenic applications. As examples of applications, liquid helium is used to cool certain metals to the extremely low temperatures required for [superconductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superconductivity "Superconductivity"), such as in [superconducting magnets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superconducting_magnet "Superconducting magnet") for [magnetic resonance imaging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_resonance_imaging "Magnetic resonance imaging"). The [Large Hadron Collider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_Hadron_Collider "Large Hadron Collider") at [CERN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CERN "CERN") uses 96 [metric tons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_ton "Metric ton") of liquid helium to maintain the temperature at 1.9 K (−271.25°C; −456.25°F).[[178]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-CERN-LHC-179)

#### Medical uses

Helium was approved for medical use in the United States in April 2020 for humans and animals.[[179]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-180)[[180]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-181)

As a contaminant
----------------

While chemically inert, helium contamination impairs the operation of [microelectromechanical systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microelectromechanical_systems "Microelectromechanical systems") (MEMS) such that iPhones may fail.[[181]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-182)

Inhalation and safety
---------------------

### Effects

Neutral helium at standard conditions is non-toxic, plays no biological role and is found in trace amounts in human blood.

The effect of helium on a human voice

The [speed of sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_sound "Speed of sound") in helium is nearly three times the speed of sound in air. Because the [natural resonance frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_frequency "Fundamental frequency") of a gas-filled cavity is proportional to the speed of sound in the gas, when helium is inhaled, a corresponding increase occurs in the [resonant frequencies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resonant_frequency "Resonant frequency") of the [vocal tract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vocal_tract "Vocal tract"), which is the amplifier of vocal sound.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-nbb-28)[[182]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-183) This increase in the resonant frequency of the amplifier (the vocal tract) gives increased amplification to the high-frequency components of the sound wave produced by the direct vibration of the vocal folds, compared to the case when the voice box is filled with air. When a person speaks after inhaling helium gas, the muscles that control the voice box still move in the same way as when the voice box is filled with air; therefore the [fundamental frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_frequency "Fundamental frequency") (sometimes called [pitch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pitch_(music) "Pitch (music)")) produced by direct vibration of the vocal folds does not change.[[183]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-184) However, the high-frequency-preferred amplification causes a change in [timbre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Timbre "Timbre") of the amplified sound, resulting in a reedy, duck-like vocal quality. The opposite effect, lowering resonant frequencies, can be obtained by inhaling a dense gas such as [sulfur hexafluoride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_hexafluoride "Sulfur hexafluoride") or [xenon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xenon "Xenon").

### Hazards

Inhaling helium can be dangerous if done to excess, since helium is a simple [asphyxiant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asphyxiant_gas "Asphyxiant gas") and so displaces oxygen needed for normal respiration.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-nbb-28)[[184]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Grass-185) Fatalities have been recorded, including a youth who suffocated in Vancouver in 2003 and two adults who suffocated in South Florida in 2006.[[185]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-sptimes.com-186)[[186]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-cbc.ca-187) In 1998, an Australian girl from Victoria fell unconscious and temporarily [turned blue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanosis "Cyanosis") after inhaling the entire contents of a party balloon.[[187]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-balloonartists.com.au-188)[[188]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-lousballoons.com.au-189)[[189]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-bouncetime.co.uk-190) Inhaling helium directly from pressurized cylinders or even balloon filling valves is extremely dangerous, as high flow rate and pressure can result in [barotrauma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barotrauma "Barotrauma"), fatally rupturing lung tissue.[[184]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-Grass-185)[[190]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-slate-191)

Death caused by helium is rare. The first media-recorded case was that of a 15-year-old girl from Texas who died in 1998 from helium inhalation at a friend's party; the exact type of helium death is unidentified.[[187]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-balloonartists.com.au-188)[[188]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-lousballoons.com.au-189)[[189]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-bouncetime.co.uk-190)

In the United States, only two fatalities were reported between 2000 and 2004, including a man who died in North Carolina of barotrauma in 2002.[[185]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-sptimes.com-186)[[190]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-slate-191) A youth asphyxiated in Vancouver during 2003, and a 27-year-old man in Australia had an embolism after breathing from a cylinder in 2000.[[185]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-sptimes.com-186) Since then, two adults asphyxiated in South Florida in 2006,[[185]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-sptimes.com-186)[[186]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-cbc.ca-187)[[191]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-192) and there were cases in 2009 and 2010, one of whom was a Californian youth who was found with a bag over his head, attached to a helium tank,[[192]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-193) and another teenager in Northern Ireland died of asphyxiation.[[193]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-194) At [Eagle Point, Oregon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eagle_Point,_Oregon "Eagle Point, Oregon") a teenage girl died in 2012 from barotrauma at a party.[[194]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-195)[[195]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-huffingtonpost.com-196)[[196]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-today.com-197) A girl from Michigan died from hypoxia later in the year.[[197]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-198)

On February 4, 2015, it was revealed that, during the recording of their main TV show on January 28, a 12-year-old member (name withheld) of Japanese all-girl singing group [3B Junior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3B_Junior "3B Junior") suffered from [air embolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_embolism "Air embolism"), losing consciousness and falling into a [coma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coma "Coma") as a result of air bubbles blocking the flow of blood to the brain after inhaling huge quantities of helium as part of a game. The incident was not made public until a week later.[[198]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-199)[[199]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-200) The staff of [TV Asahi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TV_Asahi "TV Asahi") held an emergency press conference to communicate that the member had been taken to the hospital and is showing signs of rehabilitation such as moving eyes and limbs, but her consciousness has not yet been sufficiently recovered. Police have launched an investigation due to a neglect of safety measures.[[200]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-201)[[201]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-202)

The safety issues for cryogenic helium are similar to those of [liquid nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_nitrogen "Liquid nitrogen"); its extremely low temperatures can result in [cold burns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frostbite "Frostbite"), and the liquid-to-gas expansion ratio can cause explosions if no pressure-relief devices are installed. Containers of helium gas at 5 to 10 K should be handled as if they contain liquid helium due to the rapid and significant [thermal expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_expansion "Thermal expansion") that occurs when helium gas at less than 10 K is warmed to [room temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Room_temperature "Room temperature").[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-LANL.gov-108)

At high pressures (more than about 20 atm or two[MPa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MPa "MPa")), a mixture of helium and oxygen ([heliox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliox "Heliox")) can lead to [high-pressure nervous syndrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-pressure_nervous_syndrome "High-pressure nervous syndrome"), a sort of reverse-anesthetic effect; adding a small amount of nitrogen to the mixture can alleviate the problem.[[202]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-203)[[171]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-HungerBennett-172)

1.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_ref-14 "Jump up")**A few authors dispute the placement of helium in the noble gas column, preferring to place it above [beryllium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beryllium "Beryllium") with the [alkaline earth metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkaline_earth_metal "Alkaline earth metal"). They do so on the grounds of helium's 1s 2 electron configuration, which is analogous to the ns 2 valence configurations of the alkaline earth metals, and furthermore point to some specific trends that are more regular if helium is placed in group 2.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-11) These tend to relate to [kainosymmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kainosymmetry "Kainosymmetry") and the first-row anomaly: the first orbital of any type is unusually small, since unlike its higher analogues, it does not experience interelectronic repulsion from a smaller orbital of the same type. Because of this trend in the sizes of orbitals, a large difference in atomic radii between the first and second members of each main group is seen in groups 1 and 13–17: it exists between neon and argon, and between helium and beryllium, but not between helium and neon. This similarly affects the noble gases' boiling points and solubilities in water, where helium is too close to neon, and the large difference characteristic between the first two elements of a group appears only between neon and argon. Moving helium to group 2 makes this trend consistent in groups 2 and 18 as well, by making helium the first group 2 element and neon the first group 18 element: both exhibit the characteristic properties of a kainosymmetric first element of a group.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-12) However, the classification of helium with the other noble gases remains near-universal, as its extraordinary inertness is extremely close to that of the other light noble gases neon and argon.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium#cite_note-13)
