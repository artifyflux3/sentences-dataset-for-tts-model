For the geographical antipodal point of a place on Earth, see [antipodes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antipodes "Antipodes").

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Great_circle%2C_axis%2C_and_poles.svg/330px-Great_circle%2C_axis%2C_and_poles.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Great_circle,_axis,_and_poles.svg)

The two points P and _P_' (red) are _antipodal_ because they are ends of a diameter _PP_', a segment of the _axis_ a (purple) passing through the sphere's center O (black). P and _P_' are the _poles_ of a great circle g (green) whose points are equidistant from each (with a central right angle). Any great circle s (blue) passing through the poles is _secondary_ to g.

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), two points of a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere") (or [n-sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-sphere "N-sphere"), including a [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle")) are called **antipodal** or **diametrically opposite** if they are the endpoints of a [diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diameter "Diameter"), a straight [line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment") between two points on a sphere and passing through its [center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_(geometry) "Center (geometry)").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antipodal_point#cite_note-EB1911-1)

Given any point on a sphere, its antipodal point is the unique point at greatest [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance "Distance"), whether measured intrinsically ([great-circle distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great-circle_distance "Great-circle distance") on the surface of the sphere) or extrinsically ([chordal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chord_(geometry) "Chord (geometry)") distance through the sphere's interior). Every [great circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_circle "Great circle") on a sphere passing through a point also passes through its antipodal point, and there are infinitely many great circles passing through a pair of antipodal points (unlike the situation for any non-antipodal pair of points, which have a unique great circle passing through both). Many results in spherical geometry depend on choosing non-antipodal points, and [degenerate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degeneracy_(mathematics) "Degeneracy (mathematics)") if antipodal points are allowed; for example, a [spherical triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_triangle "Spherical triangle") degenerates to an underspecified [lune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_lune "Spherical lune") if two of the vertices are antipodal.

The point antipodal to a given point is called its **antipodes**, from the [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek "Ancient Greek")ἀντίποδες (_antípodes_) meaning "opposite feet"; see [Antipodes §Etymology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antipodes#Etymology "Antipodes"). Sometimes the _s_ is dropped, and this is rendered **antipode**, a [back-formation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Back-formation "Back-formation").

The concept of _antipodal points_ is generalized to [spheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere") of any dimension: two points on the sphere are antipodal if they are opposite _through the centre_. Each line through the centre intersects the sphere in two points, one for each [ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_(geometry) "Ray (geometry)") emanating from the centre, and these two points are antipodal.

The [Borsuk–Ulam theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borsuk%E2%80%93Ulam_theorem "Borsuk–Ulam theorem") is a result from [algebraic topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_topology "Algebraic topology") dealing with such pairs of points. It says that any [continuous function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function") from ![Image 2: {\displaystyle S^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee006452a59bf1eb29983b4412348b66517a2d23) to ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) maps some pair of antipodal points in ![Image 4: {\displaystyle S^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee006452a59bf1eb29983b4412348b66517a2d23) to the same point in ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/76ef548febfc9981762740107858be9e3a5576c3) Here, ![Image 6: {\displaystyle S^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee006452a59bf1eb29983b4412348b66517a2d23) denotes the ![Image 7: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-dimensional sphere and ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) is ![Image 9: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-dimensional[real coordinate space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_coordinate_space "Real coordinate space").

The **antipodal map**![Image 10: {\displaystyle A:S^{n}\to S^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0245aeae14149693e230f9310e1a7992415096ee) sends every point on the sphere to its antipodal point. If points on the ![Image 11: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-sphere are represented as [displacement vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_(geometry) "Position (geometry)") from the sphere's center in Euclidean ![Image 12: {\displaystyle (n+1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b30a29cfd35628469f9dbffea4804f5b422f3037)-space, then two antipodal points are represented by additive inverses ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6) and ![Image 14: {\displaystyle -\mathbf {v} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9ef5df8f53514e352d687e3c2c8e3a1c8b06a319) and the antipodal map can be defined as ![Image 15: {\displaystyle A(\mathbf {x} )=-\mathbf {x} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4c8a3cd3b39eb41f7db02e31029fa888c3ca7cef) The antipodal map preserves [orientation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientability "Orientability") (is [homotopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy "Homotopy") to the [identity map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_function "Identity function"))[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antipodal_point#cite_note-2) when ![Image 16: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) is odd, and reverses it when ![Image 17: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) is even. Its [degree](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_of_a_continuous_mapping "Degree of a continuous mapping") is ![Image 18: {\displaystyle (-1)^{n+1}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ab31697e172d6365ca6a71b1f622a4b96c8a7e0)

If antipodal points are identified (considered equivalent), the sphere becomes a model of [real projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_projective_space "Real projective space").
