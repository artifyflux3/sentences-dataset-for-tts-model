**Newton's laws of motion** are three [physical laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_laws "Physical laws") that describe the relationship between the [motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion "Motion") of an object and the [forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force") acting on it. These laws, which provide the basis for **Newtonian mechanics**, can be paraphrased as follows:

1.   A body remains at rest, or in motion at a constant speed in a straight line, unless it is acted upon by a force.
2.   At any instant of time, the net force on a body is equal to the body's [acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration") multiplied by its mass or, equivalently, the rate at which the body's [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") is changing with time.
3.   If two bodies exert forces on each other, these forces have the same magnitude but opposite directions.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Thornton-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Cohen&Whitman-2)

The three laws of motion were first stated by [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton") in his _[Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophi%C3%A6\_Naturalis\_Principia\_Mathematica "Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica")_ (_Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy_), originally published in 1687.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-3) Newton used them to investigate and explain the motion of many physical objects and systems. In the time since Newton, new insights, especially around the concept of energy, built the field of [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics") on his foundations. Limitations to Newton's laws have also been discovered; new theories are necessary when objects move at very high speeds ([special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity")), are very massive ([general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity")), or are very small ([quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics")).

Prerequisites
-------------

Newton's laws are often stated in terms of _point_ or _particle_ masses, that is, bodies whose volume is negligible. This is a reasonable approximation for real bodies when the motion of internal parts can be neglected, and when the separation between bodies is much larger than the size of each. For instance, the Earth and the Sun can both be approximated as pointlike when considering the orbit of the former around the latter, but the Earth is not pointlike when considering activities on its surface.[[note 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-6)

The mathematical description of motion, or [kinematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics "Kinematics"), is based on the idea of specifying positions using numerical coordinates. Movement is represented by these numbers changing over time: a body's trajectory is represented by a function that assigns to each value of a time variable the values of all the position coordinates. The simplest case is one-dimensional, that is, when a body is constrained to move only along a straight line. Its position can then be given by a single number, indicating where it is relative to some chosen reference point. For example, a body might be free to slide along a track that runs left to right, and so its location can be specified by its distance from a convenient zero point, or [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)"), with negative numbers indicating positions to the left and positive numbers indicating positions to the right. If the body's location as a function of time is ![Image 1: {\displaystyle s(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c484de351ba40ccb9a5ad522c29c1aac5686c0df), then its average velocity over the time interval from ![Image 2: {\displaystyle t_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02d3006c4190b1939b04d9b9bb21006fb4e6fa4a) to ![Image 3: {\displaystyle t_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb0768c0bd659f2f84fb5ef9f4b74f336123d915) is[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Hughes-Hallett-7)![Image 4: {\displaystyle {\frac {\Delta s}{\Delta t}}={\frac {s(t_{1})-s(t_{0})}{t_{1}-t_{0}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd4169dfba9f6e2b80a44a4810c33910e95150d)Here, the Greek letter ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \Delta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32769037c408874e1890f77554c65f39c523ebe2) ([delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delta_(letter) "Delta (letter)")) is used, per tradition, to mean "change in". A positive average velocity means that the position coordinate ![Image 6: {\displaystyle s}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01d131dfd7673938b947072a13a9744fe997e632) increases over the interval in question, a negative average velocity indicates a net decrease over that interval, and an average velocity of zero means that the body ends the time interval in the same place as it began. [Calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus") gives the means to define an _instantaneous_ velocity, a measure of a body's speed and direction of movement at a single moment of time, rather than over an interval. One notation for the instantaneous velocity is to replace ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \Delta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32769037c408874e1890f77554c65f39c523ebe2) with the symbol ![Image 8: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab), for example,![Image 9: {\displaystyle v={\frac {ds}{dt}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6f22a6ada5b3f3d8a9450c9b85fd2d85adf0be76)This denotes that the instantaneous velocity is the [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") of the position with respect to time. It can roughly be thought of as the ratio between an infinitesimally small change in position ![Image 10: {\displaystyle ds}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5f0fb36e4308227d3e4a1f809c2571ec02527100) to the infinitesimally small time interval ![Image 11: {\displaystyle dt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ebee76a835701fd1f26047a09855f2ea36bb08fc) over which it occurs.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Thompson-8) More carefully, the velocity and all other derivatives can be defined using the concept of a [limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_(mathematics) "Limit (mathematics)").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Hughes-Hallett-7) A function ![Image 12: {\displaystyle f(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5bf044fe2fbfc4bd8d6d7230f4108430263f9fd6) has a limit of ![Image 13: {\displaystyle L}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/103168b86f781fe6e9a4a87b8ea1cebe0ad4ede8) at a given input value ![Image 14: {\displaystyle t_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02d3006c4190b1939b04d9b9bb21006fb4e6fa4a) if the difference between ![Image 15: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) and ![Image 16: {\displaystyle L}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/103168b86f781fe6e9a4a87b8ea1cebe0ad4ede8) can be made arbitrarily small by choosing an input sufficiently close to ![Image 17: {\displaystyle t_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02d3006c4190b1939b04d9b9bb21006fb4e6fa4a). One writes, ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \lim _{t\to t_{0}}f(t)=L.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/643dacc66c1939696a39541ff8eb5219e3ab0103)Instantaneous velocity can be defined as the limit of the average velocity as the time interval shrinks to zero:![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\frac {ds}{dt}}=\lim _{\Delta t\to 0}{\frac {s(t+\Delta t)-s(t)}{\Delta t}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31fa3ca5d0c15e8454f6eed22ae4a62f8311d222)_Acceleration_ is to velocity as velocity is to position: it is the derivative of the velocity with respect to time.[[note 2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-10) Acceleration can likewise be defined as a limit:![Image 20: {\displaystyle a={\frac {dv}{dt}}=\lim _{\Delta t\to 0}{\frac {v(t+\Delta t)-v(t)}{\Delta t}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca268bf87ff7da2f042785bb0b2cb40a9b983237)Consequently, the acceleration is the _second derivative_ of position,[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Thompson-8) often written ![Image 21: {\displaystyle {\frac {d^{2}s}{dt^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d47c32d30bc8ea8a898d72cb33b68c05a5308cc4).

Position, when thought of as a displacement from an origin point, is a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(mathematics_and_physics) "Vector (mathematics and physics)"): a quantity with both magnitude and direction.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:1-11): 1  Velocity and acceleration are vector quantities as well. The mathematical tools of vector algebra provide the means to describe motion in two, three or more dimensions. Vectors are often denoted with an arrow, as in ![Image 22: {\displaystyle {\vec {s}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/57ce74ecbbc0e198d0bb4b1b2ffc0cb9669b72e1), or in bold typeface, such as ![Image 23: {\displaystyle {\bf {s}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43e910e1ee0ff1b7d304b2a02dd802d23aac1e1c). Often, vectors are represented visually as arrows, with the direction of the vector being the direction of the arrow, and the magnitude of the vector indicated by the length of the arrow. Numerically, a vector can be represented as a list; for example, a body's velocity vector might be ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} =(\mathrm {3~m/s} ,\mathrm {4~m/s} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6522522fcb88bd97f1d71fa8ef069d82e3c7ad4), indicating that it is moving at 3 metres per second along the horizontal axis and 4 metres per second along the vertical axis. The same motion described in a different [coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") will be represented by different numbers, and vector algebra can be used to translate between these alternatives.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:1-11): 4

The study of mechanics is complicated by the fact that household words like _energy_ are used with a technical meaning.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-12)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-13) Moreover, words which are synonymous in everyday speech are not so in physics: _force_ is not the same as _power_ or _pressure_, for example, and _mass_ has a different meaning than _weight_.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-14)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-openstax-15): 150  The physics concept of _force_ makes quantitative the everyday idea of a push or a pull. Forces in Newtonian mechanics are often due to strings and ropes, friction, muscle effort, gravity, and so forth. Like displacement, velocity, and acceleration, force is a vector quantity.

Laws
----

### First law

[![Image 25: see caption](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Skylab_and_Earth_Limb_-_GPN-2000-001055.jpg/250px-Skylab_and_Earth_Limb_-_GPN-2000-001055.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Skylab_and_Earth_Limb_-_GPN-2000-001055.jpg)

Artificial satellites move along curved [orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit "Orbit"), rather than in straight lines, because of the Earth's [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity").

Translated from Latin, Newton's first law reads,

_Every object perseveres in its state of rest, or of uniform motion in a right line, unless it is compelled to change that state by forces impressed thereon._[[note 3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-20)
Newton's first law expresses the principle of [inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertia "Inertia"): the natural behavior of a body is to move in a straight line at constant speed. A body's motion preserves the status quo, but external forces can perturb this.

The modern understanding of Newton's first law is that no [inertial observer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertial_observer "Inertial observer") is privileged over any other. The concept of an inertial observer makes quantitative the everyday idea of feeling no effects of motion. For example, a person standing on the ground watching a train go past is an inertial observer. If the observer on the ground sees the train moving smoothly in a straight line at a constant speed, then a passenger sitting on the train will also be an inertial observer: the train passenger _feels_ no motion. The principle expressed by Newton's first law is that there is no way to say which inertial observer is "really" moving and which is "really" standing still. One observer's state of rest is another observer's state of uniform motion in a straight line, and no experiment can deem either point of view to be correct or incorrect. There is no absolute standard of rest.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-21)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:0-17): 62–63 [[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:2-22): 7–9  Newton himself believed that [absolute space and time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_space_and_time "Absolute space and time") existed, but that the only measures of space or time accessible to experiment are relative.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-23)

### Second law

_The change of motion of an object is proportional to the force impressed; and is made in the direction of the straight line in which the force is impressed._[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:0-17): 114 
By "motion", Newton meant the quantity now called [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum"), which depends upon the amount of matter contained in a body, the speed at which that body is moving, and the direction in which it is moving.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-24) In modern notation, the momentum of a body is the product of its mass and its velocity: ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} =m\mathbf {v} \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea4c38d9148891f419dc63b8de193b40e01cbda2) where all three quantities can change over time. In common cases the mass ![Image 27: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) does not change with time and the derivative acts only upon the velocity. Then force equals the product of the mass and the time derivative of the velocity, which is the acceleration:[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-25)![Image 28: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =m{\frac {d\mathbf {v} }{dt}}=m\mathbf {a} \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45cf8f928f00376240c455bf40fa783b8980f351) As the acceleration is the second derivative of position with respect to time, this can also be written ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =m{\frac {d^{2}\mathbf {s} }{dt^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7204eb97b64aea4139eb902efa1f6dacd91a1a19)

Newton's second law, in modern form, states that the time derivative of the momentum is the force:[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Kleppner-26): 4.1 ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} ={\frac {d\mathbf {p} }{dt}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99ecbac6df650ec669de4173a064ab3824920644) When applied to [systems of variable mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_mass_system "Variable mass system"), the equation above is only valid only for a fixed set of particles. Applying the derivative as in ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =m{\frac {\mathrm {d} \mathbf {v} }{\mathrm {d} t}}+\mathbf {v} {\frac {\mathrm {d} m}{\mathrm {d} t}}\ \ \mathrm {(incorrect)} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa3e96397569d067273985de479f943fdd7234b0) can lead to incorrect results.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Plastino-1992-27) For example, the momentum of a water jet system must include the momentum of the ejected water:[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-28)![Image 32: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} _{\mathrm {ext} }={\mathrm {d} \mathbf {p}  \over \mathrm {d} t}-\mathbf {v} _{\mathrm {eject} }{\frac {\mathrm {d} m}{\mathrm {d} t}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5bb28ec56b912e031b8828f26fb5617da2128b89)

[![Image 33](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d3/Free_body1.3.svg/250px-Free_body1.3.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Free_body1.3.svg)

A [free body diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_body_diagram "Free body diagram") for a block on an inclined plane, illustrating the [normal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_force "Normal force") perpendicular to the plane (_N_), the downward force of gravity (_mg_), and a force _f_ along the direction of the plane that could be applied, for example, by friction or a string

The forces acting on a body [add as vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector#Addition_and_subtraction "Euclidean vector"), and so the total force on a body depends upon both the magnitudes and the directions of the individual forces.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Kleppner-26): 58  When the net force on a body is equal to zero, then by Newton's second law, the body does not accelerate, and it is said to be in [mechanical equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_equilibrium "Mechanical equilibrium"). A state of mechanical equilibrium is _stable_ if, when the position of the body is changed slightly, the body remains near that equilibrium. Otherwise, the equilibrium is _unstable._[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:0-17): 121 [[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Kleppner-26): 174

A common visual representation of forces acting in concert is the [free body diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_body_diagram "Free body diagram"), which schematically portrays a body of interest and the forces applied to it by outside influences.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-29) For example, a free body diagram of a block sitting upon an [inclined plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inclined_plane "Inclined plane") can illustrate the combination of gravitational force, ["normal" force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_force "Normal force"), friction, and string tension.[[note 4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-30)

Newton's second law is sometimes presented as a _definition_ of force, i.e., a force is that which exists when an inertial observer sees a body accelerating. This is sometimes regarded as a potential [tautology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tautology_(logic) "Tautology (logic)") — acceleration implies force, force implies acceleration. To go beyond tautology, an equation detailing the force might also be specified, like [Newton's law of universal gravitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_law_of_universal_gravitation "Newton's law of universal gravitation"). By inserting such an expression for ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da18bef8c979f3548bb0d8976f5844012d7b8256) into Newton's second law, an equation with predictive power can be written.[[note 5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-34) Newton's second law has also been regarded as setting out a research program for physics, establishing that important goals of the subject are to identify the forces present in nature and to catalogue the constituents of matter.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:0-17): 134 [[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-FLS-32): 12-2

### Third law

_To every action, there is always opposed an equal reaction; or, the mutual actions of two bodies upon each other are always equal, and directed to contrary parts._[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:0-17): 116 [![Image 35](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Iridium-1_Launch_%2832312419215%29.jpg/250px-Iridium-1_Launch_%2832312419215%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Iridium-1_Launch_(32312419215).jpg)

[Rockets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket "Rocket") work by creating unbalanced high pressure that pushes the rocket upwards while exhaust gas exits through an open nozzle.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-35)

In other words, if one body exerts a force on a second body, the second body is also exerting a force on the first body, of equal magnitude in the opposite direction. Overly brief paraphrases of the third law, like "action equals [reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reaction_(physics) "Reaction (physics)")" might have caused confusion among generations of students: the "action" and "reaction" apply to different bodies. For example, consider a book at rest on a table. The Earth's gravity pulls down upon the book. The "reaction" to that "action" is _not_ the support force from the table holding up the book, but the gravitational pull of the book acting on the Earth.[[note 6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-42)

Newton's third law relates to a more fundamental principle, the [conservation of momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_momentum "Conservation of momentum"). The latter remains true even in cases where Newton's statement does not, for instance when [force fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force_field_(physics) "Force field (physics)") as well as material bodies carry momentum, and when momentum is defined properly, in [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") as well.[[note 7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-43) In Newtonian mechanics, if two bodies have momenta ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} _{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/279e30b6f8eba8d6ff75f984f9393eebc2ea6d6e) and ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0503cf42886fcd7909a39b737ba760b0249b3ecf) respectively, then the total momentum of the pair is ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} =\mathbf {p} _{1}+\mathbf {p} _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a232997be44e469d18176f7c6469979cf03bbfef), and the rate of change of ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd73e3862cb92b016721b8c492eadb4e8a577527) is ![Image 40: {\displaystyle {\frac {d\mathbf {p} }{dt}}={\frac {d\mathbf {p} _{1}}{dt}}+{\frac {d\mathbf {p} _{2}}{dt}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5bbd1f10c07f4a12de996016f000a4c1edab8ab0) By Newton's second law, the first term is the total force upon the first body, and the second term is the total force upon the second body. If the two bodies are isolated from outside influences, the only force upon the first body can be that from the second, and vice versa. By Newton's third law, these forces have equal magnitude but opposite direction, so they cancel when added, and ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd73e3862cb92b016721b8c492eadb4e8a577527) is constant. Alternatively, if ![Image 42: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd73e3862cb92b016721b8c492eadb4e8a577527) is known to be constant, it follows that the forces have equal magnitude and opposite direction.

### Candidates for additional laws

Various sources have proposed elevating other ideas used in classical mechanics to the status of Newton's laws. For example, in Newtonian mechanics, the total mass of a body made by bringing together two smaller bodies is the sum of their individual masses. [Frank Wilczek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_Wilczek "Frank Wilczek") has suggested calling attention to this assumption by designating it "Newton's Zeroth Law".[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-44) Another candidate for a "zeroth law" is the fact that at any instant, a body reacts to the forces applied to it at that instant.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-45) Likewise, the idea that forces add like vectors (or in other words obey the [superposition principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superposition_principle "Superposition principle")), and the idea that forces change the energy of a body, have both been described as a "fourth law".[[note 8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-50)

Moreover, some texts organize the basic ideas of Newtonian mechanics into different postulates, other than the three laws as commonly phrased, with the goal of being more clear about what is empirically observed and what is true by definition.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:2-22): 9 [[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Eisenbud-31)

### Examples

The study of the behavior of massive bodies using Newton's laws is known as Newtonian mechanics. Some example problems in Newtonian mechanics are particularly noteworthy for conceptual or historical reasons.

#### Uniformly accelerated motion

[![Image 43](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Bouncing_ball_strobe_edit.jpg/330px-Bouncing_ball_strobe_edit.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bouncing_ball_strobe_edit.jpg)

A [bouncing ball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bouncing_ball "Bouncing ball") photographed at 25 frames per second using a [stroboscopic flash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stroboscope "Stroboscope"). In between bounces, the ball's height as a function of time is close to being a [parabola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabola "Parabola"), deviating from a parabolic arc because of air resistance, spin, and deformation into a non-spherical shape upon impact.

If a body falls from rest near the surface of the Earth, then in the absence of air resistance, it will accelerate at a constant rate. This is known as [free fall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_fall "Free fall"). The speed attained during free fall is proportional to the elapsed time, and the distance traveled is proportional to the square of the elapsed time.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-51) Importantly, the acceleration is the same for all bodies, independently of their mass. This follows from combining Newton's second law of motion with his [law of universal gravitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_law_of_universal_gravitation "Newton's law of universal gravitation"). The latter states that the magnitude of the gravitational force from the Earth upon the body is ![Image 44: {\displaystyle F={\frac {GMm}{r^{2}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aa486837554264344fb8f4dff3106f1a0715054f) where ![Image 45: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) is the mass of the falling body, ![Image 46: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is the mass of the Earth, ![Image 47: {\displaystyle G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f3c8921a3b352de45446a6789b104458c9f90b) is Newton's constant, and ![Image 48: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) is the distance from the center of the Earth to the body's location, which is very nearly the radius of the Earth. Setting this equal to ![Image 49: {\displaystyle ma}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f38bd3876d01199d10b88b0b5c2eb64bbc86b3e), the body's mass ![Image 50: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) cancels from both sides of the equation, leaving an acceleration that depends upon ![Image 51: {\displaystyle G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f3c8921a3b352de45446a6789b104458c9f90b), ![Image 52: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd), and ![Image 53: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538), and ![Image 54: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) can be taken to be constant. This particular value of acceleration is typically denoted ![Image 55: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77): ![Image 56: {\displaystyle g={\frac {GM}{r^{2}}}\approx \mathrm {9.8~m/s^{2}} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f81f4af29f49765f29efaebacbc32b896b542485)

If the body is not released from rest but instead launched upwards and/or horizontally with nonzero velocity, then free fall becomes [projectile motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projectile_motion "Projectile motion").[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-52) When air resistance can be neglected, projectiles follow [parabola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabola "Parabola")-shaped trajectories, because gravity affects the body's vertical motion and not its horizontal. At the peak of the projectile's trajectory, its vertical velocity is zero, but its acceleration is ![Image 57: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) downwards, as it is at all times. Setting the wrong vector equal to zero is a common confusion among physics students.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-53)

#### Uniform circular motion

[![Image 58](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/ae/Binary_system_orbit_q%3D3_e%3D0.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Binary_system_orbit_q%3D3_e%3D0.gif)

Two objects in uniform circular motion, orbiting around the [barycenter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barycenter "Barycenter") (center of mass of both objects)

When a body is in uniform circular motion, the force on it changes the direction of its motion but not its speed. For a body moving in a circle of radius ![Image 59: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) at a constant speed ![Image 60: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597), its acceleration has a magnitude![Image 61: {\displaystyle a={\frac {v^{2}}{r}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0220b1356204c98557a8b60d275196be3e9cf0c1)and is directed toward the center of the circle.[[note 9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-55) The force required to sustain this acceleration, called the [centripetal force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centripetal_force "Centripetal force"), is therefore also directed toward the center of the circle and has magnitude ![Image 62: {\displaystyle mv^{2}/r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/778e7247959c5af93ea3197b7cf7569826979e12). Many [orbits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit "Orbit"), such as that of the Moon around the Earth, can be approximated by uniform circular motion. In such cases, the centripetal force is gravity, and by Newton's law of universal gravitation has magnitude ![Image 63: {\displaystyle GMm/r^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f0f4258974f3b3e076be88d9948712ef36b986cc), where ![Image 64: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is the mass of the larger body being orbited. Therefore, the mass of a body can be calculated from observations of another body orbiting around it.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-56): 130

[Newton's cannonball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_cannonball "Newton's cannonball") is a [thought experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thought_experiment "Thought experiment") that interpolates between projectile motion and uniform circular motion. A cannonball that is lobbed weakly off the edge of a tall cliff will hit the ground in the same amount of time as if it were dropped from rest, because the force of gravity only affects the cannonball's momentum in the downward direction, and its effect is not diminished by horizontal movement. If the cannonball is launched with a greater initial horizontal velocity, then it will travel farther before it hits the ground, but it will still hit the ground in the same amount of time. However, if the cannonball is launched with an even larger initial velocity, then the curvature of the Earth becomes significant: the ground itself will curve away from the falling cannonball. A very fast cannonball will fall away from the inertial straight-line trajectory at the same rate that the Earth curves away beneath it; in other words, it will be in orbit (imagining that it is not slowed by air resistance or obstacles).[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-57)

#### Harmonic motion

[![Image 65](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/2/25/Animated-mass-spring.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Animated-mass-spring.gif)

An undamped [spring–mass system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spring%E2%80%93mass_system "Spring–mass system") undergoes simple harmonic motion.

Consider a body of mass ![Image 66: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) able to move along the ![Image 67: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) axis, and suppose an equilibrium point exists at the position ![Image 68: {\displaystyle x=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/953917eaf52f2e1baad54c8c9e3d6f9bb3710cdc). That is, at ![Image 69: {\displaystyle x=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/953917eaf52f2e1baad54c8c9e3d6f9bb3710cdc), the net force upon the body is the zero vector, and by Newton's second law, the body will not accelerate. If the force upon the body is proportional to the displacement from the equilibrium point, and directed to the equilibrium point, then the body will perform [simple harmonic motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_harmonic_motion "Simple harmonic motion"). Writing the force as ![Image 70: {\displaystyle F=-kx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aace79ea36db4ebc9f83a00c4198f4c054f5b4fb), Newton's second law becomes ![Image 71: {\displaystyle m{\frac {d^{2}x}{dt^{2}}}=-kx\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b90b9df5b7569e73835a019b647950437b46b52d) This differential equation has the solution ![Image 72: {\displaystyle x(t)=A\cos \omega t+B\sin \omega t\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d211558e31c7d28cb619cd78ba81210c676023e1) where the frequency ![Image 73: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) is equal to ![Image 74: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {k/m}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16585c1e039075df0261172c0f8aa08fdc09dc68), and the constants ![Image 75: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) and ![Image 76: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) can be calculated knowing, for example, the position and velocity the body has at a given time, like ![Image 77: {\displaystyle t=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43469ec032d858feae5aa87029e22eaaf0109e9c).

One reason that the harmonic oscillator is a conceptually important example is that it is good approximation for many systems near a stable mechanical equilibrium.[[note 10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-59) For example, a [pendulum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum "Pendulum") has a stable equilibrium in the vertical position: if motionless there, it will remain there, and if pushed slightly, it will swing back and forth. Neglecting air resistance and friction in the pivot, the force upon the pendulum is gravity, and Newton's second law becomes ![Image 78: {\displaystyle {\frac {d^{2}\theta }{dt^{2}}}=-{\frac {g}{L}}\sin \theta ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea32e4dbf842c0151ec6ba5ed6011aa5d264afa5)where ![Image 79: {\displaystyle L}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/103168b86f781fe6e9a4a87b8ea1cebe0ad4ede8) is the length of the pendulum and ![Image 80: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) is its angle from the vertical. When the angle ![Image 81: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) is small, the [sine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sine_and_cosine "Sine and cosine") of ![Image 82: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) is nearly equal to ![Image 83: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) (see [small-angle approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small-angle_approximation "Small-angle approximation")), and so this expression simplifies to the equation for a simple harmonic oscillator with frequency ![Image 84: {\displaystyle \omega ={\sqrt {g/L}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40a199298fdb932aaff137d6c4df7ac778c00159).

A harmonic oscillator can be _damped,_ often by friction or viscous drag, in which case energy bleeds out of the oscillator and the amplitude of the oscillations decreases over time. Also, a harmonic oscillator can be _driven_ by an applied force, which can lead to the phenomenon of [resonance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resonance "Resonance").[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-60)

#### Objects with variable mass

[![Image 85](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2a/Space_Shuttle_Atlantis_launches_from_KSC_on_STS-132_side_view.jpg/250px-Space_Shuttle_Atlantis_launches_from_KSC_on_STS-132_side_view.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Space_Shuttle_Atlantis_launches_from_KSC_on_STS-132_side_view.jpg)

Rockets, like the [Space Shuttle _Atlantis_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Atlantis "Space Shuttle Atlantis"), expel mass during operation. This means that the mass being pushed, the rocket and its remaining onboard fuel supply, is constantly changing.

Newtonian physics treats matter as being neither created nor destroyed, though it may be rearranged. It can be the case that an object of interest gains or loses mass because matter is added to or removed from it. In such a situation, Newton's laws can be applied to the individual pieces of matter, keeping track of which pieces belong to the object of interest over time. For instance, if a rocket of mass ![Image 86: {\displaystyle M(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fec7adc70ea0e1d365e5f7244b6f7ddad9fbf485), moving at velocity ![Image 87: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} (t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee594765ca30167f80394d3349307a445782d012), ejects matter at a velocity ![Image 88: {\displaystyle \mathbf {u} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/261e20fe101de02a771021d9d4466c0ad3e352d7) relative to the rocket, then[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Plastino-1992-27)![Image 89: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =M{\frac {d\mathbf {v} }{dt}}-\mathbf {u} {\frac {dM}{dt}}\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47ce24484b2d0168f9b48f4278511138d9910153) where ![Image 90: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da18bef8c979f3548bb0d8976f5844012d7b8256) is the net external force (e.g., a planet's gravitational pull).[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Kleppner-26): 139

#### Fan and sail

[![Image 91](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Newtonssailboat.jpg/250px-Newtonssailboat.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Newtonssailboat.jpg)

A boat equipped with a fan and a sail

The fan and sail example is a situation studied in discussions of Newton's third law.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-j940-61) In the situation, a [fan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fan_(machine) "Fan (machine)") is attached to a cart or a [sailboat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sailboat "Sailboat") and blows on its sail. From the third law, one would reason that the force of the air pushing in one direction would cancel out the force done by the fan on the sail, leaving the entire apparatus stationary. However, because the system is not entirely enclosed, there are conditions in which the vessel will move; for example, if the sail is built in a manner that redirects the majority of the airflow back towards the fan, the net force will result in the vessel moving forward.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Stocklmayer-39)[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-l303-62)

Work and energy
---------------

The concept of [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy") was developed after Newton's time, but it has become an inseparable part of what is considered "Newtonian" physics. Energy can broadly be classified into [kinetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy"), due to a body's motion, and [potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potential_energy "Potential energy"), due to a body's position relative to others. [Thermal energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_energy "Thermal energy"), the energy carried by heat flow, is a type of kinetic energy not associated with the macroscopic motion of objects but instead with the movements of the atoms and molecules of which they are made. According to the [work-energy theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Work-energy_theorem "Work-energy theorem"), when a force acts upon a body while that body moves along the line of the force, the force does _work_ upon the body, and the amount of work done is equal to the change in the body's kinetic energy.[[note 11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-65) In many cases of interest, the net work done by a force when a body moves in a closed loop — starting at a point, moving along some trajectory, and returning to the initial point — is zero. If this is the case, then the force can be written in terms of the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient") of a function called a [scalar potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_potential "Scalar potential"):[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Boas-54): 303 ![Image 92: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =-\mathbf {\nabla } U\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/607d3633111a0851e55066c97e992cf95db3a8ca) This is true for many forces including that of gravity, but not for friction; indeed, almost any problem in a mechanics textbook that does not involve friction can be expressed in this way.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-hand-finch-58): 19  The fact that the force can be written in this way can be understood from the [conservation of energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_energy "Conservation of energy"). Without friction to dissipate a body's energy into heat, the body's energy will trade between potential and (non-thermal) kinetic forms while the total amount remains constant. Any gain of kinetic energy, which occurs when the net force on the body accelerates it to a higher speed, must be accompanied by a loss of potential energy. So, the net force upon the body is determined by the manner in which the potential energy decreases.

Rigid-body motion and rotation
------------------------------

A rigid body is an object whose size is too large to neglect and which maintains the same shape over time. In Newtonian mechanics, the motion of a rigid body is often understood by separating it into movement of the body's [center of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_mass "Center of mass") and movement around the center of mass.

### Center of mass

[![Image 93: Fork-cork-toothpick object balanced on a pen on the toothpick part](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/04/Masocentro1.jpg/250px-Masocentro1.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Masocentro1.jpg)

The total center of mass of the [forks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fork "Fork"), [cork](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stopper_(plug) "Stopper (plug)"), and [toothpick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toothpick "Toothpick") is vertically below the pen's tip.

Significant aspects of the motion of an extended body can be understood by imagining the mass of that body concentrated to a single point, known as the center of mass. The location of a body's center of mass depends upon how that body's material is distributed. For a collection of pointlike objects with masses ![Image 94: {\displaystyle m_{1},\ldots ,m_{N}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/044398703078442d1e457a82773416a8622a576f) at positions ![Image 95: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} _{1},\ldots ,\mathbf {r} _{N}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54c2b2de164ae3ddfff88535d11ba6be061f0161), the center of mass is located at ![Image 96: {\displaystyle \mathbf {R} =\sum _{i=1}^{N}{\frac {m_{i}\mathbf {r} _{i}}{M}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b9a51d32aa52dd373a603b8524ec1451b9ae98f) where ![Image 97: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is the total mass of the collection. In the absence of a net external force, the center of mass moves at a constant speed in a straight line. This applies, for example, to a collision between two bodies.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-66) If the total external force is not zero, then the center of mass changes velocity as though it were a point body of mass ![Image 98: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd). This follows from the fact that the internal forces within the collection, the forces that the objects exert upon each other, occur in balanced pairs by Newton's third law. In a system of two bodies with one much more massive than the other, the center of mass will approximately coincide with the location of the more massive body.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:2-22): 22–24

### Rotational analogues of Newton's laws

When Newton's laws are applied to rotating extended bodies, they lead to new quantities that are analogous to those invoked in the original laws. The analogue of mass is the [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia"), the counterpart of momentum is [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum"), and the counterpart of force is [torque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque").

Angular momentum is calculated with respect to a reference point.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-67) If the displacement vector from a reference point to a body is ![Image 99: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1) and the body has momentum ![Image 100: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd73e3862cb92b016721b8c492eadb4e8a577527), then the body's angular momentum with respect to that point is, using the vector [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product"), ![Image 101: {\displaystyle \mathbf {L} =\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {p} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f896b5a7319a8643044a20bef2feab2ed55c406f) Taking the time derivative of the angular momentum gives ![Image 102: {\displaystyle {\frac {d\mathbf {L} }{dt}}=\left({\frac {d\mathbf {r} }{dt}}\right)\times \mathbf {p} +\mathbf {r} \times {\frac {d\mathbf {p} }{dt}}=\mathbf {v} \times m\mathbf {v} +\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {F} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb070677a561f320e7f520f413e8da5d67682123) The first term vanishes because ![Image 103: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6) and ![Image 104: {\displaystyle m\mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65c60398405d407b172aa51baf7d27eeb8687d5a) point in the same direction. The remaining term is the torque, ![Image 105: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\tau } =\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {F} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d8f5495f78b4b87b0b45ed088817f1e44adf716) When the torque is zero, the angular momentum is constant, just as when the force is zero, the momentum is constant.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:2-22): 14–15  The torque can vanish even when the force is non-zero, if the body is located at the reference point (![Image 106: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea91abce79b986f8283514f440f89893d27b8a02)) or if the force ![Image 107: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da18bef8c979f3548bb0d8976f5844012d7b8256) and the displacement vector ![Image 108: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1) are directed along the same line.

The angular momentum of a collection of point masses, and thus of an extended body, is found by adding the contributions from each of the points. This provides a means to characterize a body's rotation about an axis, by adding up the angular momenta of its individual pieces. The result depends on the chosen axis, the shape of the body, and the rate of rotation.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:2-22): 28

### Multi-body gravitational system

[![Image 109](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Three-body_Problem_Animation.gif/250px-Three-body_Problem_Animation.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Three-body_Problem_Animation.gif)

Animation of three points or bodies attracting to each other

Newton's law of universal gravitation states that any body attracts any other body along the straight line connecting them. The size of the attracting force is proportional to the product of their masses, and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. Finding the shape of the orbits that an inverse-square force law will produce is known as the [Kepler problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler_problem "Kepler problem"). The Kepler problem can be solved in multiple ways, including by demonstrating that the [Laplace–Runge–Lenz vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace%E2%80%93Runge%E2%80%93Lenz_vector "Laplace–Runge–Lenz vector") is constant,[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-68) or by applying a duality transformation to a 2-dimensional harmonic oscillator.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-69) However it is solved, the result is that orbits will be [conic sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conic_section "Conic section"), that is, [ellipses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse") (including circles), [parabolas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabola "Parabola"), or [hyperbolas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbola "Hyperbola"). The [eccentricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_eccentricity "Orbital eccentricity") of the orbit, and thus the type of conic section, is determined by the energy and the angular momentum of the orbiting body. Planets do not have sufficient energy to escape the Sun, and so their orbits are ellipses, to a good approximation; because the planets pull on one another, actual orbits are not exactly conic sections.

If a third mass is added, the Kepler problem becomes the three-body problem, which in general has no exact solution in [closed form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed-form_expression "Closed-form expression"). That is, there is no way to start from the differential equations implied by Newton's laws and, after a finite sequence of standard mathematical operations, obtain equations that express the three bodies' motions over time.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Barrow-Green1997-70)[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Barrow-Green2008-71)[Numerical methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerical_methods_for_ordinary_differential_equations "Numerical methods for ordinary differential equations") can be applied to obtain useful, albeit approximate, results for the three-body problem.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-72) The positions and velocities of the bodies can be stored in [variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_(computer_science) "Variable (computer science)") within a computer's memory; Newton's laws are used to calculate how the velocities will change over a short interval of time, and knowing the velocities, the changes of position over that time interval can be computed. This process is [looped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Loop_(computing) "Loop (computing)") to calculate, approximately, the bodies' trajectories. Generally speaking, the shorter the time interval, the more accurate the approximation.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-73)

Chaos and unpredictability
--------------------------

### Nonlinear dynamics

[![Image 110](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e3/Demonstrating_Chaos_with_a_Double_Pendulum.gif/250px-Demonstrating_Chaos_with_a_Double_Pendulum.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Demonstrating_Chaos_with_a_Double_Pendulum.gif)

Three double pendulums, initialized with almost exactly the same initial conditions, diverge over time.

Newton's laws of motion allow the possibility of [chaos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory "Chaos theory").[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:3-74)[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-75) That is, qualitatively speaking, physical systems obeying Newton's laws can exhibit sensitive dependence upon their initial conditions: a slight change of the position or velocity of one part of a system can lead to the whole system behaving in a radically different way within a short time. Noteworthy examples include the three-body problem, the [double pendulum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_pendulum "Double pendulum"), [dynamical billiards](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamical_billiards "Dynamical billiards"), and the [Fermi–Pasta–Ulam–Tsingou problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Pasta%E2%80%93Ulam%E2%80%93Tsingou_problem "Fermi–Pasta–Ulam–Tsingou problem").

Newton's laws can be applied to [fluids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid "Fluid") by considering a fluid as composed of infinitesimal pieces, each exerting forces upon neighboring pieces. The [Euler momentum equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_equations_(fluid_dynamics) "Euler equations (fluid dynamics)") is an expression of Newton's second law adapted to fluid dynamics.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Zee2020-76)[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-77) A fluid is described by a velocity field, i.e., a function ![Image 111: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} (\mathbf {x} ,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b9c5ae6b8c41d39658de387807109d572b6dfb0) that assigns a velocity vector to each point in space and time. A small object being carried along by the fluid flow can change velocity for two reasons: first, because the velocity field at its position is changing over time, and second, because it moves to a new location where the velocity field has a different value. Consequently, when Newton's second law is applied to an infinitesimal portion of fluid, the acceleration ![Image 112: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a957216653a9ee0d0133dcefd13fb75e36b8b9d) has two terms, a combination known as a [_total_ or _material_ derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Material_derivative "Material derivative"). The mass of an infinitesimal portion depends upon the fluid [density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density "Density"), and there is a net force upon it if the fluid pressure varies from one side of it to another. Accordingly, ![Image 113: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} =\mathbf {F} /m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38034156225231c906b1c1a777fc77bbfde60f6c) becomes ![Image 114: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial v}{\partial t}}+(\mathbf {\nabla } \cdot \mathbf {v} )\mathbf {v} =-{\frac {1}{\rho }}\mathbf {\nabla } P+\mathbf {f} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d1d6eed08dc833e0f6e93333f8a48963eaa17ec9) where ![Image 115: {\displaystyle \rho }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f7d439671d1289b6a816e6af7a304be40608d64) is the density, ![Image 116: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) is the pressure, and ![Image 117: {\displaystyle \mathbf {f} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dc6194e680a4e7c521f2178c50eea302843a852d) stands for an external influence like a gravitational pull. Incorporating the effect of [viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscosity "Viscosity") turns the Euler equation into a [Navier–Stokes equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Navier%E2%80%93Stokes_equation "Navier–Stokes equation"): ![Image 118: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial v}{\partial t}}+(\mathbf {\nabla } \cdot \mathbf {v} )\mathbf {v} =-{\frac {1}{\rho }}\mathbf {\nabla } P+\nu \nabla ^{2}\mathbf {v} +\mathbf {f} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90597361faa5c92d5cd5df2af6d9f6df0226ade9) where ![Image 119: {\displaystyle \nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c15bbbb971240cf328aba572178f091684585468) is the [kinematic viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscosity#Kinematic_viscosity "Viscosity").[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Zee2020-76)

### Singularities

It is mathematically possible for a collection of point masses, moving in accord with Newton's laws, to launch some of themselves away so forcefully that they fly off to infinity in a finite time.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-78) This unphysical behavior, known as a "noncollision singularity",[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Barrow-Green2008-71) depends upon the masses being pointlike and able to approach one another arbitrarily closely, as well as the lack of a [relativistic speed limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light") in Newtonian physics.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-79)

It is not yet known whether or not the Euler and Navier–Stokes equations exhibit the analogous behavior of initially smooth solutions "blowing up" in finite time. The question of [existence and smoothness of Navier–Stokes solutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Navier%E2%80%93Stokes_existence_and_smoothness "Navier–Stokes existence and smoothness") is one of the [Millennium Prize Problems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millennium_Prize_Problems "Millennium Prize Problems").[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-80)

Relation to other formulations of classical physics
---------------------------------------------------

Classical mechanics can be mathematically formulated in multiple different ways, other than the "Newtonian" description (which itself, of course, incorporates contributions from others both before and after Newton). The physical content of these different formulations is the same as the Newtonian, but they provide different insights and facilitate different types of calculations. For example, [Lagrangian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics") helps make apparent the connection between symmetries and conservation laws, and it is useful when calculating the motion of constrained bodies, like a mass restricted to move along a curving track or on the surface of a sphere.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:2-22): 48 [Hamiltonian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics "Hamiltonian mechanics") is convenient for [statistical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_physics "Statistical physics"),[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-81)[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:5-82): 57  leads to further insight about symmetry,[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:2-22): 251  and can be developed into sophisticated techniques for [perturbation theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perturbation_theory "Perturbation theory").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:2-22): 284  Due to the breadth of these topics, the discussion here will be confined to concise treatments of how they reformulate Newton's laws of motion.

### Lagrangian

[Lagrangian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics") differs from the Newtonian formulation by considering entire trajectories at once rather than predicting a body's motion at a single instant.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:2-22): 109  It is traditional in Lagrangian mechanics to denote position with ![Image 120: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d) and velocity with ![Image 121: {\displaystyle {\dot {q}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/399dc6b6e91a780c89824ccc26b4453b289e4387). The simplest example is a massive point particle, the Lagrangian for which can be written as the difference between its kinetic and potential energies: ![Image 122: {\displaystyle L(q,{\dot {q}})=T-V,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d848e6f392a4be2e0335af8674afc81bc9cd2a2) where the kinetic energy is ![Image 123: {\displaystyle T={\frac {1}{2}}m{\dot {q}}^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41425bea2f0e678155540035df918a9b88d24f0b) and the potential energy is some function of the position, ![Image 124: {\displaystyle V(q)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5dac6b17a8a7818fb4a89bd32f85daec2eb233f5). The physical path that the particle will take between an initial point ![Image 125: {\displaystyle q_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2752dcbff884354069fe332b8e51eb0a70a531b6) and a final point ![Image 126: {\displaystyle q_{f}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d1f2657d91f2f7644c467e3e49a30cfea9c18747) is the path for which the integral of the Lagrangian is "stationary". That is, the physical path has the property that small perturbations of it will, to a first approximation, not change the integral of the Lagrangian. [Calculus of variations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus_of_variations "Calculus of variations") provides the mathematical tools for finding this path.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Boas-54): 485  Applying the calculus of variations to the task of finding the path yields the [Euler–Lagrange equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%E2%80%93Lagrange_equation "Euler–Lagrange equation") for the particle, ![Image 127: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}\left({\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {q}}}}\right)={\frac {\partial L}{\partial q}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/96b9e7b66e2a9b32f0be390fd481d1d49cc35850) Evaluating the [partial derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative "Partial derivative") of the Lagrangian gives ![Image 128: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}(m{\dot {q}})=-{\frac {dV}{dq}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a6630be7552161d69cf60b32e3b29c102528eae) which is a restatement of Newton's second law. The left-hand side is the time derivative of the momentum, and the right-hand side is the force, represented in terms of the potential energy.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:1-11): 737

[Landau and Lifshitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Course_of_Theoretical_Physics "Course of Theoretical Physics") argue that the Lagrangian formulation makes the conceptual content of classical mechanics more clear than starting with Newton's laws.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Landau-33) Lagrangian mechanics provides a convenient framework in which to prove [Noether's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noether%27s_theorem "Noether's theorem"), which relates symmetries and conservation laws.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-83) The conservation of momentum can be derived by applying Noether's theorem to a Lagrangian for a multi-particle system, and so, Newton's third law is a theorem rather than an assumption.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:2-22): 124

### Hamiltonian

[![Image 129](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Noether.jpg/250px-Noether.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Noether.jpg)

[Emmy Noether](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmy_Noether "Emmy Noether"), whose 1915 proof of [a celebrated theorem that relates symmetries and conservation laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noether%27s_theorem "Noether's theorem") was a key development in modern physics and can be conveniently stated in the language of Lagrangian or Hamiltonian mechanics

In [Hamiltonian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics "Hamiltonian mechanics"), the dynamics of a system are represented by a function called the Hamiltonian, which in many cases of interest is equal to the total energy of the system.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:1-11): 742  The Hamiltonian is a function of the positions and the momenta of all the bodies making up the system, and it may also depend explicitly upon time. The time derivatives of the position and momentum variables are given by partial derivatives of the Hamiltonian, via [Hamilton's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamilton%27s_equations "Hamilton's equations").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:2-22): 203  The simplest example is a point mass ![Image 130: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) constrained to move in a straight line, under the effect of a potential. Writing ![Image 131: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d) for the position coordinate and ![Image 132: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) for the body's momentum, the Hamiltonian is ![Image 133: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {H}}(p,q)={\frac {p^{2}}{2m}}+V(q).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ec3c30ae260b4173db99919802aed70c4da0ade) In this example, Hamilton's equations are ![Image 134: {\displaystyle {\frac {dq}{dt}}={\frac {\partial {\mathcal {H}}}{\partial p}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ca624fc3fc78b26d00a28e67ea897fb07b5bd1c) and ![Image 135: {\displaystyle {\frac {dp}{dt}}=-{\frac {\partial {\mathcal {H}}}{\partial q}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23e1f128982c8332b653cf0b1982308b3dfc61b8) Evaluating these partial derivatives, the former equation becomes ![Image 136: {\displaystyle {\frac {dq}{dt}}={\frac {p}{m}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8747695fd4a3b4722410a4084b77aee8bf15ce18) which reproduces the familiar statement that a body's momentum is the product of its mass and velocity. The time derivative of the momentum is ![Image 137: {\displaystyle {\frac {dp}{dt}}=-{\frac {dV}{dq}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7a838a49c1d0d0862911e3d00296d7cb6266641) which, upon identifying the negative derivative of the potential with the force, is just Newton's second law once again.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:3-74)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:1-11): 742

As in the Lagrangian formulation, in Hamiltonian mechanics the conservation of momentum can be derived using Noether's theorem, making Newton's third law an idea that is deduced rather than assumed.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:2-22): 251

Among the proposals to reform the standard introductory-physics curriculum is one that teaches the concept of energy before that of force, essentially "introductory Hamiltonian mechanics".[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-84)[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-85)

### Hamilton–Jacobi

The [Hamilton–Jacobi equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamilton%E2%80%93Jacobi_equation "Hamilton–Jacobi equation") provides yet another formulation of classical mechanics, one which makes it mathematically analogous to [wave optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_optics "Wave optics").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:2-22): 284 [[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-86) This formulation also uses Hamiltonian functions, but in a different way than the formulation described above. The paths taken by bodies or collections of bodies are deduced from a function ![Image 138: {\displaystyle S(\mathbf {q} _{1},\mathbf {q} _{2},\ldots ,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/78b62f7d77a6133186ad5514a08d639405f05794) of positions ![Image 139: {\displaystyle \mathbf {q} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c8ce6be9c9335b20681f1b784557c574a69f28e9) and time ![Image 140: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560). The Hamiltonian is incorporated into the Hamilton–Jacobi equation, a [differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equation "Differential equation") for ![Image 141: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2). Bodies move over time in such a way that their trajectories are perpendicular to the surfaces of constant ![Image 142: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2), analogously to how a light ray propagates in the direction perpendicular to its wavefront. This is simplest to express for the case of a single point mass, in which ![Image 143: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) is a function ![Image 144: {\displaystyle S(\mathbf {q} ,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3a9798e69ae297664214c3045d8bd0b1c0208d5), and the point mass moves in the direction along which ![Image 145: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) changes most steeply. In other words, the momentum of the point mass is the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient") of ![Image 146: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2): ![Image 147: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} ={\frac {1}{m}}\mathbf {\nabla } S.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3bf70cd53a9f7756425f53e7d58d32e765be48e9) The Hamilton–Jacobi equation for a point mass is ![Image 148: {\displaystyle -{\frac {\partial S}{\partial t}}=H\left(\mathbf {q} ,\mathbf {\nabla } S,t\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a146b7c7f7e666d8195ebf60483f9415eaaf4191) The relation to Newton's laws can Relation to other physical theories
-----------------------------------

### Thermodynamics and statistical physics

[![Image 156](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c2/Brownian_motion_large.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Brownian_motion_large.gif)

A simulation of a larger, but still microscopic, particle (in yellow) surrounded by a gas of smaller particles, illustrating [Brownian motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brownian_motion "Brownian motion")

In [statistical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_physics "Statistical physics"), the [kinetic theory of gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases "Kinetic theory of gases") applies Newton's laws of motion to large numbers (typically on the order of the [Avogadro number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avogadro_constant "Avogadro constant")) of particles. Kinetic theory can explain, for example, the [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure") that a gas exerts upon the container holding it as the aggregate of many impacts of atoms, each imparting a tiny amount of momentum.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:5-82): 62

The [Langevin equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Langevin_equation "Langevin equation") is a special case of Newton's second law, adapted for the case of describing a small object bombarded stochastically by even smaller ones.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:4-89): 235  It can be written![Image 157: {\displaystyle m\mathbf {a} =-\gamma \mathbf {v} +\mathbf {\xi } \,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f4701c269fde542fa22d40bca66e70ad383f8ae2)where ![Image 158: {\displaystyle \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a223c880b0ce3da8f64ee33c4f0010beee400b1a) is a [drag coefficient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drag_coefficient "Drag coefficient") and ![Image 159: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\xi } }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2e2a51fb5522ad140f19673a76daef706190448) is a force that varies randomly from instant to instant, representing the net effect of collisions with the surrounding particles. This is used to model [Brownian motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brownian_motion "Brownian motion").[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-90)

### Electromagnetism

Newton's three laws can be applied to phenomena involving [electricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electricity "Electricity") and [magnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetism "Magnetism"), though subtleties and caveats exist.

[Coulomb's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coulomb%27s_law "Coulomb's law") for the electric force between two stationary, [electrically charged](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge") bodies has much the same mathematical form as Newton's law of universal gravitation: the force is proportional to the product of the charges, inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them, and directed along the straight line between them. The Coulomb force that a charge ![Image 160: {\displaystyle q_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9daa41f6e8f78ea6bb5711d7ac97901ce564b94e) exerts upon a charge ![Image 161: {\displaystyle q_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fd2d05084feb02b8ba29b0673440fb673b102589) is equal in magnitude to the force that ![Image 162: {\displaystyle q_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fd2d05084feb02b8ba29b0673440fb673b102589) exerts upon ![Image 163: {\displaystyle q_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9daa41f6e8f78ea6bb5711d7ac97901ce564b94e), and it points in the exact opposite direction. Coulomb's law is thus consistent with Newton's third law.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-91)

Electromagnetism treats forces as produced by _fields_ acting upon charges. The [Lorentz force law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_force_law "Lorentz force law") provides an expression for the force upon a charged body that can be plugged into Newton's second law in order to calculate its acceleration.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-92): 85  According to the Lorentz force law, a charged body in an electric field experiences a force in the direction of that field, a force proportional to its charge ![Image 164: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d) and to the strength of the electric field. In addition, a _moving_ charged body in a magnetic field experiences a force that is also proportional to its charge, in a direction perpendicular to both the field and the body's direction of motion. Using the vector [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product"),![Image 165: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =q\mathbf {E} +q\mathbf {v} \times \mathbf {B} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6897d76dd298da4a1aaeb6ef7175661d7589e6ea)

[![Image 166](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a9/Cyclotron_motion.jpg/250px-Cyclotron_motion.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cyclotron_motion.jpg)

The Lorentz force law in effect: electrons are bent into a circular trajectory by a magnetic field.

If the electric field vanishes (![Image 167: {\displaystyle \mathbf {E} =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fac8fccc5dc45d85848483c2bc6b77b4b7033e0c)), then the force will be perpendicular to the charge's motion, just as in the case of uniform circular motion studied above, and the charge will circle (or more generally move in a [helix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helix "Helix")) around the magnetic field lines at the [cyclotron frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclotron_motion "Cyclotron motion")![Image 168: {\displaystyle \omega =qB/m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b49dfeea500cc3c3fe955713b88bc3354233e7e6).[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:4-89): 222 [Mass spectrometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_spectrometry "Mass spectrometry") works by applying electric and/or magnetic fields to moving charges and measuring the resulting acceleration, which by the Lorentz force law yields the [mass-to-charge ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass-to-charge_ratio "Mass-to-charge ratio").[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-93)

Collections of charged bodies do not always obey Newton's third law: there can be a change of one body's momentum without a compensatory change in the momentum of another. The discrepancy is accounted for by momentum carried by the electromagnetic field itself. The momentum per unit volume of the electromagnetic field is proportional to the [Poynting vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poynting_vector "Poynting vector").[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Panofsky1962-94): 184 [[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-95)

There is subtle conceptual conflict between electromagnetism and Newton's first law: [Maxwell's theory of electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations "Maxwell's equations") predicts that electromagnetic waves will travel through empty space at a constant, definite speed. Thus, some inertial observers seemingly have a privileged status over the others, namely those who measure the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light") and find it to be the value predicted by the Maxwell equations. In other words, light provides an absolute standard for speed, yet the principle of inertia holds that there should be no such standard. This tension is resolved in the theory of special relativity, which revises the notions of _space_ and _time_ in such a way that all inertial observers will agree upon the speed of light in vacuum.[[note 12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-98)

### Special relativity

In special relativity, the rule that Wilczek called "Newton's Zeroth Law" breaks down: the mass of a composite object is not merely the sum of the masses of the individual pieces.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:7-99): 33  Newton's first law, inertial motion, remains true. A form of Newton's second law, that force is the rate of change of momentum, also holds, as does the conservation of momentum. However, the definition of momentum is modified. Among the consequences of this is the fact that the more quickly a body moves, the harder it is to accelerate, and so, no matter how much force is applied, a body cannot be accelerated to the speed of light. Depending on the problem at hand, momentum in special relativity can be represented as a three-dimensional vector, ![Image 169: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} =m\gamma \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/212dc9b9ce048465636309e9442073e1c1ce0b27), where ![Image 170: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) is the body's [rest mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rest_mass "Rest mass") and ![Image 171: {\displaystyle \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a223c880b0ce3da8f64ee33c4f0010beee400b1a) is the [Lorentz factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_factor "Lorentz factor"), which depends upon the body's speed. Alternatively, momentum and force can be represented as [four-vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-vector "Four-vector").[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-100): 107

Newton's third law must be modified in special relativity. The third law refers to the forces between two bodies at the same moment in time, and a key feature of special relativity is that simultaneity is relative. Events that happen at the same time relative to one observer can happen at different times relative to another. So, in a given observer's frame of reference, action and reaction may not be exactly opposite, and the total momentum of interacting bodies may not be conserved. The conservation of momentum is restored by including the momentum stored in the field that describes the bodies' interaction.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-101)[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-102)

Newtonian mechanics is a good approximation to special relativity when the speeds involved are small compared to that of light.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-103): 131

### General relativity

[General relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") is a theory of gravity that advances beyond that of Newton. In general relativity, the gravitational force of Newtonian mechanics is reimagined as curvature of [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime"). A curved path like an orbit, attributed to a gravitational force in Newtonian mechanics, is not the result of a force deflecting a body from an ideal straight-line path, but rather the body's attempt to fall freely through a background that is itself curved by the presence of other masses. A remark by [John Archibald Wheeler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Archibald_Wheeler "John Archibald Wheeler") that has become proverbial among physicists summarizes the theory: "Spacetime tells matter how to move; matter tells spacetime how to curve."[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Wheeler-104)[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-105) Wheeler himself thought of this reciprocal relationship as a modern, generalized form of Newton's third law.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Wheeler-104) The relation between matter distribution and spacetime curvature is given by the [Einstein field equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_field_equations "Einstein field equations"), which require [tensor calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_calculus "Tensor calculus") to express.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:7-99): 43 [[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-106)

The Newtonian theory of gravity is a good approximation to the predictions of general relativity when gravitational effects are weak and objects are moving slowly compared to the speed of light.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:6-96): 327 [[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-107)

### Quantum mechanics

[Quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") is a theory of physics originally developed in order to understand microscopic phenomena: behavior at the scale of molecules, atoms or subatomic particles. Generally and loosely speaking, the smaller a system is, the more an adequate mathematical model will require understanding quantum effects. The conceptual underpinning of quantum physics is [very different from that of classical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bell%27s_theorem "Bell's theorem"). Instead of thinking about quantities like position, momentum, and energy as properties that an object _has_, one considers what result might _appear_ when a [measurement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measurement_in_quantum_mechanics "Measurement in quantum mechanics") of a chosen type is performed. Quantum mechanics allows the physicist to calculate the probability that a chosen measurement will elicit a particular result.[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-108)[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-109) The [expectation value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expectation_value_(quantum_mechanics) "Expectation value (quantum mechanics)") for a measurement is the average of the possible results it might yield, weighted by their probabilities of occurrence.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-110)

The [Ehrenfest theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ehrenfest_theorem "Ehrenfest theorem") provides a connection between quantum expectation values and Newton's second law, a connection that is necessarily inexact, as quantum physics is fundamentally different from classical. In quantum physics, position and momentum are represented by mathematical entities known as [Hermitian operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermitian_operator "Hermitian operator"), and the [Born rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Born_rule "Born rule") is used to calculate the expectation values of a position measurement or a momentum measurement. These expectation values will generally change over time; that is, depending on the time at which (for example) a position measurement is performed, the probabilities for its different possible outcomes will vary. The Ehrenfest theorem says, roughly speaking, that the equations describing how these expectation values change over time have a form reminiscent of Newton's second law. However, the more pronounced quantum effects are in a given situation, the more difficult it is to derive meaningful conclusions from this resemblance.[[note 13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-113)

### Antiquity and medieval background

#### Aristotle and "violent" motion

[![Image 175: Statue of Aristotle](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Statue_at_the_Aristotle_University_of_Thessaloniki_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Statue_at_the_Aristotle_University_of_Thessaloniki_%28cropped%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Statue_at_the_Aristotle_University_of_Thessaloniki_(cropped).jpg)

Aristotle 

(384–322 [BCE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BCE "BCE"))

The subject of physics is often traced back to [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle"), but the history of the concepts involved is obscured by multiple factors. An exact correspondence between Aristotelian and modern concepts is not simple to establish: Aristotle did not clearly distinguish what we would call speed and force, used the same term for [density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density "Density") and [viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscosity "Viscosity"), and conceived of motion as always through a medium, rather than through space. In addition, some concepts often termed "Aristotelian" might better be attributed to his followers and commentators upon him.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-119) These commentators found that Aristotelian physics had difficulty explaining projectile motion.[[note 15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-121) Aristotle divided motion into two types: "natural" and "violent". The "natural" motion of terrestrial solid matter was to fall downwards, whereas a "violent" motion could push a body sideways. Moreover, in Aristotelian physics, a "violent" motion requires an immediate cause; separated from the cause of its "violent" motion, a body would revert to its "natural" behavior. Yet, a javelin continues moving after it leaves the thrower's hand. Aristotle concluded that the air around the javelin must be imparted with the ability to move the javelin forward.

#### Philoponus and impetus

[John Philoponus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Philoponus "John Philoponus"), a [Byzantine Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Byzantine_Greek "Byzantine Greek") thinker active during the sixth century, found this absurd: the same medium, air, was somehow responsible both for sustaining motion and for impeding it. If Aristotle's idea were true, Philoponus said, armies would launch weapons by blowing upon them with bellows. Philoponus argued that setting a body into motion imparted a quality, [impetus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_impetus "Theory of impetus"), that would be contained within the body itself. As long as its impetus was sustained, the body would continue to move.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-122): 47  In the following centuries, versions of impetus theory were advanced by individuals including [Nur ad-Din al-Bitruji](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nur_ad-Din_al-Bitruji "Nur ad-Din al-Bitruji"), [Avicenna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avicenna "Avicenna"), [Abu'l-Barakāt al-Baghdādī](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abu%27l-Barak%C4%81t_al-Baghd%C4%81d%C4%AB "Abu'l-Barakāt al-Baghdādī"), [John Buridan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Buridan "John Buridan"), and [Albert of Saxony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_of_Saxony_(philosopher) "Albert of Saxony (philosopher)"). In retrospect, the idea of impetus can be seen as a forerunner of the modern concept of momentum.[[note 16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-124) The intuition that objects move according to some kind of impetus persists in many students of introductory physics.[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-125)

### Inertia and the first law

The French philosopher [René Descartes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes "René Descartes") introduced the concept of inertia by way of his "laws of nature" in _[The World](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_World\_(book) "The World (book)")_ (_Traité du monde et de la lumière_) written 1629–33. However, _The World_ purported a [heliocentric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliocentrism "Heliocentrism") worldview, and in 1633 this view had given rise a great conflict between [Galileo Galilei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galileo_Galilei "Galileo Galilei") and the [Roman Catholic Inquisition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Inquisition "Roman Inquisition"). Descartes knew about this controversy and did not wish to get involved. _The World_ was not published until 1664, ten years after his death.[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:8-126)

[![Image 176: Justus Sustermans - Portrait of Galileo Galilei](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cc/Galileo.arp.300pix.jpg/250px-Galileo.arp.300pix.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Galileo.arp.300pix.jpg)

Galileo Galilei 

(1564–1642)

The modern concept of inertia is credited to Galileo. Based on his experiments, Galileo concluded that the "natural" behavior of a moving body was to keep moving, until something else interfered with it. In _Two New Sciences_ (1638) Galileo wrote:[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-127)[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-128)

> Imagine any particle projected along a horizontal plane without friction; then we know, from what has been more fully explained in the preceding pages, that this particle will move along this same plane with a motion which is uniform and perpetual, provided the plane has no limits.

[![Image 177: Portrait of René Descartes](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Frans_Hals_-_Portret_van_Ren%C3%A9_Descartes_%28cropped%292.jpg/250px-Frans_Hals_-_Portret_van_Ren%C3%A9_Descartes_%28cropped%292.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Frans_Hals_-_Portret_van_Ren%C3%A9_Descartes_(cropped)2.jpg)

René Descartes 

(1596–1650)

Galileo recognized that in projectile motion, the Earth's gravity affects vertical but not horizontal motion.[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-129) However, Galileo's idea of inertia was not exactly the one that would be codified into Newton's first law. Galileo thought that a body moving a long distance inertially would follow the curve of the Earth. This idea was corrected by [Isaac Beeckman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Beeckman "Isaac Beeckman"), Descartes, and [Pierre Gassendi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Gassendi "Pierre Gassendi"), who recognized that inertial motion should be motion in a straight line.[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-130) Descartes published his laws of nature (laws of motion) with this correction in _[Principles of Philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principles\_of\_Philosophy "Principles of Philosophy")_ (_Principia Philosophiae_) in 1644, with the heliocentric part toned down.[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:02-131)[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:8-126)

[![Image 178](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/Breaking_String.PNG/250px-Breaking_String.PNG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Breaking_String.PNG)

Ball in circular motion has string cut and flies off tangentially.

> First Law of Nature: Each thing when left to itself continues in the same state; so any moving body goes on moving until something stops it.

> Second Law of Nature: Each moving thing if left to itself moves in a straight line; so any body moving in a circle always tends to move away from the centre of the circle.

According to American philosopher [Richard J. Blackwell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Richard_J._Blackwell "Richard J. Blackwell"), Dutch scientist [Christiaan Huygens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens "Christiaan Huygens") had worked out his own, concise version of the law in 1656.[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:9-132) It was not published until 1703, eight years after his death, in the opening paragraph of _De Motu Corporum ex Percussione_.

> Hypothesis I: Any body already in motion will continue to move perpetually with the same speed and in a straight line unless it is impeded.

According to Huygens, this law was already known by Galileo and Descartes among others.[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-:9-132)

### Force and the second law

[![Image 179](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Christiaan_Huygens-painting_%28cropped%29.jpeg/250px-Christiaan_Huygens-painting_%28cropped%29.jpeg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Christiaan_Huygens-painting_(cropped).jpeg)

Christiaan Huygens 

(1629–1695)

Christiaan Huygens, in his _[Horologium Oscillatorium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horologium\_Oscillatorium "Horologium Oscillatorium")_ (1673), put forth the hypothesis that "By the action of gravity, whatever its sources, it happens that bodies are moved by a motion composed both of a uniform motion in one direction or another and of a motion downward due to gravity." Newton's second law generalized this hypothesis from gravity to all forces.[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-133)

One important characteristic of Newtonian physics is that forces can [act at a distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_at_a_distance "Action at a distance") without requiring physical contact.[[note 17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-140) For example, the Sun and the Earth pull on each other gravitationally, despite being separated by millions of kilometres. This contrasts with the idea, championed by Descartes among others, that the Sun's gravity held planets in orbit by swirling them in a vortex of transparent matter, _[aether](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aether\_theories "Aether theories")_.[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-141) Newton considered aetherial explanations of force but ultimately rejected them.[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Newman2016-138) The study of magnetism by [William Gilbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Gilbert_(physician) "William Gilbert (physician)") and others created a precedent for thinking of _immaterial_ forces,[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-Newman2016-138) and unable to find a quantitatively satisfactory explanation of his law of gravity in terms of an aetherial model, Newton eventually declared, "[I feign no hypotheses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypotheses_non_fingo "Hypotheses non fingo")": whether or not a model like Descartes's vortices could be found to underlie the _Principia_'s theories of motion and gravity, the first grounds for judging them must be the successful predictions they made.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-142) And indeed, since Newton's time [every attempt at such a model has failed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_explanations_of_gravitation "Mechanical explanations of gravitation").

### Momentum conservation and the third law

[![Image 180: Portrait of Johannes Kepler](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/JKepler_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-JKepler_%28cropped%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:JKepler_(cropped).jpg)

Johannes Kepler 

(1571–1630)

[Johannes Kepler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Kepler "Johannes Kepler") suggested that gravitational attractions were reciprocal — that, for example, the Moon pulls on the Earth while the Earth pulls on the Moon — but he did not argue that such pairs are equal and opposite.[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-143) In his _[Principles of Philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principles\_of\_Philosophy "Principles of Philosophy")_ (1644), Descartes introduced the idea that during a collision between bodies, a "quantity of motion" remains unchanged. Descartes defined this quantity somewhat imprecisely by adding up the products of the speed and "size" of each body, where "size" for him incorporated both volume and surface area.[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-144) Moreover, Descartes thought of the universe as a [plenum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plenum_(physics) "Plenum (physics)"), that is, filled with matter, so all motion required a body to displace a medium as it moved.

During the 1650s, Huygens studied collisions between hard spheres and deduced a principle that is now identified as the conservation of momentum.[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-145)[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-146)[Christopher Wren](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christopher_Wren "Christopher Wren") would later deduce the same rules for [elastic collisions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elastic_collision "Elastic collision") that Huygens had, and [John Wallis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Wallis "John Wallis") would apply momentum conservation to study [inelastic collisions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inelastic_collision "Inelastic collision"). Newton cited the work of Huygens, Wren, and Wallis to support the validity of his third law.[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-147)

Newton arrived at his set of three laws incrementally. In a [1684 manuscript written to Huygens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_motu_corporum_in_gyrum "De motu corporum in gyrum"), he listed four laws: the principle of inertia, the change of motion by force, a statement about relative motion that would today be called [Galilean invariance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilean_invariance "Galilean invariance"), and the rule that interactions between bodies do not change the motion of their center of mass. In a later manuscript, Newton added a law of action and reaction, while saying that this law and the law regarding the center of mass implied one another. Newton probably settled on the presentation in the _Principia,_ with three primary laws and then other statements reduced to corollaries, during 1685.[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-148)

### After the _Principia_

[![Image 181](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/0/08/Page_157_from_Mechanism_of_the_Heaven%2C_Mary_Somerville_1831.png/250px-Page_157_from_Mechanism_of_the_Heaven%2C_Mary_Somerville_1831.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Page_157_from_Mechanism_of_the_Heaven,_Mary_Somerville_1831.png)

Page 157 from _Mechanism of the Heavens_ (1831), [Mary Somerville](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mary_Somerville "Mary Somerville")'s expanded version of the first two volumes of Laplace's _Traité de mécanique céleste._[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-149) Here, Somerville deduces the inverse-square law of gravity from [Kepler's laws of planetary motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion "Kepler's laws of planetary motion").

Newton expressed his second law by saying that the force on a body is proportional to its change of motion, or momentum. By the time he wrote the _Principia,_ he had already developed calculus (which he called "[the science of fluxions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluxion "Fluxion")"), but in the _Principia_ he made no explicit use of it, perhaps because he believed geometrical arguments in the tradition of [Euclid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid "Euclid") to be more rigorous.[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-150): 15 [[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-151) Consequently, the _Principia_ does not express acceleration as the second derivative of position, and so it does not give the second law as ![Image 182: {\displaystyle F=ma}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ca4e42b7d6d66f52294364928cb5f7c590f514c). This form of the second law was written (for the special case of constant force) at least as early as 1716, by [Jakob Hermann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jakob_Hermann "Jakob Hermann"); [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler") would employ it as a basic premise in the 1740s.[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-152) Euler pioneered the study of rigid bodies[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-153) and established the basic theory of fluid dynamics.[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-154)[Pierre-Simon Laplace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre-Simon_Laplace "Pierre-Simon Laplace")'s five-volume _[Traité de mécanique céleste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trait%C3%A9\_de\_m%C3%A9canique\_c%C3%A9leste "Traité de mécanique céleste")_ (1798–1825) forsook geometry and developed mechanics purely through algebraic expressions, while resolving questions that the _Principia_ had left open, like a full theory of the [tides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tide "Tide").[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-155)

The concept of energy became a key part of Newtonian mechanics in the post-Newton period. Huygens' solution of the collision of hard spheres showed that in that case, not only is momentum conserved, but kinetic energy is as well (or, rather, a quantity that in retrospect we can identify as one-half the total kinetic energy). The question of what is conserved during all other processes, like inelastic collisions and motion slowed by friction, was not resolved until the 19th century. Debates on this topic overlapped with philosophical disputes between the metaphysical views of Newton and Leibniz, and variants of the term "force" were sometimes used to denote what we would call types of energy. For example, in 1742, [Émilie du Châtelet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89milie_du_Ch%C3%A2telet "Émilie du Châtelet") wrote, "Dead force consists of a simple tendency to motion: such is that of a spring ready to relax; [living force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vis_viva "Vis viva") is that which a body has when it is in actual motion." In modern terminology, "dead force" and "living force" correspond to potential energy and kinetic energy respectively.[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-156) Conservation of energy was not established as a universal principle until it was understood that the energy of mechanical work can be dissipated into heat.[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-157)[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-158) With the concept of energy given a solid grounding, Newton's laws could then be derived within formulations of classical mechanics that put energy first, as in the Lagrangian and Hamiltonian formulations described above.

Modern presentations of Newton's laws use the mathematics of vectors, a topic that was not developed until the late 19th and early 20th centuries. Vector algebra, pioneered by [Josiah Willard Gibbs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Josiah_Willard_Gibbs "Josiah Willard Gibbs") and [Oliver Heaviside](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oliver_Heaviside "Oliver Heaviside"), stemmed from and largely supplanted the earlier system of [quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion "Quaternion") invented by [William Rowan Hamilton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Rowan_Hamilton "William Rowan Hamilton").[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-159)[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_laws_of_motion#cite_note-160)
