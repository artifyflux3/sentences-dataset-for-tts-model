The **immune system** is a network of [biological systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_systems "Biological systems") that protects an [organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organism "Organism") from [diseases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diseases "Diseases"). It detects and responds to a wide variety of [pathogens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathogen "Pathogen"), from [viruses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virus "Virus") to [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria "Bacteria"), as well as [cancer cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tumor_immunology "Tumor immunology"), [parasitic worms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parasitic_worm "Parasitic worm"), and also objects such as wood [splinters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Splinter "Splinter"), distinguishing them from the organism's own healthy [tissue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_tissue "Biological tissue"). Many species have two major subsystems of the immune system. The [innate immune system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Innate_immune_system "Innate immune system") provides a preconfigured response to broad groups of situations and stimuli. The [adaptive immune system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptive_immune_system "Adaptive immune system") provides a tailored response to each stimulus by learning to recognize molecules it has previously encountered. Both use [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humoral_immunity "Humoral immunity") and [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell-mediated_immunity "Cell-mediated immunity") to perform their functions.

Nearly all organisms have some kind of immune system. [Bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria "Bacteria") have a rudimentary immune system in the form of [enzymes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enzyme "Enzyme") that protect against [viral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteriophage "Bacteriophage") infections. Other basic immune mechanisms evolved in ancient [plants and animals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryote "Eukaryote") and remain in their modern descendants. These mechanisms include [phagocytosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phagocytosis "Phagocytosis"), [antimicrobial peptides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antimicrobial_peptides "Antimicrobial peptides") called [defensins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defensin "Defensin"), and the [complement system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complement_system "Complement system"). [Jawed vertebrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jawed_vertebrate "Jawed vertebrate"), including humans, have even more sophisticated defense mechanisms, including the ability to adapt to recognize pathogens more efficiently. Adaptive (or acquired) immunity creates an [immunological memory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunological_memory "Immunological memory") leading to an enhanced response to subsequent encounters with that same pathogen. This process of acquired immunity is the basis of [vaccination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vaccination "Vaccination").

Dysfunction of the immune system can cause [autoimmune diseases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autoimmune_disease "Autoimmune disease"), [inflammatory diseases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflammation "Inflammation") and [cancer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carcinogenesis "Carcinogenesis"). [Immunodeficiency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunodeficiency "Immunodeficiency") occurs when the immune system is less active than normal, resulting in recurring and life-threatening infections. In humans, immunodeficiency can be the result of a [genetic disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_disease "Genetic disease") such as [severe combined immunodeficiency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Severe_combined_immunodeficiency "Severe combined immunodeficiency"), acquired conditions such as [HIV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HIV "HIV")/[AIDS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AIDS "AIDS"), or the use of [immunosuppressive medication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunosuppressive_drugs "Immunosuppressive drugs"). [Autoimmunity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autoimmunity "Autoimmunity") results from a hyperactive immune system attacking normal tissues as if they were foreign organisms. Common autoimmune diseases include [Hashimoto's thyroiditis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hashimoto%27s_thyroiditis "Hashimoto's thyroiditis"), [rheumatoid arthritis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rheumatoid_arthritis "Rheumatoid arthritis"), [diabetes mellitus type 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diabetes_mellitus_type_1 "Diabetes mellitus type 1"), and [systemic lupus erythematosus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systemic_lupus_erythematosus "Systemic lupus erythematosus"). [Immunology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunology "Immunology") covers the study of all aspects of the immune system.

The immune system protects its host from [infection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infection "Infection") with layered defenses of increasing specificity. Physical barriers prevent pathogens such as [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria "Bacteria") and [viruses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virus "Virus") from entering the organism.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTESompayrac20191-1) If a pathogen breaches these barriers, the [innate immune system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Innate_immune_system "Innate immune system") provides an immediate, but non-specific response. Innate immune systems are found in all [animals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal "Animal").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Litman-2) If pathogens successfully evade the innate response, vertebrates possess a second layer of protection, the [adaptive immune system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptive_immune_system "Adaptive immune system"), which is activated by the innate response.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTESompayrac20194-3) Here, the immune system adapts its response during an infection to improve its recognition of the pathogen. This improved response is then retained after the pathogen has been eliminated, in the form of an [immunological memory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunological_memory "Immunological memory"), and allows the adaptive immune system to mount faster and stronger attacks each time this pathogen is encountered.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Restifo_2013-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Kurosaki_2015-5)

Both innate and adaptive immunity depend on the ability of the immune system to distinguish between self and non-self [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule"). In immunology, _self_ molecules are components of an organism's body that can be distinguished from foreign substances by the immune system.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTESompayrac201911-6) Conversely, _non-self_ molecules are those recognized as foreign molecules. One class of non-self molecules are called antigens (originally named for being _anti_ body _gen_ erators) and are defined as substances that bind to specific [immune receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_receptor "Immune receptor") and elicit an immune response.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTESompayrac2019146-7)

Several barriers protect organisms from infection, including mechanical, chemical, and biological barriers. The waxy [cuticle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant_cuticle "Plant cuticle") of most leaves, the [exoskeleton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exoskeleton "Exoskeleton") of insects, the [shells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eggshell "Eggshell") and membranes of externally deposited eggs, and [skin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skin "Skin") are examples of mechanical barriers that are the first line of defense against infection.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlbertsJohnsonLewisRaff2002sec._[httpswwwncbinlmnihgovbooksNBK26833A4639_%22Pathogens_Cross_Protective_Barriers_to_Colonize_the_Host%22]-8) Organisms cannot be completely sealed from their environments, so systems act to protect body openings such as the [lungs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung "Lung"), [intestines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intestine "Intestine"), and the [genitourinary tract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genitourinary_system "Genitourinary system"). In the lungs, coughing and sneezing mechanically eject pathogens and other [irritants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irritation "Irritation") from the [respiratory tract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_tract "Respiratory tract"). The flushing action of [tears](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tears "Tears") and [urine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine "Urine") also mechanically expels pathogens, while [mucus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mucus "Mucus") secreted by the respiratory and [gastrointestinal tract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastrointestinal_tract "Gastrointestinal tract") serves to trap and entangle [microorganisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism "Microorganism").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-9)

Chemical barriers also protect against infection. The skin and respiratory tract secrete [antimicrobial peptides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antimicrobial_peptides "Antimicrobial peptides") such as the β-[defensins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defensin "Defensin").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-10)[Enzymes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enzyme "Enzyme") such as [lysozyme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lysozyme "Lysozyme") and [phospholipase A2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phospholipase_A2 "Phospholipase A2") in [saliva](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saliva "Saliva"), tears, and [breast milk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breast_milk "Breast milk") are also [antibacterials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiseptic "Antiseptic").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-12)[Vaginal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vagina "Vagina") secretions serve as a chemical barrier following [menarche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Menarche "Menarche"), when they become slightly [acidic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid "Acid"), while [semen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semen "Semen") contains defensins and [zinc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zinc "Zinc") to kill pathogens.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-13)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-14) In the [stomach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stomach "Stomach"), [gastric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gastric_acid "Gastric acid") serves as a chemical defense against ingested pathogens.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-15)

Within the genitourinary and gastrointestinal tracts, [commensal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commensalism "Commensalism")[flora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gut_flora "Gut flora") serve as biological barriers by competing with pathogenic bacteria for food and space and, in some cases, changing the conditions in their environment, such as [pH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PH "PH") or available iron. As a result, the probability that pathogens will reach sufficient numbers to cause illness is reduced.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-16)

Innate immune system
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Immune_system&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Innate immune system")]

Microorganisms or toxins that successfully enter an organism encounter the cells and mechanisms of the innate immune system. The innate response is usually triggered when microbes are identified by [pattern recognition receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pattern_recognition_receptors "Pattern recognition receptors"), which recognize components that are conserved among broad groups of microorganisms,[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-pmid17943118-17) or when damaged, injured or stressed cells send out alarm signals, many of which are recognized by the same receptors as those that recognize pathogens.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-pmid11951032-18) Innate immune defenses are non-specific, meaning these systems respond to pathogens in a generic way.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlbertsJohnsonLewisRaff2002Chapter:_[httpswwwncbinlmnihgovbooksNBK26846_%22Innate_Immunity%22]-19) This system does not confer long-lasting [immunity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunity_(medical) "Immunity (medical)") against a pathogen. The innate immune system is the dominant system of host defense in most organisms,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Litman-2) and the only one in plants.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTEIriti2019xi-20)

Cells in the innate immune system use [pattern recognition receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pattern_recognition_receptor "Pattern recognition receptor") to recognize molecular structures that are produced by pathogens.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Kumar_2011-21) They are [proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein "Protein") expressed, mainly, by cells of the [innate immune system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Innate_immune_system "Innate immune system"), such as dendritic cells, macrophages, monocytes, neutrophils, and epithelial cells,[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlbertsJohnsonLewisRaff2002Chapter:_[httpswwwncbinlmnihgovbooksNBK26846_%22Innate_Immunity%22]-19)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-22) to identify two classes of molecules: [pathogen-associated molecular patterns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathogen-associated_molecular_patterns "Pathogen-associated molecular patterns") (PAMPs), which are associated with microbial [pathogens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathogens "Pathogens"), and [damage-associated molecular patterns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Damage-associated_molecular_patterns "Damage-associated molecular patterns") (DAMPs), which are associated with components of hosts' cells that are released during cell damage or cell death.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTESompayrac201920-23)

Recognition of extracellular or endosomal PAMPs is mediated by [transmembrane proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmembrane_protein "Transmembrane protein") known as [toll-like receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toll-like_receptor "Toll-like receptor") (TLRs).[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-pmid16551253-24) TLRs share a typical structural motif, the [leucine rich repeats (LRRs)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leucine-rich_repeat "Leucine-rich repeat"), which give them a curved shape.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-25) Toll-like receptors were first discovered in _[Drosophila](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drosophila\_melanogaster "Drosophila melanogaster")_ and trigger the synthesis and secretion of [cytokines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytokine "Cytokine") and activation of other host defense programs that are necessary for both innate or adaptive immune responses. Ten toll-like receptors have been described in humans.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-26)

Cells in the innate immune system have pattern recognition receptors, which detect infection or cell damage, inside. Three major classes of these "cytosolic" receptors are [NOD–like receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NOD-like_receptor "NOD-like receptor"), [RIG (retinoic acid-inducible gene)-like receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RIG-I-like_receptor "RIG-I-like receptor"), and cytosolic DNA sensors.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-27)

### Innate immune cells

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Immune_system&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Innate immune cells")]

[![Image 2: See caption](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/SEM_blood_cells.jpg/250px-SEM_blood_cells.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:SEM_blood_cells.jpg)

A [scanning electron microscope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scanning_electron_microscope "Scanning electron microscope") image of normal circulating human [blood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood "Blood"). One can see [red blood cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_blood_cell "Red blood cell"), several knobby [white blood cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_blood_cell "White blood cell"), including [lymphocytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lymphocyte "Lymphocyte"), a [monocyte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monocyte "Monocyte"), and a [neutrophil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrophil "Neutrophil"), and many small disc-shaped [platelets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platelet "Platelet").

Some [leukocytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leukocytes "Leukocytes") (white blood cells) act like independent, single-celled organisms and are the second arm of the innate immune system. The innate leukocytes include the ["professional" phagocytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phagocyte#Professional_phagocytes "Phagocyte") ([macrophages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macrophage "Macrophage"), [neutrophils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrophil "Neutrophil"), and [dendritic cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dendritic_cell "Dendritic cell")). These cells identify and eliminate pathogens, either by attacking larger pathogens through contact or by engulfing and then killing microorganisms. The other cells involved in the innate response include [innate lymphoid cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Innate_lymphoid_cell "Innate lymphoid cell"), [mast cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mast_cell "Mast cell"), [eosinophils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eosinophil_granulocyte "Eosinophil granulocyte"), [basophils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basophil_granulocyte "Basophil granulocyte"), and [natural killer cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_killer_cell "Natural killer cell").[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTESompayrac20191%E2%80%934-28)

[Phagocytosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phagocytosis "Phagocytosis") is an important feature of cellular innate immunity performed by cells called phagocytes that engulf pathogens or particles. Phagocytes generally patrol the body searching for pathogens, but can be called to specific locations by cytokines.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlbertsJohnsonLewisRaff2002sec._[httpswwwncbinlmnihgovbooksNBK26846A4686_%22Phagocytic_Cells_Seek,_Engulf,_and_Destroy_Pathogens%22]-29) Once a pathogen has been engulfed by a phagocyte, it becomes trapped in an intracellular [vesicle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vesicle_(biology) "Vesicle (biology)") called a [phagosome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phagosome "Phagosome"), which subsequently fuses with another vesicle called a [lysosome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lysosome "Lysosome") to form a [phagolysosome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phagolysosome "Phagolysosome"). The pathogen is killed by the activity of digestive enzymes or following a [respiratory burst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_burst "Respiratory burst") that releases [free radicals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_(chemistry) "Radical (chemistry)") into the phagolysosome.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-30)[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-31) Phagocytosis evolved as a means of acquiring [nutrients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrient "Nutrient"), but this role was extended in phagocytes to include engulfment of pathogens as a defense mechanism.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-32) Phagocytosis probably represents the oldest form of host defense, as phagocytes have been identified in both vertebrate and invertebrate animals.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-33)

Neutrophils and macrophages are phagocytes that travel throughout the body in pursuit of invading pathogens.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-34) Neutrophils are normally found in the [bloodstream](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circulatory_system "Circulatory system") and are the most abundant type of phagocyte, representing 50% to 60% of total circulating leukocytes.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStvrtinov%C3%A1Jakubovsk%C3%BDHul%C3%ADn1995Chapter:_[httpswebarchiveorgweb20010711220523httpnicsavbasklogosbooksscientificInffeverhtml_Inflammation_and_Fever]-35) During the acute phase of [inflammation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflammation "Inflammation"), neutrophils migrate toward the site of inflammation in a process called [chemotaxis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemotaxis "Chemotaxis") and are usually the first cells to arrive at the scene of infection. Macrophages are versatile cells that reside within tissues and produce an array of chemicals including enzymes, [complement proteins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complement_system "Complement system"), and cytokines. They can also act as scavengers that rid the body of worn-out cells and other debris and as [antigen-presenting cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antigen-presenting_cell "Antigen-presenting cell") (APCs) that activate the adaptive immune system.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Rua_2015-36)

Dendritic cells are phagocytes in tissues that are in contact with the external environment; therefore, they are located mainly in the skin, nose, lungs, stomach, and intestines.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Guermonprez-37) They are named for their resemblance to [neuronal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neuron "Neuron")[dendrites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dendrite "Dendrite"), as both have many spine-like projections. Dendritic cells serve as a link between the bodily tissues and the innate and adaptive immune systems, as they [present antigens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antigen_presentation "Antigen presentation") to [T cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T_cell "T cell"), one of the key cell types of the adaptive immune system.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Guermonprez-37)

[Granulocytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Granulocyte "Granulocyte") are leukocytes that have granules in their cytoplasm. In this category are neutrophils, mast cells, basophils, and eosinophils. Mast cells reside in [connective tissues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connective_tissue "Connective tissue") and [mucous membranes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mucous_membrane "Mucous membrane") and regulate the inflammatory response.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKrishnaswamyAjitawiChi200613%E2%80%9334-38) They are most often associated with [allergy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allergy "Allergy") and [anaphylaxis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaphylaxis "Anaphylaxis").[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStvrtinov%C3%A1Jakubovsk%C3%BDHul%C3%ADn1995Chapter:_[httpswebarchiveorgweb20010711220523httpnicsavbasklogosbooksscientificInffeverhtml_Inflammation_and_Fever]-35)[Basophils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basophil "Basophil") and [eosinophils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eosinophil "Eosinophil") are related to neutrophils. They secrete chemical mediators that are involved in defending against [parasites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parasitism "Parasitism") and play a role in allergic reactions, such as [asthma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asthma "Asthma").[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-39)

Innate lymphoid cells (ILCs) are a group of [innate immune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Innate_immune_system "Innate immune system") cells that are derived from [common lymphoid progenitor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Common_lymphoid_progenitor "Common lymphoid progenitor") and belong to the [lymphoid lineage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lymphopoiesis "Lymphopoiesis"). These cells are defined by the absence of antigen-specific [B-](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B-cell_receptor "B-cell receptor") or [T-cell receptor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T-cell_receptor "T-cell receptor") (TCR) because of the lack of [recombination activating gene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recombination_activating_gene "Recombination activating gene"). ILCs do not express myeloid or dendritic cell markers.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-40)

[Natural killer cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_killer_cells "Natural killer cells") (NK cells) are lymphocytes and a component of the innate immune system that does not directly attack invading microbes.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-pmid28078307-41) Rather, NK cells destroy compromised host cells, such as tumor cells or virus-infected cells, recognizing such cells by a condition known as "missing self". This term describes cells with low levels of a cell-surface marker called MHC I ([major histocompatibility complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Major_histocompatibility_complex "Major histocompatibility complex"))—a situation that can arise in viral infections of host cells.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertokChow2005[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidDW3V2Uc-m8ECq22missingself22immunityimmunepgPA17_17]-42) Normal body cells are not recognized and attacked by NK cells because they express intact self MHC antigens. Those MHC antigens are recognized by killer cell immunoglobulin receptors, which essentially put the brakes on NK cells.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTERajalingam2012Chapter:_Overview_of_the_killer_cell_immunoglobulin-like_receptor_system-43)

Inflammation is one of the first responses of the immune system to infection.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-autogenerated2-44) The symptoms of inflammation are redness, swelling, heat, and pain, which are caused by increased blood flow into tissue. Inflammation is produced by [eicosanoids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eicosanoid "Eicosanoid") and [cytokines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytokine "Cytokine"), which are released by injured or infected cells. Eicosanoids include [prostaglandins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prostaglandin "Prostaglandin") that produce [fever](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fever "Fever") and the [dilation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vasodilator "Vasodilator") of [blood vessels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_vessel "Blood vessel") associated with inflammation and [leukotrienes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leukotriene "Leukotriene") that attract certain [white blood cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_blood_cell "White blood cell") (leukocytes).[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-autogenerated4-45)[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-autogenerated1-46) Common cytokines include [interleukins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interleukin "Interleukin") that are responsible for communication between white blood cells; [chemokines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemokine "Chemokine") that promote [chemotaxis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemotaxis "Chemotaxis"); and [interferons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interferon "Interferon") that have [antiviral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiviral_drug "Antiviral drug") effects, such as shutting down [protein synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_biosynthesis "Protein biosynthesis") in the host cell.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-autogenerated3-47)[Growth factors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Growth_factor "Growth factor") and cytotoxic factors may also be released. These cytokines and other chemicals recruit immune cells to the site of infection and promote the healing of any damaged tissue following the removal of pathogens.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-autogenerated5-48) The pattern-recognition receptors called [inflammasomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflammasome "Inflammasome") are multiprotein complexes (consisting of an NLR, the adaptor protein ASC, and the effector molecule pro-caspase-1) that form in response to cytosolic PAMPs and DAMPs, whose function is to generate active forms of the inflammatory cytokines IL-1β and IL-18.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-49)

The complement system is a [biochemical cascade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biochemical_cascade "Biochemical cascade") that attacks the surfaces of foreign cells. It contains over 20 different proteins and is named for its ability to "complement" the killing of pathogens by [antibodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antibody "Antibody"). Complement is the major humoral component of the innate immune response.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Rus-50)[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Degn_2013-51) Many species have complement systems, including non-[mammals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mammal "Mammal") like plants, fish, and some [invertebrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invertebrate "Invertebrate").[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBertokChow2005[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidDW3V2Uc-m8ECq22complementsystem22pgPA17_112%E2%80%93113]-52) In humans, this response is activated by complement binding to antibodies that have attached to these microbes or the binding of complement proteins to [carbohydrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbohydrate "Carbohydrate") on the surfaces of [microbes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbe "Microbe"). This recognition [signal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_signaling "Cell signaling") triggers a rapid killing response.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-53) The speed of the response is a result of signal amplification that occurs after sequential [proteolytic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proteolysis "Proteolysis") activation of complement molecules, which are also proteases. After complement proteins initially bind to the microbe, they activate their protease activity, which in turn activates other complement proteases, and so on. This produces a [catalytic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalysis "Catalysis") cascade that amplifies the initial signal by controlled [positive feedback](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive_feedback "Positive feedback").[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-54) The cascade results in the production of peptides that attract immune cells, increase [vascular permeability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vascular_permeability "Vascular permeability"), and [opsonize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opsonin "Opsonin") (coat) the surface of a pathogen, marking it for destruction. This deposition of complement can also kill cells directly by disrupting their [plasma membrane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_membrane "Cell membrane") via the formation of a [membrane attack complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complement_membrane_attack_complex "Complement membrane attack complex").[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Rus-50)

Adaptive immune system
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Immune_system&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Adaptive immune system")]

[![Image 3: diagram showing the processes of activation, cell destruction and digestion, antibody production and proliferation, and response memory](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Primary_immune_response_1.png/500px-Primary_immune_response_1.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Primary_immune_response_1.png)

Overview of the processes involved in the primary immune response

The adaptive immune system evolved in early vertebrates and allows for a stronger immune response as well as [immunological memory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunological_memory "Immunological memory"), where each pathogen is "remembered" by a signature antigen.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-55) The adaptive immune response is antigen-specific and requires the recognition of specific "non-self" antigens during a process called [antigen presentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antigen_presentation "Antigen presentation"). Antigen specificity allows for the generation of responses that are tailored to specific pathogens or pathogen-infected cells. The ability to mount these tailored responses is maintained in the body by "memory cells". Should a pathogen infect the body more than once, these specific memory cells are used to quickly eliminate it.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTESompayrac201938-56)

### Recognition of antigen

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Immune_system&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Recognition of antigen")]

The cells of the adaptive immune system are special types of leukocytes, called lymphocytes. [B cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B_cell "B cell") and T cells are the major types of lymphocytes and are derived from [hematopoietic stem cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hematopoietic_stem_cell "Hematopoietic stem cell") in the [bone marrow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bone_marrow "Bone marrow").[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJaneway2005-57) B cells are involved in the [humoral immune response](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humoral_immunity "Humoral immunity"), whereas T cells are involved in [cell-mediated immune response](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell-mediated_immunity "Cell-mediated immunity"). Killer T cells only recognize antigens coupled to [Class I MHC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Major_histocompatibility_complex#MHC_class_I "Major histocompatibility complex") molecules, while helper T cells and regulatory T cells only recognize antigens coupled to [Class II MHC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Major_histocompatibility_complex#MHC_class_II "Major histocompatibility complex") molecules. These two mechanisms of antigen presentation reflect the different roles of the two types of T cell. A third, minor subtype are the [γδ T cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma/delta_T_cells "Gamma/delta T cells") that recognize intact antigens that are not bound to MHC receptors.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Holtmeier_W,_Kabelitz_D_2005_151%E2%80%9383-58) The double-positive T cells are exposed to a wide variety of [self-antigens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-protein "Self-protein") in the [thymus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymus "Thymus"), in which [iodine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iodine "Iodine") is necessary for its thymus development and activity.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-pmid19647627-59) In contrast, the B cell antigen-specific receptor is an antibody molecule on the B cell surface and recognizes native (unprocessed) antigen without any need for [antigen processing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antigen_processing "Antigen processing"). Such antigens may be large molecules found on the surfaces of pathogens, but can also be small [haptens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hapten "Hapten") (such as penicillin) attached to carrier molecule.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJanewayTraversWalport2001sec._[httpswwwncbinlmnihgovbooksNBK27112A1746_12-10]-60) Each lineage of B cell expresses a different antibody, so the complete set of B cell antigen receptors represent all the antibodies that the body can manufacture.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJaneway2005-57) When B or T cells encounter their related antigens they multiply and many "clones" of the cells are produced that target the same antigen. This is called [clonal selection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clonal_selection "Clonal selection").[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTESompayrac20195%E2%80%936-61)

### Antigen presentation to T lymphocytes

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Immune_system&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Antigen presentation to T lymphocytes")]

Both B cells and T cells carry receptor molecules that recognize specific targets. T cells recognize a "non-self" target, such as a pathogen, only after antigens (small fragments of the pathogen) have been processed and presented in combination with a "self" receptor called a major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecule.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTESompayrac201951%E2%80%9353-62)

### Cell mediated immunity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Immune_system&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Cell mediated immunity")]

There are two major subtypes of T cells: the [killer T cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytotoxic_T_cell "Cytotoxic T cell") and the [helper T cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T_helper_cell "T helper cell"). In addition there are [regulatory T cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regulatory_T_cells "Regulatory T cells") which have a role in modulating immune response.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTESompayrac20197%E2%80%938-63)

[Killer T cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Killer_T_cells "Killer T cells") are a sub-group of T cells that kill cells that are infected with viruses (and other pathogens), or are otherwise damaged or dysfunctional.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-64) As with B cells, each type of T cell recognizes a different antigen. Killer T cells are activated when their [T-cell receptor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T-cell_receptor "T-cell receptor") binds to this specific antigen in a complex with the MHC Class I receptor of another cell. Recognition of this MHC:antigen complex is aided by a [co-receptor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Co-receptor "Co-receptor") on the T cell, called [CD8](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CD8 "CD8"). The T cell then travels throughout the body in search of cells where the MHC I receptors bear this antigen. When an activated T cell contacts such cells, it releases [cytotoxins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytotoxicity "Cytotoxicity"), such as [perforin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perforin "Perforin"), which form pores in the target cell's [plasma membrane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_membrane "Cell membrane"), allowing [ions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion "Ion"), water and toxins to enter. The entry of another toxin called [granulysin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Granulysin "Granulysin") (a protease) induces the target cell to undergo [apoptosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apoptosis "Apoptosis").[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Radoja-65) T cell killing of host cells is particularly important in preventing the replication of viruses. T cell activation is tightly controlled and generally requires a very strong MHC/antigen activation signal, or additional activation signals provided by "helper" T cells (see below).[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Radoja-65)

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/55/Activation_of_T_and_B_cells.png/500px-Activation_of_T_and_B_cells.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Activation_of_T_and_B_cells.png)

Activation of macrophage or B cell by T helper cell

[Helper T cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T_helper_cell "T helper cell") regulate both the innate and adaptive immune responses and help determine which immune responses the body makes to a particular pathogen.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-66)[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-67) These cells have no cytotoxic activity and do not kill infected cells or clear pathogens directly. They instead control the immune response by directing other cells to perform these tasks.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTESompayrac20198-68)

Helper T cells express T cell receptors that recognize antigen bound to Class II MHC molecules. The MHC:antigen complex is also recognized by the helper cell's [CD4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CD4 "CD4") co-receptor, which recruits molecules inside the T cell (such as [Lck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lck "Lck")) that are responsible for the T cell's activation. Helper T cells have a weaker association with the MHC:antigen complex than observed for killer T cells, meaning many receptors (around 200–300) on the helper T cell must be bound by an MHC:antigen to activate the helper cell, while killer T cells can be activated by engagement of a single MHC:antigen molecule. Helper T cell activation also requires longer duration of engagement with an antigen-presenting cell.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-69) The activation of a resting helper T cell causes it to release cytokines that influence the activity of many cell types. Cytokine signals produced by helper T cells enhance the microbicidal function of macrophages and the activity of killer T cells.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAlbertsJohnsonLewisRaff2002Chapter._[httpswwwncbinlmnihgovbooksNBK26827_%22Helper_T_Cells_and_Lymphocyte_Activation%22]-70) In addition, helper T cell activation causes an upregulation of molecules expressed on the T cell's surface, such as CD40 ligand (also called [CD154](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CD154 "CD154")), which provide extra stimulatory signals typically required to activate antibody-producing B cells.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-71)

#### Gamma delta T cells

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Immune_system&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Gamma delta T cells")]

[Gamma delta T cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_delta_T_cell "Gamma delta T cell") (γδ T cells) possess an alternative T-cell receptor (TCR) as opposed to CD4+ and CD8+ (αβ) T cells and share the characteristics of helper T cells, cytotoxic T cells and NK cells. The conditions that produce responses from γδ T cells are not fully understood. Like other 'unconventional' T cell subsets bearing invariant TCRs, such as [CD1d](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CD1d_receptor "CD1d receptor")-restricted [natural killer T cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_killer_T_cell "Natural killer T cell"), γδ T cells straddle the border between innate and adaptive immunity.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-72) On one hand, γδ T cells are a component of adaptive immunity as they [rearrange TCR genes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V(D)J_recombination "V(D)J recombination") to produce receptor diversity and can also develop a memory phenotype. On the other hand, the various subsets are also part of the innate immune system, as restricted TCR or NK receptors may be used as [pattern recognition receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pattern_recognition_receptor "Pattern recognition receptor"). For example, large numbers of human Vγ9/Vδ2 T cells respond within hours to [common molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-peptidic_antigen "Non-peptidic antigen") produced by microbes, and highly restricted Vδ1+ T cells in [epithelia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epithelium "Epithelium") respond to stressed epithelial cells.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Holtmeier_W,_Kabelitz_D_2005_151%E2%80%9383-58)

### Humoral immune response

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Immune_system&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Humoral immune response")]

[![Image 5: diagram showing the Y-shaped antibody. The variable region, including the antigen-binding site, is the top part of the two upper light chains. The remainder is the constant region.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/ba/2220_Four_Chain_Structure_of_a_Generic_Antibody-IgG2_Structures.jpg/500px-2220_Four_Chain_Structure_of_a_Generic_Antibody-IgG2_Structures.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:2220_Four_Chain_Structure_of_a_Generic_Antibody-IgG2_Structures.jpg)

An antibody is made up of two heavy chains and two light chains. The unique variable region allows an antibody to recognize its matching antigen.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-NIAID-73)

A [B cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B_cell "B cell") identifies pathogens when antibodies on its surface bind to a specific foreign antigen.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Sproul-74) This antigen/antibody complex is taken up by the B cell and processed by [proteolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proteolysis "Proteolysis") into [peptides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peptide "Peptide"). The B cell then displays these antigenic peptides on its surface MHC class II molecules. This combination of MHC and antigen attracts a matching helper T cell, which releases [lymphokines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lymphokine "Lymphokine") and activates the B cell.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-75) As the activated B cell then begins to [divide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_division "Cell division"), its offspring ([plasma cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_cells "Plasma cells")) [secrete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secretion "Secretion") millions of copies of the antibody that recognizes this antigen. These antibodies circulate in [blood plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_plasma "Blood plasma") and [lymph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lymphatic_system "Lymphatic system"), bind to pathogens expressing the antigen and mark them for destruction by [complement activation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complement_system "Complement system") or for uptake and destruction by [phagocytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phagocyte "Phagocyte"). Antibodies can also neutralize challenges directly, by binding to bacterial toxins or by interfering with the receptors that viruses and bacteria use to infect cells.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTEMurphyWeaver2016Chapter_10:_The_Humoral_Immune_Response-76)

Newborn infants have no prior exposure to microbes and are particularly vulnerable to infection. Several layers of passive protection are provided by the mother. During pregnancy, a particular type of antibody, called [IgG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunoglobulin_G "Immunoglobulin G"), is transported from mother to baby directly through the [placenta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Placenta "Placenta"), so human babies have high levels of antibodies even at birth, with the same range of antigen specificities as their mother.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-77) Breast milk or [colostrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colostrum "Colostrum") also contains antibodies that are transferred to the gut of the infant and protect against bacterial infections until the newborn can synthesize its own antibodies.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-78) This is [passive immunity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Passive_immunization "Passive immunization") because the [fetus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fetus "Fetus") does not actually make any memory cells or antibodies—it only borrows them. This passive immunity is usually short-term, lasting from a few days up to several months. In medicine, protective passive immunity can also be [transferred artificially](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intravenous_immunoglobulin "Intravenous immunoglobulin") from one individual to another.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Keller-79)

### Immunological memory

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Immune_system&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Immunological memory")]

When B cells and T cells are activated and begin to replicate, some of their offspring become long-lived memory cells. Throughout the lifetime of an animal, these memory cells remember each specific pathogen encountered and can mount a strong response if the pathogen is detected again. T-cells recognize pathogens by small protein-based infection signals, called antigens, that bind directly to T-cell surface receptors.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-80) B-cells use the protein, immunoglobulin, to recognize pathogens by their antigens.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-81) This is "adaptive" because it occurs during the lifetime of an individual as an adaptation to infection with that pathogen and prepares the immune system for future challenges. Immunological memory can be in the form of either passive short-term memory or active long-term memory.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTESompayrac201998-82)

Physiological regulation
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Immune_system&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Physiological regulation")]

[![Image 6: The initial response involves antibody and effector T-cells. The resulting protective immunity lasts for weeks. Immunological memory often lasts for years.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/26/Immune_response2.svg/410px-Immune_response2.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Immune_response2.svg)

The time-course of an immune response begins with the initial pathogen encounter, (or initial vaccination) and leads to the formation and maintenance of active immunological memory.

The immune system is involved in many aspects of physiological regulation in the body. The immune system interacts intimately with other systems, such as the [endocrine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endocrine_system "Endocrine system")[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-83)[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-84) and the [nervous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nervous_system "Nervous system")[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-85)[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-86)[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-87) systems. The immune system also plays a crucial role in [embryogenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embryogenesis "Embryogenesis") (development of the embryo), as well as in [tissue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tissue_(biology) "Tissue (biology)") repair and [regeneration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regeneration_(biology) "Regeneration (biology)").[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-pmid28542262-88)

[Hormones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hormone "Hormone") can act as [immunomodulators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunomodulators "Immunomodulators"), altering the sensitivity of the immune system. For example, [female sex hormones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Female_sex_hormones "Female sex hormones") are known [immunostimulators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunostimulator "Immunostimulator") of both adaptive[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWiraCrane-GodreauGrant2004Chapter:_Endocrine_regulation_of_the_mucosal_immune_system_in_the_female_reproductive_tract-89) and innate immune responses.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-90) Some autoimmune diseases such as [lupus erythematosus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lupus_erythematosus "Lupus erythematosus") strike women preferentially, and their onset often coincides with [puberty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puberty "Puberty"). By contrast, [male sex hormones](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Androgen "Androgen") such as [testosterone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Testosterone "Testosterone") seem to be [immunosuppressive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunosuppressive "Immunosuppressive").[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-91) Other hormones appear to regulate the immune system as well, most notably [prolactin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prolactin "Prolactin"), [growth hormone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Growth_hormone "Growth hormone") and [vitamin D](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitamin_D "Vitamin D").[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-92)[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-93)

Although early cellular studies suggested vitamin D might influence immune responses, more recent large-scale clinical trials and meta-analyses (2022–2024) have found that vitamin D supplementation can reduce the risk and severity of autoimmune diseases such as rheumatoid arthritis and multiple sclerosis, and may modestly reduce the incidence of acute respiratory tract infections and improve tuberculosis outcomes.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-94)[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-95)[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-96) A 2011 United States [Institute of Medicine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institute_of_Medicine "Institute of Medicine") report stated that "outcomes related to ... immune functioning and [autoimmune disorders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autoimmune_disorder "Autoimmune disorder"), and infections ... could not be linked reliably with calcium or vitamin D intake and were often conflicting."[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Ross_2011-97): 5

The immune system is affected by sleep and rest, and [sleep deprivation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sleep_deprivation "Sleep deprivation") is detrimental to immune function.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-98) Complex feedback loops involving [cytokines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytokines "Cytokines"), such as [interleukin-1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interleukin-1 "Interleukin-1") and [tumor necrosis factor-α](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tumor_necrosis_factor-%CE%B1 "Tumor necrosis factor-α") produced in response to infection, appear to also play a role in the regulation of non-rapid eye movement ([NREM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-rapid_eye_movement_sleep "Non-rapid eye movement sleep")) sleep.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-99) Thus the immune response to infection may result in changes to the sleep cycle, including an increase in [slow-wave sleep](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slow-wave_sleep "Slow-wave sleep") relative to rapid eye movement ([REM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rapid_eye_movement_sleep "Rapid eye movement sleep")) sleep.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-100)

In people with sleep deprivation, [active immunizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_immunization "Active immunization") may have a diminished effect and may result in lower antibody production, and a lower immune response, than would be noted in a well-rested individual.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-pmid27077395-101)[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-102) Additionally, proteins such as [NFIL3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NFIL3 "NFIL3"), which have been shown to be closely intertwined with both T-cell differentiation and [circadian rhythms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circadian_rhythm "Circadian rhythm"), can be affected through the disturbance of natural light and dark cycles through instances of sleep deprivation. These disruptions can lead to an increase in chronic conditions such as heart disease, chronic pain, and asthma.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-pmid19075717-103)

In addition to the negative consequences of sleep deprivation, sleep and the intertwined circadian system have been shown to have strong regulatory effects on immunological functions affecting both innate and adaptive immunity. First, during the early slow-wave-sleep stage, a sudden drop in blood levels of [cortisol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cortisol "Cortisol"), [epinephrine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epinephrine "Epinephrine"), and [norepinephrine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norepinephrine "Norepinephrine") causes increased blood levels of the hormones [leptin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leptin "Leptin"), [pituitary growth hormone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Growth_hormone_1 "Growth hormone 1"), and [prolactin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prolactin "Prolactin"). These signals induce a pro-inflammatory state through the production of the pro-inflammatory cytokines interleukin-1, [interleukin-12](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interleukin-12 "Interleukin-12"), [TNF-alpha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TNF-alpha "TNF-alpha") and [IFN-gamma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IFN-gamma "IFN-gamma"). These cytokines then stimulate immune functions such as immune cell activation, proliferation, and [differentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_differentiation "Cell differentiation"). During this time of a slowly evolving adaptive immune response, there is a peak in undifferentiated or less differentiated cells, like naïve and central memory T cells. In addition to these effects, the milieu of hormones produced at this time (leptin, pituitary growth hormone, and prolactin) supports the interactions between APCs and T-cells, a shift of the [T h 1/T h 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Th1_cell "Th1 cell") cytokine balance towards one that supports T h 1, an increase in overall T h cell proliferation, and naïve T cell migration to lymph nodes. This is also thought to support the formation of long-lasting immune memory through the initiation of Th1 immune responses.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Sleep_and_immune_function-104)

During wake periods, differentiated effector cells, such as cytotoxic natural killer cells and CD45RA+ cytotoxic T lymphocytes, peak in numbers. Anti-inflammatory molecules, such as cortisol and [catecholamines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catecholamine "Catecholamine"), also peak during awake active times. Inflammation can cause [oxidative stress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidative_stress "Oxidative stress") and the presence of melatonin during sleep times could counteract free radical production during this time.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Sleep_and_immune_function-104)[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-105)

Physical exercise has a positive effect on the immune system and depending on the frequency and intensity, the pathogenic effects of diseases caused by bacteria and viruses are moderated.[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-pmid32728975-106) Immediately after intense exercise there is a transient immunodepression, where the number of circulating lymphocytes decreases and antibody production declines. This may give rise to a window of opportunity for infection and reactivation of latent virus infections,[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-pmid27909225-107) but the evidence is inconclusive.[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-pmid29713319-108)[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-pmid32139352-109)

#### Changes at the cellular level

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Immune_system&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Changes at the cellular level")]

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/09/Neutrophils.jpg/250px-Neutrophils.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Neutrophils.jpg)

Four neutrophils in a [Giemsa-stained](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanowsky_stain "Romanowsky stain") blood film

During exercise there is an increase in circulating [white blood cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leukocytes "Leukocytes") of all types. This is caused by the frictional force of blood flowing on the [endothelial cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endothelial_cell "Endothelial cell") surface and [catecholamines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catecholamine "Catecholamine") affecting [β-adrenergic receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%92-adrenergic_receptor "Β-adrenergic receptor") (βARs).[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-pmid27909225-107) The number of [neutrophils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrophils "Neutrophils") in the blood increases and remains raised for up to six hours and [immature forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Left_shift_(medicine) "Left shift (medicine)") are present. Although the increase in neutrophils ("[neutrophilia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrophilia "Neutrophilia")") is similar to that seen during bacterial infections, after exercise the cell population returns to normal by around 24 hours.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-pmid27909225-107)

The number of circulating [lymphocytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lymphocyte "Lymphocyte") (mainly [natural killer cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_killer_cells "Natural killer cells")) decreases during intense exercise but returns to normal after 4 to 6 hours. Although up to 2% of the cells [die](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apoptosis "Apoptosis") most migrate from the blood to the tissues, mainly the intestines and lungs, where [pathogens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathogen "Pathogen") are most likely to be encountered.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-pmid27909225-107)

Some [monocytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monocyte "Monocyte") leave the blood circulation and migrate to the muscles where they differentiate and become [macrophages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macrophage "Macrophage").[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-pmid27909225-107) These cells differentiate into two types: proliferative macrophages, which are responsible for increasing the number of [stem cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Myogenesis "Myogenesis") and restorative macrophages, which are involved their maturing to muscle cells.[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-pmid34786967-110)

### Repair and regeneration

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Immune_system&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Repair and regeneration")]

The immune system, particularly the innate component, plays a decisive role in tissue repair after an [insult](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insult_(medical) "Insult (medical)"). Key actors include [macrophages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macrophage "Macrophage") and [neutrophils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrophil "Neutrophil"), but other cellular actors, including [γδ T cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_delta_T_cell "Gamma delta T cell"), [innate lymphoid cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Innate_lymphoid_cell "Innate lymphoid cell") (ILCs), and [regulatory T cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regulatory_T_cell "Regulatory T cell") (Tregs), are also important. The plasticity of immune cells and the balance between pro-inflammatory and anti-inflammatory signals are crucial aspects of efficient tissue repair. Immune components and pathways are involved in regeneration as well, for example in [amphibians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amphibian "Amphibian") such as in [axolotl limb regeneration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axolotl#Regeneration "Axolotl"). According to one hypothesis, organisms that can regenerate (_e.g._, [axolotls](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axolotl "Axolotl")) could be less immunocompetent than organisms that cannot regenerate.[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-111)

Disorders of human immunity
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Immune_system&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Disorders of human immunity")]

Failures of host defense occur and fall into three broad categories: immunodeficiencies,[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTESompayrac2019120%E2%80%9324-112) autoimmunity,[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTESompayrac2019114%E2%80%9318-113) and hypersensitivities.[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTESompayrac2019111%E2%80%9314-114)

[Immunodeficiencies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunodeficiency "Immunodeficiency") occur when one or more of the components of the immune system are inactive. The ability of the immune system to respond to pathogens is diminished in both the young and the [elderly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Old_age "Old age"), with immune responses beginning to decline at around 50 years of age due to [immunosenescence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunosenescence "Immunosenescence").[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-115)[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-nutrition-116) In [developed countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developed_country "Developed country"), [obesity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obesity "Obesity"), [alcoholism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohol_abuse "Alcohol abuse"), and drug use are common causes of poor immune function, while [malnutrition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malnutrition "Malnutrition") is the most common cause of immunodeficiency in [developing countries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developing_country "Developing country").[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-nutrition-116) Diets lacking sufficient protein are associated with impaired cell-mediated immunity, complement activity, phagocyte function, [IgA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunoglobulin_A "Immunoglobulin A") antibody concentrations, and cytokine production. Additionally, the loss of the [thymus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymus "Thymus") at an early age through [genetic mutation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutation "Mutation") or surgical removal results in severe immunodeficiency and a high susceptibility to infection.[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-117) Immunodeficiencies can also be inherited or '[acquired](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunodeficiency#Acquired_immune_deficiency "Immunodeficiency")'.[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReece2011967-118)[Severe combined immunodeficiency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Severe_combined_immunodeficiency "Severe combined immunodeficiency") is a rare [genetic disorder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_disorder "Genetic disorder") characterized by the disturbed development of functional T cells and B cells caused by numerous genetic mutations.[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-five-119)[Chronic granulomatous disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronic_granulomatous_disease "Chronic granulomatous disease"), where [phagocytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phagocyte "Phagocyte") have a reduced ability to destroy pathogens, is an example of an inherited, or [congenital, immunodeficiency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_immunodeficiency "Primary immunodeficiency"). [AIDS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AIDS "AIDS") and some types of [cancer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cancer "Cancer") cause acquired immunodeficiency.[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-120)[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-121)

[![Image 8: See caption](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/70/Rheumatoid_Arthritis.JPG/250px-Rheumatoid_Arthritis.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Rheumatoid_Arthritis.JPG)

Joints of a hand swollen and deformed by [rheumatoid arthritis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rheumatoid_arthritis "Rheumatoid arthritis"), an autoimmune disorder

Overactive immune responses form the other end of immune dysfunction, particularly the [autoimmune diseases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autoimmune_diseases "Autoimmune diseases"). Here, the immune system fails to properly distinguish between [self](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-protein "Self-protein") and non-self, and attacks part of the body. Under normal circumstances, many T cells and antibodies react with "self" peptides.[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-122) One of the functions of specialized cells (located in the [thymus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymus "Thymus") and bone marrow) is to present young lymphocytes with [self antigens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-protein "Self-protein") produced throughout the body and to eliminate those cells that recognize [self-antigens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-protein "Self-protein"), preventing autoimmunity.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Sproul-74) Common autoimmune diseases include [Hashimoto's thyroiditis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hashimoto%27s_thyroiditis "Hashimoto's thyroiditis"),[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-NIH2017-123)[rheumatoid arthritis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rheumatoid_arthritis "Rheumatoid arthritis"),[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Lancet2016-124)[diabetes mellitus type 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diabetes_mellitus_type_1 "Diabetes mellitus type 1"),[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Farhy_2015-125) and [systemic lupus erythematosus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systemic_lupus_erythematosus "Systemic lupus erythematosus").[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-NIH2015-126)

[Hypersensitivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersensitivity "Hypersensitivity") is an immune response that damages the body's own tissues. It is divided into four classes (Type I– IV) based on the mechanisms involved and the time course of the hypersensitive reaction. Type I hypersensitivity is an immediate or [anaphylactic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaphylaxis "Anaphylaxis") reaction, often associated with allergy. Symptoms can range from mild discomfort to death. Type I hypersensitivity is mediated by [IgE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunoglobulin_E "Immunoglobulin E"), which triggers degranulation of [mast cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mast_cell "Mast cell") and [basophils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basophil_granulocyte "Basophil granulocyte") when cross-linked by antigen.[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-USCH-127) Type II hypersensitivity occurs when antibodies bind to antigens on the individual's own cells, marking them for destruction. This is also called antibody-dependent (or cytotoxic) hypersensitivity, and is mediated by [IgG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunoglobulin_G "Immunoglobulin G") and [IgM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunoglobulin_M "Immunoglobulin M") antibodies.[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-USCH-127)[Immune complexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_complex "Immune complex") (aggregations of antigens, complement proteins, and IgG and IgM antibodies) deposited in various tissues trigger Type III hypersensitivity reactions.[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-USCH-127) Type IV hypersensitivity (also known as cell-mediated or _delayed type hypersensitivity_) usually takes between two and three days to develop. Type IV reactions are involved in many autoimmune and infectious diseases, but may also involve [contact dermatitis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contact_dermatitis "Contact dermatitis"). These reactions are mediated by [T cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T_cell "T cell"), [monocytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monocyte "Monocyte"), and [macrophages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macrophage "Macrophage").[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-USCH-127)

### Idiopathic inflammation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Immune_system&action=edit&section=28 "Edit section: Idiopathic inflammation")]

Inflammation is one of the first responses of the immune system to infection,[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-autogenerated2-44) but it can appear without known cause.

Inflammation is produced by [eicosanoids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eicosanoid "Eicosanoid") and [cytokines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytokine "Cytokine"), which are released by injured or infected cells. Eicosanoids include [prostaglandins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prostaglandin "Prostaglandin") that produce fever and the [dilation of blood vessels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vasodilation "Vasodilation") associated with inflammation, and [leukotrienes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leukotriene "Leukotriene") that attract certain white blood cells (leukocytes).[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-autogenerated4-45)[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-autogenerated1-46) Common cytokines include [interleukins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interleukin "Interleukin") that are responsible for communication between white blood cells; [chemokines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemokine "Chemokine") that promote [chemotaxis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemotaxis "Chemotaxis"); and [interferons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interferon "Interferon") that have anti-viral effects, such as shutting down [protein synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_biosynthesis "Protein biosynthesis") in the host cell.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-autogenerated3-47)[Growth factors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Growth_factor "Growth factor") and cytotoxic factors may also be released. These cytokines and other chemicals recruit immune cells to the site of infection and promote healing of any damaged tissue following the removal of pathogens.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-autogenerated5-48)

Manipulation in medicine
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Immune_system&action=edit&section=29 "Edit section: Manipulation in medicine")]

[![Image 9: Skeletal structural formula of dexamethasone, C22 H29 F O5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1f/Dexamethasone_structure.svg/250px-Dexamethasone_structure.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Dexamethasone_structure.svg)

Skeletal structural formula of the [immunosuppressive drug](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunosuppressive_drug "Immunosuppressive drug")[dexamethasone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dexamethasone "Dexamethasone")

The immune response can be manipulated to suppress unwanted responses resulting from autoimmunity, allergy, and [transplant rejection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transplant_rejection "Transplant rejection"), and to stimulate protective responses against pathogens that largely elude the immune system (see [immunization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#Active_memory_and_immunization)) or cancer.[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTESompayrac201983%E2%80%9385-128)

[Immunosuppressive drugs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunosuppressive_drug "Immunosuppressive drug") are used to control autoimmune disorders or [inflammation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflammation "Inflammation") when excessive tissue damage occurs, and to prevent rejection after an [organ transplant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organ_transplant "Organ transplant").[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTECiccone2015Chapter_[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidTe1vCAAAQBAJq22immunosuppressivedrugs22autoimmunetissuedamagepgPA625_37]-129)[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Taylor-130)

[Anti-inflammatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-inflammatory "Anti-inflammatory") drugs are often used to control the effects of inflammation. [Glucocorticoids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glucocorticoid "Glucocorticoid") are the most powerful of these drugs and can have many undesirable [side effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adverse_effect "Adverse effect"), such as [central obesity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_obesity "Central obesity"), [hyperglycemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperglycemia "Hyperglycemia"), and [osteoporosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osteoporosis "Osteoporosis").[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-131) Their use is tightly controlled. Lower doses of anti-inflammatory drugs are often used in conjunction with cytotoxic or immunosuppressive drugs such as [methotrexate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methotrexate "Methotrexate") or [azathioprine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azathioprine "Azathioprine").

[Cytotoxic drugs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemotherapy "Chemotherapy") inhibit the immune response by killing dividing cells such as activated T cells. This killing is indiscriminate and other [constantly dividing cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constantly_dividing_cells "Constantly dividing cells") and their organs are affected, which causes toxic side effects.[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Taylor-130) Immunosuppressive drugs such as [cyclosporin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclosporin "Cyclosporin") prevent T cells from responding to signals correctly by inhibiting [signal transduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signal_transduction "Signal transduction") pathways.[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-132)

Claims made by marketers of various products and [alternative health providers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternative_medicine "Alternative medicine"), such as [chiropractors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chiropractic "Chiropractic"), [homeopaths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeopathy "Homeopathy"), and [acupuncturists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acupuncture "Acupuncture") to be able to stimulate or "boost" the immune system generally lack meaningful explanation and evidence of effectiveness.[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-133)

[![Image 10: A child receiving drops of polio vaccine in her mouth](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/aa/Polio_Vaccination_-_Egypt_%2816868521330%29.jpg/250px-Polio_Vaccination_-_Egypt_%2816868521330%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Polio_Vaccination_-_Egypt_(16868521330).jpg)

Polio vaccination in Egypt

Long-term _active_ memory is acquired following infection by activation of B and T cells. Active immunity can also be generated artificially, through [vaccination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vaccination "Vaccination"). The principle behind vaccination (also called [immunization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunization "Immunization")) is to introduce an [antigen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antigen "Antigen") from a pathogen to stimulate the immune system and develop [specific immunity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_immunity "Specific immunity") against that particular pathogen without causing disease associated with that organism.[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReece2011965-134) This deliberate induction of an immune response is successful because it exploits the natural specificity of the immune system, as well as its inducibility. With infectious disease remaining one of the leading causes of death in the human population, vaccination represents the most effective manipulation of the immune system mankind has developed.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJaneway2005-57)[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-135)

Many vaccines are based on [acellular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-cellular_life "Non-cellular life") components of micro-organisms, including harmless [toxin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxin "Toxin") components.[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTEReece2011965-134) Since many antigens derived from acellular vaccines do not strongly induce the adaptive response, most bacterial vaccines are provided with additional [adjuvants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunologic_adjuvant "Immunologic adjuvant") that activate the [antigen-presenting cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antigen-presenting_cell "Antigen-presenting cell") of the innate immune system and maximize [immunogenicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunogenicity "Immunogenicity").[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-136)

Another important role of the immune system is to identify and eliminate [tumors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tumor "Tumor"). This is called **immune surveillance**. The _transformed cells_ of tumors express [antigens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antigen#Tumor_antigens "Antigen") that are not found on normal cells. To the immune system, these antigens appear foreign, and their presence causes immune cells to attack the transformed tumor cells. The antigens expressed by tumors have several sources;[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-anderson-137) some are derived from [oncogenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oncogenic "Oncogenic") viruses like [human papillomavirus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_papillomavirus "Human papillomavirus"), which causes cancer of the [cervix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cervical_cancer "Cervical cancer"),[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-138)[vulva](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vulva_cancer "Vulva cancer"), [vagina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vaginal_cancer "Vaginal cancer"), [penis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Penis_cancer "Penis cancer"), [anus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anal_cancer "Anal cancer"), [mouth, and throat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oropharynx "Oropharynx"),[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Lj2014-139) while others are the organism's own proteins that occur at low levels in normal cells but reach high levels in tumor cells. One example is an enzyme called [tyrosinase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tyrosinase "Tyrosinase") that, when expressed at high levels, transforms certain skin cells (for example, [melanocytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melanocyte "Melanocyte")) into tumors called [melanomas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melanoma "Melanoma").[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-140)[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-romera-141) A third possible source of [tumor antigens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tumor_antigen "Tumor antigen") are proteins normally important for regulating [cell growth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_growth "Cell growth") and survival, that commonly mutate into cancer inducing molecules called [oncogenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oncogene "Oncogene").[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-anderson-137)[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-guevara-142)[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-143)

[![Image 11: See caption](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/56/Macs_killing_cancer_cell.jpg/330px-Macs_killing_cancer_cell.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Macs_killing_cancer_cell.jpg)

[Macrophages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macrophage "Macrophage") have identified a cancer cell (the large, spiky mass). Upon fusing with the cancer cell, the macrophages (smaller white cells) inject toxins that kill the tumor cell. [Immunotherapy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunotherapy "Immunotherapy") for the treatment of [cancer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cancer#Immunotherapy "Cancer") is an active area of medical research.[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-144)

The main response of the immune system to tumors is to destroy the abnormal cells using killer T cells, sometimes with the assistance of helper T cells.[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-romera-141)[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-145)[Tumor antigens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tumor_antigen "Tumor antigen") are presented on MHC class I molecules in a similar way to viral antigens. This allows killer T cells to recognize the tumor cell as abnormal.[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-seliger-146) NK cells also kill tumorous cells in a similar way, especially if the tumor cells have fewer MHC class I molecules on their surface than normal; this is a common phenomenon with tumors.[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-147) Sometimes antibodies are generated against tumor cells allowing for their destruction by the [complement system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complement_system "Complement system").[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-guevara-142)

Some tumors evade the immune system and go on to become cancers.[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Syn-2017-148)[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-selig-149) Tumor cells often have a reduced number of MHC class I molecules on their surface, thus avoiding detection by killer T cells.[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-seliger-146)[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Syn-2017-148) Some tumor cells also release products that inhibit the immune response; for example by secreting the cytokine [TGF-β](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TGF_beta "TGF beta"), which suppresses the activity of [macrophages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macrophage "Macrophage") and [lymphocytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lymphocyte "Lymphocyte").[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Syn-2017-148)[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-150) In addition, [immunological tolerance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_tolerance "Immune tolerance") may develop against tumor antigens, so the immune system no longer attacks the tumor cells.[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Syn-2017-148)[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-selig-149)

Paradoxically, macrophages can promote tumor growth[[151]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-151) when tumor cells send out cytokines that attract macrophages, which then generate cytokines and growth factors such as [tumor-necrosis factor alpha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tumor_necrosis_factor_alpha "Tumor necrosis factor alpha") that nurture tumor development or promote stem-cell-like plasticity.[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Syn-2017-148) In addition, a combination of hypoxia in the tumor and a cytokine produced by macrophages induces tumor cells to decrease production of a protein that blocks [metastasis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metastasis "Metastasis") and thereby assists spread of cancer cells.[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Syn-2017-148) Anti-tumor M1 macrophages are recruited in early phases to tumor development but are progressively differentiated to M2 with pro-tumor effect, an immunosuppressor switch. The hypoxia reduces the cytokine production for the anti-tumor response and progressively macrophages acquire pro-tumor M2 functions driven by the tumor microenvironment, including IL-4 and IL-10.[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-152)[Cancer immunotherapy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cancer_immunotherapy "Cancer immunotherapy") covers the medical ways to stimulate the immune system to attack cancer tumors.[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-pmid26325031-153)

### Predicting immunogenicity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Immune_system&action=edit&section=34 "Edit section: Predicting immunogenicity")]

Some drugs can cause a neutralizing immune response, meaning that the immune system produces [neutralizing antibodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutralizing_antibodies "Neutralizing antibodies") that counteract the action of the drugs, particularly if the drugs are administered repeatedly, or in larger doses. This limits the effectiveness of drugs based on larger peptides and proteins (which are typically larger than 6000 [Da](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dalton_(unit) "Dalton (unit)")).[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Baker2010-154) In some cases, the drug itself is not immunogenic, but may be co-administered with an immunogenic compound, as is sometimes the case for [Taxol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paclitaxel "Paclitaxel"). Computational methods have been developed to predict the immunogenicity of peptides and proteins, which are particularly useful in designing therapeutic antibodies, assessing likely virulence of mutations in viral coat particles, and validation of proposed peptide-based drug treatments. Early techniques relied mainly on the observation that [hydrophilic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrophile "Hydrophile")[amino acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid "Amino acid") are overrepresented in [epitope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epitope "Epitope") regions than [hydrophobic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrophobe "Hydrophobe") amino acids;[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Welling-155) however, more recent developments rely on [machine learning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine_learning "Machine learning") techniques using databases of existing known epitopes, usually on well-studied virus proteins, as a [training set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Training_set "Training set").[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Sollner-156) A publicly accessible database has been established for the cataloguing of epitopes from pathogens known to be recognizable by B cells.[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Saha-157) The emerging field of [bioinformatics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bioinformatics "Bioinformatics")-based studies of immunogenicity is referred to as _[immunoinformatics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunoinformatics "Immunoinformatics")_.[[158]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Flower-158)[Immunoproteomics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunoproteomics "Immunoproteomics") is the study of large sets of proteins ([proteomics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proteomics "Proteomics")) involved in the immune response.[[159]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-pmid31356379-159)

Evolution and other mechanisms
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Immune_system&action=edit&section=35 "Edit section: Evolution and other mechanisms")]

### Evolution of the immune system

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Immune_system&action=edit&section=36 "Edit section: Evolution of the immune system")]

It is likely that a multicomponent, adaptive immune system arose with the first [vertebrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertebrate "Vertebrate"), as [invertebrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invertebrate "Invertebrate") do not generate lymphocytes or an antibody-based humoral response.[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-pmid19997068-160) Immune systems evolved in [deuterostomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuterostome "Deuterostome") as shown in the cladogram.[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-pmid19997068-160)

Many species, however, use mechanisms that appear to be precursors of these aspects of vertebrate immunity. Immune systems appear even in the structurally simplest forms of life, with bacteria using a unique defense mechanism, called the [restriction modification system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Restriction_modification_system "Restriction modification system") to protect themselves from viral pathogens, called [bacteriophages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteriophage "Bacteriophage").[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-161)[Prokaryotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prokaryote "Prokaryote") ([bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria "Bacteria") and [archea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archea "Archea")) also possess acquired immunity, through a system that uses [CRISPR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CRISPR "CRISPR") sequences to retain fragments of the genomes of phage that they have come into contact with in the past, which allows them to block virus replication through a form of [RNA interference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA_interference "RNA interference").[[162]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-162)[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-163) Prokaryotes also possess other defense mechanisms.[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-164)[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-165) Offensive elements of the immune systems are also present in [unicellular eukaryotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protist "Protist"), but studies of their roles in defense are few.[[166]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-166)

[Pattern recognition receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pattern_recognition_receptor "Pattern recognition receptor") are proteins used by nearly all organisms to identify molecules associated with pathogens. [Antimicrobial peptides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antimicrobial_peptides "Antimicrobial peptides") called [defensins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Defensin "Defensin") are an evolutionarily conserved component of the innate immune response found in all animals and plants, and represent the main form of invertebrate systemic immunity.[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-pmid19997068-160) The [complement system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complement_system "Complement system") and phagocytic cells are also used by most forms of invertebrate life. [Ribonucleases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ribonuclease "Ribonuclease") and the [RNA interference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA_interference "RNA interference") pathway are conserved across all [eukaryotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryote "Eukaryote"), and are thought to play a role in the immune response to viruses.[[167]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-167)

Unlike animals, plants lack phagocytic cells, but many plant immune responses involve systemic chemical signals that are sent through a plant.[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Plant-168) Individual plant cells respond to molecules associated with pathogens known as [pathogen-associated molecular patterns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathogen-associated_molecular_patterns "Pathogen-associated molecular patterns") or PAMPs.[[169]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-169) When a part of a plant becomes infected, the plant produces a localized [hypersensitive response](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersensitive_response "Hypersensitive response"), whereby cells at the site of infection undergo rapid [apoptosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apoptosis "Apoptosis") to prevent the spread of the disease to other parts of the plant. [Systemic acquired resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systemic_acquired_resistance "Systemic acquired resistance") is a type of defensive response used by plants that renders the entire plant resistant to a particular infectious agent.[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Plant-168)[RNA silencing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RNA_interference "RNA interference") mechanisms are particularly important in this systemic response as they can block [virus replication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virus_replication "Virus replication").[[170]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-170)

### Alternative adaptive immune system

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Immune_system&action=edit&section=37 "Edit section: Alternative adaptive immune system")]

[Evolution of the adaptive immune system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptive_immune_system#Evolution "Adaptive immune system") occurred in an ancestor of the [jawed vertebrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jawed_vertebrates "Jawed vertebrates"). Many of the classical molecules of the adaptive immune system (for example, [immunoglobulins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunoglobulin "Immunoglobulin") and [T-cell receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T-cell_receptor "T-cell receptor")) exist only in jawed vertebrates. A distinct [lymphocyte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lymphocyte "Lymphocyte")-derived molecule has been discovered in primitive [jawless vertebrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agnatha "Agnatha"), such as the [lamprey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lamprey "Lamprey") and [hagfish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hagfish "Hagfish"). These animals possess a large array of molecules called [Variable lymphocyte receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_lymphocyte_receptor "Variable lymphocyte receptor") (VLRs) that, like the antigen receptors of jawed vertebrates, are produced from only a small number (one or two) of [genes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene "Gene"). These molecules are believed to bind pathogenic [antigens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antigen "Antigen") in a similar way to [antibodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antibody "Antibody"), and with the same degree of specificity.[[171]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-171)

### Manipulation by pathogens

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Immune_system&action=edit&section=38 "Edit section: Manipulation by pathogens")]

The success of any pathogen depends on its ability to elude host immune responses. Therefore, pathogens evolved several methods that allow them to successfully infect a host, while evading detection or destruction by the immune system.[[172]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Finlay-172) Bacteria often overcome physical barriers by secreting enzymes that digest the barrier, for example, by using a [type II secretion system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_II_secretion_system "Type II secretion system").[[173]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-173) Alternatively, using a [type III secretion system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_III_secretion_system "Type III secretion system"), they may insert a hollow tube into the host cell, providing a direct route for proteins to move from the pathogen to the host. These proteins are often used to shut down host defenses.[[174]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-174)

An evasion strategy used by several pathogens to avoid the innate immune system is to hide within the cells of their host (also called [intracellular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracellular "Intracellular")[pathogenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathogenesis "Pathogenesis")). Here, a pathogen spends most of its [life-cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_life_cycle "Biological life cycle") inside host cells, where it is shielded from direct contact with immune cells, antibodies and complement. Some examples of intracellular pathogens include viruses, the [food poisoning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foodborne_illness "Foodborne illness") bacterium _[Salmonella](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salmonella "Salmonella")_ and the [eukaryotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryote "Eukaryote") parasites that cause [malaria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malaria "Malaria") (_[Plasmodium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasmodium "Plasmodium") spp._) and [leishmaniasis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leishmaniasis "Leishmaniasis") (_[Leishmania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leishmania "Leishmania") spp._). Other bacteria, such as _[Mycobacterium tuberculosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mycobacterium\_tuberculosis "Mycobacterium tuberculosis")_, live inside a protective capsule that prevents [lysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lysis "Lysis") by complement.[[175]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-175) Many pathogens secrete compounds that diminish or misdirect the host's immune response.[[172]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Finlay-172) Some bacteria form [biofilms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biofilm "Biofilm") to protect themselves from the cells and proteins of the immune system. Such biofilms are present in many successful infections, such as the chronic _[Pseudomonas aeruginosa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudomonas\_aeruginosa "Pseudomonas aeruginosa")_ and _[Burkholderia cenocepacia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Burkholderia\_cenocepacia "Burkholderia cenocepacia")_ infections characteristic of [cystic fibrosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cystic_fibrosis "Cystic fibrosis").[[176]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-176) Other bacteria generate surface proteins that bind to antibodies, rendering them ineffective; examples include _[Streptococcus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Streptococcus "Streptococcus")_ (protein G), _[Staphylococcus aureus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Staphylococcus\_aureus "Staphylococcus aureus")_ (protein A), and _[Peptostreptococcus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peptostreptococcus "Peptostreptococcus") magnus_ (protein L).[[177]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-177)

The mechanisms used to evade the adaptive immune system are more complicated. The simplest approach is to rapidly change non-essential [epitopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epitope "Epitope") ([amino acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid "Amino acid") and/or sugars) on the surface of the pathogen, while keeping essential epitopes concealed. This is called [antigenic variation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antigenic_variation "Antigenic variation"). An example is HIV, which mutates rapidly, so the proteins on its [viral envelope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viral_envelope "Viral envelope") that are essential for entry into its host target cell are constantly changing. These frequent changes in antigens may explain the failures of [vaccines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vaccine "Vaccine") directed at this virus.[[178]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-178) The parasite _[Trypanosoma brucei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trypanosoma\_brucei "Trypanosoma brucei")_ uses a similar strategy, constantly switching one type of surface protein for another, allowing it to stay one step ahead of the antibody response.[[179]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-179) Masking antigens with host molecules is another common strategy for avoiding detection by the immune system. In HIV, the envelope that covers the [virion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virus "Virus") is formed from the outermost membrane of the host cell; such "self-cloaked" viruses make it difficult for the immune system to identify them as "non-self" structures.[[180]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-180)

History of immunology
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Immune_system&action=edit&section=39 "Edit section: History of immunology")]

[![Image 12: Portrait of an older, thin man with a beard wearing glasses and dressed in a suit and tie](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/05/Paul_Ehrlich_1915.jpg/250px-Paul_Ehrlich_1915.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Paul_Ehrlich_1915.jpg)

[Paul Ehrlich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Ehrlich "Paul Ehrlich") (1854–1915) was awarded a Nobel Prize in 1908 for his contributions to immunology.[[181]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-1908Nobel-181)

[Immunology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immunology "Immunology") is a science that examines the structure and function of the immune system. It originates from [medicine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medicine "Medicine") and early studies on the causes of immunity to disease. The earliest known reference to immunity was during the [plague of Athens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plague_of_Athens "Plague of Athens") in 430 BC. [Thucydides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thucydides "Thucydides") noted that people who had recovered from a previous bout of the disease could nurse the sick without contracting the illness a second time.[[182]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-182) In the 18th century, [Pierre-Louis Moreau de Maupertuis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Louis_Maupertuis "Pierre Louis Maupertuis") experimented with scorpion venom and observed that certain dogs and mice were immune to this venom.[[183]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-183) In the 10th century, Persian physician [al-Razi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muhammad_ibn_Zakariya_al-Razi "Muhammad ibn Zakariya al-Razi") (also known as Rhazes) wrote the first recorded theory of acquired immunity,[[184]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-early_trends-184)[[185]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTESilverstein19896-185) noting that a [smallpox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smallpox "Smallpox") bout protected its survivors from future infections. Although he explained the immunity in terms of "excess moisture" being expelled from the blood—therefore preventing a second occurrence of the disease—this theory explained many observations about smallpox known during this time.[[186]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-FOOTNOTESilverstein19897-186)

These and other observations of acquired immunity were later exploited by [Louis Pasteur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Pasteur "Louis Pasteur") in his development of vaccination and his proposed [germ theory of disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germ_theory_of_disease "Germ theory of disease").[[187]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-187) Pasteur's theory was in direct opposition to contemporary theories of disease, such as the [miasma theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miasma_theory_of_disease "Miasma theory of disease"). It was not until [Robert Koch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Koch "Robert Koch")'s 1891 [proofs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koch%27s_postulates "Koch's postulates"), for which he was awarded a [Nobel Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine "Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine") in 1905, that microorganisms were confirmed as the cause of [infectious disease](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infectious_disease "Infectious disease").[[188]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-188) Viruses were confirmed as human pathogens in 1901, with the discovery of the [yellow fever](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yellow_fever "Yellow fever") virus by [Walter Reed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Reed "Walter Reed").[[189]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-189)

Immunology made a great advance towards the end of the 19th century, through rapid developments in the study of [humoral immunity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humoral_immunity "Humoral immunity") and [cellular immunity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell-mediated_immunity "Cell-mediated immunity").[[190]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-Metch-190) Particularly important was the work of [Paul Ehrlich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Ehrlich "Paul Ehrlich"), who proposed the [side-chain theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Side-chain_theory "Side-chain theory") to explain the specificity of the [antigen-antibody reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antigen-antibody_reaction "Antigen-antibody reaction"); his contributions to the understanding of humoral immunity were recognized by the award of a joint Nobel Prize in 1908, along with the founder of cellular immunology, [Elie Metchnikoff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elie_Metchnikoff "Elie Metchnikoff").[[181]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-1908Nobel-181) In 1974, [Niels Kaj Jerne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niels_Kaj_Jerne "Niels Kaj Jerne") developed the [immune network theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_network_theory "Immune network theory"); he shared a Nobel Prize in 1984 with [Georges J. F. Köhler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georges_J._F._K%C3%B6hler "Georges J. F. Köhler") and [César Milstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_Milstein "César Milstein") for theories related to the immune system.[[191]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-191)[[192]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immune_system#cite_note-192)
