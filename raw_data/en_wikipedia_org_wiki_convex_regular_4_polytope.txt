The [tesseract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract "Tesseract") is one of 6 convex regular 4-polytopes

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **regular 4-polytope** or **regular polychoron** is a [regular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polytope "Regular polytope")[four-dimensional polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-polytope "4-polytope"). They are the four-dimensional analogues of the [regular polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polyhedron "Regular polyhedron") in three dimensions and the [regular polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon") in two dimensions.

There are six [convex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polytope "Convex polytope") and ten [star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_polytope "Star polytope") regular 4-polytopes, giving a total of sixteen.

The convex regular 4-polytopes were first described by the Swiss [mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician")[Ludwig Schläfli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Schl%C3%A4fli "Ludwig Schläfli") in the mid-19th century. He discovered that there are precisely six such figures.

Schläfli also found four of the regular star 4-polytopes: the [grand 120-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_120-cell "Grand 120-cell"), [great stellated 120-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_stellated_120-cell "Great stellated 120-cell"), [grand 600-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_600-cell "Grand 600-cell"), and [great grand stellated 120-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_grand_stellated_120-cell "Great grand stellated 120-cell"). He skipped the remaining six because he would not allow forms that failed the [Euler characteristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_characteristic "Euler characteristic") on cells or vertex figures (for zero-hole tori: _F_−_E_+_V_=2). That excludes cells and vertex figures such as the [great dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_dodecahedron "Great dodecahedron") {5,⁠5/2⁠} and [small stellated dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_stellated_dodecahedron "Small stellated dodecahedron") {⁠5/2⁠,5}.

[Edmund Hess](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edmund_Hess "Edmund Hess") (1843–1903) published the complete list in his 1883 German book _Einleitung in die Lehre von der Kugelteilung mit besonderer Berücksichtigung ihrer Anwendung auf die Theorie der Gleichflächigen und der gleicheckigen Polyeder_.

The existence of a regular 4-polytope ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \{p,q,r\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cbbe3060fead13d62d162d182719dff8c002e0f2) is constrained by the existence of the regular polyhedra ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \{p,q\},\{q,r\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e610d5565b8c77bd1a21e9358eba045186d591e0) which form its cells and a [dihedral angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihedral_angle "Dihedral angle") constraint

![Image 4: {\displaystyle \sin {\frac {\pi }{p}}\sin {\frac {\pi }{r}}>\cos {\frac {\pi }{q}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/173f0fb1f6aef9edd05904fabcb74b66911d65fa)
to ensure that the cells meet to form a closed 3-surface.

The six convex and ten star polytopes described are the only solutions to these constraints.

There are four nonconvex [Schläfli symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_symbol "Schläfli symbol") {p,q,r} that have valid cells {p,q} and vertex figures {q,r}, and pass the dihedral test, but fail to produce finite figures: {3,⁠5/2⁠,3}, {4,3,⁠5/2⁠}, {⁠5/2⁠,3,4}, {⁠5/2⁠,3,⁠5/2⁠}.

Regular convex 4-polytopes
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Regular_4-polytope&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Regular convex 4-polytopes")]

The regular convex 4-polytopes are the four-dimensional analogues of the [Platonic solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid "Platonic solid") in three dimensions and the convex [regular polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon") in two dimensions.

Each convex regular 4-polytope is bounded by a set of 3-dimensional _[cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell\_(mathematics) "Cell (mathematics)")_ which are all Platonic solids of the same type and size. These are fitted together along their respective faces (face-to-face) in a regular fashion, forming the _surface_ of the 4-polytope which is a closed, curved 3-dimensional space (analogous to the way the surface of the earth is a closed, curved 2-dimensional space).

Like their 3-dimensional analogues, the convex regular 4-polytopes can be naturally ordered by size as a measure of 4-dimensional content (hypervolume) for the same radius. Each greater polytope in the sequence is _rounder_ than its predecessor, enclosing more content within the same radius.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_regular_4-polytope#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoxeter1973292%E2%80%93293Table_I(ii):_The_sixteen_regular_polytopes_{''p,q,r''}_in_four_dimensions-2) The 4-simplex (5-cell) has the smallest content, and the 120-cell has the largest.

[John Conway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Horton_Conway "John Horton Conway") advocated the names simplex, orthoplex, tesseract, octaplex or polyoctahedron (pO), tetraplex or polytetrahedron (pT), and dodecaplex or polydodecahedron (pD).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_regular_4-polytope#cite_note-3)

[Norman Johnson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norman_Johnson_(mathematician) "Norman Johnson (mathematician)") advocated the names n-cell, or pentachoron, hexadecachoron, tesseract or octachoron, icositetrachoron, hexacosichoron, and hecatonicosachoron (or dodecacontachoron), coining the term _polychoron_ being a 4D analogy to the 3D polyhedron, and 2D polygon, expressed from the [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language") roots _poly_ ("many") and _choros_ ("room" or "space").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_regular_4-polytope#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_regular_4-polytope#cite_note-5)

The [Euler characteristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_characteristic "Euler characteristic") for all 4-polytopes is zero, we have the 4-dimensional analogue of Euler's polyhedral formula:

![Image 137: {\displaystyle N_{0}-N_{1}+N_{2}-N_{3}=0\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11997b1ce7e771ba18f7ee3025ac2e464b5d759c)
where _N_ _k_ denotes the number of _k_-faces in the polytope (a vertex is a 0-face, an edge is a 1-face, etc.).

The topology of any given 4-polytope is defined by its [Betti numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Betti_number "Betti number") and [torsion coefficients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_coefficient_(topology) "Torsion coefficient (topology)").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_regular_4-polytope#cite_note-richeson-6)

A regular 4-polytope can be completely described as a [configuration matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_(polytope) "Configuration (polytope)") containing counts of its component elements. The rows and columns correspond to vertices, edges, faces, and cells. The diagonal numbers (upper left to lower right) say how many of each element occur in the whole 4-polytope. The non-diagonal numbers say how many of the column's element occur in or at the row's element. For example, there are 2 vertices _in_ each edge (each edge _has_ 2 vertices), and 2 cells meet _at_ each face (each face _belongs to_ 2 cells), in any regular 4-polytope. The configuration for the dual polytope can be obtained by rotating the matrix by 180 degrees.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_regular_4-polytope#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_regular_4-polytope#cite_note-8)

Regular star (Schläfli–Hess) 4-polytopes
----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Regular_4-polytope&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Regular star (Schläfli–Hess) 4-polytopes")]

[![Image 204](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Relationship_among_regular_star_polychora.png/330px-Relationship_among_regular_star_polychora.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Relationship_among_regular_star_polychora.png)

This shows the relationships among the four-dimensional starry polytopes. The 2 convex forms and 10 starry forms can be seen in 3D as the vertices of a [cuboctahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuboctahedron "Cuboctahedron").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_regular_4-polytope#cite_note-9)

[![Image 205](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Relationship_among_regular_star_polychora-8.png/330px-Relationship_among_regular_star_polychora-8.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Relationship_among_regular_star_polychora-8.png)

A subset of relations among 8 forms from the 120-cell, polydodecahedron (pD). The three operations {a,g,s} are commutable, defining a cubic framework. There are 7 [densities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density_(geometry) "Density (geometry)") seen in vertical positioning, with 2 dual forms having the same density.

The **Schläfli–Hess 4-polytopes** are the complete set of 10 [regular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polytope "Regular polytope") self-intersecting [**star polychora**](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_polytope "Star polytope") ([four-dimensional polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-polytope "4-polytope")).[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_regular_4-polytope#cite_note-10) They are named in honor of their discoverers: [Ludwig Schläfli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Schl%C3%A4fli "Ludwig Schläfli") and [Edmund Hess](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edmund_Hess "Edmund Hess"). Each is represented by a [Schläfli symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_symbol "Schläfli symbol") {_p_,_q_,_r_} in which one of the numbers is [⁠5/2⁠](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagram "Pentagram"). They are thus analogous to the regular nonconvex [Kepler–Poinsot polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%E2%80%93Poinsot_polyhedron "Kepler–Poinsot polyhedron"), which are in turn analogous to the pentagram.

Their names given here were given by [John Conway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Horton_Conway "John Horton Conway"), extending [Cayley's](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Cayley "Arthur Cayley") names for the [Kepler–Poinsot polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%E2%80%93Poinsot_polyhedra "Kepler–Poinsot polyhedra"): along with _stellated_ and _great_, he adds a _grand_ modifier. Conway offered these operational definitions:

1.   **[stellation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellation "Stellation")** – replaces edges with longer edges in same lines. (Example: a [pentagon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagon "Pentagon") stellates into a [pentagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagram "Pentagram"))
2.   **greatening** – replaces the faces with large ones in same planes. (Example: an [icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron "Regular icosahedron") greatens into a [great icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_icosahedron "Great icosahedron"))
3.   **aggrandizement** – replaces the cells with large ones in same 3-spaces. (Example: a [600-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/600-cell "600-cell") aggrandizes into a [grand 600-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_600-cell "Grand 600-cell"))

John Conway names the 10 forms from 3 regular celled 4-polytopes: pT=polytetrahedron {3,3,5} (a tetrahedral [600-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/600-cell "600-cell")), pI=polyicosahedron {3,5,⁠5/2⁠} (an [icosahedral 120-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedral_120-cell "Icosahedral 120-cell")), and pD=polydodecahedron {5,3,3} (a dodecahedral [120-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/120-cell "120-cell")), with prefix modifiers: _g_, _a_, and _s_ for great, (ag)grand, and stellated. The final stellation, the _great grand stellated polydodecahedron_ contains them all as _gaspD_.

All ten polychora have [3,3,5] ([H 4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group#Finite_Coxeter_groups "Coxeter group")) [hexacosichoric symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexacosichoric_symmetry "Hexacosichoric symmetry"). They are generated from 6 related [Goursat tetrahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goursat_tetrahedron "Goursat tetrahedron")[rational-order symmetry groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goursat_tetrahedron#Rational_solutions "Goursat tetrahedron"): [3,5,5/2], [5,5/2,5], [5,3,5/2], [5/2,5,5/2], [5,5/2,3], and [3,3,5/2].

Each group has 2 regular star-polychora, except for two groups which are self-dual, having only one. So there are 4 dual-pairs and 2 self-dual forms among the ten regular star polychora.

Note:

*   There are 2 unique [vertex arrangements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_arrangement "Vertex arrangement"), matching those of the [120-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/120-cell "120-cell") and [600-cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/600-cell "600-cell").
*   There are 4 unique [edge arrangements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_arrangement "Edge arrangement"), which are shown as _wireframes_[orthographic projections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthographic_projection_(geometry) "Orthographic projection (geometry)").
*   There are 7 unique [face arrangements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_arrangement "Face arrangement"), shown as _solids_ (face-colored) orthographic projections.

The cells (polyhedra), their faces (polygons), the _polygonal [edge figures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge\_figure "Edge figure")_ and _polyhedral [vertex figures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex\_figure "Vertex figure")_ are identified by their [Schläfli symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_symbol "Schläfli symbol").
