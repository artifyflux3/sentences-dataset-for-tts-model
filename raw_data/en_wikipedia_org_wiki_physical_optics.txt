From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Branch of optics

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/42/Laser_Interference.JPG/250px-Laser_Interference.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Laser_Interference.JPG)

Physical optics is used to explain effects such as [diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffraction "Diffraction")

In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), **physical optics**, or **wave optics**, is the branch of [optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optics "Optics") that studies [interference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interference_(wave_propagation) "Interference (wave propagation)"), [diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffraction "Diffraction"), [polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polarization_(waves) "Polarization (waves)"), and other phenomena for which the ray approximation of [geometric optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_optics "Geometric optics") is not valid. This usage tends not to include effects such as [quantum noise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_noise "Quantum noise") in [optical communication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_communication "Optical communication"), which is studied in the sub-branch of [coherence theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coherence_theory_(optics) "Coherence theory (optics)").

Principle
---------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Physical_optics&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Principle")]

_Physical optics_ is also the name of an [approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Approximation "Approximation") commonly used in optics, [electrical engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_engineering "Electrical engineering") and [applied physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Applied_physics "Applied physics"). In this context, it is an intermediate method between [geometric optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_optics "Geometric optics"), which ignores [wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave "Wave") effects, and full wave [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism"), which is a precise [theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory "Theory"). The word "physical" means that it is more physical than [geometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric "Geometric") or [ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_(optics) "Ray (optics)") optics and not that it is an exact physical theory.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_optics#cite_note-Ufimtsev2007-1): 11–13

This approximation consists of using ray optics to estimate the field on a surface and then [integrating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral") that field over the surface to calculate the transmitted or scattered field. This resembles the [Born approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Born_approximation "Born approximation"), in that the details of the problem are treated as a [perturbation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perturbation_theory "Perturbation theory").

In optics, it is a standard way of estimating diffraction effects. In [radio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio "Radio"), this approximation is used to estimate some effects that resemble optical effects. It models several interference, diffraction and polarization effects but not the dependence of diffraction on polarization. Since this is a high-frequency approximation, it is often more accurate in optics than for radio.

In optics, it typically consists of integrating ray-estimated field over a lens, mirror or aperture to calculate the transmitted or scattered field.

In [radar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radar "Radar")[scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scattering "Scattering") it usually means taking the [current](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Current_(electricity) "Current (electricity)") that would be found on a [tangent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent "Tangent")[plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(mathematics) "Plane (mathematics)") of similar material as the current at each point on the front, i. e. the geometrically illuminated part, of a [scatterer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scattering "Scattering"). Current on the shadowed parts is taken as zero. The approximate scattered field is then obtained by an integral over these approximate currents. This is useful for bodies with large smooth [convex](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/convex "wikt:convex") shapes and for lossy (low-reflection) surfaces.

The ray-optics field or current is generally not accurate near edges or shadow boundaries, unless supplemented by diffraction and [creeping wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Creeping_wave "Creeping wave") calculations.

The standard theory of physical optics has some defects in the evaluation of scattered fields, leading to decreased accuracy away from the specular direction.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_optics#cite_note-Umul-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_optics#cite_note-3) An improved theory introduced in 2004 gives exact solutions to problems involving wave diffraction by conducting scatterers.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_optics#cite_note-Umul-2)
