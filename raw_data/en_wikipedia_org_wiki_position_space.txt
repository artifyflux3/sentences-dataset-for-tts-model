In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") and [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), there are two closely related [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space"), usually [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space") but in general of any finite dimension. **Position space** (also **real space** or **[coordinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") space**) is the set of all _[position vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position\_vector "Position vector")_**r** in [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), and has [dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantity_dimension "Quantity dimension") of [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length "Length"); a position vector defines a point in space. (If the position vector of a [point particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_particle "Point particle") varies with time, it will trace out a path, the [trajectory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trajectory "Trajectory") of a particle.) **Momentum space** is the set of all _[momentum vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum\_vector "Momentum vector")_**p** a physical system can have; the momentum vector of a particle corresponds to its motion, with dimension of mass ⋅ length ⋅ time−1.

Mathematically, the duality between position and momentum is an example of _[Pontryagin duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pontryagin\_duality "Pontryagin duality")_. In particular, if a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") is given in position space, _f_(**r**), then its [Fourier transform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_transform "Fourier transform") obtains the function in momentum space, _φ_(**p**). Conversely, the inverse Fourier transform of a momentum space function is a position space function.

These quantities and ideas transcend all of classical and quantum physics, and a physical system can be described using either the positions of the constituent particles, or their momenta, both formulations equivalently provide the same information about the system in consideration. Another quantity is useful to define in the context of [waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave "Wave"). The [wave vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_vector "Wave vector")**k** (or simply "**k**-vector") has dimensions of [reciprocal length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reciprocal_length "Reciprocal length"), making it an analogue of [angular frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_frequency "Angular frequency")_ω_ which has dimensions of reciprocal [time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time "Time"). The set of all wave vectors is **k-space**. Usually, the position vector **r** is more intuitive and simpler than the wave vector **k**, though the converse can also be true, such as in [solid-state physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid-state_physics "Solid-state physics").

[Quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") provides two fundamental examples of the duality between position and momentum, the [Heisenberg uncertainty principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncertainty_principle "Uncertainty principle") Δ _x_ Δ _p_ ≥ _ħ_/2 stating that position and momentum cannot be simultaneously known to arbitrary precision, and the [de Broglie relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Broglie_relation "De Broglie relation")**p** = _ħ_**k** which states the momentum and wavevector of a [free particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_particle "Free particle") are proportional to each other.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBallentine1998102-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHall201360-2) In this context, when it is unambiguous, the terms "[momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum")" and "wavevector" are used interchangeably. However, the de Broglie relation is not true in a crystal.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEisbergResnick198558-3)

Classical mechanics
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Position_and_momentum_spaces&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Classical mechanics")]

### Lagrangian mechanics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Position_and_momentum_spaces&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Lagrangian mechanics")]

Most often in [Lagrangian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics"), the Lagrangian _L_(**q**, _d_**q**/_dt_, _t_) is in [configuration space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(physics) "Configuration space (physics)"), where **q** = (_q_ 1, _q_ 2,..., _q n_) is an _n_-[tuple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple "Tuple") of the [generalized coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_coordinate "Generalized coordinate"). The [Euler–Lagrange equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%E2%80%93Lagrange_equation "Euler–Lagrange equation") of motion are ![Image 1: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{i}}}={\frac {\partial L}{\partial q_{i}}}\,,\quad {\dot {q}}_{i}\equiv {\frac {dq_{i}}{dt}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffbf075f7d7041a41bc0ab5f09cec263459debe2)

(One overdot indicates one [time derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_derivative "Time derivative")). Introducing the definition of canonical momentum for each generalized coordinate ![Image 2: {\displaystyle p_{i}={\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{i}}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f4c98fe2bcef30e9e045962a018cd4c8acf5972) the Euler–Lagrange equations take the form ![Image 3: {\displaystyle {\dot {p}}_{i}={\frac {\partial L}{\partial q_{i}}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bde16442a02138b4e1d052a68dbbe9b44bd87950)

The Lagrangian can be expressed in [momentum space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum_space "Momentum space") also,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_space#cite_note-4)_L_′(**p**, _d_**p**/_dt_, _t_), where **p** = (_p_ 1, _p_ 2, ..., _p n_) is an _n_-tuple of the generalized momenta. A [Legendre transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legendre_transformation "Legendre transformation") is performed to change the variables in the [total differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_differential "Total differential") of the generalized coordinate space Lagrangian; ![Image 4: {\displaystyle dL=\sum _{i=1}^{n}\left({\frac {\partial L}{\partial q_{i}}}dq_{i}+{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{i}}}d{\dot {q}}_{i}\right)+{\frac {\partial L}{\partial t}}dt=\sum _{i=1}^{n}({\dot {p}}_{i}dq_{i}+p_{i}d{\dot {q}}_{i})+{\frac {\partial L}{\partial t}}dt\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99ff6d3eca8e41967d666926072bbae5b6ff0c10) where the definition of generalized momentum and Euler–Lagrange equations have replaced the partial derivatives of _L_. The [product rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_rule "Product rule") for differentials[[nb 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_space#cite_note-5) allows the exchange of differentials in the generalized coordinates and velocities for the differentials in generalized momenta and their time derivatives, ![Image 5: {\displaystyle {\dot {p}}_{i}dq_{i}=d(q_{i}{\dot {p}}_{i})-q_{i}d{\dot {p}}_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f01b7dfc5f4086ddfe09fe910a39487180d71faa)![Image 6: {\displaystyle p_{i}d{\dot {q}}_{i}=d({\dot {q}}_{i}p_{i})-{\dot {q}}_{i}dp_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c54cff8c3f9572066a6674fa7a58b5b85a3d09cb) which after substitution simplifies and rearranges to ![Image 7: {\displaystyle d\left[L-\sum _{i=1}^{n}(q_{i}{\dot {p}}_{i}+{\dot {q}}_{i}p_{i})\right]=-\sum _{i=1}^{n}({\dot {q}}_{i}dp_{i}+q_{i}d{\dot {p}}_{i})+{\frac {\partial L}{\partial t}}dt\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4c1f5ac7bdc50b382f9244d9578627c5fd705b20)

Now, the total differential of the momentum space Lagrangian _L_′ is ![Image 8: {\displaystyle dL'=\sum _{i=1}^{n}\left({\frac {\partial L'}{\partial p_{i}}}dp_{i}+{\frac {\partial L'}{\partial {\dot {p}}_{i}}}d{\dot {p}}_{i}\right)+{\frac {\partial L'}{\partial t}}dt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43dfa170b7a9fa9f9b2c6615463b69d6a418a3e2) so by comparison of differentials of the Lagrangians, the momenta, and their time derivatives, the momentum space Lagrangian _L_′ and the generalized coordinates derived from _L_′ are respectively ![Image 9: {\displaystyle L'=L-\sum _{i=1}^{n}(q_{i}{\dot {p}}_{i}+{\dot {q}}_{i}p_{i})\,,\quad -{\dot {q}}_{i}={\frac {\partial L'}{\partial p_{i}}}\,,\quad -q_{i}={\frac {\partial L'}{\partial {\dot {p}}_{i}}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a33f5eaa594bd0b6e51fc2ddf9e86a7111f86a0)

Combining the last two equations gives the momentum space Euler–Lagrange equations ![Image 10: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial L'}{\partial {\dot {p}}_{i}}}={\frac {\partial L'}{\partial p_{i}}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ce3a9719e08ff34ae75ff29a6ef339dd24dcb08)

The advantage of the Legendre transformation is that the relation between the new and old functions and their variables are obtained in the process. Both the coordinate and momentum forms of the equation are equivalent and contain the same information about the dynamics of the system. This form may be more useful when momentum or [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum") enters the Lagrangian.

### Hamiltonian mechanics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Position_and_momentum_spaces&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Hamiltonian mechanics")]

In [Hamiltonian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics "Hamiltonian mechanics"), unlike Lagrangian mechanics which uses either all the coordinates _or_ the momenta, the Hamiltonian equations of motion place coordinates and momenta on equal footing. For a system with Hamiltonian _H_(**q**, **p**, _t_), the equations are ![Image 11: {\displaystyle {\dot {q}}_{i}={\frac {\partial H}{\partial p_{i}}}\,,\quad {\dot {p}}_{i}=-{\frac {\partial H}{\partial q_{i}}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8571e5bf501eed2cf9a11120cb91314c6c2e0497)

In [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), a particle is described by a [quantum state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state "Quantum state"). This quantum state can be represented as a [superposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_superposition "Quantum superposition") of [basis states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state#Basis_states_of_one-particle_systems "Quantum state"). In principle one is free to choose the set of basis states, as long as they [span](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_span "Linear span") the [state space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_state_space "Quantum state space"). If one chooses the [(generalized) eigenfunctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_delta_function#Quantum_mechanics "Dirac delta function") of the [position operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_operator "Position operator") as a set of basis functions, one speaks of a state as a [wave function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_function "Wave function")_ψ_(**r**) in **position space**. The familiar [Schrödinger equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation "Schrödinger equation") in terms of the position **r** is an example of quantum mechanics in the position representation.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_space#cite_note-peleg-6)

By choosing the eigenfunctions of a different operator as a set of basis functions, one can arrive at a number of different representations of the same state. If one picks the eigenfunctions of the [momentum operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum_operator "Momentum operator") as a set of basis functions, the resulting wave function ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \phi (\mathbf {k} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bc0f7b18110aa4ca9dc42e12d63dd1fb8e49a0fd) is said to be the wave function in **momentum space**.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_space#cite_note-peleg-6)

A feature of quantum mechanics is that phase spaces can come in different types: discrete-variable, rotor, and continuous-variable. The table below summarizes some relations involved in the three types of phase spaces.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_space#cite_note-phasespaces-7)

[![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/41/Phase_spaces.png/250px-Phase_spaces.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Phase_spaces.png)

Comparison and summary of relations between conjugate variables in discrete-variable (DV), rotor (ROT), and continuous-variable (CV) phase spaces (taken from arXiv:1709.04460). Most physically relevant phase spaces consist of combinations of these three. Each phase space consists of position and momentum, whose possible values are taken from a locally compact Abelian group and its dual. A quantum mechanical state can be fully represented in terms of either variables, and the transformation used to go between position and momentum spaces is, in each of the three cases, a variant of the Fourier transform. The table uses bra-ket notation as well as mathematical terminology describing Canonical commutation relations (CCR).

Reciprocal relation
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Position_and_momentum_spaces&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Reciprocal relation")]

The momentum representation of a wave function and the de Broglie relation are closely related to the [Fourier inversion theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_inversion_theorem "Fourier inversion theorem") and the concept of [frequency domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency_domain "Frequency domain"). Since a [free particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_particle "Free particle") has a [spatial frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_vector#Definition "Wave vector")![Image 14: {\displaystyle k=|\mathbf {k} |=2\pi /\lambda }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a4120d60d416466cc8b4833c8defd96ac5d0e4a4) proportional to the momentum ![Image 15: {\displaystyle p=|\mathbf {p} |=\hbar k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a1edddd1b77fa3574487f9f782485d808094b3f), describing the particle as a sum of frequency components is equivalent to describing it as the Fourier transform of a "[sufficiently nice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_inversion_theorem#Conditions_on_the_function "Fourier inversion theorem")" wave function in momentum space.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHall201360-2)

Suppose we have a three-dimensional [wave function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_function "Wave function") in position space _ψ_(**r**), then we can write this functions as a weighted sum of orthogonal basis functions _ψ_ _j_(**r**): ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \psi (\mathbf {r} )=\sum _{j}\phi _{j}\psi _{j}(\mathbf {r} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea302ccb65e88d0112d5e6d2b6eb5339646edb84) or, in the continuous case, as an [integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral")![Image 17: {\displaystyle \psi (\mathbf {r} )=\int _{\mathbf {k} {\text{-space}}}\phi (\mathbf {k} )\psi _{\mathbf {k} }(\mathbf {r} )\mathrm {d} ^{3}\mathbf {k} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f0a2477b784e422e73fab10e202a9bb7ff09b6e8) It is clear that if we specify the set of functions ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \psi _{\mathbf {k} }(\mathbf {r} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/03e6dddca702b6689eb314d18b68181659cf1d85), say as the set of eigenfunctions of the momentum operator, the function ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \phi (\mathbf {k} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bc0f7b18110aa4ca9dc42e12d63dd1fb8e49a0fd) holds all the information necessary to reconstruct _ψ_(**r**) and is therefore an alternative description for the state ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \psi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45e5789e5d9c8f7c79744f43ecaaf8ba42a8553a).

In coordinate representation the [momentum operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum_operator "Momentum operator") is given by[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBallentine199898-8)![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\hat {p}} =-i\hbar {\frac {\partial }{\partial \mathbf {r} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8373697d776c6d632beb73c3794ea026b56edc9d) (see [matrix calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_calculus#Scope "Matrix calculus") for the denominator notation) with appropriate [domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_of_a_function "Domain of a function"). The [eigenfunctions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenfunction "Eigenfunction") are ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \psi _{\mathbf {k} }(\mathbf {r} )={\frac {1}{({\sqrt {2\pi }})^{3}}}e^{i\mathbf {k} \cdot \mathbf {r} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3052f218c797bc88ffc4f553c818a3e3630ff653) and [eigenvalues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvalues "Eigenvalues")_ħ_**k**. So ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \psi (\mathbf {r} )={\frac {1}{({\sqrt {2\pi }})^{3}}}\int _{\mathbf {k} {\text{-space}}}\phi (\mathbf {k} )e^{i\mathbf {k} \cdot \mathbf {r} }\mathrm {d} ^{3}\mathbf {k} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/575b51f23d81898c7e5b4100cb1f765451b82600) and we see that the momentum representation is related to the position representation by a Fourier transform.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_space#cite_note-Penrose-9)

Conversely, a three-dimensional wave function in momentum space ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \phi (\mathbf {k} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bc0f7b18110aa4ca9dc42e12d63dd1fb8e49a0fd) can be expressed as a weighted sum of orthogonal basis functions ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \phi _{j}(\mathbf {k} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0eca228fd0296a1e3e5e7a714bb361be9bbe47b0), ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \phi (\mathbf {k} )=\sum _{j}\psi _{j}\phi _{j}(\mathbf {k} ),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9ad29425ca6a94db5430eec5f21aaf3eef68c873) or as an integral, ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \phi (\mathbf {k} )=\int _{\mathbf {r} {\text{-space}}}\psi (\mathbf {r} )\phi _{\mathbf {r} }(\mathbf {k} )\mathrm {d} ^{3}\mathbf {r} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b039303d8552b2e07eed1c6b6b89cadf47484865)

In momentum representation the [position operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_operator "Position operator") is given by[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_space#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBallentine1998127-10)![Image 28: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\hat {r}} =i\hbar {\frac {\partial }{\partial \mathbf {p} }}=i{\frac {\partial }{\partial \mathbf {k} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/37c847ed8526ec91b541f24376b8997df1e13fed) with eigenfunctions ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \phi _{\mathbf {r} }(\mathbf {k} )={\frac {1}{\left({\sqrt {2\pi }}\right)^{3}}}e^{-i\mathbf {k} \cdot \mathbf {r} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/611a19f3d8462ff8fd73ebaeb3ced4118e34b0d4) and [eigenvalues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvalues "Eigenvalues")**r**. So a similar decomposition of ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \phi (\mathbf {k} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bc0f7b18110aa4ca9dc42e12d63dd1fb8e49a0fd) can be made in terms of the eigenfunctions of this operator, which turns out to be the inverse Fourier transform,[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_space#cite_note-Penrose-9)![Image 31: {\displaystyle \phi (\mathbf {k} )={\frac {1}{({\sqrt {2\pi }})^{3}}}\int _{\mathbf {r} {\text{-space}}}\psi (\mathbf {r} )e^{-i\mathbf {k} \cdot \mathbf {r} }\mathrm {d} ^{3}\mathbf {r} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/884432f4f7954c54443b306e4c7223eac160305e)

### Unitary equivalence

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Position_and_momentum_spaces&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Unitary equivalence")]

The position and momentum operators are [unitarily equivalent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_representation "Unitary representation"), with the [unitary operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unitary_operator "Unitary operator") being given explicitly by the Fourier transform, namely a quarter-cycle rotation in phase space, generated by the oscillator Hamiltonian. Thus, they have the same [spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectrum_(functional_analysis) "Spectrum (functional analysis)"). In physical language, **p** acting on momentum space wave functions is the same as **r** acting on position space wave functions (under the [image](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_(mathematics) "Image (mathematics)") of the Fourier transform).

Reciprocal space and crystals
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Position_and_momentum_spaces&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Reciprocal space and crystals")]

For an [electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") (or other [particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle "Particle")) in a crystal, its value of **k** relates almost always to its [crystal momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal_momentum "Crystal momentum"), not its normal momentum. Therefore, **k** and **p** are not simply [proportional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportionality_(mathematics) "Proportionality (mathematics)") but play different roles. See [k·p perturbation theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C2%B7p_perturbation_theory "K·p perturbation theory") for an example. Crystal momentum is like a [wave envelope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Envelope_(waves) "Envelope (waves)") that describes how the wave varies from one [unit cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_cell "Unit cell") to the next, but does _not_ give any information about how the wave varies within each unit cell.

When **k** relates to crystal momentum instead of true momentum, the concept of **k**-space is still meaningful and extremely useful, but it differs in several ways from the non-crystal **k**-space discussed above. For example, in a crystal's **k**-space, there is an [infinite set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_set "Infinite set") of points called the [reciprocal lattice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reciprocal_lattice "Reciprocal lattice") which are "equivalent" to **k** = **0** (this is analogous to [aliasing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aliasing "Aliasing")). Likewise, the "[first Brillouin zone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_Brillouin_zone "First Brillouin zone")" is a finite volume of **k**-space, such that every possible **k** is "equivalent" to exactly one point in this region.
