From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Type of ordinary differential equation

A [differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equation "Differential equation") can be **homogeneous** in either of two respects.

A [first order differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_order_differential_equation "First order differential equation") is said to be homogeneous if it may be written

f(x,y)d y=g(x,y)d x,{\displaystyle f(x,y)\,dy=g(x,y)\,dx,}![Image 25: {\displaystyle f(x,y)\,dy=g(x,y)\,dx,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2c466332533b5c71f74fa63f95187c1af4c0ac12)
where f and g are [homogeneous functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_function "Homogeneous function") of the same degree of x and y.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_differential_equation#cite_note-Zill2012-1) In this case, the [change of variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Change_of_variable "Change of variable")_y_ = _ux_ leads to an equation of the form

d x x=h(u)d u,{\displaystyle {\frac {dx}{x}}=h(u)\,du,}![Image 26: {\displaystyle {\frac {dx}{x}}=h(u)\,du,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/593dc2e81082ab852e00e2c15c12e168b43774cd)
which is easy to solve by [integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral") of the two members.

Otherwise, a differential equation is homogeneous if it is a homogeneous function of the unknown function and its derivatives. In the case of [linear differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_differential_equation "Linear differential equation"), this means that there are no constant terms. The solutions of any linear [ordinary differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinary_differential_equation "Ordinary differential equation") of any order may be deduced by integration from the solution of the homogeneous equation obtained by removing the constant term.

History
-------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Homogeneous_differential_equation&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: History")]

The term _homogeneous_ was first applied to differential equations by [Johann Bernoulli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Bernoulli "Johann Bernoulli") in section 9 of his 1726 article _De integraionibus aequationum differentialium_ (On the integration of differential equations).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_differential_equation#cite_note-2)

A first-order [ordinary differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinary_differential_equation "Ordinary differential equation") in the form:

M(x,y)d x+N(x,y)d y=0{\displaystyle M(x,y)\,dx+N(x,y)\,dy=0}![Image 27: {\displaystyle M(x,y)\,dx+N(x,y)\,dy=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db1fa7bf618ecb784871abf9baa993f084a4875e)
is a homogeneous type if both functions _M_(_x_, _y_) and _N_(_x_, _y_) are [homogeneous functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_function "Homogeneous function") of the same degree n.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_differential_equation#cite_note-3) That is, multiplying each variable by a parameter λ, we find

M(λ x,λ y)=λ n M(x,y)and N(λ x,λ y)=λ n N(x,y).{\displaystyle M(\lambda x,\lambda y)=\lambda ^{n}M(x,y)\quad {\text{and}}\quad N(\lambda x,\lambda y)=\lambda ^{n}N(x,y)\,.}![Image 28: {\displaystyle M(\lambda x,\lambda y)=\lambda ^{n}M(x,y)\quad {\text{and}}\quad N(\lambda x,\lambda y)=\lambda ^{n}N(x,y)\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a1398ed29d9abab52892328edad4758a30e17860)
Thus,

M(λ x,λ y)N(λ x,λ y)=M(x,y)N(x,y).{\displaystyle {\frac {M(\lambda x,\lambda y)}{N(\lambda x,\lambda y)}}={\frac {M(x,y)}{N(x,y)}}\,.}![Image 29: {\displaystyle {\frac {M(\lambda x,\lambda y)}{N(\lambda x,\lambda y)}}={\frac {M(x,y)}{N(x,y)}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b15679f2bb0f87d5d9ab7aecd07bf9ee9d68627f)

### Solution method

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Homogeneous_differential_equation&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Solution method")]

In the quotient M(t x,t y)N(t x,t y)=M(x,y)N(x,y){\textstyle {\frac {M(tx,ty)}{N(tx,ty)}}={\frac {M(x,y)}{N(x,y)}}}![Image 30: {\textstyle {\frac {M(tx,ty)}{N(tx,ty)}}={\frac {M(x,y)}{N(x,y)}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e47a3d7f3810c8704f0c660bd003abe8d413087), we can let _t_ = ⁠1/_x_⁠ to simplify this quotient to a function f of the single variable ⁠_y_/_x_⁠:

M(x,y)N(x,y)=M(t x,t y)N(t x,t y)=M(1,y/x)N(1,y/x)=f(y/x).{\displaystyle {\frac {M(x,y)}{N(x,y)}}={\frac {M(tx,ty)}{N(tx,ty)}}={\frac {M(1,y/x)}{N(1,y/x)}}=f(y/x)\,.}![Image 31: {\displaystyle {\frac {M(x,y)}{N(x,y)}}={\frac {M(tx,ty)}{N(tx,ty)}}={\frac {M(1,y/x)}{N(1,y/x)}}=f(y/x)\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/415fec1eb879dc7e238eba775bf2fa9d9f28405e)
That is

d y d x=−f(y/x).{\displaystyle {\frac {dy}{dx}}=-f(y/x).}![Image 32: {\displaystyle {\frac {dy}{dx}}=-f(y/x).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c077b9d1985325230e2667923f330669a4d57c6f)
Introduce the [change of variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Change_of_variables "Change of variables")_y_ = _ux_; differentiate using the [product rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_rule "Product rule"):

d y d x=d(u x)d x=x d u d x+u d x d x=x d u d x+u.{\displaystyle {\frac {dy}{dx}}={\frac {d(ux)}{dx}}=x{\frac {du}{dx}}+u{\frac {dx}{dx}}=x{\frac {du}{dx}}+u.}![Image 33: {\displaystyle {\frac {dy}{dx}}={\frac {d(ux)}{dx}}=x{\frac {du}{dx}}+u{\frac {dx}{dx}}=x{\frac {du}{dx}}+u.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51815753b4f43e1bd4a3e9ad4475d75f0a2740be)
This transforms the original differential equation into the [separable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_variables "Separation of variables") form

x d u d x=−f(u)−u,{\displaystyle x{\frac {du}{dx}}=-f(u)-u,}![Image 34: {\displaystyle x{\frac {du}{dx}}=-f(u)-u,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4e3a6eea0571d927a6f3c319fcf352b5ac73c55c)
or

1 x d x d u=−1 f(u)+u,{\displaystyle {\frac {1}{x}}{\frac {dx}{du}}={\frac {-1}{f(u)+u}},}![Image 35: {\displaystyle {\frac {1}{x}}{\frac {dx}{du}}={\frac {-1}{f(u)+u}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/660b27c145aac2e4972e1d2e7b6a435c41951fd1)
which can now be integrated directly: ln _x_ equals the [antiderivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiderivative "Antiderivative") of the right-hand side (see [ordinary differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinary_differential_equation "Ordinary differential equation")).

### Special case

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Homogeneous_differential_equation&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Special case")]

A first order differential equation of the form (a, b, c, e, f, g are all constants)

(a x+b y+c)d x+(e x+f y+g)d y=0{\displaystyle \left(ax+by+c\right)dx+\left(ex+fy+g\right)dy=0}![Image 36: {\displaystyle \left(ax+by+c\right)dx+\left(ex+fy+g\right)dy=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/369717a783f7fd6001c37491ff71820c61f9bd65)
where _af_ ≠ _be_ can be transformed into a homogeneous type by a linear transformation of both variables (α and β are constants):

t=x+α;z=y+β,{\displaystyle t=x+\alpha ;\;\;z=y+\beta \,,}![Image 37: {\displaystyle t=x+\alpha ;\;\;z=y+\beta \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/475230eb89e723e0ebf3cb8a50cdadfee4811147)
where

α=c f−b g a f−b e;β=a g−c e a f−b e.{\displaystyle \alpha ={\frac {cf-bg}{af-be}};\;\;\beta ={\frac {ag-ce}{af-be}}\,.}![Image 38: {\displaystyle \alpha ={\frac {cf-bg}{af-be}};\;\;\beta ={\frac {ag-ce}{af-be}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/78f47ba22703ce31f6e57b72c29ae2b80ebf6467)
For cases where _af_ = _be_, introduce the [change of variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Change_of_variables "Change of variables")_u_ = _ax_ + _by_ or _u_ = _ex_ + _fy_; differentiation yields:

d u d x=a−b(a c+a u a g+e u),{\displaystyle {\frac {du}{dx}}=a-b{\bigg (}{\frac {ac+au}{ag+eu}}{\bigg )},}![Image 39: {\displaystyle {\frac {du}{dx}}=a-b{\bigg (}{\frac {ac+au}{ag+eu}}{\bigg )},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7b3140097eb291bad5e39d5b1c6cc9b667e0d558)
or

d u d x=e−f(e c+a u e g+e u),{\displaystyle {\frac {du}{dx}}=e-f{\bigg (}{\frac {ec+au}{eg+eu}}{\bigg )},}![Image 40: {\displaystyle {\frac {du}{dx}}=e-f{\bigg (}{\frac {ec+au}{eg+eu}}{\bigg )},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29b7cc370e870ad48c883bf5e14210fe5065bd91)
for each respective substitution. Both may be solved via [separation of variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_variables "Separation of variables").

Homogeneous linear differential equations
-----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Homogeneous_differential_equation&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Homogeneous linear differential equations")]

See also: [Linear differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_differential_equation "Linear differential equation")

A linear differential equation is **homogeneous** if it is a [homogeneous linear equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_linear_equation "Homogeneous linear equation") in the unknown function and its derivatives. It follows that, if _φ_(_x_) is a solution, so is _cφ_(_x_), for any (non-zero) constant c. In order for this condition to hold, each nonzero term of the linear differential equation must depend on the unknown function or any derivative of it. A linear differential equation that fails this condition is called **inhomogeneous.**

A [linear differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_differential_equation "Linear differential equation") can be represented as a [linear operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_operator "Linear operator") acting on _y_(_x_) where x is usually the independent variable and y is the dependent variable. Therefore, the general form of a [linear homogeneous differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_homogeneous_differential_equation "Linear homogeneous differential equation") is

L(y)=0{\displaystyle L(y)=0}![Image 41: {\displaystyle L(y)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8e4844a183e50ad714156b65c4d1d03bd91412c)
where L is a [differential operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_operator "Differential operator"), a sum of derivatives (defining the "0th derivative" as the original, non-differentiated function), each multiplied by a function _f_ _i_ of x:

L=∑i=0 n f i(x)d i d x i,{\displaystyle L=\sum _{i=0}^{n}f_{i}(x){\frac {d^{i}}{dx^{i}}}\,,}![Image 42: {\displaystyle L=\sum _{i=0}^{n}f_{i}(x){\frac {d^{i}}{dx^{i}}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/131d58bd7ffc723549fe889d8baade7e87e5cf20)
where _f_ _i_ may be constants, but not all _f_ _i_ may be zero.

For example, the following linear differential equation is homogeneous:

sin⁡(x)d 2 y d x 2+4 d y d x+y=0,{\displaystyle \sin(x){\frac {d^{2}y}{dx^{2}}}+4{\frac {dy}{dx}}+y=0\,,}![Image 43: {\displaystyle \sin(x){\frac {d^{2}y}{dx^{2}}}+4{\frac {dy}{dx}}+y=0\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a9228d19c15b9b086455e07ce35cedd0a35a407)
whereas the following two are inhomogeneous:

2 x 2 d 2 y d x 2+4 x d y d x+y=cos⁡(x);{\displaystyle 2x^{2}{\frac {d^{2}y}{dx^{2}}}+4x{\frac {dy}{dx}}+y=\cos(x)\,;}![Image 44: {\displaystyle 2x^{2}{\frac {d^{2}y}{dx^{2}}}+4x{\frac {dy}{dx}}+y=\cos(x)\,;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa0c1b5cfd49e8e1326bf714a83979f560669973)2 x 2 d 2 y d x 2−3 x d y d x+y=2.{\displaystyle 2x^{2}{\frac {d^{2}y}{dx^{2}}}-3x{\frac {dy}{dx}}+y=2\,.}![Image 45: {\displaystyle 2x^{2}{\frac {d^{2}y}{dx^{2}}}-3x{\frac {dy}{dx}}+y=2\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2caa893569e61e4719f198d6428faebb34129c7b)
The existence of a constant term is a sufficient condition for an equation to be inhomogeneous, as in the above example.
