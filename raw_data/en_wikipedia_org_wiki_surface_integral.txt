From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/72/Surface_integral_illustration.svg/250px-Surface_integral_illustration.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Surface_integral_illustration.svg)

The definition of the surface integral relies on splitting the surface into small surface elements.

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), particularly [multivariable calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivariable_calculus "Multivariable calculus"), a **surface integral** is a generalization of [multiple integrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_integral "Multiple integral") to [integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral") over [surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(differential_geometry) "Surface (differential geometry)"). It can be thought of as the [double integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_integral "Double integral") analogue of the [line integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_integral "Line integral"). Given a surface, one may integrate over this surface a [scalar field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field "Scalar field") (that is, a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") of position which returns a [scalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(mathematics) "Scalar (mathematics)") as a value), or a [vector field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field") (that is, a function which returns a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(geometric) "Vector (geometric)") as value). If a region R is not flat, then it is called a [_surface_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry_of_surfaces "Differential geometry of surfaces") as shown in the illustration.

Surface integrals have applications in [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), particularly in the [classical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_physics "Classical physics") theories of [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism") and [fluid mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_mechanics "Fluid mechanics").

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Surface_integral1.svg/250px-Surface_integral1.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Surface_integral1.svg)

An illustration of a single surface element. These elements are made infinitesimally small, by the limiting process, so as to approximate the surface.

Surface integrals of scalar fields
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Surface_integral&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Surface integrals of scalar fields")]

Assume that _f_ is a scalar, vector, or tensor field defined on a surface _S_. To find an explicit formula for the surface integral of _f_ over _S_, we need to [parameterize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system")_S_ by defining a system of [curvilinear coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvilinear_coordinates "Curvilinear coordinates") on _S_, like the [latitude and longitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system "Geographic coordinate system") on a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere"). Let such a parameterization be **r**(_s_, _t_), where (_s_, _t_) varies in some region T in the [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system#Cartesian_coordinates_in_two_dimensions "Cartesian coordinate system"). Then, the surface integral is given by

![Image 3: {\displaystyle \iint _{S}f\,\mathrm {d} S=\iint _{T}f(\mathbf {r} (s,t))\left\|{\partial \mathbf {r}  \over \partial s}\times {\partial \mathbf {r}  \over \partial t}\right\|\mathrm {d} s\,\mathrm {d} t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dac694426951d5e8733162c2f225e29cb949f6bf)
where the expression between bars on the right-hand side is the [magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitude_(mathematics) "Magnitude (mathematics)") of the [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product") of the [partial derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative "Partial derivative") of **r**(_s_, _t_), and is known as the surface [element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_element#Area_element_of_a_surface "Volume element") (which would, for example, yield a smaller value near the poles of a sphere, where the lines of longitude converge more dramatically, and latitudinal coordinates are more compactly spaced). The surface integral can also be expressed in the equivalent form

![Image 4: {\displaystyle \iint _{S}f\,\mathrm {d} S=\iint _{T}f(\mathbf {r} (s,t)){\sqrt {g}}\,\mathrm {d} s\,\mathrm {d} t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/26aa56b36636b638bad6ac804c8f8033ef257c0d)
where g is the determinant of the [first fundamental form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_fundamental_form "First fundamental form") of the surface mapping **r**(_s_, _t_).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_integral#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_integral#cite_note-2)

For example, if we want to find the [surface area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_area "Surface area") of the graph of some scalar function, say _z_ = _f_(_x_, _y_), we have

![Image 5: {\displaystyle A=\iint _{S}\,\mathrm {d} S=\iint _{T}\left\|{\partial \mathbf {r}  \over \partial x}\times {\partial \mathbf {r}  \over \partial y}\right\|\mathrm {d} x\,\mathrm {d} y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/60d01c1be341c724494d147a07bc7cbbcf71218b)
where **r** = (_x_, _y_, _z_) = (_x_, _y_, _f_(_x_, _y_)). So that ![Image 6: {\displaystyle {\partial \mathbf {r}  \over \partial x}=(1,0,f_{x}(x,y))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/42c3d92ff69f21c2fb6fca515e35b27dd4335f1f), and ![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\partial \mathbf {r}  \over \partial y}=(0,1,f_{y}(x,y))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d236c6d3a8e2795b7f4dc0d8feee4756429ca61). So,

![Image 8: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}A&{}=\iint _{T}\left\|\left(1,0,{\partial f \over \partial x}\right)\times \left(0,1,{\partial f \over \partial y}\right)\right\|\mathrm {d} x\,\mathrm {d} y\\&{}=\iint _{T}\left\|\left(-{\partial f \over \partial x},-{\partial f \over \partial y},1\right)\right\|\mathrm {d} x\,\mathrm {d} y\\&{}=\iint _{T}{\sqrt {\left({\partial f \over \partial x}\right)^{2}+\left({\partial f \over \partial y}\right)^{2}+1}}\,\,\mathrm {d} x\,\mathrm {d} y\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/95ce6554362c66ec8a85ea7333b3e7fa9fe72e28)
which is the standard formula for the area of a surface described this way. One can recognize the vector in the second-last line above as the [normal vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_normal "Surface normal") to the surface.

Because of the presence of the cross product, the above formulas only work for surfaces embedded in three-dimensional space.

This can be seen as integrating a [Riemannian volume form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_volume_form "Riemannian volume form") on the parameterized surface, where the [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor") is given by the [first fundamental form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_fundamental_form "First fundamental form") of the surface.

Surface integrals of vector fields
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Surface_integral&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Surface integrals of vector fields")]

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Surface_integral_-_vector_field_thru_a_surface.svg/330px-Surface_integral_-_vector_field_thru_a_surface.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Surface_integral_-_vector_field_thru_a_surface.svg)

A curved surface ![Image 10: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) with a vector field ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da18bef8c979f3548bb0d8976f5844012d7b8256) passing through it. The red arrows (vectors) represent the magnitude and direction of the field at various points on the surface

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Surface_integral_-_parametrized_surface.svg/330px-Surface_integral_-_parametrized_surface.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Surface_integral_-_parametrized_surface.svg)

Surface divided into small patches ![Image 13: {\displaystyle dS=du\,dv}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75c2b35fe7a14f3528c88e6533c9f0d8f6156fdc) by a parameterization of the surface ![Image 14: {\displaystyle [u(\mathbf {x} ),v(\mathbf {x} )]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61246aa69799b942c7eebbbed54c62552afef3f5)

[![Image 15](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Surface_integral_-_definition.svg/330px-Surface_integral_-_definition.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Surface_integral_-_definition.svg)

The total flux through the surface is found by adding up ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} \cdot \mathbf {n} \;dS}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/281869b8eeeef92fd0d4db16e1db7616a1cf9a67) for each patch. In the limit as the patches become infinitesimally small, this is the surface integral

![Image 17: {\textstyle \iint _{S}\mathbf {F\cdot n} \;dS}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/313ca219a850e0263f5fde3caf161c72cd52cd42)

Consider a vector field **v** on a surface _S_, that is, for each **r** = (_x_, _y_, _z_) in _S_, **v**(**r**) is a vector.

The integral of **v** on _S_ was defined in the previous section. Suppose now that it is desired to integrate only the [normal component](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_component "Normal component") of the vector field over the surface, the result being a scalar, usually called the [flux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flux_of_a_vector_field "Flux of a vector field") passing through the surface. For example, imagine that we have a fluid flowing through _S_, such that **v**(**r**) determines the velocity of the fluid at **r**. The [flux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flux "Flux") is defined as the quantity of fluid flowing through _S_ per unit time.

This illustration implies that if the vector field is [tangent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent "Tangent") to _S_ at each point, then the flux is zero because, on the surface _S_, the fluid just flows along _S_, and neither in nor out. This also implies that if **v** does not just flow along _S_, that is, if **v** has both a tangential and a normal component, then only the normal component contributes to the flux. Based on this reasoning, to find the flux, we need to take the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") of **v** with the unit [surface normal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_normal "Surface normal")**n** to _S_ at each point, which will give us a scalar field, and integrate the obtained field as above. In other words, we have to integrate **v** with respect to the vector surface element ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} \mathbf {s} ={\mathbf {n} }\mathrm {d} s}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5365f7e33b451a333ff329e68f0bb3b77fddc9b1), which is the vector normal to _S_ at the given point, whose magnitude is ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} s=\|\mathrm {d} {\mathbf {s} }\|.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d6deeef188f9ddc06b80fbf7f46939db900d0579)

We find the formula

![Image 20: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\iint _{S}{\mathbf {v} }\cdot \mathrm {d} {\mathbf {s} }&=\iint _{S}\left({\mathbf {v} }\cdot {\mathbf {n} }\right)\,\mathrm {d} s\\&{}=\iint _{T}\left({\mathbf {v} }(\mathbf {r} (s,t))\cdot {{\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial s}}\times {\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial t}} \over \left\|{\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial s}}\times {\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial t}}\right\|}\right)\left\|{\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial s}}\times {\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial t}}\right\|\mathrm {d} s\,\mathrm {d} t\\&{}=\iint _{T}{\mathbf {v} }(\mathbf {r} (s,t))\cdot \left({\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial s}}\times {\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} }{\partial t}}\right)\mathrm {d} s\,\mathrm {d} t.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f871d3eddd927b6caf9e54f6ab51d15acb45ecec)
The cross product on the right-hand side of the last expression is a (not necessarily unital) surface normal determined by the parametrisation.

This formula _defines_ the integral on the left (note the dot and the vector notation for the surface element).

We may also interpret this as a special case of integrating 2-forms, where we identify the vector field with a 1-form, and then integrate its [Hodge dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_dual "Hodge dual") over the surface. This is equivalent to integrating ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \left\langle \mathbf {v} ,\mathbf {n} \right\rangle \mathrm {d} S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed908ba1cc3192a72b3b6ed658bb336aef48ee11) over the immersed surface, where ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72ba425d7d7a0f229457dea3c0be4a47ea303cc3) is the induced volume form on the surface, obtained by [interior multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interior_multiplication "Interior multiplication") of the Riemannian metric of the ambient space with the outward normal of the surface.

Surface integrals of differential 2-forms
-----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Surface_integral&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Surface integrals of differential 2-forms")]

Let

![Image 23: {\displaystyle f=\mathrm {d} x\mathrm {d} y\,f_{z}+\mathrm {d} y\mathrm {d} z\,f_{x}+\mathrm {d} z\mathrm {d} x\,f_{y}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7ba6a720137480b3841b6848c2555f8f3919416)
be a [differential 2-form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form") defined on a surface _S_, and let

![Image 24: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} (s,t)=(x(s,t),y(s,t),z(s,t))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a34fa8b4746ee00d8d1251193e54fc13ee43dcbb)
be an [orientation preserving](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientability "Orientability") parametrization of _S_ with ![Image 25: {\displaystyle (s,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c4a80873677b13851457efa3447c2f412c91e425) in _D_. Changing coordinates from ![Image 26: {\displaystyle (x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41cf50e4a314ca8e2c30964baa8d26e5be7a9386) to ![Image 27: {\displaystyle (s,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c4a80873677b13851457efa3447c2f412c91e425), the differential forms transform as

![Image 28: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} x={\frac {\partial x}{\partial s}}\mathrm {d} s+{\frac {\partial x}{\partial t}}\mathrm {d} t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f327e85383a80d104fecbf6fd8d45f62295bfa7)![Image 29: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} y={\frac {\partial y}{\partial s}}\mathrm {d} s+{\frac {\partial y}{\partial t}}\mathrm {d} t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82a7e984d0346b23e472e321d792704f144700a)
So ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} x\mathrm {d} y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed00946a53fd13875675459ba4bce3ac76edc062) transforms to ![Image 31: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial (x,y)}{\partial (s,t)}}\mathrm {d} s\mathrm {d} t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73755e7b8642118b84000f884c884e67e0c09552), where ![Image 32: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial (x,y)}{\partial (s,t)}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/382d24487299367781c0f2f7df8bfdbaec8d4faa) denotes the [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant") of the [Jacobian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_matrix_and_determinant "Jacobian matrix and determinant") of the transition function from ![Image 33: {\displaystyle (s,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c4a80873677b13851457efa3447c2f412c91e425) to ![Image 34: {\displaystyle (x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41cf50e4a314ca8e2c30964baa8d26e5be7a9386). The transformation of the other forms are similar.

Then, the surface integral of _f_ on _S_ is given by

![Image 35: {\displaystyle \iint _{D}\left[f_{z}(\mathbf {r} (s,t)){\frac {\partial (x,y)}{\partial (s,t)}}+f_{x}(\mathbf {r} (s,t)){\frac {\partial (y,z)}{\partial (s,t)}}+f_{y}(\mathbf {r} (s,t)){\frac {\partial (z,x)}{\partial (s,t)}}\right]\,\mathrm {d} s\,\mathrm {d} t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/240a0ace0f1036661d8bd5521e101b3a7af8502f)
where

![Image 36: {\displaystyle {\partial \mathbf {r}  \over \partial s}\times {\partial \mathbf {r}  \over \partial t}=\left({\frac {\partial (y,z)}{\partial (s,t)}},{\frac {\partial (z,x)}{\partial (s,t)}},{\frac {\partial (x,y)}{\partial (s,t)}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f4aa8a2ff0089749a622ada91615677ef7caf74)
is the surface element normal to _S_.

Let us note that the surface integral of this 2-form is the same as the surface integral of the vector field which has as components ![Image 37: {\displaystyle f_{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb36605624ab2287dc5ec558513c625b88acfee3), ![Image 38: {\displaystyle f_{y}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/12c2e2650f381b3212314edab5a27ccab586de53) and ![Image 39: {\displaystyle f_{z}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f803642740eb529735217a23124a05f2eb2763eb).

Theorems involving surface integrals
------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Surface_integral&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Theorems involving surface integrals")]

Various useful results for surface integrals can be derived using [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry") and [vector calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus "Vector calculus"), such as the [divergence theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divergence_theorem "Divergence theorem"), [magnetic flux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_flux "Magnetic flux"), and its generalization, [Stokes' theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stokes%27_theorem "Stokes' theorem").

Dependence on parametrization
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Surface_integral&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Dependence on parametrization")]

Let us notice that we defined the surface integral by using a parametrization of the surface _S_. We know that a given surface might have several parametrizations. For example, if we move the locations of the North Pole and the South Pole on a sphere, the latitude and longitude change for all the points on the sphere. A natural question is then whether the definition of the surface integral depends on the chosen parametrization. For integrals of scalar fields, the answer to this question is simple; the value of the surface integral will be the same no matter what parametrization one uses.

For integrals of vector fields, things are more complicated because the surface normal is involved. It can be proven that given two parametrizations of the same surface, whose surface normals point in the same direction, one obtains the same value for the surface integral with both parametrizations. If, however, the normals for these parametrizations point in opposite directions, the value of the surface integral obtained using one parametrization is the negative of the one obtained via the other parametrization. It follows that given a surface, we do not need to stick to any unique parametrization, but, when integrating vector fields, we do need to decide in advance in which direction the normal will point and then choose any parametrization consistent with that direction.

Another issue is that sometimes surfaces do not have parametrizations which cover the whole surface. The obvious solution is then to split that surface into several pieces, calculate the surface integral on each piece, and then add them all up. This is indeed how things work, but when integrating vector fields, one needs to again be careful how to choose the normal-pointing vector for each piece of the surface, so that when the pieces are put back together, the results are consistent. For the cylinder, this means that if we decide that for the side region the normal will point out of the body, then for the top and bottom circular parts, the normal must point out of the body too.

Last, there are surfaces which do not admit a surface normal at each point with consistent results (for example, the [Möbius strip](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6bius_strip "Möbius strip")). If such a surface is split into pieces, on each piece a parametrization and corresponding surface normal is chosen, and the pieces are put back together, we will find that the normal vectors coming from different pieces cannot be reconciled. This means that at some junction between two pieces we will have normal vectors pointing in opposite directions. Such a surface is called [non-orientable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientability "Orientability"), and on this kind of surface, one cannot talk about integrating vector fields.
