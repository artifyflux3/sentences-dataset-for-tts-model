In [mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics "Mechanics") and [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), **simple harmonic motion** (sometimes abbreviated as **SHM**) is a special type of [periodic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodic_function "Periodic function")[motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion "Motion") an object experiences by means of a [restoring force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Restoring_force "Restoring force") whose magnitude is directly [proportional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportionality_(mathematics) "Proportionality (mathematics)") to the distance of the object from an equilibrium position and acts towards the equilibrium position. It results in an [oscillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oscillation "Oscillation") that is described by a [sinusoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sinusoid "Sinusoid") which continues indefinitely (if uninhibited by [friction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friction "Friction") or any other [dissipation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissipation "Dissipation") of [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy")).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_harmonic_motion#cite_note-1)

Simple harmonic motion can serve as a [mathematical model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_model "Mathematical model") for a variety of motions, but is typified by the oscillation of a [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") on a [spring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spring_(device) "Spring (device)") when it is subject to the linear [elastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elasticity_(physics) "Elasticity (physics)") restoring force given by [Hooke's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hooke%27s_law "Hooke's law"). The motion is [sinusoidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sinusoidal "Sinusoidal") in time and demonstrates a single [resonant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resonance "Resonance") frequency. Other phenomena can be modeled by simple harmonic motion, including the motion of a [simple pendulum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum "Pendulum"), although for it to be an accurate model, the [net force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Net_force "Net force") on the object at the end of the pendulum must be proportional to the displacement (and even so, it is only a good approximation when the angle of the swing is small; see [small-angle approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small-angle_approximation "Small-angle approximation")). Simple harmonic motion can also be used to model [molecular vibration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_vibration "Molecular vibration"). A mass-spring system is a classic example of simple harmonic motion.

Simple harmonic motion provides a basis for the characterization of more complicated periodic motion through the techniques of [Fourier analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_analysis "Fourier analysis").

The motion of a [particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle "Particle") moving along a straight line with an [acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration") whose direction is always toward a [fixed point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixed_point_(mathematics) "Fixed point (mathematics)") on the line and whose magnitude is proportional to the displacement from the fixed point is called simple harmonic motion.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_harmonic_motion#cite_note-2)

In the diagram, a [simple harmonic oscillator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_oscillator "Harmonic oscillator"), consisting of a weight attached to one end of a spring, is shown. The other end of the spring is connected to a rigid support such as a wall. If the system is left at rest at the [equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_equilibrium "Mechanical equilibrium") position then there is no net [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force") acting on the mass. However, if the mass is displaced from the equilibrium position, the spring [exerts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exertion "Exertion") a restoring [elastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elasticity_(physics) "Elasticity (physics)") force that obeys [Hooke's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hooke%27s_law "Hooke's law").

Mathematically, ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =-k\mathbf {x} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7174b725e5991247ec3ca5df61bba48171cad8bf) where **F** is the restoring elastic force exerted by the spring (in [SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") units: [N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_(unit) "Newton (unit)")), _k_ is the [spring constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hooke%27s_law "Hooke's law") ([N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_(unit) "Newton (unit)")·m−1), and **x** is the [displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Displacement_(vector) "Displacement (vector)") from the equilibrium position (in [metres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre "Metre")).

For any simple mechanical harmonic oscillator:

*   When the system is displaced from its equilibrium position, a restoring force that obeys Hooke's law tends to restore the system to equilibrium.

Once the mass is displaced from its equilibrium position, it experiences a net restoring force. As a result, it [accelerates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration") and starts going back to the equilibrium position. When the mass moves closer to the equilibrium position, the restoring force decreases. At the equilibrium position, the net restoring force vanishes. However, at _x_ = 0, the mass has [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum") because of the acceleration that the restoring force has imparted. Therefore, the mass continues past the equilibrium position, compressing the spring. A net restoring force then slows it down until its [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") reaches zero, whereupon it is accelerated back to the equilibrium position again.

As long as the system has no [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy") loss, the mass continues to oscillate. Thus simple harmonic motion is a type of [periodic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency") motion. If energy is lost in the system, then the mass exhibits [damped oscillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Damped_oscillator "Damped oscillator").

Note if the real space and [phase space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_space "Phase space") plot are not co-linear, the phase space motion becomes elliptical. The area enclosed depends on the amplitude and the maximum momentum.

In [Newtonian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newtonian_mechanics "Newtonian mechanics"), for one-dimensional simple harmonic motion, the equation of motion, which is a second-order linear [ordinary differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinary_differential_equation "Ordinary differential equation") with **constant** coefficients, can be obtained by means of [Newton's second law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_second_law "Newton's second law") and [Hooke's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hooke%27s_law "Hooke's law") for a [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") on a [spring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spring_(device) "Spring (device)").

A mass _m_ attached to a spring of spring constant _k_ exhibits simple harmonic motion in [closed space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_space "Closed space"). The equation for describing the period: ![Image 34: {\displaystyle T=2\pi {\sqrt {\frac {m}{k}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cfe96d957f67cf00dd80be7aceee0c7d89b306ad) shows the period of oscillation is independent of the amplitude, though in practice the amplitude should be small. The above equation is also valid in the case when an additional constant force is being applied on the mass, i.e. the additional constant force cannot change the period of oscillation.

### Uniform circular motion

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Simple_harmonic_motion&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Uniform circular motion")]

Simple harmonic motion can be considered the one-dimensional [projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projection_(mathematics) "Projection (mathematics)") of [uniform circular motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_circular_motion "Uniform circular motion"). If an object moves with angular speed _ω_ around a circle of radius _r_ centered at the [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)") of the _xy_-plane, then its motion along each coordinate is simple harmonic motion with amplitude _r_ and angular frequency _ω_.

The motion of a body in which it moves to and from a definite point is also called [oscillatory motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oscillatory_motion "Oscillatory motion") or vibratory motion. The time period is able to be calculated by ![Image 35: {\displaystyle T=2\pi {\sqrt {\frac {l}{g}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5413ebd719825801e48ca3b82cec455ad2cb9a6c) where l is the distance from rotation to the object's center of mass undergoing SHM and g is acceleration due to gravity. This is analogous to the mass-spring system.

### Mass of a simple pendulum

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Simple_harmonic_motion&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Mass of a simple pendulum")]

In the [small-angle approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small-angle_approximation "Small-angle approximation"), the [motion of a simple pendulum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#Small-angle_approximation "Pendulum (mechanics)") is approximated by simple harmonic motion. The period of a mass attached to a pendulum of length _l_ with gravitational acceleration ![Image 36: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) is given by ![Image 37: {\displaystyle T=2\pi {\sqrt {\frac {l}{g}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5413ebd719825801e48ca3b82cec455ad2cb9a6c)

This shows that the period of oscillation is independent of the amplitude and mass of the pendulum but not of the acceleration due to [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity"), ![Image 38: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77), therefore a pendulum of the same length on the Moon would swing more slowly due to the Moon's lower gravitational field strength. Because the value of ![Image 39: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) varies slightly over the surface of the earth, the time period will vary slightly from place to place and will also vary with height above sea level.

This approximation is accurate only for small angles because of the expression for [angular acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_acceleration "Angular acceleration")_α_ being proportional to the sine of the displacement angle:

![Image 40: {\displaystyle -mgl\sin \theta =I\alpha ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1414f794af6df00291e5a88956b6656c348fcdcd)

where _I_ is the [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia"). When _θ_ is small, sin _θ_ ≈ _θ_ and therefore the expression becomes

![Image 41: {\displaystyle -mgl\theta =I\alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca2a51bd0e7ba3850387a9dc18b7e6c08dfe14e1)

which makes angular acceleration directly proportional and opposite to _θ_, satisfying the definition of simple harmonic motion (that net force is directly proportional to the displacement from the mean position and is directed towards the mean position).

A Scotch yoke mechanism can be used to convert between rotational motion and linear reciprocating motion. The linear motion can take various forms depending on the shape of the slot, but the basic yoke with a constant rotation speed produces a linear motion that is simple harmonic in form.
