**Velocity** is a measurement of [speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed "Speed") in a certain direction of [motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion "Motion"). It is a fundamental concept in [kinematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinematics "Kinematics"), the branch of [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics") that describes the motion of [physical objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_object "Physical object"). Velocity is a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(geometry) "Vector (geometry)")[quantity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_quantity "Physical quantity"), meaning that both magnitude and direction are needed to define it. The [scalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(physics) "Scalar (physics)")[absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value") ([magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitude_(mathematics) "Magnitude (mathematics)")) of velocity is called _speed_, being a coherent derived unit whose quantity is measured in the [SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") ([metric system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_system "Metric system")) as [metres per second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metres_per_second "Metres per second") (m/s or m⋅s−1). For example, "5 metres per second" is a scalar, whereas "5 metres per second east" is a vector. If there is a change in speed, direction or both, then the object is said to be undergoing an _[acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration")_.

Definition
----------

### Average velocity

The **average velocity** of an object over a period of time is its [change in position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Displacement_(geometry) "Displacement (geometry)"), ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \Delta s}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd7783a6d29d2ca4d9f1e0f501c4c6483fc058bd), divided by the duration of the period, ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \Delta t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c28867ecd34e2caed12cf38feadf6a81a7ee542), given mathematically as[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity#cite_note-1)![Image 5: {\displaystyle {\bar {v}}={\frac {\Delta s}{\Delta t}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f850430d0dfbf91063475e6f7d1fde29b7bc4197)

### Instantaneous velocity

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Velocity_vs_time_graph.svg/330px-Velocity_vs_time_graph.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Velocity_vs_time_graph.svg)

Example of a velocity vs. time graph, and the relationship between velocity _**v**_ on the y-axis, acceleration _**a**_ (the three green [tangent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent "Tangent") lines represent the values for acceleration at different points along the curve) and displacement _**s**_ (the yellow [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area") under the curve.)

The **instantaneous****velocity** of an object is the limit average velocity as the time interval approaches zero. At any particular time _t_, it can be calculated as the [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") of the position with respect to time:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity#cite_note-2)![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {v}}=\lim _{{\Delta t}\to 0}{\frac {\Delta {\boldsymbol {s}}}{\Delta t}}={\frac {d{\boldsymbol {s}}}{dt}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c520da6821aef2c6755d33ae8768522f705bbd4)

From this derivative equation, in the one-dimensional case it can be seen that the area under a velocity vs. time (_**v**_ vs. _t_ graph) is the displacement, _**s**_. In calculus terms, the [integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral") of the velocity function _**v**_(_t_) is the displacement function _**s**_(_t_). In the figure, this corresponds to the yellow area under the curve. [![Image 8: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {s}}=\int {\boldsymbol {v}}\ dt.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f162d7c395c914f1ec977aa7e642245db69cff59)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q190291)

Although the concept of an instantaneous velocity might at first seem counter-intuitive, it may be thought of as the velocity that the object would continue to travel at if it stopped accelerating at that moment.

### Difference between speed and velocity

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/98/Kinematics.svg/330px-Kinematics.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Kinematics.svg)

Kinematic quantities of a classical particle: mass _m_, position **r**, velocity **v**, acceleration **a**.

While the terms _speed_ and _velocity_ are often colloquially used interchangeably to connote how fast an object is moving, in scientific terms they are different. Speed, the [scalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(mathematics) "Scalar (mathematics)") magnitude of a velocity vector, denotes only how fast an object is moving, while velocity indicates both an object's speed and direction.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity#cite_note-5)

To have a _constant velocity_, an object must have a constant speed in a constant direction. Constant direction constrains the object to motion in a straight path thus, a constant velocity means motion in a straight line at a constant speed.

For example, a car moving at a constant 20 kilometres per hour in a circular path has a constant speed, but does not have a constant velocity because its direction changes. Hence, the car is considered to be undergoing an acceleration.

### Units

Since the derivative of the position with respect to time gives the change in position (in [metres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre "Metre")) divided by the change in time (in [seconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second "Second")), velocity is measured in [metres per second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre_per_second "Metre per second") (m/s).

Equation of motion
------------------

### Average velocity

Velocity is defined as the rate of change of position with respect to time, which may also be referred to as the _instantaneous velocity_ to emphasize the distinction from the average velocity. In some applications the average velocity of an object might be needed, that is to say, the constant velocity that would provide the same resultant displacement as a variable velocity in the same time interval, **v**(_t_), over some time period Δ _t_. Average velocity can be calculated as:[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity#cite_note-7)

[![Image 10: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\bar {v}} ={\frac {\Delta \mathbf {x} }{\Delta t}}={\frac {\int _{t_{0}}^{t_{1}}\mathbf {v} (t)dt}{t_{1}-t_{0}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45ae34e9004440d09fd074cb2726d449fb5d270f)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q11465)
The average velocity is always less than or equal to the average speed of an object. This can be seen by realizing that while distance is always strictly increasing, displacement can increase or decrease in magnitude as well as change direction.

In terms of a displacement-time (_x_ vs. _t_) graph, the instantaneous velocity (or, simply, velocity) can be thought of as the [slope of the tangent line to the curve at any point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative"), and the average velocity as the slope of the [secant line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Secant_line "Secant line") between two points with _t_ coordinates equal to the boundaries of the time period for the average velocity.

*   When a particle moves different distances _s_ 1, _s_ 2, _s_ 3,..., _s_ _n_ with speeds _v_ 1, _v_ 2, _v_ 3,..., _v_ _n_ respectively, then the average speed of the particle over the total distance is given as[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity#cite_note-giba-8)

### Relationship to acceleration

Although velocity is defined as the rate of change of position, it is often common to start with an expression for an object's [acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration"). As seen by the three green tangent lines in the figure, an object's instantaneous acceleration at a [point in time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_in_time "Point in time") is the [slope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slope "Slope") of the [line tangent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent "Tangent") to the curve of a _**v**_(_t_) graph at that point. In other words, instantaneous acceleration is defined as the derivative of velocity with respect to time:[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity#cite_note-9)[![Image 15: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {a}}={\frac {d{\boldsymbol {v}}}{dt}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e20f5766521f9cefe67dd72659912f7fe328006)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q11376)

From there, velocity is expressed as the area under an _**a**_(_t_) acceleration vs. time graph. As above, this is done using the concept of the integral:

![Image 16: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {v}}=\int {\boldsymbol {a}}\ dt.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc962bfefbe3815ab44a4bc515d647f517fd4ade)

#### Constant acceleration

In the special case of constant acceleration, velocity can be studied using the [suvat equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equations_of_motion "Equations of motion"). By considering **a** as being equal to some arbitrary constant vector, this shows ![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {v}}={\boldsymbol {u}}+{\boldsymbol {a}}t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2383bac190384019d8ac941667388c264b0020f5) with _**v**_ as the velocity at time _t_ and _**u**_ as the velocity at time _t_ = 0. By combining this equation with the suvat equation _**x**_ = _**u**t_ + _**a**t_ 2/2, it is possible to relate the displacement and the average velocity by ![Image 18: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {x}}={\frac {({\boldsymbol {u}}+{\boldsymbol {v}})}{2}}t={\boldsymbol {\bar {v}}}t.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9ff9936117cce56447f1ab8f8282c43dbb5b9651) It is also possible to derive an expression for the velocity independent of time, known as the [Torricelli equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torricelli_equation "Torricelli equation"), as follows: ![Image 19: {\displaystyle v^{2}={\boldsymbol {v}}\cdot {\boldsymbol {v}}=({\boldsymbol {u}}+{\boldsymbol {a}}t)\cdot ({\boldsymbol {u}}+{\boldsymbol {a}}t)=u^{2}+2t({\boldsymbol {a}}\cdot {\boldsymbol {u}})+a^{2}t^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cfd7fa70b4698fcd8c12da6ac493c4adc86cfa5d)![Image 20: {\displaystyle (2{\boldsymbol {a}})\cdot {\boldsymbol {x}}=(2{\boldsymbol {a}})\cdot ({\boldsymbol {u}}t+{\tfrac {1}{2}}{\boldsymbol {a}}t^{2})=2t({\boldsymbol {a}}\cdot {\boldsymbol {u}})+a^{2}t^{2}=v^{2}-u^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8712f1af8299e0d02cca662ff168d9b6ec5e6c58)![Image 21: {\displaystyle \therefore v^{2}=u^{2}+2({\boldsymbol {a}}\cdot {\boldsymbol {x}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/37f317b20013b07b6684279ee04df6df143feb43) where _v_ = |_**v**_| etc.

The above equations are valid for both [Newtonian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newtonian_mechanics "Newtonian mechanics") and [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity"). Where Newtonian mechanics and special relativity differ is in how different observers would describe the same situation. In particular, in Newtonian mechanics, all observers agree on the value of t and the transformation rules for position create a situation in which all non-accelerating observers would describe the acceleration of an object with the same values. Neither is true for special relativity. In other words, only relative velocity can be calculated.

Quantities that are dependent on velocity
-----------------------------------------

### Momentum

In classical mechanics, [Newton's second law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion "Newton's laws of motion") defines [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum"), p, as a vector that is the product of an object's mass and velocity, given mathematically as[![Image 22: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {p}}=m{\boldsymbol {v}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0403efd1c19022e2a513bdedf3dd28c2bfac6529)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q41273)where _m_ is the mass of the object.

### Kinetic energy

The [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy") of a moving object is dependent on its velocity and is given by the equation[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity#cite_note-10)[![Image 23: {\displaystyle E_{\text{k}}={\tfrac {1}{2}}mv^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/82c6f8ceda1650271df82f27287811c32c629a68)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q46276)where _E_ k is the kinetic energy. Kinetic energy is a scalar quantity as it depends on the square of the velocity.

### Drag (fluid resistance)

In [fluid dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_dynamics "Fluid dynamics"), [drag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drag_(physics) "Drag (physics)") is a force acting opposite to the relative motion of any object moving with respect to a surrounding fluid. The drag force, ![Image 24: {\displaystyle F_{D}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ac0e98e9397b14b2f9399967a0c2154fa2c14d69), is dependent on the square of velocity and is given as[![Image 25: {\displaystyle F_{D}\,=\,{\tfrac {1}{2}}\,\rho \,v^{2}\,C_{D}\,A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5b3bf12f95f6d0174755a9248ba34e638cf90310)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q9300786)where

### Escape velocity

[Escape velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Escape_velocity "Escape velocity") is the minimum speed a ballistic object needs to escape from a massive body such as Earth. It represents the kinetic energy that, when added to the object's [gravitational potential energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_energy "Gravitational energy") (which is always negative), is equal to zero. The general formula for the escape velocity of an object at a distance _r_ from the center of a planet with mass _M_ is[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity#cite_note-12)![Image 26: {\displaystyle v_{\text{e}}={\sqrt {\frac {2GM}{r}}}={\sqrt {2gr}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf5f7091703c75b27b9e16afc50a423826172dc2)where _G_ is the [gravitational constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_constant "Gravitational constant") and _g_ is the [gravitational acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_acceleration "Gravitational acceleration"). The escape velocity from Earth's surface is about 11 200 m/s, and is irrespective of the direction of the object. This makes "escape velocity" somewhat of a misnomer, as the more correct term would be "escape speed": any object attaining a velocity of that magnitude, irrespective of atmosphere, will leave the vicinity of the base body as long as it does not intersect with something in its path.

### The Lorentz factor of special relativity

In [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity"), the dimensionless [Lorentz factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_factor "Lorentz factor") appears frequently, and is given by[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity#cite_note-13)[![Image 27: {\displaystyle \gamma ={\frac {1}{\sqrt {1-{\frac {v^{2}}{c^{2}}}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abacf009fa4db767f015172584c8fc89e7725745)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q599404)where γ is the Lorentz factor and _c_ is the speed of light.

Relative velocity
-----------------

_Relative velocity_ is a measurement of velocity between two objects as determined in a single coordinate system. Relative velocity is fundamental in both classical and modern physics, since many systems in physics deal with the relative motion of two or more particles.

Consider an object A moving with velocity [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(geometry) "Vector (geometry)")_**v**_ and an object B with velocity vector _**w**_; these _absolute velocities_ are typically expressed in the same [inertial reference frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertial_reference_frame "Inertial reference frame"). Then, the velocity of object A _relative to_ object B is defined as the difference of the two velocity vectors: ![Image 28: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {v}}_{A{\text{ relative to }}B}={\boldsymbol {v}}-{\boldsymbol {w}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/689fc010646bee4f8e33d494e8cfc14aa48f23aa) Similarly, the relative velocity of object B moving with velocity _**w**_, relative to object A moving with velocity _**v**_ is: ![Image 29: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {v}}_{B{\text{ relative to }}A}={\boldsymbol {w}}-{\boldsymbol {v}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dbcc6df6ba3093f9c27e09c9ecfb127b9a9c0181) Usually, the inertial frame chosen is that in which the latter of the two mentioned objects is in rest.

In Newtonian mechanics, the relative velocity is independent of the chosen inertial reference frame. This is not the case anymore with [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity") in which velocities depend on the choice of reference frame.

### Scalar velocities

In the one-dimensional case,[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity#cite_note-14) the velocities are scalars and the equation is either: ![Image 30: {\displaystyle v_{\text{rel}}=v-(-w),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab97e20572220b7ae089e956816bbe16838f5d07) if the two objects are moving in opposite directions, or: ![Image 31: {\displaystyle v_{\text{rel}}=v-(+w),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c0e2e942a347349b1681fe0d6a1be0369cd46e9f) if the two objects are moving in the same direction.

### Polar coordinates

[![Image 45](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/81/Radial_and_tangential.svg/180px-Radial_and_tangential.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Radial_and_tangential.svg)

Representation of radial and tangential components of velocity at different moments of linear motion with constant velocity of the object around an observer O (it corresponds, for example, to the passage of a car on a straight street around a pedestrian standing on the sidewalk). The radial component can be observed due to the [Doppler effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doppler_effect "Doppler effect"), the tangential component causes visible changes of the position of the object.

In [polar coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinate_system "Polar coordinate system"), a two-dimensional velocity is described by a _[radial velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radial\_velocity "Radial velocity")_, defined as the component of velocity away from or toward the origin, and a _transverse velocity_, perpendicular to the radial one.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity#cite_note-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity#cite_note-18) Both arise from [angular velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity"), which is the rate of rotation about the origin (with positive quantities representing counter-clockwise rotation and negative quantities representing clockwise rotation, in a right-handed coordinate system).

The radial and traverse velocities can be derived from the Cartesian velocity and displacement vectors by decomposing the velocity vector into radial and transverse components. The [transverse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transversality_(mathematics) "Transversality (mathematics)") velocity is the component of velocity along a circle centered at the origin. ![Image 46: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {v}}={\boldsymbol {v}}_{T}+{\boldsymbol {v}}_{R}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db5135b2eec7c55ccca8ee11c24c925548f3b7d5) where

*   ![Image 47: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {v}}_{T}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43c40e468c9cb22f80dec9b4d32e7be3e30596cf) is the transverse velocity
*   ![Image 48: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {v}}_{R}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ddd6bf6e6f2f67300cc226ed6be4f0e26c6b2f86) is the radial velocity.

The _radial speed_ (or magnitude of the radial velocity) is the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") of the velocity vector and the unit vector in the radial direction. ![Image 49: {\displaystyle v_{R}={\frac {{\boldsymbol {v}}\cdot {\boldsymbol {r}}}{\left|{\boldsymbol {r}}\right|}}={\boldsymbol {v}}\cdot {\hat {\boldsymbol {r}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/80fb6b950e94f3ff2837e532047a2f9773ac06ef) where ![Image 50: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {r}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6a5e169814762d75ef0dd3a3d0bc99b4a5a06e0) is position and ![Image 51: {\displaystyle {\hat {\boldsymbol {r}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c458bca4da5e11f38bc2199b9a6dde50e43d0918) is the radial direction.

The transverse speed (or magnitude of the transverse velocity) is the magnitude of the [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product") of the unit vector in the radial direction and the velocity vector. It is also the dot product of velocity and transverse direction, or the product of the [angular speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_speed "Angular speed")![Image 52: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) and the radius (the magnitude of the position). ![Image 53: {\displaystyle v_{T}={\frac {|{\boldsymbol {r}}\times {\boldsymbol {v}}|}{|{\boldsymbol {r}}|}}={\boldsymbol {v}}\cdot {\hat {\boldsymbol {t}}}=\omega |{\boldsymbol {r}}|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee965e67e15e37459eb2b374f49017d4771c6540) such that ![Image 54: {\displaystyle \omega ={\frac {|{\boldsymbol {r}}\times {\boldsymbol {v}}|}{|{\boldsymbol {r}}|^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bdfa7e988c3d9a919762eed73902a00717ca2627)

[Angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum") in scalar form is the mass times the distance to the origin times the transverse velocity, or equivalently, the mass times the distance squared times the angular speed. The sign convention for angular momentum is the same as that for angular velocity. ![Image 55: {\displaystyle L=mrv_{T}=mr^{2}\omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/240389e39df12f106643f870682e7ea86929326e) where

*   ![Image 56: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) is mass
*   ![Image 57: {\displaystyle r=|{\boldsymbol {r}}|.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2793c314e3d4c0ecd89d4c18ecb53ed970aea6b)

The expression ![Image 58: {\displaystyle mr^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ddd9d0ea2911509b014b72a7b536acb7376cb455) is known as [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia"). If forces are in the radial direction only with an inverse square dependence, as in the case of a gravitational [orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit "Orbit"), angular momentum is constant, and transverse speed is inversely proportional to the distance, angular speed is inversely proportional to the distance squared, and the rate at which area is swept out is constant. These relations are known as [Kepler's laws of planetary motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion "Kepler's laws of planetary motion").
