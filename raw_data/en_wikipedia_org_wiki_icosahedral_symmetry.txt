Rotations and reflections form the symmetry group of a [great icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_icosahedron "Great icosahedron").

In mathematics, and especially in geometry, an object has **icosahedral symmetry** if it has the same [symmetries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetries "Symmetries") as a [regular icosahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_icosahedron "Regular icosahedron"). Examples of other [polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedra "Polyhedra") with icosahedral symmetry include the [regular dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_dodecahedron "Regular dodecahedron") (the [dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polyhedron "Dual polyhedron") of the icosahedron) and the [rhombic triacontahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombic_triacontahedron "Rhombic triacontahedron").

Every polyhedron with icosahedral symmetry has 60 [rotational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_symmetry "Rotational symmetry") (or orientation-preserving) symmetries and 60 orientation-reversing symmetries (that combine a rotation and a [reflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_symmetry "Reflection symmetry")), for a total [symmetry order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_order "Symmetry order") of 120. The full [symmetry group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_group "Symmetry group") is the [Coxeter group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group "Coxeter group") of type H 3. It may be represented by [Coxeter notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_notation "Coxeter notation")[5,3] and [Coxeter diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_diagram "Coxeter diagram")![Image 34](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png)![Image 35](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/1/16/CDel_5.png)![Image 36](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png)![Image 37](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c3/CDel_3.png)![Image 38](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/5e/CDel_node.png). The set of rotational symmetries forms a subgroup that is isomorphic to the [alternating group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternating_group "Alternating group")A 5 on 5 letters.

Apart from the two infinite series of prismatic and antiprismatic symmetry, **rotational icosahedral symmetry** or **chiral icosahedral symmetry** of chiral objects and **full icosahedral symmetry** or **achiral icosahedral symmetry** are the [discrete point symmetries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_groups_in_three_dimensions "Point groups in three dimensions") (or equivalently, [symmetries on the sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_spherical_symmetry_groups "List of spherical symmetry groups")) with the largest [symmetry groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_group "Symmetry group").

Icosahedral symmetry is not compatible with [translational symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translational_symmetry "Translational symmetry"), so there are no associated [crystallographic point groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal_system#Overview_of_point_groups_by_crystal_system "Crystal system") or [space groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_group "Space group").

Every [polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron "Polyhedron") with icosahedral symmetry has 60 [rotational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_symmetry "Rotational symmetry") (or orientation-preserving) symmetries and 60 orientation-reversing symmetries (that combine a rotation and a [reflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_symmetry "Reflection symmetry")), for a total [symmetry order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_order "Symmetry order") of 120.

The **icosahedral rotation group**_**I**_ is of order 60. The group _I_ is [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphic "Isomorphic") to _A_ 5, the [alternating group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternating_group "Alternating group") of even permutations of five objects. This isomorphism can be realized by _I_ acting on various compounds, notably the [compound of five cubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_of_five_cubes "Compound of five cubes") (which inscribe in the [dodecahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedron "Dodecahedron")), the [compound of five octahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_of_five_octahedra "Compound of five octahedra"), or either of the two [compounds of five tetrahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_of_five_tetrahedra "Compound of five tetrahedra") (which are [enantiomorphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enantiomorphs "Enantiomorphs"), and inscribe in the dodecahedron). The group contains 5 versions of _T_ h with 20 versions of _D 3_ (10 axes, 2 per axis), and 6 versions of _D 5_.

The **full icosahedral group**_**I h**_ has order 120. It has _I_ as [normal subgroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_subgroup "Normal subgroup") of [index](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Index_of_a_subgroup "Index of a subgroup") 2. The group _I h_ is isomorphic to _I_ × _Z_ 2, or _A_ 5 × _Z_ 2, with the [inversion in the center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inversion_in_a_point "Inversion in a point") corresponding to element (identity,-1), where _Z_ 2 is written multiplicatively.

_I h_ acts on the [compound of five cubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_of_five_cubes "Compound of five cubes") and the [compound of five octahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_of_five_octahedra "Compound of five octahedra"), but −1 acts as the identity (as cubes and octahedra are centrally symmetric). It acts on the [compound of ten tetrahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_of_ten_tetrahedra "Compound of ten tetrahedra"): _I_ acts on the two chiral halves ([compounds of five tetrahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_of_five_tetrahedra "Compound of five tetrahedra")), and −1 interchanges the two halves. Notably, it does _not_ act as S 5, and these groups are not isomorphic; see below for details.

The group contains 10 versions of _D 3d_ and 6 versions of _D 5d_ (symmetries like antiprisms).

_I_ is also isomorphic to PSL 2(5), but _I h_ is not isomorphic to SL 2(5).

### Isomorphism of _I_ with A 5

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Icosahedral_symmetry&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Isomorphism of I with A5")]

It is useful to describe explicitly what the isomorphism between _I_ and A 5 looks like. In the following table, permutations P i and Q i act on 5 and 12 elements respectively, while the rotation matrices M i are the elements of _I_. If P k is the product of taking the permutation P i and applying P j to it, then for the same values of _i_, _j_ and _k_, it is also true that Q k is the product of taking Q i and applying Q j, and also that premultiplying a vector by M k is the same as premultiplying that vector by M i and then premultiplying that result with M j, that is M k = M j × M i. Since the permutations P i are all the 60 even permutations of 12345, the [one-to-one correspondence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijection "Bijection") is made explicit, therefore the isomorphism too.

### Commonly confused groups

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Icosahedral_symmetry&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Commonly confused groups")]

The following groups all have order 120, but are not isomorphic:

*   _S_ 5, the [symmetric group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_group "Symmetric group") on 5 elements
*   _I h_, the full icosahedral group (subject of this article, also known as _H_ 3)
*   2 _I_, the [binary icosahedral group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_icosahedral_group "Binary icosahedral group")

They correspond to the following [short exact sequences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Short_exact_sequence "Short exact sequence") (the latter of which does not split) and product

In words,

Note that ![Image 270: {\displaystyle A_{5}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e213bbb69691c65e1391fe16cd79a0029471446) has an [exceptional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exceptional_object "Exceptional object") irreducible 3-dimensional [representation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_representation "Linear representation") (as the icosahedral rotation group), but ![Image 271: {\displaystyle S_{5}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d113353a42f71fc5e7154ddef2257079ab2e25a) does not have an irreducible 3-dimensional representation, corresponding to the full icosahedral group not being the symmetric group.

*   vertex stabilizers in _I h_ give [dihedral groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihedral_symmetry_in_three_dimensions "Dihedral symmetry in three dimensions")_D_ 3
*   stabilizers of an opposite pair of vertices in _I_ give dihedral groups _D_ 3
*   stabilizers of an opposite pair of vertices in _I h_ give ![Image 384: {\displaystyle D_{3}\times \pm 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c633e910868413a255c185be4e799d2c218ab56d)

Stabilizers of an opposite pair of edges can be interpreted as stabilizers of the rectangle they generate.

Stabilizers of an opposite pair of faces can be interpreted as stabilizers of the [antiprism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiprism "Antiprism") they generate.

*   face stabilizers in _I_ give cyclic groups _C_ 5
*   face stabilizers in _I h_ give dihedral groups _D_ 5
*   stabilizers of an opposite pair of faces in _I_ give dihedral groups _D_ 5
*   stabilizers of an opposite pair of faces in _I h_ give ![Image 385: {\displaystyle D_{5}\times \pm 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/435f95647411264d39c51d9a5c1c1f61e8dad3a8)

#### Polyhedron stabilizers

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Icosahedral_symmetry&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Polyhedron stabilizers")]

For each of these, there are 5 conjugate copies, and the conjugation action gives a map, indeed an isomorphism, ![Image 386: {\displaystyle I{\stackrel {\sim }{\to }}A_{5}<S_{5}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e09e712547d5689ea9234569d52c18c556d1dd69).

*   stabilizers of the inscribed tetrahedra in _I_ are a copy of _T_
*   stabilizers of the inscribed tetrahedra in _I h_ are a copy of _T_
*   stabilizers of the inscribed cubes (or opposite pair of tetrahedra, or octahedra) in _I_ are a copy of _T_
*   stabilizers of the inscribed cubes (or opposite pair of tetrahedra, or octahedra) in _I h_ are a copy of _T h_

In the [disdyakis triacontahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disdyakis_triacontahedron "Disdyakis triacontahedron") one full face is a fundamental domain; other solids with the same symmetry can be obtained by adjusting the orientation of the faces, e.g. flattening selected subsets of faces to combine each subset into one face, or replacing each face by multiple faces, or a curved surface.

### Liquid crystals with icosahedral symmetry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Icosahedral_symmetry&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Liquid crystals with icosahedral symmetry")]

For the intermediate material phase called [liquid crystals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_crystals "Liquid crystals") the existence of icosahedral symmetry was proposed by [H. Kleinert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hagen_Kleinert "Hagen Kleinert") and K. Maki[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedral_symmetry#cite_note-2) and its structure was first analyzed in detail in that paper. See the review article [here](http://chemgroups.northwestern.edu/seideman/Publications/The%20liquid-crystalline%20blue%20phases.pdf). In aluminum, the icosahedral structure was discovered experimentally three years after this by [Dan Shechtman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dan_Shechtman "Dan Shechtman"), which earned him the Nobel Prize in 2011.

### Icosahedral nanoparticles

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Icosahedral_symmetry&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Icosahedral nanoparticles")]

At small sizes, many elements form [icosahedral nanoparticles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedral_nanoparticle "Icosahedral nanoparticle"), which are often lower in energy than [single crystals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Single_crystal "Single crystal").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedral_symmetry#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedral_symmetry#cite_note-4)

Icosahedral symmetry is equivalently the [projective special linear group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_special_linear_group "Projective special linear group") PSL(2,5), and is the symmetry group of the [modular curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modular_curve "Modular curve") X(5), and more generally PSL(2,_p_) is the symmetry group of the modular curve X(_p_). The modular curve X(5) is geometrically a dodecahedron with a cusp at the center of each polygonal face, which demonstrates the symmetry group.

This geometry, and associated symmetry group, was studied by [Felix Klein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein "Felix Klein") as the [monodromy groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monodromy_group "Monodromy group") of a Belyi surface – a Riemann surface with a holomorphic map to the Riemann sphere, ramified only at 0, 1, and infinity (a [Belyi function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belyi_function "Belyi function")) – the cusps are the points lying over infinity, while the vertices and the centers of each edge lie over 0 and 1; the degree of the covering (number of sheets) equals 5.

This arose from his efforts to give a geometric setting for why icosahedral symmetry arose in the solution of the [quintic equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quintic_equation "Quintic equation"), with the theory given in the famous ([Klein 1888](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedral_symmetry#CITEREFKlein1888)); a modern exposition is given in ([Tóth 2002](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedral_symmetry#CITEREFT%C3%B3th2002), Section 1.6, Additional Topic: Klein's Theory of the Icosahedron, [p. 66](https://books.google.com/books?id=i76mmyvDHYUC&pg=PA66)).

Klein's investigations continued with his discovery of order 7 and order 11 symmetries in ([Klein 1878](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedral_symmetry#CITEREFKlein1878)) and ([Klein 1879](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Icosahedral_symmetry#CITEREFKlein1879)) (and associated coverings of degree 7 and 11) and [dessins d'enfants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dessins_d%27enfants "Dessins d'enfants"), the first yielding the [Klein quartic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klein_quartic "Klein quartic"), whose associated geometry has a tiling by 24 heptagons (with a cusp at the center of each).

Similar geometries occur for PSL(2,_n_) and more general groups for other modular curves.

More exotically, there are special connections between the groups PSL(2,5) (order 60), [PSL(2,7)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PSL(2,7) "PSL(2,7)") (order 168) and PSL(2,11) (order 660), which also admit geometric interpretations – PSL(2,5) is the symmetries of the icosahedron (genus 0), PSL(2,7) of the [Klein quartic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klein_quartic "Klein quartic") (genus 3), and PSL(2,11) the [buckyball surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buckyball_surface "Buckyball surface") (genus 70). These groups form a "[trinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ADE_classification#Trinities "ADE classification")" in the sense of [Vladimir Arnold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vladimir_Arnold "Vladimir Arnold"), which gives a framework for the various relationships; see _[trinities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ADE\_classification#Trinities "ADE classification")_ for details.

There is a close relationship to other [Platonic solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solids "Platonic solids").
