Double integral as volume under a surface _z_ = 10 − (⁠_x_ 2 − _y_ 2/8⁠). The rectangular region at the bottom of the body is the _[domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain\_of\_a\_function "Domain of a function")_ of integration, while the **surface** is the graph of the two-variable function to be integrated.

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") (specifically [multivariable calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivariable_calculus "Multivariable calculus")), a **multiple integral** is a [definite integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Definite_integral "Definite integral") of a [function of several real variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_of_several_real_variables "Function of several real variables"), for instance, _f_(_x_, _y_) or _f_(_x_, _y_, _z_).

Integrals of a function of two variables over a region in ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd) (the [real-number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_line "Real line") plane) are called **double integrals**, and integrals of a function of three variables over a region in ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) (real-number 3D space) are called **triple integrals**.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_integral#cite_note-Stewart-1) For repeated antidifferentiation of a single-variable function, see the [Cauchy formula for repeated integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy_formula_for_repeated_integration "Cauchy formula for repeated integration").

Just as the definite integral of a positive function of one variable represents the [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area") of the region between the graph of the function and the x-axis, the **double integral** of a positive function of two variables represents the [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume") of the region between the surface defined by the function (on the three-dimensional [Cartesian plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_plane "Cartesian plane") where _z_ = _f_(_x_, _y_)) and the plane which contains its [domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_of_a_function "Domain of a function").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_integral#cite_note-Stewart-1) If there are more variables, a multiple integral will yield [hypervolumes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypervolume "Hypervolume") of multidimensional functions.

Multiple integration of a function in n variables: _f_(_x_ 1, _x_ 2, ..., _x_ _n_) over a domain D is most commonly represented by nested integral signs in the reverse order of execution (the leftmost integral sign is computed last), followed by the function and integrand arguments in proper order (the integral with respect to the rightmost argument is computed last). The domain of integration is either represented symbolically for every argument over each integral sign, or is abbreviated by a variable at the rightmost integral sign:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_integral#cite_note-2)

![Image 5: {\displaystyle \int \cdots \int _{\mathbf {D} }\,f(x_{1},x_{2},\ldots ,x_{n})\,dx_{1}\!\cdots dx_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9dbd6adc7f6c2d295f1ff64b1e12a88f8cea975e)
Since the concept of an [antiderivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiderivative "Antiderivative") is only defined for functions of a single real variable, the usual definition of the [indefinite integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indefinite_integral "Indefinite integral") does not immediately extend to the multiple integral.

Mathematical definition
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Multiple_integral&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Mathematical definition")]

For _n_> 1, consider a so-called "half-open" n-dimensional [hyperrectangular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperrectangle "Hyperrectangle") domain T, defined as

![Image 6: {\displaystyle T=[a_{1},b_{1})\times [a_{2},b_{2})\times \cdots \times [a_{n},b_{n})\subseteq \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f3893700ad858d4918116db27c4518ed7a0859c4).
[Partition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partition_(set_theory) "Partition (set theory)") each interval [_a_ _j_, _b_ _j_) into a finite family I j of non-overlapping subintervals i j α, with each subinterval closed at the left end, and open at the right end.

Then the finite family of subrectangles C given by

![Image 7: {\displaystyle C=I_{1}\times I_{2}\times \cdots \times I_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be8a3269d99ec69ac104fc2083c0eff4fd1ef6b7)
is a [partition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partition_(set_theory) "Partition (set theory)") of T; that is, the subrectangles C k are non-overlapping and their union is T.

Let _f_: _T_ → **R** be a function defined on T. Consider a partition C of T as defined above, such that C is a family of m subrectangles C m and

![Image 8: {\displaystyle T=C_{1}\cup C_{2}\cup \cdots \cup C_{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe843c1fa7472f72343cf08934098c31c7d76930)
We can approximate the total (_n_ + 1)-dimensional volume bounded below by the n-dimensional hyperrectangle T and above by the n-dimensional graph of f with the following [Riemann sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_sum "Riemann sum"):

![Image 9: {\displaystyle \sum _{k=1}^{m}f(P_{k})\,\operatorname {m} (C_{k})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d6050780567e6d787c4a509a36c4408542bb9a5e)
where P k is a point in C k and m(_C_ _k_) is the product of the lengths of the intervals whose Cartesian product is C k, also known as the measure of C k.

The **diameter** of a subrectangle C k is the largest of the lengths of the intervals whose [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product") is C k. The diameter of a given partition of T is defined as the largest of the diameters of the subrectangles in the partition. Intuitively, as the diameter of the partition C is restricted smaller and smaller, the number of subrectangles m gets larger, and the measure m(_C_ _k_) of each subrectangle grows smaller. The function f is said to be **Riemann integrable** if the [limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_(mathematics) "Limit (mathematics)")

![Image 10: {\displaystyle S=\lim _{\delta \to 0}\sum _{k=1}^{m}f(P_{k})\,\operatorname {m} (C_{k})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eeb659b556148ff612a674e247ff8b82ab1c59cb)
exists, where the limit is taken over all possible partitions of T of diameter at most δ.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_integral#cite_note-3)

If f is Riemann integrable, S is called the **Riemann integral** of f over T and is denoted

![Image 11: {\displaystyle \int \cdots \int _{T}\,f(x_{1},x_{2},\ldots ,x_{n})\,dx_{1}\!\cdots dx_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73e2a05e5766f4f0924c146fd374613bd6655121).
Frequently this notation is abbreviated as

![Image 12: {\displaystyle \int _{T}\!f(\mathbf {x} )\,d^{n}\mathbf {x} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81db33f46e5859e066d5b33f702e80d69d933dc0).
where **x** represents the n-tuple (_x_ 1, ..., _x n_) and _d_ _n_**x** is the n-dimensional volume [differential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_(infinitesimal) "Differential (infinitesimal)").

The Riemann integral of a function defined over an arbitrary bounded n-dimensional set can be defined by extending that function to a function defined over a half-open rectangle whose values are zero outside the domain of the original function. Then the integral of the original function over the original domain is defined to be the integral of the extended function over its rectangular domain, if it exists.

In what follows the Riemann integral in n dimensions will be called the **multiple integral**.

Multiple integrals have many properties common to those of integrals of functions of one variable (linearity, commutativity, monotonicity, and so on). One important property of multiple integrals is that the value of an integral is independent of the order of integrands under certain conditions. This property is popularly known as [Fubini's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fubini%27s_theorem "Fubini's theorem").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_integral#cite_note-a-4)

In the case of ![Image 13: {\displaystyle T\subseteq \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dc9effa6ad429d21c0cee3d797fa834dbfc7fef7), the integral

![Image 14: {\displaystyle l=\iint _{T}f(x,y)\,dx\,dy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/714661437572b2a29e292983a54cc3ef2fb9b26f)
is the **double integral** of f on T, and if ![Image 15: {\displaystyle T\subseteq \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e9b44712175e4ff78ccf4c69b9d1752f0a351671) the integral

![Image 16: {\displaystyle l=\iiint _{T}f(x,y,z)\,dx\,dy\,dz}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32a5f5b15042528fbdc6be0c939fd2c3fa820f9a)
is the **triple integral** of f on T.

Notice that, by convention, the double integral has two integral signs, and the triple integral has three; this is a notational convention which is convenient when computing a multiple integral as an iterated integral, as shown later in this article.

Methods of integration
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Multiple_integral&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Methods of integration")]

The resolution of problems with multiple integrals consists, in most cases, of finding a way to reduce the multiple integral to an [iterated integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iterated_integral "Iterated integral"), a series of integrals of one variable, each being directly solvable. For continuous functions, this is justified by [Fubini's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fubini%27s_theorem "Fubini's theorem"). Sometimes, it is possible to obtain the result of the integration by direct examination without any calculations.

The following are some simple methods of integration:[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_integral#cite_note-Stewart-1)

### Integrating constant functions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Multiple_integral&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Integrating constant functions")]

When the integrand is a [constant function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_function "Constant function")c, the integral is equal to the product of c and the measure of the domain of integration. If _c_ = 1 and the domain is a subregion of **R**2, the integral gives the area of the region, while if the domain is a subregion of **R**3, the integral gives the volume of the region.

> **Example.** Let _f_(_x_, _y_) = 2 and
> 
> ![Image 17: {\displaystyle D=\left\{(x,y)\in \mathbb {R} ^{2}\ :\ 2\leq x\leq 4\ ;\ 3\leq y\leq 6\right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/018a15fc965756b62ebd69a7138c8b7a0c33e658),
> in which case
> 
> ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \int _{3}^{6}\int _{2}^{4}\ 2\ dx\,dy=2\int _{3}^{6}\int _{2}^{4}\ 1\ dx\,dy=2\cdot \operatorname {area} (D)=2\cdot (2\cdot 3)=12}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c969898e0fd8b05d862556f2c86faa1e07e9c00c),
> since by definition we have:
> 
> ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \int _{3}^{6}\int _{2}^{4}\ 1\ dx\,dy=\operatorname {area} (D)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e53ec44c4e992ae3875ed0d197ab25efb62e972).

When the domain of integration is symmetric about the origin with respect to at least one of the variables of integration and the integrand is [odd](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Even_and_odd_functions "Even and odd functions") with respect to this variable, the integral is equal to zero, as the integrals over the two halves of the domain have the same absolute value but opposite signs. When the integrand is [even](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Even_and_odd_functions "Even and odd functions") with respect to this variable, the integral is equal to twice the integral over one half of the domain, as the integrals over the two halves of the domain are equal.

> **Example 1.** Consider the function _f_(_x_,_y_) = 2 sin(_x_) − 3 _y_ 3 + 5 integrated over the domain
> 
> ![Image 20: {\displaystyle T=\left\{(x,y)\in \mathbb {R} ^{2}\ :\ x^{2}+y^{2}\leq 1\right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3cecb2d267f2c250c440e07d04a15682e7983550),
> a disc with [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius")1 centered at the origin with the boundary included.
> 
> 
> Using the linearity property, the integral can be decomposed into three pieces:
> 
> ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \iint _{T}\left(2\sin x-3y^{3}+5\right)\,dx\,dy=\iint _{T}2\sin x\,dx\,dy-\iint _{T}3y^{3}\,dx\,dy+\iint _{T}5\,dx\,dy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2af84890c63ab4c82d6f89990ccabe58caf99a2).
> The function 2 sin(_x_) is an odd function in the variable x and the disc T is symmetric with respect to the y-axis, so the value of the first integral is 0. Similarly, the function 3 _y_ 3 is an odd function of y, and T is symmetric with respect to the x-axis, and so the only contribution to the final result is that of the third integral. Therefore the original integral is equal to the area of the disk times 5, or 5 π.

> **Example 2.** Consider the function _f_(_x_, _y_, _z_) = _x_ exp(_y_ 2 + _z_ 2) and as integration region the [ball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ball_(mathematics) "Ball (mathematics)") with radius 2 centered at the origin,
> 
> ![Image 22: {\displaystyle T=\left\{(x,y,z)\in \mathbb {R} ^{3}\ :\ x^{2}+y^{2}+z^{2}\leq 4\right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38e883c55f7f6db707670d7f2346fefb92381bd8).
> The "ball" is symmetric about all three axes, but it is sufficient to integrate with respect to x-axis to show that the integral is 0, because the function is an odd function of that variable.

### Normal domains on R 2

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Multiple_integral&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Normal domains on R2")]

This method is applicable to any domain D for which:

*   The [projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthographic_projection "Orthographic projection") of D onto either the x-axis or the y-axis is bounded by the two values, a and b
*   Any line perpendicular to this axis that passes between these two values intersects the domain in an interval whose endpoints are given by the graphs of two functions, α and β

Such a domain will be here called a _normal domain_. Elsewhere in the literature, normal domains are sometimes called type I or type II domains, depending on which axis the domain is fibred over. In all cases, the function to be integrated must be Riemann integrable on the domain, which is true (for instance) if the function is continuous.

If the domain D is normal with respect to the x-axis, and _f_: _D_ → **R** is a [continuous function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function"); then _α_(_x_) and _β_(_x_) (both of which are defined on the interval [_a_, _b_]) are the two functions that determine D. Then, by Fubini's theorem:[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_integral#cite_note-5)

![Image 23: {\displaystyle \iint _{D}f(x,y)\,dx\,dy=\int _{a}^{b}dx\int _{\alpha (x)}^{\beta (x)}f(x,y)\,dy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/200fa129b3d69d829fa973e6d7266dd8a77af7e7).
If D is normal with respect to the y-axis and _f_: _D_ → **R** is a continuous function; then _α_(_y_) and _β_(_y_) (both of which are defined on the interval [_a_, _b_]) are the two functions that determine D. Again, by Fubini's theorem:

![Image 24: {\displaystyle \iint _{D}f(x,y)\,dx\,dy=\int _{a}^{b}dy\int _{\alpha (y)}^{\beta (y)}f(x,y)\,dx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eae570424b402b4655bc58857076d30fbcdf71be).

#### Normal domains on R 3

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Multiple_integral&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Normal domains on R3")]

If T is a domain that is normal with respect to the xy-plane and determined by the functions _α_(_x_, _y_) and _β_(_x_, _y_), then

![Image 25: {\displaystyle \iiint _{T}f(x,y,z)\,dx\,dy\,dz=\iint _{D}\int _{\alpha (x,y)}^{\beta (x,y)}f(x,y,z)\,dz\,dx\,dy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0f25d70d5a31e647da0d7d81746d157ebc5da739).
This definition is the same for the other five normality cases on **R**3. It can be generalized in a straightforward way to domains in **R**_n_.

### Change of variables

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Multiple_integral&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Change of variables")]

The limits of integration are often not easily interchangeable (without normality or with complex formulae to integrate). One makes a [change of variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Change_of_variables "Change of variables") to rewrite the integral in a more "comfortable" region, which can be described in simpler formulae. To do so, the function must be adapted to the new coordinates.

> **Example 1a.** The function is _f_(_x_, _y_) = (_x_ − 1)2 + √_y_; if one adopts the substitution _u_ = _x_ − 1, _v_ = _y_ therefore _x_ = _u_ + 1, _y_ = _v_ one obtains the new function _f_ 2(_u_, _v_) = (_u_)2 + √_v_.

*   Similarly for the domain because it is delimited by the original variables that were transformed before (x and y in example)
*   The differentials dx and dy transform via the absolute value of the [determinant of the Jacobian matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_matrix_and_determinant "Jacobian matrix and determinant") containing the partial derivatives of the transformations regarding the new variable (consider, as an example, the differential transformation in polar coordinates)

There exist three main "kinds" of changes of variable (one in **R**2, two in **R**3); however, more general substitutions can be made using the same principle.

[![Image 26](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/38/Passaggio_in_coordinate_polari.svg/330px-Passaggio_in_coordinate_polari.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Passaggio_in_coordinate_polari.svg)

Transformation from cartesian to polar coordinates.

In **R**2 if the domain has a circular symmetry and the function has some particular characteristics one can apply the _transformation to polar coordinates_ (see the example in the picture) which means that the generic points _P_(_x_, _y_) in Cartesian coordinates switch to their respective points in polar coordinates. That allows one to change the shape of the domain and simplify the operations.

The fundamental relation to make the transformation is the following:

![Image 27: {\displaystyle f(x,y)\rightarrow f(\rho \cos \varphi ,\rho \sin \varphi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2dd7fac85795a5c686479f7366c1bc0d914f06b1).
> **Example 2a.** The function is _f_(_x_, _y_) = _x_ + _y_ and applying the transformation one obtains
> 
> ![Image 28: {\displaystyle f(x,y)=f(\rho \cos \varphi ,\rho \sin \varphi )=\rho \cos \varphi +\rho \sin \varphi =\rho (\cos \varphi +\sin \varphi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e29f0771337b22191c867815e86c05ea76e38e21).

> **Example 2b.** The function is _f_(_x_, _y_) = _x_ 2 + _y_ 2, in this case one has:
> 
> ![Image 29: {\displaystyle f(x,y)=\rho ^{2}\left(\cos ^{2}\varphi +\sin ^{2}\varphi \right)=\rho ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34671f90570f3dbf29f2341867215ecea2fcea2)
> using the [Pythagorean trigonometric identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_trigonometric_identity "Pythagorean trigonometric identity") (can be useful to simplify this operation).

The transformation of the domain is made by defining the radius' crown length and the amplitude of the described angle to define the _ρ_, _φ_ intervals starting from _x_, _y_.

[![Image 30](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c2/Esempio_trasformazione_dominio_da_cartesiano_polare.svg/250px-Esempio_trasformazione_dominio_da_cartesiano_polare.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Esempio_trasformazione_dominio_da_cartesiano_polare.svg)

Example of a domain transformation from cartesian to polar.

> **Example 2c.** The domain is _D_ = {_x_ 2 + _y_ 2 ≤ 4}, that is a circumference of radius 2; it's evident that the covered angle is the circle angle, so φ varies from 0 to 2 π, while the crown radius varies from 0 to 2 (the crown with the inside radius null is just a circle).

> **Example 2d.** The domain is _D_ = {_x_ 2 + _y_ 2 ≤ 9, _x_ 2 + _y_ 2 ≥ 4, _y_ ≥ 0}, that is the circular crown in the positive y half-plane (please see the picture in the example); φ describes a plane angle while ρ varies from 2 to 3. Therefore the transformed domain will be the following [rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle "Rectangle"):
> 
> ![Image 31: {\displaystyle T=\{2\leq \rho \leq 3,\ 0\leq \varphi \leq \pi \}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c98ce64699fbbb32e881c6ef8e2a7ac46a0c44d).
> The [Jacobian determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobian_determinant "Jacobian determinant") of that transformation is the following:
> 
> ![Image 32: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial (x,y)}{\partial (\rho ,\varphi )}}={\begin{vmatrix}\cos \varphi &-\rho \sin \varphi \\\sin \varphi &\rho \cos \varphi \end{vmatrix}}=\rho }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/562c31f4611ebe3d7829aadf32fb2c5a7247b0dc),
> which has been obtained by inserting the partial derivatives of _x_ = _ρ_ cos(_φ_), _y_ = _ρ_ sin(_φ_) in the first column respect to ρ and in the second respect to φ, so the dx dy differentials in this transformation become ρ dρ dφ.
> 
> 
> Once the function is transformed and the domain evaluated, it is possible to define the formula for the change of variables in polar coordinates:
> 
> ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \iint _{D}f(x,y)\,dx\,dy=\iint _{T}f(\rho \cos \varphi ,\rho \sin \varphi )\rho \,d\rho \,d\varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8572920311b8b30efa2c0ad19dcaea04d48454e8).
> φ is valid in the [0, 2π] interval while ρ, which is a measure of a length, can only have positive values.

> **Example 2e.** The function is _f_(_x_, _y_) = _x_ and the domain is the same as in Example 2d. From the previous analysis of D we know the intervals of ρ (from 2 to 3) and of φ (from 0 to π). Now we change the function:
> 
> ![Image 34: {\displaystyle f(x,y)=x\longrightarrow f(\rho ,\varphi )=\rho \cos \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/00fd02b947ef5bc05f2e95a3b7303c2bf0cf02c5).
> Finally let's apply the integration formula:
> 
> ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \iint _{D}x\,dx\,dy=\iint _{T}\rho \cos \varphi \rho \,d\rho \,d\varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7426b924abdd7ed28f6a3869a7219781b764ba73).
> Once the intervals are known, you have
> 
> ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \int _{0}^{\pi }\int _{2}^{3}\rho ^{2}\cos \varphi \,d\rho \,d\varphi =\int _{0}^{\pi }\cos \varphi \ d\varphi \left[{\frac {\rho ^{3}}{3}}\right]_{2}^{3}={\Big [}\sin \varphi {\Big ]}_{0}^{\pi }\ \left(9-{\frac {8}{3}}\right)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/488232a8d128256d4d7e6ae51b1d88aacd784a79).

#### Cylindrical coordinates

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Multiple_integral&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Cylindrical coordinates")]

[![Image 37](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Cylindrical_Coordinates.svg/250px-Cylindrical_Coordinates.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cylindrical_Coordinates.svg)

Cylindrical coordinates.

In **R**3 the integration on domains with a circular base can be made by the _passage to [cylindrical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylindrical\_coordinate\_system "Cylindrical coordinate system")_; the transformation of the function is made by the following relation:

![Image 38: {\displaystyle f(x,y,z)\rightarrow f(\rho \cos \varphi ,\rho \sin \varphi ,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4884f4f3733d2de9886c45ed5f53d45a711f28df)
The domain transformation can be graphically attained, because only the shape of the base varies, while the height follows the shape of the starting region.

> **Example 3a.** The region is _D_ = {_x_ 2 + _y_ 2 ≤ 9, _x_ 2 + _y_ 2 ≥ 4, 0 ≤ _z_ ≤ 5} (that is the "tube" whose base is the circular crown of Example 2d and whose height is 5); if the transformation is applied, this region is obtained:
> 
> ![Image 39: {\displaystyle T=\{2\leq \rho \leq 3,\ 0\leq \varphi \leq 2\pi ,\ 0\leq z\leq 5\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71038d84c202461685dea5b5551950ea910b04ae)
> (that is, the parallelepiped whose base is similar to the rectangle in Example 2d and whose height is 5).
> 
> 
> Because the z component is unvaried during the transformation, the dx dy dz differentials vary as in the passage to polar coordinates: therefore, they become ρ dρ dφ dz.
> 
> 
> Finally, it is possible to apply the final formula to cylindrical coordinates:
> 
> ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \iiint _{D}f(x,y,z)\,dx\,dy\,dz=\iiint _{T}f(\rho \cos \varphi ,\rho \sin \varphi ,z)\rho \,d\rho \,d\varphi \,dz}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/49d0d223cf6938118f16d564f0284f9cea2b7c83).
> This method is convenient in case of cylindrical or conical domains or in regions where it is easy to individuate the _z_ interval and even transform the circular base and the function.

> **Example 3b.** The function is _f_(_x_, _y_, _z_) = _x_ 2 + _y_ 2 + _z_ and as integration domain this [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry) "Cylinder (geometry)"): _D_ = {_x_ 2 + _y_ 2 ≤ 9, −5 ≤ _z_ ≤ 5}. The transformation of D in cylindrical coordinates is the following:
> 
> ![Image 41: {\displaystyle T=\{0\leq \rho \leq 3,\ 0\leq \varphi \leq 2\pi ,\ -5\leq z\leq 5\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/efed9a175c4880f5222b3b604aa4c746fc9889a9).
> while the function becomes
> 
> ![Image 42: {\displaystyle f(\rho \cos \varphi ,\rho \sin \varphi ,z)=\rho ^{2}+z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c107d6e6f9f2dcde208d68b6ebda57738d238f59).
> Finally one can apply the integration formula:
> 
> ![Image 43: {\displaystyle \iiint _{D}\left(x^{2}+y^{2}+z\right)\,dx\,dy\,dz=\iiint _{T}\left(\rho ^{2}+z\right)\rho \,d\rho \,d\varphi \,dz}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18ad56263c48cad4d149066ac601720189b9d947);
> developing the formula you have
> 
> ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \int _{-5}^{5}dz\int _{0}^{2\pi }d\varphi \int _{0}^{3}\left(\rho ^{3}+\rho z\right)\,d\rho =2\pi \int _{-5}^{5}\left[{\frac {\rho ^{4}}{4}}+{\frac {\rho ^{2}z}{2}}\right]_{0}^{3}\,dz=2\pi \int _{-5}^{5}\left({\frac {81}{4}}+{\frac {9}{2}}z\right)\,dz=\cdots =405\pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2500dfd50534740e5d38e2fa2c45093e78874b94).

#### Spherical coordinates

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Multiple_integral&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Spherical coordinates")]

[![Image 45](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/51/Spherical_Coordinates_%28Colatitude%2C_Longitude%29.svg/250px-Spherical_Coordinates_%28Colatitude%2C_Longitude%29.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Spherical_Coordinates_(Colatitude,_Longitude).svg)

Spherical coordinates.

In **R**3 some domains have a spherical symmetry, so it's possible to specify the coordinates of every point of the integration region by two angles and one distance. It's possible to use therefore the _passage to [spherical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical\_coordinate\_system "Spherical coordinate system")_; the function is transformed by this relation:

![Image 46: {\displaystyle f(x,y,z)\longrightarrow f(\rho \cos \theta \sin \varphi ,\rho \sin \theta \sin \varphi ,\rho \cos \varphi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3fb8eda6d7911b2e165352e7ac83b3b313e91f33).
Points on the z-axis do not have a precise characterization in spherical coordinates, so θ can vary between 0 and 2 π.

The better integration domain for this passage is the sphere.

> **Example 4a.** The domain is _D_ = _x_ 2 + _y_ 2 + _z_ 2 ≤ 16 (sphere with radius 4 and center at the origin); applying the transformation you get the region
> 
> ![Image 47: {\displaystyle T=\{0\leq \rho \leq 4,\ 0\leq \varphi \leq \pi ,\ 0\leq \theta \leq 2\pi \}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6946ea490e47f84f2de98135182348e79ced4dcb).
> The Jacobian determinant of this transformation is the following:
> 
> ![Image 48: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial (x,y,z)}{\partial (\rho ,\varphi ,\theta )}}={\begin{vmatrix}\cos \theta \sin \varphi &\rho \cos \theta \cos \varphi &-\rho \sin \theta \sin \varphi \\\sin \theta \sin \varphi &\rho \sin \theta \cos \varphi &\rho \cos \theta \sin \varphi \\\cos \varphi &-\rho \sin \varphi &0\end{vmatrix}}=\rho ^{2}\sin \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10c57ea6a8f283c24f2874b7e6d86976e9e6a9fb).
> The dx dy dz differentials therefore are transformed to _ρ_ 2 sin(_φ_) _dρ_ _dθ_ _dφ_.
> 
> 
> This yields the final integration formula:
> 
> ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \iiint _{D}f(x,y,z)\,dx\,dy\,dz=\iiint _{T}f(\rho \sin \varphi \cos \theta ,\rho \sin \varphi \sin \theta ,\rho \cos \varphi )\rho ^{2}\sin \varphi \,d\rho \,d\theta \,d\varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a9d6210207020c455779fb374d12cac871c612f).

It is better to use this method in case of spherical domains **and** in case of functions that can be easily simplified by the first fundamental relation of trigonometry extended to **R**3 (see Example 4b); in other cases it can be better to use cylindrical coordinates (see Example 4c).

![Image 50: {\displaystyle \iiint _{T}f(a,b,c)\rho ^{2}\sin \varphi \,d\rho \,d\theta \,d\varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/16dba67956000c68f90212598130689b0fb8b0b0).
The extra _ρ_ 2 and sin _φ_ come from the Jacobian.

In the following examples the roles of φ and θ have been reversed.

> **Example 4b.**D is the same region as in Example 4a and _f_(_x_, _y_, _z_) = _x_ 2 + _y_ 2 + _z_ 2 is the function to integrate. Its transformation is very easy:
> 
> ![Image 51: {\displaystyle f(\rho \sin \varphi \cos \theta ,\rho \sin \varphi \sin \theta ,\rho \cos \varphi )=\rho ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1df01ed0546200cc33f3f037bec7f5c8a13b3c06),
> while we know the intervals of the transformed region T from D:
> 
> ![Image 52: {\displaystyle T=\{0\leq \rho \leq 4,\ 0\leq \varphi \leq \pi ,\ 0\leq \theta \leq 2\pi \}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6946ea490e47f84f2de98135182348e79ced4dcb).
> We therefore apply the integration formula:
> 
> ![Image 53: {\displaystyle \iiint _{D}\left(x^{2}+y^{2}+z^{2}\right)\,dx\,dy\,dz=\iiint _{T}\rho ^{2}\,\rho ^{2}\sin \theta \,d\rho \,d\theta \,d\varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90075bd8932215bc9c0344975d99a96d3a913666),
> and, developing, we get
> 
> ![Image 54: {\displaystyle \iiint _{T}\rho ^{4}\sin \theta \,d\rho \,d\theta \,d\varphi =\int _{0}^{\pi }\sin \varphi \,d\varphi \int _{0}^{4}\rho ^{4}d\rho \int _{0}^{2\pi }d\theta =2\pi \int _{0}^{\pi }\sin \varphi \left[{\frac {\rho ^{5}}{5}}\right]_{0}^{4}\,d\varphi =2\pi \left[{\frac {\rho ^{5}}{5}}\right]_{0}^{4}{\Big [}-\cos \varphi {\Big ]}_{0}^{\pi }={\frac {4096\pi }{5}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5531a5587dfd81d00a1af111b24d7e7f21af9828).

> **Example 4c.** The domain D is the ball with center at the origin and radius 3 _a_,

### Double integral over a rectangle

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Multiple_integral&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Double integral over a rectangle")]

Let us assume that we wish to integrate a multivariable function f over a region A:

![Image 67: {\displaystyle A=\left\{(x,y)\in \mathbf {R} ^{2}\ :\ 11\leq x\leq 14\ ;\ 7\leq y\leq 10\right\}{\mbox{ and }}f(x,y)=x^{2}+4y\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0fca958ef6a70f47af9fb88c91ea7f49a3a7b46).
From this we formulate the iterated integral

![Image 68: {\displaystyle \int _{7}^{10}\int _{11}^{14}(x^{2}+4y)\,dx\,dy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/03504a4b18203c78c5b6f2b5ac21f331bf1ac165).
The inner integral is performed first, integrating with respect to x and taking y as a constant, as it is not the [variable of integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_of_integration "Variable of integration"). The result of this integral, which is a function depending only on y, is then integrated with respect to y.

![Image 69: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\int _{11}^{14}\left(x^{2}+4y\right)\,dx&=\left[{\frac {1}{3}}x^{3}+4yx\right]_{x=11}^{x=14}\\&={\frac {1}{3}}(14)^{3}+4y(14)-{\frac {1}{3}}(11)^{3}-4y(11)\\&=471+12y\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e379a90e5e09f77929215570fd91403ed6369fc)
We then integrate the result with respect to y.

![Image 70: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\int _{7}^{10}(471+12y)\ dy&={\Big [}471y+6y^{2}{\Big ]}_{y=7}^{y=10}\\&=471(10)+6(10)^{2}-471(7)-6(7)^{2}\\&=1719\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0be5e82a2ea1d74e5c4855cda9e3cc1a983a64c)
In cases where the double integral of the absolute value of the function is finite, the order of integration is interchangeable, that is, integrating with respect to _x_ first and integrating with respect to _y_ first produce the same result. That is [Fubini's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fubini%27s_theorem "Fubini's theorem"). For example, doing the previous calculation with order reversed gives the same result:

![Image 71: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\int _{11}^{14}\int _{7}^{10}\,\left(x^{2}+4y\right)\,dy\,dx&=\int _{11}^{14}{\Big [}x^{2}y+2y^{2}{\Big ]}_{y=7}^{y=10}\,dx\\&=\int _{11}^{14}\,(3x^{2}+102)\,dx\\&={\Big [}x^{3}+102x{\Big ]}_{x=11}^{x=14}\\&=1719.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6902d512bb9ffd641e8f845affaa6a7cfdf34b58)

*   **[Sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere")**: The volume of a sphere with radius R can be calculated by integrating the constant function 1 over the sphere, using spherical coordinates.

![Image 82: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\text{Volume}}&=\iiint _{D}f(x,y,z)\,dx\,dy\,dz\\&=\iiint _{D}1\,dV\\&=\iiint _{S}\rho ^{2}\sin \varphi \,d\rho \,d\theta \,d\varphi \\&=\int _{0}^{2\pi }\,d\theta \int _{0}^{\pi }\sin \varphi \,d\varphi \int _{0}^{R}\rho ^{2}\,d\rho \\&=2\pi \int _{0}^{\pi }\sin \varphi \,d\varphi \int _{0}^{R}\rho ^{2}\,d\rho \\&=2\pi \int _{0}^{\pi }\sin \varphi {\frac {R^{3}}{3}}\,d\varphi \\&={\frac {2}{3}}\pi R^{3}{\Big [}-\cos \varphi {\Big ]}_{0}^{\pi }={\frac {4}{3}}\pi R^{3}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c7c16461e99854efe8bac837727e3d7deafef419)

![Image 83: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\text{Volume}}&=\int _{0}^{\ell }dx\int _{0}^{\ell -x}\,dy\int _{0}^{\ell -x-y}\,dz\\&=\int _{0}^{\ell }dx\int _{0}^{\ell -x}(\ell -x-y)\,dy\\&=\int _{0}^{\ell }\left(l^{2}-2\ell x+x^{2}-{\frac {(\ell -x)^{2}}{2}}\right)\,dx\\&=\ell ^{3}-\ell \ell ^{2}+{\frac {\ell ^{3}}{3}}-\left[{\frac {\ell ^{2}x}{2}}-{\frac {\ell x^{2}}{2}}+{\frac {x^{3}}{6}}\right]_{0}^{\ell }\\&={\frac {\ell ^{3}}{3}}-{\frac {\ell ^{3}}{6}}={\frac {\ell ^{3}}{6}}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27d9273e17f4a9be07068ddd2c1e782008c3f0dc)This is in agreement with the formula for the volume of a [pyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyramid "Pyramid"). ![Image 84: {\displaystyle \mathrm {Volume} ={\frac {1}{3}}\times {\text{base area}}\times {\text{height}}={\frac {1}{3}}\times {\frac {\ell ^{2}}{2}}\times \ell ={\frac {\ell ^{3}}{6}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/126ed84317f001822c51c89f44c9791d4fbabca7).[![Image 85](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Dominio_improprio.svg/250px-Dominio_improprio.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Dominio_improprio.svg)

Multiple improper integral
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Multiple_integral&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Multiple improper integral")]

In case of unbounded domains or functions not bounded near the boundary of the domain, we have to introduce the **double [improper integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Improper_integral "Improper integral")** or the **triple improper integral**.

Multiple integrals and iterated integrals
-----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Multiple_integral&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Multiple integrals and iterated integrals")]

[Fubini's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fubini%27s_theorem "Fubini's theorem") states that if[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_integral#cite_note-a-4)

![Image 86: {\displaystyle \iint _{A\times B}\left|f(x,y)\right|\,d(x,y)<\infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99c4b21e7e8bd5d83fd96742444129a930ff8190),
that is, if the integral is absolutely convergent, then the multiple integral will give the same result as either of the two iterated integrals:

![Image 87: {\displaystyle \iint _{A\times B}f(x,y)\,d(x,y)=\int _{A}\left(\int _{B}f(x,y)\,dy\right)\,dx=\int _{B}\left(\int _{A}f(x,y)\,dx\right)\,dy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/15844f5992a187b6f7c46b8256ebff7734b5ea68).
In particular this will occur if |_f_(_x_, _y_)| is a [bounded function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounded_function "Bounded function") and A and B are [bounded sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounded_set "Bounded set").

If the integral is not absolutely convergent, care is needed not to confuse the concepts of _multiple integral_ and _iterated integral_, especially since the same notation is often used for either concept. The notation

![Image 88: {\displaystyle \int _{0}^{1}\int _{0}^{1}f(x,y)\,dy\,dx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4f918a4289283edb105d026270fe7620f0779b3f)
means, in some cases, an iterated integral rather than a true double integral. In an iterated integral, the outer integral

![Image 89: {\displaystyle \int _{0}^{1}\cdots \,dx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db03f664a56684d322926a3fa3517717d8fefc28)
is the integral with respect to x of the following function of x:

![Image 90: {\displaystyle g(x)=\int _{0}^{1}f(x,y)\,dy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/147f194d3bcd89402a1b97bf029a99637aef890c).
A double integral, on the other hand, is defined with respect to area in the xy-plane. If the double integral exists, then it is equal to each of the two iterated integrals (either "dy dx" or "dx dy") and one often computes it by computing either of the iterated integrals. But sometimes the two iterated integrals exist when the double integral does not, and in some such cases the two iterated integrals are different numbers, i.e., one has

![Image 91: {\displaystyle \int _{0}^{1}\int _{0}^{1}f(x,y)\,dy\,dx\neq \int _{0}^{1}\int _{0}^{1}f(x,y)\,dx\,dy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0934fb3a0e5b68055bfff65b26eef597ac2c937f).
This is an instance of rearrangement of a [conditionally convergent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conditional_convergence "Conditional convergence") integral.

On the other hand, some conditions ensure that the two iterated integrals are equal even though the double integral need not exist. By the [Fichtenholz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grigorii_Fichtenholz "Grigorii Fichtenholz")–[Lichtenstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leon_Lichtenstein "Leon Lichtenstein") theorem, if f is bounded on [0, 1] × [0, 1] and both iterated integrals exist, then they are equal. Moreover, existence of the inner integrals ensures existence of the outer integrals.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_integral#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_integral#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_integral#cite_note-8) The double integral need not exist in this case even as [Lebesgue integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_integration "Lebesgue integration"), according to [Sierpiński](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wac%C5%82aw_Sierpi%C5%84ski "Wacław Sierpiński").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_integral#cite_note-9)

The notation

![Image 92: {\displaystyle \int _{[0,1]\times [0,1]}f(x,y)\,dx\,dy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0951af4caeac621f11d9b15d72e9b4c6c866d88c)
may be used if one wishes to be emphatic about intending a double integral rather than an iterated integral.

Triple integral was demonstrated by Fubini's theorem.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_integral#cite_note-10) Drichlet theorem and Liouville 's extension theorem on Triple integral.

Some practical applications
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Multiple_integral&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Some practical applications")]

Quite generally, just as in one variable, one can use the multiple integral to find the average of a function over a given set. Given a set _D_ ⊆ **R**_n_ and an integrable function f over D, the average value of f over its domain is given by

![Image 93: {\displaystyle {\bar {f}}={\frac {1}{m(D)}}\int _{D}f(x)\,dx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83ae669c99970ea39987d7c0de49542c8fb0b79e),
where _m_(_D_) is the [measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measure_(mathematics) "Measure (mathematics)") of D.

Additionally, multiple integrals are used in many applications in [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"). The examples below also show some variations in the notation.

In [mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics "Mechanics"), the [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia") is calculated as the volume integral (triple integral) of the [density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density "Density") weighed with the square of the distance from the axis:

![Image 94: {\displaystyle I_{z}=\iiint _{V}\rho r^{2}\,dV}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f951808b9f53de837659cd6b52a85b639138d84c).
The [gravitational potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_potential "Gravitational potential") associated with a [mass distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_distribution "Mass distribution") given by a mass [measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Borel_measure "Borel measure")dm on three-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space")**R**3 is[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_integral#cite_note-11)

![Image 95: {\displaystyle V(\mathbf {x} )=-\iiint _{\mathbf {R} ^{3}}{\frac {G}{|\mathbf {x} -\mathbf {y} |}}\,dm(\mathbf {y} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10fc8bb915610e348664b3fff4ec4886dcfdce13).
If there is a continuous function _ρ_(**x**) representing the density of the distribution at **x**, so that _dm_(**x**) = _ρ_(**x**)_d_ 3**x**, where _d_ 3**x** is the Euclidean [volume element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_element "Volume element"), then the gravitational potential is

![Image 96: {\displaystyle V(\mathbf {x} )=-\iiint _{\mathbf {R} ^{3}}{\frac {G}{|\mathbf {x} -\mathbf {y} |}}\,\rho (\mathbf {y} )\,d^{3}\mathbf {y} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4358a0f56c84d0cfbaaf5d63da8a84a605dabb0).
In [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism"), [Maxwell's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations "Maxwell's equations") can be written using multiple integrals to calculate the total magnetic and electric fields.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_integral#cite_note-12) In the following example, the [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field") produced by a distribution of [charges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge") given by the volume [charge density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charge_density "Charge density")_ρ_( _r_→ ) is obtained by a _triple integral_ of a vector function:

![Image 97: {\displaystyle {\vec {E}}={\frac {1}{4\pi \varepsilon _{0}}}\iiint {\frac {{\vec {r}}-{\vec {r}}'}{\left\|{\vec {r}}-{\vec {r}}'\right\|^{3}}}\rho ({\vec {r}}')\,d^{3}r'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e5ba218981cbf6decbd80102021f9590d326d70).
This can also be written as an integral with respect to a [signed measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signed_measure "Signed measure") representing the charge distribution.
