From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Attempt to demonstrate the 4th dimension in visual arts

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Jouffret.gif/250px-Jouffret.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Jouffret.gif)

An illustration from Jouffret's _Traité élémentaire de géométrie à quatre dimensions_. The book, which influenced Picasso, was given to him by Princet.

New possibilities opened up by the concept of [four-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-dimensional_space "Four-dimensional space") (and difficulties involved in trying to visualize it) helped inspire many modern artists in the first half of the twentieth century. Early [Cubists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubists "Cubists"), [Surrealists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surrealists "Surrealists"), [Futurists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Futurists "Futurists"), and [abstract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_art "Abstract art") artists took ideas from [higher-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higher-dimensional_space "Higher-dimensional space") mathematics and used them to radically advance their work.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHenderson2013-1)

Early influence
---------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fourth_dimension_in_art&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Early influence")]

Further information: [Proto-Cubism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proto-Cubism "Proto-Cubism") and [Mathematics and art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics_and_art "Mathematics and art")

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/6/68/Picasso_Portrait_of_Daniel-Henry_Kahnweiler_1910.jpg/250px-Picasso_Portrait_of_Daniel-Henry_Kahnweiler_1910.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Picasso_Portrait_of_Daniel-Henry_Kahnweiler_1910.jpg)

[Pablo Picasso](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pablo_Picasso "Pablo Picasso"), 1910 _Portrait of [Daniel-Henry Kahnweiler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daniel-Henry\_Kahnweiler "Daniel-Henry Kahnweiler")_, [Art Institute of Chicago](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Art_Institute_of_Chicago "Art Institute of Chicago")

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/8a/Jean_Metzinger%2C_1910%2C_Nu_%C3%A0_la_chemin%C3%A9e%2C_published_in_Les_Peintres_Cubistes%2C_1913.jpg/250px-Jean_Metzinger%2C_1910%2C_Nu_%C3%A0_la_chemin%C3%A9e%2C_published_in_Les_Peintres_Cubistes%2C_1913.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Jean_Metzinger,_1910,_Nu_%C3%A0_la_chemin%C3%A9e,_published_in_Les_Peintres_Cubistes,_1913.jpg)

[Jean Metzinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Metzinger "Jean Metzinger"), 1910, _[Nu à la cheminée](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nu\_%C3%A0\_la\_chemin%C3%A9e "Nu à la cheminée") (Nude)_. Exhibited at the 1910 Salon d'Automne. Black and white scan from _[Les Peintres Cubistes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Cubist\_Painters,\_Aesthetic\_Meditations "The Cubist Painters, Aesthetic Meditations")_ by Guillaume Apollinaire, 1913. [![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e1/Albert_Gleizes%2C_1913%2C_Portrait_de_l%E2%80%99%C3%A9diteur_Eug%C3%A8ne_Figui%C3%A8re_%28The_Publisher_Eugene_Figuiere%29%2C_oil_on_canvas%2C_143.5_x_101.5_cm%2C_Mus%C3%A9e_des_Beaux-Arts_de_Lyon.jpg/250px-thumbnail.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Albert_Gleizes,_1913,_Portrait_de_l%E2%80%99%C3%A9diteur_Eug%C3%A8ne_Figui%C3%A8re_(The_Publisher_Eugene_Figuiere),_oil_on_canvas,_143.5_x_101.5_cm,_Mus%C3%A9e_des_Beaux-Arts_de_Lyon.jpg)

[Albert Gleizes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Gleizes "Albert Gleizes"), 1913, _[Portrait de l’éditeur Eugène Figuière (The Publisher Eugene Figuiere)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portrait\_de\_l%E2%80%99%C3%A9diteur\_Eug%C3%A8ne\_Figui%C3%A8re\_(Gleizes) "Portrait de l’éditeur Eugène Figuière (Gleizes)")_, [Musée des Beaux-Arts de Lyon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_des_Beaux-Arts_de_Lyon "Musée des Beaux-Arts de Lyon")

French [mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician")[Maurice Princet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maurice_Princet "Maurice Princet") was known as "le mathématicien du cubisme" ("the mathematician of cubism").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-2) An associate of the [School of Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/School_of_Paris "School of Paris")—a group of [avant-gardists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avant-garde "Avant-garde") including [Pablo Picasso](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pablo_Picasso "Pablo Picasso"), [Guillaume Apollinaire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guillaume_Apollinaire "Guillaume Apollinaire"), [Max Jacob](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Jacob "Max Jacob"), [Jean Metzinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Metzinger "Jean Metzinger"), and [Marcel Duchamp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marcel_Duchamp "Marcel Duchamp")—Princet is credited with introducing the work of [Henri Poincaré](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9 "Henri Poincaré") and the concept of the "[fourth dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-dimensional_space "Four-dimensional space")" to the cubists at the [Bateau-Lavoir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_Bateau-Lavoir "Le Bateau-Lavoir") during the first decade of the 20th century.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-Miller-3)

Princet introduced Picasso to [Esprit Jouffret](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Esprit_Jouffret "Esprit Jouffret")'s _Traité élémentaire de géométrie à quatre dimensions_ (_Elementary Treatise on the Geometry of Four Dimensions_, 1903),[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-4) a popularization of Poincaré's _Science and Hypothesis_ in which Jouffret described [hypercubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube "Hypercube") and other complex [polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron "Polyhedron") in four [dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension") and projected them onto the two-dimensional page. Picasso's _[Portrait of Daniel-Henry Kahnweiler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Portrait\_of\_Daniel-Henry\_Kahnweiler "Portrait of Daniel-Henry Kahnweiler")_ in 1910 was an important work for the artist, who spent many months shaping it.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-5) The portrait bears similarities to Jouffret's work and shows a distinct movement away from the [Proto-Cubist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proto-Cubism "Proto-Cubism")[fauvism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fauvism "Fauvism") displayed in _[Les Demoiselles d'Avignon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Les\_Demoiselles\_d%27Avignon "Les Demoiselles d'Avignon")_, to a more considered analysis of space and form.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-6)

Early cubist [Max Weber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Weber_(artist) "Max Weber (artist)") wrote an article entitled "In The Fourth Dimension from a Plastic Point of View", for [Alfred Stieglitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_Stieglitz "Alfred Stieglitz")'s July 1910 issue of _[Camera Work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camera\_Work "Camera Work")_. In the piece, Weber states,[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-7) "In plastic art, I believe, there is a fourth dimension which may be described as the consciousness of a great and overwhelming sense of space-magnitude in all directions at one time, and is brought into existence through the three known measurements."

Another influence on the School of Paris was that of [Jean Metzinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Metzinger "Jean Metzinger") and [Albert Gleizes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Gleizes "Albert Gleizes"), both painters and theoreticians. The first major treatise written on the subject of Cubism was their 1912 collaboration _[Du "Cubisme"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Du\_%22Cubisme%22 "Du \"Cubisme\"")_, which says that:[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-8)

> "If we wished to relate the space of the [Cubist] painters to geometry, we should have to refer it to the non-Euclidian mathematicians; we should have to study, at some length, certain of [Riemann's theorems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernhard_Riemann#Riemannian_geometry "Bernhard Riemann")."

The American modernist painter and photographer [Morton Livingston Schamberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morton_Livingston_Schamberg "Morton Livingston Schamberg") wrote in 1910 two letters to [Walter Pach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Pach "Walter Pach"),[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-10) parts of which were published in a review of the [1913 Armory Show](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Armory_Show "Armory Show") for _[The Philadelphia Inquirer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Philadelphia\_Inquirer "The Philadelphia Inquirer")_,[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-11) about the influence of the fourth dimension on avant-garde painting; describing how the artists' employed "harmonic use of forms" distinguishing between the "representation or rendering of space and the designing in space":[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-13)

> If we still further add to design in the third dimension, a consideration of weight, pressure, resistance, movement, as distinguished from motion, we arrive at what may legitimately be called design in the fourth dimension, or the harmonic use of what may arbitrarily be called volume. It is only at this point that we can appreciate the masterly productions of such a man as Cézanne.

Cézanne's explorations of geometric simplification and optical phenomena inspired the Cubists to experiment with [simultaneity](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Simultaneity_(art)&action=edit&redlink=1 "Simultaneity (art) (page does not exist)"), complex multiple views of the same subject, as observed from differing viewpoints at the same time.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-14)

Dimensionist manifesto
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fourth_dimension_in_art&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Dimensionist manifesto")]

In 1936 in Paris, [Charles Tamkó Sirató](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Sirato "Charles Sirato") published his _Manifeste Dimensioniste_,[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-dimensionalist-15) which described how the Dimensionist tendency has led to:

1.   Literature leaving the line and entering the plane.
2.   Painting leaving the plane and entering space.
3.   Sculpture stepping out of closed, immobile forms.
4.   The artistic conquest of four-dimensional space, which to date has been completely art-free.

The manifesto was signed by many prominent modern artists worldwide. [Hans Arp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Arp "Hans Arp"), [Francis Picabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Francis_Picabia "Francis Picabia"), [Kandinsky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wassily_Kandinsky "Wassily Kandinsky"), [Robert Delaunay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Delaunay "Robert Delaunay") and Marcel Duchamp amongst others added their names in Paris, then a short while later it was endorsed by artists abroad including [László Moholy-Nagy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L%C3%A1szl%C3%B3_Moholy-Nagy "László Moholy-Nagy"), [Joan Miró](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joan_Mir%C3%B3 "Joan Miró"), [David Kakabadze](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Kakabadze "David Kakabadze"), [Alexander Calder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Calder "Alexander Calder"), and [Ben Nicholson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ben_Nicholson "Ben Nicholson").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-dimensionalist-15)

_Crucifixion (Corpus Hypercubus)_
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fourth_dimension_in_art&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Crucifixion (Corpus Hypercubus)")]

In 1953, the [surrealist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surrealist "Surrealist")[Salvador Dalí](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salvador_Dal%C3%AD "Salvador Dalí") proclaimed his intention to paint "an explosive, nuclear and hypercubic" crucifixion scene.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-16)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-17) He said that, "This picture will be the great metaphysical work of my summer".[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-18) Completed the next year, _[Crucifixion (Corpus Hypercubus)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crucifixion\_(Corpus\_Hypercubus) "Crucifixion (Corpus Hypercubus)")_ depicts Jesus Christ upon the net of a hypercube, also known as a [tesseract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract "Tesseract"). The unfolding of a tesseract into eight cubes is analogous to unfolding the sides of a cube into six squares. The [Metropolitan Museum of Art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metropolitan_Museum_of_Art "Metropolitan Museum of Art") describes the painting as a "new interpretation of an oft-depicted subject. ..[showing] Christ's spiritual triumph over corporeal harm."[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-19)

Abstract art
------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fourth_dimension_in_art&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Abstract art")]

The concept of the fourth-dimension, and other geometries, influenced artists of the early twentieth century. The definition of the fourth-dimension differed from artist to artist: before [Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein"), artists would associate the term with an extra spatial dimension; after Einstein's theory of relativity was [vindicated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eddington_experiment "Eddington experiment") in 1919, the fourth-dimension became associated with _time_ rather than space.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHenderson2013-1)

Between 1913 to 1915, [Piet Mondrian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piet_Mondrian "Piet Mondrian") produced paintings employing the _spatial_ interpretation of this principle in his _Pier and Ocean_ series. By 1917 he had developed his theory of [Neo-plasticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neo-plasticism "Neo-plasticism") which excluded this principle in favour of colour planes and orthogonal lines on a flat surface.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKruger2007-20) His close collaborator, [Theo van Doesburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theo_van_Doesburg "Theo van Doesburg") showed an early interest in the spatial fourth-dimension, later developing his interest to include the temporal dimension in his works. Mondrian rejected both interpretations in favour of stasis ,[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBaljeu1968-21) and this difference of views played a major role in his departure from [De Stijl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Stijl "De Stijl") in 1924.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlotkamp1994147%E2%80%93148-22) Van Doesburg went on to develop [Elementarism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementarism "Elementarism"), incorporating the dynamic concept of movement and expansion.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBlotkamp198229%E2%80%9332-23)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourth_dimension_in_art#cite_note-FOOTNOTEUbink1918-24)
