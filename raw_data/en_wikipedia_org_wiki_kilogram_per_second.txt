In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") and [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering"), **mass flow rate** is the [rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temporal_rate "Temporal rate") at which [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") of a substance changes over [time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time "Time"). Its [unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_measurement "Unit of measurement") is [kilogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram "Kilogram") per [second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second "Second") (kg/s) in [SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI "SI") units, and [slug](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slug_(unit) "Slug (unit)") per second or [pound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pound_(mass) "Pound (mass)") per second in [US customary units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/US_customary_unit "US customary unit"). The common symbol is ![Image 3: {\displaystyle {\dot {m}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad59b9876301e8fb75b9ddbf08de594b87251d3b) (pronounced "m-dot"), although sometimes ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161) ([Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language") lowercase [mu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mu_(letter) "Mu (letter)")) is used.

Sometimes, mass flow rate as defined here is termed "mass flux" or "mass current".[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram_per_second#cite_note-2) Confusingly, "mass flow" is also a term for [mass flux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_flux "Mass flux"), the rate of mass flow per unit of area.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram_per_second#cite_note-3)

Mass flow rate is defined by the [limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_of_a_function "Limit of a function")[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram_per_second#cite_note-4)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram_per_second#cite_note-5)![Image 5: {\displaystyle {\dot {m}}=\lim _{\Delta t\to 0}{\frac {\Delta m}{\Delta t}}={\frac {dm}{dt}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/528b773ef954f0afef018ab871a737a53c2c90ee) i.e., the flow of mass ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \Delta m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a2559989a97e4068e9e5fb20f24f0249b79e85d) through a surface per time ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \Delta t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c28867ecd34e2caed12cf38feadf6a81a7ee542).

The overdot on ![Image 8: {\displaystyle {\dot {m}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad59b9876301e8fb75b9ddbf08de594b87251d3b) is [Newton's notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_notation "Newton's notation") for a [time derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_derivative "Time derivative"). Since mass is a [scalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(physics) "Scalar (physics)") quantity, the mass flow rate (the time derivative of mass) is also a scalar quantity. The change in mass is the amount that flows _after_ crossing the boundary for some time duration, not the initial amount of mass at the boundary minus the final amount at the boundary, since the change in mass flowing through the area would be zero for [steady flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steady_flow "Steady flow").

Alternative equations
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mass_flow_rate&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Alternative equations")]

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Volumetric-flow-rate.svg/250px-Volumetric-flow-rate.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Volumetric-flow-rate.svg)

Illustration of volume flow rate. Mass flow rate can be calculated by multiplying the volume flow rate by the mass density of the fluid, _ρ_. The volume flow rate is calculated by multiplying the flow velocity of the mass elements, **v**, by the cross-sectional vector area, **A**.

Mass flow rate can also be calculated by

![Image 10: {\displaystyle {\dot {m}}=\rho \cdot {\dot {V}}=\rho \cdot \mathbf {v} \cdot \mathbf {A} =\mathbf {j} _{\text{m}}\cdot \mathbf {A} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c88d82027330d669de1b77a31149e17f86c0c56f)

where

The above equation is only true for a flat, plane area. In general, including cases where the area is curved, the equation becomes a [surface integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_integral "Surface integral"): ![Image 11: {\displaystyle {\dot {m}}=\iint _{A}\rho \mathbf {v} \cdot d\mathbf {A} =\iint _{A}\mathbf {j} _{\text{m}}\cdot d\mathbf {A} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458558a2f47edb7d6023f389b81bae6b0294374b)

The [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area") required to calculate the mass flow rate is real or imaginary, flat or curved, either as a cross-sectional area or a surface, e.g. for substances passing through a [filter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Filter_paper "Filter paper") or a [membrane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_membrane "Artificial membrane"), the real surface is the (generally curved) surface area of the filter, [macroscopically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macroscopic_scale "Macroscopic scale") - ignoring the area spanned by the holes in the filter/membrane. The spaces would be cross-sectional areas. For liquids passing through a pipe, the area is the cross-section of the pipe, at the section considered. The [vector area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_area "Vector area") is a combination of the magnitude of the area through which the mass passes through, ![Image 12: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3), and a [unit vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector") normal to the area, ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\hat {n}} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4eb84e133d15551d660800ec29b44783ff36e19d). The relation is ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathbf {A} =A\mathbf {\hat {n}} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/176a3541048716f82aa79f9bdbb137fb3c44219e).

The reason for the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") is as follows. The only mass flowing _through_ the cross-section is the amount normal to the area, i.e. [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_(geometry) "Parallel (geometry)") to the unit normal. This amount is

![Image 15: {\displaystyle {\dot {m}}=\rho vA\cos \theta ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c75a86d08f6d98a7082abd8ac153bbc8719076ed)
where ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) is the angle between the unit normal ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\hat {n}} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4eb84e133d15551d660800ec29b44783ff36e19d) and the velocity of mass elements. The amount passing through the cross-section is reduced by the factor ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \cos \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/611e5c70de1d1cf4ebc3b70d2b5467f45d17a483), as ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) increases less mass passes through. All mass which passes in tangential directions to the area, that is [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") to the unit normal, _doesn't_ actually pass _through_ the area, so the mass passing through the area is zero. This occurs when ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \theta =\pi /2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9cc4628b0f731f81bfabcd8edaca00aa186f03bd): ![Image 21: {\displaystyle {\dot {m}}=\rho vA\cos(\pi /2)=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d2a706a73c706f9164878cc7e1e9967e8053e2cd) These results are equivalent to the equation containing the dot product. Sometimes these equations are used to define the mass flow rate.

Considering flow through porous media, a special quantity, superficial mass flow rate, can be introduced. It is related with [superficial velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superficial_velocity "Superficial velocity"), ![Image 22: {\displaystyle v_{s}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5b59adb76ac7f03c89867f225a55fd4bbde0cbdd), with the following relationship:[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram_per_second#cite_note-6)![Image 23: {\displaystyle {\dot {m}}_{s}=v_{s}\cdot \rho ={\dot {m}}/A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/943fb33803bac841321d6b7f7cbea92c08ca610e) The quantity can be used in [particle Reynolds number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reynolds_number#Object_in_a_fluid "Reynolds number") or [mass transfer coefficient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_transfer_coefficient "Mass transfer coefficient") calculation for fixed and fluidized bed systems.

In the elementary form of the [continuity equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuity_equation "Continuity equation") for mass, in [hydrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrodynamics "Hydrodynamics"):[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram_per_second#cite_note-7)![Image 24: {\displaystyle \rho _{1}\mathbf {v} _{1}\cdot \mathbf {A} _{1}=\rho _{2}\mathbf {v} _{2}\cdot \mathbf {A} _{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/63ff89338ee54be50b1f3150a56f6c2a23cd8670)

In elementary classical mechanics, mass flow rate is encountered when dealing with [objects of variable mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable-mass_system "Variable-mass system"), such as a rocket ejecting spent fuel. Often, descriptions of such objects erroneously[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram_per_second#cite_note-Halliday-8) invoke [Newton's second law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_second_law "Newton's second law")![Image 25: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =d(m\mathbf {v} )/dt}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5738b5663d798d25c1acc2cfa1561bc4dd2ab61d) by treating both the mass ![Image 26: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) and the velocity ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6) as time-dependent and then applying the derivative product rule. A correct description of such an object requires the application of Newton's second law to the entire, constant-mass system consisting of both the object and its ejected mass.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram_per_second#cite_note-Halliday-8)

Mass flow rate can be used to calculate the energy flow rate of a fluid:[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram_per_second#cite_note-9)![Image 28: {\displaystyle {\dot {E}}={\dot {m}}e,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27b46fbfcdae4de927985e4171bbd18d4c795f19) where ![Image 29: {\displaystyle e}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd253103f0876afc68ebead27a5aa9867d927467) is the unit mass energy of a system.

Energy flow rate has SI units of [kilojoule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilojoule "Kilojoule") per second or [kilowatt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilowatt "Kilowatt").
