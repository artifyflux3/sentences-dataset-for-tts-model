From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

**Fermi–Dirac statistics** is a type of [quantum statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_statistics "Quantum statistics") that applies to the [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") of a [system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_system "Physical system") consisting of many non-interacting, [identical particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identical_particles "Identical particles") that obey the [Pauli exclusion principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauli_exclusion_principle "Pauli exclusion principle"). A result is the Fermi–Dirac distribution of particles over [energy states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_level "Energy level"). It is named after [Enrico Fermi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enrico_Fermi "Enrico Fermi") and [Paul Dirac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Dirac "Paul Dirac"), each of whom derived the distribution independently in 1926.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution#cite_note-Fermi1926-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution#cite_note-Dirac1926-2) Fermi–Dirac statistics is a part of the field of [statistical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistical_mechanics "Statistical mechanics") and uses the principles of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics").

Fermi–Dirac statistics applies to identical and indistinguishable particles with [half-integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Half-integer "Half-integer")[spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)") (1/2, 3/2, etc.), called [fermions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermion "Fermion"), in [thermodynamic equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium "Thermodynamic equilibrium"). For the case of negligible interaction between particles, the system can be described in terms of single-particle [energy states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_eigenstates "Energy eigenstates"). A result is the Fermi–Dirac distribution of particles over these states where no two particles can occupy the same state, which has a considerable effect on the properties of the system. Fermi–Dirac statistics is most commonly applied to [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron"), a type of fermion with [spin 1/2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_1/2 "Spin 1/2").

A counterpart to Fermi–Dirac statistics is [Bose–Einstein statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bose%E2%80%93Einstein_statistics "Bose–Einstein statistics"), which applies to identical and indistinguishable particles with integer spin (0, 1, 2, etc.) called [bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bosons "Bosons"). In classical physics, [Maxwell–Boltzmann statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%E2%80%93Boltzmann_statistics "Maxwell–Boltzmann statistics") is used to describe particles that are identical and treated as distinguishable. For both Bose–Einstein and Maxwell–Boltzmann statistics, more than one particle can occupy the same state, unlike Fermi–Dirac statistics.

Before the introduction of Fermi–Dirac statistics in 1926, understanding some aspects of electron behavior was difficult due to seemingly contradictory phenomena. For example, the electronic [heat capacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_capacity "Heat capacity") of a metal at [room temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Room_temperature "Room temperature") seemed to come from 100 times fewer [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") than were in the [electric current](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_current "Electric current").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution#cite_note-Kittel1971Cel249-3) It was also difficult to understand why the [emission currents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_electron_emission "Field electron emission") generated by applying high electric fields to metals at room temperature were almost independent of temperature.

The difficulty encountered by the [Drude model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drude_model "Drude model"), the electronic theory of metals at that time, was due to considering that electrons were (according to classical statistics theory) all equivalent. In other words, it was believed that each electron contributed to the specific heat an amount on the order of the [Boltzmann constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_constant "Boltzmann constant")_k_ B. This problem remained unsolved until the development of Fermi–Dirac statistics.

Fermi–Dirac statistics was first published in 1926 by [Enrico Fermi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enrico_Fermi "Enrico Fermi")[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution#cite_note-Fermi1926-1) and [Paul Dirac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Dirac "Paul Dirac").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution#cite_note-Dirac1926-2) According to [Max Born](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Born "Max Born"), [Pascual Jordan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascual_Jordan "Pascual Jordan") developed in 1925 the same statistics, which he called _[Pauli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang\_Pauli "Wolfgang Pauli") statistics_, but it was not published in a timely manner.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution#cite_note-Science-Week2000-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution#cite_note-6) According to Dirac, it was first studied by Fermi, and Dirac called it "Fermi statistics" and the corresponding particles "fermions".[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution#cite_note-Dirac1958-7)

Fermi–Dirac statistics was applied in 1926 by [Ralph Fowler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ralph_Fowler "Ralph Fowler") to describe the collapse of a [star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star") to a [white dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf "White dwarf").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution#cite_note-Fowler1926-8) In 1927 [Arnold Sommerfeld](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arnold_Sommerfeld "Arnold Sommerfeld") applied it to electrons in metals and developed the [free electron model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_electron_model "Free electron model"),[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution#cite_note-sommerfeld1927-9) and in 1928 Fowler and [Lothar Nordheim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lothar_Wolfgang_Nordheim "Lothar Wolfgang Nordheim") applied it to [field electron emission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_electron_emission "Field electron emission") from metals.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution#cite_note-Fowler1928-10) Fermi–Dirac statistics continue to be an important part of physics.

Fermi–Dirac distribution
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_statistics&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Fermi–Dirac distribution")]

For a system of identical fermions in thermodynamic equilibrium, the average number of fermions in a single-particle state i is given by the **Fermi–Dirac (F–D) distribution**:[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution#cite_note-Reif1965dist341-11)[[nb 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution#cite_note-12)

The distribution is normalized by the condition

At zero absolute temperature, μ is equal to the [Fermi energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi_energy "Fermi energy") plus the potential energy per fermion, provided it is in a [neighbourhood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighbourhood_(mathematics) "Neighbourhood (mathematics)") of positive spectral density. The Fermi–Dirac distribution is only valid if the number of fermions in the system is large enough so that adding one more fermion to the system has negligible effect on μ.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution#cite_note-Reif1965dist-16) Since the Fermi–Dirac distribution was derived using the [Pauli exclusion principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauli_exclusion_principle "Pauli exclusion principle"), which allows at most one fermion to occupy each possible state, a result is that ![Image 11: {\displaystyle 0<{\bar {n}}_{i}<1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/558a9c69482dd7735a488bbc7be0b8afaa36717d).[[nb 2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution#cite_note-17)

Quantum and classical regimes
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_statistics&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Quantum and classical regimes")]

The Fermi–Dirac distribution approaches the [Maxwell–Boltzmann distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%E2%80%93Boltzmann_statistics#Applicability "Maxwell–Boltzmann statistics") in the limit of high temperature and low particle density, without the need for any ad hoc assumptions:

The classical regime, where [Maxwell–Boltzmann statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%E2%80%93Boltzmann_statistics "Maxwell–Boltzmann statistics") can be used as an approximation to Fermi–Dirac statistics, is found by considering the situation that is far from the limit imposed by the [Heisenberg uncertainty principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heisenberg_uncertainty_principle "Heisenberg uncertainty principle") for a particle's position and [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum"). For example, in physics of semiconductor, when the density of states of conduction band is much higher than the doping concentration, the energy gap between conduction band and fermi level could be calculated using Maxwell-Boltzmann statistics. Otherwise, if the doping concentration is not negligible compared to density of states of conduction band, the Fermi–Dirac distribution should be used instead for accurate calculation. It can then be shown that the classical situation prevails when the [concentration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_concentration "Quantum concentration") of particles corresponds to an [average interparticle separation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_inter-particle_distance "Mean inter-particle distance")![Image 39: {\displaystyle {\bar {R}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/771ade41bcd0ef92e246bb14daeb44dc41a447b9) that is much greater than the average [de Broglie wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Broglie_wavelength "De Broglie wavelength")![Image 40: {\displaystyle {\bar {\lambda }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b40caf100572a9cc91ebf6ff7079ca8dd9eeb59) of the particles:[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution#cite_note-Reif1965classical-25)

![Image 41: {\displaystyle {\bar {R}}\gg {\bar {\lambda }}\approx {\frac {h}{\sqrt {3mk_{\rm {B}}T}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d82f630a8f9244c7d7611ba760508cd2d9dccd7b)
where h is the [Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_constant "Planck constant"), and m is the [mass of a particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle").

For the case of conduction electrons in a typical metal at T = 300[K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin") (i.e. approximately room temperature), the system is far from the classical regime because ![Image 42: {\displaystyle {\bar {R}}\approx {\bar {\lambda }}/25}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af031cb0905f1f55e07c0296b52f94a81412e0a2) . This is due to the small mass of the electron and the high concentration (i.e. small ![Image 43: {\displaystyle {\bar {R}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/771ade41bcd0ef92e246bb14daeb44dc41a447b9)) of conduction electrons in the metal. Thus Fermi–Dirac statistics is needed for conduction electrons in a typical metal.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution#cite_note-Reif1965classical-25)

Another example of a system that is not in the classical regime is the system that consists of the electrons of a star that has collapsed to a white dwarf. Although the temperature of white dwarf is high (typically T = 10 000 K on its surface[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution#cite_note-Mukai1997-26)), its high electron concentration and the small mass of each electron precludes using a classical approximation, and again Fermi–Dirac statistics is required.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution#cite_note-Fowler1926-8)

### Grand canonical ensemble

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_statistics&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Grand canonical ensemble")]

The Fermi–Dirac distribution, which applies only to a quantum system of non-interacting fermions, is easily derived from the [grand canonical ensemble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grand_canonical_ensemble "Grand canonical ensemble").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution#cite_note-sriva-27) In this ensemble, the system is able to exchange energy and exchange particles with a reservoir (temperature _T_ and chemical potential _μ_ fixed by the reservoir).

Due to the non-interacting quality, each available single-particle level (with energy level _ϵ_) forms a separate thermodynamic system in contact with the reservoir. In other words, each single-particle level is a separate, tiny grand canonical ensemble. By the Pauli exclusion principle, there are only two possible [microstates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microstate_(statistical_mechanics) "Microstate (statistical mechanics)") for the single-particle level: no particle (energy _E_ = 0), or one particle (energy _E_ = _ε_). The resulting [partition function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partition_function_(statistical_mechanics) "Partition function (statistical mechanics)") for that single-particle level therefore has just two terms:

![Image 44: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\mathcal {Z}}&=\exp {\big (}0(\mu -\varepsilon )/k_{\rm {B}}T{\big )}+\exp {\big (}1(\mu -\varepsilon )/k_{\rm {B}}T{\big )}\\&=1+\exp {\big (}(\mu -\varepsilon )/k_{\rm {B}}T{\big )},\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24441bc2837dea13963b2c7a793f37177821cc5c)
and the average particle number for that single-particle level substate is given by

![Image 45: {\displaystyle \langle N\rangle =k_{\rm {B}}T{\frac {1}{\mathcal {Z}}}\left({\frac {\partial {\mathcal {Z}}}{\partial \mu }}\right)_{V,T}={\frac {1}{\exp {\big (}(\varepsilon -\mu )/k_{\rm {B}}T{\big )}+1}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0484d9e3913924c5ef0999c29aae215882c7a387)
This result applies for each single-particle level, and thus gives the Fermi–Dirac distribution for the entire state of the system.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution#cite_note-sriva-27)

The variance in particle number (due to [thermal fluctuations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_fluctuations "Thermal fluctuations")) may also be derived (the particle number has a simple [Bernoulli distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernoulli_distribution "Bernoulli distribution")):

![Image 46: {\displaystyle {\big \langle }(\Delta N)^{2}{\big \rangle }=k_{\rm {B}}T\left({\frac {d\langle N\rangle }{d\mu }}\right)_{V,T}=\langle N\rangle {\big (}1-\langle N\rangle {\big )}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f015279e2b47f12c355bbf78b90dd734360dbd4c)
This quantity is important in transport phenomena such as the [Mott relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seebeck_coefficient "Seebeck coefficient") for electrical conductivity and [thermoelectric coefficient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermoelectric_effect#Charge_carrier_diffusion "Thermoelectric effect") for an [electron gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_gas "Electron gas"),[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution#cite_note-28) where the ability of an energy level to contribute to transport phenomena is proportional to ![Image 47: {\displaystyle {\big \langle }(\Delta N)^{2}{\big \rangle }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/569adbdbeb46f267d24431adb55148275e4e1288).

It is also possible to derive Fermi–Dirac statistics in the [canonical ensemble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_ensemble "Canonical ensemble"). ### Microcanonical ensemble

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_statistics&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Microcanonical ensemble")]

A result can be achieved by directly analyzing the multiplicities of the system and using [Lagrange multipliers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_multipliers "Lagrange multipliers").[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%E2%80%93Dirac_distribution#cite_note-Blakemore2002p343%E2%80%935-33)

Suppose we have a number of energy levels, labeled by index _i_, each level having energy ε _i_ and containing a total of _n i_ particles. Suppose each level contains _g i_ distinct sublevels, all of which have the same energy, and which are distinguishable. For example, two particles may have different momenta (i.e. their momenta may be along different directions), in which case they are distinguishable from each other, yet they can still have the same energy. The value of _g i_ associated with level _i_ is called the "degeneracy" of that energy level. The [Pauli exclusion principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauli_exclusion_principle "Pauli exclusion principle") states that only one fermion can occupy any such sublevel.

The number of ways of distributing _n i_ indistinguishable particles among the _g i_ sublevels of an energy level, with a maximum of one particle per sublevel, is given by the [binomial coefficient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binomial_coefficient "Binomial coefficient"), using its [combinatorial interpretation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binomial_coefficient#Combinatorial_interpretation "Binomial coefficient"):

![Image 107: {\displaystyle w(n_{i},g_{i})={\frac {g_{i}!}{n_{i}!(g_{i}-n_{i})!}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9dfd4695d7f4b7807d6a0d70ab43cb67578b061c)
For example, distributing two particles in three sublevels will give population numbers of 110, 101, or 011 for a total of three ways which equals 3!/(2!1!).

The number of ways that a set of occupation numbers _n_ _i_ can be realized is the product of the ways that each individual energy level can be populated:

![Image 108: {\displaystyle W=\prod _{i}w(n_{i},g_{i})=\prod _{i}{\frac {g_{i}!}{n_{i}!(g_{i}-n_{i})!}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9b40e0d9ad6161d38472437d31d78e7bae1cd314)
Following the same procedure used in deriving the [Maxwell–Boltzmann statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%E2%80%93Boltzmann_statistics "Maxwell–Boltzmann statistics"), we wish to find the set of _n i_ for which _W_ is maximized, subject to the constraint that there be a fixed number of particles and a fixed energy. We constrain our solution using [Lagrange multipliers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_multipliers "Lagrange multipliers") forming the function:

![Image 109: {\displaystyle f(n_{i})=\ln W+\alpha \left(N-\sum n_{i}\right)+\beta \left(E-\sum n_{i}\varepsilon _{i}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/999a00282bb73de2b8fdb2ae2cbbc5901113fbc7)
Using [Stirling's approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stirling%27s_approximation "Stirling's approximation") for the factorials, taking the derivative with respect to _n i_, setting the result to zero, and solving for _n i_ yields the Fermi–Dirac population numbers:

![Image 110: {\displaystyle n_{i}={\frac {g_{i}}{e^{\alpha +\beta \varepsilon _{i}}+1}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e79e557008a0f31e2cb6bb8555eb91f192e0c7c3)
By a process similar to that outlined in the [Maxwell–Boltzmann statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%E2%80%93Boltzmann_statistics "Maxwell–Boltzmann statistics") article, it can be shown thermodynamically that ![Image 111: {\displaystyle \beta ={\tfrac {1}{k_{\text{B}}T}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e135afe638e0805fa986c52393edf9944b2fdb7) and ![Image 112: {\displaystyle \alpha =-{\tfrac {\mu }{k_{\text{B}}T}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/17c50bf7da8af9e74dde22ca8059d6cc0369e75b), so that finally, the probability that a state will be occupied is

![Image 113: {\displaystyle {\bar {n}}_{i}={\frac {n_{i}}{g_{i}}}={\frac {1}{e^{(\varepsilon _{i}-\mu )/k_{\text{B}}T}+1}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2548a0499dea556c58980b3c3ff030fcd7188d99)
