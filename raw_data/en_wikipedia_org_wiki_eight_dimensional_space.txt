In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a sequence of _n_[real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") can be understood as a [location](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)") in _n_-[dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(vector_space) "Dimension (vector space)") space. When _n_ = 8, the set of all such locations is called **8-dimensional space**. Often such spaces are studied as [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space"), without any notion of distance. Eight-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") is eight-dimensional space equipped with the [Euclidean metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_metric "Euclidean metric").

More generally the term may refer to an eight-dimensional vector space over any [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)"), such as an eight-dimensional [complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") vector space, which has 16 real dimensions. It may also refer to an eight-dimensional [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold") such as an [8-sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/8-sphere "8-sphere"), or a variety of other geometric constructions.

A [polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytope "Polytope") in eight dimensions is called an 8-polytope. The most studied are the [regular polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polytope "Regular polytope"), of which there are only [three in eight dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_regular_polytopes#Convex_4 "List of regular polytopes"): the [8-simplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/8-simplex "8-simplex"), [8-cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/8-cube "8-cube"), and [8-orthoplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/8-orthoplex "8-orthoplex"). A broader family are the [uniform 8-polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_8-polytope "Uniform 8-polytope"), constructed from fundamental symmetry domains of reflection, each domain defined by a [Coxeter group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter_group "Coxeter group"). Each uniform polytope is defined by a ringed [Coxeter-Dynkin diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter-Dynkin_diagram "Coxeter-Dynkin diagram"). The [8-demicube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/8-demicube "8-demicube") is a unique polytope from the D 8 family, and [4 21](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4_21_polytope "4 21 polytope"), [2 41](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2_41_polytope "2 41 polytope"), and [1 42](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1_42_polytope "1 42 polytope") polytopes from the E 8 family.

The [7-sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-sphere "N-sphere") or hypersphere in eight dimensions is the seven-dimensional surface equidistant from a point, e.g. the origin. It has symbol _S_ 7, with formal definition for the 7-sphere with radius _r_ of ![Image 105: {\displaystyle S^{7}=\left\{x\in \mathbb {R} ^{8}:\|x\|=r\right\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/84dd9319f34aa89816a24cabd566a1bf0a50ced6)

The volume of the space bounded by this 7-sphere is ![Image 106: {\displaystyle V_{8}\,={\frac {\pi ^{4}}{24}}\,R^{8}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04bdff6d3b9a3e017359e868209ef40ed334ab32) which is 4.05871 × _r_ 8, or 0.01585 of the [8-cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/8-cube "8-cube") that contains the 7-sphere.

### Kissing number problem

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Eight-dimensional_space&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Kissing number problem")]

The [kissing number problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kissing_number_problem "Kissing number problem") has been solved in eight dimensions, thanks to the existence of the [4 21](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4_21_polytope "4 21 polytope") polytope and its associated [lattice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lattice_(group) "Lattice (group)"). The kissing number in eight dimensions is [240](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/240_(number) "240 (number)").

The octonions are a [normed division algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normed_division_algebra "Normed division algebra") over the real numbers, the largest such algebra. Mathematically they can be specified by 8-tuplets of real numbers, so form an 8-dimensional vector space over the reals, with addition of vectors being the addition in the algebra. A normed algebra is one with a product that satisfies

![Image 107: {\displaystyle \|xy\|\leq \|x\|\|y\|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6bad911e3ff52eae383146215c976a73f43085db)
for all _x_ and _y_ in the algebra. A normed [division algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_algebra "Division algebra") additionally must be finite-dimensional, and have the property that every non-zero vector has a unique multiplicative inverse. [Hurwitz's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hurwitz%27s_theorem_(composition_algebras) "Hurwitz's theorem (composition algebras)") prohibits such a structure from existing in dimensions other than 1, 2, 4, or 8.

The complexified [quaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion "Quaternion")![Image 108: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} \otimes \mathbb {H} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41421172aa6699dbcb38403713316e918dc3c253), or "[biquaternions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biquaternion "Biquaternion")," are an eight-dimensional algebra dating to [William Rowan Hamilton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Rowan_Hamilton "William Rowan Hamilton")'s work in the 1850s. This algebra is equivalent (that is, [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphic "Isomorphic")) to the [Clifford algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clifford_algebra "Clifford algebra")![Image 109: {\displaystyle C\ell _{2}(\mathbb {C} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/111948bf07ed18f70bd7718f97d3d8d1baf48b29) and the [Pauli algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pauli_algebra "Pauli algebra"). It has also been proposed as a practical or pedagogical tool for doing calculations in [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity"), and in that context goes by the name [Algebra of physical space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra_of_physical_space "Algebra of physical space") (not to be confused with the [Spacetime algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime_algebra "Spacetime algebra"), which is 16-dimensional.)
