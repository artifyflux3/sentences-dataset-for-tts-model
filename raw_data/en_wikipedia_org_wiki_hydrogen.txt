**Hydrogen** is a [chemical element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element"); it has [symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_symbol "Chemical symbol")**H** and [atomic number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_number "Atomic number") 1. It is the lightest and [most abundant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abundance_of_the_chemical_elements "Abundance of the chemical elements") chemical element in the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe"), constituting about 75% of all [normal matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon "Baryon"). Under [standard conditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_conditions "Standard conditions"), hydrogen is a [gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas "Gas") of [diatomic molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_molecule "Diatomic molecule") with the [formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")H 2, called **dihydrogen**, or sometimes **hydrogen gas**, **molecular hydrogen**, or simply hydrogen. Dihydrogen is colorless, odorless, non-toxic, and highly [combustible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combustible "Combustible"). [Stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stars "Stars"), including the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun"), mainly consist of hydrogen in a [plasma state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_state "Plasma state"), while on Earth, hydrogen is found as the gas H 2 (dihydrogen) and in [molecular forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_form "Molecular form"), such as in [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water "Water") and [organic compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compounds "Organic compounds"). The most common [isotope of hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope_of_hydrogen "Isotope of hydrogen") (1 H) consists of one [proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton"), one [electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron"), and no [neutrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron").

Hydrogen gas was first produced artificially in the 17th century by the reaction of acids with metals. [Henry Cavendish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Cavendish "Henry Cavendish"), in 1766–1781, identified hydrogen gas as a distinct substance and discovered its property of producing water when burned; hence its name means 'water-former' in Greek. Understanding the colors of light absorbed and emitted by hydrogen was a crucial part of developing [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics").

Hydrogen, typically [nonmetallic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonmetallic "Nonmetallic") except under [extreme pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_pressure "High pressure"), readily forms [covalent bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covalent_bonds "Covalent bonds") with most nonmetals, contributing to the formation of compounds like water and various organic substances. Its role is crucial in [acid-base reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid-base_reactions "Acid-base reactions"), which mainly involve proton exchange among soluble molecules. In [ionic compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionic_compounds "Ionic compounds"), hydrogen can take the form of either a negatively charged [anion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anion "Anion"), where it is known as [hydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydride "Hydride"), or as a positively charged [cation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cation "Cation"), H+, called a proton. Although tightly bonded to water molecules, protons strongly affect the behavior of [aqueous solutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aqueous_solution "Aqueous solution"), as reflected in the importance of pH. Hydride, on the other hand, is rarely observed because it tends to deprotonate solvents, yielding H 2.

In the [early universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_universe "Early universe"), neutral hydrogen atoms formed about 370,000 years after the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang") as the universe expanded and plasma had cooled enough for electrons to remain bound to protons. Once stars formed most of the atoms in the [intergalactic medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intergalactic_medium "Intergalactic medium") re-ionized.

Nearly all [hydrogen production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_production "Hydrogen production") is done by transforming fossil fuels, particularly [steam reforming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steam_reforming "Steam reforming") of [natural gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_gas "Natural gas"). It can also be produced from water or saline by [electrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrolysis "Electrolysis"), but this process is more expensive. Its main industrial uses include [fossil fuel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil_fuel "Fossil fuel") processing and [ammonia production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia_production "Ammonia production") for fertilizer. Emerging uses for hydrogen include the use of [fuel cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fuel_cell "Fuel cell") to generate electricity.

### Atomic hydrogen

#### Electron energy levels

The [ground state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_state "Ground state")[energy level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_level "Energy level") of the electron in a hydrogen atom is −13.6[eV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt "Electronvolt"),[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-11) equivalent to an [ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet")[photon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") of roughly 91 nm wavelength.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-12) The energy levels of hydrogen are referred to by consecutive [quantum numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_number "Quantum number"), with ![Image 5: {\displaystyle n=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9ec7e1edc2e6d98f5aec2a39ae5f1c99d1e1425) being the ground state. The [hydrogen spectral series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_spectral_series "Hydrogen spectral series") corresponds to emission of light due to transitions from higher to lower energy levels.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-13): 105  Each energy level is further split by [spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)") interactions between the electron and proton into 4 [hyperfine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperfine_structure "Hyperfine structure") levels.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-14)

High precision values for the hydrogen atom energy levels are required for definitions of physical constants. Quantum calculations have identified 9 contributions to the energy levels. The eigenvalue from the [Dirac equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_equation "Dirac equation") is the largest contribution. Other terms include [relativistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics "Relativistic quantum mechanics") recoil, the [self-energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-energy "Self-energy"), and the [vacuum polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_polarization "Vacuum polarization") terms.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-15)

H) have been synthesized in the laboratory but not observed in nature.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Gurov-16)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Korsheninnikov-17)

**1

H** is the most common hydrogen isotope, with an abundance of >99.98%. Because the [nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus") of this isotope consists of only a single proton, it is given the descriptive but rarely used formal name _protium_.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-18) It is the only stable isotope with no neutrons; see [diproton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotopes_of_helium#Helium-2_(diproton) "Isotopes of helium") for a discussion of why others do not exist.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-19)

**2

H**, the other stable hydrogen isotope, is known as [deuterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuterium "Deuterium") and contains one proton and one [neutron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron") in the nucleus. Nearly all deuterium nuclei in the universe is thought to have been produced at the time of the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang"), and has endured since then.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-20): 24.2  Deuterium is not radioactive, and is not a significant toxicity hazard. Water enriched in molecules that include deuterium instead of normal hydrogen is called [heavy water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heavy_water "Heavy water"). Deuterium and its compounds are used as a non-radioactive label in chemical experiments and in solvents for 1

H-[NMR spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NMR_spectroscopy "NMR spectroscopy").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-21) Heavy water is used as a [neutron moderator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_moderator "Neutron moderator") and coolant for nuclear reactors. Deuterium is also a potential fuel for commercial [nuclear fusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_fusion "Nuclear fusion").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-22)

**3

H** is known as [tritium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tritium "Tritium") and contains one proton and two neutrons in its nucleus. It is radioactive, decaying into [helium-3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium-3 "Helium-3") through [beta decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_decay "Beta decay") with a [half-life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Half-life "Half-life") of 12.32 years.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Miessler-23) It is radioactive enough to be used in [luminous paint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radioluminescent_paint "Radioluminescent paint") to enhance the visibility of data displays, such as for painting the hands and dial-markers of watches. The watch glass prevents the small amount of radiation from escaping the case.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Traub95-24) Small amounts of tritium are produced naturally by cosmic rays striking atmospheric gases; tritium has also been released in [nuclear weapons tests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_testing "Nuclear testing").[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-25) It is used in nuclear fusion,[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-26) as a tracer in [isotope geochemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope_geochemistry "Isotope geochemistry"),[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-27) and in specialized [self-powered lighting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-powered_lighting "Self-powered lighting") devices.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-28) Tritium has also been used in chemical and biological labeling experiments as a [radiolabel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiolabel "Radiolabel").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-holte-29)

Unique among the elements, distinct names are assigned to its isotopes in common use. During the early study of radioactivity, heavy radioisotopes were given their own names, but these are mostly no longer used. The symbols D and T (instead of 2

H and 3

H) are sometimes used for deuterium and tritium, but the symbol P was already used for [phosphorus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus "Phosphorus") and thus was not available for protium.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-30) In its [nomenclatural](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IUPAC_nomenclature "IUPAC nomenclature") guidelines, the [International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Union_of_Pure_and_Applied_Chemistry "International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry") (IUPAC) allows any of D, T, 2

H, and 3

H to be used, though 2

H and 3

H are preferred.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-31)

[Antihydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antihydrogen "Antihydrogen") (H) is the [antimatter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antimatter "Antimatter") counterpart to hydrogen. It consists of an [antiproton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiproton "Antiproton") with a [positron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positron "Positron").[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-char15-32)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Keller15-33) The [exotic atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exotic_atom "Exotic atom")[muonium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muonium "Muonium") (symbol Mu), composed of an anti[muon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muon "Muon") and an [electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron"), is analogous hydrogen and IUPAC nomenclature incorporates such hypothetical compounds as muonium chloride (MuCl) and sodium muonide (NaMu), analogous to [hydrogen chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride "Hydrogen chloride") and [sodium hydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_hydride "Sodium hydride") respectively.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-iupac-34)

### Dihydrogen

Under [standard conditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_conditions "Standard conditions"), hydrogen is a [gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas "Gas") of [diatomic molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_molecule "Diatomic molecule") with the [formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")H 2, officially called "dihydrogen",[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-35): 308  but also called "molecular hydrogen",[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-britannica-36) or simply hydrogen. Dihydrogen is a colorless, odorless, flammable gas.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-britannica-36)

#### Combustion

Combustion of hydrogen with the oxygen in the air. When the bottom cap is removed, allowing air to enter, hydrogen in the container rises and burns as it mixes with the air.

Hydrogen gas is highly flammable, reacting with [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") in air, to produce liquid water:

2 H 2(g) + O 2(g) → 2 H 2 O(l)
The [amount of heat released](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enthalpy_of_combustion "Enthalpy of combustion") per [mole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mole_(unit) "Mole (unit)") of hydrogen is −286 kJ or 141.865 MJ for a kilogram mass.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-37)

Hydrogen gas forms explosive mixtures with air in concentrations from 4–74%[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-38) and with chlorine at 5–95%. The hydrogen [autoignition temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autoignition_temperature "Autoignition temperature"), the temperature of spontaneous ignition in air, is 500°C (932°F).[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-39) In a high-pressure hydrogen leak, the shock wave from the leak itself can heat air to the autoignition temperature, leading to flaming and possibly explosion.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-40)

Hydrogen flames emit faint blue and [ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet") light.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-41)[Flame detectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flame_detector "Flame detector") are used to detect hydrogen fires as they are nearly invisible to the naked eye in daylight.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-42)[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-spinoff-2016-43)

#### Spin isomers

Molecular H 2 exists as two [nuclear isomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_isomer "Nuclear isomer") that differ in the [spin states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)") of their nuclei.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-uigi-44) In the **orthohydrogen** form, the spins of the two nuclei are parallel, forming a spin [triplet state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triplet_state "Triplet state") having a [total molecular spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_quantum_number#Total_spin_of_an_atom_or_molecule "Spin quantum number")![Image 7: {\displaystyle S=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5e2b58c1aaaf2718fb801e97bf21d1f72726372); in the **parahydrogen** form the spins are antiparallel and form a spin [singlet state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singlet_state "Singlet state") having spin ![Image 8: {\displaystyle S=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4ae0fc3192b864f55a46749d8a64e7cf7783d04c). The equilibrium ratio of ortho- to para-hydrogen depends on temperature. At room temperature or warmer, equilibrium hydrogen gas contains about 25% of the para form and 75% of the ortho form.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Green2012-45) The ortho form is an [excited state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excited_state "Excited state"), having higher energy than the para form by 1.455 kJ/mol,[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-PlanckInstitut-46) and it converts to the para form over the course of several minutes when cooled to low temperature.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-47) The thermal properties of these isomers differ because each has distinct [rotational quantum states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational%E2%80%93vibrational_spectroscopy "Rotational–vibrational spectroscopy").[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-NASA-48)

The ortho-to-para ratio in H 2 is an important consideration in the [liquefaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquefaction "Liquefaction") and storage of [liquid hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen "Liquid hydrogen"): the conversion from ortho to para is [exothermic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exothermic "Exothermic") and produces sufficient heat to evaporate most of the liquid if not converted first to parahydrogen during the cooling process.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Amos98-49)[Catalysts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalyst "Catalyst") for the ortho-para interconversion, such as [ferric oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferric_oxide "Ferric oxide") and [activated carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Activated_carbon "Activated carbon") compounds, are used during hydrogen cooling to avoid this loss of liquid.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Svadlenak-50)

#### Phases

[![Image 9: Phase diagram of hydrogen on logarithmic scales. Lines show boundaries between phases, with the end of the liquid-gas line indicating the critical point. The triple point of hydrogen is just off-scale to the left.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/31/Phase_diagram_of_hydrogen.png/250px-Phase_diagram_of_hydrogen.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Phase_diagram_of_hydrogen.png)

[Phase diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_diagram "Phase diagram") of hydrogen with a [logarithmic scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logarithmic_scale "Logarithmic scale") The left edge corresponds about one atmosphere.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-51)

[Liquid hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen "Liquid hydrogen") can exist at temperatures below hydrogen's [critical point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_point_(thermodynamics) "Critical point (thermodynamics)") of 33[K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvins "Kelvins").[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-52) However, for it to be in a fully liquid state at [atmospheric pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_pressure "Atmospheric pressure"), H 2 needs to be cooled to 20.28 K (−252.87°C; −423.17°F). Hydrogen was liquefied by [James Dewar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Dewar "James Dewar") in 1898 by using [regenerative cooling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regenerative_cooling "Regenerative cooling") and his invention, the [vacuum flask](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_flask "Vacuum flask").[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-53)

Liquid hydrogen becomes [solid hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_hydrogen "Solid hydrogen") at [standard pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_pressure "Standard pressure") below hydrogen's [melting point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melting_point "Melting point") of 14.01 K (−259.14°C; −434.45°F). Distinct solid phases exist, known as Phase I through Phase V, each exhibiting a characteristic molecular arrangement.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Helled2020-54) Liquid and solid phases can exist in combination at the [triple point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple_point "Triple point"), a substance known as [slush hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Slush_hydrogen "Slush hydrogen").[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-55)

[Metallic hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metallic_hydrogen "Metallic hydrogen"), a phase obtained at extremely high pressures (in excess of 400 gigapascals (3,900,000 atm; 58,000,000 psi)), is an electrical conductor. It is believed to exist deep within [giant planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_planet "Giant planet") like [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter").[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Helled2020-54)[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-56)

When [ionized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionization "Ionization"), hydrogen becomes a [plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)"). This is the form in which hydrogen exists within [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star").[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-57)

History
-------

### 18th century

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/61/Portret_van_Robert_Boyle%2C_RP-P-OB-4578_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Portret_van_Robert_Boyle%2C_RP-P-OB-4578_%28cropped%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Portret_van_Robert_Boyle,_RP-P-OB-4578_(cropped).jpg)

[Robert Boyle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Boyle "Robert Boyle"), who discovered the reaction between [iron filings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_filings "Iron filings") and dilute acids

In 1671, Irish scientist [Robert Boyle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Boyle "Robert Boyle") discovered and described the reaction between [iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron "Iron") filings and dilute [acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid "Acid"), which results in the production of hydrogen gas.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-60)[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-61) Boyle did not note that the gas was inflammable, but hydrogen would play a key role in overturning the [phlogiston theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phlogiston_theory "Phlogiston theory") of combustion.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Ramsay-1896-62)

In 1766, [Henry Cavendish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Cavendish "Henry Cavendish") was the first to recognize hydrogen gas as a discrete substance, by naming the gas from a [metal-acid reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal-acid_reaction "Metal-acid reaction") "inflammable air". He speculated that "inflammable air" was in fact identical to the hypothetical substance "[phlogiston](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phlogiston_theory "Phlogiston theory")"[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-63)[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-cav766-64) and further finding in 1781 that the gas produces water when burned. He is usually given credit for the discovery of hydrogen as an element.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Nostrand-10)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-nbb-9)

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Antoine-Laurent_Lavoisier_by_Louis_Jean_Desire_Delaistre_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Antoine-Laurent_Lavoisier_by_Louis_Jean_Desire_Delaistre_%28cropped%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Antoine-Laurent_Lavoisier_by_Louis_Jean_Desire_Delaistre_(cropped).jpg)

[Antoine Lavoisier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antoine_Lavoisier "Antoine Lavoisier"), who identified the element that came to be known as hydrogen

In 1783, [Antoine Lavoisier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antoine_Lavoisier "Antoine Lavoisier") identified the element that came to be known as hydrogen[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-65) when he and [Laplace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre-Simon_Laplace "Pierre-Simon Laplace") reproduced Cavendish's finding that water is produced when hydrogen is burned.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-nbb-9) Lavoisier produced hydrogen for his experiments on mass conservation by treating metallic [iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron "Iron") with a stream of H 2 O through an incandescent iron tube heated in a fire. Anaerobic oxidation of iron by the protons of water at high temperature can be schematically represented by the set of following reactions:

*   Fe + H 2 O → FeO + H 2
*   2Fe + 3 H 2 O → Fe 2 O 3 + 3 H 2
*   3Fe + 4 H 2 O → Fe 3 O 4 + 4 H 2

Many metals react similarly with water leading to the production of hydrogen.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-66) In some situations, this H 2-producing process is problematic as is the case of zirconium cladding on nuclear fuel rods.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-67)

### 19th century

By 1806 hydrogen was used to fill balloons.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-68)[François Isaac de Rivaz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Isaac_de_Rivaz "François Isaac de Rivaz") built the first [de Rivaz engine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Rivaz_engine "De Rivaz engine"), an internal combustion engine powered by a mixture of hydrogen and oxygen in 1806. [Edward Daniel Clarke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Daniel_Clarke "Edward Daniel Clarke") invented the hydrogen gas blowpipe in 1819. The [Döbereiner's lamp](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%C3%B6bereiner%27s_lamp "Döbereiner's lamp") and [limelight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limelight "Limelight") were invented in 1823. Hydrogen was [liquefied](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen "Liquid hydrogen") for the first time by [James Dewar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Dewar "James Dewar") in 1898 by using [regenerative cooling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regenerative_cooling "Regenerative cooling") and his invention, the [vacuum flask](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_flask "Vacuum flask"). He produced [solid hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_hydrogen "Solid hydrogen") the next year.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-nbb-9)

One of the first [quantum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") effects to be explicitly noticed (but not understood at the time) was [James Clerk Maxwell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Clerk_Maxwell "James Clerk Maxwell")'s observation that the [specific heat capacity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_heat_capacity "Specific heat capacity") of H 2 unaccountably departs from that of a [diatomic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic "Diatomic") gas below room temperature and begins to increasingly resemble that of a monatomic gas at cryogenic temperatures. According to quantum theory, this behavior arises from the spacing of the (quantized) rotational energy levels, which are particularly wide-spaced in H 2 because of its low mass. These widely spaced levels inhibit equal partition of heat energy into rotational motion in hydrogen at low temperatures. Diatomic gases composed of heavier atoms do not have such widely spaced levels and do not exhibit the same effect.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Berman-69)

### 20th century

The existence of the [hydride anion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydride "Hydride") was suggested by [Gilbert N. Lewis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gilbert_N._Lewis "Gilbert N. Lewis") in 1916 for group 1 and 2 salt-like compounds. In 1920, Moers electrolyzed molten [lithium hydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium_hydride "Lithium hydride") (LiH), producing a [stoichiometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoichiometry "Stoichiometry") quantity of hydrogen at the anode.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Moers-70)

[![Image 12: A line spectrum showing black background with narrow lines superimposed on it: one violet, one blue, one cyan, and one red.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Emission_spectrum-H_labeled.svg/250px-Emission_spectrum-H_labeled.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Emission_spectrum-H_labeled.svg)

Hydrogen emission spectrum lines in the four visible lines of the [Balmer series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balmer_series "Balmer series")

Because of its simple atomic structure, consisting only of a proton and an electron, the [hydrogen atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_atom "Hydrogen atom"), together with the spectrum of light produced from it or absorbed by it, has been central to the [development of the theory of atomic structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_atomic_theory "History of atomic theory").[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-71) The energy levels of hydrogen can be calculated fairly accurately using the [Bohr model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohr_model "Bohr model") of the atom, in which the electron "orbits" the proton, like how Earth orbits the Sun. However, the electron and proton are held together by electrostatic attraction, while planets and celestial objects are held by [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity"). Due to the discretization of [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum") postulated in early [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") by Bohr, the electron in the Bohr model can only occupy certain allowed distances from the proton, and therefore only certain allowed energies.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-72)

Hydrogen's unique position as the only neutral atom for which the [Schrödinger equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation "Schrödinger equation") can be directly solved, has significantly contributed to the understanding of quantum mechanics through the exploration of its energetics.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Laursen04-73) Furthermore, study of the corresponding simplicity of the hydrogen molecule and the corresponding cation [H+2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H2%2B "H2+") brought understanding of the nature of the chemical bond, which followed shortly after the quantum mechanical treatment of the hydrogen atom had been developed in the mid-1920s.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-74)

#### Hydrogen-lifted airship

[![Image 13: Airship Hindenburg over New York](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Hindenburg_over_New_York_1937_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-Hindenburg_over_New_York_1937_%28cropped%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hindenburg_over_New_York_1937_(cropped).jpg)

The [Hindenburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindenburg-class_airship "Hindenburg-class airship") over [New York City](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_York_City "New York City") in 1937

Because H 2 is only 7% the density of air, it was once widely used as a [lifting gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lifting_gas "Lifting gas") in balloons and [airships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airship "Airship").[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Almqvist03-75) The first hydrogen-filled [balloon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balloon "Balloon") was invented by [Jacques Charles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacques_Charles "Jacques Charles") in 1783. Hydrogen provided the lift for the first reliable form of air-travel following the 1852 invention of the first hydrogen-lifted [airship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airship "Airship") by [Henri Giffard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Giffard "Henri Giffard"). German count [Ferdinand von Zeppelin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ferdinand_von_Zeppelin "Ferdinand von Zeppelin") promoted the idea of rigid airships lifted by hydrogen that later were called [Zeppelins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeppelin "Zeppelin"); the first of which had its maiden flight in 1900.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-nbb-9) Regularly scheduled flights started in 1910 and by the outbreak of World War I in August 1914, they had carried 35,000 passengers without a serious incident. Hydrogen-lifted airships in the form of [blimps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blimps "Blimps") were used as observation platforms and bombers during the War II, especially on the US Eastern seaboard.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-76)

The first non-stop transatlantic crossing was made by the British airship _[R34](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R34\_(airship) "R34 (airship)")_ in 1919 and regular passenger service resumed in the 1920s. Hydrogen was used in the [_Hindenburg_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LZ_129_Hindenburg "LZ 129 Hindenburg") airship, which caught fire over [New Jersey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Jersey "New Jersey") on 6 May 1937.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-nbb-9) The hydrogen that filled the airship was ignited, possibly by static electricity, and burst into flames.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-77) Following this [Hindenburg disaster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindenburg_disaster "Hindenburg disaster"), commercial hydrogen airship travel [ceased](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_airship#Demise "Rigid airship"). Hydrogen is still used, in preference to non-flammable but more expensive [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium"), as a lifting gas for [weather balloons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weather_balloon#Materials_and_equipment "Weather balloon").[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-78)

#### Deuterium and tritium

[Deuterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuterium "Deuterium") was discovered in December 1931 by [Harold Urey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Urey "Harold Urey"), and [tritium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tritium "Tritium") was prepared in 1934 by [Ernest Rutherford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernest_Rutherford "Ernest Rutherford"), [Mark Oliphant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mark_Oliphant "Mark Oliphant"), and [Paul Harteck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Harteck "Paul Harteck").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Nostrand-10)[Heavy water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heavy_water "Heavy water"), which consists of deuterium in the place of regular hydrogen, was discovered by Urey's group in 1932.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-nbb-9)

Chemistry
---------

### Reactions of H 2

[![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/84/HFe_H2_dppe_2.svg/250px-HFe_H2_dppe_2.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:HFe_H2_dppe_2.svg)

A [dihydrogen complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydrogen_complex "Dihydrogen complex") of iron, [HFe(H 2)(dppe)2]+.

H 2 is relatively unreactive. The thermodynamic basis of this low reactivity is the very strong H–H bond, with a [bond dissociation energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_dissociation_energy "Bond dissociation energy") of 435.7 kJ/mol.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-79) It does form coordination complexes called [dihydrogen complexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydrogen_complex "Dihydrogen complex"). These species provide insights into the early steps in the interactions of hydrogen with metal catalysts. According to [neutron diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_diffraction "Neutron diffraction"), the metal and two H atoms form a triangle in these complexes. The H-H bond remains intact but is elongated. They are acidic.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-80)

Although exotic on Earth, the H+3 ion is common in the universe. It is a triangular species, like the aforementioned dihydrogen complexes. It is known as [protonated molecular hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation "Trihydrogen cation") or the trihydrogen cation.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Carrington-81)

Hydrogen reacts with [chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine") to produce [HCl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HCl "HCl") and with [bromine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bromine "Bromine") to produce [HBr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_bromide "Hydrogen bromide") by a [chain reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chain_reaction "Chain reaction"). The reaction requires initiation. For example, in the case of Br 2, the diatomic molecule is broken into atoms, Br 2 + (UV light) → 2Br•. Propagating reactions consume hydrogen molecules and produce HBr, as well as Br and H atoms:

Br• + H 2 → HBr + H H + Br 2 → HBr +Br
Finally the terminating reaction:

H + HBr → H 2 + Br•2Br• → Br 2.
consumes the remaining atoms.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-82): 289

The addition of H 2 to unsaturated organic compounds, such as [alkenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkene "Alkene") and [alkynes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkyne "Alkyne"), is called [hydrogenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogenation "Hydrogenation"). Even if the reaction is [energetically favorable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exothermic "Exothermic"), it does not take place even at higher temperatures. In the presence of a [catalyst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalyst "Catalyst") like finely divided [platinum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platinum "Platinum") or [nickel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nickel "Nickel"), the reaction proceeds at room temperature.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-83): 477

### Hydrogen-containing compounds

Hydrogen can exist in both +1 and −1 [oxidation states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidation_states "Oxidation states"), forming compounds through [ionic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionic_bonding "Ionic bonding") and [covalent bonding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covalent_bonding "Covalent bonding"). It is a part of a wide range of substances, including water, [hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbons "Hydrocarbons"), and numerous other [organic compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compounds "Organic compounds").[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-hydrocarbon-84) The H+ ion—commonly referred to as a proton due to its single proton and absence of electrons—is central to [acid–base chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid%E2%80%93base_reaction "Acid–base reaction"), although the proton does not move freely. In the [Brønsted–Lowry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Br%C3%B8nsted%E2%80%93Lowry_acids "Brønsted–Lowry acids") framework, acids are defined by their ability to donate H+ ions to bases.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-85)

Hydrogen forms a vast variety of compounds with [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") known as hydrocarbons, and an even greater diversity with other elements ([heteroatoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heteroatoms "Heteroatoms")), giving rise to the broad class of organic compounds often associated with living organisms.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-hydrocarbon-84)

[![Image 15](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/7/7a/NaH.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:NaH.jpg)

A sample of [sodium hydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_hydride "Sodium hydride")

Hydrogen compounds with hydrogen in the oxidation state −1 are known as hydrides, which are usually formed between hydrogen and metals. The hydrides can be ionic (aka saline), covalent, nor metallic. With heating, H 2 reacts efficiently with the alkali and alkaline earth metals to give the [ionic hydrides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydride#Ionic_hydrides "Hydride") of the formula MH and MH 2, respectively. These salt-like crystalline compounds have high melting points and all react with water to liberate hydrogen. Covalent hydrides are include [boranes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boranes "Boranes") and polymeric [aluminium hydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium_hydride "Aluminium hydride"). [Transition metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transition_metals "Transition metals") form [metal hydrides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal_hydrides "Metal hydrides") via continuous dissolution of hydrogen into the metal.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-UllmannH2-86) A well known hydride is [lithium aluminium hydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium_aluminium_hydride "Lithium aluminium hydride"), the [AlH 4]− anion carries hydridic centers firmly attached to the Al(III).[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-87) Perhaps the most extensive series of hydrides are the [boranes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boranes "Boranes"), compounds consisting only of boron and hydrogen.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Downs-88)

Hydrides can bond to these electropositive elements not only as a terminal [ligand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ligand "Ligand") but also as [bridging ligands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bridging_ligand "Bridging ligand"). In diborane (B 2 H 6), four H's are terminal and two bridge between the two B atoms.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Miessler-23)

### Hydrogen bonding

When bonded to a more electronegative element, particularly [fluorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorine "Fluorine"), [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen"), or [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen"), hydrogen can participate in a form of medium-strength noncovalent bonding with another electronegative element with a lone pair like oxygen or nitrogen, a phenomenon called hydrogen bonding that is critical to the stability of many biological molecules.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-89): 375 [[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-90) Hydrogen bonding alters molecule structures, [viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscosity "Viscosity"), [solubility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solubility "Solubility"), as well as melting and boiling points even protein folding dynamics.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-91)

### Protons and acids

[![Image 16](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/db/Base_pair_AT.svg/330px-Base_pair_AT.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Base_pair_AT.svg)

 An "A-T base pair" in DNA illustrating how hydrogen bonds are critical to the [genetic code](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_code "Genetic code"). The drawing illustrates that in many chemical depictions, C-H bonds are not always shown explicitly, an indication of their pervasiveness.

In water, hydrogen bonding plays an important role in reaction thermodynamics. A hydrogen bond can shift over to proton transfer. Under the [Brønsted–Lowry acid–base theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Br%C3%B8nsted%E2%80%93Lowry_acid%E2%80%93base_theory "Brønsted–Lowry acid–base theory"), acids are proton donors, while bases are proton acceptors.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-92): 28  A bare proton, H+ essentially cannot exist in anything other than a vacuum. Otherwise it attaches to other atoms, ions, or molecules. Even species as inert as methane can be protonated. The term 'proton' is used loosely and metaphorically to refer to refer to solvated H+" without any implication that any single protons exist freely as a species. To avoid the implication of the naked proton in solution, acidic aqueous solutions are sometimes considered to contain the "[hydronium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydronium "Hydronium") ion" ([H 3 O]+) or still more accurately, [H 9 O 4]+.[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Okumura-93) Other [oxonium ions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxonium_ion "Oxonium ion") are found when water is in acidic solution with other solvents.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Perdoncin-94)

The concentration of these solvated protons determines the [pH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PH "PH") of a solution, a [logarithmic scale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logarithmic_scale "Logarithmic scale") that reflects its acidity or basicity. Lower pH values indicate higher concentrations of hydronium ions, corresponding to more acidic conditions.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-housecroft-95)

Occurrence
----------

### Cosmic

[![Image 17: A white-green cotton-like clog on black background.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7a/Nursery_of_New_Stars_-_GPN-2000-000972.jpg/250px-Nursery_of_New_Stars_-_GPN-2000-000972.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Nursery_of_New_Stars_-_GPN-2000-000972.jpg)

[NGC 604](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NGC_604 "NGC 604"), a giant [region of ionized hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H_II_region "H II region") in the [Triangulum Galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangulum_Galaxy "Triangulum Galaxy")

Hydrogen, as atomic H, is the most [abundant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_abundance "Natural abundance")[chemical element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_element "Chemical element") in the universe, making up 75% of [normal matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon "Baryon") by [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass")[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-96) and >90% by number of atoms.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-97) In the [early universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_universe "Early universe"), the [protons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protons "Protons") formed in the first second after the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang"); neutral hydrogen atoms formed about 370,000 years later during the [recombination epoch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recombination_(cosmology) "Recombination (cosmology)") as the universe expanded and plasma had cooled enough for electrons to remain bound to protons.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-98)

In astrophysics, neutral hydrogen in the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium") is called _H I_ and ionized hydrogen is called _H II_.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-99) Radiation from stars ionizes H I to H II, creating [spheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Str%C3%B6mgren_sphere "Strömgren sphere") of ionized H II around stars. In the [chronology of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronology_of_the_universe "Chronology of the universe") neutral hydrogen dominated until the birth of stars during the era of [reionization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reionization "Reionization") led to bubbles of ionized hydrogen that grew and merged over 500 million of years.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-100) They are the source of the 21-cm [hydrogen line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_line "Hydrogen line") at 1420 MHz that is detected in order to probe primordial hydrogen. The large amount of neutral hydrogen found in the [damped Lyman-alpha systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Damped_Lyman-alpha_system "Damped Lyman-alpha system") is thought to dominate the [cosmological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_cosmology "Physical cosmology")[baryonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon "Baryon") density of the universe up to a [redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift") of _z_=4.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-101)

Hydrogen is found in great abundance in stars and [gas giant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_giant "Gas giant") planets. [Molecular clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_cloud "Molecular cloud") of H 2 are associated with [star formation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_formation "Star formation"). Hydrogen plays a vital role in powering [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star") through the [proton-proton reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton-proton_reaction "Proton-proton reaction") in lower-mass stars, and through the [CNO cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CNO_cycle "CNO cycle") of [nuclear fusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_fusion "Nuclear fusion") in case of stars more massive than the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun").[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-102)

A molecular form called [protonated molecular hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonated_molecular_hydrogen "Protonated molecular hydrogen") (H+3) is found in the interstellar medium, where it is generated by ionization of molecular hydrogen from [cosmic rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_ray "Cosmic ray"). This ion has also been observed in the [upper atmosphere of Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_atmosphere "Primary atmosphere"). The ion is long-lived in outer space due to the low temperature and density. H+3 is one of the most abundant ions in the universe, and it plays a notable role in the chemistry of the interstellar medium.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-103) Neutral [triatomic hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triatomic_hydrogen "Triatomic hydrogen")H 3 can exist only in an excited form and is unstable.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-couple-104)

### Terrestrial

Hydrogen is the third most abundant element on the Earth's surface,[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-ArgonneBasic-105) mostly in the form of [chemical compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_compound "Chemical compound") such as [hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon") and water.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Miessler-23) Elemental hydrogen is normally in the form of a gas, H 2. It is present in a very low concentration in Earth's atmosphere (around 0.53 [ppm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Part_per_million "Part per million") on a molar basis[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Grinter-106)) because of its light weight, which enables it to [escape the atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_escape "Atmospheric escape") more rapidly than heavier gases. Despite its low concentration in our atmosphere, terrestrial hydrogen is sufficiently abundant to support the metabolism of several bacteria.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-107)

Large underground deposits of hydrogen gas have been discovered in several countries including [Mali](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mali "Mali"), [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France "France") and [Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Australia "Australia").[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Pearce-2024-108) As of 2024, it is uncertain how much underground hydrogen can be extracted economically.[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Pearce-2024-108)

Production and storage
----------------------

### Industrial routes

Nearly all of the world's current supply of hydrogen gas (H 2) is created from fossil fuels.[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-109)[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-rosenow-2022-110): 1  Many methods exist for producing H 2, but three dominate commercially: steam reforming often coupled to water-gas shift, partial oxidation of hydrocarbons, and water electrolysis.[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-KO-111)

#### Steam reforming

[![Image 18](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/eb/SMR%2BWGS-1.png/250px-SMR%2BWGS-1.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:SMR%2BWGS-1.png)

Inputs and outputs of steam reforming (SMR) and water gas shift (WGS) reaction of natural gas, a process used in hydrogen production

Hydrogen is mainly produced by [steam methane reforming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steam_reforming "Steam reforming") (SMR), the reaction of water and methane.[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-rotech-112)[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Oxtoby-113) Thus, at high temperature (1000–1400 K, 700–1100°C or 1300–2000°F), steam (water vapor) reacts with [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane") to yield [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide") and H 2.

CH 4 + H 2 O → CO + 3 H 2
Producing one tonne of hydrogen through this process emits 6.6–9.3 tonnes of carbon dioxide.[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Bonheure-2021-114) The production of natural gas feedstock also produces emissions such as [vented](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_venting "Gas venting") and [fugitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fugitive_gas_emissions "Fugitive gas emissions")[methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane"), which further contributes to the overall carbon footprint of hydrogen.[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Griffiths-20212-115)

This reaction is favored at low pressures, Nonetheless, conducted at high pressures (2.0 MPa, 20 atm or 600[inHg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/InHg "InHg")) because high-pressure H 2 is the most marketable product, and [pressure swing adsorption](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure_swing_adsorption "Pressure swing adsorption") (PSA) purification systems work better at higher pressures. The product mixture is known as "[synthesis gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthesis_gas "Synthesis gas")" because it is often used directly for the production of [methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol") and many other compounds. [Hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon") other than methane can be used to produce synthesis gas with varying product ratios. One of the many complications to this highly optimized technology is the formation of coke or carbon:

CH 4 → C + 2 H 2
Therefore, steam reforming typically employs an excess of H 2 O. Additional hydrogen can be recovered from the steam by using carbon monoxide through the [water gas shift reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_gas_shift_reaction "Water gas shift reaction") (WGS). This process requires an [iron oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_oxide "Iron oxide") catalyst:[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Oxtoby-113)

CO + H 2 O → CO 2 + H 2
Hydrogen is sometimes produced and consumed in the same industrial process, without being separated. In the [Haber process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haber_process "Haber process") for [ammonia production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia_production "Ammonia production"), hydrogen is generated from natural gas.[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-116)

#### Partial oxidation of hydrocarbons

Other methods for CO and H 2 production include partial oxidation of hydrocarbons:[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-uigi-44)

2 CH 4 + O 2 → 2 CO + 4 H 2
Although less important commercially, coal can serve as a prelude to the shift reaction above:[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Oxtoby-113)

C + H 2 O → CO + H 2
Olefin production units may produce substantial quantities of byproduct hydrogen particularly from cracking light feedstocks like [ethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethane "Ethane") or [propane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propane "Propane").[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-117)

#### Water electrolysis

[![Image 19](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cf/Hydrogen_production_via_Electrolysis.png/250px-Hydrogen_production_via_Electrolysis.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hydrogen_production_via_Electrolysis.png)

Inputs and outputs of the electrolysis of water production of hydrogen

[Electrolysis of water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water "Electrolysis of water") is a conceptually simple method of producing hydrogen.

2 H 2 O(l) → 2 H 2(g) + O 2(g)
Commercial [electrolyzers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrolyzer "Electrolyzer") use [nickel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nickel "Nickel")-based catalysts in strongly alkaline solution. Platinum is a better catalyst but is expensive.[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-118) The hydrogen created through electrolysis using renewable energy is commonly referred to as "[green hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_hydrogen "Green hydrogen")".[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-RoyalSociety-2021-119)

[Electrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrolysis "Electrolysis") of [brine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brine "Brine") to yield [chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine")[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-120) also produces high purity hydrogen as a co-product, which is used for a variety of transformations such as [hydrogenations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogenation "Hydrogenation").[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-121)

The [electrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrolysis "Electrolysis") process is more expensive than producing hydrogen from methane without [carbon capture and storage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_capture_and_storage "Carbon capture and storage").[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Evans-2020-122)

Innovation in [hydrogen electrolyzers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water "Electrolysis of water") could make large-scale production of hydrogen from electricity more cost-competitive.[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-123)

#### Methane pyrolysis

Hydrogen can be produced by [pyrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrolysis "Pyrolysis") of natural gas (methane), producing hydrogen gas and solid carbon with the aid a catalyst and 74 kJ/mol input heat:

CH 4(g) → C(s) + 2 H 2(g) (ΔH° = 74 kJ/mol)
The carbon may be sold as a manufacturing feedstock or fuel, or landfilled. This route could have a lower carbon footprint than existing hydrogen production processes, but mechanisms for removing the carbon and preventing it from reacting with the catalyst remain obstacles for industrial scale use.[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-124): 17 [[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-125)

#### Thermochemical

[Water splitting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_splitting "Water splitting") is the process by which water is decomposed into its components. Relevant to the biological scenario is this simple equation:

2 H 2 O → 4 H+ + O 2 + 4e−
The reaction occurs in the [light reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-dependent_reactions "Light-dependent reactions") in all [photosynthetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthetic "Photosynthetic") organisms. A few organisms, including the alga _[Chlamydomonas reinhardtii](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlamydomonas\_reinhardtii "Chlamydomonas reinhardtii")_ and [cyanobacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanobacteria "Cyanobacteria"), have evolved a second step in the [dark reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_reaction "Dark reaction") in which protons and electrons are reduced to form H 2 gas by specialized hydrogenases in the [chloroplast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloroplast "Chloroplast").[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-126)

Efforts have been undertaken to genetically modify cyanobacterial hydrogenases to more efficiently generate H 2 gas even in the presence of oxygen.[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-127) Efforts have also been undertaken with genetically modified [alga in a bioreactor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biological_hydrogen_production_(Algae) "Biological hydrogen production (Algae)").[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-128)

Relevant to the thermal water-splitting scenario is this simple equation:

2 H 2 O → 2 H 2 + O 2
More than 200 thermochemical cycles can be used for [water splitting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_splitting "Water splitting"). Many of these cycles such as the [iron oxide cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_oxide_cycle "Iron oxide cycle"), [cerium(IV) oxide–cerium(III) oxide cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cerium(IV)_oxide%E2%80%93cerium(III)_oxide_cycle "Cerium(IV) oxide–cerium(III) oxide cycle"), [zinc zinc-oxide cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zinc_zinc-oxide_cycle "Zinc zinc-oxide cycle"), [sulfur-iodine cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur-iodine_cycle "Sulfur-iodine cycle"), [copper-chlorine cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper-chlorine_cycle "Copper-chlorine cycle") and [hybrid sulfur cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hybrid_sulfur_cycle "Hybrid sulfur cycle") have been evaluated for their commercial potential to produce hydrogen and oxygen from water and heat without using electricity.[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-129) A number of labs (including in [France](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/France "France"), [Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany "Germany"), [Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greece "Greece"), [Japan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japan "Japan"), and the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States")) are developing thermochemical methods to produce hydrogen from solar energy and water.[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-130)

### Natural routes

#### Biohydrogen

H 2 is produced by enzymes called [hydrogenases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogenase "Hydrogenase"). This process allows the host organism to use [fermentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermentation "Fermentation") as a source of energy.[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-131) These same enzymes also can oxidize H 2, such that the host organisms can subsist by reducing oxidized substrates using electrons extracted from H 2.[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-132)

The hydrogenase enzyme feature [iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron "Iron") or [nickel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nickel "Nickel")-iron centers at their [active sites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_site "Active site").[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-133) The natural cycle of hydrogen production and consumption by organisms is called the [hydrogen cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cycle "Hydrogen cycle").[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Rhee6-134)

Some bacteria such as _[Mycobacterium smegmatis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mycobacterium\_smegmatis "Mycobacterium smegmatis")_ can use the small amount of hydrogen in the atmosphere as a source of energy when other sources are lacking. Their hydrogenase are designed with small channels that exclude oxygen and so permits the reaction to occur even though the hydrogen concentration is very low and the oxygen concentration is as in normal air.[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Grinter-106)[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-135)

Confirming the existence of hydrogenases in the human gut, H 2 occurs in human breath. The concentration in the breath of fasting people at rest is typically less than 5 [parts per million](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts_per_million "Parts per million") (ppm) but can be 50 ppm when people with intestinal disorders consume molecules they cannot absorb during diagnostic [hydrogen breath tests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_breath_test "Hydrogen breath test").[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-136)

#### Serpentinization

[Serpentinization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serpentinization "Serpentinization") is a geological mechanism that produce highly [reducing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reduction_(chemistry) "Reduction (chemistry)") conditions.[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-FrostBeard2007-137) Under these conditions, water is capable of oxidizing ferrous (Fe 2+

) ions in [fayalite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fayalite "Fayalite"), generating hydrogen gas:[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Dincer-2015-138)[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-139)

Fe 2 SiO 4 + H 2 O → 2 Fe 3 O 4 + SiO 2 + H 2
Closely related to this geological process is the [Schikorr reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schikorr_reaction "Schikorr reaction"):

3 Fe(OH)2 → Fe 3 O 4 + 2 H 2 O + H 2
This process also is relevant to the corrosion of [iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron "Iron") and [steel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steel "Steel") in [oxygen-free](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anoxic_waters "Anoxic waters")[groundwater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Groundwater "Groundwater") and in reducing [soils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soil "Soil") below the [water table](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_table "Water table").[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-140)

### Laboratory syntheses

H 2 is produced in laboratory settings, such as in the small-scale [electrolysis of water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrolysis_of_water "Electrolysis of water") using metal [electrodes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrodes "Electrodes") and water containing an [electrolyte](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrolyte "Electrolyte"), which liberates hydrogen gas at the [cathode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cathode "Cathode"):[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-housecroft-95)

2H+(aq) + 2e− → H 2(g)
Hydrogen is also often a by-product of other reactions. Many metals react with water to produce H 2, but the rate of hydrogen evolution depends on the metal, the pH, and the presence of alloying agents. Most often, hydrogen evolution is induced by acids. The alkali and alkaline earth metals, aluminium, zinc, manganese, and iron react readily with aqueous acids.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-housecroft-95)

Zn + 2 H+ → Zn 2+ + H 2
Many metals, such as [aluminium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium "Aluminium"), are slow to react with water because they form passivated oxide coatings of oxides. An alloy of aluminium and [gallium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gallium "Gallium"), however, does react with water. At high pH, aluminium can produce H 2:[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-housecroft-95)

2 Al + 6 H 2 O + 2 OH− → 2 [Al(OH)4]− + 3 H 2
### Storage

If H 2 is to be used as an energy source, its storage is important. It dissolves only poorly in solvents. For example, at room temperature and 0.1 M[pascal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit) "Pascal (unit)"), ca. 0.05 moles dissolves in one kilogram of [diethyl ether](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diethyl_ether "Diethyl ether").[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-UllmannH2-86) The H 2 can be stored in compressed form, although compressing costs energy. Liquifaction is impractical given its low [critical temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_temperature "Critical temperature"). In contrast, ammonia and many hydrocarbons can be liquified at room temperature under pressure. For these reasons, hydrogen _carriers_ - materials that reversibly bind H 2 - have attracted much attention. The key question is then the weight percent of H 2-equivalents within the carrier material. For example, hydrogen can be reversibly absorbed into many [rare earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rare_earth_element "Rare earth element") and [transition metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transition_metal "Transition metal")[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Takeshita-141) and is soluble in both nanocrystalline and [amorphous metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amorphous_metal "Amorphous metal").[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Kirchheim1-142) Hydrogen [solubility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solubility "Solubility") in metals is influenced by local distortions or impurities in the [crystal lattice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystal_lattice "Crystal lattice").[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Kirchheim2-143) These properties may be useful when hydrogen is purified by passage through hot [palladium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Palladium "Palladium") disks, but the gas's high solubility is also a metallurgical problem, contributing to the [embrittlement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_embrittlement "Hydrogen embrittlement") of many metals,[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Rogers_1999_1057%E2%80%931064-144) complicating the design of pipelines and storage tanks.[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Christensen-145)

The most problematic aspect of metal hydrides for storage is their modest H 2 content, often on the order of 1%. For this reason, there is interest in storage of H 2 in compounds of low [molecular weight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_weight "Molecular weight"). For example, [ammonia borane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia_borane "Ammonia borane") (H 3 N−BH 3) contains 19.8 weight percent of H 2. The problem with this material is that after release of H 2, the resulting boron nitride does not re-add H 2, i.e. ammonia borane is an irreversible hydrogen carrier.[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-146) More attractive, somewhat ironically, are [hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon") such as [tetrahydroquinoline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahydroquinoline "Tetrahydroquinoline"), which reversibly release some H 2 when heated in the presence of a catalyst:[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-147)

C 9 H 10 NH ⇌ C 9 H 7 N + 2H 2
Applications
------------

[![Image 20](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/2023-10-19_Clean_Hydrogen_Ladder_5.0.png/520px-2023-10-19_Clean_Hydrogen_Ladder_5.0.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:2023-10-19_Clean_Hydrogen_Ladder_5.0.png)

Hydrogen Ladder: Ranking of hydrogen applications and uses in the medium term, but analysts disagree[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-148)

### Petrochemical industry

Large quantities of H 2 are used in the "upgrading" of [fossil fuels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil_fuels "Fossil fuels"). Key consumers of H 2 include [hydrodesulfurization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrodesulfurization "Hydrodesulfurization"), and [hydrocracking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocracking "Hydrocracking"). Many of these reactions can be classified as [hydrogenolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogenolysis "Hydrogenolysis"), i.e., the cleavage of bonds by hydrogen. Illustrative is the separation of sulfur from liquid fossil fuels:[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-KO-111)[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-UllmannHuse-149)

R 2 S + 2 H 2 → H 2 S + 2 RH
### Hydrogenation

[Hydrogenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogenation "Hydrogenation"), the addition of H 2 to various substrates, is done on a large scale. Hydrogenation of N 2 produces ammonia by the [Haber process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haber_process "Haber process"):[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-UllmannHuse-149)

N 2 + 3 H 2 → 2 NH 3
This process consumes a few percent of the energy budget in the entire industry and is the biggest consumer of hydrogen. The resulting ammonia is used in fertilizers critical to the supply of protein consumed by humans.[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Smil_2004_Enriching-150) Hydrogenation is also used to convert [unsaturated fats](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unsaturated_fat "Unsaturated fat") and [oils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vegetable_oil "Vegetable oil") to saturated fats and oils. The major application is the production of [margarine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Margarine "Margarine"). [Methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol") is produced by hydrogenation of carbon dioxide; the mixture of hydrogen and carbon dioxide used for this process is known as [syngas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syngas "Syngas"). It is similarly the source of hydrogen in the manufacture of [hydrochloric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid "Hydrochloric acid"). H 2 is also used as a [reducing agent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reducing_agent "Reducing agent") for the conversion of some [ores](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ore "Ore") to the metals.[[151]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-151)[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-housecroft-95)

### Fuel

The potential for using hydrogen (H 2) as a fuel has been widely discussed. Hydrogen can be used in [fuel cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fuel_cells "Fuel cells") to produce electricity,[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-152) or burned to generate heat.[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Lewis-2021-153) When hydrogen is consumed in fuel cells, the only emission at the point of use is water vapor.[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Lewis-2021-153) When burned, hydrogen produces relatively little pollution at the point of combustion, but can lead to thermal formation of harmful [nitrogen oxides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NOx "NOx").[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Lewis-2021-153)

If hydrogen is produced with low or zero greenhouse gas emissions ([green hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_hydrogen "Green hydrogen")), it can play a significant role in decarbonizing energy systems where there are challenges and limitations to replacing fossil fuels with direct use of electricity.[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-IPCC-2022-154)[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Evans-2020-122)

Hydrogen fuel can produce the intense heat required for industrial production of steel, cement, glass, and chemicals, thus contributing to the decarbonization of industry alongside other technologies, such as [electric arc furnaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_arc_furnace "Electric arc furnace") for steelmaking.[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-155) However, it is likely to play a larger role in providing industrial feedstock for cleaner production of ammonia and organic chemicals.[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-IPCC-2022-154) For example, in [steelmaking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steelmaking "Steelmaking"), hydrogen could function as a clean fuel and also as a low-carbon catalyst, replacing coal-derived [coke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coke_(fuel) "Coke (fuel)") (carbon):[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-156)

2FeO + C → 2Fe + CO 2 vs FeO + H 2 → Fe + H 2 O
Hydrogen used to decarbonize transportation is likely to find its largest applications in shipping, aviation and, to a lesser extent, heavy goods vehicles, through the use of hydrogen-derived synthetic fuels such as [ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_ammonia "Green ammonia") and [methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_methanol "Green methanol") and fuel cell technology.[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-IPCC-2022-154) For light-duty vehicles including cars, hydrogen is far behind other [alternative fuel vehicles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternative_fuel_vehicle "Alternative fuel vehicle"), especially compared with the rate of adoption of [battery electric vehicles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Battery_electric_vehicles "Battery electric vehicles"), and may not play a significant role in future.[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-157)

[![Image 21: A black inverted funnel with blue glow emerging from its opening.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/18/Shuttle_Main_Engine_Test_Firing_cropped_edited_and_reduced.jpg/250px-Shuttle_Main_Engine_Test_Firing_cropped_edited_and_reduced.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Shuttle_Main_Engine_Test_Firing_cropped_edited_and_reduced.jpg)

[Space Shuttle Main Engine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Main_Engine "Space Shuttle Main Engine") burning hydrogen with oxygen, produces a nearly invisible flame at full thrust.

[Liquid hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_hydrogen "Liquid hydrogen") and [liquid oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_oxygen "Liquid oxygen") together serve as [cryogenic propellants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryogenic_propellant "Cryogenic propellant") in [liquid-propellant rockets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid-propellant_rocket "Liquid-propellant rocket"), as in the [Space Shuttle main engines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RS-25 "RS-25"). [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA") has investigated the use of [rocket propellant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket_propellant "Rocket propellant") made from atomic hydrogen, boron or carbon that is frozen into solid molecular hydrogen particles suspended in liquid helium. Upon warming, the mixture vaporizes to allow the atomic species to recombine, heating the mixture to high temperature.[[158]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-158)

Hydrogen produced when there is a surplus of [variable renewable electricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_renewable_energy "Variable renewable energy") could in principle be stored and later used to generate heat or to re-generate electricity.[[159]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-159) It can be further transformed into [synthetic fuels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthetic_fuel "Synthetic fuel") such as [ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia") and [methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol").[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-160) Disadvantages of hydrogen fuel include high costs of storage and distribution due to hydrogen's explosivity, its large volume compared to other fuels, and its tendency to make pipes brittle.[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Griffiths-20212-115)

### Nickel–hydrogen battery

The very long-lived, rechargeable [nickel–hydrogen battery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nickel%E2%80%93hydrogen_battery "Nickel–hydrogen battery") developed for satellite power systems uses pressurized gaseous H 2.[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-161) The [International Space Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Space_Station "International Space Station"),[[162]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-162)[Mars Odyssey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2001_Mars_Odyssey "2001 Mars Odyssey")[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-163) and the [Mars Global Surveyor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars_Global_Surveyor "Mars Global Surveyor")[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-164) are equipped with nickel-hydrogen batteries. In the dark part of its orbit, the [Hubble Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope "Hubble Space Telescope") is also powered by nickel-hydrogen batteries, which were finally replaced in May 2009,[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-165) more than 19 years after launch and 13 years beyond their design life.[[166]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-166)

### Semiconductor industry

Hydrogen is employed to saturate broken ("dangling") bonds of [amorphous silicon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amorphous_silicon "Amorphous silicon") and [amorphous carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amorphous_carbon "Amorphous carbon") that helps stabilizing material properties.[[167]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-167) Hydrogen, introduced as an unintended side-effect of production, acts as a shallow [electron donor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_donor "Electron donor") leading to [n-type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-type_semiconductor "N-type semiconductor") conductivity in [ZnO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ZnO "ZnO"), with important uses in [transducers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transducers "Transducers") and [phosphors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphors "Phosphors").[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-168)[[169]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-169) Detailed analysis of ZnO and of [MgO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MgO "MgO") show evidence of four and six-fold hydrogen multicentre bonds.[[170]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-170) The doping behavior of hydrogen varies with the material.[[171]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-171)[[172]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-172)

### Niche and evolving uses

Other than the uses mentioned above, hydrogen is also used in smaller scales in the following applications:

*   Shielding gas: Hydrogen is used as a [shielding gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shielding_gas "Shielding gas") in [welding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Welding "Welding") methods such as [atomic hydrogen welding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_hydrogen_welding "Atomic hydrogen welding").[[173]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-173)[[174]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-174)

*   Coolant: Hydrogen is used as a [coolant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coolant "Coolant") in large power stations generators due to its high [thermal conductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_conductivity "Thermal conductivity") and low density.[[175]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-175) The first [hydrogen-cooled turbogenerator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen-cooled_turbogenerator "Hydrogen-cooled turbogenerator") went into service using gaseous hydrogen as a [coolant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coolant "Coolant") in the rotor and the stator in 1937 at [Dayton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dayton,_Ohio "Dayton, Ohio"), Ohio.[[176]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-176)
*   Cryogenic research: Liquid H 2 is used in [cryogenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryogenic "Cryogenic") research, including [superconductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superconductivity "Superconductivity") studies.[[177]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-177)
*   Leak detection: Pure or mixed with nitrogen (sometimes called [forming gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forming_gas "Forming gas")), hydrogen is a [tracer gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tracer_gas "Tracer gas") for [detection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leak_detection "Leak detection") of minute leaks. Applications can be found in the automotive, chemical, power generation, aerospace, and telecommunications industries.[[178]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-178) Hydrogen is an authorized food additive (E 949) that allows food package leak testing, as well as having anti-oxidizing properties.[[179]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-179)

*   Neutron moderation: [Deuterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuterium "Deuterium") (hydrogen-2) is used in [nuclear fission applications](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CANDU_reactor "CANDU reactor") as a [moderator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_moderator "Neutron moderator") to slow [neutrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron").
*   Nuclear fusion fuel: Deuterium is used in [nuclear fusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_fusion "Nuclear fusion") reactions.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-nbb-9)
*   Isotopic labeling: Deuterium compounds have applications in chemistry and biology in studies of [isotope effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_isotope_effect "Kinetic isotope effect") on reaction rates.[[180]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-180)

*   Tritium uses: [Tritium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tritium "Tritium") (hydrogen-3), produced in [nuclear reactors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_reactor "Nuclear reactor"), is used in the production of [hydrogen bombs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_bomb "Hydrogen bomb"),[[181]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-181) as an isotopic label in the biosciences,[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-holte-29) and as a source of [beta radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_particle "Beta particle") in [radioluminescent paint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tritium_radioluminescence "Tritium radioluminescence") for instrument dials and emergency signage.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Traub95-24)

In hydrogen pipelines and steel storage vessels, hydrogen molecules are prone to react with metals, causing [hydrogen embrittlement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_embrittlement "Hydrogen embrittlement") and leaks in the pipeline or storage vessel.[[183]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Li-2022-183) Since it is lighter than air, hydrogen does not easily accumulate to form a combustible gas mixture.[[183]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Li-2022-183) However, even without ignition sources, high-pressure hydrogen leakage may cause spontaneous combustion and [detonation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detonation "Detonation").[[183]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Li-2022-183)

Hydrogen is flammable when mixed even in small amounts with air. Ignition can occur at a volumetric ratio of hydrogen to air as low as 4%.[[184]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-184) In approximately 70% of hydrogen ignition accidents, the ignition source cannot be found, and it is widely believed by scholars that spontaneous ignition of hydrogen occurs.[[183]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-Li-2022-183)

Hydrogen fire, while being extremely hot, is almost invisible, and thus can lead to accidental burns.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-spinoff-2016-43) Hydrogen is non-toxic,[[185]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-185) but like most gases it can cause [asphyxiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asphyxiation "Asphyxiation") in the absence of adequate ventilation.[[186]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen#cite_note-186)
