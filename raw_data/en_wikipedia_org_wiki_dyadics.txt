From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), specifically [multilinear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilinear_algebra "Multilinear algebra"), a **dyadic** or **dyadic tensor** is a second [order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_(intrinsic_definition)#Definition_via_tensor_products_of_vector_spaces "Tensor (intrinsic definition)")[tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor"), written in a notation that fits in with [vector algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_calculus "Vector calculus").

There are numerous ways to multiply two [Euclidean vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector"). The [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") takes in two vectors and returns a [scalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(physics) "Scalar (physics)"), while the [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product")[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dyadics#cite_note-1) returns a [pseudovector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudovector "Pseudovector"). Both of these have various significant geometric interpretations and are widely used in mathematics, [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), and [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering"). The **dyadic product** takes in two vectors and returns a second order tensor called a _dyadic_ in this context. A dyadic can be used to contain physical or geometric information, although in general there is no direct way of geometrically interpreting it.

The dyadic product is [distributive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributive_property "Distributive property") over [vector addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_addition "Vector addition"), and [associative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative "Associative") with [scalar multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_multiplication "Scalar multiplication"). Therefore, the dyadic product is [linear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear "Linear") in both of its operands. In general, two dyadics can be added to get another dyadic, and [multiplied](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_multiplication "Scalar multiplication") by numbers to scale the dyadic. However, the product is not [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative "Commutative"); changing the order of the vectors results in a different dyadic.

The formalism of **dyadic algebra** is an extension of vector algebra to include the dyadic product of vectors. The dyadic product is also associative with the dot and cross products with other vectors, which allows the dot, cross, and dyadic products to be combined to obtain other scalars, vectors, or dyadics.

It also has some aspects of [matrix algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_algebra "Matrix algebra"), as the numerical components of vectors can be arranged into [row and column vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Row_and_column_vectors "Row and column vectors"), and those of second order tensors in [square matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_matrix "Square matrix"). Also, the dot, cross, and dyadic products can all be expressed in matrix form. Dyadic expressions may closely resemble the matrix equivalents.

The dot product of a dyadic with a vector gives another vector, and taking the dot product of this result gives a scalar derived from the dyadic. The effect that a given dyadic has on other vectors can provide indirect physical or geometric interpretations.

Dyadic notation was first established by [Josiah Willard Gibbs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Josiah_Willard_Gibbs "Josiah Willard Gibbs") in 1884. The notation and terminology are relatively obsolete today. Its uses in physics include [continuum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics "Continuum mechanics") and [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism").

In this article, upper-case bold variables denote dyadics (including dyads) whereas lower-case bold variables denote vectors. An alternative notation uses respectively double and single over- or underbars.

### Dyadic, outer, and tensor products

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dyadics&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Dyadic, outer, and tensor products")]

A _dyad_ is a [tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") of [order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_order "Tensor order") two and [rank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_rank "Tensor rank") one, and is the dyadic product of two [vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") ([complex vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_vector "Complex vector") in general), whereas a _dyadic_ is a general [tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") of [order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_order "Tensor order") two (which may be full rank or not).

There are several equivalent terms and notations for this product:

In the dyadic context they all have the same definition and meaning, and are used synonymously, although the **tensor product** is an instance of the more general and abstract use of the term.

#### Three-dimensional Euclidean space

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dyadics&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Three-dimensional Euclidean space")]

To illustrate the equivalent usage, consider [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space")[Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), letting:

be two vectors where **i**, **j**, **k** (also denoted **e**1, **e**2, **e**3) are the standard [basis vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_vectors "Basis vectors") in this [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") (see also [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates")). Then the dyadic product of **a** and **b** can be represented as a sum:

![Image 2: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {ab} =\qquad &a_{1}b_{1}\mathbf {ii} +a_{1}b_{2}\mathbf {ij} +a_{1}b_{3}\mathbf {ik} \\{}+{}&a_{2}b_{1}\mathbf {ji} +a_{2}b_{2}\mathbf {jj} +a_{2}b_{3}\mathbf {jk} \\{}+{}&a_{3}b_{1}\mathbf {ki} +a_{3}b_{2}\mathbf {kj} +a_{3}b_{3}\mathbf {kk} \end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f8060098342c51acb4cb0bb26189e9b535fff18)
or by extension from row and column vectors, a 3×3 matrix (also the result of the outer product or tensor product of **a** and **b**):

![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbf {ab} \equiv \mathbf {a} \otimes \mathbf {b} \equiv \mathbf {ab} ^{\mathsf {T}}={\begin{pmatrix}a_{1}\\a_{2}\\a_{3}\end{pmatrix}}{\begin{pmatrix}b_{1}&b_{2}&b_{3}\end{pmatrix}}={\begin{pmatrix}a_{1}b_{1}&a_{1}b_{2}&a_{1}b_{3}\\a_{2}b_{1}&a_{2}b_{2}&a_{2}b_{3}\\a_{3}b_{1}&a_{3}b_{2}&a_{3}b_{3}\end{pmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f77886ef8e95e092094a4b364c589730addd2256)

Just as the standard basis (and unit) vectors **i**, **j**, **k**, have the representations:

(which can be transposed), the _standard basis (and unit) dyads_ have the representation:

#### _N_-dimensional Euclidean space

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dyadics&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: N-dimensional Euclidean space")]

If the Euclidean space is _N_-[dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension"), and

![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {a} &=\sum _{i=1}^{N}a_{i}\mathbf {e} _{i}=a_{1}\mathbf {e} _{1}+a_{2}\mathbf {e} _{2}+{\ldots }+a_{N}\mathbf {e} _{N}\\\mathbf {b} &=\sum _{j=1}^{N}b_{j}\mathbf {e} _{j}=b_{1}\mathbf {e} _{1}+b_{2}\mathbf {e} _{2}+\ldots +b_{N}\mathbf {e} _{N}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e223b2ecbe051a9201ba7f0d9158f16c3078e08f)
where **e**_i_ and **e**_j_ are the [standard basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_basis "Standard basis") vectors in _N_-dimensions (the index _i_ on **e**_i_ selects a specific vector, not a component of the vector as in _a i_), then in algebraic form their dyadic product is:

![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mathbf {ab} =\sum _{j=1}^{N}\sum _{i=1}^{N}a_{i}b_{j}\mathbf {e} _{i}\mathbf {e} _{j}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/636c05a7c69e35a608f6421ccdd7907c78eb708b)
This is known as the _nonion form_ of the dyadic. Their outer/tensor product in matrix form is:

![Image 9: {\displaystyle \mathbf {ab} =\mathbf {ab} ^{\mathsf {T}}={\begin{pmatrix}a_{1}\\a_{2}\\\vdots \\a_{N}\end{pmatrix}}{\begin{pmatrix}b_{1}&b_{2}&\cdots &b_{N}\end{pmatrix}}={\begin{pmatrix}a_{1}b_{1}&a_{1}b_{2}&\cdots &a_{1}b_{N}\\a_{2}b_{1}&a_{2}b_{2}&\cdots &a_{2}b_{N}\\\vdots &\vdots &\ddots &\vdots \\a_{N}b_{1}&a_{N}b_{2}&\cdots &a_{N}b_{N}\end{pmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad4b70465daf4dd188d93df178b4575309411239)
A _dyadic polynomial_**A**, otherwise known as a dyadic, is formed from multiple vectors **a**_i_ and **b**_j_:

![Image 10: {\displaystyle \mathbf {A} =\sum _{i}\mathbf {a} _{i}\mathbf {b} _{i}=\mathbf {a} _{1}\mathbf {b} _{1}+\mathbf {a} _{2}\mathbf {b} _{2}+\mathbf {a} _{3}\mathbf {b} _{3}+\ldots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51d18d0fa8839b9f2da9ff13e2fdc7723386445d)
A dyadic which cannot be reduced to a sum of less than _N_ dyads is said to be complete. In this case, the forming vectors are non-coplanar,[_[dubious](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy\_dispute#Disputed\_statement "Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute") – [discuss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Talk:Dyadics#Dubious "Talk:Dyadics")_] see [Chen (1983)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dyadics#Chen).

#### Double-cross product

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dyadics&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Double-cross product")]

We can see that, for any dyad formed from two vectors **a** and **b**, its double cross product is zero.

![Image 31: {\displaystyle \left(\mathbf {ab} \right){}_{\times }^{\times }\left(\mathbf {ab} \right)=\left(\mathbf {a} \times \mathbf {a} \right)\left(\mathbf {b} \times \mathbf {b} \right)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d9e96d00523d8900763be5b40be70e553b290cd)
However, by definition, a dyadic double-cross product on itself will generally be non-zero. For example, a dyadic **A** composed of six different vectors

![Image 32: {\displaystyle \mathbf {A} =\sum _{i=1}^{3}\mathbf {a} _{i}\mathbf {b} _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f937bf8fbd3f60ae6cfa3c704fb2e842533ebcca)
has a non-zero self-double-cross product of

The corresponding matrix is

![Image 44: {\displaystyle \mathbf {I} ={\begin{pmatrix}1&0&0\\0&1&0\\0&0&1\\\end{pmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f64d0bfec401006e07ffe75348214ddfa2c42fff)
This can be put on more careful foundations (explaining what the logical content of "juxtaposing notation" could possibly mean) using the language of tensor products. If _V_ is a finite-dimensional [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space"), a dyadic tensor on _V_ is an elementary tensor in the tensor product of _V_ with its [dual space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_space "Dual space").

The tensor product of _V_ and its dual space is [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphic "Isomorphic") to the space of [linear maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map") from _V_ to _V_: a dyadic tensor _vf_ is simply the linear map sending any _w_ in _V_ to _f_(_w_)_v_. When _V_ is Euclidean _n_-space, we can use the [inner product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product "Inner product") to identify the dual space with _V_ itself, making a dyadic tensor an elementary tensor product of two vectors in Euclidean space.

In this sense, the unit dyadic **ij** is the function from 3-space to itself sending _a_ 1**i** + _a_ 2**j** + _a_ 3**k** to _a_ 2**i**, and **jj** sends this sum to _a_ 2**j**. Now it is revealed in what (precise) sense **ii** + **jj** + **kk** is the identity: it sends _a_ 1**i** + _a_ 2**j** + _a_ 3**k** to itself because its effect is to sum each unit vector in the standard basis scaled by the coefficient of the vector in that basis.

### Vector projection and rejection

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dyadics&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Vector projection and rejection")]

A nonzero vector **a** can always be split into two perpendicular components, one parallel (‖) to the direction of a [unit vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector")**n**, and one perpendicular (⊥) to it;

![Image 46: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} =\mathbf {a} _{\parallel }+\mathbf {a} _{\perp }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/308188bb843e84b11c7398bdc51373c814ce54fa)
The parallel component is found by [vector projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_projection "Vector projection"), which is equivalent to the dot product of **a** with the dyadic **nn**,

![Image 47: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} _{\parallel }=\mathbf {n} (\mathbf {n} \cdot \mathbf {a} )=(\mathbf {nn} )\cdot \mathbf {a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b58ee0d2b3d055643037a2ec2d8dd7696545745)
and the perpendicular component is found from [vector rejection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_rejection "Vector rejection"), which is equivalent to the dot product of **a** with the dyadic **I** − **nn**,

![Image 48: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} _{\perp }=\mathbf {a} -\mathbf {n} (\mathbf {n} \cdot \mathbf {a} )=(\mathbf {I} -\mathbf {nn} )\cdot \mathbf {a} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd201a471a83118c59579b0d4ad3adafa0cc6480)
The dyadic

![Image 49: {\displaystyle \mathbf {J} =\mathbf {ji} -\mathbf {ij} ={\begin{pmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb65617aeb3bbd237e25f25293c6683f7713935e)
is a 90° anticlockwise [rotation operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_operator_(vector_space) "Rotation operator (vector space)") in 2d. It can be left-dotted with a vector **r** = _x_**i** + _y_**j** to produce the vector,

![Image 50: {\displaystyle (\mathbf {ji} -\mathbf {ij} )\cdot (x\mathbf {i} +y\mathbf {j} )=x\mathbf {ji} \cdot \mathbf {i} -x\mathbf {ij} \cdot \mathbf {i} +y\mathbf {ji} \cdot \mathbf {j} -y\mathbf {ij} \cdot \mathbf {j} =-y\mathbf {i} +x\mathbf {j} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/67853dcf7b82ce3808e8e2efc2c4eee3b12dcbf5)
in summary

![Image 51: {\displaystyle \mathbf {J} \cdot \mathbf {r} =\mathbf {r} _{\mathrm {rot} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2e326b924282daa91e9b9df37824ebb4b1c04323)
or in matrix notation

![Image 52: {\displaystyle {\begin{pmatrix}0&-1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}}{\begin{pmatrix}x\\y\end{pmatrix}}={\begin{pmatrix}-y\\x\end{pmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/416b05b5b69b6d0e053f261f28b170ab0e467afe)
For any angle _θ_, the 2d rotation dyadic for a rotation anti-clockwise in the plane is

![Image 53: {\displaystyle \mathbf {R} =\mathbf {I} \cos \theta +\mathbf {J} \sin \theta =(\mathbf {ii} +\mathbf {jj} )\cos \theta +(\mathbf {ji} -\mathbf {ij} )\sin \theta ={\begin{pmatrix}\cos \theta &-\sin \theta \\\sin \theta &\;\cos \theta \end{pmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f2c3966a6751de3f85daff42aeb75d04939432e3)
