C/2014 Q2 (Lovejoy) Comet Lovejoy as imaged 19 January 2015 [ 1 ] Discovery [ 2 ] Discovered by Terry Lovejoy 0.2-m Schmidt Discovery site Birkdale Observatory ( Q80 ) Discovery date 17 August 2014 Designations Alternative designations CK14Q020 [ 2 ] Orbital characteristics [ 5 ] [ 4 ] Epoch 30 March 2015 ( JD 2457111.5) Aphelion ~1,160 AU Perihelion 1.291 AU Semi-major axis ~580 AU Eccentricity 0.99811 Orbital period ~11,000 years (inbound) [ 3 ] ~8,000 years (outbound) [ 3 ] Inclination 80.301° Longitude of ascending node 94.975° Argument of periapsis 12.395° Mean anomaly 0.004° Last perihelion 30 January 2015 [ 4 ] T Jupiter 0.246 Earth MOID 0.320 AU Jupiter MOID 3.811 AU Physical characteristics [ 6 ] [ 7 ] Dimensions 9.8–14.2 km (6.1–8.8 mi) Mean diameter 12 km (7.5 mi) Synodic rotation period 17.89 ± 0.17 hours Comet total magnitude (M1) 9.1 Apparent magnitude 4.0 (2015 apparition) C/2014 Q2 (Lovejoy) is a non-periodic comet discovered on 17 August 2014 by Terry Lovejoy using a 0.2-meter (8 in) Schmidt–Cassegrain telescope .

[ 2 ] It was discovered as a 15th-magnitude object in the southern constellation of Puppis .

[ 2 ] It is the fifth comet discovered by Terry Lovejoy.

History [ edit ] By December 2014, the comet had brightened to roughly magnitude 7.4, [ 8 ] making it a small telescope and binoculars target. By mid-December, the comet was visible to the naked eye for experienced observers with dark skies and keen eyesight.

[ 9 ] On 28–29 December 2014, the comet passed 1/3° from globular cluster Messier 79 .

[ 10 ] In January 2015, it brightened to roughly magnitude 4, [ 11 ] and became one of the brightest comets located high in a dark sky since comet C/1995 O1 ( Hale–Bopp ) in 1997. On 7 January 2015, the comet passed 0.469 AU (70.2 million km; 43.6 million mi) from Earth.

[ 5 ] It crossed the celestial equator on 9 January 2015, becoming better seen from the Northern Hemisphere .

[ 12 ] The comet came to perihelion (closest approach to the Sun) on 30 January 2015, at a distance of 1.29 AU (193 million km; 120 million mi) from the Sun.

[ 4 ] At perihelion, its water production rate exceeded 20 metric tons per second.

[ 13 ] C/2014 Q2 originated from the Oort cloud , [ 13 ] but is not a dynamically new comet.

[ 14 ] Before entering the planetary region ( epoch 1950), C/2014 Q2 had an orbital period of about 11,000 years, with an aphelion about 995 AU (148.8 billion km; 92.5 billion mi) from the Sun.

[ 3 ] After leaving the planetary region (epoch 2050), it will have an orbital period of about 8,000 years, with aphelion of about 800 AU.

[ 3 ] The comet was observed to release 21 different organic molecules in gas, including ethanol and glycolaldehyde , a simple sugar.

[ 13 ] [ 15 ] The presence of organic molecules suggests that they are preserved materials synthesized in the outskirts of the solar nebula or at earlier stages of the Solar System formation .

[ 13 ] Its blue-green glow is the result of organic molecules (mostly Diatomic carbon ) and water released by the comet fluorescing under the intense UV and optical light of the Sun as it passes through space.
