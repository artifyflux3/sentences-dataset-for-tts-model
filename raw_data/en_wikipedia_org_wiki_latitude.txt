Earth's [_graticule_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graticule_(cartography) "Graticule (cartography)"). The vertical lines from pole to pole are lines of constant [longitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longitude "Longitude"), or _meridians_. The circles parallel to the [equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator "Equator") are lines of constant latitude, or _parallels_. The graticule shows the latitude and longitude of points on the surface. In this example meridians are spaced at 6° intervals and parallels at 4° intervals.

In [geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography "Geography"), **latitude** is a [geographic coordinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system "Geographic coordinate system") that specifies the [north](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North "North")-[south](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South "South") position of a point on the surface of [the Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Earth "The Earth") or another celestial body. Latitude is given as an angle that ranges from −90° at the south pole to 90° at the north pole, with 0° at the [Equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator "Equator"). [Lines of constant latitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_(latitude) "Parallel (latitude)"), or _parallels_, run east-west as circles parallel to the equator. Latitude and [longitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longitude "Longitude") are used together as a coordinate pair to specify a location on the surface of the Earth.

On its own, the term "latitude" normally refers to the _geodetic latitude_ as defined below. Briefly, the geodetic latitude of a point is the angle formed between the vector perpendicular (or _[normal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal\_(geometry) "Normal (geometry)")_) to the ellipsoidal surface from the point, and the [plane of the equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equatorial_plane "Equatorial plane").

Two levels of abstraction are employed in the definitions of latitude and longitude. In the first step the physical surface is modeled by the [geoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geoid "Geoid"), a surface which approximates the [mean sea level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sea_level "Sea level") over the oceans and its continuation under the land masses. The second step is to approximate the geoid by a mathematically simpler reference surface. The simplest choice for the reference surface is a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere"), but the geoid is more accurately modeled by an [ellipsoid of revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid_of_revolution "Ellipsoid of revolution"). The definitions of latitude and longitude on such reference surfaces are detailed in the following sections. Lines of constant latitude and longitude together constitute a [graticule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system "Geographic coordinate system") on the reference surface. The latitude of a point on the _actual_ surface is that of the corresponding point on the reference surface, the correspondence being along the [normal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_(geometry) "Normal (geometry)") to the reference surface, which passes through the point on the physical surface. Latitude and longitude together with some specification of [height](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Altitude "Altitude") constitute a [geographic coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system "Geographic coordinate system") as defined in the specification of the ISO 19111 standard.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-1)

Since there are many different [reference ellipsoids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reference_ellipsoid "Reference ellipsoid"), the precise latitude of a feature on the surface is not unique: this is stressed in the ISO standard which states that "without the full specification of the coordinate reference system, coordinates (that is latitude and longitude) are ambiguous at best and meaningless at worst". This is of great importance in accurate applications, such as a [Global Positioning System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Global_Positioning_System "Global Positioning System") (GPS), but in common usage, where high accuracy is not required, the reference ellipsoid is not usually stated.

In English texts, the latitude angle, defined below, is usually denoted by the Greek lower-case letter [phi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phi_(letter) "Phi (letter)") (ϕ or φ). It is measured in [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_(angle) "Degree (angle)"), [minutes and seconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arcminute "Arcminute"), or [decimal degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_degrees "Decimal degrees"), north or south of the equator. For navigational purposes positions are given in degrees and decimal minutes. For instance, [The Needles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Needles "The Needles") lighthouse is at 50°39.734′ N 001°35.500′ W.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-2)

This article relates to coordinate systems for the Earth: it may be adapted to cover the Moon, planets and other celestial objects ([planetographic latitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetographic_latitude "Planetographic latitude")).

For a brief history, see [History of latitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_latitude "History of latitude").

In [celestial navigation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_navigation "Celestial navigation"), latitude is determined with the [meridian altitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meridian_altitude "Meridian altitude") method. More precise measurement of latitude requires an understanding of the gravitational field of the Earth, either to set up [theodolites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodolite "Theodolite") or to determine GPS satellite orbits. The study of the [figure of the Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth "Figure of the Earth") together with its gravitational field is the science of [geodesy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesy "Geodesy").

### The graticule on the sphere

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Latitude&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: The graticule on the sphere")]

The graticule is formed by the lines of constant latitude and constant longitude, which are constructed with reference to the rotation axis of the Earth. The primary reference points are the [poles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographical_pole "Geographical pole") where the axis of rotation of the Earth intersects the reference surface. Planes which contain the rotation axis intersect the surface at the [meridians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meridian_(geography) "Meridian (geography)"); and the angle between any one meridian plane and that through Greenwich (the [Prime Meridian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_Meridian "Prime Meridian")) defines the longitude: meridians are lines of constant longitude. The plane through the centre of the Earth and perpendicular to the rotation axis intersects the surface at a great circle called the [Equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator "Equator"). Planes parallel to the equatorial plane intersect the surface in circles of constant latitude; these are the parallels. The Equator has a latitude of 0°, the [North Pole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Pole "North Pole") has a latitude of 90° North (written 90°N or +90°), and the [South Pole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Pole "South Pole") has a latitude of 90° South (written 90°S or −90°). The latitude of an arbitrary point is the angle between the equatorial plane and the normal to the surface at that point: the normal to the surface of the sphere is along the radial vector.

The latitude, as defined in this way for the sphere, is often termed the spherical latitude, to avoid ambiguity with the geodetic latitude and the auxiliary latitudes defined in subsequent sections of this article.

### Named latitudes on the Earth

The orientation of the Earth at the December solstice

Besides the equator, four other parallels are of significance:

The plane of the Earth's orbit about the Sun is called the [ecliptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecliptic "Ecliptic"), and the plane perpendicular to the rotation axis of the Earth is the equatorial plane. The angle between the ecliptic and the equatorial plane is called variously the axial tilt, the obliquity, or the inclination of the ecliptic, and it is conventionally denoted by i. The latitude of the tropical circles is equal to i and the latitude of the polar circles is its complement (90° - _i_). The axis of rotation varies slowly over time and the values given here are those for the current [epoch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epoch_(astronomy) "Epoch (astronomy)"). The time variation is discussed more fully in the article on [axial tilt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axial_tilt "Axial tilt").[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-3)

The figure shows the geometry of a [cross-section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_section_(geometry) "Cross section (geometry)") of the plane perpendicular to the ecliptic and through the centres of the Earth and the Sun at the December [solstice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solstice "Solstice") when the Sun is overhead at some point of the [Tropic of Capricorn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropic_of_Capricorn "Tropic of Capricorn"). The south polar latitudes below the [Antarctic Circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antarctic_Circle "Antarctic Circle") are in daylight, whilst the north polar latitudes above the Arctic Circle are in night. The situation is reversed at the June solstice, when the Sun is overhead at the Tropic of Cancer. Only at latitudes in between the two [tropics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropics "Tropics") is it possible for the Sun to be directly overhead (at the [zenith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zenith "Zenith")).

On [map projections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Map_projections "Map projections") there is no universal rule as to how meridians and parallels should appear. On the former the parallels are horizontal and the meridians are vertical, whereas on the latter there is no exact relationship of parallels and meridians with horizontal and vertical: both are complicated curves.

Latitude on the ellipsoid
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Latitude&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Latitude on the ellipsoid")]

In 1687 [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton") published the _[Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophi%C3%A6\_Naturalis\_Principia\_Mathematica "Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica")_, in which he proved that a rotating self-gravitating fluid body in equilibrium takes the form of an [oblate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblate_spheroid "Oblate spheroid") ellipsoid.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-newton-4) (This article uses the term _ellipsoid_ in preference to the older term _spheroid_.) Newton's result was confirmed by geodetic measurements in the 18th century. (See [Meridian arc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meridian_arc "Meridian arc").) An oblate ellipsoid is the three-dimensional surface generated by the rotation of an ellipse about its shorter axis (minor axis). "Oblate ellipsoid of revolution" is abbreviated to 'ellipsoid' in the remainder of this article. (Ellipsoids which do not have an axis of symmetry are termed [triaxial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triaxial_ellipsoid "Triaxial ellipsoid").)

Many different [reference ellipsoids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth "Figure of the Earth") have been used in the history of [geodesy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesy "Geodesy"). In pre-satellite days they were devised to give a good fit to the [geoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geoid "Geoid") over the limited area of a survey but, with the advent of [GPS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GPS "GPS"), it has become natural to use reference ellipsoids (such as [WGS84](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WGS84 "WGS84")) with centre at the centre of mass of the Earth and minor axis aligned to the rotation axis of the Earth. These geocentric ellipsoids are usually within 100 m (330 ft) of the geoid. Since latitude is defined with respect to an ellipsoid, the position of a given point is different on each ellipsoid: one cannot exactly specify the latitude and longitude of a geographical feature without specifying the ellipsoid used. Many maps maintained by national agencies are based on older ellipsoids, so one must know how the latitude and longitude values are transformed from one ellipsoid to another. GPS handsets include software to carry out [datum transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Datum_(geodesy) "Datum (geodesy)") which link WGS84 to the local reference ellipsoid with its associated grid.

### The geometry of the ellipsoid

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Latitude&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: The geometry of the ellipsoid")]

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Ellipsoid_parametric_euler_mono.svg/250px-Ellipsoid_parametric_euler_mono.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ellipsoid_parametric_euler_mono.svg)

A sphere of radius _a_ compressed along the _z_ axis to form an oblate ellipsoid of revolution.

The shape of an ellipsoid of revolution is determined by the shape of the [ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse") which is rotated about its minor (shorter) axis. Two parameters are required. One is invariably the equatorial radius, which is the [semi-major axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse"), a. The other parameter is usually (1)the polar radius or [semi-minor axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse"), b; or (2)the (first) [flattening](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flattening "Flattening"), f; or (3)the [eccentricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse"), e. These parameters are not independent: they are related by

![Image 9: {\displaystyle f={\frac {a-b}{a}},\qquad e^{2}=2f-f^{2},\qquad b=a(1-f)=a{\sqrt {1-e^{2}}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d176b5a394f9d591c6242a6e8754f0c3a6e76e90)
Many other parameters (see [ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse"), [ellipsoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid "Ellipsoid")) appear in the study of geodesy, geophysics and map projections but they can all be expressed in terms of one or two members of the set a, b, f and e. Both f and e are small and often appear in series expansions in calculations; they are of the order ⁠1/298⁠ and 0.0818 respectively. Values for a number of ellipsoids are given in [Figure of the Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth "Figure of the Earth"). Reference ellipsoids are usually defined by the semi-major axis and the _inverse_ flattening, ⁠1/_f_⁠. For example, the defining values for the [WGS84](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WGS84 "WGS84") ellipsoid, used by all GPS devices, are[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-5)

*   a (equatorial radius): 6 378 137.0 m exactly
*   ⁠1/_f_⁠ (inverse flattening): 298.257 223 563 exactly

from which are derived

*   b (polar radius): 6 356 752.314 25 m
*   _e_ 2 (eccentricity squared): 0.006 694 379 990 14

The difference between the semi-major and semi-minor axes is about 21 km (13 miles) and as fraction of the semi-major axis it equals the flattening; on a computer monitor the ellipsoid could be sized as 300 by 299 pixels. This would barely be distinguishable from a 300-by-300-pixel sphere, so illustrations usually exaggerate the flattening.

### Geodetic and geocentric latitudes

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Latitude&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Geodetic and geocentric latitudes")]

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8c/Latitude_and_longitude_graticule_on_an_ellipsoid.svg/250px-Latitude_and_longitude_graticule_on_an_ellipsoid.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Latitude_and_longitude_graticule_on_an_ellipsoid.svg)

The definition of geodetic latitude (![Image 11: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4)) and longitude (![Image 12: {\displaystyle \lambda }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b43d0ea3c9c025af1be9128e62a18fa74bedda2a)) on an ellipsoid. The normal to the surface does not pass through the centre, except at the equator and at the poles.

The graticule on the ellipsoid is constructed in exactly the same way as on the sphere. The normal at a point on the surface of an ellipsoid does not pass through the centre, except for points on the equator or at the poles, but the definition of latitude remains unchanged as the angle between the normal and the equatorial plane. The terminology for latitude must be made more precise by distinguishing:

*   _[Geodetic latitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodetic\_latitude "Geodetic latitude")_: the angle between the normal and the equatorial plane. The standard notation in English publications is ϕ. This is the definition assumed when the word latitude is used without qualification. The definition must be accompanied with a specification of the ellipsoid.
*   _[Geocentric latitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geocentric\_latitude "Geocentric latitude")_ (also known as _spherical latitude_, after the [3D polar angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3D_polar_angle "3D polar angle")): the angle between the radius (from centre to the point on the surface) and the equatorial plane. (Figure [below](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#Geocentric_latitude)). There is no standard notation: examples from various texts include θ, ψ, q, ϕ′, _ϕ_ c, _ϕ_ g. This article uses θ.

**Geographic latitude** must be used with care, as some authors use it as a synonym for geodetic latitude whilst others use it as an alternative to the [astronomical latitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#Astronomical_latitude). "Latitude" (unqualified) should normally refer to the geodetic latitude.

The importance of specifying the reference datum may be illustrated by a simple example. On the reference ellipsoid for WGS84, the centre of the [Eiffel Tower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eiffel_Tower "Eiffel Tower") has a geodetic latitude of 48°51′29″N, or 48.8583°N and longitude of 2°17′40″E or 2.2944°E. The same coordinates on the datum [ED50](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ED50 "ED50") define a point on the ground which is 140 metres (460 feet) distant from the tower.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] A web search may produce several different values for the latitude of the tower; the reference ellipsoid is rarely specified.

The length of a degree of latitude depends on the [figure of the Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Figure_of_the_Earth "Figure of the Earth") assumed.

### Meridian distance on the sphere

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Latitude&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Meridian distance on the sphere")]

On the sphere the normal passes through the centre and the latitude (ϕ) is therefore equal to the angle subtended at the centre by the meridian arc from the equator to the point concerned. If the [meridian distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meridian_arc "Meridian arc") is denoted by _m_(_ϕ_) then ![Image 13: {\displaystyle m(\phi )={\frac {\pi }{180^{\circ }}}R\phi _{\mathrm {degrees} }=R\phi _{\mathrm {radians} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/508adf4208d93637637efa0bca3d57f2a6613bc0) where R denotes the [mean radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_radius#Mean_radii "Earth radius") of the Earth. R is equal to 6,371 km or 3,959 miles. No higher accuracy is appropriate for R since higher-precision results necessitate an ellipsoid model. With this value for R the meridian length of 1 degree of latitude on the sphere is 111.2 km (69.1 statute miles) (60.0 nautical miles). The length of one minute of latitude is 1.853 km (1.151 statute miles) (1.00 nautical miles), while the length of 1 second of latitude is 30.8 m or 101 feet (see [nautical mile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nautical_mile "Nautical mile")).

Auxiliary latitudes
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Latitude&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Auxiliary latitudes")]

There are six **auxiliary latitudes** that have applications to special problems in geodesy, geophysics and the theory of map projections:

*   [Geocentric latitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geocentric_latitude "Geocentric latitude")
*   Parametric (or reduced) latitude
*   Rectifying latitude
*   [Authalic latitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authalic_latitude "Authalic latitude")
*   Conformal latitude
*   Isometric latitude

The definitions given in this section all relate to locations on the reference ellipsoid but the first two auxiliary latitudes, like the geodetic latitude, can be extended to define a three-dimensional [geographic coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system "Geographic coordinate system") as discussed [below](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#Latitude_and_coordinate_systems). The remaining latitudes are not used in this way; they are used _only_ as intermediate constructs in map projections of the reference ellipsoid to the plane or in calculations of geodesics on the ellipsoid. Their numerical values are not of interest. For example, no one would need to calculate the authalic latitude of the Eiffel Tower.

The expressions below give the auxiliary latitudes in terms of the geodetic latitude, the semi-major axis, a, and the eccentricity, e. (For inverses see [below](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#Inverse_formulae_and_series).) The forms given are, apart from notational variants, those in the standard reference for map projections, namely "Map projections: a working manual" by J. P. Snyder.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-snyder-10) Derivations of these expressions may be found in Adams[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-adams1921-11) and online publications by Osborne[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-osborne-7) and Rapp.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-rapp-8)

### Geocentric latitude

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Latitude&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Geocentric latitude")]

See also: [§Geodetic and geocentric latitudes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#Geodetic_and_geocentric_latitudes)

[![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Geocentric_coords_03.svg/290px-Geocentric_coords_03.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Geocentric_coords_03.svg)

The definition of geodetic latitude (ϕ) and geocentric latitude (θ)

The **geocentric latitude** is the angle between the equatorial plane and the radius from the centre to a point of interest.

When the point is on the surface of the ellipsoid, the relation between the geocentric latitude (θ) and the geodetic latitude (ϕ) is:

![Image 24: {\displaystyle \theta (\phi )=\tan ^{-1}\left(\left(1-e^{2}\right)\tan \phi \right)=\tan ^{-1}\left((1-f)^{2}\tan \phi \right)\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be12bdcfb52b31afe5eb175478a51743a38ce0f5)
For points not on the surface of the ellipsoid, the relationship involves additionally the [ellipsoidal height](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoidal_height "Ellipsoidal height")_h_:

![Image 25: {\displaystyle \theta (\phi ,h)=\tan ^{-1}\left({\frac {N(1-f)^{2}+h}{N+h}}\tan \phi \right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b2c91758246aec3c0613c68412150d7d776ded5d)
where N is the prime vertical radius of curvature. The geodetic and geocentric latitudes are equal at the equator and at the poles but at other latitudes they differ by a few minutes of arc. Taking the value of the squared eccentricity as 0.0067 (it depends on the choice of ellipsoid) the maximum difference of ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \phi {-}\theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fad050a806bb61857c84d009cfc72a3fec9ec8b4) may be shown to be about 11.5 minutes of arc at a geodetic latitude of approximately 45°6′.[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-12)

### Parametric latitude (or reduced latitude)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Latitude&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Parametric latitude (or reduced latitude)")]

[![Image 27](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e9/Ellipsoid_reduced_angle_definition.svg/250px-Ellipsoid_reduced_angle_definition.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ellipsoid_reduced_angle_definition.svg)

Definition of the parametric latitude (β) on the ellipsoid

The **parametric latitude** or **reduced latitude**, β, is defined by the radius drawn from the centre of the ellipsoid to that point Q on the surrounding sphere (of radius a) which is the projection parallel to the Earth's axis of a point P on the ellipsoid at latitude ϕ. It was introduced by Legendre[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-legendre-13) and Bessel[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-bessel-14) who solved problems for geodesics on the ellipsoid by transforming them to an equivalent problem for spherical geodesics by using this smaller latitude. Bessel's notation, _u_(_ϕ_), is also used in the current literature. The parametric latitude is related to the geodetic latitude by:[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-osborne-7)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-rapp-8)

![Image 28: {\displaystyle \beta (\phi )=\tan ^{-1}\left({\sqrt {1-e^{2}}}\tan \phi \right)=\tan ^{-1}\left((1-f)\tan \phi \right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73a42a66b39844ce024d63ac0ffeb3c4ac3b08d4)
The alternative name arises from the parameterization of the equation of the ellipse describing a meridian section. In terms of Cartesian coordinates p, the distance from the minor axis, and z, the distance above the equatorial plane, the equation of the [ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse") is:

![Image 29: {\displaystyle {\frac {p^{2}}{a^{2}}}+{\frac {z^{2}}{b^{2}}}=1\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9b55d30cf33f6eccc1558881edcfd2dfe423c5dd)
The Cartesian coordinates of the point are parameterized by

![Image 30: {\displaystyle p=a\cos \beta \,,\qquad z=b\sin \beta \,;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab1ea1af54648e83df7ac4fba6091bbc9bc65b34)
Cayley suggested the term _parametric latitude_ because of the form of these equations.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-cayley-15)

The parametric latitude is not used in the theory of map projections. Its most important application is in the theory of ellipsoid geodesics, ([Vincenty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vincenty%27s_formulae "Vincenty's formulae"), Karney[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-Karney-16)).

### Rectifying latitude

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Latitude&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Rectifying latitude")]

The **rectifying latitude**, μ, is the meridian distance scaled so that its value at the poles is equal to 90 degrees or ⁠π/2⁠ radians:

![Image 31: {\displaystyle \mu (\phi )={\frac {\pi }{2}}{\frac {m(\phi )}{m_{\mathrm {p} }}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/349ea19e122303e5b69ddfd8be93244b25788c3e)
where the meridian distance from the equator to a latitude ϕ is (see [Meridian arc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meridian_arc "Meridian arc"))

![Image 32: {\displaystyle m(\phi )=a\left(1-e^{2}\right)\int _{0}^{\phi }\left(1-e^{2}\sin ^{2}\phi '\right)^{-{\frac {3}{2}}}\,d\phi '\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d68ac2a2a87186fe170c3413ad1d6b214ad387c)
and the length of the meridian quadrant from the equator to the pole (the [polar distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meridian_arc#Polar_distance "Meridian arc")) is

![Image 33: {\displaystyle m_{\mathrm {p} }=m\left({\frac {\pi }{2}}\right)\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/feaed75d16edd488d2ae9b3fcf3d164198d1d4fc)
Using the rectifying latitude to define a latitude on a sphere of radius

![Image 34: {\displaystyle R={\frac {2m_{\mathrm {p} }}{\pi }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f3c5625835b98da55e6ea02beb168219a8abef06)
defines a projection from the ellipsoid to the sphere such that all meridians have true length and uniform scale. The sphere may then be projected to the plane with an [equirectangular projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equirectangular_projection "Equirectangular projection") to give a double projection from the ellipsoid to the plane such that all meridians have true length and uniform meridian scale. An example of the use of the rectifying latitude is the [equidistant conic projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equidistant_conic_projection "Equidistant conic projection"). (Snyder, Section 16).[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-snyder-10) The rectifying latitude is also of great importance in the construction of the [Transverse Mercator projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transverse_Mercator_projection "Transverse Mercator projection").

The **authalic latitude** (after the Greek for "[same area](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/authalic "wiktionary:authalic")"), ξ, gives an [equal-area projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equal-area_projection "Equal-area projection") to a sphere.

![Image 35: {\displaystyle \xi (\phi )=\sin ^{-1}\left({\frac {q(\phi )}{q_{\mathrm {p} }}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc893a2c538edce543f6f2c8e4f8eed929ea83d5)
where

![Image 36: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}q(\phi )&={\frac {\left(1-e^{2}\right)\sin \phi }{1-e^{2}\sin ^{2}\phi }}-{\frac {1-e^{2}}{2e}}\ln \left({\frac {1-e\sin \phi }{1+e\sin \phi }}\right)\\[2pt]&={\frac {\left(1-e^{2}\right)\sin \phi }{1-e^{2}\sin ^{2}\phi }}+{\frac {1-e^{2}}{e}}\tanh ^{-1}(e\sin \phi )\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b256b2b2f95ca49231dfb329dd724cdbd2de7d1)
and

![Image 37: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}q_{\mathrm {p} }=q\left({\frac {\pi }{2}}\right)&=1-{\frac {1-e^{2}}{2e}}\ln \left({\frac {1-e}{1+e}}\right)\\&=1+{\frac {1-e^{2}}{e}}\tanh ^{-1}e\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7447963fa3c0dd43284a844f87ac2ae8732810b2)
and the radius of the sphere is taken as

![Image 38: {\displaystyle R_{q}=a{\sqrt {\frac {q_{\mathrm {p} }}{2}}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/410e3824d4063dc2ceef3089f832b3f559284a28)
An example of the use of the authalic latitude is the [Albers equal-area conic projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albers_projection "Albers projection").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-snyder-10): §14

The **conformal latitude**, χ, gives an angle-preserving ([conformal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformal_map "Conformal map")) transformation to the sphere. [[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-17)

![Image 39: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\chi (\phi )&=2\tan ^{-1}\left[\left({\frac {1+\sin \phi }{1-\sin \phi }}\right)\left({\frac {1-e\sin \phi }{1+e\sin \phi }}\right)^{e}\right]^{\frac {1}{2}}-{\frac {\pi }{2}}\\[2pt]&=2\tan ^{-1}\left[\tan \left({\frac {\phi }{2}}+{\frac {\pi }{4}}\right)\left({\frac {1-e\sin \phi }{1+e\sin \phi }}\right)^{\frac {e}{2}}\right]-{\frac {\pi }{2}}\\[2pt]&=\tan ^{-1}\left[\sinh \left(\sinh ^{-1}(\tan \phi )-e\tanh ^{-1}(e\sin \phi )\right)\right]\\&=\operatorname {gd} \left[\operatorname {gd} ^{-1}(\phi )-e\tanh ^{-1}(e\sin \phi )\right]\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eea5beec16e6a78772ab9d8ffd9123b9ae6e89e0)
where gd(_x_) is the [Gudermannian function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gudermannian_function "Gudermannian function"). (See also [Mercator projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercator_projection#Alternative_expressions "Mercator projection").)

The conformal latitude defines a transformation from the ellipsoid to a sphere of _arbitrary_ radius such that the angle of intersection between any two lines on the ellipsoid is the same as the corresponding angle on the sphere (so that the shape of _small_ elements is well preserved). A further conformal transformation from the sphere to the plane gives a conformal double projection from the ellipsoid to the plane. This is not the only way of generating such a conformal projection. For example, the 'exact' version of the [Transverse Mercator projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transverse_Mercator_projection "Transverse Mercator projection") on the ellipsoid is not a double projection. (It does, however, involve a generalisation of the conformal latitude to the complex plane).

The **isometric latitude**, ψ, is used in the development of the ellipsoidal versions of the normal [Mercator projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercator_projection "Mercator projection") and the [Transverse Mercator projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transverse_Mercator_projection "Transverse Mercator projection"). The name "isometric" arises from the fact that at any point on the ellipsoid equal increments of ψ and longitude λ give rise to equal distance displacements along the meridians and parallels respectively. The [graticule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geographic_coordinate_system "Geographic coordinate system") defined by the lines of constant ψ and constant λ, divides the surface of the ellipsoid into a mesh of squares (of varying size). The isometric latitude is zero at the equator but rapidly diverges from the geodetic latitude, tending to infinity at the poles. The conventional notation is given in Snyder (page 15):[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-snyder-10)

![Image 40: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\psi (\phi )&=\ln \left[\tan \left({\frac {\pi }{4}}+{\frac {\phi }{2}}\right)\right]+{\frac {e}{2}}\ln \left[{\frac {1-e\sin \phi }{1+e\sin \phi }}\right]\\&=\sinh ^{-1}(\tan \phi )-e\tanh ^{-1}(e\sin \phi )\\&=\operatorname {gd} ^{-1}(\phi )-e\tanh ^{-1}(e\sin \phi ).\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e2999bd8461a831ee601ecad69062c5b30adec5f)
For the _normal_ Mercator projection (on the ellipsoid) this function defines the spacing of the parallels: if the length of the equator on the projection is E (units of length or pixels) then the distance, y, of a parallel of latitude ϕ from the equator is

![Image 41: {\displaystyle y(\phi )={\frac {E}{2\pi }}\psi (\phi )\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4c3c8fdb803b473efe5685cc97cdcfe6622aa472)
The isometric latitude ψ is closely related to the conformal latitude χ:

![Image 42: {\displaystyle \psi (\phi )=\operatorname {gd} ^{-1}\chi (\phi )\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7eaf60be567d5882f6b8a5b07f11fc3aafa2c157)

### Inverse formulae and series

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Latitude&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Inverse formulae and series")]

The formulae in the previous sections give the auxiliary latitude in terms of the geodetic latitude. The expressions for the geocentric and parametric latitudes may be inverted directly but this is impossible in the four remaining cases: the rectifying, authalic, conformal, and isometric latitudes. There are two methods of proceeding.

*   The first is a numerical inversion of the defining equation for each and every particular value of the auxiliary latitude. The methods available are [fixed-point iteration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixed-point_iteration "Fixed-point iteration") and [Newton–Raphson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_method "Newton's method") root finding. 
    *   When converting from isometric or conformal to geodetic, two iterations of Newton-Raphson gives [double precision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_precision "Double precision") accuracy.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-18)

*   The other, more useful, approach is to express the auxiliary latitude as a series in terms of the geodetic latitude and then invert the series by the method of [Lagrange reversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_reversion "Lagrange reversion"). Such series are presented by Adams who uses Taylor series expansions and gives coefficients in terms of the eccentricity.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-adams1921-11) Orihuela[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-19) gives series for the conversions between all pairs of auxiliary latitudes in terms of the third flattening, _n_ = (_a_ - _b_)/(_a_ + _b_). Karney[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-20) establishes that the truncation errors for such series are consistently smaller that the equivalent series in terms of the eccentricity. The series method is not applicable to the isometric latitude and one must find the conformal latitude in an intermediate step.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-osborne-7)

Latitude and coordinate systems
-------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Latitude&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Latitude and coordinate systems")]

The geodetic latitude, or any of the auxiliary latitudes defined on the reference ellipsoid, constitutes with longitude a two-dimensional [coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") on that ellipsoid. To define the position of an arbitrary point it is necessary to extend such a coordinate system into three dimensions. Three latitudes are used in this way: the geodetic, geocentric and parametric latitudes are used in geodetic coordinates, spherical polar coordinates and ellipsoidal coordinates respectively.

### Geodetic coordinates

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Latitude&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Geodetic coordinates")]

[![Image 44](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Geodetic_coordinates.svg/250px-Geodetic_coordinates.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Geodetic_coordinates.svg)

 Geodetic coordinates P(_ɸ_,_λ_,_h_)

At an arbitrary point P consider the line PN which is normal to the reference ellipsoid. The geodetic coordinates P(_ɸ_,_λ_,_h_) are the latitude and longitude of the point N on the ellipsoid and the distance PN. This height differs from the height above the geoid or a reference height such as that above mean sea level at a specified location. The direction of PN will also differ from the direction of a vertical plumb line. The relation of these different heights requires knowledge of the shape of the geoid and also the gravity field of the Earth.

### Spherical polar coordinates

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Latitude&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Spherical polar coordinates")]

[![Image 45](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/88/Geocentric_coords_02.svg/250px-Geocentric_coords_02.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Geocentric_coords_02.svg)

 Geocentric coordinate related to spherical polar coordinates P(_r_,_θ_′,_λ_)

The geocentric latitude θ is the complement of the _polar angle_ or _[colatitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Colatitude "Colatitude")_ θ′ in conventional [spherical polar coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_polar_coordinates "Spherical polar coordinates") in which the coordinates of a point are P(_r_,_θ_′,_λ_) where r is the distance of P from the centre O, θ′ is the angle between the radius vector and the polar axis and λ is longitude. Since the normal at a general point on the ellipsoid does not pass through the centre it is clear that points P' on the normal, which all have the same geodetic latitude, will have differing geocentric latitudes. Spherical polar coordinate systems are used in the analysis of the gravity field.

### Ellipsoidal-harmonic coordinates

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Latitude&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: Ellipsoidal-harmonic coordinates")]

[![Image 46](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Ellipsoidal_coordinates.svg/250px-Ellipsoidal_coordinates.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ellipsoidal_coordinates.svg)

Ellipsoidal coordinates P(_u_,_β_,_λ_)

The parametric latitude can also be extended to a three-dimensional coordinate system. For a point P not on the reference ellipsoid (semi-axes OA and OB) construct an auxiliary ellipsoid which is confocal (same foci F, F′) with the reference ellipsoid: the necessary condition is that the product ae of semi-major axis and eccentricity is the same for both ellipsoids. Let u be the semi-minor axis (OD) of the auxiliary ellipsoid. Further let β be the parametric latitude of P on the auxiliary ellipsoid. The set (_u_,_β_,_λ_) define the **ellipsoidal-harmonic coordinates**[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-21) or simply _ellipsoidal coordinates_[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-torge-6): §4.2.2  (although that term is also used to refer to geodetic coordinate). These coordinates are the natural choice in models of the gravity field for a rotating ellipsoidal body. The above applies to a biaxial ellipsoid (a spheroid, as in [oblate spheroidal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblate_spheroidal_coordinates "Oblate spheroidal coordinates")); for a generalization, see [triaxial ellipsoidal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triaxial_ellipsoidal_coordinates "Triaxial ellipsoidal coordinates").

### Coordinate conversions

Astronomical latitude
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Latitude&action=edit&section=27 "Edit section: Astronomical latitude")]

**Astronomical latitude** (Φ) is the angle between the equatorial plane and the true [vertical direction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertical_direction "Vertical direction") at a point on the surface. The true vertical, the direction of a [plumb line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plumb_line "Plumb line"), is also the [gravity direction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity_direction "Gravity direction") (the resultant of the [gravitational acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_acceleration "Gravitational acceleration") (mass-based) and the [centrifugal acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centrifugal_acceleration "Centrifugal acceleration")) at that latitude.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-torge-6) Astronomic latitude is calculated from angles measured between the [zenith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zenith "Zenith") and stars whose [declination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Declination "Declination") is accurately known.

In general the true vertical at a point on the surface does not exactly coincide with either the normal to the reference ellipsoid or the normal to the geoid. The geoid is an idealized, theoretical shape "at mean sea level". Points on land do not lie precisely on the geoid, and the vertical at a point at a specific time is influenced by tidal forces which the theoretical geoid averages out. The angle between the astronomic and geodetic normals is called _[vertical deflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertical\_deflection "Vertical deflection")_ and is usually a few seconds of arc but it is important in geodesy.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-torge-6)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latitude#cite_note-wellenhofmoritz-22)

Astronomical latitude is not to be confused with [declination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Declination "Declination"), the coordinate [astronomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomer "Astronomer") use in a similar way to specify the angular position of stars north-south of the [celestial equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_equator "Celestial equator") (see [equatorial coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equatorial_coordinates "Equatorial coordinates")), nor with [ecliptic latitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecliptic_latitude "Ecliptic latitude"), the coordinate that astronomers use to specify the angular position of stars north-south of the [ecliptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecliptic "Ecliptic") (see [ecliptic coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecliptic_coordinates "Ecliptic coordinates")).
