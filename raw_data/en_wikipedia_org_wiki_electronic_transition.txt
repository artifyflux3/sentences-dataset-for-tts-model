From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7f/Bohr-atom-electron-to-jump.svg/250px-Bohr-atom-electron-to-jump.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bohr-atom-electron-to-jump.svg)

An electron in a [Bohr model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bohr_model "Bohr model") atom, moving from [quantum level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_number "Quantum number")_n_ = 3 to _n_ = 2 and releasing a [photon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon"). The energy of an electron is determined by its orbit around the atom, The n = 0 orbit, commonly referred to as the [ground state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ground_state "Ground state"), has the lowest energy of all states in the system. 

In [atomic physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_physics "Atomic physics") and [chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry"), an **atomic electron transition** (also called an atomic transition, quantum jump, or quantum leap) is an [electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") changing from one [energy level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_level "Energy level") to another within an [atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom")[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_transition#cite_note-1) or [artificial atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_atom "Artificial atom").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_transition#cite_note-2) The time scale of a quantum jump has not been measured experimentally. However, the [Franck–Condon principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franck%E2%80%93Condon_principle "Franck–Condon principle") binds the upper limit of this parameter to the order of [attoseconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attosecond "Attosecond").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_transition#cite_note-3)

Electrons can _relax_ into states of lower energy by emitting [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation") in the form of a photon. Electrons can also absorb passing photons, which _excites_ the electron into a state of higher energy. The larger the energy separation between the electron's initial and final state, the shorter the photons' [wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength "Wavelength").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_transition#cite_note-:0-4)

Danish physicist [Niels Bohr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niels_Bohr "Niels Bohr") first theorized that electrons can perform quantum jumps in 1913.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_transition#cite_note-5) Soon after, [James Franck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Franck "James Franck") and [Gustav Ludwig Hertz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustav_Ludwig_Hertz "Gustav Ludwig Hertz")[proved experimentally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franck%E2%80%93Hertz_experiment "Franck–Hertz experiment") that atoms have quantized energy states.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_transition#cite_note-6)

The observability of quantum jumps was predicted by [Hans Dehmelt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Dehmelt "Hans Dehmelt") in 1975, and they were first observed using [trapped ions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrupole_ion_trap "Quadrupole ion trap") of [barium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barium "Barium") at [University of Hamburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Hamburg "University of Hamburg") and [mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(element) "Mercury (element)") at [NIST](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NIST "NIST") in 1986.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_transition#cite_note-:0-4)

An atom interacts with the oscillating [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field"):

![Image 2: {\displaystyle E(t)=|{\textbf {E}}_{0}|Re(e^{-i{\omega }t}{\hat {\textbf {e}}}_{\mathrm {rad} })}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3aaca5c93377f4cfee70096e6599c86e6a1402b8)1

with amplitude ![Image 3: {\displaystyle |{\textbf {E}}_{0}|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5bf50f5fb033b414befad2cc6ee490fbf0d7225b), angular frequency ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8), and polarization vector ![Image 5: {\displaystyle {\hat {\textbf {e}}}_{\mathrm {rad} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02afb9cb2f02176b9aa58e26d53099944304136a).[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_transition#cite_note-7) Note that the actual phase is ![Image 6: {\displaystyle (\omega t-{\textbf {k}}\cdot {\textbf {r}})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5808c0f91f1f09a8e4a023acf566af0cf06409b0). However, in many cases, the variation of ![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\textbf {k}}\cdot {\textbf {r}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c352bc3bfd54b26e32922a0f3bb32694e5b8d9b3) is small over the atom (or equivalently, the radiation wavelength is much greater than the size of an atom) and this term can be ignored. This is called the dipole approximation. The atom can also interact with the oscillating [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field") produced by the radiation, although much more weakly.

The Hamiltonian for this interaction, analogous to the energy of a classical dipole in an electric field, is ![Image 8: {\displaystyle H_{I}=e{\textbf {r}}\cdot {\textbf {E}}(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/98203b3aac4d20795693d74db249a2981476afb4). The stimulated transition rate can be calculated using [time-dependent perturbation theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time-dependent_perturbation_theory "Time-dependent perturbation theory"); however, the result can be summarized using [Fermi's golden rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi%27s_golden_rule "Fermi's golden rule"): ![Image 9: {\displaystyle Rate\propto |eE_{0}|^{2}\times |\langle 2|{\textbf {r}}\cdot {\hat {\textbf {e}}}_{\mathrm {rad} }|1\rangle |^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a701c4e994528d14f4b9f093c8d243f2ae3cafb) The dipole matrix element can be decomposed into the product of the radial integral and the angular integral. The angular integral is zero unless the [selection rules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Selection_rules "Selection rules") for the atomic transition are satisfied.

In 2019, it was demonstrated in an experiment with a superconducting [artificial atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artificial_atom "Artificial atom") consisting of two strongly-hybridized [transmon qubits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmon "Transmon") placed inside a readout resonator cavity at 15 m[K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin"), that the evolution of some jumps is continuous, coherent, deterministic, and reversible.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_transition#cite_note-8) On the other hand, other quantum jumps are inherently unpredictable.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_transition#cite_note-9)
