**Prolate spheroidal coordinates** are a three-dimensional [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates")[coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") that results from rotating the two-dimensional [elliptic coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_coordinates "Elliptic coordinates") about the focal axis of the ellipse, i.e., the symmetry axis on which the foci are located. Rotation about the other axis produces [oblate spheroidal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oblate_spheroidal_coordinates "Oblate spheroidal coordinates"). Prolate spheroidal coordinates can also be considered as a [limiting case](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limiting_case_(mathematics) "Limiting case (mathematics)") of [ellipsoidal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoidal_coordinates "Ellipsoidal coordinates") in which the two smallest [principal axes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoid "Ellipsoid") are equal in length.

Prolate spheroidal coordinates can be used to solve various [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equation "Partial differential equation") in which the boundary conditions match its symmetry and shape, such as solving for a field produced by two centers, which are taken as the foci on the _z_-axis. One example is solving for the [wavefunction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavefunction "Wavefunction") of an [electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") moving in the [electromagnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_field "Electromagnetic field") of two positively charged [nuclei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus"), as in the [hydrogen molecular ion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_molecular_ion "Hydrogen molecular ion"), H 2+. Another example is solving for the [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field") generated by two small [electrode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrode "Electrode") tips. Other limiting cases include areas generated by a line segment (_Î¼_=0) or a line with a missing segment (Î½=0). The electronic structure of general diatomic molecules with many electrons can also be solved to excellent precision in the prolate spheroidal coordinate system.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prolate_spheroidal_coordinates#cite_note-diatomicreview-1)

In principle, a definition of prolate spheroidal coordinates could be degenerate. In other words, a single set of coordinates might correspond to two points in [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system"); this is illustrated here with two black spheres, one on each sheet of the hyperboloid and located at (_x_, _y_, Â±_z_). However, neither of the definitions presented here are degenerate.
