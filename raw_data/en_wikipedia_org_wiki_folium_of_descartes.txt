The folium of Descartes (green) with asymptote (blue), when ![Image 2: {\textstyle a=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c8689677e3330ed59e4c8750f989e0940d357d19)

In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), the **folium of Descartes** (from [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_language "Latin language")_folium_'[leaf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leaf "Leaf")'; named for [René Descartes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes "René Descartes")) is an [algebraic curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_curve "Algebraic curve") defined by the [implicit equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Implicit_equation "Implicit equation")

![Image 3: {\displaystyle x^{3}+y^{3}-3a\cdot xy=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27c5cfe3c8b7c394aad3aebeef25b10ab13d4f46)

[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Folium_of_Descartes#cite_note-1)![Image 4: {\displaystyle dy/dx=(x^{2}-ay)/(ax-y^{2}),\,dx/dy=(ax-y^{2})/(x^{2}-ay).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/97ccea776d6e7b1ed4d953475486c9b926b68565)

The curve was first proposed and studied by [René Descartes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes "René Descartes") in 1638.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Folium_of_Descartes#cite_note-:0-2) Its claim to fame lies in an incident in the development of [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"). Descartes challenged [Pierre de Fermat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_de_Fermat "Pierre de Fermat") to find the tangent line to the curve at an arbitrary point, since Fermat had recently discovered a method for finding tangent lines. Fermat solved the problem easily, something Descartes was unable to do.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Folium_of_Descartes#cite_note-3) Since the invention of calculus, the slope of the tangent line can be found easily using [implicit differentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Implicit_differentiation "Implicit differentiation").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Folium_of_Descartes#cite_note-:1-4) Mayor [Johan(nes) Hudde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Hudde "Johannes Hudde")'s second letter on maxima and minima (1658) mentions his calculation of the maximum width of the closed loop, part of _Mathematical Exercitions_, 5 books (1656/57 Leyden) p. 498, by [Frans van Schooten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frans_van_Schooten "Frans van Schooten") Jnr.

Another way of plotting the function can be derived from symmetry over ![Image 14: {\displaystyle y=x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0abe2e7da593fb7b41d44e87a97fefdd8998b77). The symmetry can be seen directly from its equation (x and y can be interchanged). By applying rotation of 45° clockwise for example, one can plot the function symmetric over rotated x axis.

This operation is equivalent to a substitution:![Image 15: {\displaystyle x={{u+v} \over {\sqrt {2}}},\,y={{u-v} \over {\sqrt {2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48d9fb8eb4f53a739813c9770b5f616a9d29371c)and yields![Image 16: {\displaystyle v=\pm u{\sqrt {\frac {3a{\sqrt {2}}-2u}{6u+3a{\sqrt {2}}}}}\,,\,u<3a/{\sqrt {2}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed572c15603793014fb4bbe4377ae8be8c02f71e)Plotting in the Cartesian system of ![Image 17: {\displaystyle (u,v)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eadf12294edccd7a29c99cfc1765e4a14bf47e58) gives the _folium_ rotated by 45° and therefore symmetric by ![Image 18: {\displaystyle u}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3e6bb763d22c20916ed4f0bb6bd49d7470cffd8)-axis.

It forms a loop in the first quadrant with a [double point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singular_point_of_a_curve "Singular point of a curve") at the origin and has [asymptote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asymptote "Asymptote")![Image 19: {\displaystyle x+y=-a\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/93b860c5b6ebe8fd498dc0ca38ce93c00343a477)It is symmetrical about the line ![Image 20: {\displaystyle y=x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0abe2e7da593fb7b41d44e87a97fefdd8998b77). As such, the curve and this line intersect at the origin and at the point ![Image 21: {\textstyle (3a/2,3a/2).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/58582cbf88c705f5fbe47cb2955996fdff762413)

Implicit differentiation gives the formula for the slope of the tangent line to this curve to be[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Folium_of_Descartes#cite_note-:1-4)

![Image 22: {\displaystyle {\frac {dy}{dx}}={\frac {ay-x^{2}}{y^{2}-ax}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/15e21206d9f29758195ed76eabd4699af6c2553b) with poles ![Image 23: {\textstyle x=y^{2}/a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f8d2d0c0185aa1fca133d8010938c611a9a881d9) and value 0 or ±∞ at origin (0,0).

Using either one of the polar representations above, the area of the interior of the loop is found to be ![Image 24: {\textstyle 1{\frac {1}{2}}a\cdot a.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0c131aebad456e6408f92996b4cf01f037aecd9) Moreover, the area between the "wings" of the curve and its slanted asymptote is also ![Image 25: {\textstyle 3a^{2}/2.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e67a2cab398c067c212d1992a24a0fc7cd188541)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Folium_of_Descartes#cite_note-:0-2)

[![Image 26](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4b/MaclaurinTrisectrix.SVG/250px-MaclaurinTrisectrix.SVG.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:MaclaurinTrisectrix.SVG)

Trisectrix of Maclaurin: top angle is ⁠2/3⁠ φ. Length ⁠1+1/2⁠_a_ on hor. axis, _O_ to turning point.

The folium of Descartes is related to the [trisectrix of Maclaurin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trisectrix_of_Maclaurin "Trisectrix of Maclaurin") by [affine transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_transformation "Affine transformation"). To see this, start with the equation![Image 27: {\displaystyle x^{3}+y^{3}=3a\cdot xy\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3c1e759b6513aa68dfe75eb8188c5b7e99011cfd)and change variables to find the equation in a coordinate system rotated 45 degrees. This amounts to setting

![Image 28: {\displaystyle x={{X+Y} \over {\sqrt {2}}},y={{X-Y} \over {\sqrt {2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2356ffd6cba174a343f8a9971276cc76131ed8f2)In the ![Image 29: {\displaystyle X,Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8705438171d938b7f59cd1bfa5b7d99b6afa5cd) plane the equation is![Image 30: {\displaystyle 2X(X^{2}+3Y^{2})=3{\sqrt {2}}a(X^{2}-Y^{2}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81d05a95d1f52d3af16b1dc33454c9fdf96546f5)

If we stretch the curve in the ![Image 31: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) direction by a factor of ![Image 32: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {3}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b19c09494138b5082459afac7f9a8d99c546fcd) this becomes![Image 33: {\displaystyle 2X(X^{2}+Y^{2})=a{\sqrt {2}}(3X^{2}-Y^{2}),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/315b866c212557e2e0265505713f68d287005742)which is the equation of the trisectrix of Maclaurin.
