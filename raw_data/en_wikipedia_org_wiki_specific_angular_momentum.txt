In [celestial mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_mechanics "Celestial mechanics"), the **specific relative angular momentum** (often denoted ![Image 1: {\displaystyle {\vec {h}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04767cd7e050d91159e8537029e967f15b08532f) or ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \mathbf {h} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1fce1c8de3a01ae39379db83781850619c4c0987)) of a body is the [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum") of that body divided by its mass.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_angular_momentum#cite_note-Vallado-1) In the case of two [orbiting bodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbiting_body "Orbiting body") it is the [vector product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_product "Vector product") of their relative position and relative [linear momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum"), divided by the mass of the body in question.

Specific relative angular momentum plays a pivotal role in the analysis of the [two-body problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-body_problem "Two-body problem"), as it remains constant for a given orbit under ideal conditions. "[Specific](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_(disambiguation)#Physical_natural_sciences,_including_physiology,_and_engineering "Specific (disambiguation)")" in this context indicates angular momentum per unit mass. The [SI unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_units "SI units") for specific relative angular momentum is square meter per second.

The specific relative angular momentum is defined as the [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product") of the relative [position vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_position_vector "Orbital position vector")![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1) and the relative [velocity vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_velocity_vector "Orbital velocity vector")![Image 4: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6). ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathbf {h} =\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {v} ={\frac {\mathbf {L} }{m}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ddf737d02f89792ad1618f4abf5282a2ebfa908f)

where ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \mathbf {L} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6f5f750865376a1a4ae2b15a00b4ff9c75a66630) is the angular momentum vector, defined as ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} \times m\mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf57e5fac6e6237541aa1430d7da17ce7a107a79).

The ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mathbf {h} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1fce1c8de3a01ae39379db83781850619c4c0987) vector is always perpendicular to the instantaneous [osculating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Osculating_orbit "Osculating orbit")[orbital plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_plane_(astronomy) "Orbital plane (astronomy)"), which coincides with the instantaneous [perturbed orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy) "Perturbation (astronomy)"). It is not necessarily perpendicular to the average orbital plane over time.

Proof of constancy in the two body case
---------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Specific_angular_momentum&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Proof of constancy in the two body case")]

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/af/FlightPathAngle.svg/250px-FlightPathAngle.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:FlightPathAngle.svg)

Distance vector ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1), velocity vector ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6), [true anomaly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/True_anomaly "True anomaly")![Image 12: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) and flight path angle ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \phi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/72b1f30316670aee6270a28334bdf4f5072cdde4) of ![Image 14: {\displaystyle m_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ecebe334d5cadc3ffcf245eb02919034d7a2ec8) in orbit around ![Image 15: {\displaystyle m_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31aafa60e48d39ccce922404c0b80340b2cc777a). The most important measures of the [ellipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipse "Ellipse") are also depicted (among which, note that the [true anomaly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/True_anomaly "True anomaly")![Image 16: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) is labeled as ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \nu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c15bbbb971240cf328aba572178f091684585468)).

Under certain conditions, it can be proven that the specific angular momentum is constant. The conditions for this proof include:

*   The mass of one object is much greater than the mass of the other one. (![Image 18: {\displaystyle m_{1}\gg m_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24a5c7dbf6371920422d5564e85afb81f24974e5))
*   The coordinate system is [inertial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertial_frame_of_reference "Inertial frame of reference").
*   Each object can be treated as a spherically symmetrical [point mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_particle "Point particle").
*   No other forces act on the system other than the gravitational force that connects the two bodies.

The proof starts with the [two body equation of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-body_problem "Two-body problem"), derived from [Newton's law of universal gravitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_law_of_universal_gravitation "Newton's law of universal gravitation"):

![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\ddot {\mathbf {r} }}+{\frac {Gm_{1}}{r^{2}}}{\frac {\mathbf {r} }{r}}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/859d90d687c18b1335ba20b8a7975fd389ee40e3)

where:

The cross product of the position vector with the equation of motion is:

![Image 20: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} \times {\ddot {\mathbf {r} }}+\mathbf {r} \times {\frac {Gm_{1}}{r^{2}}}{\frac {\mathbf {r} }{r}}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fea308257bd2207de87446e30cb6f3269e959a25)

Because ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {r} =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/77f2ec65b2d686b2b15ec0800cf81568790c3af9) the second term vanishes:

![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} \times {\ddot {\mathbf {r} }}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/291900725417381d59017ae22c0e6f9dc2497158)

It can also be derived that: ![Image 23: {\displaystyle {\frac {\mathrm {d} }{\mathrm {d} t}}\left(\mathbf {r} \times {\dot {\mathbf {r} }}\right)={\dot {\mathbf {r} }}\times {\dot {\mathbf {r} }}+\mathbf {r} \times {\ddot {\mathbf {r} }}=\mathbf {r} \times {\ddot {\mathbf {r} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7486402a9e99293a8c1ebd43eefb8a8fbb091763)

Combining these two equations gives: ![Image 24: {\displaystyle {\frac {\mathrm {d} }{\mathrm {d} t}}\left(\mathbf {r} \times {\dot {\mathbf {r} }}\right)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bcd0d5e7ba00b0d53faea4036c811575880ee3c5)

Since the time derivative is equal to zero, the quantity ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} \times {\dot {\mathbf {r} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed6363b880dd8a0d92cec53e7eb89296508c00f6) is constant. Using the velocity vector ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6) in place of the rate of change of position, and ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \mathbf {h} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1fce1c8de3a01ae39379db83781850619c4c0987) for the specific angular momentum: ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \mathbf {h} =\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d0dd0e6d48228ff8844ff554ba14312d5ad880d) is constant.

This is different from the normal construction of momentum, ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {p} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0c7ec7c9fe1ab68c6ec7bf58b6ca625d305e477), because it does not include the mass of the object in question.

Kepler's laws of planetary motion
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Specific_angular_momentum&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Kepler's laws of planetary motion")]

Kepler's laws of planetary motion can be proved almost directly with the above relationships.

The proof starts again with the equation of the two-body problem. This time the cross product is multiplied with the specific relative angular momentum ![Image 30: {\displaystyle {\ddot {\mathbf {r} }}\times \mathbf {h} =-{\frac {\mu }{r^{2}}}{\frac {\mathbf {r} }{r}}\times \mathbf {h} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c64af6d6c506a63ecab69cfa66827b2cb72534b6)

The left hand side is equal to the derivative ![Image 31: {\textstyle {\frac {\mathrm {d} }{\mathrm {d} t}}\left({\dot {\mathbf {r} }}\times \mathbf {h} \right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae9fd4567743a7e7b52e560c3c9af49c276d6b23) because the angular momentum is constant.

After some steps (which includes using the [vector triple product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple_product#Vector_triple_product "Triple product") and defining the scalar ![Image 32: {\displaystyle {\dot {r}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99b51a39764cf6daa615ef2144ac420ecff01cfc) to be the _radial velocity_, as opposed to the norm of the vector ![Image 33: {\displaystyle {\dot {\mathbf {r} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4e3b5c7bbc882098c055a39ed483572fbbe79352)) the right hand side becomes: ![Image 34: {\displaystyle -{\frac {\mu }{r^{3}}}\left(\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {h} \right)=-{\frac {\mu }{r^{3}}}\left(\left(\mathbf {r} \cdot \mathbf {v} \right)\mathbf {r} -r^{2}\mathbf {v} \right)=-\left({\frac {\mu }{r^{2}}}{\dot {r}}\mathbf {r} -{\frac {\mu }{r}}\mathbf {v} \right)=\mu {\frac {\mathrm {d} }{\mathrm {d} t}}\left({\frac {\mathbf {r} }{r}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a4906b5244f596964f62be5834da2b2c4d65dfa5)

Setting these two expression equal and integrating over time leads to (with the constant of integration ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \mathbf {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11de80478fce9090e43eed19100b37cc841661e8)) ![Image 36: {\displaystyle {\dot {\mathbf {r} }}\times \mathbf {h} =\mu {\frac {\mathbf {r} }{r}}+\mathbf {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/34f4bce7cb14c8c79ff75f5fa5631f9bec6267c1)

Now this equation is multiplied ([dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product")) with ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eca0f46511c4c986c48b254073732c0bd98ae0c1) and rearranged ![Image 38: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\mathbf {r} \cdot \left({\dot {\mathbf {r} }}\times \mathbf {h} \right)&=\mathbf {r} \cdot \left(\mu {\frac {\mathbf {r} }{r}}+\mathbf {C} \right)\\\Rightarrow \left(\mathbf {r} \times {\dot {\mathbf {r} }}\right)\cdot \mathbf {h} &=\mu r+rC\cos \theta \\\Rightarrow h^{2}&=\mu r+rC\cos \theta \end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7b17f7c88716f31ff3cac06e03193328b52aeee8)

Finally one gets the [orbit equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit_equation "Orbit equation")[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_angular_momentum#cite_note-Vallado-1)![Image 39: {\displaystyle r={\frac {\frac {h^{2}}{\mu }}{1+{\frac {C}{\mu }}\cos \theta }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/74d48b791548221b9dec95ca3c1d639a97c270c8)

which is the [equation of a conic section in polar coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conic_Sections_in_Polar_Coordinates "Conic Sections in Polar Coordinates") with [semi-latus rectum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-latus_rectum "Semi-latus rectum")![Image 40: {\textstyle p={\frac {h^{2}}{\mu }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c8d28ef6d476572c84db51749849a3d608790d0) and [eccentricity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eccentricity_(mathematics) "Eccentricity (mathematics)")![Image 41: {\textstyle e={\frac {C}{\mu }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e3e1cb043ca22cfb444384bcc460cb4ad4ad29b9).

The second law follows instantly from the second of the three equations to calculate the absolute value of the specific relative angular momentum.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_angular_momentum#cite_note-Vallado-1)

If one connects this form of the equation ![Image 42: {\textstyle \mathrm {d} t={\frac {r^{2}}{h}}\,\mathrm {d} \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/becb84aed0e64b93c32a819734740cc11d5a7063) with the relationship ![Image 43: {\textstyle \mathrm {d} A={\frac {r^{2}}{2}}\,\mathrm {d} \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9f21421a0edc7ff4cd5f41211c32fdf4809d23a6) for the area of a sector with an infinitesimal small angle ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e0b437b3bf78478d8912728490a3a6209ad28a8) (triangle with one very small side), the equation ![Image 45: {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} t={\frac {2}{h}}\,\mathrm {d} A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c25906d41c741c7d81e8bbad73a22fda9da81d9b)

Kepler's third is a direct consequence of the second law. Integrating over one revolution gives the [orbital period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_period "Orbital period")[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_angular_momentum#cite_note-Vallado-1)![Image 46: {\displaystyle T={\frac {2\pi ab}{h}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5de778a8b248a9d2bb28962b642e7255af01a51a)

for the area ![Image 47: {\displaystyle \pi ab}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e49e097530aa054f7782fc7de33b0a60418ff964) of an ellipse. Replacing the semi-minor axis with ![Image 48: {\displaystyle b={\sqrt {ap}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1dd3fe27effa1661de425d1a7b520cbf8c31ca64) and the specific relative angular momentum with ![Image 49: {\displaystyle h={\sqrt {\mu p}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/98e64c228e1a6f7b17f92d6ad3f9d063a415f575) one gets ![Image 50: {\displaystyle T=2\pi {\sqrt {\frac {a^{3}}{\mu }}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/84a962a4e5da8183f6a38ef23d0a5a9452667148)

There is thus a relationship between the semi-major axis and the orbital period of a satellite that can be reduced to a constant of the central body.
