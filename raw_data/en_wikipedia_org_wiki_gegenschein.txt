**Gegenschein** (; German:[[ˈɡeːɡn̩ˌʃaɪn]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German "Help:IPA/Standard German"); lit.'counter-shine') or **counterglow** is a faintly bright spot in the [night sky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Night_sky "Night sky") centered at the [antisolar point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antisolar_point "Antisolar point"). The [backscatter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Backscatter "Backscatter") of [sunlight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunlight "Sunlight") by [interplanetary dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust "Interplanetary dust") causes this [optical phenomenon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_phenomenon "Optical phenomenon"), being a [zodiacal light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zodiacal_light "Zodiacal light") and part of its zodiacal light band.

Like [zodiacal light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zodiacal_light "Zodiacal light"), gegenschein is sunlight [scattered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_scattering_by_particles "Light scattering by particles") by [interplanetary dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud "Interplanetary dust cloud"). Most of this dust orbits the Sun near the [ecliptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecliptic "Ecliptic") plane, with a possible concentration of particles centered at the [L 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_point#L2 "Lagrange point") point of the Earth–Sun system.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gegenschein#cite_note-1)

Gegenschein is distinguished from zodiacal light by its high [angle of reflection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle_of_incidence_(optics) "Angle of incidence (optics)") of the incident sunlight on the dust particles. It forms a slightly brighter elliptical spot of 8–10° across[directly opposite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opposition_(astronomy) "Opposition (astronomy)") the Sun within the dimmer band of zodiacal light and zodiac constellation.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gegenschein#cite_note-Sky_&_Telescope_2015-2) The intensity of the gegenschein is [relatively enhanced](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opposition_surge "Opposition surge") because each dust particle is seen at [full phase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_angle_(astronomy) "Phase angle (astronomy)"),[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gegenschein#cite_note-3) having a difficult to measure [apparent magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_magnitude "Apparent magnitude") of +5 to +6, with a very low [surface brightness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_brightness "Surface brightness") in the +10 to +12 magnitude range.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gegenschein#cite_note-Dickinson_2016-4)

It is commonly stated that the gegenschein was first described by the French [Jesuit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesuit "Jesuit") astronomer and professor [Esprit Pézenas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Esprit_P%C3%A9zenas "Esprit Pézenas")[[fr](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Esprit_P%C3%A9zenas "fr:Esprit Pézenas")] (1692–1776) in 1730. Further observations were supposedly made by the German explorer [Alexander von Humboldt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_von_Humboldt "Alexander von Humboldt") during his South American journey from 1799 to 1803. It was Humboldt who first used the German term _Gegenschein_.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gegenschein#cite_note-Sheehan-5) However, research conducted in 2021 by [Texas State University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Texas_State_University "Texas State University") astronomer and professor [Donald Olson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donald_Olson_(astronomer) "Donald Olson (astronomer)") discovered that the Danish astronomer [Theodor Brorsen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodor_Brorsen "Theodor Brorsen") was actually the first person to observe and describe one in 1854, although Brorsen had thought that Pézenas had observed it first.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gegenschein#cite_note-6) Olson believes what Pézenas actually observed was an auroral event, as he described the phenomenon as having a red glow; Olson found many other reports of auroral activity from around Europe and Asia on the same date Pézenas made his observation. Humboldt's report instead described glowing triangular patches on both the western and eastern horizons shortly after sunset, while true gegenschein is most visible near local midnight when it is highest in the sky.

Brorsen published the first thorough investigations of the gegenschein in 1854.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gegenschein#cite_note-7) T. W. Backhouse discovered it independently in 1876, as did [Edward Emerson Barnard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Emerson_Barnard "Edward Emerson Barnard") in 1882.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gegenschein#cite_note-ley196104-8) In modern times, the gegenschein is not visible in most inhabited regions of the world due to [light pollution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_pollution "Light pollution").
