From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

The **intrinsic dimension** for a data set can be thought of as the minimal number of variables needed to represent the data set. Similarly, in [signal processing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signal_processing "Signal processing") of multidimensional signals, the **intrinsic dimension** of the signal describes how many variables are needed to generate a good approximation of the signal.

When estimating intrinsic dimension, however, a slightly broader definition based on manifold dimension is often used, where a representation in the intrinsic dimension does only need to exist locally. Such intrinsic dimension estimation methods can thus handle data sets with different intrinsic dimensions in different parts of the data set. This is often referred to as [local intrinsic dimensionality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_dimension#Local_Intrinsic_Dimensionality).

The intrinsic dimension can be used as a lower bound of what dimension it is possible to compress a data set into through dimension reduction, but it can also be used as a measure of the complexity of the data set or signal. For a data set or signal of _N_ variables, its intrinsic dimension _M_ satisfies _0 ≤ M ≤ N_, although estimators may yield higher values.

Let _![Image 1: {\textstyle f(x\_{1},x\_{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/ddffa325810e2fb8d07ebcbbb0cd9f3706b19d86)_ be a [two-variable function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_of_several_variables "Function of several variables") (or [signal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signal "Signal")) which is of the form ![Image 2: {\textstyle f(x_{1},x_{2})=g(x_{1})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a81729b580c830ecad5348def8488996c66e9103) for some [one-variable function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_of_a_real_variable "Function of a real variable")_g_ which is not [constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_function "Constant function"). This means that _f_ varies, in accordance to _g_, with the first variable or along the first [coordinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinates_(mathematics) "Coordinates (mathematics)"). On the other hand, _f_ is constant with respect to the second variable or along the second coordinate. It is only necessary to know the value of one, namely the first, variable in order to determine the value of _f_. Hence, it is a two-variable function but its intrinsic dimension is one.

A slightly more complicated example is![Image 3: {\textstyle f(x_{1},x_{2})=g(x_{1}+x_{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a45cbb44bcbf3425c8e0649ebce6714631b1105). _f_ is still intrinsic one-dimensional, which can be seen by making a [variable transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Change_of_variables "Change of variables")![Image 4: {\textstyle y_{1}=x_{1}+x_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/67d4a6ba3f6da3bb79a6191051dab709d89bb4ef) and ![Image 5: {\textstyle y_{2}=x_{1}-x_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18787b2a98592ba7ccb0748ac858ce8ff8e7c9e9) which gives ![Image 6: {\textstyle f\left({\frac {y_{1}+y_{2}}{2}},{\frac {y_{1}-y_{2}}{2}}\right)=g\left(y_{1}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b827beaed6acbd9eca8a70816b3268d779f08440). Since the variation in _f_ can be described by the single variable _y 1_ its intrinsic dimension is one.

For the case that _f_ is constant, its intrinsic dimension is zero since no variable is needed to describe variation. For the general case, when the intrinsic dimension of the two-variable function _f_ is neither zero or one, it is two.

In the literature, functions which are of intrinsic dimension zero, one, or two are sometimes referred to as _i0D_, _i1D_ or _i2D_, respectively.

Formal definition for signals
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Intrinsic_dimension&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Formal definition for signals")]

For an _N_-variable function _f_, the set of variables can be represented as an _N_-dimensional vector **x**: ![Image 7: {\textstyle f=f\left(\mathbf {x} \right){\text{ where }}\mathbf {x} =\left(x_{1},\dots ,x_{N}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f218203e103cf2e01b0752b2bed069fcee3d862e).

If for some _M_-variable function _g_ and _M × N_ matrix **A** it is the case that

*   for all **x**; ![Image 8: {\textstyle f(\mathbf {x} )=g(\mathbf {Ax} ),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f3f5b3df2cf2d39768a8b8d63ae4e4e603036357)
*   _M_ is the smallest number for which the above relation between _f_ and _g_ can be found,

then the intrinsic dimension of _f_ is _M_.

The intrinsic dimension is a characterization of _f_, it is not an unambiguous characterization of _g_ nor of **A**. That is, if the above relation is satisfied for some _f_, _g_, and **A**, it must also be satisfied for the same _f_ and _g′_ and **A′** given by ![Image 9: {\textstyle g'\left(\mathbf {y} \right)=g\left(\mathbf {By} \right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01a078d71cc03d0514618ba9a8e9e69444ad4151) and ![Image 10: {\textstyle \mathbf {A'} =\mathbf {B} ^{-1}\mathbf {A} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e61f168c519a3c52a127c2880b88f55715f1512f) where **B** is a non-singular _M × M_ matrix, since ![Image 11: {\textstyle f\left(\mathbf {x} \right)=g'\left(\mathbf {A'x} \right)=g\left(\mathbf {BA'x} \right)=g\left(\mathbf {Ax} \right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/49254b82433ff2778f2549fda6a94c2dd7dc9adc).

The Fourier transform of signals of low intrinsic dimension
-----------------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Intrinsic_dimension&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: The Fourier transform of signals of low intrinsic dimension")]

An _N_ variable function which has intrinsic dimension _M < N_ has a characteristic [Fourier transform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_transform "Fourier transform"). Intuitively, since this type of function is constant along one or several dimensions its Fourier transform must appear like an [impulse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_delta_function "Dirac delta function") (the Fourier transform of a constant) along the same dimension in the [frequency domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency_domain "Frequency domain").

Let _f_ be a two-variable function which is i1D. This means that there exists a normalized vector ![Image 12: {\textstyle \mathbf {n} \in \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dc6a4bcdf099270a52417596208dfc69ceac1bd1) and a one-variable function _g_ such that ![Image 13: {\textstyle f(\mathbf {x} )=g(\mathbf {n} ^{\operatorname {T} }\mathbf {x} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6fe03173ced46a0435c3a2d649f70bc569846ef4) for all ![Image 14: {\textstyle \mathbf {x} \in \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52980ed4aae2f348b16c2a62b4f6c6fd363e6b6e). If _F_ is the Fourier transform of _f_ (both are two-variable functions) it must be the case that ![Image 15: {\textstyle F\left(\mathbf {u} \right)=G\left(\mathbf {n} ^{\mathrm {T} }\mathbf {u} \right)\cdot \delta \left(\mathbf {m} ^{\mathrm {T} }\mathbf {u} \right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/14ae2106bd1cd3dba18ae6a1ebab8bb3c9d344e0).

Here _G_ is the Fourier transform of _g_ (both are one-variable functions), _δ_ is the [Dirac impulse function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_delta_function "Dirac delta function") and **m** is a normalized vector in ![Image 16: {\textstyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8f9dcb350bb8fb6a28ddc4aebfb5c4b03ce8784f) perpendicular to **n**. This means that _F_ vanishes everywhere except on a line which passes through the origin of the frequency domain and is parallel to **m**. Along this line _F_ varies according to _G_.

Let _f_ be an _N_-variable function which has intrinsic dimension _M_, that is, there exists an _M_-variable function _g_ and _M × N_ matrix **A** such that ![Image 17: {\textstyle f(\mathbf {x} )=g(\mathbf {Ax} )\quad \forall \mathbf {x} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e831f337003f5ffc00166c44fde4df2d65c7a10e).

Its Fourier transform _F_ can then be described as follows:

*   _F_ vanishes everywhere except for a subspace of dimension _M_
*   The subspace _M_ is spanned by the rows of the matrix **A**
*   In the subspace, _F_ varies according to _G_ the Fourier transform of _g_

The type of intrinsic dimension described above assumes that a [linear transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_transformation "Linear transformation") is applied to the coordinates of the _N_-variable function _f_ to produce the _M_ variables which are necessary to represent every value of _f_. This means that _f_ is constant along lines, planes, or hyperplanes, depending on _N_ and _M_.

In a general case, _f_ has intrinsic dimension _M_ if there exist _M_ functions _a 1_, _a 2_, ..., _a M_ and an _M_-variable function _g_ such that

*   ![Image 18: {\textstyle f(\mathbf {x} )=g\left(a_{1}(\mathbf {x} ),a_{2}(\mathbf {x} ),\dots ,a_{M}(\mathbf {x} )\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/955079f48a8ab4152d5b910fed6ff9fc966f5557)for all **x**
*   _M_ is the smallest number of functions which allows the above transformation

A simple example is transforming a 2-variable function _f_ to [polar coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_coordinates "Polar coordinates"):

![Image 19: {\displaystyle f\left({\frac {y_{1}+y_{2}}{2}},{\frac {y_{1}-y_{2}}{2}}\right)=g\left(y_{1}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1bffa790c34d09b65995baae7dc6fc7c33f5e7dd)

For the general case, a simple description of either the point sets for which _f_ is constant or its Fourier transform is usually not possible.

Local Intrinsic Dimensionality
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Intrinsic_dimension&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Local Intrinsic Dimensionality")]

Local intrinsic dimensionality (LID) refers to the observation that often data is distributed on a lower-dimensional manifold when only considering a nearby subset of the data. For example the function ![Image 20: {\displaystyle f(x,y)=x+\max\{0,|y|-1\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71545ec1b39c89523e5ddbd3ad34d7c335be2fee) can be considered one-dimensional when _y_ is close to 0 (with one variable _x_), two-dimensional when _y_ is close to 1, and again one-dimensional when _y_ is positive and much larger than 1 (with variable _x+y_).

Local intrinsic dimensionality is often used with respect to data. It then usually is estimated based on the _k_ nearest neighbors of a data point,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_dimension#cite_note-1) often based on a concept related to the [doubling dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doubling_space "Doubling space") in mathematics. Since the volume of a _d_-sphere grows exponentially in _d_, the rate at which new neighbors are found as the search radius is increased can be used to estimate the local intrinsic dimensionality (e.g., GED estimation[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_dimension#cite_note-2)). However, alternate approaches of estimation have been proposed, for example angle-based estimation.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_dimension#cite_note-3)

### Intrinsic dimension estimation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Intrinsic_dimension&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Intrinsic dimension estimation")]

Intrinsic dimension of data manifolds can be estimated by many methods, depending on assumptions of the data manifold. A 2016 review is.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_dimension#cite_note-4)

The two-nearest neighbors (TwoNN) method is a method for estimating the intrinsic dimension of an immersed [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold"). The algorithm is as follows:[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_dimension#cite_note-5)

> Scatter some points on the manifold.
> 
> 
> Measure ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mu =r_{2}/r_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a35e4a2f1b3db1aca8755aa0ed4ea1f060f96d9c) for many points, where ![Image 22: {\displaystyle r_{1},r_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d00a857c907f8c4b3858f06a1b5c1db4282ab027) are the distances to the point's two closest neighbors.
> 
> 
> Fit the [empirical CDF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empirical_distribution_function "Empirical distribution function") of ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161) to ![Image 24: {\displaystyle 1-\mu ^{-d}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23207a7c6de1c5fa78cc38d5797c71cf345a5aa1).
> 
> 
> Return ![Image 25: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab).

During the 1950s so called "scaling" methods were developed in the [social sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_sciences "Social sciences") to explore and summarize multidimensional data sets.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_dimension#cite_note-Torgerson-6) After Shepard introduced non-metric multidimensional scaling in 1962[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_dimension#cite_note-7) one of the major research areas within multi-dimensional scaling (MDS) was estimation of the intrinsic dimension.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_dimension#cite_note-8) The topic was also studied in [information theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_theory "Information theory"), pioneered by Bennet in 1965 who coined the term "intrinsic dimension" and wrote a computer program to estimate it.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_dimension#cite_note-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_dimension#cite_note-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_dimension#cite_note-11)

During the 1970s intrinsic dimensionality estimation methods were constructed that did not depend on dimensionality reductions such as MDS: based on local eigenvalues.,[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_dimension#cite_note-12) based on distance distributions,[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_dimension#cite_note-13) and based on other dimension-dependent geometric properties[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_dimension#cite_note-14)

Estimating intrinsic dimension of sets and probability measures has also been extensively studied since around 1980 in the field of dynamical systems, where dimensions of (strange) attractors have been the subject of interest.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_dimension#cite_note-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_dimension#cite_note-16)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_dimension#cite_note-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_dimension#cite_note-18) For strange attractors there is no manifold assumption, and the dimension measured is some version of fractal dimension — which also can be non-integer. However, definitions of fractal dimension yield the manifold dimension for manifolds.

In the 2000s the "curse of dimensionality" has been exploited to estimate intrinsic dimension.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_dimension#cite_note-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_dimension#cite_note-20)

The case of a two-variable signal which is i1D appears frequently in [computer vision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_vision "Computer vision") and [image processing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_processing "Image processing") and captures the idea of local image regions which contain lines or edges. The analysis of such regions has a long history, but it was not until a more formal and theoretical treatment of such operations began that the concept of intrinsic dimension was established, even though the name has varied.

For example, the concept which here is referred to as an _image neighborhood of intrinsic dimension 1_ or _i1D neighborhood_ is called _1-dimensional_ by Knutsson (1982),[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_dimension#cite_note-21)_linear symmetric_ by Bigün & Granlund (1987)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_dimension#cite_note-22) and _simple neighborhood_ in Granlund & Knutsson (1995).[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intrinsic_dimension#cite_note-23)
