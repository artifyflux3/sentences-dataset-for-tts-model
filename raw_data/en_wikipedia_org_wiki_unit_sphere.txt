In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **unit sphere** is a [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere") of unit [radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius"): the [set of points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Locus_(mathematics) "Locus (mathematics)") at [Euclidean distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance "Euclidean distance")[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1 "1") from some [center point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_(geometry) "Center (geometry)") in [three-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space"). More generally, the _unit ⁠![Image 2: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-sphere_ is an [⁠![Image 3: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-sphere "N-sphere") of unit radius in ⁠![Image 4: {\displaystyle (n+1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b30a29cfd35628469f9dbffea4804f5b422f3037)⁠-[dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension")[Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"); the [unit circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_circle "Unit circle") is a special case, the unit ⁠![Image 5: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf)⁠-sphere in the [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_plane "Euclidean plane"). An ([open](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set")) **unit ball** is the region inside of a unit sphere, the set of points of distance less than 1 from the center.

A sphere or ball with unit radius and center at the [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)") of the space is called _the_ unit sphere or _the_ unit ball. Any arbitrary sphere can be transformed to the unit sphere by a combination of [translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry) "Translation (geometry)") and [scaling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scaling_(geometry) "Scaling (geometry)"), so the study of spheres in general can often be reduced to the study of the unit sphere.

The unit sphere is often used as a model for [spherical geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_geometry "Spherical geometry") because it has constant [sectional curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sectional_curvature "Sectional curvature") of 1, which simplifies calculations. In [trigonometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometry "Trigonometry"), circular [arc length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_length "Arc length") on the unit circle is called [radians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian") and used for measuring [angular distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_distance "Angular distance"); in [spherical trigonometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_trigonometry "Spherical trigonometry") surface area on the unit sphere is called [steradians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steradian "Steradian") and used for measuring [solid angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_angle "Solid angle").

In more general contexts, a _unit sphere_ is the set of points of [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance "Distance") 1 from a fixed central point, where different [norms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norm_(mathematics) "Norm (mathematics)") can be used as general notions of "distance", and an (open) _unit ball_ is the region inside.

It is the [topological interior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interior_(topology) "Interior (topology)") of the **closed unit ball** of ![Image 85: {\displaystyle (V,\|\cdot \|)\colon }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/34284f7e2dddc8337b2613f749758aed0adbefcb)![Image 86: {\displaystyle \{x\in V:\|x\|\leq 1\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52442e128feeb5dfdf756da43b48812f4c747dff)

The latter is the disjoint union of the former and their common border, the **unit sphere** of ![Image 87: {\displaystyle (V,\|\cdot \|)\colon }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/34284f7e2dddc8337b2613f749758aed0adbefcb)![Image 88: {\displaystyle \{x\in V:\|x\|=1\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/00f9c8e38c6832a42f9b52a6b2d74271eb338ff8)

The "shape" of the _unit ball_ is entirely dependent on the chosen norm; it may well have "corners", and for example may look like ![Image 89: {\displaystyle [-1,1]^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a008254b1bf6d63ac3b13548c4c31180bcd43de) in the case of the max-norm in ⁠![Image 90: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d)⁠. One obtains a naturally _round ball_ as the unit ball pertaining to the usual [Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space") norm, based in the finite-dimensional case on the [Euclidean distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance "Euclidean distance"); its boundary is what is usually meant by the _unit sphere_.

Let ![Image 91: {\displaystyle x=(x_{1},...x_{n})\in \mathbb {R} ^{n}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86661b34262250238b6fdc87225ad70f9253195b) Define the usual ⁠![Image 92: {\displaystyle \ell _{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8265d5df21cb4dab10dd3cd69a19895d649f5b45)⁠-norm for ![Image 93: {\displaystyle p\geq 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf93c1353080a21b276e79058d82c19c40310653) as: ![Image 94: {\displaystyle \|x\|_{p}={\biggl (}\sum _{k=1}^{n}|x_{k}|^{p}{\biggr )}^{1/p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92bc596f9581bd37035e70db6c290d5d9544a127)

Then ![Image 95: {\displaystyle \|x\|_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64198280094b8bdaac094dd8f5681d62b2b4d767) is the usual [Hilbert space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space") norm. ![Image 96: {\displaystyle \|x\|_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50ca585aadc8cee1acad7d68fdab962d3a18ae7e) is called the Hamming norm, or ⁠![Image 97: {\displaystyle \ell _{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/361ddd720474aa41cb05453e03424fb7999d3b02)⁠-norm. The condition ![Image 98: {\displaystyle p\geq 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf93c1353080a21b276e79058d82c19c40310653) is necessary in the definition of the ![Image 99: {\displaystyle \ell _{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8265d5df21cb4dab10dd3cd69a19895d649f5b45) norm, as the unit ball in any normed space must be [convex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_set "Convex set") as a consequence of the [triangle inequality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle_inequality "Triangle inequality"). Let ![Image 100: {\displaystyle \|x\|_{\infty }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52addef01b4eb8d8e0c79f11dc907e86837e23e5) denote the max-norm or ⁠![Image 101: {\displaystyle \ell _{\infty }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1989b3bca9a0361579a2b9aec109b16c9daa7d18)⁠-norm of ⁠![Image 102: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4)⁠.

Note that for the one-dimensional circumferences ![Image 103: {\displaystyle C_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6bc37470431fbf62081b69ba870ad3f855178361) of the two-dimensional unit balls, we have: ![Image 104: {\displaystyle C_{1}=4{\sqrt {2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f833aa82efb99684516c211120502b83414b8757) is the minimum value. ![Image 105: {\displaystyle C_{2}=2\pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/627321c9a32dc994b64bc0fbcf28802b182b2adb)![Image 106: {\displaystyle C_{\infty }=8}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/283fd81ef6eeb7eb728041bba1df5178cec8a9d4) is the maximum value.

All three of the above definitions can be straightforwardly generalized to a [metric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space"), with respect to a chosen origin. However, topological considerations (interior, closure, border) need not apply in the same way (e.g., in [ultrametric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultrametric "Ultrametric") spaces, all of the three are simultaneously open and closed sets), and the unit sphere may even be empty in some metric spaces.

If ⁠![Image 107: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845)⁠ is a linear space with a real [quadratic form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_form "Quadratic form")![Image 108: {\displaystyle F:V\to \mathbb {R} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c70ac6ed5747aadeb434bee0cd669b5cda4650ea) then ![Image 109: {\displaystyle \{p\in V:F(p)=1\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75de7c967a5a9bb4181eab0f69ee011419cee3ab) may be called the unit sphere[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_sphere#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_sphere#cite_note-4) or [unit quasi-sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperboloid#Relation_to_the_sphere "Hyperboloid") of ![Image 110: {\displaystyle V.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b2661a49b86bd1a5548e527bbfb068aa9f59978) For example, the quadratic form ⁠![Image 111: {\displaystyle x^{2}-y^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a7f07ef330c7bbc2bd1c835de4093c922c1ce1d6)⁠, when set equal to one, produces the [unit hyperbola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_hyperbola "Unit hyperbola"), which plays the role of the "unit circle" in the plane of [split-complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Split-complex_number "Split-complex number"). Similarly, the quadratic form ![Image 112: {\displaystyle x^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf0bf28fd28f45d07e1ceb909ce333c18c558c93) yields a pair of lines for the unit sphere in the [dual number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_number "Dual number") plane.
