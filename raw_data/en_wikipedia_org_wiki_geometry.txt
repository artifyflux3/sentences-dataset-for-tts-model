**Geometry** (from [Ancient Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greek_language "Ancient Greek language")[γεωμετρία](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B3%CE%B5%CF%89%CE%BC%CE%B5%CF%84%CF%81%CE%AF%CE%B1#Ancient_Greek "wikt:γεωμετρία")_(\_geōmetría\_)_'land measurement'; from [γῆ](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%B3%E1%BF%86#Ancient_Greek "wikt:γῆ")_(\_gê\_)_'earth, land' and [μέτρον](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/%CE%BC%CE%AD%CF%84%CF%81%CE%BF%CE%BD#Ancient_Greek "wikt:μέτρον")_(\_métron\_)_'a measure')[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-1) is a branch of [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") concerned with properties of space such as the distance, shape, size, and relative position of figures.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Risi2015-2) Geometry is, along with [arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic "Arithmetic"), one of the oldest branches of mathematics. A mathematician who works in the field of geometry is called a _[geometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List\_of\_geometers "List of geometers")_. Until the 19th century, geometry was almost exclusively devoted to [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry"),[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-3) which includes the notions of [point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)"), [line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_(geometry) "Line (geometry)"), [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(geometry) "Plane (geometry)"), [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance "Distance"), [angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle"), [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(mathematics) "Surface (mathematics)"), and [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve"), as fundamental concepts.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Tabak_2014_xiv-4)

Originally developed to model the physical world, geometry has applications in almost all sciences, and also in art, [architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Architecture "Architecture"), and other activities that are related to graphics.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Meyer2006-5) Geometry also has applications in areas of mathematics that are apparently unrelated. For example, methods of algebraic geometry are fundamental in [Wiles's proof](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wiles%27s_proof_of_Fermat%27s_Last_Theorem "Wiles's proof of Fermat's Last Theorem") of [Fermat's Last Theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermat%27s_Last_Theorem "Fermat's Last Theorem"), a problem that was stated in terms of [elementary arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_arithmetic "Elementary arithmetic"), and remained unsolved for several centuries.

During the 19th century several discoveries enlarged dramatically the scope of geometry. One of the oldest such discoveries is [Carl Friedrich Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss "Carl Friedrich Gauss")'s [Theorema Egregium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorema_Egregium "Theorema Egregium") ("remarkable theorem") that asserts roughly that the [Gaussian curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_curvature "Gaussian curvature") of a surface is independent from any specific [embedding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embedding "Embedding") in a [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"). This implies that surfaces can be studied _intrinsically_, that is, as stand-alone spaces, and has been expanded into the theory of [manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold") and [Riemannian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_geometry "Riemannian geometry"). Later in the 19th century, it appeared that geometries without the [parallel postulate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_postulate "Parallel postulate") ([non-Euclidean geometries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Euclidean_geometries "Non-Euclidean geometries")) can be developed without introducing any contradiction. The geometry that underlies [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") is a famous application of non-Euclidean geometry.

Since the late 19th century, the scope of geometry has been greatly expanded, and the field has been split in many subfields that depend on the underlying methods—[differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), [computational geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_geometry "Computational geometry"), [algebraic topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_topology "Algebraic topology"), [discrete geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_geometry "Discrete geometry") (also known as _combinatorial geometry_), etc.—or on the properties of Euclidean spaces that are disregarded—[projective geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_geometry "Projective geometry") that consider only alignment of points but not distance and parallelism, [affine geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_geometry "Affine geometry") that omits the concept of angle and distance, [finite geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_geometry "Finite geometry") that omits [continuity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuity_(mathematics) "Continuity (mathematics)"), and others. This enlargement of the scope of geometry led to a change of meaning of the word "space", which originally referred to the three-dimensional [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space "Space") of the physical world and its [model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Model "Model") provided by Euclidean geometry; presently a **geometric space**, or simply a _space_ is a [mathematical structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_structure "Mathematical structure") on which some geometry is defined.

History
-------

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/45/Westerner_and_Arab_practicing_geometry_15th_century_manuscript.jpg/250px-Westerner_and_Arab_practicing_geometry_15th_century_manuscript.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Westerner_and_Arab_practicing_geometry_15th_century_manuscript.jpg)

A [European](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnic_groups_in_Europe "Ethnic groups in Europe") and an [Arab](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arab "Arab") practicing geometry in the 15th century

The earliest recorded beginnings of geometry can be traced to ancient [Mesopotamia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mesopotamia "Mesopotamia") and [Egypt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt "Ancient Egypt") in the 2nd millennium BC.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-6)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-7) Early geometry was a collection of empirically discovered principles concerning lengths, angles, areas, and volumes, which were developed to meet some practical need in [surveying](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surveying "Surveying"), [construction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Construction "Construction"), [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy"), and various crafts. The earliest known texts on geometry are the [Egyptian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptian_mathematics "Egyptian mathematics")[Rhind Papyrus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhind_Mathematical_Papyrus "Rhind Mathematical Papyrus") (2000–1800 BC) and [Moscow Papyrus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moscow_Mathematical_Papyrus "Moscow Mathematical Papyrus") (c. 1890 BC), and the [Babylonian clay tablets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylonian_mathematics "Babylonian mathematics"), such as [Plimpton 322](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plimpton_322 "Plimpton 322") (1900 BC). For example, the Moscow Papyrus gives a formula for calculating the volume of a truncated pyramid, or [frustum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frustum "Frustum").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Boyer_1991_loc=Egypt_p._19-8) Later clay tablets (350–50 BC) demonstrate that Babylonian astronomers implemented [trapezoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid "Trapezoid") procedures for computing Jupiter's position and [motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Displacement_(vector) "Displacement (vector)") within time-velocity space. These geometric procedures anticipated the [Oxford Calculators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxford_Calculators "Oxford Calculators"), including the [mean speed theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_speed_theorem "Mean speed theorem"), by 14 centuries.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-9) South of Egypt the [ancient Nubians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nubia "Nubia") established a system of geometry including early versions of sun clocks.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-10)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-11)

In the 7th century BC, the [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_mathematics "Greek mathematics") mathematician [Thales of Miletus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thales_of_Miletus "Thales of Miletus") used geometry to solve problems such as calculating the height of pyramids and the distance of ships from the shore. He is credited with the first use of deductive reasoning applied to geometry, by deriving four corollaries to [Thales's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thales%27s_theorem "Thales's theorem").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Boyer_1991_loc=Ionia_and_the_Pythagoreans_p._43-12)[Pythagoras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoras "Pythagoras") established the [Pythagorean School](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoreans "Pythagoreans"), which is credited with the first proof of the [Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem"),[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-13) though the statement of the theorem has a long history.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-14)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-15)[Eudoxus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eudoxus_of_Cnidus "Eudoxus of Cnidus") (408–c. 355 BC) developed the [method of exhaustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Method_of_exhaustion "Method of exhaustion"), which allowed the calculation of areas and volumes of curvilinear figures,[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-16) as well as a theory of ratios that avoided the problem of [incommensurable magnitudes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incommensurable_magnitudes "Incommensurable magnitudes"), which enabled subsequent geometers to make significant advances. Around 300 BC, geometry was revolutionized by Euclid, whose _[Elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s\_Elements "Euclid's Elements")_, widely considered the most successful and influential textbook of all time,[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-17) introduced [mathematical rigor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_rigor "Mathematical rigor") through the [axiomatic method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiomatic_method "Axiomatic method") and is the earliest example of the format still used in mathematics today, that of definition, axiom, theorem, and proof. Although most of the contents of the _Elements_ were already known, Euclid arranged them into a single, coherent logical framework.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Boyer_1991_loc=Euclid_of_Alexandria_p._104-18) The _Elements_ was known to all educated people in the West until the middle of the 20th century and its contents are still taught in geometry classes today.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-19)[Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes") (c. 287–212 BC) of [Syracuse, Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syracuse,_Italy "Syracuse, Italy") used the method of exhaustion to calculate the [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area") under the arc of a [parabola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabola "Parabola") with the [summation of an infinite series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Series_(mathematics) "Series (mathematics)"), and gave remarkably accurate approximations of [pi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi "Pi").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-20) He also studied the [spiral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes_spiral "Archimedes spiral") bearing his name and obtained formulas for the [volumes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume") of [surfaces of revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_of_revolution "Surface of revolution").

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Woman_teaching_geometry.jpg/250px-Woman_teaching_geometry.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Woman_teaching_geometry.jpg)

_Woman teaching geometry_. Illustration at the beginning of a medieval translation of [Euclid's Elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements "Euclid's Elements"), (c. 1310).

[Indian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_mathematics "Indian mathematics") mathematicians also made many important contributions in geometry. The _[Shatapatha Brahmana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shatapatha\_Brahmana "Shatapatha Brahmana")_ (3rd century BC) contains rules for ritual geometric constructions that are similar to the _[Sulba Sutras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shulba\_Sutras "Shulba Sutras")_.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Staal_1999-21) According to ([Hayashi 2005](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#CITEREFHayashi2005), p.363), the _Śulba Sūtras_ contain "the earliest extant verbal expression of the Pythagorean Theorem in the world, although it had already been known to the Old Babylonians. They contain lists of [Pythagorean triples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_triples "Pythagorean triples"),[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-22) which are particular cases of [Diophantine equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diophantine_equations "Diophantine equations").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-cooke198-23) In the [Bakhshali manuscript](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bakhshali_manuscript "Bakhshali manuscript"), there are a handful of geometric problems (including problems about volumes of irregular solids). The Bakhshali manuscript also "employs a decimal place value system with a dot for zero."[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-hayashi2005-371-24)[Aryabhata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aryabhata "Aryabhata")'s _[Aryabhatiya](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aryabhatiya "Aryabhatiya")_ (499) includes the computation of areas and volumes. [Brahmagupta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahmagupta "Brahmagupta") wrote his astronomical work _[\_Brāhmasphuṭasiddhānta\_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahmasphutasiddhanta "Brahmasphutasiddhanta")_ in 628. Chapter 12, containing 66 [Sanskrit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanskrit "Sanskrit") verses, was divided into two sections: "basic operations" (including cube roots, fractions, ratio and proportion, and barter) and "practical mathematics" (including mixture, mathematical series, plane figures, stacking bricks, sawing of timber, and piling of grain).[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-hayashi2003-p121-122-25) In the latter section, he stated his famous theorem on the diagonals of a [cyclic quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_quadrilateral "Cyclic quadrilateral"). Chapter 12 also included a formula for the area of a cyclic quadrilateral (a generalization of [Heron's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heron%27s_formula "Heron's formula")), as well as a complete description of [rational triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_triangle "Rational triangle") (_i.e._ triangles with rational sides and rational areas).[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-hayashi2003-p121-122-25)

In the [Middle Ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Ages "Middle Ages"), [mathematics in medieval Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics_in_medieval_Islam "Mathematics in medieval Islam") contributed to the development of geometry, especially [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry").[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-26)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-27)[Al-Mahani](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Al-Mahani "Al-Mahani") (b. 853) conceived the idea of reducing geometrical problems such as duplicating the cube to problems in algebra.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-28)[Thābit ibn Qurra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Th%C4%81bit_ibn_Qurra "Thābit ibn Qurra") (known as Thebit in [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin")) (836–901) dealt with [arithmetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic "Arithmetic") operations applied to [ratios](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ratio "Ratio") of geometrical quantities, and contributed to the development of [analytic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_geometry "Analytic geometry").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-ReferenceA-29)[Omar Khayyam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omar_Khayyam "Omar Khayyam") (1048–1131) found geometric solutions to [cubic equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubic_equation "Cubic equation").[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-30) The theorems of [Ibn al-Haytham](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ibn_al-Haytham "Ibn al-Haytham") (Alhazen), Omar Khayyam and [Nasir al-Din al-Tusi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nasir_al-Din_al-Tusi "Nasir al-Din al-Tusi") on [quadrilaterals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral "Quadrilateral"), including the [Lambert quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambert_quadrilateral "Lambert quadrilateral") and [Saccheri quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saccheri_quadrilateral "Saccheri quadrilateral"), were part of a line of research on the [parallel postulate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_postulate "Parallel postulate") continued by later European geometers, including [Vitello](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitello "Vitello") (c. 1230– c. 1314), [Gersonides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gersonides "Gersonides") (1288–1344), Alfonso, [John Wallis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Wallis "John Wallis"), and [Giovanni Girolamo Saccheri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giovanni_Girolamo_Saccheri "Giovanni Girolamo Saccheri"), that by the 19th century led to the discovery of [hyperbolic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry "Hyperbolic geometry").[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-31)

In the early 17th century, there were two important developments in geometry. The first was the creation of analytic geometry, or geometry with [coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system") and [equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equation "Equation"), by [René Descartes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes "René Descartes") (1596–1650) and [Pierre de Fermat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_de_Fermat "Pierre de Fermat") (1601–1665).[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Boyer2012-32) This was a necessary precursor to the development of [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus") and a precise quantitative science of [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Edwards2012-33) The second geometric development of this period was the systematic study of [projective geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_geometry "Projective geometry") by [Girard Desargues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Girard_Desargues "Girard Desargues") (1591–1661).[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-FieldGray2012-34) Projective geometry studies properties of shapes which are unchanged under [projections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projection_(linear_algebra) "Projection (linear algebra)") and [sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Section_(fiber_bundle) "Section (fiber bundle)"), especially as they relate to [artistic perspective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perspective_(graphical) "Perspective (graphical)").[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Wylie2011-35)

Two developments in geometry in the 19th century changed the way it had been studied previously.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Gray2011-36) These were the discovery of [non-Euclidean geometries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Euclidean_geometry "Non-Euclidean geometry") by Nikolai Ivanovich Lobachevsky, János Bolyai and Carl Friedrich Gauss and of the formulation of [symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry "Symmetry") as the central consideration in the [Erlangen programme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erlangen_programme "Erlangen programme") of [Felix Klein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein "Felix Klein") (which generalized the Euclidean and non-Euclidean geometries). Two of the master geometers of the time were [Bernhard Riemann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernhard_Riemann "Bernhard Riemann") (1826–1866), working primarily with tools from [mathematical analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_analysis "Mathematical analysis"), and introducing the [Riemann surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_surface "Riemann surface"), and [Henri Poincaré](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Poincar%C3%A9 "Henri Poincaré"), the founder of [algebraic topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_topology "Algebraic topology") and the geometric theory of [dynamical systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamical_system "Dynamical system"). As a consequence of these major changes in the conception of geometry, the concept of "[space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_(mathematics) "Space (mathematics)")" became something rich and varied, and the natural background for theories as different as [complex analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis "Complex analysis") and [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics").[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Bayro-Corrochano2018-37)

Main concepts
-------------

The following are some of the most important concepts in geometry.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Tabak_2014_xiv-4)[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Kline1990-38)

### Axioms

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ed/Parallel_postulate_en.svg/250px-Parallel_postulate_en.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Parallel_postulate_en.svg)

An illustration of Euclid's [parallel postulate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_postulate "Parallel postulate")

[Euclid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid "Euclid") took an abstract approach to geometry in his [Elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements "Euclid's Elements"),[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Katz2000-39) one of the most influential books ever written.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Berlinski2014-40) Euclid introduced certain [axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom "Axiom"), or [postulates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Postulate "Postulate"), expressing primary or self-evident properties of points, lines, and planes.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Hartshorne2013-41) He proceeded to rigorously deduce other properties by mathematical reasoning. The characteristic feature of Euclid's approach to geometry was its rigor, and it has come to be known as _axiomatic_ or _[synthetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthetic\_geometry "Synthetic geometry")_ geometry.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-HerbstFujita2017-42) At the start of the 19th century, the discovery of [non-Euclidean geometries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-Euclidean_geometries "Non-Euclidean geometries") by [Nikolai Ivanovich Lobachevsky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nikolai_Ivanovich_Lobachevsky "Nikolai Ivanovich Lobachevsky") (1792–1856), [János Bolyai](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J%C3%A1nos_Bolyai "János Bolyai") (1802–1860), [Carl Friedrich Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss "Carl Friedrich Gauss") (1777–1855) and others[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Yaglom2012-43) led to a revival of interest in this discipline, and in the 20th century, [David Hilbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Hilbert "David Hilbert") (1862–1943) employed axiomatic reasoning in an attempt to provide a modern foundation of geometry.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Holme2010-44)

### Spaces and subspaces

#### Points

Points are generally considered fundamental objects for building geometry. They may be defined by the properties that they must have, as in Euclid's definition as "that which has no part",[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-EuclidAll-45) or in [synthetic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthetic_geometry "Synthetic geometry"). In modern mathematics, they are generally defined as [elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Element_(set_theory) "Element (set theory)") of a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") called [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_(mathematics) "Space (mathematics)"), which is itself [axiomatically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiomatically "Axiomatically") defined.

With these modern definitions, every geometric shape is defined as a set of points; this is not the case in synthetic geometry, where a line is another fundamental object that is not viewed as the set of the points through which it passes.

However, there are modern geometries in which points are not primitive objects, or even without points.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-46)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-47) One of the oldest such geometries is [Whitehead's point-free geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitehead%27s_point-free_geometry "Whitehead's point-free geometry"), formulated by [Alfred North Whitehead](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_North_Whitehead "Alfred North Whitehead") in 1919–1920.

#### Lines

[Euclid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid "Euclid") described a line as "breadthless length" which "lies equally with respect to the points on itself".[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-EuclidAll-45) In modern mathematics, given the multitude of geometries, the concept of a line is closely tied to the way the geometry is described. For instance, in [analytic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_geometry "Analytic geometry"), a line in the plane is often defined as the set of points whose coordinates satisfy a given [linear equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_equation "Linear equation"),[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-48) but in a more abstract setting, such as [incidence geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incidence_geometry "Incidence geometry"), a line may be an independent object, distinct from the set of points which lie on it.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-49) In differential geometry, a [geodesic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic "Geodesic") is a generalization of the notion of a line to [curved spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold").[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-50)

#### Planes

In Euclidean geometry a plane is a flat, two-dimensional surface that extends infinitely;[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-EuclidAll-45) the definitions for other types of geometries are generalizations of that. Planes are used in many areas of geometry. For instance, planes can be studied as a [topological surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(topology) "Surface (topology)") without reference to distances or angles;[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Munkres-51) it can be studied as an [affine space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_space "Affine space"), where collinearity and ratios can be studied but not distances;[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-52) it can be studied as the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane") using techniques of [complex analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis "Complex analysis");[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-53) and so on.

#### Curves

A [curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve_(geometry) "Curve (geometry)") is a 1-dimensional object that may be straight (like a line) or not; curves in 2-dimensional space are called [plane curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_curve "Plane curve") and those in 3-dimensional space are called [space curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_curve "Space curve").[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-54)

In topology, a curve is defined by a function from an interval of the real numbers to another space.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Munkres-51) In differential geometry, the same definition is used, but the defining function is required to be differentiable.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Carmo-55) Algebraic geometry studies [algebraic curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_curve "Algebraic curve"), which are defined as [algebraic varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_varieties "Algebraic varieties") of [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_of_an_algebraic_variety "Dimension of an algebraic variety") one.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-mumford-56)

#### Surfaces

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Sphere_wireframe.svg/250px-Sphere_wireframe.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Sphere_wireframe.svg)

A sphere is a surface that can be defined parametrically (by _x_ = _r_ sin _θ_ cos _φ_,_y_ = _r_ sin _θ_ sin _φ_,_z_ = _r_ cos _θ_) or implicitly (by _x_ 2 + _y_ 2 + _z_ 2 − _r_ 2 = 0).

A [surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(mathematics) "Surface (mathematics)") is a two-dimensional object, such as a sphere or paraboloid.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-57) In [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry")[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Carmo-55) and [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology"),[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Munkres-51) surfaces are described by two-dimensional 'patches' (or [neighborhoods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighborhood_(topology) "Neighborhood (topology)")) that are assembled by [diffeomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffeomorphism "Diffeomorphism") or [homeomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphism "Homeomorphism"), respectively. In algebraic geometry, surfaces are described by [polynomial equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_equation "Polynomial equation").[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-mumford-56)

#### Solids

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/cf/Blue_ball.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Blue_ball.png)

In [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), a ball is the volume bounded by a sphere.

A [solid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_(mathematics) "Solid (mathematics)") is a three-dimensional object bounded by a closed surface; for example, a [ball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ball_(mathematics) "Ball (mathematics)") is the volume bounded by a sphere.

#### Manifolds

A [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold") is a generalization of the concepts of curve and surface. In [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology"), a manifold is a [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space") where every point has a [neighborhood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighborhood_(topology) "Neighborhood (topology)") that is [homeomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphism "Homeomorphism") to Euclidean space.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Munkres-51) In [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), a [differentiable manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold") is a space where each neighborhood is [diffeomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffeomorphism "Diffeomorphism") to Euclidean space.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Carmo-55)

Manifolds are used extensively in physics, including in [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") and [string theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_theory "String theory").[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-58)

### Angles

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/Angle_obtuse_acute_straight.svg/250px-Angle_obtuse_acute_straight.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Angle_obtuse_acute_straight.svg)

Acute (a), obtuse (b), and straight (c) angles. The acute and obtuse angles are also known as oblique angles.

[Euclid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid "Euclid") defines a plane angle as the inclination to each other, in a plane, of two lines which meet each other, and do not lie straight with respect to each other.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-EuclidAll-45) In modern terms, an angle is the figure formed by two [rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ray_(geometry) "Ray (geometry)"), called the _sides_ of the angle, sharing a common endpoint, called the _[vertex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex\_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)")_ of the angle.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-59) The size of an angle is formalized as an [angular measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_measure "Angular measure").

In [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry"), angles are used to study [polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon "Polygon") and [triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle "Triangle"), as well as forming an object of study in their own right.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-EuclidAll-45) The study of the angles of a triangle or of angles in a [unit circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_circle "Unit circle") forms the basis of [trigonometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonometry "Trigonometry").[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-60)

In [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry") and [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"), the angles between [plane curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_curve "Plane curve") or [space curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_curve "Space curve") or [surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(geometry) "Surface (geometry)") can be calculated using the [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative_(calculus) "Derivative (calculus)").[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Stewart-61)[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-62)

### Measures: length, area, and volume

[Length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length "Length"), [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area"), and [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume") describe the size or extent of an object in one dimension, two dimension, and three dimensions respectively.

In [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry") and [analytic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_geometry "Analytic geometry"), the length of a line segment can often be calculated by the [Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem").[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Cannon2017-63)

Area and volume can be defined as fundamental quantities separate from length, or they can be described and calculated in terms of lengths in a plane or 3-dimensional space. Mathematicians have found many explicit [formulas for area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area#List_of_formulas "Area") and [formulas for volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume#Formulas "Volume") of various geometric objects. In [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus"), area and volume can be defined in terms of [integrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral"), such as the [Riemann integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_integral "Riemann integral")[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Strang1991-64) or the [Lebesgue integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_integral "Lebesgue integral").[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Bear2002-65)

Other geometrical measures include the [curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature "Curvature") and [compactness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compactness_measure "Compactness measure").

#### Metrics and measures

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c3/Chinese_pythagoras.jpg/250px-Chinese_pythagoras.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Chinese_pythagoras.jpg)

Visual checking of the [Pythagorean theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagorean_theorem "Pythagorean theorem") for the (3, 4, 5) [triangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle "Triangle") as in the [Zhoubi Suanjing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhoubi_Suanjing "Zhoubi Suanjing") 500–200 BC. The Pythagorean theorem is a consequence of the [Euclidean metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_metric "Euclidean metric").

The concept of length or distance can be generalized, leading to the idea of [metrics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space").[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-66) For instance, the [Euclidean metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_metric "Euclidean metric") measures the distance between points in the [Euclidean plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_plane "Euclidean plane"), while the [hyperbolic metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_metric "Hyperbolic metric") measures the distance in the [hyperbolic plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_plane "Hyperbolic plane"). Other important examples of metrics include the [Lorentz metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_metric "Lorentz metric") of [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity") and the semi-[Riemannian metrics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_metric "Riemannian metric") of [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity").[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-67)

In a different direction, the concepts of length, area and volume are extended by [measure theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measure_theory "Measure theory"), which studies methods of assigning a size or _measure_ to [sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)"), where the measures follow rules similar to those of classical area and volume.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Tao2011-68)

### Congruence and similarity

[Congruence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_(geometry) "Congruence (geometry)") and [similarity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Similarity_(geometry) "Similarity (geometry)") are concepts that describe when two shapes have similar characteristics.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Libeskind2008-69) In Euclidean geometry, similarity is used to describe objects that have the same shape, while congruence is used to describe objects that are the same in both size and shape.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Freitag2013-70)[Hilbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert "Hilbert"), in his work on creating a more rigorous foundation for geometry, treated congruence as an undefined term whose properties are defined by [axioms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axiom "Axiom").

Congruence and similarity are generalized in [transformation geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformation_geometry "Transformation geometry"), which studies the properties of geometric objects that are preserved by different kinds of transformations.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Martin2012-71)

### Compass and straightedge constructions

Classical geometers paid special attention to constructing geometric objects that had been described in some other way. Classically, the only instruments used in most geometric constructions are the [compass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compass_(drafting) "Compass (drafting)") and [straightedge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruler "Ruler").[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-72) Also, every construction had to be complete in a finite number of steps. However, some problems turned out to be difficult or impossible to solve by these means alone, and ingenious constructions using [neusis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neusis_construction "Neusis construction"), parabolas and other curves, or mechanical devices, were found.

### Rotation and orientation

The geometrical concepts of rotation and orientation define part of the placement of objects embedded in the plane or in space.

### Dimension

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Von_Koch_curve.gif/250px-Von_Koch_curve.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Von_Koch_curve.gif)

The [Koch snowflake](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Koch_snowflake "Koch snowflake"), with [fractal dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_dimension "Fractal dimension")=log4/log3 and [topological dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_dimension "Topological dimension")=1

Traditional geometry allowed dimensions 1 (a [line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_(geometry) "Line (geometry)") or curve), 2 (a [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(mathematics) "Plane (mathematics)") or surface), and 3 (our ambient world conceived of as [three-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space")). Furthermore, mathematicians and physicists have used [higher dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higher_dimension "Higher dimension") for nearly two centuries.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Blacklock2018-73) One example of a mathematical use for higher dimensions is the [configuration space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(physics) "Configuration space (physics)") of a physical system, which has a dimension equal to the system's [degrees of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom "Degrees of freedom"). For instance, the configuration of a screw can be described by five coordinates.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Joly1895-74)

In [general topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_topology "General topology"), the concept of dimension has been extended from [natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number"), to infinite dimension ([Hilbert spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert_space "Hilbert space"), for example) and positive [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") (in [fractal geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fractal_geometry "Fractal geometry")).[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Temam2013-75) In [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), the [dimension of an algebraic variety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_of_an_algebraic_variety "Dimension of an algebraic variety") has received a number of apparently different definitions, which are all equivalent in the most common cases.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-JacobLam1994-76)

### Symmetry

[![Image 9](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e6/Order-3_heptakis_heptagonal_tiling.png/250px-Order-3_heptakis_heptagonal_tiling.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Order-3_heptakis_heptagonal_tiling.png)

A [tiling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order-3_bisected_heptagonal_tiling "Order-3 bisected heptagonal tiling") of the [hyperbolic plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry "Hyperbolic geometry")

The theme of [symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry "Symmetry") in geometry is nearly as old as the science of geometry itself.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Stewart2008-77) Symmetric shapes such as the [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle"), [regular polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon") and [platonic solids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platonic_solid "Platonic solid") held deep significance for many ancient philosophers[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Alexey2009-78) and were investigated in detail before the time of Euclid.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Hartshorne2013-41) Symmetric patterns occur in nature and were artistically rendered in a multitude of forms, including the graphics of [Leonardo da Vinci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci "Leonardo da Vinci"), [M. C. Escher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M._C._Escher "M. C. Escher"), and others.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Hahn1998-79) In the second half of the 19th century, the relationship between symmetry and geometry came under intense scrutiny. [Felix Klein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein "Felix Klein")'s [Erlangen program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erlangen_program "Erlangen program") proclaimed that, in a very precise sense, symmetry, expressed via the notion of a transformation [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)"), determines what geometry _is_.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Cantwell2002-80) Symmetry in classical [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry") is represented by [congruences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_(geometry) "Congruence (geometry)") and rigid motions, whereas in [projective geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_geometry "Projective geometry") an analogous role is played by [collineations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collineation "Collineation"), [geometric transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_transformation "Geometric transformation") that take straight lines into straight lines.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-RosenfeldWiebe2013-81) However it was in the new geometries of Bolyai and Lobachevsky, Riemann, [Clifford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Kingdon_Clifford "William Kingdon Clifford") and Klein, and [Sophus Lie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sophus_Lie "Sophus Lie") that Klein's idea to 'define a geometry via its [symmetry group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_group "Symmetry group")' found its inspiration.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Pesic2007-82) Both discrete and continuous symmetries play prominent roles in geometry, the former in [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") and [geometric group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_group_theory "Geometric group theory"),[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Kaku2012-83)[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-BestvinaSageev2014-84) the latter in [Lie theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_theory "Lie theory") and [Riemannian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_geometry "Riemannian geometry").[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Steeb1996-85)[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Misner2005-86)

A different type of symmetry is the principle of [duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duality_(projective_geometry) "Duality (projective geometry)") in [projective geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_geometry "Projective geometry"), among other fields. This meta-phenomenon can roughly be described as follows: in any [theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theorem "Theorem"), exchange _point_ with _plane_, _join_ with _meet_, _lies in_ with _contains_, and the result is an equally true theorem.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Dowling1917-87) A similar and closely related form of duality exists between a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") and its [dual space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_space "Dual space").[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Gierz2006-88)

Contemporary geometry
---------------------

### Euclidean geometry

[Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry") is geometry in its classical sense.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-ButtsBrown2012-89) As it models the space of the physical world, it is used in many scientific areas, such as [mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics "Mechanics"), [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy"), [crystallography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography "Crystallography"),[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-90) and many technical fields, such as [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering"),[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Abbot2013-91)[architecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Architecture "Architecture"),[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-HerseyHersey2001-92)[geodesy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesy "Geodesy"),[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Van%C3%ADcekKrakiwsky2015-93)[aerodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerodynamics "Aerodynamics"),[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-CummingsMorton2015-94) and [navigation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Navigation "Navigation").[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Williams1998-95) The mandatory educational curriculum of the majority of nations includes the study of Euclidean concepts such as [points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)"), [lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_(geometry) "Line (geometry)"), [planes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(mathematics) "Plane (mathematics)"), [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle"), [triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangle "Triangle"), [congruence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_(geometry) "Congruence (geometry)"), [similarity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Similarity_(geometry) "Similarity (geometry)"), [solid figures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_figure "Solid figure"), [circles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle"), and [analytic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_geometry "Analytic geometry").[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Schmidt,_W._2002-96)

#### Euclidean vectors

Euclidean vectors are used for a myriad of applications in physics and engineering, such as [position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_(geometry) "Position (geometry)"), [displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Displacement_(geometry) "Displacement (geometry)"), [deformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deformation_(physics) "Deformation (physics)"), [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity"), [acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration"), [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force"), etc.

### Differential geometry

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/89/Hyperbolic_triangle.svg/250px-Hyperbolic_triangle.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hyperbolic_triangle.svg)

[Differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry") uses tools from [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus") to study problems involving curvature.

[Differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry") uses techniques of [calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus") and [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra") to study problems in geometry.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Walschap2015-97) It has applications in [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"),[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Flanders2012-98)[econometrics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Econometrics "Econometrics"),[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-MarriottSalmon2000-99) and [bioinformatics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bioinformatics "Bioinformatics"),[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-HePetoukhov2011-100) among others.

In particular, differential geometry is of importance to [mathematical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_physics "Mathematical physics") due to [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein")'s [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") postulation that the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe") is [curved](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature "Curvature").[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Dirac2016-101) Differential geometry can either be _intrinsic_ (meaning that the spaces it considers are [smooth manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_manifold "Smooth manifold") whose geometric structure is governed by a [Riemannian metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_metric "Riemannian metric"), which determines how distances are measured near each point) or _extrinsic_ (where the object under study is a part of some ambient flat Euclidean space).[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-AyJost2017-102)

#### Non-Euclidean geometry

### Topology

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Trefoil_knot_arb.png/250px-Trefoil_knot_arb.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Trefoil_knot_arb.png)

A thickening of the [trefoil knot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trefoil_knot "Trefoil knot")

Topology is the field concerned with the properties of [continuous mappings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_mapping "Continuous mapping"),[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Crossley2011-103) and can be considered a generalization of Euclidean geometry.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-NashSen1988-104) In practice, topology often means dealing with large-scale properties of spaces, such as [connectedness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connectedness "Connectedness") and [compactness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_(topology) "Compact (topology)").[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Munkres-51)

The field of topology, which saw massive development in the 20th century, is in a technical sense a type of [transformation geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformation_geometry "Transformation geometry"), in which transformations are [homeomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphism "Homeomorphism").[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Martin1996-105) This has often been expressed in the form of the saying 'topology is rubber-sheet geometry'. Subfields of topology include [geometric topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_topology "Geometric topology"), [differential topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_topology "Differential topology"), [algebraic topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_topology "Algebraic topology") and [general topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_topology "General topology").[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-May1999-106)

### Algebraic geometry

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Calabi_yau.jpg/250px-Calabi_yau.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Calabi_yau.jpg)

Quintic [Calabi–Yau threefold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabi%E2%80%93Yau_manifold "Calabi–Yau manifold")

Algebraic geometry is fundamentally the study by means of [algebraic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra "Algebra") methods of some geometrical shapes, called [algebraic sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_set "Algebraic set"), and defined as common [zeros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_of_a_function "Zero of a function") of [multivariate polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivariate_polynomial "Multivariate polynomial").[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-AHartshorne2013-107) Algebraic geometry became an autonomous subfield of geometry c. 1900, with a theorem called [Hilbert's Nullstellensatz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hilbert%27s_Nullstellensatz "Hilbert's Nullstellensatz") that establishes a strong correspondence between algebraic sets and [ideals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_(ring_theory) "Ideal (ring theory)") of [polynomial rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_ring "Polynomial ring"). This led to a parallel development of algebraic geometry, and its algebraic counterpart, called [commutative algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_algebra "Commutative algebra").[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Dieudonne1985-108) From the late 1950s through the mid-1970s algebraic geometry had undergone major foundational development, with the introduction by [Alexander Grothendieck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Grothendieck "Alexander Grothendieck") of [scheme theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scheme_theory "Scheme theory"), which allows using [topological methods](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_topology "Algebraic topology"), including [cohomology theories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cohomology_theory "Cohomology theory") in a purely algebraic context.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Dieudonne1985-108) Scheme theory allowed to solve many difficult problems not only in geometry, but also in [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory"). [Wiles' proof of Fermat's Last Theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wiles%27_proof_of_Fermat%27s_Last_Theorem "Wiles' proof of Fermat's Last Theorem") is a famous example of a long-standing problem of [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory") whose solution uses scheme theory and its extensions such as [stack theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stack_(mathematics) "Stack (mathematics)"). One of seven [Millennium Prize problems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millennium_Prize_problems "Millennium Prize problems"), the [Hodge conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_conjecture "Hodge conjecture"), is a question in algebraic geometry.[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-CarlsonCarlson2006-109)

Algebraic geometry has applications in many areas, including [cryptography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptography "Cryptography")[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-HoweLauter2017-110) and [string theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_theory "String theory").[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-MarinoThaddeus2008-111)

### Complex geometry

[Complex geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_geometry "Complex geometry") studies the nature of geometric structures modelled on, or arising out of, the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane").[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-112)[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-113)[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-114) Complex geometry lies at the intersection of differential geometry, algebraic geometry, and analysis of [several complex variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Several_complex_variables "Several complex variables"), and has found applications to [string theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_theory "String theory") and [mirror symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mirror_symmetry_(string_theory) "Mirror symmetry (string theory)").[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-115)

Complex geometry first appeared as a distinct area of study in the work of [Bernhard Riemann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernhard_Riemann "Bernhard Riemann") in his study of [Riemann surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_surface "Riemann surface").[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-116)[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-117)[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-118) Work in the spirit of Riemann was carried out by the [Italian school of algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_school_of_algebraic_geometry "Italian school of algebraic geometry") in the early 1900s. Contemporary treatment of complex geometry began with the work of [Jean-Pierre Serre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Pierre_Serre "Jean-Pierre Serre"), who introduced the concept of [sheaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheaf_(mathematics) "Sheaf (mathematics)") to the subject, and illuminated the relations between complex geometry and algebraic geometry.[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-119)[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-120) The primary objects of study in complex geometry are [complex manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_manifold "Complex manifold"), [complex algebraic varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_algebraic_varieties "Complex algebraic varieties"), and [complex analytic varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analytic_varieties "Complex analytic varieties"), and [holomorphic vector bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holomorphic_vector_bundles "Holomorphic vector bundles") and [coherent sheaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coherent_sheaves "Coherent sheaves") over these spaces. Special examples of spaces studied in complex geometry include Riemann surfaces, and [Calabi–Yau manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calabi%E2%80%93Yau_manifold "Calabi–Yau manifold"), and these spaces find uses in string theory. In particular, [worldsheets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Worldsheet "Worldsheet") of strings are modelled by Riemann surfaces, and [superstring theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superstring_theory "Superstring theory") predicts that the extra 6 dimensions of 10 dimensional [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") may be modelled by Calabi–Yau manifolds.

### Discrete geometry

[![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2e/Closepacking.svg/250px-Closepacking.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Closepacking.svg)

Discrete geometry includes the study of various [sphere packings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere_packing "Sphere packing").

[Discrete geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_geometry "Discrete geometry") is a subject that has close connections with [convex geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_geometry "Convex geometry").[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Matou%C5%A1ek2013-121)[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Zong2006-122)[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Gruber2007-123) It is concerned mainly with questions of relative position of simple geometric objects, such as points, lines and circles. Examples include the study of [sphere packings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere_packing "Sphere packing"), [triangulations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangulation_(geometry) "Triangulation (geometry)"), the Kneser-Poulsen conjecture, etc.[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-DevadossO'Rourke2011-124)[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Bezdek2010-125) It shares many methods and principles with [combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combinatorics "Combinatorics").

### Computational geometry

[Computational geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computational_geometry "Computational geometry") deals with [algorithms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algorithm "Algorithm") and their [implementations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Implementation_(computer_science) "Implementation (computer science)") for manipulating geometrical objects. Important problems historically have included the [travelling salesman problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Travelling_salesman_problem "Travelling salesman problem"), [minimum spanning trees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimum_spanning_tree "Minimum spanning tree"), [hidden-line removal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hidden-line_removal "Hidden-line removal"), and [linear programming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_programming "Linear programming").[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-PreparataShamos2012-126)

Although being a young area of geometry, it has many applications in [computer vision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_vision "Computer vision"), [image processing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_processing "Image processing"), [computer-aided design](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer-aided_design "Computer-aided design"), [medical imaging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medical_imaging "Medical imaging"), etc.[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-GuYau2008-127)

### Geometric group theory

[![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d2/Cayley_graph_of_F2.svg/250px-Cayley_graph_of_F2.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cayley_graph_of_F2.svg)

The Cayley graph of the [free group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_group "Free group") on two generators _a_ and _b_

Groups have been understood as geometric objects since [Klein's Erlangen programme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erlangen_program "Erlangen program"). [Geometric group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_group_theory "Geometric group theory") studies [group actions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_action "Group action") on objects that are regarded as geometric (significantly, isometric actions on [metric spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space")) to study [finitely generated groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finitely_generated_group "Finitely generated group"), often involving large-scale geometric techniques[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-L%C3%B6h2017-128) and borrowing from topology, geometry, dynamics and analysis.[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-129) It had a significant impact on [low-dimensional topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low-dimensional_topology "Low-dimensional topology"), a celebrated result being Agol's proof of the [virtually Haken conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtually_Haken_conjecture "Virtually Haken conjecture") that combines [Perelman geometrization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrization_conjecture "Geometrization conjecture") with [cubulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubical_complex "Cubical complex") techniques.[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-130)

Group actions on their [Cayley graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cayley_graph "Cayley graph") are foundational examples of isometric group actions. Other major topics include [quasi-isometries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasi-isometry "Quasi-isometry"), [Gromov-hyperbolic groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gromov-hyperbolic_group "Gromov-hyperbolic group") and their generalizations ([relatively](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relatively_hyperbolic_group "Relatively hyperbolic group") and [acylindrically hyperbolic groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acylindrically_hyperbolic_group "Acylindrically hyperbolic group")), [free groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_group "Free group") and [their automorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Out(Fn) "Out(Fn)"), [groups acting on trees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bass%E2%80%93Serre_theory "Bass–Serre theory"), various notions of nonpositive curvature for groups ([CAT(0) groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CAT(0)_group "CAT(0) group"), [Dehn functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dehn_function "Dehn function"), [automaticity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automatic_group "Automatic group")...), [right angled Artin groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angled_Artin_group "Right angled Artin group"), and topics close to [combinatorial group theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combinatorial_group_theory "Combinatorial group theory") such as [small cancellation theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_cancellation_theory "Small cancellation theory") and algorithmic problems (e.g. the [word](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word_problem_for_groups "Word problem for groups"), [conjugacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjugacy_problem "Conjugacy problem"), and [isomorphism problems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_isomorphism_problem "Group isomorphism problem")). Other group-theoretic topics like [mapping class groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mapping_class_group_of_a_surface "Mapping class group of a surface"), [property (T)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kazhdan%27s_property_(T) "Kazhdan's property (T)"), [solvability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvable_group "Solvable group"), [amenability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amenable_group "Amenable group") and [lattices in Lie groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lattice_(discrete_subgroup) "Lattice (discrete subgroup)") are sometimes regarded as strongly geometric as well.[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-L%C3%B6h2017-128)[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Wise2012-131)[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-132)[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-133)

### Convex geometry

[Convex geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_geometry "Convex geometry") investigates [convex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_set "Convex set") shapes in the Euclidean space and its more abstract analogues, often using techniques of [real analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_analysis "Real analysis") and [discrete mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_mathematics "Discrete mathematics").[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Meurant2014-134) It has close connections to [convex analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_analysis "Convex analysis"), [optimization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optimization "Optimization") and [functional analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_analysis "Functional analysis") and important applications in [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory").

Convex geometry dates back to antiquity.[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Meurant2014-134)[Archimedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archimedes "Archimedes") gave the first known precise definition of convexity. The [isoperimetric problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isoperimetric_problem "Isoperimetric problem"), a recurring concept in convex geometry, was studied by the Greeks as well, including [Zenodorus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zenodorus_(mathematician) "Zenodorus (mathematician)"). Archimedes, [Plato](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plato "Plato"), [Euclid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid "Euclid"), and later [Kepler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler "Kepler") and [Coxeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter "Coxeter") all studied [convex polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polytope "Convex polytope") and their properties. From the 19th century on, mathematicians have studied other areas of convex mathematics, including higher-dimensional polytopes, volume and surface area of convex bodies, [Gaussian curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_curvature "Gaussian curvature"), [algorithms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algorithms "Algorithms"), [tilings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tiling_(geometry) "Tiling (geometry)") and [lattices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lattice_(group) "Lattice (group)").

Applications
------------

Geometry has found applications in many fields, some of which are described below.

### Art

[![Image 15](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b5/Fes_Medersa_Bou_Inania_Mosaique2.jpg/250px-Fes_Medersa_Bou_Inania_Mosaique2.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Fes_Medersa_Bou_Inania_Mosaique2.jpg)

Bou Inania Madrasa, Fes, Morocco, zellige mosaic tiles forming elaborate geometric tessellations

Mathematics and art are related in a variety of ways. For instance, the theory of [perspective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perspective_(graphical) "Perspective (graphical)") showed that there is more to geometry than just the metric properties of figures: perspective is the origin of [projective geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_geometry "Projective geometry").[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Richter-Gebert2011-135)

Artists have long used concepts of [proportion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportionality_(mathematics) "Proportionality (mathematics)") in design. [Vitruvius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitruvius "Vitruvius") developed a complicated theory of _ideal proportions_ for the human figure.[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Elam2001-136) These concepts have been used and adapted by artists from [Michelangelo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michelangelo "Michelangelo") to modern comic book artists.[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Guigar2004-137)

The [golden ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_ratio "Golden ratio") is a particular proportion that has had a controversial role in art. Often claimed to be the most aesthetically pleasing ratio of lengths, it is frequently stated to be incorporated into famous works of art, though the most reliable and unambiguous examples were made deliberately by artists aware of this legend.[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Livio2008-138)

[Tilings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tiling_(geometry) "Tiling (geometry)"), or tessellations, have been used in art throughout history. [Islamic art](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_art "Islamic art") makes frequent use of tessellations, as did the art of [M. C. Escher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M._C._Escher "M. C. Escher").[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-EmmerSchattschneider2007-139) Escher's work also made use of [hyperbolic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry "Hyperbolic geometry").

[Cézanne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C%C3%A9zanne "Cézanne") advanced the theory that all images can be built up from the [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere"), the [cone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cone "Cone"), and the [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder "Cylinder"). This is still used in art theory today, although the exact list of shapes varies from author to author.[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-CapitoloSchwab2004-140)[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Gelineau2011-141)

### Architecture

Geometry has many applications in architecture. In fact, it has been said that geometry lies at the core of architectural design.[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-CeccatoHesselgren2016-142)[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Pottmann2007-143) Applications of geometry to architecture include the use of [projective geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_geometry "Projective geometry") to create [forced perspective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Forced_perspective "Forced perspective"),[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-MoffettFazio2003-144) the use of [conic sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conic_section "Conic section") in constructing domes and similar objects,[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-HerseyHersey2001-92) the use of [tessellations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tessellations "Tessellations"),[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-HerseyHersey2001-92) and the use of symmetry.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-HerseyHersey2001-92)

### Physics

The field of [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy"), especially as it relates to mapping the positions of [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star") and [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet") on the [celestial sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_sphere "Celestial sphere") and describing the relationship between movements of celestial bodies, have served as an important source of geometric problems throughout history.[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-GreenGreen1985-145)

[Riemannian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_geometry "Riemannian geometry") and [pseudo-Riemannian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian "Pseudo-Riemannian") geometry are used in [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity").[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Alekseevski%C4%AD2008-146)[String theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_theory "String theory") makes use of several variants of geometry,[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-YauNadis2010-147) as does [quantum information theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_information_theory "Quantum information theory").[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-148)

### Other fields of mathematics

[![Image 16](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a6/Square_root_of_2_triangle.svg/250px-Square_root_of_2_triangle.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Square_root_of_2_triangle.svg)

The Pythagoreans discovered that the sides of a triangle could have [incommensurable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commensurability_(mathematics) "Commensurability (mathematics)") lengths.

[Calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus "Calculus") was strongly influenced by geometry.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Boyer2012-32) For instance, the introduction of [coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinates "Coordinates") by [René Descartes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes "René Descartes") and the concurrent developments of [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra "Algebra") marked a new stage for geometry, since geometric figures such as [plane curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_curve "Plane curve") could now be represented [analytically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_geometry "Analytic geometry") in the form of functions and equations. This played a key role in the emergence of [infinitesimal calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinitesimal_calculus "Infinitesimal calculus") in the 17th century. Analytic geometry continues to be a mainstay of pre-calculus and calculus curriculum.[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-FlandersPrice2014-149)[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-RogawskiAdams2015-150)

Another important area of application is [number theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_theory "Number theory").[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Lozano-Robledo2019-151) In [ancient Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece") the [Pythagoreans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pythagoreans "Pythagoreans") considered the role of numbers in geometry. However, the discovery of incommensurable lengths contradicted their philosophical views.[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-Sangalli2009-152) Since the 19th century, geometry has been used for solving problems in number theory, for example through the [geometry of numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry_of_numbers "Geometry of numbers") or, more recently, [scheme theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scheme_theory "Scheme theory"), which is used in [Wiles's proof of Fermat's Last Theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wiles%27s_proof_of_Fermat%27s_Last_Theorem "Wiles's proof of Fermat's Last Theorem").[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry#cite_note-CornellSilverman2013-153)

For equations of higher degree than three, Omar Khayyam evidently did not envision similar geometric methods, for space does not contain more than three dimensions, ... The decisive step in this direction came much later with Descartes, but Omar Khayyam was moving in this direction when he wrote, "Whoever thinks algebra is a trick in obtaining unknowns has thought it in vain. No attention should be paid to the fact that algebra and geometry are different in appearance. Algebras are geometric facts which are proved."".
