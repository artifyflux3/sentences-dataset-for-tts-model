The **_Encyclopædia Britannica_ Eleventh Edition** (1910–1911) is a 29-volume reference work, an edition of the _[Encyclopædia Britannica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia\_Britannica "Encyclopædia Britannica")_. It was developed during the encyclopaedia's transition from a British to an American publication. Some of its articles were written by the best-known scholars of the time. This edition of the encyclopaedia, containing 40,000 entries, has entered the [public domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_domain "Public domain") and is readily available on the Internet. Its use in modern scholarship and as a reliable source has been deemed problematic due to the outdated nature of some of its content.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-1) Nevertheless, the 11th edition has retained considerable value as a time capsule of scientific and historical information, as well as scholarly attitudes of the era immediately preceding [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I").

_Encyclopædia Britannica_ Eleventh Edition

The 1911 eleventh edition was assembled with the management of American publisher [Horace Everett Hooper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Horace_Everett_Hooper "Horace Everett Hooper"). [Hugh Chisholm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hugh_Chisholm "Hugh Chisholm"), who had edited the previous edition, was appointed editor-in-chief, with [Walter Alison Phillips](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Alison_Phillips "Walter Alison Phillips") as his principal assistant editor.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-2)

Originally, Hooper bought the rights to the 25-volume [9th edition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Ninth_Edition "Encyclopædia Britannica Ninth Edition") and persuaded the British newspaper _[The Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Times "The Times")_ to issue its reprint, with eleven additional volumes (35 volumes total) as the tenth edition, which was published in 1902. Hooper's association with _The Times_ ceased in 1909, and he negotiated with the [Cambridge University Press](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cambridge_University_Press "Cambridge University Press") to publish the 29-volume eleventh edition. Though it is generally perceived as a quintessentially British work, the eleventh edition had substantial American influences, in not only the increased amount of American and Canadian content, but also the efforts made to make it more popular.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-3) American marketing methods also assisted sales. Some 14% of the contributors (214 of 1507) were from North America, and a New York office was established to coordinate their work.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-4)

The initials of the encyclopaedia's contributors appear at the end of selected articles or at the end of a section in the case of longer articles, such as that on China, and a key is given in each volume to these initials. Some articles were written by the best-known scholars of the time, such as [Edmund Gosse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edmund_Gosse "Edmund Gosse"), [J. B. Bury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._B._Bury "J. B. Bury"), [Algernon Charles Swinburne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algernon_Charles_Swinburne "Algernon Charles Swinburne"), [John Muir](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Muir "John Muir"), [Peter Kropotkin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Kropotkin "Peter Kropotkin"), [T. H. Huxley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T._H._Huxley "T. H. Huxley"), [James Hopwood Jeans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Hopwood_Jeans "James Hopwood Jeans") and [William Michael Rossetti](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Michael_Rossetti "William Michael Rossetti"). Among the then lesser-known contributors were some who would later become distinguished, such as [Ernest Rutherford](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernest_Rutherford "Ernest Rutherford") and [Bertrand Russell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bertrand_Russell "Bertrand Russell"). Many articles were carried over from the [9th edition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Ninth_Edition "Encyclopædia Britannica Ninth Edition"), some with minimal updating. Some of the book-length articles were divided into smaller parts for easier reference, yet others were much abridged. The best-known authors generally contributed only a single article or part of an article. Most of the work was done by journalists, [British Museum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Museum "British Museum") scholars and other scholars. The 1911 edition was the first edition of the encyclopaedia to include more than just a handful of female contributors, with 34 women contributing articles to the edition.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-thomas_1992-5) These included [Adelaide Anderson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adelaide_Anderson "Adelaide Anderson"), [Gertrude Bell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gertrude_Bell "Gertrude Bell"), [Margaret Bryant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Margaret_Bryant "Margaret Bryant"), [Constance Jocelyn Ffoulkes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constance_Jocelyn_Ffoulkes "Constance Jocelyn Ffoulkes"), [Harriette Lombard Hennessy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harriette_Lombard_Hennessy "Harriette Lombard Hennessy"), and [Eleanor Mildred Sidgwick](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eleanor_Mildred_Sidgwick "Eleanor Mildred Sidgwick").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-thomas_1992-5)

The eleventh edition introduced a number of changes of the format of the _Britannica_. It was the first to be published complete, instead of the previous method of volumes being released as they were ready. The [print type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Letterpress_printing "Letterpress printing") was kept in [galley proofs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galley_proof "Galley proof") and subject to continual updating until publication. It was the first edition of _Britannica_ to be issued with a comprehensive index volume in which was added a categorical index, where like topics were listed. It was the first not to include long treatise-length articles. Even though the overall length of the work was about the same as that of its predecessor, the number of articles had increased from 17,000 to 40,000. It was also the first edition of _Britannica_ to include biographies of living people. Sixteen maps of the famous 9th edition of _[Stielers Handatlas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stielers\_Handatlas "Stielers Handatlas")_ were exclusively translated to English, converted to [imperial units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperial_units "Imperial units"), printed in [Gotha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gotha "Gotha"), Germany, by [Justus Perthes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Justus_Perthes "Justus Perthes") and the maps became a part of this edition. Later editions only included Perthes' maps as low-quality reproductions.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-6)

According to Coleman and Simmons,[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-Coleman_and_Simmons-7) the content of the encyclopaedia was distributed as follows:

| Subject | Content |
| --- | --- |
| [Geography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geography "Geography") | 29% |
| [Pure and applied science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science "Science") | 17% |
| History | 17% |
| Literature | 11% |
| Fine art | 9% |
| [Social science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_science "Social science") | 7% |
| [Psychology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychology "Psychology") | 1.7% |
| Philosophy | 0.8% |

Hooper sold the rights to [Sears, Roebuck and Company](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sears "Sears") of Chicago in 1920, completing the _Britannica_'s transition to becoming a substantially American publication.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-Stewart-8) In 1922, an additional three volumes (also edited by Hugh Chisholm) where published, covering the events of the intervening years, including [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I"). These, together with a reprint of the eleventh edition, formed the twelfth edition of the work. A similar thirteenth edition, consisting of three volumes plus a reprint of the twelfth edition, was published in 1926. The London editor was [J.L. Garvin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Louis_Garvin "James Louis Garvin"), as Chisholm had died.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-Stewart-8) The twelfth and thirteenth editions were closely related to the eleventh edition and shared much of the same content. However, it became increasingly apparent that a more thorough update of the work was required.

The fourteenth edition, published in 1929, was considerably revised, with much text eliminated or abridged to make room for new topics. Nevertheless, the eleventh edition was the basis of every later version of the _Encyclopædia Britannica_ until the completely new fifteenth edition was published in 1974, using modern information presentation.

The eleventh edition's articles are still of value and interest to modern readers and scholars, especially as a [cultural artifact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cultural_artifact "Cultural artifact"): the [British Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Empire "British Empire") was at its maximum, [imperialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imperialism "Imperialism") was largely unchallenged, much of the world was still ruled by monarchs, and the tumultuous [world wars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_war "World war") were still in the future. They are a resource for topics omitted from modern encyclopaedias, particularly for biography and the history of science and technology. As a literary text, the encyclopaedia has value as an example of early 20th-century prose. For example, it employs [literary devices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Literary_device "Literary device"), such as [pathetic fallacy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathetic_fallacy "Pathetic fallacy") (attribution of human-like traits to impersonal forces or inanimate objects), which are not as common in modern reference texts.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-Coleman_and_Simmons-7)

In 1917, using the pseudonym of S. S. Van Dine, the US art critic and author [Willard Huntington Wright](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Willard_Huntington_Wright "Willard Huntington Wright") published _[Misinforming a Nation](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Misinforming\_a\_Nation "s:Misinforming a Nation")_, a 200+ page criticism of inaccuracies and biases of the _Encyclopædia Britannica_ eleventh edition. Wright claimed that _Britannica_ was "characterized by misstatements, inexcusable omissions, rabid and patriotic prejudices, personal animosities, blatant errors of fact, scholastic ignorance, gross neglect of non-British culture, an astounding egotism, and an undisguised contempt for American progress".[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-9)

[Amos Urban Shirk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amos_Urban_Shirk "Amos Urban Shirk"), known for having read the eleventh and fourteenth editions in their entirety, said he found the fourteenth edition to be a "big improvement" over the eleventh, stating that "most of the material had been completely rewritten".

[Robert Collison](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Robert_Collison&action=edit&redlink=1 "Robert Collison (page does not exist)"), in _Encyclopaedias: Their History Throughout The Ages_ (1966), wrote of the eleventh edition that it "was probably the finest edition of the _Britannica_ ever issued, and it ranks with the _[Enciclopedia Italiana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enciclopedia\_Italiana "Enciclopedia Italiana")_ and the _[Espasa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enciclopedia\_universal\_ilustrada\_europeo-americana "Enciclopedia universal ilustrada europeo-americana")_ as one of the three greatest encyclopaedias. It was the last edition to be produced almost in its entirety in Britain, and its position in time as a summary of the world's knowledge just before the outbreak of World War I is particularly valuable".

Sir [Kenneth Clark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenneth_Clark "Kenneth Clark"), in _Another Part of the Wood_ (1974), wrote of the eleventh edition, "One leaps from one subject to another, fascinated as much by the play of mind and the [idiosyncrasies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idiosyncrasy "Idiosyncrasy") of their authors as by the facts and dates. It must be the last encyclopaedia in the tradition of [Diderot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denis_Diderot "Denis Diderot") which assumes that information can be made memorable only when it is slightly coloured by prejudice. When [T. S. Eliot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T._S._Eliot "T. S. Eliot") wrote 'Soul curled up on the window seat reading the _Encyclopædia Britannica_,' he was certainly thinking of the eleventh edition." (Clark refers to Eliot's 1929 poem "[Animula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T._S._Eliot%27s_Ariel_poems#%22Animula%22 "T. S. Eliot's Ariel poems")".) It was one of [Jorge Luis Borges](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jorge_Luis_Borges "Jorge Luis Borges")'s favourite works, and was a source of information and enjoyment for his entire working life.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-10)

In 1912, mathematician [L. C. Karpinski](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/L._C._Karpinski "L. C. Karpinski") criticised the eleventh edition for inaccuracies in articles on the [history of mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_mathematics "History of mathematics"), none of which had been written by specialists.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-11)

English writer and former priest [Joseph McCabe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_McCabe "Joseph McCabe") claimed in _Lies and Fallacies of the Encyclopædia Britannica_ (1947) that _Britannica_ was censored under pressure from the [Roman Catholic Church](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catholic_Church "Catholic Church") after the 11th edition.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-12) Initially, the eleventh edition received criticism from members of the Roman Catholic Church, who accused it of misrepresenting and being [biased against Catholics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-Catholicism "Anti-Catholicism").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-:1-13) The most "vociferous" American Catholic critics of the eleventh edition were editors of the [Christian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christians "Christians") magazine _[America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/America\_(magazine) "America (magazine)")_.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-:1-13)

Authorities ranging from [Virginia Woolf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virginia_Woolf "Virginia Woolf") to professors criticised the 11th edition for having [bourgeois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bourgeois "Bourgeois") and old-fashioned opinions on art, literature, and social sciences.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-thomas_1992-5) A contemporary [Cornell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cornell_University "Cornell University") professor, [Edward B. Titchener](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_B._Titchener "Edward B. Titchener"), wrote in 1912, "the new _Britannica_ does not reproduce the psychological atmosphere of its day and generation... Despite the halo of authority, and despite the scrutiny of the staff, the great bulk of the secondary articles in general psychology... are not adapted to the requirements of the intelligent reader".[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-14)

In an April 2012 article, Nate Pederson of _[The Guardian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Guardian "The Guardian")_ said that the eleventh edition represented "a peak of colonial optimism before the slaughter of war" and that the edition "has acquired an almost mythic reputation among collectors".[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-:0-15)

Critics have charged several editions with racism,[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-16)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-17)[sexism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sexism "Sexism"),[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-thomas_1992-5) and [antisemitism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antisemitism "Antisemitism").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-:0-15) The eleventh edition [characterises](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/1911_Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica/Ku_Klux_Klan "s:1911 Encyclopædia Britannica/Ku Klux Klan") the [Ku Klux Klan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ku_Klux_Klan "Ku Klux Klan") as protecting the white race and restoring order to the [American South](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confederate_States_of_America "Confederate States of America") after the [American Civil War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Civil_War "American Civil War"), citing the need to "control the negro", and "the frequent occurrence of the crime of rape by negro men upon white women".[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-18)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-19) Similarly, [the "Civilization" article](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/1911_Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica/Civilization "s:1911 Encyclopædia Britannica/Civilization") argues for [eugenics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eugenics "Eugenics"), stating that it is irrational to "propagate low orders of intelligence, to feed the ranks of paupers, defectives and criminals... which to-day constitute so threatening an obstacle to racial progress".[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-20) The eleventh edition has no biography of [Marie Curie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marie_Curie "Marie Curie"), despite her winning the [Nobel Prize in Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physics "Nobel Prize in Physics") in 1903 and the [Nobel Prize in Chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Chemistry "Nobel Prize in Chemistry") in 1911, although she is mentioned briefly under the biography of her husband [Pierre Curie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Curie "Pierre Curie").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-21) The _Britannica_ employed a large female editorial staff that wrote hundreds of articles for which they were not given credit.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-thomas_1992-5)

The 1911 edition is no longer restricted by [copyright](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copyright "Copyright"), and it is therefore freely available in several more modern forms. While it may once have been a reliable description of the academic consensus of its time,[_[according to whom?](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual\_of\_Style/Words\_to\_watch#Unsupported\_attributions "Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch")_] many modern readers find fault with the _Encyclopedia_ for several major errors, [ethnocentric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnocentrism "Ethnocentrism") and [racist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Racism "Racism") remarks, and other issues:

*   Contemporary opinions of [race](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Race_(human_categorization) "Race (human categorization)") and [ethnicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethnic_group "Ethnic group") are included in the _Encyclopædia_'s articles. For example, the entry for "[Negro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Negro "Negro")" states, "Mentally the negro is inferior to the white... the arrest or even deterioration of mental development [after adolescence] is no doubt very largely due to the fact that after puberty sexual matters take the first place in the negro's life and thoughts."[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-22) The article about the [American Revolutionary War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Revolutionary_War "American Revolutionary War") attributes the success of the United States in part to "a population mainly of good English blood and instincts".[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Eleventh_Edition#cite_note-23)
*   Many articles are now outdated factually, in particular those concerning science, technology, [international](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_law "International law") and [municipal law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Municipal_law "Municipal law"), and medicine. For example, the article on the vitamin deficiency disease [beriberi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beriberi "Beriberi") speculates that it is caused by a fungus, [vitamins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitamin "Vitamin") not having been discovered at the time.
*   Even where the facts might still be accurate, new information, theories and perspectives developed since 1911 have substantially changed the way the same facts might be interpreted. For example, the modern interpretation of the history of the [Visigoths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visigoths "Visigoths") is now very different from that of 1911; readers of the eleventh edition who want to know about the social customs and political life of the tribe and its warriors are told to look up the entry for their king, [Alaric I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alaric_I "Alaric I").

The eleventh edition of _Encyclopædia Britannica_ has become a commonly quoted source, both because of the reputation of the _Britannica_ and because it is now in the [public domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Public_domain "Public domain") and has been made available on the Internet. It has been used as a source by many modern projects, including Wikipedia and the _Project Gutenberg Encyclopedia_.
