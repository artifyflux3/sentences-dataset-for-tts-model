**Ammonia** is an [inorganic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inorganic "Inorganic")[chemical compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_compound "Chemical compound") of [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") and [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") with the [formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")[N](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen")[H](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen")3. A [stable binary hydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_compounds_of_hydrogen "Binary compounds of hydrogen") and the simplest [pnictogen hydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pnictogen_hydride "Pnictogen hydride"), ammonia is a colourless [gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas "Gas") with a distinctive pungent smell.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Myers-13) It is widely used in fertilizers, refrigerants, explosives, cleaning agents, and is a precursor for numerous chemicals.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Myers-13) Biologically, it is a common [nitrogenous waste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogenous_waste "Nitrogenous waste"), and it contributes significantly to the [nutritional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nutrition "Nutrition") needs of terrestrial organisms by serving as a precursor to [fertilisers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fertiliser "Fertiliser").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-14) Around 70% of ammonia produced industrially is used to make fertilisers[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-15) in various forms and composition, such as [urea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urea "Urea") and [diammonium phosphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diammonium_phosphate "Diammonium phosphate"). Ammonia in pure form is also applied directly into the soil.

Ammonia, either directly or indirectly, is also a building block for the synthesis of many chemicals. In many countries, it is classified as an [extremely hazardous substance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_extremely_hazardous_substances "List of extremely hazardous substances").[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Gov-16) Ammonia is toxic, causing damage to cells and tissues. For this reason it is excreted by most animals in the urine, in the form of dissolved urea.

Ammonia is produced biologically in a process called [nitrogen fixation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation "Nitrogen fixation"), but even more is generated industrially by the [Haber process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haber_process "Haber process"). The process helped revolutionize agriculture by providing cheap fertilizers. The global industrial production of ammonia in 2021 was 235 million tonnes.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-18) Industrial ammonia is transported by road in [tankers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tank_truck "Tank truck"), by rail in [tank wagons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tank_car "Tank car"), by sea in [gas carriers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_carrier "Gas carrier"), or in [cylinders](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bottled_gas "Bottled gas").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-19) Ammonia occurs in nature and has been detected in the interstellar medium.

Ammonia boils at −33.34°C (−28.012°F) at a pressure of one [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_atmosphere_(unit) "Standard atmosphere (unit)"), but the liquid can often be handled in the laboratory without external cooling. Household ammonia or [ammonium hydroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_hydroxide "Ammonium hydroxide") is a solution of ammonia in water.

The name _ammonia_ is derived from the name of the Egyptian deity _Amun_ (_Ammon_ in Greek) since priests and travelers of those temples would burn soils rich in ammonium chloride, which came from animal dung and urine.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Myers-13)[Pliny](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder "Pliny the Elder"), in Book XXXI of his [Natural History](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_History_(Pliny) "Natural History (Pliny)"), refers to a salt named _[hammoniacum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salammoniac "Salammoniac")_, so called because of the proximity of its source to the Temple of [Jupiter Amun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amun "Amun") ([Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language") Ἄμμων _Ammon_) in the Roman province of [Cyrenaica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crete_and_Cyrenaica "Crete and Cyrenaica").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-20) However, the description Pliny gives of the salt does not conform to the properties of [ammonium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_chloride "Ammonium chloride"). According to [Herbert Hoover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbert_Hoover "Herbert Hoover")'s commentary in his English translation of [Georgius Agricola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georgius_Agricola "Georgius Agricola")'s _[De re metallica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De\_re\_metallica "De re metallica")_, it is likely to have been common sea salt.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-21) In any case, that salt ultimately gave ammonia and [ammonium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium "Ammonium") compounds their name.

Natural occurrence (abiological)
--------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ammonia&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Natural occurrence (abiological)")]

Traces of ammonia/ammonium are found in rainwater. [Ammonium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_chloride "Ammonium chloride") ([sal ammoniac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sal_ammoniac "Sal ammoniac")), and [ammonium sulfate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_sulfate "Ammonium sulfate") are found in volcanic districts. Crystals of [ammonium bicarbonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_bicarbonate "Ammonium bicarbonate") have been found in [Patagonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patagonia "Patagonia")[guano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guano "Guano").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911861-22)

Ammonia is found throughout the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") on [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars"), [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter"), [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn"), [Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus "Uranus"), [Neptune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune "Neptune"), and [Pluto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pluto "Pluto"), among other places: on smaller, icy [bodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minor_planet "Minor planet") such as Pluto, ammonia can act as a geologically important antifreeze, as a mixture of water and ammonia can have a melting point as low as −100°C (−148°F; 173 K) if the ammonia concentration is high enough and thus allow such bodies to retain internal oceans and active geology at a far lower temperature than would be possible with water alone.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-23)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-24) Substances containing ammonia, or those that are similar to it, are called _ammoniacal_.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-25)

Solid NH 3

Two visible states of NH 3

Ammonia is a colourless [gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas "Gas") with a characteristically [pungent smell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pungency "Pungency"). It is [lighter than air](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lighter_than_air "Lighter than air"), its density being 0.589 times that of [air](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air "Air"). It is easily liquefied due to the strong [hydrogen bonding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_bond "Hydrogen bond") between molecules. Gaseous ammonia turns to a colourless [liquid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid "Liquid"), which [boils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boiling_point "Boiling point") at −33.1°C (−27.58°F), and [freezes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melting_point "Melting point") to colourless crystals[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911861-22) at −77.7°C (−107.86°F). Little data is available at very high temperatures and pressures, but the [liquid-vapor critical point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercritical_fluid "Supercritical fluid") occurs at 405 K and 11.35 MPa.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-26)

The crystal symmetry is cubic, [Pearson symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pearson_symbol "Pearson symbol") cP16, [space group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_group "Space group") P2 1 3 No.198, lattice constant 0.5125[nm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanometre "Nanometre").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-27)

[Liquid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid "Liquid") ammonia possesses strong [ionising](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion "Ion") powers reflecting its high [_ε_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dielectric_constant "Dielectric constant") of 22 at −35°C (−31°F).[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-28) Liquid ammonia has a very high [standard enthalpy change of vapourization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enthalpy_of_vaporization "Enthalpy of vaporization") (23.5[kJ/mol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/KJ/mol "KJ/mol");[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-29) for comparison, [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Properties_of_water "Properties of water")'s is 40.65 kJ/mol, methane 8.19 kJ/mol and [phosphine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphine "Phosphine") 14.6 kJ/mol) and can be transported in pressurized or refrigerated vessels; however, at [standard temperature and pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_temperature_and_pressure "Standard temperature and pressure") liquid anhydrous ammonia will vaporize.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-30)

Liquid ammonia is a widely studied nonaqueous ionising solvent. Its most conspicuous property is its ability to dissolve alkali metals to form highly coloured, electrically conductive solutions containing [solvated electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvated_electron "Solvated electron"). Apart from these remarkable solutions, much of the chemistry in liquid ammonia can be classified by analogy with related reactions in [aqueous solutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aqueous_solution "Aqueous solution"). Comparison of the physical properties of NH 3 with those of water shows NH 3 has the lower melting point, boiling point, density, [viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscosity "Viscosity"), [dielectric constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dielectric_constant "Dielectric constant") and [electrical conductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_conductivity "Electrical conductivity"). These differences are attributed at least in part to the weaker hydrogen bonding in NH 3. The ionic self-[dissociation constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissociation_constant "Dissociation constant") of liquid NH 3 at −50°C is about 10−33.

Liquid ammonia is an ionising solvent, although less so than water, and dissolves a range of ionic compounds, including many [nitrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrate "Nitrate"), [nitrites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrite "Nitrite"), [cyanides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanide "Cyanide"), [thiocyanates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thiocyanate "Thiocyanate"), [metal cyclopentadienyl complexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclopentadienyl_complex "Cyclopentadienyl complex") and [metal bis(trimethylsilyl)amides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal_bis(trimethylsilyl)amides "Metal bis(trimethylsilyl)amides").[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Stalke,_D.-2016-33) Most ammonium salts are soluble and act as acids in liquid ammonia solutions. The solubility of [halide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halide "Halide") salts increases from [fluoride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluoride "Fluoride") to [iodide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iodide "Iodide"). A saturated solution of [ammonium nitrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_nitrate "Ammonium nitrate") (**Divers' solution**, named after [Edward Divers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Divers "Edward Divers")) contains 0.83 mol solute per mole of ammonia and has a [vapour pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vapour_pressure "Vapour pressure") of less than 1 bar even at 25°C (77°F). However, few [oxyanion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxyanion "Oxyanion") salts with other cations dissolve.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-35)

Liquid ammonia will dissolve all of the [alkali metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkali_metal "Alkali metal") and other [electropositive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronegativity "Electronegativity") metals such as [Ca](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium "Calcium"),[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-36)[Sr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strontium "Strontium"), [Ba](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barium "Barium"), [Eu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europium "Europium") and [Yb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ytterbium "Ytterbium") (also [Mg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnesium "Magnesium") using an electrolytic process[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Combellas-2001-34)). At low concentrations (<0.06 mol/L), deep blue solutions are formed: these contain metal cations and [solvated electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvated_electron "Solvated electron"), free electrons that are surrounded by a cage of ammonia molecules.

These solutions are strong reducing agents. At higher concentrations, the solutions are metallic in appearance and in electrical conductivity. At low temperatures, the two types of solution can coexist as [immiscible](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/immiscible "wiktionary:immiscible") phases.

The range of thermodynamic stability of liquid ammonia solutions is very narrow, as the potential for oxidation to dinitrogen, [_E_°](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_electrode_potential "Standard electrode potential") (N 2 + 6 [NH 4]+ + 6 e− ⇌ 8 NH 3), is only +0.04 V. In practice, both oxidation to dinitrogen and reduction to dihydrogen are slow. This is particularly true of reducing solutions: the solutions of the alkali metals mentioned above are stable for several days, slowly decomposing to the [metal amide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amide "Amide") and dihydrogen. Most studies involving liquid ammonia solutions are done in reducing conditions; although oxidation of liquid ammonia is usually slow, there is still a risk of explosion, particularly if [transition metal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transition_metal "Transition metal") ions are present as possible catalysts.

[![Image 25](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5c/NH3-Dipole-Moment.png/250px-NH3-Dipole-Moment.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:NH3-Dipole-Moment.png)

Molecular structure of ammonia and its three-dimensional shape. It has a net dipole moment of 1.484[D](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debye "Debye").

[![Image 26](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/08/Ammonia-2D-dot-cross.svg/250px-Ammonia-2D-dot-cross.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ammonia-2D-dot-cross.svg)

[Dot and cross](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_and_cross_diagram "Dot and cross diagram") structure of ammonia

The ammonia molecule has a [trigonal pyramidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trigonal_pyramidal "Trigonal pyramidal") shape, as predicted by the [valence shell electron pair repulsion theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Valence_shell_electron_pair_repulsion_theory "Valence shell electron pair repulsion theory") (VSEPR theory) with an experimentally determined bond angle of 106.7°.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-CRC-2013-37) The central nitrogen atom has five outer electrons with an additional electron from each hydrogen atom. This gives a total of eight electrons, or four electron pairs that are arranged [tetrahedrally](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron "Tetrahedron"). Three of these [electron pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_pair "Electron pair") are used as bond pairs, which leaves one [lone pair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lone_pair "Lone pair") of electrons. The lone pair repels more strongly than bond pairs; therefore, the bond angle is not 109.5°, as expected for a regular tetrahedral arrangement, but 106.7°.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-CRC-2013-37) This shape gives the molecule a [dipole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dipole "Dipole") moment and makes it [polar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar_molecule "Polar molecule"). The molecule's polarity, and especially its ability to form [hydrogen bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_bond "Hydrogen bond"), makes ammonia highly miscible with water. The lone pair makes ammonia a [base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_(chemistry) "Base (chemistry)"), a proton acceptor. Ammonia is moderately basic; a 1.0[M](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molar_concentration "Molar concentration")[aqueous solution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aqueous_solution "Aqueous solution") has a [pH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PH "PH") of 11.6, and if a strong acid is added to such a solution until the solution is neutral (pH = 7), 99.4% of the ammonia molecules are [protonated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonated "Protonated"). Temperature and [salinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salinity "Salinity") also affect the proportion of [ammonium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium "Ammonium")[NH 4]+. The latter has the shape of a regular [tetrahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahedron "Tetrahedron") and is [isoelectronic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isoelectronic "Isoelectronic") with [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane").

The ammonia molecule readily undergoes [nitrogen inversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_inversion "Nitrogen inversion") at room temperature; a useful analogy is an [umbrella](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Umbrella "Umbrella") turning itself inside out in a strong wind. The [energy barrier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_barrier "Energy barrier") to this inversion is 24.7 kJ/mol, and the [resonance frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resonance_frequency "Resonance frequency") is 23.79[GHz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GHz "GHz"), corresponding to [microwave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave "Microwave") radiation of a [wavelength](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength "Wavelength") of 1.260 cm. The absorption at this frequency was the first [microwave spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave_spectroscopy "Microwave spectroscopy") to be observed[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Cleeton-1934-38) and was used in the first [maser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maser "Maser").

One of the most characteristic properties of ammonia is its [basicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basicity "Basicity"). Ammonia is considered to be a weak base. It combines with [acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid "Acid") to form [ammonium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium "Ammonium")[salts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt_(chemistry) "Salt (chemistry)"); thus, with [hydrochloric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid "Hydrochloric acid") it forms [ammonium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_chloride "Ammonium chloride") (sal ammoniac); with [nitric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitric_acid "Nitric acid"), [ammonium nitrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_nitrate "Ammonium nitrate"), etc. Perfectly dry ammonia gas will not combine with perfectly dry [hydrogen chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride "Hydrogen chloride") gas; moisture is necessary to bring about the reaction.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911862-39)[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-40)

As a demonstration experiment under air with ambient moisture, opened bottles of concentrated ammonia and [hydrochloric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid "Hydrochloric acid") solutions produce a cloud of [ammonium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_chloride "Ammonium chloride"), which seems to appear 'out of nothing' as the salt [aerosol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aerosol "Aerosol") forms where the two [diffusing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffusion "Diffusion") clouds of reagents meet between the two bottles.

NH 3 + HCl → [NH 4]Cl

The salts produced by the action of ammonia on acids are known as the [ammonium salts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category:Ammonium_compounds "Category:Ammonium compounds") and all contain the [ammonium ion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_ion "Ammonium ion") ([NH 4]+).[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911862-39)

Although ammonia is well known as a weak base, it can also act as an extremely weak acid. It is a [protic substance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protic "Protic") and is capable of formation of [amides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amide "Amide") (which contain the NH−2 ion). For example, [lithium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium "Lithium") dissolves in [liquid ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_ammonia "Liquid ammonia") to give a blue solution ([solvated electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvated_electron "Solvated electron")) of [lithium amide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium_amide "Lithium amide"):

2 Li + 2 NH 3 → 2 LiNH 2 + H 2

Like water, liquid ammonia undergoes [molecular autoionisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_autoionisation "Molecular autoionisation") to form its [acid and base conjugates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjugate_acid "Conjugate acid"):

2 NH 3 ⇌ NH+4 + NH−2

Ammonia often functions as a [weak base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_base "Weak base"), so it has some [buffering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buffer_solution "Buffer solution") ability. Shifts in pH will cause more or fewer [ammonium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium "Ammonium") cations (NH+4) and [amide anions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azanide "Azanide") (NH−2) to be present in [solution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solution_(chemistry) "Solution (chemistry)"). At standard pressure and temperature,

K =

[NH+4] × [NH−2] = 10−30.

Heated [Cr 2 O 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromium(III)_oxide "Chromium(III) oxide") catalyzes the combustion of a flask of ammonia.

Ammonia does not burn readily or sustain [combustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combustion "Combustion"), except under narrow fuel-to-air mixtures of 15–28% ammonia by volume in air.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-41) When mixed with [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen"), it burns with a pale yellowish-green flame. Ignition occurs when [chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine") is passed into ammonia, forming nitrogen and [hydrogen chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride "Hydrogen chloride"); if chlorine is present in excess, then the highly explosive [nitrogen trichloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_trichloride "Nitrogen trichloride") (NCl 3) is also formed.

The [combustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combustion "Combustion") of ammonia to form nitrogen and water is [exothermic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exothermic "Exothermic"):

4 NH 3 + 3 O 2 → 2 N 2 + 6 H 2 O(g), [Δ _H_°r](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_enthalpy_of_reaction "Standard enthalpy of reaction") = −1267.20 kJ (or −316.8 kJ/mol if expressed per mol of

NH 3)

The [standard enthalpy change of combustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_enthalpy_change_of_combustion "Standard enthalpy change of combustion"), Δ _H_°c, expressed per [mole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mole_(unit) "Mole (unit)") of ammonia and with condensation of the water formed, is −382.81 kJ/mol. Dinitrogen is the thermodynamic product of [combustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combustion "Combustion"): all [nitrogen oxides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_oxide "Nitrogen oxide") are unstable with respect to N 2 and O 2, which is the principle behind the [catalytic converter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalytic_converter "Catalytic converter"). Nitrogen oxides can be formed as [kinetic products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_kinetics "Chemical kinetics") in the presence of appropriate [catalysts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalysts "Catalysts"), a reaction of great industrial importance in the production of [nitric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitric_acid "Nitric acid"):

4 NH 3 + 5 O 2 → 4 NO + 6 H 2 O

A subsequent reaction leads to NO 2:

2 NO + O 2 → 2 NO 2

The combustion of ammonia in air is very difficult in the absence of a [catalyst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalyst "Catalyst") (such as [platinum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platinum "Platinum") gauze or warm [chromium(III) oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromium(III)_oxide "Chromium(III) oxide")), due to the relatively low [heat of combustion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_of_combustion "Heat of combustion"), a lower laminar burning velocity, high [auto-ignition temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auto-ignition_temperature "Auto-ignition temperature"), high [heat of vapourization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat_of_vaporization "Heat of vaporization"), and a narrow [flammability range](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flammability_limit "Flammability limit"). However, recent studies have shown that efficient and stable combustion of ammonia can be achieved using swirl combustors, thereby rekindling research interest in ammonia as a fuel for thermal power production.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Kobayashi-2019-42) The flammable range of ammonia in dry air is 15.15–27.35% and in 100% relative humidity air is 15.95–26.55%.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-43)[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] For studying the [kinetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_kinetics "Chemical kinetics") of ammonia combustion, knowledge of a detailed reliable reaction mechanism is required, but this has been challenging to obtain.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-44)

### Precursor to organonitrogen compounds

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ammonia&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Precursor to organonitrogen compounds")]

Ammonia is a direct or indirect precursor to most [manufactured nitrogen-containing compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#Precursor_to_nitrogenous_compounds). It is the precursor to nitric acid, which is the source for most N-substituted aromatic compounds.

[Amines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amine "Amine") can be formed by the reaction of ammonia with [alkyl halides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkyl_halide "Alkyl halide") or, more commonly, with [alcohols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alcohol_(chemistry) "Alcohol (chemistry)"):

CH 3 OH + NH 3 → CH 3 NH 2 + H 2 O

Its ring-opening reaction with [ethylene oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_oxide "Ethylene oxide") give [ethanolamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanolamine "Ethanolamine"), [diethanolamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diethanolamine "Diethanolamine"), and [triethanolamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triethanolamine "Triethanolamine").

[Amides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amide "Amide") can be prepared by the reaction of ammonia with [carboxylic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboxylic_acid "Carboxylic acid") and their derivatives. For example, ammonia reacts with [formic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formic_acid "Formic acid") (HCOOH) to yield [formamide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formamide "Formamide") (HCONH 2) when heated. [Acyl chlorides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acyl_chloride "Acyl chloride") are the most reactive, but the ammonia must be present in at least a twofold excess to neutralise the [hydrogen chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride "Hydrogen chloride") formed. [Esters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ester "Ester") and [anhydrides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anhydride "Anhydride") also react with ammonia to form amides. Ammonium salts of carboxylic acids can be [dehydrated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dehydration_reaction "Dehydration reaction") to amides by heating to 150–200°C as long as no thermally sensitive groups are present.

*   [Amino acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid "Amino acid"), using [Strecker amino-acid synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strecker_amino-acid_synthesis "Strecker amino-acid synthesis")
*   [Acrylonitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrylonitrile "Acrylonitrile"), in the [Sohio process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sohio_process "Sohio process")

Other organonitrogen compounds include [alprazolam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alprazolam "Alprazolam"), [ethanolamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanolamine "Ethanolamine"), [ethyl carbamate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethyl_carbamate "Ethyl carbamate") and [hexamethylenetetramine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexamethylenetetramine "Hexamethylenetetramine").

### Precursor to inorganic nitrogenous compounds

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ammonia&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Precursor to inorganic nitrogenous compounds")]

[Nitric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitric_acid "Nitric acid") is generated via the [Ostwald process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ostwald_process "Ostwald process") by [oxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidation "Oxidation") of ammonia with air over a [platinum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platinum "Platinum") catalyst at 700–850°C (1,292–1,562°F), ≈9 atm. [Nitric oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitric_oxide "Nitric oxide") and [nitrogen dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide "Nitrogen dioxide") are intermediate in this conversion:[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-45)

NH 3 + 2 O 2 → HNO 3 + H 2 O

Nitric acid is used for the production of [fertilisers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fertiliser "Fertiliser"), [explosives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explosive "Explosive"), and many organonitrogen compounds.

The hydrogen in ammonia is susceptible to replacement by a myriad substituents. Ammonia gas reacts with metallic [sodium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium "Sodium") to give [sodamide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodamide "Sodamide"), NaNH 2.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911862-39)

With chlorine, [monochloramine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monochloramine "Monochloramine") is formed.

Pentavalent ammonia is known as λ 5-amine, [nitrogen pentahydride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_pentahydride "Nitrogen pentahydride") decomposes spontaneously into trivalent ammonia (λ 3-amine) and hydrogen gas at normal conditions. This substance was once investigated as a possible solid [rocket fuel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket_fuel "Rocket fuel") in 1966.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-46)

Ammonia is also used to make the following compounds:

*   [Hydrazine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrazine "Hydrazine"), in the [Olin Raschig process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olin_Raschig_process "Olin Raschig process") and the [peroxide process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peroxide_process "Peroxide process")
*   [Hydrogen cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide "Hydrogen cyanide"), in the [BMA process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BMA_process "BMA process") and the [Andrussow process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrussow_process "Andrussow process")
*   [Hydroxylamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxylamine "Hydroxylamine") and [ammonium carbonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_carbonate "Ammonium carbonate"), in the [Raschig process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raschig_hydroxylamine_process "Raschig hydroxylamine process")
*   [Urea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urea "Urea"), in the [Bosch–Meiser urea process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bosch%E2%80%93Meiser_urea_process "Bosch–Meiser urea process") and in [Wöhler synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W%C3%B6hler_synthesis "Wöhler synthesis")
*   [ammonium perchlorate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_perchlorate "Ammonium perchlorate"), [ammonium nitrate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_nitrate "Ammonium nitrate"), and [ammonium bicarbonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_bicarbonate "Ammonium bicarbonate")

[![Image 27](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/80/Cisplatin-stereo.svg/250px-Cisplatin-stereo.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cisplatin-stereo.svg)

[Cisplatin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cisplatin "Cisplatin") ([Pt(NH 3)2 Cl 2) is a widely used [anticancer drug](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anticancer_drug "Anticancer drug").

Ammonia is a [ligand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ligand "Ligand") forming [metal ammine complexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal_ammine_complex "Metal ammine complex"). For historical reasons, ammonia is named **ammine** in the nomenclature of [coordination compounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordination_compound "Coordination compound"). One notable ammine complex is [cisplatin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cisplatin "Cisplatin") (Pt(NH 3)2 Cl 2, a widely used anticancer drug. Ammine complexes of [chromium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromium "Chromium")(III) formed the basis of [Alfred Werner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_Werner "Alfred Werner")'s revolutionary theory on the structure of coordination compounds. Werner noted only two [isomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomer "Isomer") (_fac_- and _mer_-) of the complex [CrCl 3(NH 3)3] could be formed, and concluded the ligands must be arranged around the metal ion at the [vertices](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/vertex "wikt:vertex") of an [octahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octahedron "Octahedron").

Ammonia forms 1:1 [adducts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adduct "Adduct") with a variety of [Lewis acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lewis_acid "Lewis acid") such as [I 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iodine "Iodine"), [phenol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenol "Phenol"), and [Al(CH 3)3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trimethyl_aluminium "Trimethyl aluminium"). Ammonia is a [hard base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hard_base "Hard base") (HSAB theory) and its [E & C parameters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ECW_model "ECW model") are E B = 2.31 and C B = 2.04. Its relative donor strength toward a series of acids, versus other Lewis bases, can be illustrated by [C-B plots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ECW_model "ECW model").

Detection and determination
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ammonia&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Detection and determination")]

This section is about detection in the laboratory. For detection in astronomy, see [§In astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#In_astronomy).

### Ammonia in solution

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ammonia&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Ammonia in solution")]

Ammonia and ammonium salts can be readily detected, in very minute traces, by the addition of [Nessler's solution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nessler%27s_solution "Nessler's solution"), which gives a distinct yellow colouration in the presence of the slightest trace of ammonia or ammonium salts. The amount of ammonia in ammonium salts can be estimated quantitatively by distillation of the salts with [sodium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide "Sodium hydroxide") (NaOH) or [potassium hydroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide "Potassium hydroxide") (KOH), the ammonia evolved being absorbed in a known volume of standard [sulfuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid") and the excess of acid then determined [volumetrically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volumetric_analysis "Volumetric analysis"); or the ammonia may be absorbed in [hydrochloric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid "Hydrochloric acid") and the ammonium chloride so formed precipitated as [ammonium hexachloroplatinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_hexachloroplatinate "Ammonium hexachloroplatinate"), [NH 4]2[PtCl 6].[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911863-47)

[Sulfur sticks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_sticks "Sulfur sticks") are burnt to detect small leaks in industrial ammonia refrigeration systems. Larger quantities can be detected by warming the salts with a caustic alkali or with [quicklime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quicklime "Quicklime"), when the characteristic smell of ammonia will be at once apparent.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911863-47) Ammonia is an irritant and irritation increases with concentration; the [permissible exposure limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit "Permissible exposure limit") is 25[ppm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts-per_notation "Parts-per notation"), and lethal above 500 ppm by volume.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-48) Higher concentrations are hardly detected by conventional detectors, the type of detector is chosen according to the sensitivity required (e.g. semiconductor, catalytic, electrochemical). Holographic sensors have been proposed for detecting concentrations up to 12.5% in volume.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Hurtado-2014-49)

In a laboratorial setting, gaseous ammonia can be detected by using concentrated hydrochloric acid or gaseous hydrogen chloride. A dense white fume (which is [ammonium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_chloride "Ammonium chloride") vapor) arises from the reaction between ammonia and HCl(g).[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-50)

### Ammoniacal nitrogen (NH 3–N)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ammonia&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Ammoniacal nitrogen (NH3–N)")]

[Ammoniacal nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammoniacal_nitrogen "Ammoniacal nitrogen") (NH 3–N) is a measure commonly used for testing the quantity of [ammonium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium "Ammonium") ions, derived naturally from ammonia, and returned to ammonia via organic processes, in water or waste liquids. It is a measure used mainly for quantifying values in [waste treatment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waste_treatment "Waste treatment") and [water purification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_purification "Water purification") systems, as well as a measure of the health of natural and man-made water reserves. It is measured in units of mg/L ([milligram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milligram "Milligram") per [litre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Litre "Litre")).

[![Image 28](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Man_Florence%2C_Ashburnham_1166_fol_12r.jpg/250px-Man_Florence%2C_Ashburnham_1166_fol_12r.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Man_Florence,_Ashburnham_1166_fol_12r.jpg)

Jabir ibn Hayyan wrote about ammonia in the 9th century

[![Image 29](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Ammoniak_Reaktor_BASF.jpg/250px-Ammoniak_Reaktor_BASF.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ammoniak_Reaktor_BASF.jpg)

This high-pressure ammonia reactor was built in 1921 by [BASF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BASF "BASF") in [Ludwigshafen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwigshafen "Ludwigshafen") and was re-erected on the premises of the [University of Karlsruhe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Karlsruhe "University of Karlsruhe") in Germany.

The ancient Greek historian [Herodotus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herodotus "Herodotus") mentioned that there were [outcrops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outcrop "Outcrop") of salt in an area of Libya that was inhabited by a people called the 'Ammonians' (now the [Siwa oasis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Siwa_oasis "Siwa oasis") in northwestern Egypt, where salt lakes still exist).[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-51)[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-52) The Greek geographer [Strabo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strabo "Strabo") also mentioned the salt from this region. However, the ancient authors [Dioscorides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dioscorides "Dioscorides"), [Apicius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apicius "Apicius"), [Arrian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arrian "Arrian"), [Synesius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synesius "Synesius"), and [Aëtius of Amida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/A%C3%ABtius_of_Amida "Aëtius of Amida") described this salt as forming clear crystals that could be used for cooking and that were essentially [rock salt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rock_salt "Rock salt").[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-53)_Hammoniacus sal_ appears in the writings of [Pliny](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pliny_the_Elder "Pliny the Elder"),[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-54) although it is not known whether the term is equivalent to the more modern sal ammoniac (ammonium chloride).[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911861-22)[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Webmineral-55)[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-56)

The fermentation of [urine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine "Urine") by bacteria produces a [solution of ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia_solution "Ammonia solution"); hence fermented urine was used in [Classical Antiquity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_Antiquity "Classical Antiquity") to wash cloth and clothing, to remove hair from hides in preparation for tanning, to serve as a [mordant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mordant "Mordant") in dyeing cloth, and to remove rust from iron.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-57) It was also used by [ancient dentists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dentistry_in_ancient_Rome "Dentistry in ancient Rome") to wash teeth.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-58)[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-59)[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-60)

In the form of sal ammoniac (نشادر, _nushadir_), ammonia was important to the [Muslim alchemists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alchemy_and_chemistry_in_medieval_Islam "Alchemy and chemistry in medieval Islam"). It was mentioned in the _Book of Stones_, likely written in the 9th century and attributed to [Jābir ibn Hayyān](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J%C4%81bir_ibn_Hayy%C4%81n "Jābir ibn Hayyān").[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Haq-1995-61) It was also important to the European [alchemists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alchemists "Alchemists") of the 13th century, being mentioned by [Albertus Magnus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albertus_Magnus "Albertus Magnus").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911861-22) It was also used by [dyers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dye "Dye") in the [Middle Ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Ages "Middle Ages") in the form of fermented [urine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine "Urine") to alter the colour of vegetable dyes. In the 15th century, [Basilius Valentinus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basilius_Valentinus "Basilius Valentinus") showed that ammonia could be obtained by the action of alkalis on sal ammoniac.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-62) At a later period, when sal ammoniac was obtained by distilling the hooves and horns of oxen and neutralizing the resulting carbonate with [hydrochloric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid "Hydrochloric acid"), the name 'spirit of hartshorn' was applied to ammonia.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911861-22)[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-63)

Gaseous ammonia was first isolated by [Joseph Black](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Black "Joseph Black") in 1756 by reacting _sal ammoniac_ ([ammonium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_chloride "Ammonium chloride")) with _calcined magnesia_ ([magnesium oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnesium_oxide "Magnesium oxide")).[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-64)[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-65) It was isolated again by [Peter Woulfe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Woulfe "Peter Woulfe") in 1767,[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-66)[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-67) by [Carl Wilhelm Scheele](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Scheele "Carl Wilhelm Scheele") in 1770[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-68) and by [Joseph Priestley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Priestley "Joseph Priestley") in 1773 and was termed by him 'alkaline air'.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911861-22)[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-69) Eleven years later in 1785, [Claude Louis Berthollet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claude_Louis_Berthollet "Claude Louis Berthollet") ascertained its composition.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-70)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911861-22)

The production of ammonia from nitrogen in the air (and hydrogen) was invented by [Fritz Haber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fritz_Haber "Fritz Haber") and Robert LeRossignol. The patent was sent in 1909 (USPTO Nr 1,202,995) and awarded in 1916. Later, [Carl Bosch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Bosch "Carl Bosch") developed the industrial method for ammonia production ([Haber–Bosch process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haber%E2%80%93Bosch_process "Haber–Bosch process")). It was first used on an industrial scale in [Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germany "Germany") during [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I"),[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Max_Appl-2006-71) following the allied blockade that cut off the supply of [nitrates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrate "Nitrate") from [Chile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chile "Chile"). The ammonia was used to produce explosives to sustain war efforts.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Smith-2001-72) The Nobel Prize in Chemistry 1918 was awarded to Fritz Haber "for the synthesis of ammonia from its elements".

Before the availability of natural gas, hydrogen as a precursor to [ammonia production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia_production "Ammonia production") was produced via the [electrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrolysis "Electrolysis") of water or using the [chloralkali process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloralkali_process "Chloralkali process").

With the advent of the [steel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steel "Steel") industry in the 20th century, ammonia became a byproduct of the production of [coking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coking "Coking") coal.

In the US as of 2019, approximately 88% of ammonia was used as [fertilisers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fertiliser "Fertiliser") either as its salts, solutions or [anhydrously](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anhydrous "Anhydrous").[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-USGS-2020-73) When applied to soil, it helps provide increased yields of [crops](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crop "Crop") such as [maize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maize "Maize") and [wheat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wheat "Wheat").[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-74) 30% of agricultural nitrogen applied in the US is in the form of anhydrous ammonia, and worldwide, 110 million tonnes are applied each year.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-75) Solutions of ammonia ranging from 16% to 25% are used in the [fermentation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_fermentation "Industrial fermentation") industry as a source of nitrogen for microorganisms and to adjust pH during fermentation.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-76)

Because of ammonia's vapourization properties, it is a useful [refrigerant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refrigerant "Refrigerant").[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Max_Appl-2006-71) It was commonly used before the popularisation of [chlorofluorocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon "Chlorofluorocarbon") (Freons). Anhydrous ammonia is widely used in industrial refrigeration applications and hockey rinks because of its high [energy efficiency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_conversion_efficiency "Energy conversion efficiency") and low cost. It suffers from the disadvantage of toxicity, and requiring corrosion resistant components, which restricts its domestic and small-scale use. Along with its use in modern [vapour-compression refrigeration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vapour-compression_refrigeration "Vapour-compression refrigeration") it is used in a mixture along with hydrogen and water in [absorption refrigerators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorption_refrigerator "Absorption refrigerator"). The [Kalina cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kalina_cycle "Kalina cycle"), which is of growing importance to geothermal power plants, depends on the wide boiling range of the ammonia–water mixture.

Ammonia coolant is also used in the radiators aboard the [International Space Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Space_Station "International Space Station") in loops that are used to regulate the internal temperature and enable temperature-dependent experiments.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-77)[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-78) The ammonia is under sufficient pressure to remain liquid throughout the process. Single-phase ammonia cooling systems also serve the power electronics in each pair of solar arrays.

The potential importance of ammonia as a refrigerant has increased with the discovery that vented CFCs and HFCs are potent and stable greenhouse gases.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-79)

### Antimicrobial agent for food products

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ammonia&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Antimicrobial agent for food products")]

As early as in 1895, it was known that ammonia was 'strongly [antiseptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiseptic "Antiseptic"); it requires 1.4 grams per litre to preserve [beef tea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Broth "Broth") (broth).'[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-80) In one study, anhydrous ammonia destroyed 99.999% of [zoonotic bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zoonotic_bacteria "Zoonotic bacteria") in three types of [animal feed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_feed "Compound feed"), but not [silage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silage "Silage").[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-81)[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-82) Anhydrous ammonia is currently used commercially to reduce or eliminate [microbial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microbial "Microbial") contamination of [beef](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beef "Beef").[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-83)[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-84) Lean finely textured beef (popularly known as '[pink slime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pink_slime "Pink slime")') in the beef industry is made from fatty [beef trimmings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beef_trimmings "Beef trimmings") (c. 50–70% fat) by removing the fat using heat and [centrifugation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centrifugation "Centrifugation"), then treating it with ammonia to kill _[E. coli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E.\_coli "E. coli")_. The process was deemed effective and safe by the [US Department of Agriculture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/US_Department_of_Agriculture "US Department of Agriculture") based on a study that found that the treatment reduces _E. coli_ to undetectable levels.[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-85) There have been safety concerns about the process as well as consumer complaints about the taste and smell of ammonia-treated beef.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-86)

[![Image 30](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/65/AmmoniacalGasEngineStreetcarARWaud.jpeg/250px-AmmoniacalGasEngineStreetcarARWaud.jpeg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:AmmoniacalGasEngineStreetcarARWaud.jpeg)

Ammoniacal Gas Engine [Streetcar in New Orleans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Streetcars_in_New_Orleans "Streetcars in New Orleans") drawn by [Alfred Waud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_Waud "Alfred Waud") in 1871

Ammonia has been used as fuel, and is a proposed alternative to fossil fuels and hydrogen, especially in [maritime transport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maritime_transport "Maritime transport"). Being liquid at ambient temperature under its own vapour pressure and having high volumetric and gravimetric energy density, ammonia is considered a suitable carrier for hydrogen,[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-87) and may be cheaper than direct transport of liquid hydrogen.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-88)

Compared to hydrogen, ammonia is easier to store. Compared to [hydrogen as a fuel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_as_a_fuel "Hydrogen as a fuel"), ammonia is much more energy efficient, and could be produced, stored and delivered at a much lower cost than hydrogen, which must be kept compressed or as a cryogenic liquid.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Lan-2014-89)[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-90) The raw [energy density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_density "Energy density") of liquid ammonia is 11.5 MJ/L,[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Lan-2014-89) which is about a third that of [diesel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diesel_fuel "Diesel fuel"). Ammonia can also be converted back to hydrogen to be used to power hydrogen fuel cells, or it may be used directly within high-temperature [solid oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_oxide_fuel_cell "Solid oxide fuel cell") direct ammonia fuel cells to provide efficient power sources that do not emit [greenhouse gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas "Greenhouse gas").[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-91)[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Afif-2016-92) Ammonia to hydrogen conversion can be achieved through the [sodium amide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_amide "Sodium amide") process[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-David-2014-93) or the catalytic decomposition of ammonia using solid catalysts.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-94)

Ammonia production currently creates 1.8% of global CO 2 emissions. 'Green ammonia' is ammonia produced by using [green hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_hydrogen "Green hydrogen") (hydrogen produced by electrolysis with electricity from [renewable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Renewable_energy "Renewable energy") energy), whereas 'blue ammonia' is ammonia produced using [blue hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blue_hydrogen "Blue hydrogen") (hydrogen produced by steam methane reforming) where the carbon dioxide has been captured and stored.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-95) In a world first in 2020, [Saudi Arabia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saudi_Arabia "Saudi Arabia") shipped 40 [metric tons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tons "Metric tons") of liquid 'blue ammonia' to Japan for use as a fuel.[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-96) It was produced as a by-product by petrochemical industries, and can be burned without giving off [greenhouse gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greenhouse_gas "Greenhouse gas"). Its energy density by volume is nearly double that of liquid hydrogen. If the process of creating it can be scaled up via purely renewable resources, producing green ammonia, it could make a major difference in [avoiding climate change](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avoiding_climate_change "Avoiding climate change").[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-97)

#### Ships and maritime transport

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ammonia&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Ships and maritime transport")]

Green ammonia is considered as a potential fuel for new [ships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ships "Ships"), including future [container ships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Container_ships "Container ships"). Ammonia is expected to increase in usage as a fuel source for shipping [[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-s218-98) The [IEA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Energy_Agency "International Energy Agency") forecasts that ammonia will meet approximately 45% of shipping fuel demands by 2050.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-99)

In 2020, the companies [DSME](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DSME "DSME") and [MAN Energy Solutions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MAN_Energy_Solutions "MAN Energy Solutions") announced the construction of an ammonia-based ship, DSME plans to commercialize it by 2025.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-100) The use of ammonia as a potential alternative fuel for [aircraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aircraft "Aircraft")[jet engines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jet_engine "Jet engine") is also being explored.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-101) Japan is implementing a plan to develop ammonia co-firing technology that can increase the use of ammonia in power generation, as part of efforts to assist domestic and other Asian utilities to accelerate their transition to [carbon neutrality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_neutrality "Carbon neutrality").[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-102) In October 2021, the first International Conference on Fuel Ammonia (ICFA2021) was held.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-103)[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-104)

In June 2022, [IHI Corporation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IHI_Corporation "IHI Corporation") succeeded in reducing greenhouse gases by over 99% during combustion of liquid ammonia in a 2,000-kilowatt-class gas turbine achieving truly CO 2-free power generation.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-105) In July 2022, [Quad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral_Security_Dialogue "Quadrilateral Security Dialogue") nations of Japan, the U.S., Australia and India agreed to promote technological development for clean-burning hydrogen and ammonia as fuels at the security grouping's first energy meeting.[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-106) As of 2022, however, significant amounts of NO _x_ are produced.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-107)[Nitrous oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrous_oxide "Nitrous oxide") may also be a problem as it is a "_greenhouse gas that is known to possess up to 300 times the Global Warming Potential (GWP) of carbon dioxide_".[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-108)

#### Vehicles, aviation and space

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ammonia&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: Vehicles, aviation and space")]

[![Image 31](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/82/X-15.jpg/250px-X-15.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:X-15.jpg)

The [X-15](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-15 "X-15")[aircraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aircraft "Aircraft") used ammonia as one component [fuel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fuel "Fuel") of its [rocket engine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket_engine "Rocket engine")

Ammonia engines or ammonia motors, using ammonia as a [working fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Working_fluid "Working fluid"), have been proposed and occasionally used.[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-109) The principle is similar to that used in a [fireless locomotive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fireless_locomotive "Fireless locomotive"), but with ammonia as the working fluid, instead of steam or compressed air. Ammonia engines were used experimentally in the 19th century by [Goldsworthy Gurney](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goldsworthy_Gurney "Goldsworthy Gurney") in the UK and the [St. Charles Streetcar Line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St._Charles_Streetcar_Line "St. Charles Streetcar Line") in [New Orleans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Orleans "New Orleans") in the 1870s and 1880s,[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Elbridge_Harper_Charlton-1965-110) and during [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II") ammonia was used to power buses in [Belgium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Belgium "Belgium").[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Olson-2007-111)

Ammonia is sometimes proposed as a practical alternative to [fossil fuel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fossil_fuel "Fossil fuel") for [internal combustion engines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Internal_combustion_engine "Internal combustion engine").[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Olson-2007-111)[[112]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Lee-2017-112)[[113]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Brohi-2014-113)[[114]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-114) However, ammonia cannot be easily used in existing [Otto cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otto_cycle "Otto cycle") engines because of its very narrow [flammability range](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#Combustion). Despite this, several tests have been run.[[115]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-115)[[116]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-116)[[117]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-117) Its high [octane rating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octane_rating "Octane rating") of 120[[118]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-bris.ac.uk-118) and low flame temperature[[119]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Zacharakis-Jutz-2013-119) allows the use of high compression ratios without a penalty of high [NO _x_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NOx "NOx") production. Since ammonia contains no carbon, its combustion cannot produce [carbon dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide"), [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide"), [hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbons "Hydrocarbons"), or [soot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soot "Soot"). At high temperature and in the presence of a suitable [catalyst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalyst "Catalyst") ammonia decomposes into its constituent elements.[[120]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-White-1905-120) Decomposition of ammonia is a slightly endothermic process requiring 23 kJ/mol (5.5[kcal/mol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kcal/mol "Kcal/mol")) of ammonia, and yields [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") and [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") gas.

Rocket engines have also been fueled by ammonia. The [Reaction Motors XLR99](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reaction_Motors_XLR99 "Reaction Motors XLR99") rocket engine that powered the [X-15](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-15 "X-15") hypersonic research aircraft used liquid ammonia. Although not as powerful as other fuels, it left no [soot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soot "Soot") in the reusable rocket engine, and its density approximately matches the density of the oxidiser, [liquid oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid_oxygen "Liquid oxygen"), which simplified the aircraft's design.

The company [ACWA Power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ACWA_Power "ACWA Power") and the city of [Neom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neom "Neom") have announced the construction of a green hydrogen and ammonia plant in 2020.[[121]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-121)

#### Remediation of gaseous emissions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ammonia&action=edit&section=28 "Edit section: Remediation of gaseous emissions")]

Ammonia is used to scrub SO 2 from the burning of fossil fuels, and the resulting product is converted to [ammonium sulfate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_sulfate "Ammonium sulfate") for use as fertiliser. Ammonia neutralises the nitrogen oxide (NO _x_) pollutants emitted by diesel engines. This technology, called SCR ([selective catalytic reduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Selective_catalytic_reduction "Selective catalytic reduction")), relies on a [vanadia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vanadia "Vanadia")-based catalyst.[[122]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-122)

Ammonia may be used to mitigate gaseous spills of [phosgene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosgene "Phosgene").[[123]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-123)

[![Image 32](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/cd/Meth_ammonia_tank_Otley_iowa.JPG/250px-Meth_ammonia_tank_Otley_iowa.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Meth_ammonia_tank_Otley_iowa.JPG)

Anti-[meth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methamphetamine "Methamphetamine") sign on tank of anhydrous ammonia, [Otley, Iowa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otley,_Iowa "Otley, Iowa"). Anhydrous ammonia is a common farm fertiliser that is also a critical ingredient in making methamphetamine. In 2005, Iowa used grant money to provide thousands of locks to prevent criminals from gaining access to the tanks.[[124]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-124)

Ammonia, as the vapour released by [smelling salts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smelling_salts "Smelling salts"), has found significant use as a respiratory stimulant. Ammonia is commonly used in the illegal manufacture of [methamphetamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methamphetamine "Methamphetamine") through a [Birch reduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birch_reduction "Birch reduction").[[125]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-125) The Birch method of making methamphetamine is dangerous because the alkali metal and liquid ammonia are both extremely reactive, and the temperature of liquid ammonia makes it susceptible to explosive boiling when reactants are added.[[126]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-126)

Liquid ammonia is used for treatment of cotton materials, giving properties like [mercerisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercerisation "Mercerisation"), using alkalis. In particular, it is used for prewashing of wool.[[127]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-127)

At standard temperature and pressure, ammonia is less dense than atmosphere and has approximately 45–48% of the lifting power of hydrogen or [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium"). Ammonia has sometimes been used to fill balloons as a [lifting gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lifting_gas "Lifting gas"). Because of its relatively high boiling point (compared to helium and hydrogen), ammonia could potentially be refrigerated and liquefied aboard an [airship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Airship "Airship") to reduce lift and add ballast (and returned to a gas to add lift and reduce ballast).[[128]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-128)

Ammonia has been used to darken quartersawn white oak in Arts & Crafts and Mission-style furniture. Ammonia fumes react with the natural [tannins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tannin "Tannin") in the [wood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wood "Wood") and cause it to change colour.[[129]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-129)

[![Image 33](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/Ammiakoprovod_NS.jpg/250px-Ammiakoprovod_NS.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ammiakoprovod_NS.jpg)

The world's longest ammonia [pipeline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pipeline_transport "Pipeline transport") (roughly 2400 km long),[[130]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-130) running from the [TogliattiAzot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TogliattiAzot "TogliattiAzot") plant in [Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russia "Russia") to [Odesa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odesa "Odesa") in [Ukraine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ukraine "Ukraine")

The US [Occupational Safety and Health Administration (OSHA)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration "Occupational Safety and Health Administration") has set a 15-minute exposure limit for gaseous ammonia of 35 ppm by volume in the environmental air and an 8-hour exposure limit of 25 ppm by volume.[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Toxic_FAQ_Sheet_for_Ammonia-2004-131) The [National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health "National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health") (NIOSH) recently reduced the IDLH (Immediately Dangerous to Life or Health, the level to which a healthy worker can be exposed for 30 minutes without suffering irreversible health effects) from 500 to 300 ppm based on recent more conservative interpretations of original research in 1943. Other organisations have varying exposure levels. US Navy Standards [U.S. Bureau of Ships 1962] maximum allowable concentrations (MACs): for continuous exposure (60 days) is 25 ppm; for exposure of 1 hour is 400 ppm.[[132]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-132)

Ammonia vapour has a sharp, irritating, pungent odor that acts as a warning of potentially dangerous exposure. The average odor threshold is 5 ppm, well below any danger or damage. Exposure to very high concentrations of gaseous ammonia can result in lung damage and death.[[131]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Toxic_FAQ_Sheet_for_Ammonia-2004-131) Ammonia is regulated in the US as a non-flammable gas, but it meets the definition of a material that is toxic by inhalation and requires a hazardous safety permit when transported in quantities greater than 3,500 US gallons (13,000 L; 2,900 imp gal).[[133]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-133)

Liquid ammonia is dangerous because it is [hygroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hygroscopic "Hygroscopic") and because it can cause [caustic burns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caustic_burn "Caustic burn"). See [Gas carrier §Health effects of specific cargoes carried on gas carriers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_carrier#Health_effects_of_specific_cargoes_carried_on_gas_carriers "Gas carrier") for more information.

The toxicity of ammonia solutions does not usually cause problems for humans and other mammals, as a specific mechanism exists to prevent its build-up in the bloodstream. Ammonia is converted to [carbamoyl phosphate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbamoyl_phosphate "Carbamoyl phosphate") by the enzyme [carbamoyl phosphate synthetase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbamoyl_phosphate_synthetase "Carbamoyl phosphate synthetase"), and then enters the [urea cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urea_cycle "Urea cycle") to be either incorporated into [amino acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid "Amino acid") or excreted in the urine.[[134]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-134)[Fish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fish "Fish") and [amphibians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amphibian "Amphibian") lack this mechanism, as they can usually eliminate ammonia from their bodies by direct excretion. Ammonia even at dilute concentrations is highly toxic to aquatic animals, and for this reason it is [classified](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directive_67/548/EEC "Directive 67/548/EEC") as _"dangerous for the environment"_. Atmospheric ammonia plays a key role in the formation of [fine particulate matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fine_particulate_matter "Fine particulate matter").[[135]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-135)

Ammonia is a constituent of [tobacco smoke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tobacco_smoke "Tobacco smoke").[[136]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Talhout-2011-136)

Ammonia is present in coking wastewater streams, as a liquid by-product of the production of [coke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coke_(fuel) "Coke (fuel)") from [coal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal "Coal").[[137]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-137) In some cases, the ammonia is discharged to the [marine environment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marine_environment "Marine environment") where it acts as a pollutant. The [Whyalla Steelworks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whyalla_Steelworks "Whyalla Steelworks") in [South Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Australia "South Australia") is one example of a coke-producing facility that discharges ammonia into marine waters.[[138]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Rajaram-2005-138)

Ammonia toxicity is believed to be a cause of otherwise unexplained losses in [fish hatcheries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fish_hatcheries "Fish hatcheries"). Excess ammonia may accumulate and cause alteration of metabolism or increases in the body pH of the exposed organism. Tolerance varies among fish species.[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Oram-139) At lower concentrations, around 0.05 mg/L, un-ionised ammonia is harmful to fish species and can result in poor growth and feed conversion rates, reduced fecundity and fertility and increase stress and susceptibility to bacterial infections and diseases.[[140]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-140) Exposed to excess ammonia, fish may suffer loss of equilibrium, hyper-excitability, increased respiratory activity and oxygen uptake and increased heart rate.[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Oram-139) At concentrations exceeding 2.0 mg/L, ammonia causes gill and tissue damage, extreme lethargy, convulsions, coma, and death.[[139]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Oram-139)[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Sergeant-2014-141) Experiments have shown that the lethal concentration for a variety of fish species ranges from 0.2 to 2.0 mg/L.[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Sergeant-2014-141)

During winter, when reduced feeds are administered to aquaculture stock, ammonia levels can be higher. Lower ambient temperatures reduce the rate of algal photosynthesis so less ammonia is removed by any algae present. Within an aquaculture environment, especially at large scale, there is no fast-acting remedy to elevated ammonia levels. Prevention rather than correction is recommended to reduce harm to farmed fish[[141]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Sergeant-2014-141) and in open water systems, the surrounding environment.

### Storage information

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ammonia&action=edit&section=37 "Edit section: Storage information")]

Similar to [propane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propane "Propane"), [anhydrous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anhydrous "Anhydrous") ammonia boils below room temperature when at atmospheric pressure. A storage vessel capable of 250[psi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pound_per_square_inch "Pound per square inch") (1.7[MPa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal_(unit) "Pascal (unit)")) is suitable to contain the liquid.[[142]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-142) Ammonia is used in numerous different industrial applications requiring carbon or stainless steel storage vessels. Ammonia with at least 0.2% by weight water content is not corrosive to carbon steel. NH 3[carbon steel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_steel "Carbon steel") construction storage tanks with 0.2% by weight or more of water could last more than 50 years in service.[[143]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-143) Experts warn that ammonium compounds not be allowed to come in contact with [bases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_(chemistry) "Base (chemistry)") (unless in an intended and contained reaction), as dangerous quantities of ammonia gas could be released.

[![Image 34](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c4/Ammonia_solution_%2825-28%25%29.jpg/250px-Ammonia_solution_%2825-28%25%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ammonia_solution_(25-28%25).jpg)

A standard laboratory solution of 28% ammonia

The hazards of ammonia solutions depend on the concentration: 'dilute' ammonia solutions are usually 5–10% by weight (<5.62 mol/L); 'concentrated' solutions are usually prepared at >25% by weight. A 25% (by weight) solution has a density of 0.907 g/cm 3, and a solution that has a lower density will be more concentrated. The ammonia vapour from concentrated ammonia solutions is severely irritating to the eyes and the [respiratory tract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_tract "Respiratory tract"), and experts warn that these solutions only be handled in a [fume hood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fume_hood "Fume hood"). Saturated ('0.880'–see _[§Properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#Properties)_) solutions can develop a significant pressure inside a closed bottle in warm weather, and experts also warn that the bottle be opened with care. This is not usually a problem for 25% ('0.900') solutions.

Experts warn that ammonia solutions not be mixed with [halogens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halogen "Halogen"), as toxic and/or explosive products are formed. Experts also warn that prolonged contact of ammonia solutions with [silver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silver "Silver"), [mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(element) "Mercury (element)") or [iodide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iodide "Iodide") salts can also lead to explosive products: such mixtures are often formed in [qualitative inorganic analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qualitative_inorganic_analysis "Qualitative inorganic analysis"), and that it needs to be lightly acidified but not concentrated (<6%w/v) before disposal once the test is completed.

### Laboratory use of anhydrous ammonia (gas or liquid)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ammonia&action=edit&section=39 "Edit section: Laboratory use of anhydrous ammonia (gas or liquid)")]

Anhydrous ammonia is classified as toxic (**T**) and dangerous for the environment (**N**). The gas is flammable ([autoignition temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Autoignition_temperature "Autoignition temperature"): 651°C) and can form explosive mixtures with air (16–25%). The [permissible exposure limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permissible_exposure_limit "Permissible exposure limit") (PEL) in the United States is 50[ppm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts_per_million "Parts per million") (35 mg/m 3), while the [IDLH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IDLH "IDLH") concentration is estimated at 300 ppm. Repeated exposure to ammonia lowers the sensitivity to the smell of the gas: normally the odour is detectable at concentrations of less than 50 ppm, but desensitised individuals may not detect it even at concentrations of 100 ppm. Anhydrous ammonia corrodes [copper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Copper "Copper")- and [zinc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zinc "Zinc")-containing [alloys](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alloy "Alloy"), which makes [brass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brass "Brass") fittings not appropriate for handling the gas. Liquid ammonia can also attack [rubber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubber "Rubber") and certain plastics.

Ammonia reacts violently with the [halogens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halogen "Halogen"). [Nitrogen triiodide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_triiodide "Nitrogen triiodide"), a [primary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Primary_explosive "Primary explosive")[high explosive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High_explosive "High explosive"), is formed when ammonia comes in contact with [iodine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iodine "Iodine"). Ammonia causes the explosive [polymerisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymerisation "Polymerisation") of [ethylene oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene_oxide "Ethylene oxide"). It also forms explosive [fulminating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detonation "Detonation") compounds with compounds of [gold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold "Gold"), [silver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silver "Silver"), [mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(element) "Mercury (element)"), [germanium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germanium "Germanium") or [tellurium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tellurium "Tellurium"), and with [stibine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stibine "Stibine"). Violent reactions have also been reported with [acetaldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetaldehyde "Acetaldehyde"), [hypochlorite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypochlorite "Hypochlorite") solutions, [potassium ferricyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_ferricyanide "Potassium ferricyanide") and [peroxides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peroxide "Peroxide").

This section is about industrial synthesis. For synthesis in certain organisms, see [§Biosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#Biosynthesis).

Global ammonia production 1950–2020 (expressed as fixed nitrogen in U.S. tons)[[144]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-144)

Ammonia has one of the highest rates of production of any inorganic chemical. Production is sometimes expressed in terms of "fixed nitrogen". Global production was estimated as being 160 million tonnes in 2020 (147 tons of fixed nitrogen).[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-U.S._National_Minerals_Information_Center-145) China accounted for 26.5% of that, followed by Russia at 11.0%, the United States at 9.5%, and India at 8.3%.[[145]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-U.S._National_Minerals_Information_Center-145)

Before the start of [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I"), most ammonia was obtained by the [dry distillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dry_distillation "Dry distillation")[[146]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-146) of nitrogenous vegetable and animal waste products, including [camel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Camel "Camel")[dung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manure "Manure"), where it was [distilled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distilled "Distilled") by the reduction of [nitrous acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrous_acid "Nitrous acid") and [nitrites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrite "Nitrite") with hydrogen; in addition, it was produced by the distillation of [coal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal "Coal"), and also by the decomposition of ammonium salts by [alkaline hydroxides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkali_hydroxide "Alkali hydroxide")[[147]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-147) such as [quicklime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quicklime "Quicklime"):[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911861-22)

2 [NH 4]Cl + 2 CaO → [CaCl 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CaCl2 "CaCl2") + Ca(OH)2 + 2 NH 3([g](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas "Gas"))

For small scale laboratory synthesis, one can heat [urea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urea "Urea") and [calcium hydroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_hydroxide "Calcium hydroxide") or [sodium hydroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_hydroxide "Sodium hydroxide"):

(NH 2)2 CO + Ca(OH)2 → CaCO 3 + 2 NH 3

[![Image 41](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1e/Fritz_Haber.png/250px-Fritz_Haber.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Fritz_Haber.png)

[Fritz Haber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fritz_Haber "Fritz Haber"), 1918

The [Haber process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Haber_process "Haber process"),[[148]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-148) also called the Haber–Bosch process, is the main industrial procedure for the [production of ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia_production "Ammonia production").[[149]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-149)[[150]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Haber_process_Appl-150) It converts atmospheric [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen") (N 2) to ammonia (NH 3) by a reaction with [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") (H 2) using finely divided [iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron "Iron") metal as a catalyst:

![Image 42: {\displaystyle {\mathrm {N} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{2}}{}+{}3\,\mathrm {H} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{2}}{}\mathrel {\longrightleftharpoons } {}2\,\mathrm {NH} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{3}}}\qquad {\Delta H_{\mathrm {298~K} }^{\circ }=-92.28~{\text{kJ per mole of }}{\mathrm {N} {\vphantom {A}}_{\smash[{t}]{2}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba3d04f7488f3eb39ea50f0c05bccf43243ae4fe)

This reaction is [exothermic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exothermic "Exothermic") but disfavored in terms of [entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy "Entropy") because four equivalents of reactant gases are converted into two equivalents of product gas. As a result, sufficiently high pressures and temperatures are needed to [drive the reaction forward](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Le_Chatelier%27s_principle "Le Chatelier's principle").

The German chemists [Fritz Haber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fritz_Haber "Fritz Haber") and [Carl Bosch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Bosch "Carl Bosch") developed the process in the first decade of the 20th century, and its improved efficiency over existing methods such as the [Birkeland-Eyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birkeland%E2%80%93Eyde_process "Birkeland–Eyde process") and [Frank-Caro](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank%E2%80%93Caro_process "Frank–Caro process") processes was a major advancement in the industrial production of ammonia.[[151]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Haber_process_Smil_2004_Enriching-151)[[152]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Haber_process_hager-152)[[153]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-153)

The Haber process can be combined with [steam reforming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steam_reforming "Steam reforming") to produce ammonia with just three chemical inputs: water, [natural gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_gas "Natural gas"), and atmospheric nitrogen. Both Haber and Bosch were eventually awarded the [Nobel Prize in Chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Nobel_laureates_in_Chemistry "List of Nobel laureates in Chemistry"): Haber in 1918 for ammonia synthesis specifically, and Bosch in 1931 for related contributions to [high-pressure chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-pressure_chemistry "High-pressure chemistry").

The [electrochemical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrochemical "Electrochemical") synthesis of ammonia involves the reductive formation of [lithium nitride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lithium_nitride "Lithium nitride"), which can be [protonated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonated "Protonated") to ammonia, given a [proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") source. The first use of this chemistry was reported in 1930, where lithium solutions in ethanol were used to produce ammonia at pressures of up to 1000 bar, with ethanol acting as the proton source.[[154]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-154) Beyond simply mediating proton transfer to the nitrogen reduction reaction, ethanol has been found to play a multifaceted role, influencing electrolyte transformations and contributing to the formation of the solid electrolyte interphase, which enhances overall reaction efficiency.[[155]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-155)[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Lazouski-2019-156)

In 1994, Tsuneto et al. used lithium electrodeposition in [tetrahydrofuran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrahydrofuran "Tetrahydrofuran") to synthesize ammonia at more moderate pressures with reasonable [Faradaic efficiency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faradaic_efficiency "Faradaic efficiency").[[157]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-157) Subsequent studies have further explored the ethanol–tetrahydrofuran system for electrochemical ammonia synthesis.[[156]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Lazouski-2019-156)[[158]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-158)

In 2020, a solvent-agnostic [gas diffusion electrode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_diffusion_electrode "Gas diffusion electrode") was shown to improve nitrogen transport to the reactive lithium. NH 3 production rates of up to 30 ± 5 nmol/(s⋅cm 2) and Faradaic efficiencies of up to 47.5 ± 4% at ambient temperature and 1 bar pressure were achieved.[[159]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-159)

In 2021, it was demonstrated that ethanol could be replaced with a tetraalkyl [phosphonium salt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphonium_salt "Phosphonium salt").[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Suryanto-2021-160) The study observed NH 3 production rates of 53 ± 1 nmol/(s⋅cm 2) at 69 ± 1% Faradaic efficiency experiments under 0.5 bar hydrogen and 19.5 bar nitrogen [partial pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_pressure "Partial pressure") at ambient temperature.[[160]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Suryanto-2021-160) Technology based on this electrochemistry is being developed for commercial fertiliser and fuel production.[[161]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-161)[[162]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-162)

In 2022, ammonia was produced via the lithium mediated process in a continuous-flow electrolyzer also demonstrating the hydrogen gas as proton source. The study synthesized ammonia at 61 ± 1% Faradaic efficiency at a current density of −6 mA/cm 2 at 1 bar and room temperature.[[163]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Fu-2022-163)

Biochemistry and medicine
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ammonia&action=edit&section=43 "Edit section: Biochemistry and medicine")]

[![Image 43](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/76/Symptoms_of_hyperammonemia.svg/330px-Symptoms_of_hyperammonemia.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Symptoms_of_hyperammonemia.svg)

Main symptoms of hyperammonemia (ammonia reaching toxic concentrations).[[164]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-164)

Ammonia is essential for life.[[165]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-165) For example, it is required for the formation of [amino acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid "Amino acid") and [nucleic acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleic_acid "Nucleic acid"), fundamental building blocks of life. Ammonia is however quite toxic. Nature thus uses carriers for ammonia. Within a cell, [glutamate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glutamate "Glutamate") serves this role. In the bloodstream, [glutamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glutamine "Glutamine") is a source of ammonia.[[166]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-166)

Ethanolamine, required for cell membranes, is the substrate for [ethanolamine ammonia-lyase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethanolamine_ammonia-lyase "Ethanolamine ammonia-lyase"), which produces ammonia:[[167]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-167)

H 2 NCH 2 CH 2 OH → NH 3 + CH 3 CHO

Ammonia is both a [metabolic waste](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolic_waste "Metabolic waste") and a metabolic input throughout the [biosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosphere "Biosphere"). It is an important source of nitrogen for living systems. Although atmospheric nitrogen abounds (more than 75%), few living creatures are capable of using atmospheric nitrogen in its [diatomic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic "Diatomic") form, N 2 gas. Therefore, [nitrogen fixation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation "Nitrogen fixation") is required for the synthesis of amino acids, which are the building blocks of [protein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein "Protein"). Some plants rely on ammonia and other nitrogenous wastes incorporated into the soil by decaying matter. Others, such as nitrogen-fixing [legumes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legume "Legume"), benefit from [symbiotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symbiotic "Symbiotic") relationships with [rhizobia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhizobia "Rhizobia") bacteria that create ammonia from atmospheric nitrogen.[[168]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-168)

In humans, inhaling ammonia in high concentrations can be fatal. Exposure to ammonia can cause [headaches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Headaches "Headaches"), [edema](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edema "Edema"), impaired memory, [seizures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seizures "Seizures") and [coma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coma "Coma") as it is [neurotoxic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neurotoxic "Neurotoxic") in nature.[[169]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-169)

In certain organisms, ammonia is produced from atmospheric nitrogen by [enzymes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enzyme "Enzyme") called [nitrogenases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogenase "Nitrogenase"). The overall process is called [nitrogen fixation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_fixation "Nitrogen fixation"). Intense effort has been directed toward understanding the mechanism of biological nitrogen fixation. The scientific interest in this problem is motivated by the unusual structure of the [active site](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_site "Active site") of the enzyme, which consists of an Fe 7 MoS 9 ensemble.[[170]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-170)

Ammonia is also a metabolic product of [amino acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid "Amino acid")[deamination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deamination "Deamination") catalyzed by enzymes such as [glutamate dehydrogenase 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glutamate_dehydrogenase_1#Function "Glutamate dehydrogenase 1"). Ammonia excretion is common in aquatic animals. In humans, it is quickly converted to [urea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urea "Urea") (by [liver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liver "Liver")), which is much less toxic, particularly less [basic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#Basicity). This urea is a major component of the dry weight of [urine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine "Urine"). Most reptiles, birds, insects, and snails excrete [uric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uric_acid "Uric acid") solely as nitrogenous waste.

Ammonia plays a role in both normal and abnormal animal [physiology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physiology "Physiology"). It is [biosynthesised](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biosynthesis "Biosynthesis") through normal amino acid metabolism and is toxic in high concentrations. The [liver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liver "Liver") converts ammonia to [urea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urea "Urea") through a series of reactions known as the [urea cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urea_cycle "Urea cycle"). Liver dysfunction, such as that seen in [cirrhosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cirrhosis "Cirrhosis"), may lead to elevated amounts of ammonia in the blood ([hyperammonemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperammonemia "Hyperammonemia")). Likewise, defects in the enzymes responsible for the urea cycle, such as [ornithine transcarbamylase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ornithine_transcarbamylase "Ornithine transcarbamylase"), lead to [hyperammonemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperammonemia "Hyperammonemia"). Hyperammonemia contributes to the confusion and [coma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coma "Coma") of [hepatic encephalopathy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hepatic_encephalopathy "Hepatic encephalopathy"), as well as the neurological disease common in people with urea cycle defects and [organic acidurias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_aciduria "Organic aciduria").[[171]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-171)

Ammonia is important for normal animal acid/base balance. After formation of ammonium from [glutamine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glutamine "Glutamine"), [α-ketoglutarate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%CE%91-ketoglutarate "Α-ketoglutarate") may be degraded to produce two [bicarbonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bicarbonate "Bicarbonate") ions, which are then available as buffers for dietary acids. Ammonium is excreted in the urine, resulting in net acid loss. Ammonia may itself diffuse across the [renal tubules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nephron "Nephron"), combine with a hydrogen ion, and thus allow for further acid [excretion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excretion "Excretion").[[172]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-172)

Ammonium ions are a [toxic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxic "Toxic") waste product of [metabolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metabolism "Metabolism") in [animals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Animal "Animal"). In fish and aquatic invertebrates, it is excreted directly into the water. In mammals, sharks, and amphibians, it is converted in the [urea cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urea_cycle "Urea cycle") to [urea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urea "Urea"), which is less toxic and can be stored more efficiently. In birds, reptiles, and terrestrial snails, metabolic ammonium is converted into [uric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uric_acid "Uric acid"), which is solid and can therefore be excreted with minimal water loss.[[173]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-173)

[![Image 44](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e2/Jupiter.jpg/250px-Jupiter.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Jupiter.jpg)

Ammonia occurs in the [atmospheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_body_atmosphere "Celestial body atmosphere") of the outer giant planets such as [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter") (0.026% ammonia), [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn") (0.012% ammonia), and in the atmospheres and [ices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ice_giant "Ice giant") of [Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus "Uranus") and [Neptune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune "Neptune").

Ammonia has been detected in the atmospheres of the [giant planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_planet "Giant planet")[Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter"), [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn"), [Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus "Uranus") and [Neptune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune "Neptune"), along with other gases such as [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane"), [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen"), and [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium"). The interior of [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn") may include frozen ammonia crystals.[[174]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-174) It is found on [Deimos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deimos_(moon) "Deimos (moon)") and [Phobos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phobos_(moon) "Phobos (moon)")–the two [moons of Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moons_of_Mars "Moons of Mars").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Ammonia was first detected in interstellar space in 1968, based on [microwave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave "Microwave") emissions from the direction of the [galactic core](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way").[[175]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-175) This was the first [polyatomic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyatomic "Polyatomic") molecule to be so detected. The sensitivity of the molecule to a broad range of excitations and the ease with which it can be observed in a number of regions has made ammonia one of the most important molecules for studies of [molecular clouds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_cloud "Molecular cloud").[[176]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Ho-1983-176) The relative intensity of the ammonia lines can be used to measure the temperature of the emitting medium.

The following isotopic species of ammonia have been detected: NH 3,15 NH 3, NH 2[D](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuterium "Deuterium"), NHD 2, and ND 3. The detection of triply [deuterated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deuterium "Deuterium") ammonia was considered a surprise as deuterium is relatively scarce. It is thought that the low-temperature conditions allow this molecule to survive and accumulate.[[177]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-177)

Since its interstellar discovery, NH 3 has proved to be an invaluable spectroscopic tool in the study of the interstellar medium. With a large number of transitions sensitive to a wide range of excitation conditions, NH 3 has been widely astronomically detected–its detection has been reported in hundreds of journal articles. Listed below is a sample of journal articles that highlights the range of detectors that have been used to identify ammonia.

The study of interstellar ammonia has been important to a number of areas of research in the last few decades. Some of these are delineated below and primarily involve using ammonia as an interstellar thermometer.

#### Interstellar formation mechanisms

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ammonia&action=edit&section=49 "Edit section: Interstellar formation mechanisms")]

The interstellar abundance for ammonia has been measured for a variety of environments. The [NH 3]/[H 2] ratio has been estimated to range from 10−7 in small dark clouds[[178]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-178) up to 10−5 in the dense core of the [Orion molecular cloud complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_molecular_cloud_complex "Orion molecular cloud complex").[[179]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-179) Although a total of 18 total production routes have been proposed,[[180]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-180) the principal formation mechanism for interstellar NH 3 is the reaction:

[NH 4]+ + e− → NH 3 + H

The rate constant, _k_, of this reaction depends on the temperature of the environment, with a value of ![Image 45: {\displaystyle 5.2\times 10^{-6}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7589274d6876d4b1b31f51dfa3f166e2153b4713) at 10 K.[[181]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-181) The rate constant was calculated from the formula ⁠![Image 46: {\displaystyle k=a(T/300)^{B}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0795eb6f35c2293f678ceb85f3541dfd2e5568d)⁠. For the primary formation reaction, _a_=1.05×10−6 and _B_ = −0.47. Assuming an NH+4 abundance of ![Image 47: {\displaystyle 3\times 10^{-7}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/34b3bf5cffa431fe84d99317a7c9b8ee3eefaa5d)and an electron abundance of 10−7 typical of molecular clouds, the formation will proceed at a rate of 1.6×10−9 cm−3 s−1 in a molecular cloud of total density 10 5 cm−3.[[182]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-182)

All other proposed formation reactions have rate constants of between two and 13 orders of magnitude smaller, making their contribution to the abundance of ammonia relatively insignificant.[[183]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-183) As an example of the minor contribution other formation reactions play, the reaction:

H 2 + NH 2 → NH 3 + H

has a rate constant of 2.2×10−15. Assuming H 2 densities of 10 5 and [NH 2]/[H 2] ratio of 10−7, this reaction proceeds at a rate of 2.2×10−12, more than three orders of magnitude slower than the primary reaction above.

Some of the other possible formation reactions are:

H− + [NH 4]+ → NH 3 + H 2

[PNH 3]+ + e− → P + NH 3

#### Interstellar destruction mechanisms

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ammonia&action=edit&section=50 "Edit section: Interstellar destruction mechanisms")]

There are 113 total proposed reactions leading to the destruction of NH 3. Of these, 39 were tabulated in extensive tables of the chemistry among C, N and O compounds.[[184]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-184) A review of interstellar ammonia cites the following reactions as the principal dissociation mechanisms:[[176]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-Ho-1983-176)

NH 3 + [H 3]+ → [NH 4]+ + H 2 1

NH 3 + HCO+ → [NH 4]+ + CO 2

with rate constants of 4.39×10−9[[185]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-185) and 2.2×10−9,[[186]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-186) respectively. The above equations (**[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#math_1)**, **[2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#math_2)**) run at a rate of 8.8×10−9 and 4.4×10−13, respectively. These calculations assumed the given rate constants and abundances of [NH 3]/[H 2] = 10−5, [[H 3]+]/[H 2] = 2×10−5, [HCO+]/[H 2] = 2×10−9, and total densities of _n_ = 10 5, typical of cold, dense, molecular clouds.[[187]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-187) Clearly, between these two primary reactions, equation (**[1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#math_1)**) is the dominant destruction reaction, with a rate ≈10,000 times faster than equation (**[2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#math_2)**). This is due to the relatively high abundance of [H 3]+.

#### Single antenna detections

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ammonia&action=edit&section=51 "Edit section: Single antenna detections")]

Radio observations of NH 3 from the [Effelsberg 100-m Radio Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effelsberg_100-m_Radio_Telescope "Effelsberg 100-m Radio Telescope") reveal that the ammonia line is separated into two components–a background ridge and an unresolved core. The background corresponds well with the locations previously detected CO.[[188]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-188) The 25 m [Chilbolton telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chilbolton_Observatory "Chilbolton Observatory") in [England](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/England "England") detected radio signatures of ammonia in [H II regions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H_II_region "H II region"), HNH 2 O [masers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maser "Maser"), H–H objects, and other objects associated with star formation. A comparison of emission line widths indicates that turbulent or systematic velocities do not increase in the central cores of molecular clouds.[[189]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-189)

Microwave radiation from ammonia was observed in several galactic objects including W3(OH), [Orion A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(constellation) "Orion (constellation)"), [W43](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Westerhout_43 "Westerhout 43"), [W51](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Westerhout_51 "Westerhout 51"), and five sources in the galactic centre. The high detection rate indicates that this is a common molecule in the interstellar medium and that high-density regions are common in the galaxy.[[190]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-190)

#### Interferometric studies

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ammonia&action=edit&section=52 "Edit section: Interferometric studies")]

[VLA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Very_Large_Array "Very Large Array") observations of NH 3 in seven regions with high-velocity gaseous outflows revealed condensations of less than 0.1[pc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec") in L1551, S140, and [Cepheus A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cepheus_(constellation) "Cepheus (constellation)"). Three individual condensations were detected in Cepheus A, one of them with a highly elongated shape. They may play an important role in creating the bipolar outflow in the region.[[191]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-191)

Extragalactic ammonia was imaged using the VLA in [IC 342](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IC_342 "IC 342"). The hot gas has temperatures above 70 K, which was inferred from ammonia line ratios and appears to be closely associated with the innermost portions of the nuclear bar seen in CO.[[192]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-192)NH 3 was also monitored by VLA toward a sample of four galactic ultracompact HII regions: G9.62+0.19, G10.47+0.03, G29.96−0.02, and G31.41+0.31. Based upon temperature and density diagnostics, it is concluded that in general such clumps are probably the sites of massive star formation in an early evolutionary phase prior to the development of an ultracompact HII region.[[193]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-193)

#### Infrared detections

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ammonia&action=edit&section=53 "Edit section: Infrared detections")]

Absorption at 2.97 micrometres due to solid ammonia was recorded from interstellar grains in the [Becklin–Neugebauer Object](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Becklin%E2%80%93Neugebauer_Object "Becklin–Neugebauer Object") and probably in NGC 2264-IR as well. This detection helped explain the physical shape of previously poorly understood and related ice absorption lines.[[194]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-194)

A spectrum of the disk of Jupiter was obtained from the [Kuiper Airborne Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kuiper_Airborne_Observatory "Kuiper Airborne Observatory"), covering the 100 to 300 cm−1 spectral range. Analysis of the spectrum provides information on global mean properties of ammonia gas and an ammonia ice haze.[[195]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-195)

A total of 149 dark cloud positions were surveyed for evidence of 'dense cores' by using the (J,K) = (1,1) rotating inversion line of NH 3. In general, the cores are not spherically shaped, with aspect ratios ranging from 1.1 to 4.4. It is also found that cores with stars have broader lines than cores without stars.[[196]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-196)

Ammonia has been detected in the [Draco Nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Draco_(constellation) "Draco (constellation)") and in one or possibly two molecular clouds, which are associated with the high-latitude galactic [infrared cirrus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared_cirrus "Infrared cirrus"). The finding is significant because they may represent the birthplaces for the Population I metallicity B-type stars in the galactic halo that could have been borne in the galactic disk.[[197]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-197)

#### Observations of nearby dark clouds

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Ammonia&action=edit&section=54 "Edit section: Observations of nearby dark clouds")]

By balancing and stimulated emission with spontaneous emission, it is possible to construct a relation between [excitation temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excitation_temperature "Excitation temperature") and density. Moreover, since the transitional levels of ammonia can be approximated by a 2-level system at low temperatures, this calculation is fairly simple. This premise can be applied to dark clouds, regions suspected of having extremely low temperatures and possible sites for future star formation. Detections of ammonia in dark clouds show very narrow lines– indicative not only of low temperatures, but also of a low level of inner-cloud turbulence. Line ratio calculations provide a measurement of cloud temperature that is independent of previous CO observations. The ammonia observations were consistent with CO measurements of rotation temperatures of ≈10 K. With this, densities can be determined, and have been calculated to range between 10 4 and 10 5 cm−3 in dark clouds. Mapping of NH 3 gives typical clouds sizes of 0.1[pc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec") and masses near 1 solar mass. These cold, dense cores are the sites of future star formation.

Ultra-compact HII regions are among the best tracers of high-mass star formation. The dense material surrounding UCHII regions is likely primarily molecular. Since a complete study of massive star formation necessarily involves the cloud from which the star formed, ammonia is an invaluable tool in understanding this surrounding molecular material. Since this molecular material can be spatially resolved, it is possible to constrain the heating/ionising sources, temperatures, masses, and sizes of the regions. Doppler-shifted velocity components allow for the separation of distinct regions of molecular gas that can trace outflows and hot cores originating from forming stars.

Ammonia has been detected in external galaxies,[[198]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-198)[[199]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia#cite_note-199) and by simultaneously measuring several lines, it is possible to directly measure the gas temperature in these galaxies. Line ratios imply that gas temperatures are warm (≈50 K), originating from dense clouds with sizes of tens of parsecs. This picture is consistent with the picture within our [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") galaxy– hot dense molecular cores form around newly forming stars embedded in larger clouds of molecular material on the scale of several hundred parsecs (giant molecular clouds; GMCs).
