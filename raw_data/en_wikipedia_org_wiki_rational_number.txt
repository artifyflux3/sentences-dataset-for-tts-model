The rational numbers ⁠![Image 2: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5909f0b54e4718fa24d5fd34d54189d24a66e9a)⁠ are included in the [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_numbers "Real numbers")⁠![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc)⁠, which are included in the [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_numbers "Complex numbers")⁠![Image 4: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7)⁠, while rationals include the [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integers "Integers")⁠![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc)⁠, which in turn include the [natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_numbers "Natural numbers")⁠![Image 6: {\displaystyle \mathbb {N} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdf9a96b565ea202d0f4322e9195613fb26a9bed)⁠.

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **rational number** is a [number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number "Number") that can be expressed as the [quotient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient "Quotient") or [fraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fraction_(mathematics) "Fraction (mathematics)")⁠![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {p}{q}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b38d2684323653daafdd152b7e988594003897d)⁠ of two [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer"), a [numerator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerator "Numerator")p and a non-zero [denominator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denominator "Denominator")q.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-Rosen-1) For example, ⁠![Image 8: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {3}{7}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/15f2b824decf224d9a3143d4271666c7fba7ac83)⁠ is a rational number, as is every integer (for example, ![Image 9: {\displaystyle -5={\tfrac {-5}{1}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bef5aab2ced61ba1099051521167fbbe0d346e75)). The [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") of all rational numbers is often referred to as "**the rationals**",[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-2) and is [closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closure_(mathematics) "Closure (mathematics)") under [addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition "Addition"), [subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction "Subtraction"), [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication "Multiplication"), and [division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_(mathematics) "Division (mathematics)") by a nonzero rational number. It is a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)") under these operations and therefore also called the **field of rationals**[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-3) or the **field of rational numbers**. It is usually denoted by boldface **Q**, or [blackboard bold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blackboard_bold "Blackboard bold")⁠![Image 10: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/869719f08f506bf866043442858fb3da1d4b4b5b)⁠

A rational number is a [real number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number"). The real numbers that are rational are those whose [decimal expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal_expansion "Decimal expansion") either terminates after a finite number of [digits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Numerical_digit "Numerical digit") (example: 3/4 = 0.75), or eventually begins to [repeat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Repeating_decimal "Repeating decimal") the same finite [sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence "Sequence") of digits over and over (example: 9/44 = 0.20454545...).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-4) This statement is true not only in [base 10](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal "Decimal"), but also in every other integer [base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radix "Radix"), such as the [binary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_numeral_system "Binary numeral system") and [hexadecimal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexadecimal "Hexadecimal") ones (see [Repeating decimal §Extension to other bases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Repeating_decimal#Extension_to_other_bases "Repeating decimal")).

A [real number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") that is not rational is called [irrational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irrational_number "Irrational number").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-:0-5) Irrational numbers include the [square root of 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_root_of_2 "Square root of 2")(⁠![Image 11: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4afc1e27d418021bf10898eb44a7f5f315735ff)⁠),[π](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi "Pi"), [e](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E_(mathematical_constant) "E (mathematical constant)"), and the [golden ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golden_ratio "Golden ratio") (φ). Since the set of rational numbers is [countable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Countable_set "Countable set"), and the set of real numbers is [uncountable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncountable_set "Uncountable set"), [almost all](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almost_all "Almost all") real numbers are irrational.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-Rosen-1)

The field of rational numbers is the unique field that contains the [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer"), and is contained in any field containing the integers. In other words, the field of rational numbers is a [prime field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_field "Prime field"). A field has [characteristic zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_zero "Characteristic zero") if and only if it contains the rational numbers as a subfield. Finite [extensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_extension "Field extension") of ⁠![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5909f0b54e4718fa24d5fd34d54189d24a66e9a)⁠ are called [algebraic number fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_number_field "Algebraic number field"), and the [algebraic closure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_closure "Algebraic closure") of ⁠![Image 13: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5909f0b54e4718fa24d5fd34d54189d24a66e9a)⁠ is the field of [algebraic numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_number "Algebraic number").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-Gilbert-6)

In [mathematical analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_analysis "Mathematical analysis"), the rational numbers form a [dense subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dense_set "Dense set") of the real numbers. The real numbers can be constructed from the rational numbers by [completion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Completion_(metric_space) "Completion (metric space)"), using [Cauchy sequences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy_sequence "Cauchy sequence"), [Dedekind cuts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dedekind_cut "Dedekind cut"), or infinite [decimals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decimal "Decimal") (see [Construction of the real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Construction_of_the_real_numbers "Construction of the real numbers")).

In mathematics, "rational" is often used as a noun abbreviating "rational number". The adjective _rational_ sometimes means that the [coefficients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coefficient "Coefficient") are rational numbers. For example, a [rational point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_point "Rational point") is a point with rational [coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinates "Coordinates") (i.e., a point whose coordinates are rational numbers); a _rational matrix_ is a [matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") of rational numbers; a _rational polynomial_ may be a polynomial with rational coefficients, although the term "polynomial over the rationals" is generally preferred, to avoid confusion between "[rational expression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_fraction "Rational fraction")" and "[rational function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_function "Rational function")" (a [polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial") is a rational expression and defines a rational function, even if its coefficients are not rational numbers). However, a [rational curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_curve "Rational curve")_is not_ a curve defined over the rationals, but a curve which can be parameterized by rational functions.

Although nowadays _rational numbers_ are defined in terms of _ratios_, the term _rational_ is not a [derivation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphological_derivation "Morphological derivation") of _ratio_. On the contrary, it is _ratio_ that is derived from _rational_: the first use of _ratio_ with its modern meaning was attested in English about 1660,[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-7) while the use of _rational_ for qualifying numbers appeared almost a century earlier, in 1570.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-8) This meaning of _rational_ came from the mathematical meaning of _irrational_, which was first used in 1551, and it was used in "translations of Euclid (following his peculiar use of ἄλογος)".[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-10)

This unusual history originated in the fact that [ancient Greeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_mathematics "Greek mathematics") "avoided heresy by forbidding themselves from thinking of those [irrational] lengths as numbers".[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-11) So such lengths were _irrational_, in the sense of _illogical_, that is "not to be spoken about" (ἄλογος in Greek).[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-12)

### Irreducible fraction

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rational_number&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Irreducible fraction")]

Every rational number may be expressed in a unique way as an [irreducible fraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irreducible_fraction "Irreducible fraction")⁠![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {a}{b}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c62b49e759a120ce9f412ae2488478c38a87e5b6)⁠ where a and b are [coprime integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coprime_integers "Coprime integers") and _b_> 0. This is often called the [canonical form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_form "Canonical form") of the rational number.

Starting from a rational number ⁠![Image 15: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {a}{b}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c62b49e759a120ce9f412ae2488478c38a87e5b6)⁠ its canonical form may be obtained by dividing a and b by their [greatest common divisor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greatest_common_divisor "Greatest common divisor"), and, if _b_< 0, changing the sign of the resulting numerator and denominator.

Formal construction
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rational_number&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Formal construction")]

[![Image 50](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9c/Rational_Representation.svg/330px-Rational_Representation.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Rational_Representation.svg)

A diagram showing a representation of the equivalent classes of pairs of integers

The rational numbers may be built as [equivalence classes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_class "Equivalence class") of [ordered pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair "Ordered pair") of [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-:1-13)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-:2-14)

More precisely, let ⁠![Image 51: {\displaystyle (\mathbb {Z} \times (\mathbb {Z} \setminus \{0\}))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d79cffb163db19f1277ddd053a2e233c278bc89)⁠ be the set of the pairs (_m, n_) of integers such _n_ ≠ 0. An [equivalence relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_relation "Equivalence relation") is defined on this set by

![Image 52: {\displaystyle (m_{1},n_{1})\sim (m_{2},n_{2})\iff m_{1}n_{2}=m_{2}n_{1}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99e9ba99775e3f75b381e050dc0dceb24f3317a2)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-:1-13)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-:2-14)
Addition and multiplication can be defined by the following rules:

![Image 53: {\displaystyle (m_{1},n_{1})+(m_{2},n_{2})\equiv (m_{1}n_{2}+n_{1}m_{2},n_{1}n_{2}),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ca49c05f6cc6de96c5ffa8a6b6a9989210cbb3f)![Image 54: {\displaystyle (m_{1},n_{1})\times (m_{2},n_{2})\equiv (m_{1}m_{2},n_{1}n_{2}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/14439a52170bda9827a8940beb7cee8600ff0c6c)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-:1-13)
This equivalence relation is a [congruence relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_relation "Congruence relation"), which means that it is compatible with the addition and multiplication defined above; the set of rational numbers ⁠![Image 55: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5909f0b54e4718fa24d5fd34d54189d24a66e9a)⁠ is the defined as the [quotient set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_set "Quotient set") by this equivalence relation, ⁠![Image 56: {\displaystyle (\mathbb {Z} \times (\mathbb {Z} \backslash \{0\}))/\sim ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf3a1ed833ecfeeadbbff09a2888091e2d3a3c88)⁠ equipped with the addition and the multiplication induced by the above operations. (This construction can be carried out with any [integral domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_domain "Integral domain") and produces its [field of fractions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_of_fractions "Field of fractions").)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-:1-13)

The equivalence class of a pair (_m, n_) is denoted ⁠![Image 57: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {m}{n}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eced668d03c131e22957a0ab8bf8e56fe5906d99)⁠ Two pairs (_m_ 1, _n_ 1) and (_m_ 2, _n_ 2) belong to the same equivalence class (that is are equivalent) if and only if

![Image 58: {\displaystyle m_{1}n_{2}=m_{2}n_{1}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b6b852917408d0eb19e2a7610a77ce70eec0d30)
This means that

![Image 59: {\displaystyle {\frac {m_{1}}{n_{1}}}={\frac {m_{2}}{n_{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c4141c8183b703bed494d4a2b4960e69b02c9d33)
if and only if[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-:1-13)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-:2-14)

![Image 60: {\displaystyle m_{1}n_{2}=m_{2}n_{1}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b6b852917408d0eb19e2a7610a77ce70eec0d30)
Every equivalence class ⁠![Image 61: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {m}{n}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1413d4c83a94e754258e428fcd72d4fc6bcc2e3e)⁠ may be represented by infinitely many pairs, since

![Image 62: {\displaystyle \cdots ={\frac {-2m}{-2n}}={\frac {-m}{-n}}={\frac {m}{n}}={\frac {2m}{2n}}=\cdots .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/520ab75c19f8ac1b15a8590462c2539521cf5f01)
Each equivalence class contains a unique _[canonical representative element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representative\_(mathematics) "Representative (mathematics)")_. The canonical representative is the unique pair (_m, n_) in the equivalence class such that m and n are [coprime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coprime "Coprime"), and _n_> 0. It is called the [representation in lowest terms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irreducible_fraction "Irreducible fraction") of the rational number.

The integers may be considered to be rational numbers identifying the integer n with the rational number ⁠![Image 63: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {n}{1}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c0a51ba7c4a4f8abd44967459eefee6ae21ca2a)⁠

A [total order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_order "Total order") may be defined on the rational numbers, that extends the natural order of the integers. One has

![Image 64: {\displaystyle {\frac {m_{1}}{n_{1}}}\leq {\frac {m_{2}}{n_{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83c564a2e7b7ee8756878e5d98ec12e05a43ae0e)
If

![Image 65: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}&(n_{1}n_{2}>0\quad {\text{and}}\quad m_{1}n_{2}\leq n_{1}m_{2})\\&\qquad {\text{or}}\\&(n_{1}n_{2}<0\quad {\text{and}}\quad m_{1}n_{2}\geq n_{1}m_{2}).\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18d84d6896d62ad3a2a0830d20f79d3c068af0fd)
The set ⁠![Image 66: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5909f0b54e4718fa24d5fd34d54189d24a66e9a)⁠ of all rational numbers, together with the addition and multiplication operations shown above, forms a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-:1-13)

⁠![Image 67: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5909f0b54e4718fa24d5fd34d54189d24a66e9a)⁠ has no [field automorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_automorphism "Field automorphism") other than the identity. (A field automorphism must fix 0 and 1; as it must fix the sum and the difference of two fixed elements, it must fix every integer; as it must fix the quotient of two fixed elements, it must fix every rational number, and is thus the identity.)

⁠![Image 68: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5909f0b54e4718fa24d5fd34d54189d24a66e9a)⁠ is a [prime field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_field "Prime field"), which is a field that has no subfield other than itself.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-15) The rationals are the smallest field with [characteristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_(algebra) "Characteristic (algebra)") zero. Every field of characteristic zero contains a unique subfield isomorphic to ⁠![Image 69: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/869719f08f506bf866043442858fb3da1d4b4b5b)⁠

With the order defined above, ⁠![Image 70: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5909f0b54e4718fa24d5fd34d54189d24a66e9a)⁠ is an [ordered field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_field "Ordered field")[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-:2-14) that has no subfield other than itself, and is the smallest ordered field, in the sense that every ordered field contains a unique subfield [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism "Isomorphism") to ⁠![Image 71: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/869719f08f506bf866043442858fb3da1d4b4b5b)⁠

⁠![Image 72: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5909f0b54e4718fa24d5fd34d54189d24a66e9a)⁠ is the [field of fractions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_of_fractions "Field of fractions") of the [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer")⁠![Image 73: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89f4f38f32c2068bca9dc701d13b03dd4a5d52ab)⁠[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-16) The [algebraic closure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_closure "Algebraic closure") of ⁠![Image 74: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91185244fbdded6ea99a5e9e6603299128b10928)⁠ i.e. the field of roots of rational polynomials, is the field of [algebraic numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_number "Algebraic number").

The rationals are a [densely ordered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Densely_ordered "Densely ordered") set: between any two rationals, there sits another one, and, therefore, infinitely many other ones.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-:1-13) For example, for any two fractions such that

![Image 75: {\displaystyle {\frac {a}{b}}<{\frac {c}{d}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7c2a1c2dfed2e4b4b819fe015c24e839580c0385)
(where ![Image 76: {\displaystyle b,d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df7e58bcff2e2b515311524d5bf62b63f24afac5) are positive), we have

![Image 77: {\displaystyle {\frac {a}{b}}<{\frac {a+c}{b+d}}<{\frac {c}{d}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/390412ffa31cb47f35a26be80ac14277c8ee2d44)
Any [totally ordered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Totally_ordered "Totally ordered") set which is countable, dense (in the above sense), and has no least or greatest element is [order isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_isomorphism "Order isomorphism") to the rational numbers.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-17)

[![Image 78](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/85/Diagonal_argument.svg/250px-Diagonal_argument.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Diagonal_argument.svg)

Illustration of the countability of the positive rationals

The set of positive rational numbers is [countable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Countable "Countable"), as is illustrated in the figure.

More precisely, one can sort the fractions by increasing values of the sum of the numerator and the denominator, and, for equal sums, by increasing numerator or denominator. This produces a [sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence "Sequence") of fractions, from which one can remove the reducible fractions (in red on the figure), for getting a sequence that contains each rational number exactly once. This establishes a bijection between the rational numbers and the natural numbers, which maps each rational number to its rank in the sequence.

A similar method can be used for numbering all rational numbers (positive and negative).

As the set of all rational numbers is countable, and the set of all real numbers (as well as the set of irrational numbers) is uncountable, the set of rational numbers is a [null set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_set "Null set"), that is, [almost all](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almost_all "Almost all") real numbers are irrational, in the sense of [Lebesgue measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebesgue_measure "Lebesgue measure").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-18)

Real numbers and topological properties
---------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Rational_number&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Real numbers and topological properties")]

The rationals are a [dense subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dense_set "Dense set") of the [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_numbers "Real numbers"); every real number has rational numbers arbitrarily close to it.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-:1-13) A related property is that rational numbers are the only numbers with [finite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_set "Finite set") expansions as [regular continued fractions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continued_fraction "Continued fraction").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-19)

In the usual [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") of the real numbers, the rationals are neither an [open set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set") nor a [closed set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_set "Closed set").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-20)

By virtue of their order, the rationals carry an [order topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_topology "Order topology"). The rational numbers, as a subspace of the real numbers, also carry a [subspace topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subspace_topology "Subspace topology"). The rational numbers form a [metric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space") by using the [absolute difference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_difference "Absolute difference") metric ![Image 79: {\displaystyle d(x,y)=|x-y|,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4e52a0f0a6de665968d8e001b1c8796679e60e87) and this yields a third topology on ⁠![Image 80: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/869719f08f506bf866043442858fb3da1d4b4b5b)⁠ All three topologies coincide and turn the rationals into a [topological field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_field "Topological field"). The rational numbers are an important example of a space which is not [locally compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Locally_compact "Locally compact"). The rationals are characterized topologically as the unique [countable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Countable "Countable")[metrizable space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_property "Topological property") without [isolated points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isolated_point "Isolated point"). The space is also [totally disconnected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Totally_disconnected_space "Totally disconnected space"). The rational numbers do not form a [complete metric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Completeness_(topology) "Completeness (topology)"), and the [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_numbers "Real numbers") are the completion of ⁠![Image 81: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5909f0b54e4718fa24d5fd34d54189d24a66e9a)⁠ under the metric ![Image 82: {\displaystyle d(x,y)=|x-y|}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/994ad8add8719c1d111342afeb970648ffff41f2) above.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-:2-14)

In addition to the absolute value metric mentioned above, there are other metrics which turn ⁠![Image 83: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5909f0b54e4718fa24d5fd34d54189d24a66e9a)⁠ into a topological field:

Let p be a [prime number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prime_number "Prime number") and for any non-zero integer a, let ![Image 84: {\displaystyle |a|_{p}=p^{-n},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ee463f5ac05728239e7d02a166f6991c34ae412) where p n is the highest power of p[dividing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Divisor "Divisor")a.

In addition set ![Image 85: {\displaystyle |0|_{p}=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d1d5397ccca35ad2edd8646408aee241666695e) For any rational number ⁠![Image 86: {\displaystyle {\frac {a}{b}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/101faa653872fb89cf12cbea8c0a99b1c507943a)⁠ we set

![Image 87: {\displaystyle \left|{\frac {a}{b}}\right|_{p}={\frac {|a|_{p}}{|b|_{p}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5ad6485731d3d5e96ce8e14e0164e5d7b1a42c9)
Then

![Image 88: {\displaystyle d_{p}(x,y)=|x-y|_{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5338fdfb334c39fa5d6d6db14cd43ff677cc06f)
defines a [metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space") on ⁠![Image 89: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/869719f08f506bf866043442858fb3da1d4b4b5b)⁠[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number#cite_note-21)

The metric space ⁠![Image 90: {\displaystyle (\mathbb {Q} ,d_{p})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5150dba67390218f05dfaa8717f5e2c081ba98b7)⁠ is not complete, and its completion is the [p-adic number field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P-adic_number "P-adic number")⁠![Image 91: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} _{p}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eb4b0959c6687de4ffc0158e0b1d35d2137774d0)⁠[Ostrowski's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ostrowski%27s_theorem "Ostrowski's theorem") states that any non-trivial [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value_(algebra) "Absolute value (algebra)") on the rational numbers ⁠![Image 92: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c5909f0b54e4718fa24d5fd34d54189d24a66e9a)⁠ is equivalent to either the usual real absolute value or a [p-adic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P-adic_number "P-adic number") absolute value. Unlike in case of Euclidean norm completion, algebraic closure of p-adic completion is again not complete, and its completion not algebraically closed.
