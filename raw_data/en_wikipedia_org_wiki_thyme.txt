**Thyme** ([/t aɪ m/](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English "Help:IPA/English")) is a [culinary herb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culinary_herb "Culinary herb") consisting of the dried aerial parts of some members of the genus _[Thymus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymus\_(plant) "Thymus (plant)")_ of [flowering plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flowering_plant "Flowering plant") in the mint family [Lamiaceae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lamiaceae "Lamiaceae"). Thymes are native to [Eurasia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eurasia "Eurasia") and north Africa. Thymes have culinary, medicinal, and ornamental uses. The species most commonly cultivated and used for culinary purposes is _[Thymus vulgaris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymus\_vulgaris "Thymus vulgaris")_, native to Southeast Europe.

History
-------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Thyme&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: History")]

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1b/Flowering_thyme.JPG/250px-Flowering_thyme.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Flowering_thyme.JPG)

Flowering thyme

[Wild thyme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymus_serpyllum "Thymus serpyllum") grows in the [Levant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levant "Levant"), where it might have been first cultivated. [Ancient Egyptians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Egypt "Ancient Egypt") used common thyme (_Thymus vulgaris_) for [embalming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embalming "Embalming").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thyme#cite_note-1) The [ancient Greeks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Greece "Ancient Greece") used it in their baths and burnt it as [incense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incense "Incense") in their temples, believing it was a source of [courage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Courage "Courage").

The spread of thyme throughout Europe was thought to be due to the [Romans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Rome "Ancient Rome"), as they used it to purify their rooms and to "give an aromatic flavour to cheese and liqueurs".[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thyme#cite_note-Maud-2) In the European [Middle Ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Ages "Middle Ages"), the herb was placed beneath pillows to aid sleep and ward off nightmares.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thyme#cite_note-Huxley-3) In this period, women also often gave [knights](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knight "Knight") and warriors gifts that included thyme leaves, as it was believed to bring courage to the bearer. Thyme was also used as incense and placed on [coffins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coffin "Coffin") during [funerals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Funerals "Funerals"), as it was supposed to assure passage into the next life.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thyme#cite_note-4)

The name of the genus of fish _[Thymallus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymallus "Thymallus")_, first given to the [grayling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymallus_thymallus "Thymallus thymallus") (_T. thymallus_, described in the [1758 edition of _Systema Naturae_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/10th_edition_of_Systema_Naturae "10th edition of Systema Naturae") by Swedish zoologist [Carl Linnaeus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus "Carl Linnaeus")), originates from the faint smell of thyme that emanates from the flesh.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thyme#cite_note-5)

Cultivation
-----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Thyme&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Cultivation")]

Thyme is best cultivated in a hot, sunny location with well-drained soil. It is generally planted in the spring, and thereafter grows as a [perennial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perennial "Perennial"). It can be propagated by seed, cuttings, or dividing rooted sections of the plant. It tolerates drought well.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thyme#cite_note-6) It can be pruned after flowering to keep from getting woody.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thyme#cite_note-7)

Culinary use
------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Thyme&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Culinary use")]

[![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f2/Seombaengnihyang-cha.jpg/250px-Seombaengnihyang-cha.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Seombaengnihyang-cha.jpg)

_Seombaengnihyang-cha_ ([Ulleungdo thyme](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Thymus_japonicus&action=edit&redlink=1 "Thymus japonicus (page does not exist)") tea)

Thyme is a common component of the _[bouquet garni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bouquet\_garni "Bouquet garni")_, and of _[herbes de Provence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herbes\_de\_Provence "Herbes de Provence")_.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thyme#cite_note-8)

Thyme is sold both fresh and dried. While summer-seasonal, fresh greenhouse thyme is often available year-round. The fresh form is more flavourful but also less convenient; storage life is rarely more than a week. However, the fresh form can last many months if carefully frozen,[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thyme#cite_note-9) and thyme retains its flavour on drying better than many other herbs.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thyme#cite_note-10)

Fresh thyme is commonly sold in bunches of sprigs. A sprig is a single stem snipped from the plant.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thyme#cite_note-11) It is composed of a woody stem with paired leaf or flower clusters ("leaves") spaced 15 to 25 millimetres (1⁄2 to 1 inch) apart. A recipe may measure thyme by the bunch (or fraction thereof), or by the sprig, or by the tablespoon or teaspoon. Dried thyme is widely used in Armenia in [tisanes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tisane "Tisane").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thyme#cite_note-12)

Depending on how it is used in a dish, the whole sprig may be used, or the leaves removed and the stems discarded. Usually, when a recipe mentions a bunch or sprig, it means the whole form; when it mentions spoons, it means the leaves. It is perfectly acceptable to substitute dried for whole thyme. Leaves may be removed from stems either by scraping with the back of a knife, or by pulling through the fingers or tines of a fork.

Chemical and antimicrobial properties
-------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Thyme&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Chemical and antimicrobial properties")]

[![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/5d/Thymol2.svg/130px-Thymol2.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Thymol2.svg)

[Thymol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymol "Thymol") is the principal aromatic component of thyme.

The chemical composition of _Thymus_ (thyme) includes a variety of essential oils, flavonoids, phenolic acids, triterpenes, and other compounds. The essential oils found in thyme include [thymol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymol "Thymol"), which is a major component responsible for the plant's antiseptic properties, and carvacrol, another primary component with similar functions. Other essential oils present are p-cymene, γ-terpinene, linalool, and 1,8-cineole. [Gas chromatographic analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_chromatographic_analysis "Gas chromatographic analysis") reveals that the most abundant [volatile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound "Volatile organic compound") component of thyme leaves is [thymol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymol "Thymol"), at 8.55mg/g. Other components are [carvacrol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carvacrol "Carvacrol"), [linalool](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linalool "Linalool"), [α-terpineol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terpineol "Terpineol"), and 1,8-[cineole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cineole "Cineole").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thyme#cite_note-13)

Some of these compounds have beneficial properties. In particular, thymol has been historically used as an [antibiotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antibiotic "Antibiotic") and [antiseptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiseptic "Antiseptic"), especially in traditional medicine.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thyme#cite_note-14) Oil of thyme, the [essential oil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Essential_oil "Essential oil") of common thyme, contains 20–54% thymol.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thyme#cite_note-vulgaris-15) Thymol is an active ingredient in various commercially produced [mouthwashes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mouthwash "Mouthwash"), such as [Listerine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Listerine "Listerine").[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thyme#cite_note-16)

Flavonoids in thyme include luteolin-7-O-glucoside,[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thyme#cite_note-:0-17) a glycoside known for its antioxidant and anti-inflammatory properties, as well as apigenin, quercetin, and kaempferol. Phenolic acids such as rosmarinic acid,[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thyme#cite_note-:0-17) which is known for its antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antimicrobial activities, along with caffeic acid and chlorogenic acid, are also present in thyme.

Triterpenes, such as oleanolic acid and ursolic acid, are part of thyme's composition, contributing to its overall health benefits. Additionally, thyme contains tannins, which contribute to its astringent properties, as well as saponins and other minor compounds.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Important species and cultivars
-------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Thyme&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Important species and cultivars")]

For a longer list of species, see [Thymus (plant)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymus_(plant) "Thymus (plant)").

[![Image 15](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/91/Variegated_Lemon_Thyme_Thymus_citriodorus_variegata_Leaves_3264px.JPG/250px-Variegated_Lemon_Thyme_Thymus_citriodorus_variegata_Leaves_3264px.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Variegated_Lemon_Thyme_Thymus_citriodorus_variegata_Leaves_3264px.JPG)

Variegated lemon thyme

*   _[Thymus citriodorus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymus\_citriodorus "Thymus citriodorus")_ – various lemon thymes, orange thymes, lime thyme[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]
*   _[Thymus herba-barona](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymus\_herba-barona "Thymus herba-barona")_ (caraway thyme) is used both as a culinary herb and a ground cover, and has a very strong [caraway](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caraway "Caraway") scent due to the chemical [carvone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carvone "Carvone").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thyme#cite_note-18)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thyme#cite_note-19)
*   _[Thymus praecox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymus\_praecox "Thymus praecox")_ (mother of thyme, wild thyme), is cultivated as an ornamental, but is in [Iceland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iceland "Iceland") also gathered as a wild herb for cooking, and drunk as a warm [infusion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infusion "Infusion").
*   _[Thymus pseudolanuginosus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymus\_pseudolanuginosus "Thymus pseudolanuginosus")_ (woolly thyme) is not a culinary herb, but is grown as a ground cover.
*   _[Thymus serpyllum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymus\_serpyllum "Thymus serpyllum")_ (wild thyme, creeping thyme) is an important [nectar source](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nectar_source "Nectar source") plant for [honeybees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Honeybee "Honeybee"). All thyme species are nectar sources, but wild thyme covers large areas of droughty, rocky soils in southern Europe (both [Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greece "Greece") and Malta are especially famous for wild thyme honey) and North Africa, as well as in similar landscapes in the [Berkshire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berkshire_Mountains "Berkshire Mountains") and [Catskill Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catskill_Mountains "Catskill Mountains") of the northeastern US. The lowest growing of the widely used thyme is good for walkways. It is also an important caterpillar food plant for large and common blue butterflies.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thyme#cite_note-20)
*   _[Thymus vulgaris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thymus\_vulgaris "Thymus vulgaris")_ (common thyme, English thyme, summer thyme, winter thyme, French thyme,[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thyme#cite_note-21) or garden thyme)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thyme#cite_note-22) is a commonly used [culinary herb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Herb "Herb"). Common thyme is a [Mediterranean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_region "Mediterranean region") perennial which is best suited to well-drained soils and full sun.
