[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Traslazione_OK.svg/250px-Traslazione_OK.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Traslazione_OK.svg)

A translation moves every point of a figure or a space by the same amount in a given direction.

In [Euclidean geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry"), a **translation** is a [geometric transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_transformation "Geometric transformation") that moves every point of a figure, shape or space by the same [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distance_geometry "Distance geometry") in a given [direction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direction_(geometry) "Direction (geometry)"). A translation can also be interpreted as the addition of a constant [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") to every point, or as shifting the [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)") of the [coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system"). In a [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), any translation is an [isometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isometry "Isometry").

If ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6) is a fixed vector, known as the _translation vector_, and ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd73e3862cb92b016721b8c492eadb4e8a577527) is the initial position of some object, then the translation function ![Image 4: {\displaystyle T_{\mathbf {v} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9caf87fd0b979b2a34fabb83c43f78c3702d6d8b) will work as ![Image 5: {\displaystyle T_{\mathbf {v} }(\mathbf {p} )=\mathbf {p} +\mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d2ef190970526b4786ba110e009049bb934d3015).

If ![Image 6: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0) is a translation, then the [image](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_(mathematics) "Image (mathematics)") of a subset ![Image 7: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) under the [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)")![Image 8: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0) is the **translate** of ![Image 9: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) by ![Image 10: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0). The translate of ![Image 11: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) by ![Image 12: {\displaystyle T_{\mathbf {v} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9caf87fd0b979b2a34fabb83c43f78c3702d6d8b) is often written as ![Image 13: {\displaystyle A+\mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b1cbdd363a411f561d1df1d6ece16a792732ebe).

### Application in classical physics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Translation_(geometry)&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Application in classical physics")]

In [classical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_physics "Classical physics"), translational motion is movement that changes the [position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_(geometry) "Position (geometry)") of an object, as opposed to [rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation"). For example, according to Whittaker:[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry)#cite_note-Whittaker-1)

> If a body is moved from one position to another, and if the lines joining the initial and final points of each of the points of the body are a set of parallel straight lines of length _ℓ_, so that the orientation of the body in space is unaltered, the displacement is called a _translation parallel to the direction of the lines, through a distance ℓ_.

A translation is the operation changing the positions of all points ![Image 14: {\displaystyle (x,y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22a8c93372e8f8b6e24d523bd5545aed3430baf4) of an object according to the formula

![Image 15: {\displaystyle (x,y,z)\to (x+\Delta x,y+\Delta y,z+\Delta z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e9b242a68ba548eb06d7f4a6c0c96a63cc2270c2)
where ![Image 16: {\displaystyle (\Delta x,\ \Delta y,\ \Delta z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a1e88155be8e90e86498eb270fef6fc6636d1c9b) is the same [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") for each point of the object. The translation vector ![Image 17: {\displaystyle (\Delta x,\ \Delta y,\ \Delta z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a1e88155be8e90e86498eb270fef6fc6636d1c9b) common to all points of the object describes a particular type of [displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Displacement_(vector) "Displacement (vector)") of the object, usually called a _linear_ displacement to distinguish it from displacements involving rotation, called _angular_ displacements.

When considering [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime"), a change of [time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time "Time") coordinate is considered to be a translation.

The [translation operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shift_operator "Shift operator") turns a function of the original position, ![Image 18: {\displaystyle f(\mathbf {v} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/387ffaa6b5e65940435d2ccc9228c471621a4bb2), into a function of the final position, ![Image 19: {\displaystyle f(\mathbf {v} +\mathbf {\delta } )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/645c3deb2646de978c7b8d0c49c6955ae1e410d8). In other words, ![Image 20: {\displaystyle T_{\mathbf {\delta } }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75a6730ac2e36afbbc5c42952ed336bb698195bb) is defined such that ![Image 21: {\displaystyle T_{\mathbf {\delta } }f(\mathbf {v} )=f(\mathbf {v} +\mathbf {\delta } ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/36a613b788721a990e94d2ab4bbfa34d304ff483) This [operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operator_(mathematics) "Operator (mathematics)") is more abstract than a function, since ![Image 22: {\displaystyle T_{\mathbf {\delta } }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75a6730ac2e36afbbc5c42952ed336bb698195bb) defines a relationship between two functions, rather than the underlying vectors themselves. The translation operator can act on many kinds of functions, such as when the [translation operator acts on a wavefunction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_operator_(quantum_mechanics) "Translation operator (quantum mechanics)"), which is studied in the field of quantum mechanics.

The set of all translations forms the **translation group**![Image 23: {\displaystyle \mathbb {T} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c039979935c00b3b216cbb065999207872677f6), which is isomorphic to the space itself, and a [normal subgroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_subgroup "Normal subgroup") of [Euclidean group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_group "Euclidean group")![Image 24: {\displaystyle E(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a8b3b6ec0effe4cf5a6a54bd424fb04984dbe97d). The [quotient group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_group "Quotient group") of ![Image 25: {\displaystyle E(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a8b3b6ec0effe4cf5a6a54bd424fb04984dbe97d) by ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \mathbb {T} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c039979935c00b3b216cbb065999207872677f6) is isomorphic to the group of rigid motions which fix a particular origin point, the [orthogonal group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_group "Orthogonal group")![Image 27: {\displaystyle O(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/34109fe397fdcff370079185bfdb65826cb5565a):

![Image 28: {\displaystyle E(n)/\mathbb {T} \cong O(n)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0f0cb95c20928b99fbfd6d9f8a34c08c559854d7)
Because translation is [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative "Commutative"), the translation group is [abelian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group"). There are an infinite number of possible translations, so the translation group is an [infinite group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_group "Infinite group").

In the [theory of relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_relativity "Theory of relativity"), due to the treatment of space and time as a single [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime"), translations can also refer to changes in the [time coordinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_time "Coordinate time"). For example, the [Galilean group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilean_group "Galilean group") and the [Poincaré group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_group "Poincaré group") include translations with respect to time.

One kind of [subgroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subgroup "Subgroup") of the three-dimensional translation group are the [lattice groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lattice_(group) "Lattice (group)"), which are [infinite groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_group "Infinite group"), but unlike the translation groups, are [finitely generated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finitely_generated_group "Finitely generated group"). That is, a finite [generating set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generating_set_of_a_group "Generating set of a group") generates the entire group.

Matrix representation
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Translation_(geometry)&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Matrix representation")]

A translation is an [affine transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_transformation "Affine transformation") with _no_[fixed points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixed_point_(mathematics) "Fixed point (mathematics)"). Matrix multiplications _always_ have the [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)") as a fixed point. Nevertheless, there is a common [workaround](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Workaround "Workaround") using [homogeneous coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_coordinates "Homogeneous coordinates") to represent a translation of a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") with [matrix multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_multiplication "Matrix multiplication"): Write the 3-dimensional vector ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} =(v_{x},v_{y},v_{z})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0744af00f6b08dd2f26a2c76271389a3c25c5cb6) using 4 homogeneous coordinates as ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} =(v_{x},v_{y},v_{z},1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a7aef15200ee6dc28cde107cdc09a604c362af7).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry)#cite_note-2)

To translate an object by a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(geometry) "Vector (geometry)")![Image 31: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35c1866e359fbfd2e0f606c725ba5cc37a5195d6), each homogeneous vector ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd73e3862cb92b016721b8c492eadb4e8a577527) (written in homogeneous coordinates) can be multiplied by this **translation matrix**:

![Image 33: {\displaystyle T_{\mathbf {v} }={\begin{bmatrix}1&0&0&v_{x}\\0&1&0&v_{y}\\0&0&1&v_{z}\\0&0&0&1\end{bmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f8291f8c9e148126f931b49fa4fa9a96827aceeb)
As shown below, the multiplication will give the expected result:

![Image 34: {\displaystyle T_{\mathbf {v} }\mathbf {p} ={\begin{bmatrix}1&0&0&v_{x}\\0&1&0&v_{y}\\0&0&1&v_{z}\\0&0&0&1\end{bmatrix}}{\begin{bmatrix}p_{x}\\p_{y}\\p_{z}\\1\end{bmatrix}}={\begin{bmatrix}p_{x}+v_{x}\\p_{y}+v_{y}\\p_{z}+v_{z}\\1\end{bmatrix}}=\mathbf {p} +\mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a7bb4edd686c84393b22e27b4507f2e1ebc03aac)
The inverse of a translation matrix can be obtained by reversing the direction of the vector:

![Image 35: {\displaystyle T_{\mathbf {v} }^{-1}=T_{-\mathbf {v} }.\!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ed2142aa1a48d83d35e67221cf40430956784ed)
Similarly, the product of translation matrices is given by adding the vectors:

![Image 36: {\displaystyle T_{\mathbf {v} }T_{\mathbf {w} }=T_{\mathbf {v} +\mathbf {w} }.\!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/794b2b94e0ae0d7fb594270c932d6c031b06c1e1)
Because addition of vectors is [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative "Commutative"), multiplication of translation matrices is therefore also commutative (unlike multiplication of arbitrary matrices).

Translation of axes
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Translation_(geometry)&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Translation of axes")]

While geometric translation is often viewed as an [active transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_transformation "Active transformation") that changes the position of a geometric object, a similar result can be achieved by a [passive transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Passive_transformation "Passive transformation") that moves the coordinate system itself but leaves the object fixed. The passive version of an active geometric translation is known as a _[translation of axes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation\_of\_axes "Translation of axes")_.

Translational symmetry
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Translation_(geometry)&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Translational symmetry")]

An object that looks the same before and after translation is said to have [translational symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translational_symmetry "Translational symmetry"). A common example is a [periodic function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodic_function "Periodic function"), which is an [eigenfunction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenfunction "Eigenfunction") of a translation operator.

Translations of a graph
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Translation_(geometry)&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Translations of a graph")]

"Vertical translation" redirects here. For the concept in physics, see [Vertical separation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertical_separation "Vertical separation").

[![Image 37](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Translated_graph_of_a_function.png/330px-Translated_graph_of_a_function.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Translated_graph_of_a_function.png)

Compared to the graph _y_ = _f_(_x_), the graph _y_ = _f_(_x_ − _a_) has been translated horizontally by a, while the graph _y_ = _f_(_x_) + b has been translated vertically by b.

The [graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_function "Graph of a function") of a [real function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_function "Real function")f, the set of points ⁠![Image 38: {\displaystyle (x,f(x))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b21dd0c5c5815bc0516f679f631fd588ceb458d6)⁠, is often pictured in the [real coordinate plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_coordinate_plane "Real coordinate plane") with x as the horizontal coordinate and ⁠![Image 39: {\displaystyle y=f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2311a6a75c54b0ea085a381ba472c31d59321514)⁠ as the vertical coordinate.

Starting from the graph of f, a **horizontal translation** means [composing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_composition "Function composition")f with a function ⁠![Image 40: {\displaystyle x\mapsto x-a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5fb1a42aba777da68a7af2ecb9679a0226b4d5fb)⁠, for some constant number a, resulting in a graph consisting of points ⁠![Image 41: {\displaystyle (x,f(x-a))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/beb4406b0b71bc6c906c1c9a7e0cb9714e1cafc0)⁠. Each point ⁠![Image 42: {\displaystyle (x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41cf50e4a314ca8e2c30964baa8d26e5be7a9386)⁠ of the original graph corresponds to the point ⁠![Image 43: {\displaystyle (x+a,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bbd627858d06c17459b4d8872bdfc36073bbbb3c)⁠ in the new graph, which pictorially results in a horizontal shift.

A **vertical translation** means composing the function ⁠![Image 44: {\displaystyle y\mapsto y+b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb5f7d080dd0d2e259e958cf8639c98083f7f867)⁠ with f, for some constant b, resulting in a graph consisting of the points ⁠![Image 45: {\displaystyle {\bigl (}x,f(x)+b{\bigr )}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d143bba84225ddebe9bc64cf4e8c4c088a59159d)⁠. Each point ⁠![Image 46: {\displaystyle (x,y)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41cf50e4a314ca8e2c30964baa8d26e5be7a9386)⁠ of the original graph corresponds to the point ⁠![Image 47: {\displaystyle (x,y+b)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83b67b30a359932520ea7a581fa95eb26e8eb97e)⁠ in the new graph, which pictorially results in a vertical shift.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry)#cite_note-3)

For example, taking the [quadratic function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadratic_function "Quadratic function")⁠![Image 48: {\displaystyle y=x^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad1108c4c9ee8ac7de90b77f9bd27415b13b6bf1)⁠, whose graph is a [parabola](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabola "Parabola") with vertex at ⁠![Image 49: {\displaystyle (0,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d630d3e781a53b0a3559ae7e5b45f9479a3141a)⁠, a horizontal translation 5 units to the right would be the new function ⁠![Image 50: {\displaystyle y=(x-5)^{2}=x^{2}-10x+25}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4db1642234a168bc4f0611f7bcd52c8d19bdecf)⁠ whose vertex has coordinates ⁠![Image 51: {\displaystyle (5,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47a6520c29d84cc00fbe6235cbdcb0a8e79b95e8)⁠. A vertical translation 3 units upward would be the new function ⁠![Image 52: {\displaystyle y=x^{2}+3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/931e4a245570a2590951a53eec38296778a172b2)⁠ whose vertex has coordinates ⁠![Image 53: {\displaystyle (0,3)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/facd8877b12aa8477bf175fbfd33ce75a93955cd)⁠.

The [antiderivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiderivative "Antiderivative") of a function all differ from each other by a [constant of integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_of_integration "Constant of integration") and are therefore vertical translates of each other.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry)#cite_note-4)

For describing [vehicle dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vehicle_dynamics "Vehicle dynamics") (or movement of any [rigid body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body "Rigid body")), including [ship dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ship_motions "Ship motions") and [aircraft dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aircraft_principal_axes "Aircraft principal axes"), it is common to use a mechanical model consisting of six [degrees of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(mechanics) "Degrees of freedom (mechanics)"), which includes translations along three reference axes (as well as rotations about those three axes). These translations are often called [_surge_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ship_motions#Surge "Ship motions"), [_sway_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ship_motions#Sway "Ship motions"), and [_heave_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ship_motions#Heave "Ship motions").
