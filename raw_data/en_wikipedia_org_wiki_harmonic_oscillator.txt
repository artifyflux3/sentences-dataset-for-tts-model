In [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics"), a **harmonic oscillator** is a system that, when displaced from its [equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_equilibrium "Mechanical equilibrium") position, experiences a [restoring force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Restoring_force "Restoring force")_F_[proportional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportionality_(mathematics) "Proportionality (mathematics)") to the [displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Displacement_(geometry) "Displacement (geometry)")_x_: [![Image 1: {\displaystyle {\vec {F}}=-k{\vec {x}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f3424046f2aac1a7fe8e20ffb88be83fe16a9be4)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q170282) where _k_ is a [positive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive_number "Positive number")[constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coefficient "Coefficient").

The harmonic oscillator model is important in physics, because any mass subject to a force in stable equilibrium acts as a harmonic oscillator for small vibrations. Harmonic oscillators occur widely in nature and are exploited in many manmade devices, such as [clocks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clock "Clock") and radio circuits.

If _F_ is the only force acting on the system, the system is called a **simple harmonic oscillator**, and it undergoes [simple harmonic motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_harmonic_motion "Simple harmonic motion"): [sinusoidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sinusoidal "Sinusoidal")[oscillations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oscillation "Oscillation") about the [equilibrium point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equilibrium_point "Equilibrium point"), with a constant [amplitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amplitude "Amplitude") and a constant [frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency") (which does not depend on the amplitude).

If a frictional force ([damping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Damping_ratio "Damping ratio")) proportional to the [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") is also present, the harmonic oscillator is described as a **damped oscillator**. Depending on the friction coefficient, the system can:

*   Oscillate with a frequency lower than in the [undamped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Damping_ratio "Damping ratio") case, and an [amplitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amplitude "Amplitude") decreasing with time ([underdamped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Damping_ratio "Damping ratio") oscillator).
*   Decay to the equilibrium position, without oscillations ([overdamped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Damping_ratio "Damping ratio") oscillator).

The [boundary solution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boundary_value_problem "Boundary value problem") between an underdamped oscillator and an overdamped oscillator occurs at a particular value of the friction coefficient and is called [critically damped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critically_damped "Critically damped").

If an external time-dependent force is present, the harmonic oscillator is described as a _driven oscillator_.

Mechanical examples include [pendulums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum "Pendulum") (with [small angles of displacement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small-angle_approximation#Motion_of_a_pendulum "Small-angle approximation")), masses connected to [springs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spring_(device) "Spring (device)"), and [acoustical systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acoustics "Acoustics"). Other [analogous systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_oscillator#Equivalent_systems) include electrical harmonic oscillators such as [RLC circuits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RLC_circuit "RLC circuit"). They are the source of virtually all sinusoidal vibrations and waves.

Simple harmonic oscillator
--------------------------

Mass-spring harmonic oscillator

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/74/Simple_harmonic_motion_animation.gif/330px-Simple_harmonic_motion_animation.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Simple_harmonic_motion_animation.gif)

Simple harmonic motion

A simple harmonic oscillator is an oscillator that is neither driven nor [damped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Damping_ratio "Damping ratio"). It consists of a mass _m_, which experiences a single force _F_, which pulls the mass in the direction of the point _x_ = 0 and depends only on the position _x_ of the mass and a constant _k_. Balance of forces ([Newton's second law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_second_law "Newton's second law")) for the system is ![Image 4: {\displaystyle F=ma=m{\frac {\mathrm {d} ^{2}x}{\mathrm {d} t^{2}}}=m{\ddot {x}}=-kx.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e700293120d5c492003ea0f6fc075e23fe54481a)

Solving this [differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equation "Differential equation"), we find that the motion is described by the function [![Image 5: {\displaystyle x(t)=A\sin(\omega t+\varphi ),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0f6e24b31e650030cd66a261ee55c4d3d3fd6256)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q3299367) where [![Image 6: {\displaystyle \omega ={\sqrt {\frac {k}{m}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e98b4f368cf0f8db5deae9c10c2423ab28d2e6f8)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q834020)

The motion is [periodic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Periodic_function "Periodic function"), repeating itself in a [sinusoidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sine_wave "Sine wave") fashion with constant amplitude _A_. In addition to its amplitude, the motion of a simple harmonic oscillator is characterized by its [period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency")![Image 7: {\displaystyle T=2\pi /\omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/792661b202d08156c24a8784b65fa7105d66a444), the time for a single oscillation or its frequency ![Image 8: {\displaystyle f=1/T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6fd00fa0666739704cda30cc0ba290da82a6336c), the number of cycles per unit time. The position at a given time _t_ also depends on the [phase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_(waves) "Phase (waves)")_φ_, which determines the starting point on the sine wave. The period and frequency are determined by the size of the mass _m_ and the force constant _k_, while the amplitude and phase are determined by the starting position and [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity").

The velocity and [acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration") of a simple harmonic oscillator oscillate with the same frequency as the position, but with shifted phases. The velocity is maximal for zero displacement, while the acceleration is in the direction opposite to the displacement.

The potential energy stored in a simple harmonic oscillator at position _x_ is [![Image 9: {\displaystyle U={\tfrac {1}{2}}kx^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/14f0bb59c73b586c232ed523f0a6da798b477c5a)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q891408)

In real oscillators, friction, or damping, slows the motion of the system. Due to frictional force, the velocity decreases in proportion to the acting frictional force. While in a simple undriven harmonic oscillator the only force acting on the mass is the restoring force, in a damped harmonic oscillator there is in addition a frictional force which is always in a direction to oppose the motion. In many vibrating systems the frictional force _F_ f can be modeled as being proportional to the velocity _v_ of the object: _F_ f = −_cv_, where _c_ is called the _[viscous damping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscous\_damping "Viscous damping") coefficient_.

Driven harmonic oscillators
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Harmonic_oscillator&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Driven harmonic oscillators")]

[![Image 18](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d1/Driven_harmonic_oscillator_simulation_-_Square-wave%2C_large_mass.svg/330px-Driven_harmonic_oscillator_simulation_-_Square-wave%2C_large_mass.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Driven_harmonic_oscillator_simulation_-_Square-wave,_large_mass.svg)

Example simulation of a damped harmonic oscillator driven by a square wave.

Driven harmonic oscillators are damped oscillators further affected by an externally applied force _F_(_t_).

[Newton's second law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_second_law "Newton's second law") takes the form ![Image 19: {\displaystyle F(t)-kx-c{\frac {\mathrm {d} x}{\mathrm {d} t}}=m{\frac {\mathrm {d} ^{2}x}{\mathrm {d} t^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/794547ed5f938a625560845f14862c7bd5fbf745)

It is usually rewritten into the form ![Image 20: {\displaystyle {\frac {\mathrm {d} ^{2}x}{\mathrm {d} t^{2}}}+2\zeta \omega _{0}{\frac {\mathrm {d} x}{\mathrm {d} t}}+\omega _{0}^{2}x={\frac {F(t)}{m}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/61e55619b011f1893671806f3df229287dbc5333)

This equation can be solved exactly for any driving force, using the solutions _z_(_t_) that satisfy the unforced equation ![Image 21: {\displaystyle {\frac {\mathrm {d} ^{2}z}{\mathrm {d} t^{2}}}+2\zeta \omega _{0}{\frac {\mathrm {d} z}{\mathrm {d} t}}+\omega _{0}^{2}z=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/150594f17c0298d287b4016de14c02553047b27a)

and which can be expressed as damped sinusoidal oscillations: ![Image 22: {\displaystyle z(t)=Ae^{-\zeta \omega _{0}t}\sin \left({\sqrt {1-\zeta ^{2}}}\omega _{0}t+\varphi \right),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4204eef9ed6026571b2a4bc5f33db2c859706d44) in the case where _ζ_ ≤ 1. The amplitude _A_ and phase _φ_ determine the behavior needed to match the initial conditions.

In the case _ζ_< 1 and a unit step input with _x_(0) = 0: ![Image 23: {\displaystyle {\frac {F(t)}{m}}={\begin{cases}\omega _{0}^{2}&t\geq 0\\0&t<0\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e156202dfcfd10c658088932fc472c3fca9a3e65) the solution is ![Image 24: {\displaystyle x(t)=1-e^{-\zeta \omega _{0}t}{\frac {\sin \left({\sqrt {1-\zeta ^{2}}}\omega _{0}t+\varphi \right)}{\sin(\varphi )}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9aa0b7d0dc1648e04d0eea9d6bd65fb444dded1c)

with phase _φ_ given by

![Image 25: {\displaystyle \cos \varphi =\zeta .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f1fdf9b55ccd57a35c80cb9c17a29ceb68887da)

The time an oscillator needs to adapt to changed external conditions is of the order _τ_ = 1/(_ζω_ 0). In physics, the adaptation is called [relaxation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relaxation_(physics) "Relaxation (physics)"), and _τ_ is called the relaxation time.

In electrical engineering, a multiple of _τ_ is called the _settling time_, i.e. the time necessary to ensure the signal is within a fixed departure from final value, typically within 10%. The term _overshoot_ refers to the extent the response maximum exceeds final value, and _undershoot_ refers to the extent the response falls below final value for times following the response maximum.

### Sinusoidal driving force

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Harmonic_oscillator&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Sinusoidal driving force")]

[![Image 26](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/77/Mplwp_resonance_zeta_envelope.svg/330px-Mplwp_resonance_zeta_envelope.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Mplwp_resonance_zeta_envelope.svg)

Steady-state variation of amplitude with relative frequency ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \omega /\omega _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c152d9a23c80a377f843ca1190c8982bdfa90ff2) and damping ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \zeta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5c3916703cae7938143d38865f78f27faadd4ae) of a driven harmonic oscillator. This plot is also called the harmonic oscillator spectrum or motional spectrum.

In the case of a sinusoidal driving force: ![Image 29: {\displaystyle {\frac {\mathrm {d} ^{2}x}{\mathrm {d} t^{2}}}+2\zeta \omega _{0}{\frac {\mathrm {d} x}{\mathrm {d} t}}+\omega _{0}^{2}x={\frac {1}{m}}F_{0}\sin(\omega t),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/589fc8e2118cc5bfdcbdda8ce3d172afe508ac7a) where ![Image 30: {\displaystyle F_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f58df5f4307605e8fa07ae29d6262393b3b0c19) is the driving amplitude, and ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) is the driving [frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency") for a sinusoidal driving mechanism. This type of system appears in [AC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternating_current "Alternating current")-driven [RLC circuits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RLC_circuit "RLC circuit") ([resistor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_resistance "Electrical resistance")–[inductor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inductor "Inductor")–[capacitor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capacitor "Capacitor")) and driven spring systems having internal mechanical resistance or external [air resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_resistance "Air resistance").

The general solution is a sum of a [transient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transient_(oscillation) "Transient (oscillation)") solution that depends on initial conditions, and a [steady state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steady_state "Steady state") that is independent of initial conditions and depends only on the driving amplitude ![Image 32: {\displaystyle F_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f58df5f4307605e8fa07ae29d6262393b3b0c19), driving frequency ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8), undamped angular frequency ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \omega _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a713d16c489051d4f515e12b1f86061c6be799b), and the damping ratio ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \zeta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5c3916703cae7938143d38865f78f27faadd4ae).

The steady-state solution is proportional to the driving force with an induced phase change ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e): ![Image 37: {\displaystyle x(t)={\frac {F_{0}}{mZ_{m}\omega }}\sin(\omega t+\varphi ),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e3f9c4e21d1d3f4d9bbe35d26ab047cef8469b7f) where [![Image 38: {\displaystyle Z_{m}={\sqrt {\left(2\omega _{0}\zeta \right)^{2}+{\frac {1}{\omega ^{2}}}(\omega _{0}^{2}-\omega ^{2})^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a3d1c966b09c91a9feca34771c91345ba8b62e7)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q6421317) is the [absolute value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value") of the [impedance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_impedance "Mechanical impedance") or [linear response function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_response_function "Linear response function"), and ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \varphi =\arctan \left({\frac {2\omega \omega _{0}\zeta }{\omega ^{2}-\omega _{0}^{2}}}\right)+n\pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a9bb9a1ff792515288b8b57664b106f93f4cda57)

is the [phase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_(waves) "Phase (waves)") of the oscillation relative to the driving force. The phase value is usually taken to be between −180° and 0 (that is, it represents a phase lag, for both positive and negative values of the arctan argument).

For a particular driving frequency called the [resonance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resonance "Resonance"), or resonant frequency ![Image 40: {\textstyle \omega _{r}=\omega _{0}{\sqrt {1-2\zeta ^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b0df616e03368a6da7bd4a6f65a07020b68a74d), the amplitude (for a given ![Image 41: {\displaystyle F_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f58df5f4307605e8fa07ae29d6262393b3b0c19)) is maximal. This resonance effect only occurs when ![Image 42: {\displaystyle \zeta <1/{\sqrt {2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fb2a3ad351a6939c7406d2cd8fb171107289a27), i.e. for significantly underdamped systems. For strongly underdamped systems the value of the amplitude can become quite large near the resonant frequency.

The transient solutions are the same as the unforced (![Image 43: {\displaystyle F_{0}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/58ebe8b2d5551fb272cd4258940fe1e492592d02)) damped harmonic oscillator and represent the system's response to other events that occurred previously.

Parametric oscillators
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Harmonic_oscillator&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Parametric oscillators")]

A [parametric oscillator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametric_oscillator "Parametric oscillator") is a driven harmonic oscillator in which the drive energy is provided by varying the parameters of the oscillator, such as the damping or restoring force. A familiar example of parametric oscillation is "pumping" on a playground [swing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Swing_(seat) "Swing (seat)").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_oscillator#cite_note-Case-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_oscillator#cite_note-Case96-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_oscillator#cite_note-Roura-6) A person on a moving swing can increase the amplitude of the swing's oscillations without any external drive force (pushes) being applied, by changing the moment of inertia of the swing by rocking back and forth ("pumping") or alternately standing and squatting, in rhythm with the swing's oscillations. The varying of the parameters drives the system. Examples of parameters that may be varied are its resonance frequency ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) and damping ![Image 45: {\displaystyle \beta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ed48a5e36207156fb792fa79d29925d2f7901e8).

Parametric oscillators are used in many applications. The classical [varactor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Varactor "Varactor") parametric oscillator oscillates when the [diode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diode "Diode")'s capacitance is varied periodically. The circuit that varies the diode's capacitance is called the "pump" or "driver". In microwave electronics, [waveguide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waveguide_(electromagnetism) "Waveguide (electromagnetism)")/[YAG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yttrium_aluminum_garnet "Yttrium aluminum garnet") based parametric oscillators operate in the same fashion. The designer varies a parameter periodically to induce oscillations.

Parametric oscillators have been developed as low-noise amplifiers, especially in the radio and microwave frequency range. Thermal noise is minimal, since a reactance (not a resistance) is varied. Another common use is frequency conversion, e.g., conversion from audio to radio frequencies. For example, the [Optical parametric oscillator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_parametric_oscillator "Optical parametric oscillator") converts an input [laser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laser "Laser") wave into two output waves of lower frequency (![Image 46: {\displaystyle \omega _{s},\omega _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4583a193ecca57473b8366a3e6a700d337f324b6)).

Parametric resonance occurs in a mechanical system when a system is parametrically excited and oscillates at one of its resonant frequencies. Parametric excitation differs from forcing, since the action appears as a time varying modification on a system parameter. This effect is different from regular resonance because it exhibits the [instability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instability "Instability") phenomenon.

Universal oscillator equation
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Harmonic_oscillator&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Universal oscillator equation")]

The equation ![Image 47: {\displaystyle {\frac {\mathrm {d} ^{2}q}{\mathrm {d} \tau ^{2}}}+2\zeta {\frac {\mathrm {d} q}{\mathrm {d} \tau }}+q=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/56021e35216133259e03ce7065dbdc0ee41dd925) is known as the **universal oscillator equation**, since all second-order linear oscillatory systems can be reduced to this form.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] This is done through [nondimensionalization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nondimensionalization "Nondimensionalization").

If the forcing function is _f_(_t_) = cos(_ωt_) = cos(_ωt c τ_) = cos(_ωτ_), where _ω_ = _ωt_ _c_, the equation becomes ![Image 48: {\displaystyle {\frac {\mathrm {d} ^{2}q}{\mathrm {d} \tau ^{2}}}+2\zeta {\frac {\mathrm {d} q}{\mathrm {d} \tau }}+q=\cos(\omega \tau ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90ce673dc7d4fa2239a3471c9e5515f0b59ea6b4)

The solution to this differential equation contains two parts: the "transient" and the "steady-state".

The solution based on solving the [ordinary differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinary_differential_equation "Ordinary differential equation") is for arbitrary constants _c_ 1 and _c_ 2

![Image 49: {\displaystyle q_{t}(\tau )={\begin{cases}e^{-\zeta \tau }\left(c_{1}e^{\tau {\sqrt {\zeta ^{2}-1}}}+c_{2}e^{-\tau {\sqrt {\zeta ^{2}-1}}}\right)&\zeta >1{\text{ (overdamping)}}\\e^{-\zeta \tau }(c_{1}+c_{2}\tau )=e^{-\tau }(c_{1}+c_{2}\tau )&\zeta =1{\text{ (critical damping)}}\\e^{-\zeta \tau }\left[c_{1}\cos \left({\sqrt {1-\zeta ^{2}}}\tau \right)+c_{2}\sin \left({\sqrt {1-\zeta ^{2}}}\tau \right)\right]&\zeta <1{\text{ (underdamping)}}\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0fd653786f035970a17b47e3015316babc405831)

The transient solution is independent of the forcing function.

### Steady-state solution

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Harmonic_oscillator&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Steady-state solution")]

Apply the "[complex variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis "Complex analysis") method" by solving the [auxiliary equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auxiliary_equation "Auxiliary equation") below and then finding the real part of its solution: ![Image 50: {\displaystyle {\frac {\mathrm {d} ^{2}q}{\mathrm {d} \tau ^{2}}}+2\zeta {\frac {\mathrm {d} q}{\mathrm {d} \tau }}+q=\cos(\omega \tau )+i\sin(\omega \tau )=e^{i\omega \tau }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/efa75e7ba8cdbff426fbc3cde00e9c5220212fb2)

Supposing the solution is of the form ![Image 51: {\displaystyle q_{s}(\tau )=Ae^{i(\omega \tau +\varphi )}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f35563ef4d5958f9528da0ba4e871d5e2b2836ae)

Its derivatives from zeroth to second order are ![Image 52: {\displaystyle q_{s}=Ae^{i(\omega \tau +\varphi )},\quad {\frac {\mathrm {d} q_{s}}{\mathrm {d} \tau }}=i\omega Ae^{i(\omega \tau +\varphi )},\quad {\frac {\mathrm {d} ^{2}q_{s}}{\mathrm {d} \tau ^{2}}}=-\omega ^{2}Ae^{i(\omega \tau +\varphi )}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cdeea0c3b292ecd518efa5be2b244b4ff6f84341)

Substituting these quantities into the differential equation gives ![Image 53: {\displaystyle -\omega ^{2}Ae^{i(\omega \tau +\varphi )}+2\zeta i\omega Ae^{i(\omega \tau +\varphi )}+Ae^{i(\omega \tau +\varphi )}=(-\omega ^{2}A+2\zeta i\omega A+A)e^{i(\omega \tau +\varphi )}=e^{i\omega \tau }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd6144d28f5bf31bac970571179185802ee0adea)

Dividing by the exponential term on the left results in ![Image 54: {\displaystyle -\omega ^{2}A+2\zeta i\omega A+A=e^{-i\varphi }=\cos \varphi -i\sin \varphi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d839c4048506752b15fc147ab7a25fa1646c2f69)

Equating the real and imaginary parts results in two independent equations ![Image 55: {\displaystyle A(1-\omega ^{2})=\cos \varphi ,\quad 2\zeta \omega A=-\sin \varphi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c13aaea3ec1d1bc3ff450e0110a67f349a6235c5)

[![Image 56](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Harmonic_oscillator_gain.svg/250px-Harmonic_oscillator_gain.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Harmonic_oscillator_gain.svg)

[Bode plot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bode_plot "Bode plot") of the frequency response of an ideal harmonic oscillator

Squaring both equations and adding them together gives ![Image 57: {\displaystyle \left.{\begin{aligned}A^{2}(1-\omega ^{2})^{2}&=\cos ^{2}\varphi \\(2\zeta \omega A)^{2}&=\sin ^{2}\varphi \end{aligned}}\right\}\Rightarrow A^{2}[(1-\omega ^{2})^{2}+(2\zeta \omega )^{2}]=1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f51f64c7377f3d6b2f875fb3d71d65d3cbcbaaf)

Therefore, ![Image 58: {\displaystyle A=A(\zeta ,\omega )=\operatorname {sgn} \left({\frac {-\sin \varphi }{2\zeta \omega }}\right){\frac {1}{\sqrt {(1-\omega ^{2})^{2}+(2\zeta \omega )^{2}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e2f028f27ec7b8eee85c37554f93e6c4bb75e032)

Compare this result with the theory section on [resonance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resonance "Resonance"), as well as the "magnitude part" of the [RLC circuit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RLC_circuit "RLC circuit"). This amplitude function is particularly important in the analysis and understanding of the [frequency response](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency_response "Frequency response") of second-order systems.

To solve for _φ_, divide both equations to get ![Image 59: {\displaystyle \tan \varphi =-{\frac {2\zeta \omega }{1-\omega ^{2}}}={\frac {2\zeta \omega }{\omega ^{2}-1}}~~\implies ~~\varphi \equiv \varphi (\zeta ,\omega )=\arctan \left({\frac {2\zeta \omega }{\omega ^{2}-1}}\right)+n\pi .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a7b7c58d2523b4b5efd3b3108a6720e1627d027)

This phase function is particularly important in the analysis and understanding of the [frequency response](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency_response "Frequency response") of second-order systems.

Combining the amplitude and phase portions results in the steady-state solution ![Image 60: {\displaystyle q_{s}(\tau )=A(\zeta ,\omega )\cos(\omega \tau +\varphi (\zeta ,\omega ))=A\cos(\omega \tau +\varphi ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1fa76593380517dbc1f7b91ec7bbd46d71a5f4c1)

The solution of original universal oscillator equation is a [superposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superposition_principle "Superposition principle") (sum) of the transient and steady-state solutions: ![Image 61: {\displaystyle q(\tau )=q_{t}(\tau )+q_{s}(\tau ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/58a3be21fc2ceb4a5aa790adac45af6c7892bbee)

Application to a conservative force
-----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Harmonic_oscillator&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Application to a conservative force")]

The problem of the simple harmonic oscillator occurs frequently in physics, because a mass at equilibrium under the influence of any [conservative force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservative_force "Conservative force"), in the limit of small motions, behaves as a simple harmonic oscillator.

A conservative force is one that is associated with a [potential energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potential_energy "Potential energy"). The potential-energy function of a harmonic oscillator is ![Image 104: {\displaystyle V(x)={\tfrac {1}{2}}kx^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6234c75a74a8c3ffb4d34563a3731bd3c2a25a27)

Given an arbitrary potential-energy function ![Image 105: {\displaystyle V(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4ab3e825c2bf9c80d11d12e070a4626d48e03c61), one can do a [Taylor expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor_series "Taylor series") in terms of ![Image 106: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) around an energy minimum (![Image 107: {\displaystyle x=x_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04e899fc6eba0b387b91f070adc7bc4fe5a706cb)) to model the behavior of small perturbations from equilibrium.

![Image 108: {\displaystyle V(x)=V(x_{0})+V'(x_{0})\cdot (x-x_{0})+{\tfrac {1}{2}}V''(x_{0})\cdot (x-x_{0})^{2}+O(x-x_{0})^{3}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/204bb25699a930fa71c0d240c13472fadeda3549)

Because ![Image 109: {\displaystyle V(x_{0})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c1e14fd8d981e0c4c50d553845c7af7ed4cf32a6) is a minimum, the first derivative evaluated at ![Image 110: {\displaystyle x_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86f21d0e31751534cd6584264ecf864a6aa792cf) must be zero, so the linear term drops out: ![Image 111: {\displaystyle V(x)=V(x_{0})+{\tfrac {1}{2}}V''(x_{0})\cdot (x-x_{0})^{2}+O(x-x_{0})^{3}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4bda4789e7da0e74cdb47328673d6f5beb4883fd)

The [constant term](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_term "Constant term")_V_(_x_ 0) is arbitrary and thus may be dropped, and a coordinate transformation allows the form of the simple harmonic oscillator to be retrieved: ![Image 112: {\displaystyle V(x)\approx {\tfrac {1}{2}}V''(0)\cdot x^{2}={\tfrac {1}{2}}kx^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33b77bc4d24f90638f0f582393499c4c8d0f82b4)

Thus, given an arbitrary potential-energy function ![Image 113: {\displaystyle V(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4ab3e825c2bf9c80d11d12e070a4626d48e03c61) with a non-vanishing [second derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_derivative "Second derivative"), one can use the solution to the simple harmonic oscillator to provide an approximate solution for small perturbations around the equilibrium point.

[![Image 114](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Simple_pendulum_height.svg/250px-Simple_pendulum_height.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Simple_pendulum_height.svg)

A [simple pendulum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_pendulum "Simple pendulum") exhibits approximately simple harmonic motion under the conditions of no damping and small amplitude.

Assuming no damping, the differential equation governing a simple pendulum of length ![Image 115: {\displaystyle l}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/829091f745070b9eb97a80244129025440a1cfac), where ![Image 116: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) is the local [acceleration of gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_acceleration "Gravitational acceleration"), is [![Image 117: {\displaystyle {\frac {d^{2}\theta }{dt^{2}}}+{\frac {g}{l}}\sin \theta =0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79b04bf93ee1a9ae3f07f1312c2b5f492fca1ae1)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q20702)

If the maximal displacement of the pendulum is small, we can use the approximation ![Image 118: {\displaystyle \sin \theta \approx \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a604432d08fc1bd289e40ba08eed0c7ec697ea3) and instead consider the equation ![Image 119: {\displaystyle {\frac {d^{2}\theta }{dt^{2}}}+{\frac {g}{l}}\theta =0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d7e78dcacc84a3609d23398a7225a007a6ab933)

The general solution to this differential equation is ![Image 120: {\displaystyle \theta (t)=A\cos \left({\sqrt {\frac {g}{l}}}t+\varphi \right),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3bc62b0e8f4ca40dd80b619dae53c590ca8c0308) where ![Image 121: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) and ![Image 122: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e) are constants that depend on the initial conditions. Using as initial conditions ![Image 123: {\displaystyle \theta (0)=\theta _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3c35778b64cada0ad9674cc9ab59d2e45720c140) and ![Image 124: {\displaystyle {\dot {\theta }}(0)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0621c9db54a6d1a80709bc088248da2604be227), the solution is given by ![Image 125: {\displaystyle \theta (t)=\theta _{0}\cos \left({\sqrt {\frac {g}{l}}}t\right),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abaca26e5aaba63ed9c7404b1f177c86ccd6c65a) where ![Image 126: {\displaystyle \theta _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18b67de6bf25dba7a24e66967ff6319858798734) is the largest angle attained by the pendulum (that is, ![Image 127: {\displaystyle \theta _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18b67de6bf25dba7a24e66967ff6319858798734) is the amplitude of the pendulum). The [period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sine "Sine"), the time for one complete oscillation, is given by the expression [![Image 128: {\displaystyle \tau =2\pi {\sqrt {\frac {l}{g}}}={\frac {2\pi }{\omega }},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f45df9b311ed2583f84b70c828d4ef61a65c37a0)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q3382125) which is a good approximation of the actual period when ![Image 129: {\displaystyle \theta _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18b67de6bf25dba7a24e66967ff6319858798734) is small. Notice that in this approximation the period ![Image 130: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) is independent of the amplitude ![Image 131: {\displaystyle \theta _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18b67de6bf25dba7a24e66967ff6319858798734). In the above equation, ![Image 132: {\displaystyle \omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48eff443f9de7a985bb94ca3bde20813ea737be8) represents the angular frequency.

[![Image 133](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/22/Harmonic_oscillator.svg/250px-Harmonic_oscillator.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Harmonic_oscillator.svg)

Spring–mass system in equilibrium (A), compressed (B) and stretched (C) states

When a spring is stretched or compressed by a mass, the spring develops a restoring force. [Hooke's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hooke%27s_law "Hooke's law") gives the relationship of the force exerted by the spring when the spring is compressed or stretched a certain length: ![Image 134: {\displaystyle F(t)=-kx(t),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ef16df8a16c99d211a068dcbcfa0fc119017f3f) where _F_ is the force, _k_ is the spring constant, and _x_ is the displacement of the mass with respect to the equilibrium position. The minus sign in the equation indicates that the force exerted by the spring always acts in a direction that is opposite to the displacement (i.e. the force always acts towards the zero position), and so prevents the mass from flying off to infinity.

By using either force balance or an energy method, it can be readily shown that the motion of this system is given by the following differential equation: ![Image 135: {\displaystyle F(t)=-kx(t)=m{\frac {\mathrm {d} ^{2}}{\mathrm {d} t^{2}}}x(t)=ma,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27c6d512b060acf4ece55644c3fd51dfda4189dd) the latter being [Newton's second law of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion#Newton's_second_law "Newton's laws of motion").

If the initial displacement is _A_, and there is no initial velocity, the solution of this equation is given by ![Image 136: {\displaystyle x(t)=A\cos \left({\sqrt {\frac {k}{m}}}t\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af1aa3a3ba4541e207d8b6f34c968d81df96a442)

Given an ideal massless spring, ![Image 137: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) is the mass on the end of the spring. If the spring itself has mass, its [effective mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effective_mass_(spring-mass_system) "Effective mass (spring-mass system)") must be included in ![Image 138: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc).

#### Energy variation in the spring–damping system

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Harmonic_oscillator&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Energy variation in the spring–damping system")]

In terms of energy, all systems have two types of energy: [potential energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potential_energy "Potential energy") and [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy"). When a spring is stretched or compressed, it stores elastic potential energy, which is then transferred into kinetic energy. The potential energy within a spring is determined by the equation ![Image 139: {\textstyle U={\frac {1}{2}}kx^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f3a540617ac396620151201809774f6ec3f60635)

When the spring is stretched or compressed, kinetic energy of the mass gets converted into potential energy of the spring. By [conservation of energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_energy "Conservation of energy"), assuming the datum is defined at the equilibrium position, when the spring reaches its maximal potential energy, the kinetic energy of the mass is zero. When the spring is released, it tries to return to equilibrium, and all its potential energy converts to kinetic energy of the mass.
