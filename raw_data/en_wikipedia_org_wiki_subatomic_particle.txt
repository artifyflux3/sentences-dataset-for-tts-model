From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Quark_structure_proton.svg/250px-Quark_structure_proton.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Quark_structure_proton.svg)

A composite particle [proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") is made of two [up quarks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Up_quark "Up quark") and one [down quark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Down_quark "Down quark"), which are [elementary particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle").

In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), a **subatomic particle** is a [particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle "Particle") smaller than an [atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle#cite_note-1) According to the [Standard Model of particle physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model "Standard Model"), a subatomic particle can be either a [composite particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composite_particle "Composite particle"), which is composed of other particles (for example, a [baryon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon "Baryon"), like a [proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") or a [neutron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron"), composed of three [quarks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quarks "Quarks"); or a [meson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meson "Meson"), composed of two [quarks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quark "Quark")), or an [elementary particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle"), which is not composed of other particles (for example, quarks; or [electrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrons "Electrons"), [muons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muons "Muons"), and [tau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tau_(particle) "Tau (particle)") particles, which are called [leptons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leptons "Leptons")).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle#cite_note-2)[Particle physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_physics "Particle physics") and [nuclear physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_physics "Nuclear physics") study these particles and how they interact.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle#cite_note-3) Most force-carrying particles like [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photons "Photons") or [gluons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gluons "Gluons") are called [bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bosons "Bosons") and, although they have quanta of energy, do not have rest mass or discrete diameters (other than pure energy wavelength) and are unlike the former particles that have rest mass and cannot overlap or combine which are called [fermions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermions "Fermions"). The [W and Z bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W_and_Z_bosons "W and Z bosons"), however, are an exception to this rule and have relatively large rest masses at approximately 80[GeV/_c_ 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronvolt#Mass "Electronvolt") and 90 GeV/_c_ 2 respectively.

Experiments show that light could behave like a [stream of particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stream_of_particles "Stream of particles") (called [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon")) as well as exhibiting wave-like properties. This led to the concept of [wave–particle duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave%E2%80%93particle_duality "Wave–particle duality") to reflect that quantum-scale _particles_ behave both like particles and like [waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave "Wave"); they are occasionally called _wavicles_ to reflect this.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle#cite_note-4)

Another concept, the [uncertainty principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncertainty_principle "Uncertainty principle"), states that some of their properties taken together, such as their simultaneous [position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_(vector) "Position (vector)") and [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum"), cannot be measured exactly.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle#cite_note-5) Interactions of particles in the framework of [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory") are understood as creation and annihilation of _[quanta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum "Quantum")_ of corresponding [fundamental interactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_interaction "Fundamental interaction"). This blends particle physics with [field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory").

Even among [particle physicists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_physics "Particle physics"), the exact definition of a particle has diverse descriptions. These professional attempts at the definition of a particle include:[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle#cite_note-6)

*   A particle is a [collapsed wave function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_function_collapse "Wave function collapse")
*   A particle is an excitation of a [quantum field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory")
*   A particle is an [irreducible representation of the Poincaré group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_group "Poincaré group")
*   A particle is an observed thing

Subatomic particles are either "elementary", i.e. not made of multiple other particles, or "composite" and made of more than one elementary particle bound together.

The elementary particles of the [Standard Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model "Standard Model") are:[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle#cite_note-IntroSM1-7)

*   Six "[flavors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flavour_(particle_physics) "Flavour (particle physics)")" of [quarks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quark "Quark"): [up](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Up_quark "Up quark"), [down](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Down_quark "Down quark"), [strange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strange_quark "Strange quark"), [charm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charm_quark "Charm quark"), [bottom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bottom_quark "Bottom quark"), and [top](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Top_quark "Top quark");
*   Six types of [leptons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lepton "Lepton"): [electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron"), [electron neutrino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_neutrino "Electron neutrino"), [muon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muon "Muon"), [muon neutrino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muon_neutrino "Muon neutrino"), [tau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tau_(particle) "Tau (particle)"), [tau neutrino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tau_neutrino "Tau neutrino");
*   Twelve [gauge bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauge_boson "Gauge boson") (force carriers): the photon of [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism"), the three W and Z bosons of the [weak force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weak_interaction "Weak interaction"), and the eight gluons of the [strong force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strong_force "Strong force");
*   The [Higgs boson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higgs_boson "Higgs boson").

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/00/Standard_Model_of_Elementary_Particles.svg/500px-Standard_Model_of_Elementary_Particles.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Standard_Model_of_Elementary_Particles.svg)

The [Standard Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_Model "Standard Model") classification of elementary particles

All of these have now been discovered through experiments, with the latest being the top quark (1995), tau neutrino (2000), and Higgs boson (2012).

Various [extensions of the Standard Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics_beyond_the_Standard_Model "Physics beyond the Standard Model") predict the existence of an elementary [graviton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graviton "Graviton") particle and [many other elementary particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_elementary_particles#Hypothetical_particles "List of elementary particles"), but none have been discovered as of 2021.

The word hadron comes from Greek and was introduced in 1962 by [Lev Okun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lev_Okun "Lev Okun").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle#cite_note-8) Nearly all composite particles contain multiple quarks (and/or antiquarks) bound together by gluons (with a few exceptions with no quarks, such as [positronium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positronium "Positronium") and [muonium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muonium "Muonium")). Those containing few (≤5) quarks (including antiquarks) are called [hadrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadron "Hadron"). Due to a property known as [color confinement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Color_confinement "Color confinement"), quarks are never found singly but always occur in hadrons containing multiple quarks. The hadrons are divided by number of quarks (including antiquarks) into the [baryons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryons "Baryons") containing an odd number of quarks (almost always 3), of which the [proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton") and [neutron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron") (the two [nucleons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleons "Nucleons")) are by far the best known; and the [mesons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meson "Meson") containing an even number of quarks (almost always 2, one quark and one antiquark), of which the [pions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pion "Pion") and [kaons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaon "Kaon") are the best known.

Except for the proton and neutron, all other hadrons are unstable and decay into other particles in microseconds or less. A proton is made of two [up quarks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Up_quark "Up quark") and one [down quark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Down_quark "Down quark"), while the neutron is made of two down quarks and one up quark. These commonly bind together into an atomic nucleus, e.g. a helium-4 nucleus is composed of two protons and two neutrons. Most hadrons do not live long enough to bind into nucleus-like composites; those that do (other than the proton and neutron) form [exotic nuclei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exotic_nuclei "Exotic nuclei").

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Bosons-Hadrons-Fermions-RGB.svg/500px-Bosons-Hadrons-Fermions-RGB.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Bosons-Hadrons-Fermions-RGB.svg)

Overlap between [bosons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boson "Boson"), [hadrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadron "Hadron"), and [fermions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermion "Fermion")

Any subatomic particle, like any particle in the [three-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space") that obeys the [laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scientific_law "Scientific law") of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), can be either a boson (with integer [spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)")) or a fermion (with odd half-integer spin).

In the Standard Model, all the elementary fermions have spin 1/2, and are divided into the quarks which carry [color charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Color_charge "Color charge") and therefore feel the strong interaction, and the [leptons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leptons "Leptons") which do not. The elementary bosons comprise the gauge bosons (photon, W and Z, gluons) with spin 1, while the Higgs boson is the only elementary particle with spin zero.

The hypothetical graviton is required theoretically to have spin 2, but is not part of the Standard Model. Some extensions such as [supersymmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supersymmetry "Supersymmetry") predict additional elementary particles with spin 3/2, but none have been discovered as of 2023.

Due to the laws for spin of composite particles, the baryons (3 quarks) have spin either 1/2 or 3/2 and are therefore fermions; the mesons (2 quarks) have integer spin of either 0 or 1 and are therefore bosons.

In [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity"), the [energy of a particle at rest equals its mass times the speed of light squared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass%E2%80%93energy_equivalence "Mass–energy equivalence"), _E_ = _mc_ 2. That is, [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") can be expressed in terms of [energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy "Energy") and vice versa. If a particle has a [frame of reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_of_reference "Frame of reference") in which it lies [at rest](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rest_(physics) "Rest (physics)"), then it has a positive [rest mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rest_mass "Rest mass") and is referred to as _massive_.

All composite particles are massive. Baryons (meaning "heavy") tend to have greater mass than mesons (meaning "intermediate"), which in turn tend to be heavier than leptons (meaning "lightweight"), but the heaviest lepton (the [tau particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tau_particle "Tau particle")) is heavier than the two lightest flavours of baryons ([nucleons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleon "Nucleon")). It is also certain that any particle with an [electric charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge") is massive.

When originally defined in the 1950s, the terms baryons, mesons and leptons referred to masses; however, after the quark model became accepted in the 1970s, it was recognised that baryons are composites of three quarks, mesons are composites of one quark and one antiquark, while leptons are elementary and are defined as the elementary fermions with no color charge.

All [massless particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massless_particle "Massless particle") (particles whose [invariant mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_mass "Invariant mass") is zero) are elementary. These include the photon and gluon, although the latter cannot be isolated.

Most subatomic particles are not stable. All leptons, as well as baryons [decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_decay "Particle decay") by either the strong force or weak force (except for the proton). Protons are not known to [decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton_decay "Proton decay"), although whether they are "truly" stable is unknown, as some very important Grand Unified Theories (GUTs) actually require it. The μ and τ muons, as well as their antiparticles, decay by the weak force. Neutrinos (and antineutrinos) do not decay, but a related phenomenon of [neutrino oscillations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino_oscillation "Neutrino oscillation") is thought to exist even in vacuums. The electron and its antiparticle, the [positron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positron "Positron"), are theoretically stable due to [charge conservation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charge_conservation "Charge conservation") unless a lighter particle having [magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_value "Absolute value") of electric charge ≤[_e_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_charge "Elementary charge") exists (which is unlikely). Its charge is not shown yet.

All observable subatomic particles have their electric charge an [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer") multiple of the [elementary charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_charge "Elementary charge"). The Standard Model's quarks have "non-integer" electric charges, namely, multiple of ⁠1/3⁠_e_, but quarks (and other combinations with non-integer electric charge) cannot be isolated due to [color confinement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Color_confinement "Color confinement"). For baryons, mesons, and their antiparticles the constituent quarks' charges sum up to an integer multiple of _e_.

Through the work of [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein"), [Satyendra Nath Bose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satyendra_Nath_Bose "Satyendra Nath Bose"), [Louis de Broglie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_de_Broglie "Louis de Broglie"), and many others, current scientific theory holds that _all_ particles also have a wave nature.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle#cite_note-9) This has been verified not only for elementary particles but also for compound particles like atoms and even molecules. In fact, according to traditional formulations of non-relativistic quantum mechanics, wave–particle duality applies to all objects, even macroscopic ones; although the wave properties of macroscopic objects cannot be detected due to their small wavelengths.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle#cite_note-10)

Interactions between particles have been scrutinized for many centuries, and a few simple laws underpin how particles behave in collisions and interactions. The most fundamental of these are the laws of [conservation of energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_energy "Conservation of energy") and [conservation of momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_momentum "Conservation of momentum"), which let us make calculations of particle interactions on scales of magnitude that range from stars to quarks.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle#cite_note-11) These are the prerequisite basics of [Newtonian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newtonian_mechanics "Newtonian mechanics"), a series of statements and equations in _[Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophiae\_Naturalis\_Principia\_Mathematica "Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica")_, originally published in 1687.

The negatively charged electron has a mass of about ⁠1/1836⁠ of that of a [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") atom. The remainder of the hydrogen atom's mass comes from the positively charged [proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton"). The [atomic number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_number "Atomic number") of an element is the number of protons in its nucleus. Neutrons are neutral particles having a mass slightly greater than that of the proton. Different [isotopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope "Isotope") of the same element contain the same number of protons but different numbers of neutrons. The [mass number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_number "Mass number") of an isotope is the total number of [nucleons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleon "Nucleon") (neutrons and protons collectively).

[Chemistry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemistry "Chemistry") concerns itself with how electron sharing binds atoms into structures such as crystals and [molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule"). The subatomic particles considered important in the understanding of chemistry are the [electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron"), the [proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton "Proton"), and the [neutron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron"). [Nuclear physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nuclear_physics "Nuclear physics") deals with how protons and neutrons arrange themselves in nuclei. The study of subatomic particles, atoms and molecules, and their structure and interactions, requires [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"). Analyzing processes that change the numbers and types of particles requires [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory"). The study of subatomic particles _per se_ is called [particle physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_physics "Particle physics"). The term _[high-energy physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-energy\_physics "High-energy physics")_ is nearly synonymous to "particle physics" since creation of particles requires high energies: it occurs only as a result of [cosmic rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_ray "Cosmic ray"), or in [particle accelerators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_accelerator "Particle accelerator"). [Particle phenomenology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenomenology_(particle_physics) "Phenomenology (particle physics)") systematizes the knowledge about subatomic particles obtained from these experiments.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle#cite_note-12)

The term "_subatomic_ particle" is largely a [retronym](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retronym "Retronym") of the 1960s, used to distinguish a large number of [baryons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon "Baryon") and [mesons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meson "Meson") (which comprise [hadrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hadron "Hadron")) from particles that are now thought to be [truly elementary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_particle "Elementary particle"). Before that hadrons were usually classified as "elementary" because their composition was unknown.
