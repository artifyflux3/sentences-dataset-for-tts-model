Only the edges of the higher-dimensional cells are shown.

In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **hyperrectangle** (also called a **box**, **hyperbox**, **![Image 10: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-cell** or **orthotope**[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperrectangle#cite_note-regpoly-2)), is the generalization of a [rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle "Rectangle") (a [plane figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_figure "Plane figure")) and the [rectangular cuboid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangular_cuboid "Rectangular cuboid") (a [solid figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_figure "Solid figure")) to [higher dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higher_dimension "Higher dimension"). A [necessary and sufficient condition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Necessary_and_sufficient_condition "Necessary and sufficient condition") is that it is [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_(geometry) "Congruence (geometry)") to the [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product") of finite [intervals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperrectangle#cite_note-3) This means that a ![Image 11: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-dimensional rectangular solid has each of its edges equal to one of the closed intervals used in the definition. Every ![Image 12: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-cell is [compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_(mathematics) "Compact (mathematics)").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperrectangle#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperrectangle#cite_note-5)

If all of the edges are equal length, it is a _[hypercube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube "Hypercube")_. A hyperrectangle is a special case of a [parallelotope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelohedron#Related_shapes "Parallelohedron").

For every integer ![Image 13: {\displaystyle i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/add78d8608ad86e54951b8c8bd6c8d8416533d20) from ![Image 14: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf) to ![Image 15: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40), let ![Image 16: {\displaystyle a_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bc77764b2e74e64a63341054fa90f3e07db275f) and ![Image 17: {\displaystyle b_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40a8c2db2990a53c683e75961826167c5adac7c3) be [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_numbers "Real numbers") such that ![Image 18: {\displaystyle a_{i}<b_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab5c5211ef5ece8280e4d8421dc4c779e577b757). The set of all points ![Image 19: {\displaystyle x=(x_{1},\dots ,x_{k})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd77c98584c3731108ea1df6ad29b354025fd482) in ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1bcd8908c9fa46eb979ef7b67d1bb65eb3692cbb) whose coordinates satisfy the inequalities ![Image 21: {\displaystyle a_{i}\leq x_{i}\leq b_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9540ac74746402c9766380c81e789c25f0fd2935) is a **![Image 22: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-cell**.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperrectangle#cite_note-6)

A ![Image 23: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-cell of dimension ![Image 24: {\displaystyle k\leq 3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/868d6282f4395dba7c18e6d57371e291429e15e5) is especially simple. For example, a 1-cell is simply the interval ![Image 25: {\displaystyle [a,b]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c4b788fc5c637e26ee98b45f89a5c08c85f7935) with ![Image 26: {\displaystyle a<b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91a7698e4c7401bb321f97888b872b583a9e4642). A 2-cell is the rectangle formed by the Cartesian product of two closed intervals, and a 3-cell is a rectangular solid.

The sides and edges of a ![Image 27: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-cell need not be equal in (Euclidean) length; although the [unit cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_cube "Unit cube") (which has boundaries of equal Euclidean length) is a 3-cell, the set of all 3-cells with equal-length edges is a strict subset of the set of all 3-cells.

A four-dimensional orthotope is likely a hypercuboid.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperrectangle#cite_note-7)

The special case of an n-dimensional orthotope where all edges have equal length is the n-[cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube "Cube") or hypercube.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperrectangle#cite_note-regpoly-2)

By analogy, the term "hyperrectangle" can refer to Cartesian products of [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal "Orthogonal") intervals of other kinds, such as ranges of keys in [database theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Database_theory "Database theory") or ranges of [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer"), rather than [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperrectangle#cite_note-8)

The [dual polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polytope "Dual polytope") of an n-orthotope has been variously called a rectangular n-[orthoplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthoplex "Orthoplex"), rhombic n-[fusil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fusil_(geometry) "Fusil (geometry)"), or n-[lozenge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lozenge_(shape) "Lozenge (shape)"). It is constructed by 2 _n_ points located in the center of the orthotope rectangular faces.

An n-fusil's [SchlÃ¤fli symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_symbol "SchlÃ¤fli symbol") can be represented by a sum of n orthogonal line segments: { } + { } + ... + { }  or _n_{ }.

A 1-fusil is a [line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment"). A 2-fusil is a [rhombus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombus "Rhombus"). Its plane cross selections in all pairs of axes are [rhombi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombus "Rhombus").
