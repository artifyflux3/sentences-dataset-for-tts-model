[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ad/Ruled_hyperboloid.jpg/330px-Ruled_hyperboloid.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Ruled_hyperboloid.jpg)

A string model of a portion of a regulus and its opposite to show the rules on a hyperboloid of one sheet

In three-dimensional space, a **regulus**_R_ is a set of [skew lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew_lines "Skew lines"), every point of which is on a [transversal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transversal_(combinatorics) "Transversal (combinatorics)") which intersects an element of _R_ only once, and such that every point on a transversal lies on a line of _R_.

The set of transversals of _R_ forms an **opposite regulus**_S_. In ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) the union _R_ ∪ _S_ is the [ruled surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ruled_surface "Ruled surface") of a [hyperboloid of one sheet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperboloid_of_one_sheet "Hyperboloid of one sheet").

Three skew lines determine a regulus:

The locus of lines meeting three given skew lines is called a _regulus_. [Gallucci's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Skew_lines#Gallucci's_theorem "Skew lines") shows that the lines meeting the generators of the regulus (including the original three lines) form another "associated" regulus, such that every generator of either regulus meets every generator of the other. The two reguli are the two systems of generators of a _ruled quadric_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regulus_(geometry)#cite_note-1)
According to [Charlotte Scott](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlotte_Scott "Charlotte Scott"), "The regulus supplies extremely simple proofs of the properties of a conic...the theorems of Chasles, [Brianchon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brianchon%27s_theorem "Brianchon's theorem"), and [Pascal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pascal%27s_theorem "Pascal's theorem") ..."[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regulus_(geometry)#cite_note-2)

In a [finite geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_geometry "Finite geometry") PG(3, _q_), a regulus has _q_ + 1 lines.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regulus_(geometry)#cite_note-3) For example, in 1954 [William Edge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Edge_(mathematician) "William Edge (mathematician)") described a pair of reguli of four lines each in PG(3,3).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regulus_(geometry)#cite_note-4)

[Robert J. T. Bell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_J._T._Bell "Robert J. T. Bell") described how the regulus is generated by a moving straight line. First, the hyperboloid ![Image 3: {\displaystyle {\frac {x^{2}}{a^{2}}}+{\frac {y^{2}}{b^{2}}}-{\frac {z^{2}}{c^{2}}}\ =\ 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/84da41b9e0588dbf60d34f9a35fc564b0c1ee8c5) is factored as

![Image 4: {\displaystyle \left({\frac {x}{a}}+{\frac {z}{c}}\right)\left({\frac {x}{a}}-{\frac {z}{c}}\right)\ =\ \left(1+{\frac {y}{b}}\right)\left(1-{\frac {y}{b}}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99a20a20d7c66ed0e86fef4e346ec5de76a156d9)
Then two systems of lines, parametrized by λ and μ satisfy this equation:

![Image 5: {\displaystyle {\frac {x}{a}}+{\frac {z}{c}}\ =\ \lambda \left(1+{\frac {y}{b}}\right),\quad {\frac {x}{a}}-{\frac {z}{c}}\ =\ {\frac {1}{\lambda }}\left(1-{\frac {y}{b}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e2f730caf78f6352e87b7580db9e74b7eefb73c7) and![Image 6: {\displaystyle {\frac {x}{a}}-{\frac {z}{c}}\ =\ \mu \left(1+{\frac {y}{b}}\right),\quad {\frac {x}{a}}+{\frac {z}{c}}\ =\ {\frac {1}{\mu }}\left(1-{\frac {y}{b}}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1262b04c580795aed5c2eee4acef5610459848f2)
No member of the first set of lines is a member of the second. As λ or μ varies, the hyperboloid is generated. The two sets represent a regulus and its opposite. Using [analytic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytic_geometry "Analytic geometry"), Bell proves that no two generators in a set intersect, and that any two generators in opposite reguli do intersect and form the plane tangent to the hyperboloid at that point. (page 155).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regulus_(geometry)#cite_note-5)
