Chemical compound

The **hydroperoxyl [radical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_(chemistry) "Radical (chemistry)")**, also known as the **hydrogen superoxide**, is the [protonated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonated "Protonated") form of [superoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superoxide "Superoxide") with the [chemical formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula") HO 2, also written HOO•. This species plays an important role in the atmosphere and as a reactive oxygen species in cell biology.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroperoxyl#cite_note-CR-2)

Structure and reactions
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydroperoxyl&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Structure and reactions")]

The molecule has a bent structure.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroperoxyl#cite_note-3)

The superoxide anion, •

O−

2, and the hydroperoxyl radical exist in [equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_equilibrium "Chemical equilibrium") in [aqueous solution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aqueous_solution "Aqueous solution"):

•

O−

2 + H

2 O ⇌ HO•

2 + HO−

The [p _K_ a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid_dissociation_constant "Acid dissociation constant") of HO 2 is 4.88. Therefore, about 0.3% of any superoxide present in the cytosol of a typical cell is in the protonated form.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroperoxyl#cite_note-4)

It oxidizes [nitric oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitric_oxide "Nitric oxide") to nitrogen dioxide:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroperoxyl#cite_note-CR-2)

•

NO + HO•

2 → •

NO

2 + HO•

### Reactive oxygen species in biology

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydroperoxyl&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Reactive oxygen species in biology")]

Together with its [conjugate base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjugate_base "Conjugate base")[superoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superoxide "Superoxide"), hydroperoxyl is an important [reactive oxygen species](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reactive_oxygen_species "Reactive oxygen species"). Unlike •

O−

2, which has reducing properties, HO•

2 can act as an oxidant in a number of biologically important reactions, such as the abstraction of hydrogen atoms from [tocopherol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tocopherol "Tocopherol") and [polyunstaturated fatty acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyunstaturated_fatty_acid "Polyunstaturated fatty acid") in the [lipid bilayer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipid_bilayer "Lipid bilayer"). As such, it may be an important initiator of [lipid peroxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipid_peroxidation "Lipid peroxidation").

### Importance for atmospheric chemistry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydroperoxyl&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Importance for atmospheric chemistry")]

Gaseous hydroperoxyl is involved in reaction cycles that destroy stratospheric [ozone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ozone "Ozone"). It is also present in the troposphere, where it is essentially a byproduct of the oxidation of carbon monoxide and of [hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon") by the [hydroxyl radical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyl_radical "Hydroxyl radical").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroperoxyl#cite_note-amsglossary-5)

Because dielectric constant has a strong effect on p _K_ a, and the dielectric constant of air is quite low, superoxide produced (photochemically) in the atmosphere is almost exclusively present as HO•

2. As hydroperoxyl is quite reactive, it acts as a "cleanser" of the atmosphere by degrading certain organic pollutants. As such, the chemistry of HO•

2 is of considerable geochemical importance.
