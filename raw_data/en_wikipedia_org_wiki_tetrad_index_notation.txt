The **tetrad formalism** is an approach to [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") that generalizes the choice of [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)") for the [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle") from a [coordinate basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_basis "Coordinate basis") to the less restrictive choice of a local basis, i.e. a locally defined set of four[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrad_(index_notation)#cite_note-1) linearly independent [vector fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field") called a _[tetrad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrad\_(general\_relativity) "Tetrad (general relativity)")_ or _vierbein_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrad_(index_notation)#cite_note-2) It is a special case of the more general idea of a _vielbein formalism_, which is set in [(pseudo-)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold")[Riemannian geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_geometry "Riemannian geometry"). This article as currently written makes frequent mention of general relativity; however, almost everything it says is equally applicable to [(pseudo-)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold")[Riemannian manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold") in general, and even to [spin manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_manifold "Spin manifold"). Most statements hold by substituting arbitrary ![Image 1: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) for ![Image 2: {\displaystyle n=4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d928ec15aeef83aade867992ee473933adb6139d). In German, "_vier_" translates to "four", "_viel_" to "many", and "_bein_" to "leg".

The general idea is to write the [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor") as the product of two _vielbeins_, one on the left, and one on the right. The effect of the vielbeins is to change the coordinate system used on the [tangent manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_manifold "Tangent manifold") to one that is simpler or more suitable for calculations. It is frequently the case that the vielbein coordinate system is orthonormal, as that is generally the easiest to use. Most tensors become simple or even trivial in this coordinate system; thus the complexity of most expressions is revealed to be an artifact of the choice of coordinates, rather than a innate property or physical effect[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]. That is, as a formalism, it does not alter predictions; it is rather a calculational technique.

The advantage of the tetrad formalism over the standard coordinate-based approach to general relativity lies in the ability to choose the tetrad basis to reflect important physical aspects of the spacetime. The [abstract index notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_index_notation "Abstract index notation") denotes tensors as if they were represented by their coefficients with respect to a fixed local tetrad. Compared to a [completely coordinate free notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connection_form "Connection form"), which is often conceptually clearer, it allows an easy and computationally explicit way to denote contractions.

The significance of the tetradic formalism appear in the [Einstein–Cartan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein%E2%80%93Cartan_theory "Einstein–Cartan theory") formulation of general relativity. The tetradic formalism of the theory is more fundamental than its metric formulation as one can _not_ convert between the tetradic and metric formulations of the fermionic actions despite this being possible for bosonic actions [_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]. This is effectively because Weyl spinors can be very naturally defined on a Riemannian manifold[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrad_(index_notation)#cite_note-3)[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] and their natural setting leads to the [spin connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_connection "Spin connection"). Those spinors take form in the vielbein coordinate system, and not in the manifold coordinate system.

The privileged tetradic formalism also appears in the _deconstruction_ of _higher dimensional_[Kaluza–Klein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaluza%E2%80%93Klein_theory "Kaluza–Klein theory") gravity theories[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrad_(index_notation)#cite_note-4) and [massive gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massive_gravity "Massive gravity") theories, in which the extra-dimension(s) is/are replaced by series of N [lattice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lattice_gauge_theory "Lattice gauge theory") sites such that the higher dimensional metric is replaced by a set of interacting metrics that depend only on the 4D components.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrad_(index_notation)#cite_note-5) Vielbeins commonly appear in other general settings in physics and mathematics. Vielbeins can be understood as [solder forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solder_form "Solder form").

Mathematical formulation
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tetrad_formalism&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Mathematical formulation")]

The tetrad formulation is a special case of a more general formulation, known as the vielbein or n-bein formulation, with n=4. Vielbien is spelt with an "l", not an "r": in German, "viel" means "many", not to be confused with "vier", meaning "four".

In the vielbein formalism,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrad_(index_notation)#cite_note-eguchi-6) an [open cover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_cover "Open cover") of the [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") manifold ![Image 3: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) and a local basis for each of those open sets is chosen: a set of ![Image 4: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) independent [vector fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field")

![Image 5: {\displaystyle e_{a}=e_{a}{}^{\mu }\partial _{\mu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7bd2bdde3268af97056abef0665eae1385564ef2)
for ![Image 6: {\displaystyle a=1,\ldots ,n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/903cda7a9d2a5af308edc2773d550c9f1d2d3d60) that together span the ![Image 7: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-dimensional [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle") at each point in the set. Dually, a vielbein (or tetrad in 4 dimensions) determines (and is determined by) a dual co-vielbein (co-tetrad) — a set of ![Image 8: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) independent [1-forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1-form "1-form").

![Image 9: {\displaystyle e^{a}=e^{a}{}_{\mu }dx^{\mu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5bc3096fbe963f5d68a5906ca2f452fafd33a7c3)
such that

![Image 10: {\displaystyle e^{a}(e_{b})=e^{a}{}_{\mu }e_{b}{}^{\mu }=\delta _{b}^{a},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/76b435b22064f6c4bc3e757937a1263459743d73)
where ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \delta _{b}^{a}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a9315c6e6279895d9ab85576333df50d3456f237) is the [Kronecker delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta "Kronecker delta"). A vielbein is usually specified by its coefficients ![Image 12: {\displaystyle e^{\mu }{}_{a}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2e7bac2a318074a7dabe166cd4fcdfba8a298d30) with respect to a coordinate basis, despite the choice of a set of (local) coordinates ![Image 13: {\displaystyle x^{\mu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/684350815d8cc05d6862ce3edf1fb819c1774b46) being unnecessary for the specification of a tetrad. Each covector is a [solder form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solder_form "Solder form").

From the point of view of the [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry") of [fiber bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_bundles "Fiber bundles"), the n vector fields ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \{e_{a}\}_{a=1\dots n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ae3be407a78bb6fa3b9faae56233cef672592f1) define a section of the [frame bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_bundle "Frame bundle")_i.e._ a [parallelization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelization_(mathematics) "Parallelization (mathematics)") of ![Image 15: {\displaystyle U\subset M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/edd7b08ced14b1958599bba595488f7c314e59da) which is equivalent to an isomorphism ![Image 16: {\displaystyle TU\cong U\times {\mathbb {R} ^{n}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e360b28f725d43d0eef5ea94281b90cbddbf802). Since not every manifold is parallelizable, a vielbein can generally only be chosen locally (_i.e._ only on a [coordinate chart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_chart "Coordinate chart")![Image 17: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) and not all of ![Image 18: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd).)

All tensors of the theory can be expressed in the vector and covector basis, by expressing them as linear combinations of members of the (co)vielbein. For example, the spacetime metric tensor can be transformed from a coordinate basis to the [tetrad](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrad_(general_relativity) "Tetrad (general relativity)")[basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(mathematics) "Basis (mathematics)").

Popular tetrad bases in general relativity include [orthonormal tetrads](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_fields_in_general_relativity "Frame fields in general relativity") and null tetrads. Null tetrads are composed of four [null vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Null_vector "Null vector"), so are used frequently in problems dealing with radiation, and are the basis of the [Newman–Penrose formalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newman%E2%80%93Penrose_formalism "Newman–Penrose formalism") and the [GHP formalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GHP_formalism "GHP formalism").

Relation to standard formalism
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tetrad_formalism&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Relation to standard formalism")]

The standard formalism of [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry") (and general relativity) consists of using the **coordinate tetrad** in the tetrad formalism. The coordinate tetrad is the canonical set of vectors associated with the [coordinate chart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_chart "Coordinate chart"). The coordinate tetrad is commonly denoted ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \{\partial _{\mu }\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a8438e7bcfa90d89e6b68ee28a9689eeb0b9635) whereas the dual cotetrad is denoted ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \{dx^{\mu }\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/42b12f5fb58fd156823d9a810b47be4a8136fc1d). These [tangent vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space "Tangent space") are usually defined as [directional derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Directional_derivative "Directional derivative") operators: given a chart ![Image 21: {\displaystyle {\varphi =(\varphi ^{1},\ldots ,\varphi ^{n})}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/475fd625b8b33169e3b78ccbfb2ff6b97c43f3d7) which maps a subset of the [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold") into coordinate space ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d), and any [scalar field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field "Scalar field")![Image 23: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61), the coordinate vectors are such that:

![Image 24: {\displaystyle \partial _{\mu }[f]\equiv {\frac {\partial (f\circ \varphi ^{-1})}{\partial x^{\mu }}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3f65e74bfd1eae1ff42ee1a1fba7f4a587075c6)
The definition of the cotetrad uses the usual abuse of notation ![Image 25: {\displaystyle dx^{\mu }=d\varphi ^{\mu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e42b838d52ba417c8617fcc4c44cf84cd7e45de2) to define covectors (1-forms) on ![Image 26: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd). The involvement of the coordinate tetrad is not usually made explicit in the standard formalism. In the tetrad formalism, instead of writing tensor equations out fully (including tetrad elements and [tensor products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_products "Tensor products")![Image 27: {\displaystyle \otimes }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de29098f5a34ee296a505681a0d5e875070f2aea) as above) only _components_ of the tensors are mentioned. For example, the metric is written as "![Image 28: {\displaystyle g_{ab}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e69d450dd2c6434f120d72eba8865a4492121a2)". When the tetrad is unspecified this becomes a matter of specifying the type of the tensor called [abstract index notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_index_notation "Abstract index notation"). It allows to easily specify contraction between tensors by repeating indices as in the Einstein summation convention.

Changing tetrads is a routine operation in the standard formalism, as it is involved in every coordinate transformation (i.e., changing from one coordinate tetrad basis to another). Switching between multiple coordinate charts is necessary because, except in trivial cases, it is not possible for a single coordinate chart to cover the entire manifold. Changing to and between general tetrads is much similar and equally necessary (except for [parallelizable manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelizable_manifold "Parallelizable manifold")). Any [tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") can locally be written in terms of this coordinate tetrad or a general (co)tetrad.

For example, the [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor")![Image 29: {\displaystyle \mathbf {g} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8cdf843789e9564a867aee3ff184453b72ecbafe) can be expressed as:

![Image 30: {\displaystyle \mathbf {g} =g_{\mu \nu }dx^{\mu }dx^{\nu }\qquad {\text{where}}~g_{\mu \nu }=\mathbf {g} (\partial _{\mu },\partial _{\nu }).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bc044e73d872d4d278a1258fee9bb37666f1bad3)
(Here we use the [Einstein summation convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_summation_convention "Einstein summation convention")). Likewise, the metric can be expressed with respect to an arbitrary (co)tetrad as

![Image 31: {\displaystyle \mathbf {g} =g_{ab}e^{a}e^{b}\qquad {\text{where}}~g_{ab}=\mathbf {g} \left(e_{a},e_{b}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f1d69099a94d890137113cc8bb9cae0cc0d1e2e4)
Here, we use choice of alphabet ([Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_alphabet "Latin alphabet") and [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_alphabet "Greek alphabet")) for the index variables to distinguish the applicable basis.

We can translate from a general co-tetrad to the coordinate co-tetrad by expanding the covector ![Image 32: {\displaystyle e^{a}=e^{a}{}_{\mu }dx^{\mu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5bc3096fbe963f5d68a5906ca2f452fafd33a7c3). We then get

![Image 33: {\displaystyle \mathbf {g} =g_{ab}e^{a}e^{b}=g_{ab}e^{a}{}_{\mu }e^{b}{}_{\nu }dx^{\mu }dx^{\nu }=g_{\mu \nu }dx^{\mu }dx^{\nu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/70ac66f1330763d885d24621da909459982813d7)
from which it follows that ![Image 34: {\displaystyle g_{\mu \nu }=g_{ab}e^{a}{}_{\mu }e^{b}{}_{\nu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/821b99b1fed6f6394316fd95f959100cc839ff01). Likewise expanding ![Image 35: {\displaystyle dx^{\mu }=e^{\mu }{}_{a}e^{a}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b5788f5d5ce4fa3a070a3d6138e3c852baef6e7) with respect to the general tetrad, we get

![Image 36: {\displaystyle \mathbf {g} =g_{\mu \nu }dx^{\mu }dx^{\nu }=g_{\mu \nu }e^{\mu }{}_{a}e^{\nu }{}_{b}e^{a}e^{b}=g_{ab}e^{a}e^{b}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa8876c01720e6c717789800023cc25c05087276)
which shows that ![Image 37: {\displaystyle g_{ab}=g_{\mu \nu }e^{\mu }{}_{a}e^{\nu }{}_{b}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/821f265320cfa43bf0ce75777e26fab56801242f).

### Manipulation of indices

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tetrad_formalism&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Manipulation of indices")]

The manipulation with tetrad coefficients shows that abstract index formulas can, in principle, be obtained from tensor formulas with respect to a coordinate tetrad by "replacing greek by latin indices". However care must be taken that a coordinate tetrad formula defines a genuine tensor when differentiation is involved. Since the coordinate vector fields have vanishing [Lie bracket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_bracket_of_vector_fields "Lie bracket of vector fields") (i.e. commute: ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \partial _{\mu }\partial _{\nu }=\partial _{\nu }\partial _{\mu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d1779d70f5f60855aabb9b6f9a68ac149460a7ba)), naive substitutions of formulas that correctly compute tensor coefficients with respect to a coordinate tetrad may not correctly define a tensor with respect to a general tetrad because the Lie bracket is non-vanishing: ![Image 39: {\displaystyle [e_{a},e_{b}]\neq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48c0205787327ed4b2e8d86fffddc08f3d6782ce). Thus, it is sometimes said that tetrad coordinates provide a [non-holonomic basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holonomic_basis "Holonomic basis").

For example, the [Riemann curvature tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_curvature_tensor "Riemann curvature tensor") is defined for general vector fields ![Image 40: {\displaystyle X,Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8705438171d938b7f59cd1bfa5b7d99b6afa5cd) by

![Image 41: {\displaystyle R(X,Y)=\left(\nabla _{X}\nabla _{Y}-\nabla _{Y}\nabla _{X}-\nabla _{[X,Y]}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40158f86b7e4af3a2ac28ebf05337b51ff68bd69).
In a coordinate tetrad this gives tensor coefficients

![Image 42: {\displaystyle R_{\ \nu \sigma \tau }^{\mu }=dx^{\mu }\left((\nabla _{\sigma }\nabla _{\tau }-\nabla _{\tau }\nabla _{\sigma })\partial _{\nu }\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/614a576c9a8bd1a879f680983b13665f26ec5e56)
The naive "Greek to Latin" substitution of the latter expression

![Image 43: {\displaystyle R_{\ bcd}^{a}=e^{a}\left((\nabla _{c}\nabla _{d}-\nabla _{d}\nabla _{c})e_{b}\right)\qquad {\text{(wrong!)}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38303863550a39563c5fd5e810c1abc919837da7)
is incorrect because for fixed _c_ and _d_, ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \left(\nabla _{c}\nabla _{d}-\nabla _{d}\nabla _{c}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed0f2437e5a3d42ddc89b88635461f73bd9b4185) is, in general, a first order differential operator rather than a zeroth order operator which defines a tensor coefficient. Substituting a general tetrad basis in the abstract formula we find the proper definition of the curvature in abstract index notation, however:

![Image 45: {\displaystyle R_{\ bcd}^{a}=e^{a}\left((\nabla _{c}\nabla _{d}-\nabla _{d}\nabla _{c}-f_{cd}{}^{e}\nabla _{e})e_{b}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed10343529505c9a60ef9aac1455c89e3a0635d8)
where ![Image 46: {\displaystyle [e_{a},e_{b}]=f_{ab}{}^{c}e_{c}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a6594615d3314c0bfced96cd010766e0089d608). Note that the expression ![Image 47: {\displaystyle \left(\nabla _{c}\nabla _{d}-\nabla _{d}\nabla _{c}-f_{cd}{}^{e}\nabla _{e}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d2429cc08530adcad4d41e3362ccd5e1e33876b) is indeed a zeroth order operator, hence (the (_c_ _d_)-component of) a tensor. Since it agrees with the coordinate expression for the curvature when specialised to a coordinate tetrad it is clear, even without using the abstract definition of the curvature, that it defines the same tensor as the coordinate basis expression.

Example: Lie groups
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tetrad_formalism&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Example: Lie groups")]

Given a vector (or covector) in the tangent (or cotangent) manifold, the [exponential map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_map_(Riemannian_geometry) "Exponential map (Riemannian geometry)") describes the corresponding [geodesic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic "Geodesic") of that tangent vector. Writing ![Image 48: {\displaystyle X\in TM}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdbc7bfd23b1afaa10470f1ed6a14bb15fe818c9), the [parallel transport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_transport "Parallel transport") of a differential corresponds to

![Image 49: {\displaystyle e^{-X}de^{X}=dX-{\frac {1}{2!}}\left[X,dX\right]+{\frac {1}{3!}}[X,[X,dX]]-{\frac {1}{4!}}[X,[X,[X,dX]]]+\cdots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65e0f075f1bf1a615ae274ea592d16ef00ed5b50)
The above can be readily verified simply by taking ![Image 50: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) to be a matrix.

For the special case of a [Lie algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra "Lie algebra"), the ![Image 51: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) can be taken to be an element of the algebra, the exponential is the [exponential map of a Lie group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponential_map_(Lie_group) "Exponential map (Lie group)"), and group elements correspond to the geodesics of the tangent vector. Choosing a basis ![Image 52: {\displaystyle e_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ebdc3a9cb1583d3204eff8918b558c293e0d2cf3) for the Lie algebra and writing ![Image 53: {\displaystyle X=X^{i}e_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29caff8194a90c11ae61db41000443ac9782f00a) for some functions ![Image 54: {\displaystyle X^{i},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f483f1c43da09704fbff9f126f024a96a238aabc) the commutators can be explicitly written out. One readily computes that

![Image 55: {\displaystyle e^{-X}de^{X}=dX^{i}e_{i}-{\frac {1}{2!}}X^{i}dX^{j}{f_{ij}}^{k}e_{k}+{\frac {1}{3!}}X^{i}X^{j}dX^{k}{f_{jk}}^{l}{f_{il}}^{m}e_{m}-\cdots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02bacf42b1702eab685086856ddbfa067242ec8f)
for ![Image 56: {\displaystyle [e_{i},e_{j}]={f_{ij}}^{k}e_{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f13bba187eec31077299d03ab56a956d718823d) the [structure constants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structure_constant "Structure constant") of the Lie algebra. The series can be written more compactly as

with the infinite series

![Image 58: {\displaystyle W=\sum _{n=0}^{\infty }{\frac {(-1)^{n}M^{n}}{(n+1)!}}=(I-e^{-M})M^{-1}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/76c1bcf9ec209b243369c3be16d861117d883a4c)
Here, ![Image 59: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is a matrix whose matrix elements are ![Image 60: {\displaystyle {M_{j}}^{k}=X^{i}{f_{ij}}^{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/28ec651071a816308fc7b00130d14c7dc5882c0d). The matrix ![Image 61: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7) is then the vielbein; it expresses the differential ![Image 62: {\displaystyle dX^{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7640a9eff0dba721cd7cd6900cf5b4b1a85a201d) in terms of the "flat coordinates" (orthonormal, at that) ![Image 63: {\displaystyle e_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ebdc3a9cb1583d3204eff8918b558c293e0d2cf3).

Given some map ![Image 64: {\displaystyle N\to G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5980f6705a7ffae8d06961accc91eb1a5957f5b8) from some manifold ![Image 65: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) to some Lie group ![Image 66: {\displaystyle G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f3c8921a3b352de45446a6789b104458c9f90b), the metric tensor on the manifold ![Image 67: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) becomes the pullback of the metric tensor ![Image 68: {\displaystyle B_{mn}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22e69f7390c8a73ab7ffc4ef78fc3746bec3f201) on the Lie group ![Image 69: {\displaystyle G}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5f3c8921a3b352de45446a6789b104458c9f90b):

![Image 70: {\displaystyle g_{ij}={W_{i}}^{m}B_{mn}{W^{n}}_{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5449a058add898f19661aea483607b6d90bb911d)
The metric tensor ![Image 71: {\displaystyle B_{mn}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22e69f7390c8a73ab7ffc4ef78fc3746bec3f201) on the Lie group is the Cartan metric, aka the [Killing form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Killing_form "Killing form"). Note that, as a matrix, the second W is the transpose. For ![Image 72: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) a (pseudo-)[Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold"), the metric is a (pseudo-)[Riemannian metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_metric "Riemannian metric"). The above generalizes to the case of [symmetric spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_space "Symmetric space").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrad_(index_notation)#cite_note-7) These vielbeins are used to perform calculations in [sigma models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigma_model "Sigma model"), of which the [supergravity theories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supergravity "Supergravity") are a special case.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetrad_(index_notation)#cite_note-8)
