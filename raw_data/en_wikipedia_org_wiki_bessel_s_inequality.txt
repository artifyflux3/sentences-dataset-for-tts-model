The inequality is named for F. W. Bessel , who derived a special case of it in 1828.

[ 1 ] Conceptually, the inequality is a generalization of the Pythagorean theorem to infinite-dimensional spaces. It states that the " energy " of a vector x {\displaystyle x} , given by ‖ ‖ x ‖ ‖ 2 {\displaystyle \|x\|^{2}} , is greater than or equal to the sum of the energies of its projections onto a set of perpendicular basis directions. The value | ⟨ ⟨ x , e k ⟩ ⟩ | 2 {\displaystyle |\langle x,e_{k}\rangle |^{2}} represents the energy contribution along a specific direction e k {\displaystyle e_{k}} , and the inequality guarantees that the sum of these contributions cannot exceed the total energy of x {\displaystyle x} .

When the orthonormal sequence forms a complete orthonormal basis , Bessel's inequality becomes an equality known as Parseval's identity . This signifies that the sum of the energies of the projections equals the total energy of the vector, meaning no energy is "lost." The inequality is a crucial tool for establishing the convergence of Fourier series and other series expansions in Hilbert spaces.

Statement of the inequality [ edit ] Let H {\displaystyle H} be a Hilbert space and let e 1 , e 2 , … … {\displaystyle e_{1},e_{2},\dots } be an orthonormal sequence in H {\displaystyle H} . Then for any vector x {\displaystyle x} in H {\displaystyle H} , Bessel's inequality states: ∑ ∑ k = 1 ∞ ∞ | ⟨ x , e k ⟩ | 2 ≤ ≤ ‖ x ‖ 2 {\displaystyle \sum _{k=1}^{\infty }\left\vert \left\langle x,e_{k}\right\rangle \right\vert ^{2}\leq \left\Vert x\right\Vert ^{2}} where ⟨·,·⟩ denotes the inner product in the Hilbert space H {\displaystyle H} , and ‖ ‖ ⋅ ⋅ ‖ ‖ {\displaystyle \|\cdot \|} denotes the norm induced by the inner product.

[ 2 ] [ 3 ] [ 4 ] The terms ⟨ ⟨ x , e k ⟩ ⟩ {\displaystyle \langle x,e_{k}\rangle } are the Fourier coefficients of x {\displaystyle x} with respect to the sequence ( e k ) {\displaystyle (e_{k})} . The inequality implies that the series of the squared magnitudes of these coefficients converges. This allows for the definition of the vector x ′ {\displaystyle x'} , which is the projection of x {\displaystyle x} onto the subspace spanned by the orthonormal sequence: x ′ = ∑ ∑ k = 1 ∞ ∞ ⟨ x , e k ⟩ e k {\displaystyle x'=\sum _{k=1}^{\infty }\left\langle x,e_{k}\right\rangle e_{k}} Bessel's inequality guarantees that this series converges . If the sequence ( e k ) {\displaystyle (e_{k})} is a complete orthonormal basis , then x ′ = x {\displaystyle x'=x} , and the inequality becomes an equality known as Parseval's identity .

Proof [ edit ] The inequality follows from the non-negativity of the norm of a vector. For any natural number n {\displaystyle n} , let x n = ∑ ∑ k = 1 n ⟨ ⟨ x , e k ⟩ ⟩ e k {\displaystyle x_{n}=\sum _{k=1}^{n}\langle x,e_{k}\rangle e_{k}} This vector x n {\displaystyle x_{n}} is the projection of x {\displaystyle x} onto the subspace spanned by the first n {\displaystyle n} basis vectors. The vector x − − x n {\displaystyle x-x_{n}} is orthogonal to this subspace, and thus orthogonal to x n {\displaystyle x_{n}} itself. By the Pythagorean theorem for inner product spaces , we have ‖ ‖ x ‖ ‖ 2 = ‖ ‖ x n ‖ ‖ 2 + ‖ ‖ x − − x n ‖ ‖ 2 {\displaystyle \|x\|^{2}=\|x_{n}\|^{2}+\|x-x_{n}\|^{2}} . Since the partial sums are non-negative and bounded above by ‖ ‖ x ‖ ‖ 2 {\displaystyle \|x\|^{2}} , the series ∑ ∑ k = 1 ∞ ∞ | ⟨ ⟨ x , e k ⟩ ⟩ | 2 {\displaystyle \sum _{k=1}^{\infty }|\langle x,e_{k}\rangle |^{2}} converges and its sum is less than or equal to ‖ ‖ x ‖ ‖ 2 {\displaystyle \|x\|^{2}} .
