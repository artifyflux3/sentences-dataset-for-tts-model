From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

The **parameter space** is the [space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_(mathematics) "Space (mathematics)") of all possible parameter values that define a particular [mathematical model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_model "Mathematical model"). It is also sometimes called **weight space**, and is often a [subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset") of finite-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space").

In [statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics "Statistics"), parameter spaces are particularly useful for describing [parametric families](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametric_family "Parametric family") of [probability distributions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_distribution "Probability distribution"). They also form the background for [parameter estimation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter_estimation "Parameter estimation"). In the case of [extremum estimators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extremum_estimator "Extremum estimator") for [parametric models](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parametric_model "Parametric model"), a certain [objective function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Objective_function "Objective function") is maximized or minimized over the parameter space.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter_space#cite_note-Hayashi_p446-1) Theorems of [existence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existence_theorem "Existence theorem") and [consistency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consistent_estimator "Consistent estimator") of such estimators require some assumptions about the [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") of the parameter space. For instance, [compactness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compactness "Compactness") of the parameter space, together with [continuity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function") of the objective function, suffices for the existence of an extremum estimator.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter_space#cite_note-Hayashi_p446-1)

Sometimes, parameters are analyzed to view how they affect their statistical model. In that context, they can be viewed as inputs of a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)"), in which case the technical term for the parameter space is [domain of a function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_of_a_function "Domain of a function"). The ranges of values of the parameters may form the axes of a [plot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plot_(graphics) "Plot (graphics)"), and particular outcomes of the model may be plotted against these axes to illustrate how different regions of the parameter space produce different types of behavior in the model.

*   A simple model of health deterioration after developing [lung cancer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lung_cancer "Lung cancer") could include the two parameters gender[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter_space#cite_note-one-2) and smoker/non-smoker, in which case the parameter space is the following set of four possibilities: {(Male, Smoker), (Male, Non-smoker), (Female, Smoker), (Female, Non-smoker)} .

*   The [logistic map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logistic_map "Logistic map")![Image 1: {\displaystyle x_{n+1}=rx_{n}(1-x_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b84673eee90dff3113ee6263cc20a628979cc11d) has one parameter, _r_, which can take any positive value. The parameter space is therefore [positive real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive_real_numbers "Positive real numbers").

For some values of _r_, this function ends up cycling around a few values or becomes fixed on one value. These long-term values can be plotted against _r_ in a [bifurcation diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bifurcation_diagram "Bifurcation diagram") to show the different behaviours of the function for different values of _r_.
*   In [complex dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_dynamics "Complex dynamics"), the parameter space is the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane")**C** = { _z_ = _x_ + _y_ i: x, y ∈ **R** }, where i 2 = −1.

The famous [Mandelbrot set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandelbrot_set "Mandelbrot set") is a [subset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subset "Subset") of this parameter space, consisting of the points in the complex plane which give a [bounded set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounded_set "Bounded set") of numbers when a particular [iterated function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iterated_function "Iterated function") is repeatedly applied from that starting point. The remaining points, which are not in the set, give an unbounded set of numbers (they tend to infinity) when this function is repeatedly applied from that starting point.
*   In [machine learning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Machine_learning "Machine learning"), [hyperparameters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperparameter_(machine_learning) "Hyperparameter (machine learning)") are used to describe models. In [deep learning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deep_learning "Deep learning"), the parameters of a deep network are called **weights.** Due to the layered structure of deep networks, their weight space has a complex structure and geometry.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter_space#cite_note-:0-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter_space#cite_note-4) For example, in [multilayer perceptrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilayer_perceptrons "Multilayer perceptrons"), the same function is preserved when [permuting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permutation "Permutation") the nodes of a hidden layer, amounting to permuting weight matrices of the network. This property is known as _[equivariance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivariance "Equivariance") to permutation_ of [deep weight spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Deep_weight_spaces&action=edit&redlink=1 "Deep weight spaces (page does not exist)").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter_space#cite_note-:0-3) The study seeks [hyperparameter optimization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperparameter_optimization "Hyperparameter optimization").

Parameter space contributed to the liberation of [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry") from the confines of [three-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space"). For instance, the parameter space of [spheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere_(geometry) "Sphere (geometry)") in three dimensions, has four dimensions—three for the sphere center and another for the radius. According to [Dirk Struik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirk_Struik "Dirk Struik"), it was the book _Neue Geometrie des Raumes_ (1849) by [Julius Plücker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julius_Pl%C3%BCcker "Julius Plücker") that showed

...geometry need not solely be based on points as basic elements. Lines, planes, circles, spheres can all be used as the elements (_Raumelemente_) on which a geometry can be based. This fertile conception threw new light on both synthetic and algebraic geometry and created new forms of duality. The number of dimensions of a particular form of geometry could now be any positive number, depending on the number of parameters necessary to define the "element".[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter_space#cite_note-DS-5): 165 
The requirement for higher dimensions is illustrated by [Plücker's line geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pl%C3%BCcker_coordinates "Plücker coordinates"). Struik writes

[Plücker's] geometry of lines in three-space could be considered as a four-dimensional geometry, or, as [Klein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Klein "Felix Klein") has stressed, as the geometry of a four-dimensional [quadric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadric "Quadric") in a five-dimensional space.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter_space#cite_note-DS-5): 168 
Thus the [Klein quadric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Klein_quadric "Klein quadric") describes the parameters of lines in space.
