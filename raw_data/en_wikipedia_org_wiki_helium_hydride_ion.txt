The **helium hydride ion**, **hydridohelium(1+) ion**, or **helonium** is a [cation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion#Anions_and_cations "Ion") ([positively charged](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_charge "Electric charge")[ion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion "Ion")) with [chemical formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula") HeH+. It consists of a [helium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium "Helium") atom [bonded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covalent_bond "Covalent bond") to a [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") atom, with one [electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron") removed. It can also be viewed as protonated helium. It is the lightest [heteronuclear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heteronuclear_molecule "Heteronuclear molecule") ion, and is believed to be the first compound formed in the [Universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe") after the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-EngelEtAl-3)

The ion was first produced in a laboratory in 1925. It is stable in isolation, but extremely reactive, and cannot be prepared in bulk, because it would react with any other molecule with which it came into contact. Noted as the strongest known [acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid "Acid")—stronger than even [fluoroantimonic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluoroantimonic_acid "Fluoroantimonic acid")—its occurrence in the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium") had been conjectured since the 1970s,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-chebi33689-4) and it was finally detected in April 2019 using the airborne [SOFIA telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stratospheric_Observatory_for_Infrared_Astronomy "Stratospheric Observatory for Infrared Astronomy").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-stut2019-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-DSCVR-20191222-6)

Physical properties
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Helium_hydride_ion&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Physical properties")]

The helium hydrogen ion is [isoelectronic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isoelectronicity "Isoelectronicity") with molecular [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") (H

2).[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-hogn1925-7)

Unlike the [dihydrogen ion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihydrogen_cation "Dihydrogen cation")H+

2, the helium hydride ion has a permanent [dipole moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dipole#Molecular_dipoles "Dipole"), which makes its spectroscopic characterization easier.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-coxo1999-8) The calculated dipole moment of HeH+ is 2.26 or 2.84[D](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Debye "Debye").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-dias2019-9) The electron density in the ion is higher around the helium nucleus than the hydrogen. 80% of the electron charge is closer to the helium nucleus than to the hydrogen nucleus.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-10)

Spectroscopic detection is hampered, because one of its most prominent spectral lines, at 149.14[μm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre "Micrometre"), coincides with a doublet of spectral lines belonging to the [methylidyne radical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylidyne_radical "Methylidyne radical")**⫶**CH.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-EngelEtAl-3)

The length of the [covalent bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covalent_bond "Covalent bond") in the ion is 0.772[Å](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angstrom "Angstrom")[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-Coyne-11) or 77.2 [pm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Picometre "Picometre").

The helium hydride ion has six relatively stable [isotopologues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotopologue "Isotopologue"), that differ in the [isotopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isotope "Isotope") of the two elements, and hence in the total atomic [mass number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_number "Mass number") (_A_) and the total number of [neutrons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron "Neutron") (_N_) in the two nuclei:

*   [3 He 1 H]+ or [3 HeH]+ (_A_ = 4, _N_ = 1)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-cant1956-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-tung2012-13)
*   [3 He 2 H]+ or [3 HeD]+ (_A_ = 5, _N_ = 2)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-cant1956-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-tung2012-13)
*   [3 He 3 H]+ or [3 HeT]+ (_A_ = 6, _N_ = 3; radioactive)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-schw1955-14)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-cant1956-12)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-snel1957-15)
*   [4 He 1 H]+ or [4 HeH]+ (_A_ = 5, _N_ = 2)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-hogn1925-7)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-bain1933-16)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-bern1982-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-pach2012-18)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-tung2012-13)
*   [4 He 2 H]+ or [4 HeD]+ (_A_ = 6, _N_ = 3)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-bain1933-16)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-tung2012-13)
*   [4 He 3 H]+ or [4 HeT]+ (_A_ = 7, _N_ = 4; radioactive)

They all have three protons and two electrons. The first three are generated by radioactive decay of [tritium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tritium "Tritium") in the molecules HT = 1 H 3 H, DT = 2 H 3 H, and T 2 = 3 H 2, respectively. The last three can be generated by ionizing the appropriate isotopologue of H 2 in the presence of helium-4.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-hogn1925-7)

The following isotopologues of the helium hydride ion, of the dihydrogen ion H+2, and of the [trihydrogen ion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trihydrogen_cation "Trihydrogen cation")H+3 have the same total atomic mass number _A_:

*   [3 HeH]+, [D 2]+, [TH]+, [DH 2]+ (_A_ = 4)
*   [3 HeD]+, [4 HeH]+, [DT]+, [TH 2]+, [D 2 H]+ (_A_ = 5)
*   [3 HeT]+, [4 HeD]+, [T 2]+, [TDH]+, [D 3]+ (_A_ = 6)
*   [4 HeT]+, [TD 2]+, [T 2 H]+ (_A_ = 7)

The masses in each row above are not equal, though, because the binding energies in the nuclei are different.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-bain1933-16)

Unlike the helium hydride ion, the neutral helium hydride _molecule_ HeH is not stable in the ground state. However, it does exist in an excited state as an [excimer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excimer "Excimer") (HeH*), and its spectrum was first observed in the mid-1980s.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-mull1985-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-nobe2001-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-kette1985-21)

The neutral molecule is the first entry in the [Gmelin database](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gmelin_database "Gmelin database").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-chebi33689-4)

Chemical properties and reactions
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Helium_hydride_ion&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Chemical properties and reactions")]

Since HeH+ reacts with every substance, it cannot be stored in any container. As a result, its chemistry must be studied by creating it [_in situ_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/In_situ#Chemistry_and_chemical_engineering "In situ").

Reactions with organic substances can be studied by substituting hydrogen in the desired organic compound with [tritium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tritium "Tritium"). The decay of tritium to 3 He+ followed by its extraction of a hydrogen atom from the compound yields 3 HeH+, which is then surrounded by the organic material and will in turn react.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-Gran004-22)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-fulv1970-23)

TR → 3 He+ + R• ([beta decay](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_decay "Beta decay"))3 He+ + HR → 3 HeH+ + R• ([hydrogen abstraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_atom_abstraction "Hydrogen atom abstraction"))
HeH+ cannot be prepared in a [condensed phase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_phase "Condensed phase"), as it would [donate a proton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonation "Protonation") to any [anion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anion "Anion"), molecule or atom that it came in contact with. It has been shown to protonate [O 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen"), [NH 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia"), [SO 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide "Sulfur dioxide"), [H 2 O](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water "Water"), and [CO 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_dioxide "Carbon dioxide"), giving [HO+ 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dioxidanylium "Dioxidanylium"), [NH+ 4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium "Ammonium"), [HSO+ 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sulfanetriiumdione&action=edit&redlink=1 "Sulfanetriiumdione (page does not exist)"), [H 3 O+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydronium "Hydronium"), and [HCO+ 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Methyliumdione&action=edit&redlink=1 "Methyliumdione (page does not exist)") respectively.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-Gran004-22) Other molecules such as [nitric oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitric_oxide "Nitric oxide"), [nitrogen dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen_dioxide "Nitrogen dioxide"), [nitrous oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrous_oxide "Nitrous oxide"), [hydrogen sulfide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_sulfide "Hydrogen sulfide"), [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane"), [acetylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene "Acetylene"), [ethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene "Ethylene"), [ethane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethane "Ethane"), [methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol") and [acetonitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetonitrile "Acetonitrile") react but break up due to the large amount of energy produced.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-Gran004-22)

In fact, HeH+ is the strongest known [acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid "Acid"), with a [proton affinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proton_affinity "Proton affinity") of 177.8 kJ/mol, or a [p _K_ a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid_dissociation_constant "Acid dissociation constant") of −63.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-Epa-24)

### Other helium-hydrogen ions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Helium_hydride_ion&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Other helium-hydrogen ions")]

Additional helium atoms can attach to HeH+ to form larger clusters such as He 2 H+, He 3 H+, He 4 H+, He 5 H+ and He 6 H+.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-Gran004-22)

The dihelium hydride cation, He 2 H+, is formed by the reaction of [dihelium cation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihelium_cation "Dihelium cation") with molecular hydrogen:

He+

2 + H 2 → He 2 H+ + H
It is a linear ion with hydrogen in the centre.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-Gran004-22)

The hexahelium hydride ion, He 6 H+, is particularly stable.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-Gran004-22)

Other helium hydride ions are known or have been studied theoretically. Helium dihydride ion, or **dihydridohelium(1+)**, HeH+

2, has been observed using microwave spectroscopy.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-carr1996-25) It has a calculated binding energy of 25.1 kJ/mol, while **trihydridohelium(1+)**, HeH+

3, has a calculated binding energy of 0.42 kJ/mol.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-pauz2007-26)

### Discovery in ionization experiments

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Helium_hydride_ion&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Discovery in ionization experiments")]

Hydridohelium(1+), specifically [4 He 1 H]+, was first detected indirectly in 1925 by [T. R. Hogness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thorfin_R._Hogness "Thorfin R. Hogness") and E. G. Lunn. They were injecting protons of known energy into a rarefied mixture of hydrogen and helium, in order to study the formation of hydrogen ions like H+

, H+

2 and H+

3. They observed that H+

3 appeared at the same beam energy (16 [eV](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron-volt "Electron-volt")) as H+

2, and its concentration increased with pressure much more than that of the other two ions. From these data, they concluded that the H+

2 ions were transferring a proton to molecules that they collided with, including helium.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-hogn1925-7)

In 1933, [K. Bainbridge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kenneth_Bainbridge "Kenneth Bainbridge") used [mass spectrometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_spectrometry "Mass spectrometry") to compare the masses of the ions [4 He 1 H]+ (helium hydride ion) and [2 H 2 1 H]+ (twice-deuterated trihydrogen ion) in order to obtain an accurate measurement of the atomic mass of deuterium relative to that of helium. Both ions have 3 protons, 2 neutrons, and 2 electrons. He also compared [4 He 2 H]+ (helium deuteride ion) with [2 H 3]+ ([trideuterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trideuterium "Trideuterium") ion), both with 3 protons and 3 neutrons.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-bain1933-16)

### Early theoretical studies

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Helium_hydride_ion&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Early theoretical studies")]

The first attempt to compute the structure of the HeH+ ion (specifically, [4 He 1 H]+) by quantum mechanical theory was made by J. Beach in 1936.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-beac1936-27) Improved computations were sporadically published over the next decades.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-stoh1940-28)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-evet1956-29)

### Tritium decay methods in chemistry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Helium_hydride_ion&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Tritium decay methods in chemistry")]

H. Schwartz observed in 1955 that the decay of the tritium molecule T 2 = 3 H 2 should generate the helium hydride ion [3 HeT]+ with high probability.

In 1963, [F. Cacace](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fulvio_Cacace "Fulvio Cacace") at the [Sapienza University of Rome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sapienza_University_of_Rome "Sapienza University of Rome") conceived the [decay technique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decay_technique "Decay technique") for preparing and studying organic [radicals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical_(chemistry) "Radical (chemistry)") and [carbenium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbenium "Carbenium") ions.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-fcac1990-30) In a variant of that technique, exotic species like [methanium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanium "Methanium") are produced by reacting organic compounds with the [3 HeT]+ that is produced by the decay of T 2 that is mixed with the desired reagents. Much of what we know about the chemistry of [HeH]+ came through this technique.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-sper1993-31)

### Implications for neutrino mass experiments

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Helium_hydride_ion&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Implications for neutrino mass experiments")]

In 1980, V. Lubimov (Lyubimov) at the [ITEP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institute_for_Theoretical_and_Experimental_Physics "Institute for Theoretical and Experimental Physics") laboratory in Moscow claimed to have detected a mildly significant rest mass (30 ± 16) eV for the [neutrino](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutrino "Neutrino"), by analyzing the energy spectrum of the β decay of tritium.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-lyub1980-32) The claim was disputed, and several other groups set out to check it by studying the decay of molecular tritium T

2. It was known that some of the energy released by that decay would be diverted to the excitation of the decay products, including [3 HeT]+; and this phenomenon could be a significant source of error in that experiment. This observation motivated numerous efforts to precisely compute the expected energy states of that ion in order to reduce the uncertainty of those measurements.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] Many have improved the computations since then, and now there is quite good agreement between computed and experimental properties; including for the isotopologues [4 He 2 H]+, [3 He 1 H]+, and [3 He 2 H]+.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-pach2012-18)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-tung2012-13)

### Spectral predictions and detection

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Helium_hydride_ion&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Spectral predictions and detection")]

In 1956, M. Cantwell predicted theoretically that the spectrum of vibrations of that ion should be observable in the infrared; and the spectra of the deuterium and common hydrogen isotopologues ([3 HeD]+ and [3 He 1 H]+) should lie closer to visible light and hence easier to observe.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-cant1956-12) The first detection of the spectrum of [4 He 1 H]+ was made by D. Tolliver and others in 1979, at wavenumbers between 1,700 and 1,900 cm−1.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-toll1979-33) In 1982, P. Bernath and T. Amano detected nine infrared lines between 2,164 and 3,158 waves per cm.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-bern1982-17)

HeH+ has been conjectured since the 1970s to exist in the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium").[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-fern2007-34) Its first detection, in the nebula [NGC 7027](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NGC_7027 "NGC 7027"), was reported in an article published in the journal _Nature_ in April 2019.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-stut2019-5)

### From decay of tritium

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Helium_hydride_ion&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: From decay of tritium")]

The helium hydride ion is formed during the decay of [tritium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tritium "Tritium") in the molecule HT or tritium molecule T 2. Although excited by the recoil from the beta decay, the molecule remains bound together.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-man1993-35)

### Interstellar medium

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Helium_hydride_ion&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Interstellar medium")]

It is believed to be the first compound to have formed in the universe,[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-EngelEtAl-3) and is of fundamental importance in understanding the chemistry of the early universe.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-LiuEtAl-36) This is because hydrogen and helium were almost the only types of atoms formed in [Big Bang nucleosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang_nucleosynthesis "Big Bang nucleosynthesis"). Stars formed from the primordial material should contain HeH+, which could influence their formation and subsequent evolution. In particular, its strong [dipole moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_dipole_moment "Molecular dipole moment") makes it relevant to the opacity of [zero-metallicity stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metallicity#Stars "Metallicity").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-EngelEtAl-3) HeH+ is also thought to be an important constituent of the atmospheres of helium-rich white dwarfs, where it increases the opacity of the gas and causes the star to cool more slowly.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-HarrisEtAl-37)

HeH+ could be formed in the cooling gas behind dissociative shocks in dense interstellar clouds, such as the shocks caused by [stellar winds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_wind "Stellar wind"), [supernovae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova "Supernova") and outflowing material from young stars. If the speed of the shock is greater than about 90 kilometres per second (56 mi/s), quantities large enough to detect might be formed. If detected, the emissions from HeH+ would then be useful tracers of the shock.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-NeufeldDelgarno-38)

Several locations had been suggested as possible places HeH+ might be detected. These included cool [helium stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_star "Helium star"),[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-EngelEtAl-3)[H II regions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H_II_region "H II region"),[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-RobergeDelgarno-39) and dense [planetary nebulae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_nebula "Planetary nebula"),[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-RobergeDelgarno-39) like [NGC 7027](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NGC_7027 "NGC 7027"),[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-LiuEtAl-36) where, in April 2019, HeH+ was reported to have been detected.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helium_hydride_ion#cite_note-stut2019-5)
