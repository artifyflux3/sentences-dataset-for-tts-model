In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **prism** is a [polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron "Polyhedron") comprising an n-sided polygon [base](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_(geometry) "Base (geometry)"), a second base which is a [translated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry) "Translation (geometry)") copy (rigidly moved without rotation) of the first, and n other [faces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_(geometry) "Face (geometry)"), necessarily all [parallelograms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram"), joining [corresponding sides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corresponding_sides "Corresponding sides") of the two bases. All [cross-sections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_section_(geometry) "Cross section (geometry)") parallel to the bases are translations of the bases. Prisms are named after their bases, e.g. a prism with a [pentagonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagonal "Pentagonal") base is called a pentagonal prism. Prisms are a subclass of [prismatoids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prismatoid "Prismatoid").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_(geometry)#cite_note-prismatoid-2)

Like many basic geometric terms, the word _prism_ (from [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language")πρίσμα _(prisma)_'something sawed') was first used in [Euclid's _Elements_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements "Euclid's Elements"). Euclid defined the term in Book XI as "a solid figure contained by two opposite, equal and parallel planes, while the rest are parallelograms". However, this definition has been criticized for not being specific enough in regard to the nature of the bases (a cause of some confusion amongst generations of later geometry writers).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_(geometry)#cite_note-malton-1774-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_(geometry)#cite_note-elliot-1845-4)

An **oblique prism** is a prism in which the joining edges and faces are _**not [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular")**_ to the base faces.

Example: a [parallelepiped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelepiped "Parallelepiped") is an oblique prism whose base is a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram"), or equivalently a polyhedron with six parallelogram faces.

Right prism

A **_right_ prism** is a prism in which the joining edges and faces are _[perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular")_ to the base faces.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_(geometry)#cite_note-mensuration-5) This applies [if and only if](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If_and_only_if "If and only if") all the joining faces are _[rectangular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangular "Rectangular")_.

The [dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polyhedron "Dual polyhedron") of a _right_ n-prism is a _right_ n-[bipyramid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipyramid "Bipyramid").

A right prism (with rectangular sides) with [regular n-gon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon") bases has Schläfli symbol { }×{_n_}. It approaches a [cylinder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cylinder_(geometry) "Cylinder (geometry)") as n approaches [infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinity "Infinity").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_(geometry)#cite_note-cylinder-6)

*   A right rectangular prism (with a rectangular base) is also called a _[cuboid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cuboid "Cuboid")_, or informally a _rectangular box_. A right rectangular prism has [Schläfli symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_symbol "Schläfli symbol"){ }×{ }×{ }.
*   A right square prism (with a square base) is also called a _square cuboid_, or informally a _square box_.

Note: some texts may apply the term _rectangular prism_ or _square prism_ to both a right rectangular-based prism and a right square-based prism.

A **regular prism** is a prism with [regular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon") bases.

A **uniform prism** or **semiregular prism** is a right prism with regular bases and all edges of the same length.

Thus all the side faces of a uniform prism are [squares](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square "Square").

Thus all the faces of a uniform prism are regular polygons. Also, such prisms are [isogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isogonal_figure "Isogonal figure"); thus they are [uniform polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_polyhedra "Uniform polyhedra"). They form one of the two infinite series of [semiregular polyhedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semiregular_polyhedra "Semiregular polyhedra"), the other series being formed by the [antiprisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiprism "Antiprism").

A uniform n-gonal prism has [Schläfli symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_symbol "Schläfli symbol")t{2,_n_}.

The [volume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume "Volume") of a prism is the product of the [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area") of the base by the height, i.e. the distance between the two base faces (in the case of a non-right prism, note that this means the perpendicular distance).

The volume is therefore:

![Image 89: {\displaystyle V=Bh,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f754448c12a2ba6ad7d54b71174f59d9b42e9c5d)
where B is the base area and h is the height.

The volume of a prism whose base is an n-sided [regular polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polygon "Regular polygon") with side length s is therefore: ![Image 90: {\displaystyle V={\frac {n}{4}}hs^{2}\cot {\frac {\pi }{n}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f195f80e4de4453ce292e0c3c90d3b1b68cb1743)

The surface [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area") of a right prism is:

![Image 91: {\displaystyle 2B+Ph,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ac3c81a9c4ebc1d8c24dcabcc347c4b6e29088b1)
where B is the area of the base, h the height, and P the base [perimeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perimeter "Perimeter").

The surface area of a right prism whose base is a regular n-sided [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon "Polygon") with side length s, and with height h, is therefore:

![Image 92: {\displaystyle A={\frac {n}{2}}s^{2}\cot {\frac {\pi }{n}}+nsh.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/50ab0acd64c55d17e81421d6f11a283f639339fe)
The [symmetry group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_group "Symmetry group") of a right n-sided prism with regular base is [D _n_ h](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihedral_group "Dihedral group") of order 4 _n_, except in the case of a cube, which has the larger symmetry group [O h](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octahedral_symmetry "Octahedral symmetry") of order 48, which has three versions of D 4h as [subgroups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subgroup "Subgroup"). The [rotation group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_groups_in_three_dimensions#Rotation_groups "Point groups in three dimensions") is D _n_ of order 2 _n_, except in the case of a cube, which has the larger symmetry group O of order 24, which has three versions of D 4 as subgroups.

The symmetry group D _n_ h contains [inversion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_reflection "Point reflection")[iff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If_and_only_if "If and only if")n is even.

The [hosohedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hosohedron "Hosohedron") and [dihedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dihedron "Dihedron") also possess dihedral symmetry, and an n-gonal prism can be constructed via the [geometrical truncation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Truncation_(geometry) "Truncation (geometry)") of an n-gonal hosohedron, as well as through the [cantellation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cantellation "Cantellation") or [expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expansion_(geometry) "Expansion (geometry)") of an n-gonal dihedron.

[![Image 93](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ca/TruncatedTriangularPrism.svg/250px-TruncatedTriangularPrism.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:TruncatedTriangularPrism.svg)

Example of a truncated triangular prism. Its top face is truncated at an oblique angle, but it is not an [oblique prism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_(geometry)#Oblique_prism).

A **truncated prism** is formed when prism is sliced by a plane that is not [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_(geometry) "Parallel (geometry)") to its bases. A truncated prism's bases are not [congruent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Congruence_(geometry) "Congruence (geometry)"), and its sides are not parallelograms.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_(geometry)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKernBland193881-7)

A **twisted prism** is a nonconvex polyhedron constructed from a uniform n-prism with each side face bisected on the square diagonal, by twisting the top, usually (but not necessarily) by ⁠π/_n_⁠ radians (⁠180/_n_⁠ degrees). If the bisectors are slanted to the left, then twisting the top base in the right direction (looking at the top of the prism) by a small angle gives nonconvex polyhedron and twisting it in the left direction, a convex polyhedron (see twisted square prism on the image). If the bisectors are slanted to the right, then twisting the top base in the left direction gives nonconvex polyhedron, in the right direction, convex one (see twisted dodecagonal prism).[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_(geometry)#cite_note-gorini-2003-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prism_(geometry)#cite_note-9)

A twisted prism cannot be [dissected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissection_(geometry) "Dissection (geometry)") into tetrahedra without adding new vertices. The simplest twisted prism has triangle bases and is called a [Schönhardt polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sch%C3%B6nhardt_polyhedron "Schönhardt polyhedron").

An n-gonal _twisted prism_ is topologically identical to the n-gonal uniform [antiprism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiprism "Antiprism"), but has half the [symmetry group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_spherical_symmetry_groups#Dihedral_symmetry "List of spherical symmetry groups"): D _n_, [_n_,2]+, order 2 _n_. It can be seen as a nonconvex antiprism, with tetrahedra removed between pairs of triangles. Any twisted n-gonal prism is an antiprism, so the twisted square prism and twisted dodecagonal prism shown on the image are both antiprisms.

A **crossed prism** is a nonconvex polyhedron constructed from a prism, where the vertices of one base are [inverted around the center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_reflection "Point reflection") of this base (or rotated by 180°). This transforms the side rectangular faces into [crossed rectangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crossed_rectangle "Crossed rectangle"). For a regular polygon base, the appearance is an n-gonal [hour glass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hour_glass "Hour glass"). All oblique edges pass through a single body center. Note: no vertex is at this body centre. A crossed prism is topologically identical to an n-gonal prism.

A **toroidal prism** is a nonconvex polyhedron like a _crossed prism_, but without bottom and top base faces, and with simple rectangular side faces closing the polyhedron. This can only be done for even-sided base polygons. These are topological tori, with [Euler characteristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_characteristic "Euler characteristic") of zero. The topological [polyhedral net](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedral_net "Polyhedral net") can be cut from two rows of a [square tiling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_tiling "Square tiling") (with [vertex configuration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_configuration "Vertex configuration")4.4.4.4): a band of n squares, each attached to a [crossed rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle "Rectangle"). An n-gonal toroidal prism has 2 _n_ vertices, 2 _n_ faces: n squares and n crossed rectangles, and 4 _n_ edges. It is topologically [self-dual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-dual_polyhedron "Self-dual polyhedron").

A _prismatic [polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytope "Polytope")_ is a higher-dimensional generalization of a prism. An n-dimensional prismatic polytope is constructed from two (_n_ − 1)-dimensional polytopes, translated into the next dimension.

The prismatic n-polytope elements are doubled from the (_n_ − 1)-polytope elements and then creating new elements from the next lower element.

Take an n-polytope with F i[i-face](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Face_(geometry) "Face (geometry)") elements (_i_ = 0, ..., _n_). Its (_n_ + 1)-polytope prism will have 2 _F i_ + _F_ _i_−1 i-face elements. (With _F_−1 = 0, _F n_ = 1.)

By dimension:

*   Take a [polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon "Polygon") with n vertices, n edges. Its prism has 2 _n_ vertices, 3 _n_ edges, and 2 + _n_ faces.
*   Take a [polyhedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron "Polyhedron") with V vertices, E edges, and F faces. Its prism has 2 _V_ vertices, 2 _E_ + _V_ edges, 2 _F_ + _E_ faces, and 2 + _F_ cells.
*   Take a [polychoron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polychoron "Polychoron") with V vertices, E edges, F faces, and C cells. Its prism has 2 _V_ vertices, 2 _E_ + _V_ edges, 2 _F_ + _E_ faces, 2 _C_ + _F_ cells, and 2 + _C_ hypercells.

### Uniform prismatic polytope

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Prism_(geometry)&action=edit&section=19 "Edit section: Uniform prismatic polytope")]

A regular n-polytope represented by [Schläfli symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_symbol "Schläfli symbol"){_p_,_q_,...,_t_}  can form a uniform prismatic (_n_ + 1)-polytope represented by a [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product") of [two Schläfli symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_symbol#Prismatic_forms "Schläfli symbol"): {_p_,_q_,...,_t_}×{ }.

By dimension:

*   A 0-polytopic prism is a [line segment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment"), represented by an empty [Schläfli symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schl%C3%A4fli_symbol "Schläfli symbol"){ }.[![Image 116](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/96/Complete_graph_K2.svg/60px-Complete_graph_K2.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Complete_graph_K2.svg)
*   A 1-polytopic prism is a [rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle "Rectangle"), made from 2 translated line segments. It is represented as the product Schläfli symbol { }×{ }. If it is [square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square "Square"), symmetry can be reduced: { }×{ } = {4}.Example: [![Image 117](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d4/Square_diagonals.svg/60px-Square_diagonals.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Square_diagonals.svg), Square, { }×{ }, two parallel line segments, connected by two line segment _sides_.
*   A [polygonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon "Polygon") prism is a 3-dimensional prism made from two translated polygons connected by rectangles. A regular polygon {_p_}  can construct a uniform n-gonal prism represented by the product {_p_}×{ }. If _p_ = 4, with square sides symmetry it becomes a [cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube "Cube"): {4}×{ } = {4,3}.Example: [![Image 118](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Pentagonal_prism.png/60px-Pentagonal_prism.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pentagonal_prism.png), [Pentagonal prism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagonal_prism "Pentagonal prism"), {5}×{ }, two parallel [pentagons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pentagon "Pentagon") connected by 5 rectangular _sides_.
*   A [polyhedral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyhedron "Polyhedron") prism is a 4-dimensional prism made from two translated polyhedra connected by 3-dimensional prism cells. A regular polyhedron {_p_,_q_}  can construct the uniform polychoric prism, represented by the product {_p_,_q_}×{ }. If the polyhedron and the sides are cubes, it becomes a [tesseract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract "Tesseract"): {4,3}×{ } = {4,3,3}.Example: [![Image 119](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Dodecahedral_prism.png/60px-Dodecahedral_prism.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Dodecahedral_prism.png), [Dodecahedral prism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedral_prism "Dodecahedral prism"), {5,3}×{ }, two parallel [dodecahedra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dodecahedra "Dodecahedra") connected by 12 pentagonal prism _sides_.
*   ...

A {23}×{29}  duoprism, showing edges in [stereographic projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stereographic_projection "Stereographic projection"). The squares make a 23×29 grid [flat torus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flat_torus "Flat torus").

Higher order prismatic polytopes also exist as [cartesian products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product") of any two or more polytopes. The dimension of a product polytope is the sum of the dimensions of its elements. The first examples of these exist in 4-dimensional space; they are called [duoprisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duoprism "Duoprism") as the product of two polygons in 4-dimensions.

Regular duoprisms are represented as {_p_}×{_q_}, with pq vertices, 2 _pq_ edges, pq square faces, p q-gon faces, q p-gon faces, and bounded by p q-gonal prisms and q p-gonal prisms.

For example, {4}×{4}, a _4-4 duoprism_ is a lower symmetry form of a [tesseract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract "Tesseract"), as is {4,3}×{ }, a _cubic prism_. {4}×{4}×{ }  (4-4 duoprism prism), {4,3}×{4}  (cube-4 duoprism) and {4,3,3}×{ }  (tesseractic prism) are lower symmetry forms of a [5-cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/5-cube "5-cube").
