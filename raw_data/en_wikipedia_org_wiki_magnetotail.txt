[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Cusp-animation.gif/250px-Cusp-animation.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Cusp-animation.gif)

Artist's impression of a magnetosphere

In [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy") and [planetary science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_science "Planetary science"), a **magnetosphere** is a region of space surrounding an [astronomical object](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_object "Astronomical object"), such as a planet or other object, in which [charged particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charged_particle "Charged particle") are affected by that object's [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-NASA-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-Ratcliffe-2) It is created by a [celestial body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_body "Celestial body") with an active interior [dynamo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamo_theory "Dynamo theory").

In the space environment close to a planetary body with a [dipole magnetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dipole_magnet "Dipole magnet") field such as Earth, the field lines resemble a simple [magnetic dipole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_dipole "Magnetic dipole"). Farther out, [field lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_line "Field line") can be significantly distorted by the flow of [electrically conducting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrically_conducting "Electrically conducting")[plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)"), as emitted from the Sun (i.e., the [solar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind "Solar wind")) or a nearby star.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-Britannica-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-Van_Allen-4) Planets having active magnetospheres, like the Earth, are capable of mitigating or blocking the effects of [solar radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_radiation "Solar radiation") or [cosmic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_radiation "Cosmic radiation").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-5) Interactions of particles and atmospheres with magnetospheres are studied under the specialized scientific subjects of [plasma physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_physics "Plasma physics"), [space physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_physics "Space physics"), and [aeronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aeronomy "Aeronomy").

Study of Earth's magnetosphere began in 1600, when [William Gilbert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Gilbert_(astronomer) "William Gilbert (astronomer)") discovered that the magnetic field on the surface of Earth resembled that of a [terrella](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terrella "Terrella"), a small, magnetized sphere. In the 1940s, [Walter M. Elsasser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_M._Elsasser "Walter M. Elsasser") proposed the model of [dynamo theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamo_theory "Dynamo theory"), which attributes [Earth's magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_magnetic_field "Earth's magnetic field") to the motion of Earth's [iron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron "Iron")[outer core](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_core "Outer core"). Through the use of [magnetometers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetometer "Magnetometer"), scientists were able to study the variations in Earth's magnetic field as functions of both time and latitude and longitude.

Beginning in the late 1940s, rockets were used to study [cosmic rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_rays "Cosmic rays"). In 1958, [Explorer 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explorer_1 "Explorer 1"), the first of the Explorer series of space missions, was launched to study the intensity of cosmic rays above the atmosphere and measure the fluctuations in this activity. This mission observed the existence of the [Van Allen radiation belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belt "Van Allen radiation belt") (located in the inner region of Earth's magnetosphere), with the follow-up [Explorer 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explorer_3 "Explorer 3") later that year definitively proving its existence. Also during 1958, [Eugene Parker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eugene_Parker "Eugene Parker") proposed the idea of the [solar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind "Solar wind"), with the term 'magnetosphere' being proposed by [Thomas Gold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Gold "Thomas Gold") in 1959 to explain how the solar wind interacted with the Earth's magnetic field. The later mission of [Explorer 12](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explorer_12 "Explorer 12") in 1961 led by the Cahill and Amazeen observation in 1963 of a sudden decrease in magnetic field strength near the noon-time meridian, later was named the [magnetopause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetopause "Magnetopause"). By 1983, the [International Cometary Explorer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Cometary_Explorer "International Cometary Explorer") observed the [magnetotail](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#Magnetotail), or the distant magnetic field.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-Van_Allen-4)

Structure and behavior
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Magnetosphere&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Structure and behavior")]

The structure of magnetospheres are dependent on several factors: the type of astronomical object, the nature of sources of [plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)") and [momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum"), the [period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency") of the object's spin, the nature of the axis about which the object spins, the axis of the magnetic dipole, and the magnitude and direction of the flow of [solar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind "Solar wind").

The planetary distance where the magnetosphere can withstand the solar wind pressure is called the Chapman–Ferraro distance. This is usefully modeled by the formula wherein ![Image 2: {\displaystyle R_{\rm {P}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9ad7b72722849e7f3718ded81b6eafe4bd41f259) represents the radius of the planet, ![Image 3: {\displaystyle B_{\rm {surf}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/998ad11f77912e0e991dfffbb780620bf16f0416) represents the magnetic field on the surface of the planet at the equator, ![Image 4: {\displaystyle V_{\rm {SW}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d53f8f50a3f4050bdc17e3a379bf19ec300a85a) represents the [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") of the solar wind, ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \rho }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f7d439671d1289b6a816e6af7a304be40608d64) is the particle density of solar wind, and ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \mu _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe2fd9b8decb38a3cd158e7b6c0c6e2d987fefcc) is the [vacuum permeability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_permeability "Vacuum permeability") constant:

![Image 7: {\displaystyle R_{\rm {CF}}=R_{\rm {P}}\left({\frac {B_{\rm {surf}}^{2}}{\mu _{0}\rho V_{\rm {SW}}^{2}}}\right)^{\frac {1}{6}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a907e6de46d531bf1193b1b6007fd372800c8c27)
A magnetosphere is classified as "intrinsic" when ![Image 8: {\displaystyle R_{\rm {CF}}\gg R_{\rm {P}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6d67d32855e183b131ab19af6179883fd7cf05b), or when the primary opposition to the flow of solar wind is the magnetic field of the object. [Mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(planet) "Mercury (planet)"), Earth, [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter"), [Ganymede](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ganymede_(moon) "Ganymede (moon)"), [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn"), [Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus "Uranus"), and [Neptune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune "Neptune"), for example, exhibit intrinsic magnetospheres. A magnetosphere is classified as "induced" when ![Image 9: {\displaystyle R_{\rm {CF}}\ll R_{\rm {P}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d02d6d6187e7dd1a863ecb2657b11616e868e608), or when the solar wind is not opposed by the object's magnetic field. In this case, the solar wind interacts with the atmosphere or ionosphere of the planet (or surface of the planet, if the planet has no atmosphere). [Venus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venus "Venus") has an induced magnetic field, which means that because Venus appears to have no [internal dynamo effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamo_theory "Dynamo theory"), the only magnetic field present is that formed by the solar wind's wrapping around the physical obstacle of Venus (see also [Venus' induced magnetosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Venus#Induced_magnetosphere "Atmosphere of Venus")). When ![Image 10: {\displaystyle R_{\rm {CF}}\approx R_{\rm {P}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a822959fa2d55a2e9792eac859f4a482ebf178c), the planet itself and its magnetic field both contribute. It is possible that [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars") is of this type.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-6)

### Dawn-dusk asymmetry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Magnetosphere&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Dawn-dusk asymmetry")]

When viewed from the Sun, a celestial body's orbital motion can compress its otherwise symmetrical magnetosphere slightly, and stretch it out in the direction opposite its motion (in Earth's example, from west to east). This is known as _dawn-dusk asymmetry_. [[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-Wiley_3017-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-Troms%C3%B8_2023-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-Liu_et_al_2019-9)

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Magnetosphere_Levels.svg/250px-Magnetosphere_Levels.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Magnetosphere_Levels.svg)

An artist's rendering of the structure of a magnetosphere: 1)Bow shock. 2)Magnetosheath. 3)Magnetopause. 4)Magnetosphere. 5)Northern tail lobe. 6)Southern tail lobe. 7)Plasmasphere.

[![Image 12](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1a/Red_Giant_Plunging_Through_Space.jpg/250px-Red_Giant_Plunging_Through_Space.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Red_Giant_Plunging_Through_Space.jpg)

[Infrared image](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermographic_camera "Thermographic camera") and artist's concept of the bow shock around [R Hydrae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R_Hydrae "R Hydrae")

The bow shock forms the outermost layer of the magnetosphere; the boundary between the magnetosphere and the surrounding medium. For stars, this is usually the boundary between the [stellar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_wind "Stellar wind") and [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium"); for planets, the speed of the solar wind there decreases as it approaches the magnetopause.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-10) Due to interactions with the bow shock, the [stellar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_wind "Stellar wind")[plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)") gains a substantial [anisotropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anisotropy "Anisotropy"), leading to various [plasma instabilities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_instabilities "Plasma instabilities") upstream and downstream of the bow shock. [[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-11)

The magnetosheath is the region of the magnetosphere between the bow shock and the magnetopause. It is formed mainly from shocked solar wind, though it contains a small amount of plasma from the magnetosphere.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-cluster-12) It is an area exhibiting high particle [energy flux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Energy_flux "Energy flux"), where the direction and magnitude of the magnetic field varies erratically. This is caused by the collection of solar wind gas that has effectively undergone [thermalization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermalization "Thermalization"). It acts as a cushion that transmits the pressure from the flow of the solar wind and the barrier of the magnetic field from the object.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-Van_Allen-4)

The magnetopause is the area of the magnetosphere wherein the pressure from the planetary magnetic field is balanced with the pressure from the solar wind.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-Britannica-3) It is the convergence of the shocked solar wind from the magnetosheath with the magnetic field of the object and plasma from the magnetosphere. Because both sides of this convergence contain magnetized plasma, the interactions between them are complex. The structure of the magnetopause depends upon the [Mach number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mach_number "Mach number") and [beta ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_(plasma_physics) "Beta (plasma physics)") of the plasma, as well as the magnetic field.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-13) The magnetopause changes size and shape as the pressure from the solar wind fluctuates.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-14)

Opposite the compressed magnetic field is the magnetotail, where the magnetosphere extends far beyond the astronomical object. It contains two lobes, referred to as the _northern_ and _southern_ tail lobes. Magnetic field lines in the northern tail lobe point towards the object while those in the southern tail lobe point away. The tail lobes are almost empty, with few charged particles opposing the flow of the solar wind. The two lobes are separated by a plasma sheet, an area where the magnetic field is weaker, and the density of charged particles is higher.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-tail-15)

### Earth's magnetosphere

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Magnetosphere&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Earth's magnetosphere")]

[![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f3/Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg/250px-Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Magnetosphere_rendition.jpg)

Artist's rendition of Earth's magnetosphere

[![Image 14](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bb/Structure_of_the_magnetosphere_LanguageSwitch.svg/500px-Structure_of_the_magnetosphere_LanguageSwitch.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=File:Structure_of_the_magnetosphere_LanguageSwitch.svg&lang=en)

Diagram of Earth's magnetosphere

Over Earth's [equator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equator "Equator"), the magnetic field lines become almost horizontal, then return to reconnect at high latitudes. However, at high altitudes, the magnetic field is significantly distorted by the solar wind and its solar magnetic field. On the dayside of Earth, the magnetic field is significantly compressed by the solar wind to a distance of approximately 65,000 kilometers (40,000 mi). Earth's bow shock is about 17 kilometers (11 mi) thick[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-16) and located about 90,000 kilometers (56,000 mi) from Earth.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-17) The magnetopause exists at a distance of several hundred kilometers above Earth's surface. Earth's magnetopause has been compared to a [sieve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sieve "Sieve") because it allows solar wind particles to enter. [Kelvin–Helmholtz instabilities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin%E2%80%93Helmholtz_instability "Kelvin–Helmholtz instability") occur when large swirls of plasma travel along the edge of the magnetosphere at different velocities from the magnetosphere, causing the plasma to slip past. This results in [magnetic reconnection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_reconnection "Magnetic reconnection"), and as the magnetic field lines break and reconnect, solar wind particles are able to enter the magnetosphere.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-18) On Earth's nightside, the magnetic field extends in the magnetotail, which lengthwise exceeds 6,300,000 kilometers (3,900,000 mi).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-Britannica-3) Earth's magnetotail is the primary source of the [polar aurora](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy) "Aurora (astronomy)").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-tail-15) Also, NASA scientists have suggested that Earth's magnetotail might cause "dust storms" on the Moon by creating a potential difference between the day side and the night side.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-19)

Many astronomical objects generate and maintain magnetospheres. In the Solar System this includes the Sun, [Mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(planet) "Mercury (planet)"), [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth"), [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter"), [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn"), [Uranus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uranus "Uranus"), [Neptune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neptune "Neptune"),[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-Planetary_Shields:_Magnetospheres-20) and [Ganymede](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ganymede_(moon) "Ganymede (moon)"). The [magnetosphere of Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetosphere_of_Jupiter "Magnetosphere of Jupiter") is the largest planetary magnetosphere in the Solar System, extending up to 7,000,000 kilometers (4,300,000 mi) on the dayside and almost to the orbit of [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn") on the nightside.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-21) Jupiter's magnetosphere is stronger than Earth's by an [order of magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Order_of_magnitude "Order of magnitude"), and its [magnetic moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_moment "Magnetic moment") is approximately 18,000 times larger.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-22)[Venus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venus "Venus"), [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars"), and [Pluto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pluto "Pluto"), on the other hand, have no _intrinsic_ magnetic field. This may have had significant effects on their geological history. It is hypothesized that Venus and Mars may have lost their primordial water to [photodissociation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photodissociation "Photodissociation") and the solar wind. A strong magnetosphere, were it present, would greatly slow down this process.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-Planetary_Shields:_Magnetospheres-20)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-23)

Magnetospheres generated by [exoplanets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exoplanet "Exoplanet") are thought to be common, though the first discoveries did not come until the 2010s. In 2014, a magnetic field around [HD 209458 b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HD_209458_b "HD 209458 b") was inferred from the way [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") was evaporating from the planet.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-25)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-26) In 2019, the strength of the surface magnetic fields of 4 [hot Jupiters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hot_Jupiter "Hot Jupiter") were estimated and ranged between 20 and 120 [gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss_(unit) "Gauss (unit)") compared to Jupiter's surface magnetic field of 4.3 gauss.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-27)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-28) In 2020, a radio emission in the 14-30 MHz band was detected from the [Tau Boötis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tau_Bo%C3%B6tis "Tau Boötis") system, likely associated with [cyclotron radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclotron_radiation "Cyclotron radiation") from the poles of [Tau Boötis b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tau_Bo%C3%B6tis_b "Tau Boötis b") which might be a signature of a planetary magnetic field.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-29)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-30) In 2021 a magnetic field generated by the [hot Neptune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hot_Neptune "Hot Neptune")[HAT-P-11b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HAT-P-11b "HAT-P-11b") became the first to be confirmed.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-sedHatp11b-31) The first unconfirmed detection of a magnetic field generated by a terrestrial exoplanet was found in 2023 on [YZ Ceti b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/YZ_Ceti_b "YZ Ceti b").[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-Pineda2023-32)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-Trigilio2023-33)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-34)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetotail#cite_note-35)
