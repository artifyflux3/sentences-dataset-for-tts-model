In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), a **couple** or torque is a pair of forces that are equal in magnitude but opposite in their direction of action. A couple produce a pure [rotational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation") motion without any [translational](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation "Translation") form.

[![Image 18](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/35/Couple_phys.jpg/250px-Couple_phys.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Couple_phys.jpg)

Two forces acting on opposite direction with equal magnitude.

Simple couple
-------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Couple_(mechanics)&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Simple couple")]

The simplest kind of couple consists of two equal and opposite [forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force") whose [lines of action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_of_action "Line of action") do not coincide. This is called a "simple couple".[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Couple_(mechanics)#cite_note-Kane-1) The forces have a turning effect or moment called a [torque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque") about an axis which is [normal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_(geometry) "Normal (geometry)") (perpendicular) to the plane of the forces. The [SI unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_unit "SI unit") for the torque of the couple is [newton metre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_metre "Newton metre").

If the two forces are F and −_F_, then the [magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") of the torque is given by the following formula: τ=F d{\displaystyle \tau =Fd}![Image 19: {\displaystyle \tau =Fd}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d304432ea8558d9fd598c7d4a0e34579bcfe351e) where

*   τ{\displaystyle \tau }![Image 20: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) is the moment of couple
*   F is the magnitude of the force
*   d is the perpendicular distance (moment) between the two parallel forces

The magnitude of the torque is equal to _F_ • _d_, with the direction of the torque given by the [unit vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector")e^{\displaystyle {\hat {e}}}![Image 21: {\displaystyle {\hat {e}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ac09328845eecc01a117acbf303c1bc1decc4a1), which is perpendicular to the plane containing the two forces and positive being a counter-clockwise couple. When d is taken as a vector between the points of action of the forces, then the torque is the [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product") of d and F, i.e. τ=|d×F|.{\displaystyle \mathbf {\tau } =|\mathbf {d} \times \mathbf {F} |.}![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\tau } =|\mathbf {d} \times \mathbf {F} |.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fc7ec168819bb1579757989211a43ff2ffbae613)

Independence of reference point
-------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Couple_(mechanics)&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Independence of reference point")]

The moment of a force is only defined with respect to a certain point P (it is said to be the "moment about P") and, in general, when P is changed, the moment changes. However, the moment (torque) of a _couple_ is _independent_ of the reference point P: Any point will give the same moment.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Couple_(mechanics)#cite_note-Kane-1) In other words, a couple, unlike any more general moments, is a "free vector". (This fact is called _[Varignon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre\_Varignon "Pierre Varignon")'s Second Moment Theorem_.)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Couple_(mechanics)#cite_note-2)

The [proof](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_proof "Geometric proof") of this claim is as follows: Suppose there are a set of force vectors **F**1, **F**2, etc. that form a couple, with position vectors (about some origin P), **r**1, **r**2, etc., respectively. The moment about P is

M=r 1×F 1+r 2×F 2+⋯{\displaystyle M=\mathbf {r} _{1}\times \mathbf {F} _{1}+\mathbf {r} _{2}\times \mathbf {F} _{2}+\cdots }![Image 23: {\displaystyle M=\mathbf {r} _{1}\times \mathbf {F} _{1}+\mathbf {r} _{2}\times \mathbf {F} _{2}+\cdots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21bb8024e8f411fbd4a3d40587455c818de3c1c0)
Now we pick a new reference point P' that differs from P by the vector **r**. The new moment is

M′=(r 1+r)×F 1+(r 2+r)×F 2+⋯{\displaystyle M'=(\mathbf {r} _{1}+\mathbf {r} )\times \mathbf {F} _{1}+(\mathbf {r} _{2}+\mathbf {r} )\times \mathbf {F} _{2}+\cdots }![Image 24: {\displaystyle M'=(\mathbf {r} _{1}+\mathbf {r} )\times \mathbf {F} _{1}+(\mathbf {r} _{2}+\mathbf {r} )\times \mathbf {F} _{2}+\cdots }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65b399172f218fb692f3f26ee9ff73a20357ae86)
Now the [distributive property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distributive_property "Distributive property") of the [cross product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_product "Cross product") implies

M′=(r 1×F 1+r 2×F 2+⋯)+r×(F 1+F 2+⋯).{\displaystyle M'=\left(\mathbf {r} _{1}\times \mathbf {F} _{1}+\mathbf {r} _{2}\times \mathbf {F} _{2}+\cdots \right)+\mathbf {r} \times \left(\mathbf {F} _{1}+\mathbf {F} _{2}+\cdots \right).}![Image 25: {\displaystyle M'=\left(\mathbf {r} _{1}\times \mathbf {F} _{1}+\mathbf {r} _{2}\times \mathbf {F} _{2}+\cdots \right)+\mathbf {r} \times \left(\mathbf {F} _{1}+\mathbf {F} _{2}+\cdots \right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f9b8035c680aad15569387496216d3298fe098d)
However, the definition of a force couple means that

F 1+F 2+⋯=0.{\displaystyle \mathbf {F} _{1}+\mathbf {F} _{2}+\cdots =0.}![Image 26: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} _{1}+\mathbf {F} _{2}+\cdots =0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/525b52991f29f227cd1337aebc2568014c2ffb76)
Therefore,

M′=r 1×F 1+r 2×F 2+⋯=M{\displaystyle M'=\mathbf {r} _{1}\times \mathbf {F} _{1}+\mathbf {r} _{2}\times \mathbf {F} _{2}+\cdots =M}![Image 27: {\displaystyle M'=\mathbf {r} _{1}\times \mathbf {F} _{1}+\mathbf {r} _{2}\times \mathbf {F} _{2}+\cdots =M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d786fd638c119c9ad1c358961983b58c65ae71f0)
This proves that the moment is independent of reference point, which is proof that a couple is a free vector.

Forces and couples
------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Couple_(mechanics)&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Forces and couples")]

[![Image 28](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e4/Force_and_couple.PNG/250px-Force_and_couple.PNG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Force_and_couple.PNG)

A force _F_ applied to a rigid body at a distance _d_ from the center of mass has the same effect as the same force applied directly to the center of mass and a couple _Cℓ = Fd_. The couple produces an [angular acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_acceleration "Angular acceleration") of the rigid body at right angles to the plane of the couple.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Couple_(mechanics)#cite_note-DuBois-3) The force at the center of mass accelerates the body in the direction of the force without change in orientation. The general theorems are:[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Couple_(mechanics)#cite_note-DuBois-3)

A single force acting at any point _O′_ of a rigid body can be replaced by an equal and parallel force _F_ acting at any given point _O_ and a couple with forces parallel to _F_ whose moment is _M = Fd_, _d_ being the separation of _O_ and _O′_. Conversely, a couple and a force in the plane of the couple can be replaced by a single force, appropriately located.Any couple can be replaced by another in the same plane of the same direction and moment, having any desired force or any desired arm.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Couple_(mechanics)#cite_note-DuBois-3)

Applications
------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Couple_(mechanics)&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Applications")]

Couples are very important in [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering") and the physical sciences. A few examples are:

*   The forces exerted by one's hand on a screw-driver
*   The forces exerted by the tip of a screwdriver on the head of a screw
*   Drag forces acting on a spinning [propeller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propeller "Propeller")
*   Forces on an [electric dipole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_dipole "Electric dipole") in a uniform electric field
*   The [reaction control system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reaction_control_system "Reaction control system") on a spacecraft
*   Force exerted by hands on [steering wheel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steering_wheel "Steering wheel")
*   'Rocking couples' are a regular imbalance giving rise to vibration
