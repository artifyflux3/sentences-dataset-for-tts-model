Temperature of the cosmic background radiation spectrum based on [COBE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Background_Explorer "Cosmic Background Explorer") data: uncorrected (top); corrected for the dipole term due to our [peculiar velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peculiar_velocity "Peculiar velocity") (middle); corrected additionally for contributions from our galaxy (bottom).

**Cosmic background radiation** is [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation") that fills all space. The origin of this radiation depends on the region of the [spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum "Electromagnetic spectrum") that is observed. One component is the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background"). This component is [redshifted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshifted "Redshifted")[photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") that have freely streamed from an [epoch when the Universe became transparent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recombination_(cosmology) "Recombination (cosmology)") for the first time to radiation. Its discovery and detailed observations of its properties are considered one of the major confirmations of the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_background_radiation#cite_note-1) Background radiation is largely homogeneous and isotropic. A slight detectable [anisotropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anisotropy "Anisotropy") is present which correlates to [galaxy filaments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_filaments "Galaxy filaments") and [voids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Void_(astronomy) "Void (astronomy)").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_background_radiation#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_background_radiation#cite_note-3) The discovery (by chance in 1965) of the cosmic background radiation suggests that the early universe was dominated by a radiation field, a field of extremely high temperature and pressure.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_background_radiation#cite_note-4)

There is background radiation observed across all wavelength regimes, peaking in [microwave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave "Microwave"), but also notable in [infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared") and [X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray") regimes. Fluctuations in cosmic background radiation across regimes create parameters for the amount of baryonic matter in the universe.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_background_radiation#cite_note-5) See [cosmic infrared background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_infrared_background "Cosmic infrared background") and [X-ray background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_background "X-ray background"). See also [cosmic neutrino background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background "Cosmic neutrino background") and [extragalactic background light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extragalactic_background_light "Extragalactic background light").

The [Sunyaev–Zel'dovich effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunyaev%E2%80%93Zel%27dovich_effect "Sunyaev–Zel'dovich effect") shows the phenomena of radiant cosmic background radiation interacting with "[electron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron "Electron")" clouds distorting the spectrum of the radiation.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_background_radiation#cite_note-6)

Timeline of significant events
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Cosmic_background_radiation&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Timeline of significant events")]

1896: [Charles Édouard Guillaume](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_%C3%89douard_Guillaume "Charles Édouard Guillaume") estimates the "radiation of the stars" to be 5.6[K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_background_radiation#cite_note-history-7)

1926: Sir [Arthur Eddington](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arthur_Eddington "Arthur Eddington") estimates the non-thermal radiation of [starlight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Starlight "Starlight") in the galaxy has an effective temperature of 3.2 K. [[1]](http://adsabs.harvard.edu/cgi-bin/nph-bib_query?bibcode=1927Sci....66...81E&db_key=AST&data_type=HTML&format=&high=42ca922c9c22437)

1930s: [Erich Regener](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erich_Regener "Erich Regener") calculates that the non-thermal spectrum of cosmic rays in the galaxy has an effective temperature of 2.8 K.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_background_radiation#cite_note-history-7)

1931: The term _microwave_ first appears in print: "When trials with wavelengths as low as 18 cm were made known, there was undisguised surprise that the problem of the micro-wave had been solved so soon." _Telegraph & Telephone Journal_ XVII. 179/1"

1938: [Walther Nernst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walther_Nernst "Walther Nernst") re-estimates the cosmic ray temperature as 0.75 K.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_background_radiation#cite_note-history-7)

1946: The term "[microwave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microwave "Microwave")" is first used in print in an astronomical context in an article "Microwave Radiation from the Sun and Moon" by [Robert Dicke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Dicke "Robert Dicke") and [Robert Beringer](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Robert_Beringer&action=edit&redlink=1 "Robert Beringer (page does not exist)").

1946: [Robert Dicke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Dicke "Robert Dicke") predicts a microwave background radiation temperature of 20 K (ref: Helge Kragh)

1946: Robert Dicke predicts a microwave background radiation temperature of "less than 20 K" but later revised to 45 K (ref: Stephen G. Brush).

1946: [George Gamow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Gamow "George Gamow") estimates a temperature of 50 K.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_background_radiation#cite_note-history-7)

1948: [Ralph Alpher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ralph_Alpher "Ralph Alpher") and [Robert Herman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Herman "Robert Herman") re-estimate Gamow's estimate at 5 K.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_background_radiation#cite_note-history-7)

1949: Ralph Alpher and Robert Herman re-re-estimate Gamow's estimate at 28 K.

1960s: Robert Dicke re-estimates a MBR (microwave background radiation) temperature of 40 K (ref: Helge Kragh).

1965: [Arno Penzias](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arno_Penzias "Arno Penzias") and [Robert Woodrow Wilson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Woodrow_Wilson "Robert Woodrow Wilson") measure the temperature to be approximately 3 K. Robert Dicke, [P. J. E. Peebles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P._J._E._Peebles "P. J. E. Peebles"), P. G. Roll and [D. T. Wilkinson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Todd_Wilkinson "David Todd Wilkinson") interpret this radiation as a signature of the Big Bang.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_background_radiation#cite_note-history-7)
