In [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra"), a **coordinate vector** is a representation of a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(mathematics_and_physics) "Vector (mathematics and physics)") as an ordered list of numbers (a [tuple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple "Tuple")) that describes the vector in terms of a particular [ordered basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_basis "Ordered basis").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_vector#cite_note-AntonRorres2010-1) An easy example may be a position such as (5, 2, 1) in a 3-dimensional [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") with the basis as the axes of this system. Coordinates are always specified relative to an ordered basis. Bases and their associated coordinate representations let one realize [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") and [linear transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_transformation "Linear transformation") concretely as [column vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Column_vector "Column vector"), [row vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Row_vector "Row vector"), and [matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)"); hence, they are useful in calculations.

The idea of a coordinate vector can also be used for infinite-dimensional vector spaces, as addressed below.

Let _V_ be a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") of [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(vector_space) "Dimension (vector space)")_n_ over a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)")_F_ and let

Basis transformation matrix
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Coordinate_vector&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Basis transformation matrix")]

Let _B_ and _C_ be two different bases of a vector space _V_, and let us mark with ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \lbrack M\rbrack _{C}^{B}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b837005840f03c5faf444852e2af32c72f373c44) the [matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") which has columns consisting of the _C_ representation of basis vectors _b 1, b 2, …, b n_:

![Image 30: {\displaystyle \lbrack M\rbrack _{C}^{B}={\begin{bmatrix}\lbrack b_{1}\rbrack _{C}&\cdots &\lbrack b_{n}\rbrack _{C}\end{bmatrix}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0c748250f890cb3ac55083fd5e5699e84b3769a)
This matrix is referred to as the **basis transformation matrix** from _B_ to _C_. It can be regarded as an [automorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automorphism "Automorphism") over ![Image 31: {\displaystyle F^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/caeaf2cf3b94c8a21721478bcd52964068489df9). Any vector _v_ represented in _B_ can be transformed to a representation in _C_ as follows:

![Image 32: {\displaystyle \lbrack v\rbrack _{C}=\lbrack M\rbrack _{C}^{B}\lbrack v\rbrack _{B}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6dd9bf78f908e2bcd3124b61c20219fb1c0abaf6)
Under the transformation of basis, notice that the superscript on the transformation matrix, _M_, and the subscript on the coordinate vector, _v_, are the same, and seemingly cancel, leaving the remaining subscript. While this may serve as a memory aid, it is important to note that no such cancellation, or similar mathematical operation, is taking place.

The matrix _M_ is an [invertible matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invertible_matrix "Invertible matrix") and _M_−1 is the basis transformation matrix from _C_ to _B_. In other words,

Infinite-dimensional vector spaces
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Coordinate_vector&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Infinite-dimensional vector spaces")]

Suppose _V_ is an infinite-dimensional vector space over a field _F_. If the dimension is _κ_, then there is some basis of _κ_ elements for _V_. After an order is chosen, the basis can be considered an ordered basis. The elements of _V_ are finite linear combinations of elements in the basis, which give rise to unique coordinate representations exactly as described before. The only change is that the indexing set for the coordinates is not finite. Since a given vector _v_ is a _finite_ linear combination of basis elements, the only nonzero entries of the coordinate vector for _v_ will be the nonzero coefficients of the linear combination representing _v_. Thus the coordinate vector for _v_ is zero except in finitely many entries.

The linear transformations between (possibly) infinite-dimensional vector spaces can be modeled, analogously to the finite-dimensional case, with [infinite matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinite_matrix#Infinite_matrices "Infinite matrix"). The special case of the transformations from _V_ into _V_ is described in the [full linear ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Full_linear_ring "Full linear ring") article.
