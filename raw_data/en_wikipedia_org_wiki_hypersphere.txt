Just as a [stereographic projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stereographic_projection "Stereographic projection") can project a sphere's surface to a plane, it can also project a 3-sphere into 3-space. This image shows three coordinate directions projected to 3-space: parallels (red), [meridians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meridian_(geography) "Meridian (geography)") (blue), and hypermeridians (green). Due to the [conformal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformal_map "Conformal map") property of the stereographic projection, the curves intersect each other orthogonally (in the yellow points) as in 4D. All of the curves are circles: the curves that intersect ⟨0,0,0,1⟩ have an infinite radius (= straight line).

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), an **n-sphere** or **hypersphere** is an ⁠![Image 3: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-[dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension") generalization of the ⁠![Image 4: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf)⁠-dimensional [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle") and ⁠![Image 5: {\displaystyle 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/901fc910c19990d0dbaaefe4726ceb1a4e217a0f)⁠-dimensional [sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sphere "Sphere") to any non-negative [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer")⁠![Image 6: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠.

The circle is considered [1-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1-dimensional "1-dimensional") and the sphere [2-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2-dimensional "2-dimensional") because a point within them has one and two degrees of freedom respectively. However, the typical [embedding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Embedding "Embedding") of the 1-dimensional circle is in 2-dimensional space, the 2-dimensional sphere is usually depicted embedded in [3-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3-dimensional_space "3-dimensional space"), and a general ⁠![Image 7: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-sphere is embedded in an ⁠![Image 8: {\displaystyle n+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a135e65a42f2d73cccbfc4569523996ca0036f1)⁠-dimensional space. The term _hyper_ sphere is commonly used to distinguish spheres of dimension ⁠![Image 9: {\displaystyle n\geq 3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73136e4a27fe39c123d16a7808e76d3162ce42bb)⁠ which are thus embedded in a space of dimension ⁠![Image 10: {\displaystyle n+1\geq 4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1c6111ad5cf4c702e80a6a05dff5772e0a867b75)⁠, which means that they cannot be easily visualized. The ⁠![Image 11: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-sphere is the setting for ⁠![Image 12: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-dimensional [spherical geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_geometry "Spherical geometry").

Considered extrinsically, as a [hypersurface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersurface "Hypersurface") embedded in ⁠![Image 13: {\displaystyle (n+1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b30a29cfd35628469f9dbffea4804f5b422f3037)⁠-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space"), an ⁠![Image 14: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-sphere is the [locus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Locus_(mathematics) "Locus (mathematics)") of [points](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_(geometry) "Point (geometry)") at equal [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_distance "Euclidean distance") (the _[radius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radius "Radius")_) from a given _[center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center\_(geometry) "Center (geometry)")_ point. Its [interior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interior_(topology) "Interior (topology)"), consisting of all points closer to the center than the radius, is an ⁠![Image 15: {\displaystyle (n+1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b30a29cfd35628469f9dbffea4804f5b422f3037)⁠-dimensional [ball](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ball_(mathematics) "Ball (mathematics)"). In particular:

Given a [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system"), the _[unit ⁠![Image 16: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit\_n-sphere "Unit n-sphere")_ of radius ⁠![Image 17: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf)⁠ can be defined as:

![Image 18: {\displaystyle S^{n}=\left\{x\in \mathbb {R} ^{n+1}:\left\|x\right\|=1\right\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eb703228072160936e1e27d159a5a72c2f81b78f)
Considered intrinsically, when ⁠![Image 19: {\displaystyle n\geq 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d8ce9ce38d06f6bf5a3fe063118c09c2b6202bfe)⁠, the ⁠![Image 20: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-sphere is a [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold") of positive [constant curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_curvature "Constant curvature"), and is [orientable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientable_manifold "Orientable manifold"). The [geodesics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesics "Geodesics") of the ⁠![Image 21: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-sphere are called [great circles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_circle "Great circle").

The [stereographic projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stereographic_projection "Stereographic projection") maps the ⁠![Image 22: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-sphere onto ⁠![Image 23: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-space with a single adjoined [point at infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_at_infinity "Point at infinity"); under the [metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor") thereby defined, ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}\cup \{\infty \}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c37a2739ce1aef37fee545471937a0f20927832) is a model for the ⁠![Image 25: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-sphere.

In the more general setting of [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology"), any [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space") that is [homeomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphic "Homeomorphic") to the unit ⁠![Image 26: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-sphere is called an ⁠![Image 27: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-_sphere_. Under inverse stereographic projection, the ⁠![Image 28: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-sphere is the [one-point compactification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/One-point_compactification "One-point compactification") of ⁠![Image 29: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-space. The ⁠![Image 30: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-spheres admit several other topological descriptions: for example, they can be constructed by gluing two ⁠![Image 31: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-dimensional spaces together, by identifying the boundary of an [⁠![Image 32: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube "Hypercube") with a point, or (inductively) by forming the [suspension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Suspension_(topology) "Suspension (topology)") of an ⁠![Image 33: {\displaystyle (n-1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df88c6333caaf6471cf277f24b802ff9931b133e)⁠-sphere. When ⁠![Image 34: {\displaystyle n\geq 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6bf67f9d06ca3af619657f8d20ee1322da77174)⁠ it is [simply connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simply_connected "Simply connected"); the ⁠![Image 35: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf)⁠-sphere (circle) is not simply connected; the ⁠![Image 36: {\displaystyle 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2aae8864a3c1fec9585261791a809ddec1489950)⁠-sphere is not even connected, consisting of two discrete points.

For any [natural number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number")⁠![Image 37: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠, an ⁠![Image 38: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-sphere of radius ⁠![Image 39: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538)⁠ is defined as the set of points in ⁠![Image 40: {\displaystyle (n+1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b30a29cfd35628469f9dbffea4804f5b422f3037)⁠-dimensional [Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space") that are at distance ⁠![Image 41: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538)⁠ from some fixed point ⁠![Image 42: {\displaystyle \mathbf {c} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8798d172f59e21f2ce193a3118d4063d19353ded)⁠, where ⁠![Image 43: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538)⁠ may be any [positive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Positive_number "Positive number")[real number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") and where ⁠![Image 44: {\displaystyle \mathbf {c} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8798d172f59e21f2ce193a3118d4063d19353ded)⁠ may be any point in ⁠![Image 45: {\displaystyle (n+1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b30a29cfd35628469f9dbffea4804f5b422f3037)⁠-dimensional space. ### Topological description

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=N-sphere&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Topological description")]

[Topologically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology"), an ⁠![Image 68: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-sphere can be constructed as a [one-point compactification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexandroff_extension "Alexandroff extension") of ⁠![Image 69: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-dimensional Euclidean space. Briefly, the ⁠![Image 70: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-sphere can be described as ⁠![Image 71: {\displaystyle S^{n}=\mathbb {R} ^{n}\cup \{\infty \}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a600bc930718b61fd04cba4a35f540834b3d8ad)⁠, which is ⁠![Image 72: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-dimensional Euclidean space plus a single point representing infinity in all directions. In particular, if a single point is removed from an ⁠![Image 73: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-sphere, it becomes [homeomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphism "Homeomorphism") to ![Image 74: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d). This forms the basis for [stereographic projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stereographic_projection "Stereographic projection").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersphere#cite_note-1)

Spherical coordinates
---------------------

We may define a coordinate system in an ⁠![Image 131: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-dimensional Euclidean space which is analogous to the [spherical coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinates "Spherical coordinates") defined for ⁠![Image 132: {\displaystyle 3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/991e33c6e207b12546f15bdfee8b5726eafbbb2f)⁠-dimensional Euclidean space, in which the coordinates consist of a radial coordinate ⁠![Image 133: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538)⁠, and ⁠![Image 134: {\displaystyle n-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fbd0b0f32b28f51962943ee9ede4fb34198a2521)⁠ angular coordinates ⁠![Image 135: {\displaystyle \varphi _{1},\varphi _{2},\ldots ,\varphi _{n-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc60d61ffbfefc816c755d7ef3ce5301067407e2)⁠, where the angles ⁠![Image 136: {\displaystyle \varphi _{1},\varphi _{2},\ldots ,\varphi _{n-2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06e828080ca8b39ea86847260508a09c8015a697)⁠ range over ⁠![Image 137: {\displaystyle [0,\pi ]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e2a912eda6ef1afe46a81b518fe9da64a832751)⁠ radians (or ⁠![Image 138: {\displaystyle [0,180]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d573d37a907c3c4c266cda6e0b59c0431fa4145)⁠ degrees) and ⁠![Image 139: {\displaystyle \varphi _{n-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec9f7e94e042b8740be987fec53f620872378d46)⁠ ranges over ⁠![Image 140: {\displaystyle [0,2\pi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ec72cfde732f42822df3cbbe175b7465887eb80)⁠ radians (or ⁠![Image 141: {\displaystyle [0,360)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9ff16276acc475a467e0edafc8b7fb4a9d2d3f1)⁠ degrees). If ⁠![Image 142: {\displaystyle x_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e87000dd6142b81d041896a30fe58f0c3acb2158)⁠ are the Cartesian coordinates, then we may compute ⁠![Image 143: {\displaystyle x_{1},\ldots ,x_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/737e02a5fbf8bc31d443c91025339f9fd1de1065)⁠ from ⁠![Image 144: {\displaystyle r,\varphi _{1},\ldots ,\varphi _{n-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21187dedd77fb0628c306bb6ce4a9595a6428687)⁠ with:[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersphere#cite_note-3)[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersphere#cite_note-4)

Therefore the determinant of ⁠![Image 193: {\displaystyle J_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22b79b47e08e4e16510d309639ef56a24c28696c)⁠ is

Induction then gives a [closed-form expression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed-form_expression "Closed-form expression") for the volume element in spherical coordinates

The natural choice of an orthogonal basis over the angular coordinates is a product of [ultraspherical polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gegenbauer_polynomial "Gegenbauer polynomial"),

### Polyspherical coordinates

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=N-sphere&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Polyspherical coordinates")]

The standard spherical coordinate system arises from writing ⁠![Image 205: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d)⁠ as the product ⁠![Image 206: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} \times \mathbb {R} ^{n-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f1ecbbb937f19249ac689398b26ae0396edc45d0)⁠. These two factors may be related using polar coordinates. For each point ⁠![Image 207: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32adf004df5eb0a8c7fd8c0b6b7405183c5a5ef2)⁠ of ![Image 208: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d), the standard Cartesian coordinates

![Image 209: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} =(x_{1},\dots ,x_{n})=(y_{1},z_{1},\dots ,z_{n-1})=(y_{1},\mathbf {z} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4dd785a631f66f3c14cf1b3936f3100e7ec80ef4)
can be transformed into a mixed polar–Cartesian coordinate system:

![Image 210: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} =(r\sin \theta ,(r\cos \theta ){\hat {\mathbf {z} }}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18b3d184718dcc85de2a6c1863f3a00517abe57a)
This says that points in ⁠![Image 211: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d)⁠ may be expressed by taking the ray starting at the origin and passing through ![Image 212: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {z} }}=\mathbf {z} /\lVert \mathbf {z} \rVert \in S^{n-2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d482d51511afac3aa1acc6233c3467463127fd60), rotating it towards ![Image 213: {\displaystyle (1,0,\dots ,0)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a0eb67241d4f0054ada838fbf3d07c009dfd762) by ![Image 214: {\displaystyle \theta =\arcsin y_{1}/r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d00011a650ba4a7f622f1a85bf0889ff453bb77b), and traveling a distance ![Image 215: {\displaystyle r=\lVert \mathbf {x} \rVert }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5af92d83f4b6f7dca41ff0466ea3dd67566e251e) along the ray. Repeating this decomposition eventually leads to the standard spherical coordinate system.

Polyspherical coordinate systems arise from a generalization of this construction.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersphere#cite_note-5) The space ⁠![Image 216: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d)⁠ is split as the product of two Euclidean spaces of smaller dimension, but neither space is required to be a line. Specifically, suppose that ⁠![Image 217: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36)⁠ and ⁠![Image 218: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d)⁠ are positive integers such that ⁠![Image 219: {\displaystyle n=p+q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89e4fd18a70ca1ce43f1066e935d594b50cba260)⁠. Then ⁠![Image 220: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}=\mathbb {R} ^{p}\times \mathbb {R} ^{q}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a88342d9321089c8492bab594bc8c86af020fb0)⁠. Using this decomposition, a point ⁠![Image 221: {\displaystyle x\in \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c520ee2cb6ccf8a93c89a8c58a8378796bd52e53)⁠ may be written as

![Image 222: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} =(x_{1},\dots ,x_{n})=(y_{1},\dots ,y_{p},z_{1},\dots ,z_{q})=(\mathbf {y} ,\mathbf {z} ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f73c4d0633f2fc80bfe21b9615898455a9140704)
This can be transformed into a mixed polar–Cartesian coordinate system by writing:

![Image 223: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} =((r\sin \theta ){\hat {\mathbf {y} }},(r\cos \theta ){\hat {\mathbf {z} }}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6717ea0dcee88205518340b3152ed970ff9b86f0)
Here ![Image 224: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {y} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3826fc591cfe42348f58b15e4c23152f30931ef) and ![Image 225: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {z} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d4717aa1fda3698bf5489269af7a5904c39c07d) are the unit vectors associated to ⁠![Image 226: {\displaystyle \mathbf {y} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb25a040b592282dc2a254c3117e792c3c81161f)⁠ and ⁠![Image 227: {\displaystyle \mathbf {z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/82eca5d0928078d5a61b9e7e98cc73db31070909)⁠. This expresses ⁠![Image 228: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32adf004df5eb0a8c7fd8c0b6b7405183c5a5ef2)⁠ in terms of ⁠![Image 229: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {y} }}\in S^{p-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e429d3b56368fe387b9f26a2366011b3d56f93cc)⁠, ⁠![Image 230: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {z} }}\in S^{q-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c0e5b163605cd8002f34b375708f4b716455ef17)⁠, ⁠![Image 231: {\displaystyle r\geq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aa96c19954fcda2695f988938ccf091d2bc2bbae)⁠, and an angle ⁠![Image 232: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af)⁠. It can be shown that the domain of ⁠![Image 233: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af)⁠ is ⁠![Image 234: {\displaystyle [0,2\pi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ec72cfde732f42822df3cbbe175b7465887eb80)⁠ if ⁠![Image 235: {\displaystyle p=q=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aa155a43d5edd39c8c9350646ed93aa16ea83a12)⁠, ⁠![Image 236: {\displaystyle [0,\pi ]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e2a912eda6ef1afe46a81b518fe9da64a832751)⁠ if exactly one of ⁠![Image 237: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36)⁠ and ⁠![Image 238: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d)⁠ is ⁠![Image 239: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf)⁠, and ⁠![Image 240: {\displaystyle [0,\pi /2]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/db58baa407ae179d23402f61cb3edcfd7e4fa5b1)⁠ if neither ⁠![Image 241: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36)⁠ nor ⁠![Image 242: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d)⁠ are ⁠![Image 243: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf)⁠. The inverse transformation is

![Image 244: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}r&=\lVert \mathbf {x} \rVert ,\\\theta &=\arcsin {\frac {\lVert \mathbf {y} \rVert }{\lVert \mathbf {x} \rVert }}=\arccos {\frac {\lVert \mathbf {z} \rVert }{\lVert \mathbf {x} \rVert }}=\arctan {\frac {\lVert \mathbf {y} \rVert }{\lVert \mathbf {z} \rVert }}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d28de356db718d7eb2205ef7b0c20817f714ff0)
These splittings may be repeated as long as one of the factors involved has dimension two or greater. A **polyspherical coordinate system** is the result of repeating these splittings until there are no Cartesian coordinates left. Splittings after the first do not require a radial coordinate because the domains of ![Image 245: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {y} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3826fc591cfe42348f58b15e4c23152f30931ef) and ![Image 246: {\displaystyle {\hat {\mathbf {z} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d4717aa1fda3698bf5489269af7a5904c39c07d) are spheres, so the coordinates of a polyspherical coordinate system are a non-negative radius and ⁠![Image 247: {\displaystyle n-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fbd0b0f32b28f51962943ee9ede4fb34198a2521)⁠ angles. The possible polyspherical coordinate systems correspond to binary trees with ⁠![Image 248: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠ leaves. Each non-leaf node in the tree corresponds to a splitting and determines an angular coordinate. For instance, the root of the tree represents ⁠![Image 249: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d)⁠, and its immediate children represent the first splitting into ⁠![Image 250: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40a670215fd4556c78acd92bdc55d472548b7a21)⁠ and ⁠![Image 251: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{q}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1fc1b66f646d2486ff1b0e5512ecafb5c23e8b7c)⁠. Leaf nodes correspond to Cartesian coordinates for ⁠![Image 252: {\displaystyle S^{n-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/742bebb03fe630674b18823a59d2c75efd0066e7)⁠. The formulas for converting from polyspherical coordinates to Cartesian coordinates may be determined by finding the paths from the root to the leaf nodes. These formulas are products with one factor for each branch taken by the path. For a node whose corresponding angular coordinate is ⁠![Image 253: {\displaystyle \theta _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/302b19204ed378e99ff4575341a67eebdbe5a555)⁠, taking the left branch introduces a factor of ⁠![Image 254: {\displaystyle \sin \theta _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f61609835c34e95d42c3bdb7109995c158136db3)⁠ and taking the right branch introduces a factor of ⁠![Image 255: {\displaystyle \cos \theta _{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bc4c79242da0cf5e5a46dfc7e97e9ffc20d69c7a)⁠. The inverse transformation, from polyspherical coordinates to Cartesian coordinates, is determined by grouping nodes. Every pair of nodes having a common parent can be converted from a mixed polar–Cartesian coordinate system to a Cartesian coordinate system using the above formulas for a splitting.

Polyspherical coordinates also have an interpretation in terms of the [special orthogonal group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_orthogonal_group "Special orthogonal group"). A splitting ⁠![Image 256: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}=\mathbb {R} ^{p}\times \mathbb {R} ^{q}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a88342d9321089c8492bab594bc8c86af020fb0)⁠ determines a subgroup

![Image 257: {\displaystyle \operatorname {SO} _{p}(\mathbb {R} )\times \operatorname {SO} _{q}(\mathbb {R} )\subseteq \operatorname {SO} _{n}(\mathbb {R} ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b541232f5c30202dc24f7d2af77304ff7f3482ef)
This is the subgroup that leaves each of the two factors ![Image 258: {\displaystyle S^{p-1}\times S^{q-1}\subseteq S^{n-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0820cdc8d3c73dcf5cebd93833c01832c1c1ac1d) fixed. Choosing a set of [coset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coset "Coset") representatives for the quotient is the same as choosing representative angles for this step of the polyspherical coordinate decomposition.

The area measure has the form:

where the factors ⁠![Image 263: {\displaystyle F_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18f9a5ed62f131c8ad9acdf7f8539f103f1e0ec9)⁠ are determined by the tree. Similarly, the volume measure is

The corresponding factor ⁠![Image 267: {\displaystyle F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545fd099af8541605f7ee55f08225526be88ce57)⁠ depends on the values of ⁠![Image 268: {\displaystyle n_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ee784b70e772f55ede5e6e0bdc929994bff63413)⁠ and ⁠![Image 269: {\displaystyle n_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/840e456e3058bc0be28e5cf653b170cdbfcc3be4)⁠. When the area measure is normalized so that the area of the sphere is ⁠![Image 270: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf)⁠, these factors are as follows. Probability distributions
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=N-sphere&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Probability distributions")]

### Uniformly at random on the (_n_ − 1)-sphere

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=N-sphere&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Uniformly at random on the (n − 1)-sphere")]

See also: [Von Mises–Fisher distribution §The uniform hypersphere distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Von_Mises%E2%80%93Fisher_distribution#The_uniform_hypersphere_distribution "Von Mises–Fisher distribution").

[![Image 298](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/2sphere-uniform.png/250px-2sphere-uniform.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:2sphere-uniform.png)

A set of points drawn from a uniform distribution on the surface of a unit 2-sphere, generated using Marsaglia's algorithm.

To generate uniformly distributed random points on the unit ⁠![Image 299: {\displaystyle (n-1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df88c6333caaf6471cf277f24b802ff9931b133e)⁠-sphere (that is, the surface of the unit ⁠![Image 300: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-ball), [Marsaglia (1972)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersphere#CITEREFMarsaglia1972) gives the following algorithm.

Generate an ⁠![Image 301: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-dimensional vector of [normal deviates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normal_distribution "Normal distribution") (it suffices to use ⁠![Image 302: {\displaystyle N(0,1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/763663a6c2dbaa4cf12b99c5710e01ff2504f193)⁠, although in fact the choice of the variance is arbitrary), ⁠![Image 303: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} =(x_{1},x_{2},\ldots ,x_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae4a713bd817434a854daeb28d6aa8909c05be6c)⁠. Now calculate the "radius" of this point:

![Image 304: {\displaystyle r={\sqrt {x_{1}^{2}+x_{2}^{2}+\cdots +x_{n}^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5077e70adee62701df03c2d070bb150c1ce1a2a8)
The vector ⁠![Image 305: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {1}{r}}\mathbf {x} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/691d3a9f69bea19ad018673d0f1284c90c895c2c)⁠ is uniformly distributed over the surface of the unit ⁠![Image 306: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-ball.

An alternative given by Marsaglia is to uniformly randomly select a point ⁠![Image 307: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} =(x_{1},x_{2},\ldots ,x_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae4a713bd817434a854daeb28d6aa8909c05be6c)⁠ in the unit [n-cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube "Hypercube") by sampling each ⁠![Image 308: {\displaystyle x_{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e87000dd6142b81d041896a30fe58f0c3acb2158)⁠ independently from the [uniform distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_uniform_distribution "Continuous uniform distribution") over ⁠![Image 309: {\displaystyle (-1,1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e120a3bd60fc89b495dd7ef6039465b7e6a703b1)⁠, computing ⁠![Image 310: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538)⁠ as above, and rejecting the point and resampling if ⁠![Image 311: {\displaystyle r\geq 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b1e87b13257cf2fecf01cadc7ad9ca50c6fedfd)⁠ (i.e., if the point is not in the ⁠![Image 312: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-ball), and when a point in the ball is obtained scaling it up to the spherical surface by the factor ⁠![Image 313: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {1}{r}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dc177c75043de5867200268f273b23f2796162fa)⁠; then again ⁠![Image 314: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {1}{r}}\mathbf {x} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/691d3a9f69bea19ad018673d0f1284c90c895c2c)⁠ is uniformly distributed over the surface of the unit ⁠![Image 315: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-ball. This method becomes very inefficient for higher dimensions, as a vanishingly small fraction of the [unit cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_cube "Unit cube") is contained in the sphere. In ten dimensions, less than 2% of the cube is filled by the sphere, so that typically more than 50 attempts will be needed. In seventy dimensions, less than ![Image 316: {\displaystyle 10^{-24}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c1efba49dcc13f3842a3e4ef5c0db5ff0369cb36) of the cube is filled, meaning typically a trillion quadrillion trials will be needed, far more than a computer could ever carry out.

### Uniformly at random within the _n_-ball

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=N-sphere&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Uniformly at random within the n-ball")]

With a point selected uniformly at random from the surface of the unit ⁠![Image 317: {\displaystyle (n-1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df88c6333caaf6471cf277f24b802ff9931b133e)⁠-sphere (e.g., by using Marsaglia's algorithm), one needs only a radius to obtain a point uniformly at random from within the unit ⁠![Image 318: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-ball. If ⁠![Image 319: {\displaystyle u}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3e6bb763d22c20916ed4f0bb6bd49d7470cffd8)⁠ is a number generated uniformly at random from the interval ⁠![Image 320: {\displaystyle [0,1]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/738f7d23bb2d9642bab520020873cccbef49768d)⁠ and ⁠![Image 321: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32adf004df5eb0a8c7fd8c0b6b7405183c5a5ef2)⁠ is a point selected uniformly at random from the unit ⁠![Image 322: {\displaystyle (n-1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df88c6333caaf6471cf277f24b802ff9931b133e)⁠-sphere, then ⁠![Image 323: {\displaystyle u^{1/n}\mathbf {x} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/045e428779407dcbcb9b23909d526e40ce6c0318)⁠ is uniformly distributed within the unit ⁠![Image 324: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-ball.

Alternatively, points may be sampled uniformly from within the unit ⁠![Image 325: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-ball by a reduction from the unit ⁠![Image 326: {\displaystyle (n+1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b30a29cfd35628469f9dbffea4804f5b422f3037)⁠-sphere. In particular, if ⁠![Image 327: {\displaystyle (x_{1},x_{2},\ldots ,x_{n+2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b895526e0bd79ba442a6aac26d3bfa120c5f3b3f)⁠ is a point selected uniformly from the unit ⁠![Image 328: {\displaystyle (n+1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b30a29cfd35628469f9dbffea4804f5b422f3037)⁠-sphere, then ⁠![Image 329: {\displaystyle (x_{1},x_{2},\ldots ,x_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47d8b3ec62633086002489cad8df4214e9585880)⁠ is uniformly distributed within the unit ⁠![Image 330: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-ball (i.e., by simply discarding two coordinates).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersphere#cite_note-6)

If ⁠![Image 331: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠ is sufficiently large, most of the volume of the ⁠![Image 332: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-ball will be contained in the region very close to its surface, so a point selected from that volume will also probably be close to the surface. This is one of the phenomena leading to the so-called [curse of dimensionality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curse_of_dimensionality "Curse of dimensionality") that arises in some numerical and other applications.

The **octahedral ⁠![Image 360: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-sphere** is defined similarly to the ⁠![Image 361: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-sphere but using the [1-norm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1_norm "1 norm")

![Image 362: {\displaystyle S^{n}=\left\{x\in \mathbb {R} ^{n+1}:\left\|x\right\|_{1}=1\right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/153ac3d6bb2560736a878152259b51e819b45699)
In general, it takes the shape of a [cross-polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-polytope "Cross-polytope").

The octahedral ⁠![Image 363: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf)⁠-sphere is a square (without its interior). The octahedral ⁠![Image 364: {\displaystyle 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/901fc910c19990d0dbaaefe4726ceb1a4e217a0f)⁠-sphere is a regular [octahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octahedron "Octahedron"); hence the name. The octahedral ⁠![Image 365: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)⁠-sphere is the [topological join](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_join "Topological join") of ⁠![Image 366: {\displaystyle n+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a135e65a42f2d73cccbfc4569523996ca0036f1)⁠ pairs of isolated points.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersphere#cite_note-:7-10) Intuitively, the topological join of two pairs is generated by drawing a segment between each point in one pair and each point in the other pair; this yields a square. To join this with a third pair, draw a segment between each point on the square and each point in the third pair; this gives a octahedron.

*   [Conformal geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformal_geometry "Conformal geometry")– Study of angle-preserving transformations of a geometric space
*   [Exotic sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exotic_sphere "Exotic sphere")– Smooth manifold that is homeomorphic but not diffeomorphic to a sphere
*   [Homology sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homology_sphere "Homology sphere")– Topological manifold whose homology coincides with that of a sphere
*   [Homotopy groups of spheres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy_groups_of_spheres "Homotopy groups of spheres")– How spheres of various dimensions can wrap around each other
*   [Inversive geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inversive_geometry "Inversive geometry")– Study of angle-preserving transformations
*   [Möbius transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6bius_transformation "Möbius transformation")– Rational function of the form (az + b)/(cz + d)
