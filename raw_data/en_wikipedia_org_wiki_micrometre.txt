The **micrometre** ([Commonwealth English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/English_in_the_Commonwealth_of_Nations "English in the Commonwealth of Nations")) or **micrometer** ([American English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_English "American English")) ([SI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI "SI") symbol: **μm**) is a unit of [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length "Length") in the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") (SI) equalling 10−6[metre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre "Metre") (SI standard prefix "[micro-](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micro- "Micro-")" = 10−6); that is, one millionth of a [metre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre "Metre") (or one thousandth of a [millimetre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millimetre "Millimetre"), 0.001 mm, or about 0.000 04[inch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inch "Inch")).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre#cite_note-Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Online-1)

The nearest smaller common [SI unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_Unit "SI Unit") is the [nanometre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanometre "Nanometre"), equivalent to one thousandth of a micrometre, one millionth of a millimetre or one billionth of a metre (0.000 000 001 m).

The micrometre is a common unit of [measurement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measurement "Measurement") for [wavelengths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wavelength "Wavelength") of [infrared radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared_radiation "Infrared radiation") as well as sizes of biological [cells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cell_(biology) "Cell (biology)") and [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria "Bacteria"),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre#cite_note-Encyclop%C3%A6dia_Britannica_Online-1) and for grading [wool](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wool "Wool") by the diameter of the fibres.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre#cite_note-NSW_Department_of_Education_and_Communities-2) The width of a single [human hair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_hair "Human hair") ranges from approximately 20 to 200 μm.

How big is 1 micrometre?

Between 1 μm and 10 μm:

*   1–10 μm – length of a typical [bacterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacterium "Bacterium")
*   3–8 μm – width of strand of [spider web silk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spider_silk "Spider silk")[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre#cite_note-3)
*   5 μm – length of a typical human [spermatozoon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spermatozoon "Spermatozoon")'s head[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre#cite_note-4)
*   6–8 μm – diameter of a typical [red blood cell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_blood_cell "Red blood cell")[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre#cite_note-5)
*   10 μm – size of [fungal hyphae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypha "Hypha")
*   about 10 μm – size of a [fog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fog "Fog"), [mist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mist "Mist"), or [cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cloud "Cloud") water droplet

Between 10 μm and 100 μm:

*   about 10–12 μm – thickness of [plastic wrap (cling wrap)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plastic_wrap "Plastic wrap")
*   10 to 55 μm – width of [wool](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wool "Wool") fibre[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre#cite_note-ISTAG-6)
*   17 to 181 μm – diameter of human hair[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre#cite_note-Physics_Factbook-7)
*   70 to 180 μm – thickness of paper

The term _micron_ and [the symbol μ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mu_(letter) "Mu (letter)") were officially accepted for use in isolation to denote the micrometre in 1879, but officially revoked by the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") (SI) in 1967.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre#cite_note-BIPM-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre#cite_note-9) This became necessary because the older usage was incompatible with the official adoption of the unit prefix _micro-_, denoted μ, during the creation of the SI in 1960.

In the SI, the systematic name _micrometre_ became the official name of the unit, and μm became the official unit symbol.

In [American English](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_English "American English"), the use of _micron_ may help to differentiate the unit from the [micrometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometer_(device) "Micrometer (device)"), a measuring device, because the unit's name in [American spelling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_and_British_English_spelling_differences#-re.2C_-er "American and British English spelling differences") is a [homograph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homograph "Homograph") of the device's name. In spoken English, they are distinguished by pronunciation, as the name of the measuring device is stressed on the second syllable ( [_my-KROM-it-ər_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key")), whereas the unit name places the stress on the first syllable ( [_MY-kroh-meet-ər_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key")).[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre#cite_note-10)

The plural of _micron_ is normally _microns_, though _micra_ was occasionally used before 1950.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre#cite_note-12)

The official symbol for the [SI prefix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_prefix "Metric prefix")_micro-_ is a [Greek lowercase mu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mu_(letter) "Mu (letter)").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre#cite_note-13)[Unicode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode "Unicode") has inherited U+00B5 µ MICRO SIGN from [ISO/IEC 8859-1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISO/IEC_8859-1 "ISO/IEC 8859-1"), distinct from the [code point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Code_point "Code point")U+03BC μ GREEK SMALL LETTER MU. According to the [Unicode Consortium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode_Consortium "Unicode Consortium"), the Greek letter character is preferred,[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre#cite_note-14) but implementations must recognize the micro sign as well for [compatibility with legacy character sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode_compatibility_characters "Unicode compatibility characters"). Most fonts use the same [glyph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glyph#Typography "Glyph") for the two [characters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Character_(computing) "Character (computing)").

Before [desktop publishing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desktop_publishing "Desktop publishing") became commonplace, it was customary to render the symbol μ in texts produced with mechanical [typewriters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Typewriter "Typewriter") by combining a slightly lowered slash with the letter u. For example, "15 μm" would appear as "15/um". This gave rise in early [word processing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Word_processing "Word processing") to substituting just the letter u for the symbol if the Greek letter μ was not available, as in "15 um".[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Micrometre#cite_note-15)

The Unicode [CJK Compatibility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CJK_Compatibility "CJK Compatibility") block contains square forms of some Japanese [katakana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Katakana "Katakana") measure and currency units. U+3348㍈SQUARE MIKURON corresponds to ミクロン _mikuron_.
