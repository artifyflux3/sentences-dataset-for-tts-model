In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **4-manifold** is a 4-dimensional [topological manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_manifold "Topological manifold"). A **smooth 4-manifold** is a 4-manifold with a [smooth structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_structure "Smooth structure"). In dimension four, in marked contrast with lower dimensions, topological and smooth manifolds are quite different. There exist some topological 4-manifolds which admit no smooth structure, and even if there exists a smooth structure, it need not be unique (i.e. there are smooth 4-manifolds which are [homeomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphic "Homeomorphic") but not [diffeomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffeomorphic "Diffeomorphic")).

4-manifolds are important in physics because in [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") is modeled as a [pseudo-Riemannian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian "Pseudo-Riemannian") 4-manifold.

Topological 4-manifolds
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=4-manifold&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Topological 4-manifolds")]

The [homotopy type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homotopy_type "Homotopy type") of a [simply connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simply_connected "Simply connected") compact 4-manifold only depends on the [intersection form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intersection_form_(4-manifold) "Intersection form (4-manifold)") on the middle dimensional homology. A famous theorem of [Michael Freedman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Freedman "Michael Freedman")([1982](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-manifold#CITEREFFreedman1982)) implies that the [homeomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphism "Homeomorphism") type of the manifold only depends on this intersection form, and on a ![Image 1: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} /2\mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b3bb21abe942aa9c0c63bae35a0c38905e1712c) invariant called the [Kirby–Siebenmann invariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kirby%E2%80%93Siebenmann_invariant "Kirby–Siebenmann invariant"), and moreover that every combination of [unimodular form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unimodular_lattice "Unimodular lattice") and Kirby–Siebenmann invariant can arise, except that if the form is even, then the Kirby–Siebenmann invariant must be the signature/8 (mod 2).

Examples:

*   In the special case when the form is 0, this implies the 4-dimensional topological [Poincaré conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_conjecture "Poincaré conjecture").
*   If the form is the [E8 lattice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E8_lattice "E8 lattice"), this gives a manifold called the [E8 manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E8_manifold "E8 manifold"), a manifold not homeomorphic to any [simplicial complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplicial_complex "Simplicial complex").
*   If the form is ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc), there are two manifolds depending on the Kirby–Siebenmann invariant: one is 2-dimensional complex projective space, and the other is a fake projective space, with the same homotopy type but not homeomorphic (and with no smooth structure).
*   When the rank of the form is greater than about 28, [the number of positive definite unimodular forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unimodular_lattice#Classification "Unimodular lattice") starts to increase extremely rapidly with the rank, so there are huge numbers of corresponding simply connected topological 4-manifolds (most of which seem to be of almost no interest).

Freedman's classification can be extended to some cases when the fundamental group is not too complicated; for example, when it is ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc), there is a classification similar to the one above using Hermitian forms over the group ring of ![Image 4: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc). If the fundamental group is too large (for example, a free group on 2 generators), then Freedman's techniques seem to fail and very little is known about such manifolds.

For any [finitely presented group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finitely_presented_group "Finitely presented group") it is easy to construct a (smooth) compact 4-manifold with it as its fundamental group.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-manifold#cite_note-1) (More specifically, for any finitely presented group, one constructs a manifold with the given fundamental group, such that two manifolds in this family are homeomorphic if and only if the fundamental groups are isomorphic.) As there [can be no algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Undecidable_problem "Undecidable problem") to tell whether two finitely presented groups are isomorphic (even if one is known to be trivial), there can be no algorithm to tell if two 4-manifolds have the same fundamental group. This is one reason why much of the work on 4-manifolds just considers the simply connected case: the general case of many problems is already known to be intractable.

For manifolds of dimension at most 6, any [piecewise linear (PL) structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piecewise_linear_structure "Piecewise linear structure") can be smoothed in an essentially unique way,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-manifold#cite_note-2) so in particular the theory of 4 dimensional [PL manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PL_manifold "PL manifold") is much the same as the theory of 4 dimensional smooth manifolds.

A major open problem in the theory of smooth 4-manifolds is to classify the simply connected compact ones. As the topological ones are known, this breaks up into two parts:

1.   Which topological manifolds are smoothable?
2.   Classify the different smooth structures on a smoothable manifold.

There is an almost complete answer to the first problem asking which simply connected compact 4-manifolds have smooth structures. First, the [Kirby–Siebenmann class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kirby%E2%80%93Siebenmann_class "Kirby–Siebenmann class") must vanish.

*   If the intersection form is definite [Donaldson's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Donaldson%27s_theorem "Donaldson's theorem") ([Donaldson 1983](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-manifold#CITEREFDonaldson1983)) gives a complete answer: there is a smooth structure if and only if the form is diagonalizable.
*   If the form is indefinite and odd there is a smooth structure.
*   If the form is indefinite and even we may as well assume that it is of nonpositive signature by changing orientations if necessary, in which case it is isomorphic to a sum of _m_ copies of II 1,1 and 2 _n_ copies of E 8(−1) for some _m_ and _n_. If _m_ ≥ 3 _n_ (so that the dimension is at least 11/8 times the |signature|) then there is a smooth structure, given by taking a connected sum of _n_[K3 surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K3_surface "K3 surface") and _m_−3 _n_ copies of _S_ 2×_S_ 2. If _m_ ≤ 2 _n_ (so the dimension is at most 10/8 times the |signature|) then Furuta proved that no smooth structure exists ([Furuta 2001](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-manifold#CITEREFFuruta2001)). This leaves a small gap between 10/8 and 11/8 where the answer is mostly unknown. (The smallest case not covered above has _n_=2 and _m_=5, but this has also been ruled out, so the smallest lattice for which the answer is not currently known is the lattice II 7,55 of rank 62 with _n_=3 and _m_=7. See [[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-manifold#cite_note-3) for recent (as of 2019) progress in this area.) The "11/8 conjecture" states that smooth structures do not exist if the dimension is less than 11/8 times the |signature|.

In contrast, very little is known about the second question of classifying the smooth structures on a smoothable 4-manifold; in fact, there is not a single smoothable 4-manifold where the answer is fully known. Donaldson showed that there are some simply connected compact 4-manifolds, such as [Dolgachev surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dolgachev_surface "Dolgachev surface"), with a countably infinite number of different smooth structures. There are an uncountable number of different smooth structures on **R**4; see [exotic **R**4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exotic_R4 "Exotic R4"). Fintushel and Stern showed how to use surgery to construct large numbers of different smooth structures (indexed by arbitrary integral polynomials) on many different manifolds, using [Seiberg–Witten invariants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seiberg%E2%80%93Witten_invariant "Seiberg–Witten invariant") to show that the smooth structures are different. Their results suggest that any classification of simply connected smooth 4-manifolds will be very complicated. There are currently no plausible conjectures about what this classification might look like. (Some early conjectures that all simply connected smooth 4-manifolds might be connected sums of algebraic surfaces, or [symplectic manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symplectic_manifold "Symplectic manifold"), possibly with orientations reversed, have been disproved.)

Special phenomena in 4 dimensions
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=4-manifold&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Special phenomena in 4 dimensions")]

There are several fundamental theorems about manifolds that can be proved by low-dimensional methods in dimensions at most 3, and by completely different high-dimensional methods in dimension at least 5, but which are false in dimension 4. Here are some examples:

*   In dimensions other than 4, the [Kirby–Siebenmann invariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kirby%E2%80%93Siebenmann_invariant "Kirby–Siebenmann invariant") provides the obstruction to the existence of a PL structure; in other words a compact topological manifold has a PL structure if and only if its Kirby–Siebenmann invariant in H 4(_M_,**Z**/2**Z**) vanishes. In dimension 3 and lower, every topological manifold admits an essentially unique PL structure. In dimension 4 there are many examples with vanishing Kirby–Siebenmann invariant but no PL structure.
*   In any dimension other than 4, a compact topological manifold has only a finite number of essentially distinct PL or smooth structures. In dimension 4, compact manifolds can have a countably-infinite number of non-diffeomorphic smooth structures.
*   Four is the only dimension _n_ for which **R**_n_ can have an exotic smooth structure. **R**4 has an uncountable number of exotic smooth structures; see [exotic **R**4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exotic_R4 "Exotic R4").
*   The solution to the smooth [Poincaré conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_conjecture "Poincaré conjecture") is known in all dimensions other than 4 (it is usually false in dimensions at least 7; see [exotic sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exotic_sphere "Exotic sphere")). The Poincaré conjecture for [PL manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PL_manifold "PL manifold") has been proved for all dimensions other than 4. In 4 dimensions, the PL Poincaré conjecture is equivalent to the smooth Poincaré conjecture, and its truth is unknown.
*   The smooth [h-cobordism theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H-cobordism_theorem "H-cobordism theorem") holds for cobordisms provided that neither the cobordism nor its boundary has dimension 4. It can fail if the boundary of the cobordism has dimension 4 (as shown by [Donaldson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simon_Donaldson "Simon Donaldson")).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-manifold#cite_note-4) If the cobordism has dimension 4, then it is unknown whether the h-cobordism theorem holds.
*   A topological manifold of dimension not equal to 4 has a [handlebody](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Handlebody "Handlebody") decomposition. Manifolds of dimension 4 have a handlebody decomposition if and only if they are smoothable.
*   There are compact 4-dimensional topological manifolds that are not homeomorphic to any [simplicial complex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplicial_complex "Simplicial complex"). [Ciprian Manolescu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ciprian_Manolescu "Ciprian Manolescu") showed that there are topological manifolds in each dimension greater than or equal to 5, that are not homeomorphic to a simplicial complex.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-manifold#cite_note-5)

Failure of the Whitney trick in dimension 4
-------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=4-manifold&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Failure of the Whitney trick in dimension 4")]

According to [Frank Quinn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frank_Quinn_(mathematician) "Frank Quinn (mathematician)"), "Two _n_-dimensional submanifolds of a manifold of dimension 2 _n_ will usually intersect themselves and each other in isolated points. The ["Whitney trick"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whitney_embedding_theorem#A_little_about_the_proof "Whitney embedding theorem") uses an isotopy across an embedded 2-disk to simplify these intersections. Roughly speaking this reduces the study of _n_-dimensional embeddings to embeddings of 2-disks. But this is not a reduction when the dimension is 4: the 2-disks themselves are middle-dimensional, so trying to embed them encounters exactly the same problems they are supposed to solve. This is the phenomenon that separates dimension 4 from others."[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-manifold#cite_note-6)

Geometrization in dimension four
--------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=4-manifold&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Geometrization in dimension four")]

The [uniformization theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniformization_theorem "Uniformization theorem") for two-dimensional [surfaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surface_(topology) "Surface (topology)"), which states that every [simply connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simply_connected_space "Simply connected space")[Riemann surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_surface "Riemann surface") can be given one of three geometries ([Euclidean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry"), [spherical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_geometry "Spherical geometry"), or [hyperbolic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry "Hyperbolic geometry")). In dimension 3 it is not always possible to assign a geometry to a closed [3-manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/3-manifold "3-manifold") but the resolution of the [Geometrization conjecture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrization_conjecture "Geometrization conjecture"), proposed by [William Thurston](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Thurston "William Thurston")([1982](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-manifold#CITEREFThurston1982)), implies that closed 3-manifolds can be decomposed into geometric _pieces_.

Each of these pieces can have one of 8 possible geometries: spherical ![Image 5: {\displaystyle S^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01e57c690f890937838c10ba57853ff21bf30ec8), Euclidean ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \mathbb {E} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b1f81bfef66c67c01e157738c3bd11dc82a98be0), hyperbolic ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \mathbf {H} _{\mathbb {R} }^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/829db812d0791a942ab6d09d32be5d15e0a9318c), Nil geometry ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Nil} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a555b4f58ef04c45d1208d09bed46267f87b854a), Sol geometry ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Sol} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f0b4c023da061474bce6bddad8619e731fe478b), ![Image 10: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {\mathrm {SL} _{2}(\mathbb {R} )}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e0b9fc7fa5e850bd3c551a55a90f0149c59d486), and the products ![Image 11: {\displaystyle S^{2}\times \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b5c1893f59344438acdda1bdca99488e3da75e1), and ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mathbf {H} _{\mathbb {R} }^{2}\times \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ed53d94d72435e219fc5791683f48fdaea4ae68).

In dimension four the situation is more complicated. Not every closed 4-manifold can be uniformized by a [Lie group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_group "Lie group") or even decomposed into geometrizable pieces. This follows from unsolvability of the homeomorphism problem for 4-manifolds.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-manifold#cite_note-7) But, there is still a classification of 4-dimensional geometries due to Richard Filipkiewicz.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-manifold#cite_note-Proof4Dim-8) These fall into 18 distinct geometries and one infinite family. An in depth discussion of the geometries and the manifolds that afford them is given in Hillman's book.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-manifold#cite_note-HillmanBook-9) The study of complex structures on geometrizable 4-manifolds was initiated by Wall [[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-manifold#cite_note-Wall1985-10)

The Four Dimensional Geometries
-------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=4-manifold&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: The Four Dimensional Geometries")]

The distinction in to the following classes is somewhat arbitrary, the emphasis has been placed on properties of the fundamental group and the uniformizing Lie group. The classification of the geometries is taken from.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-manifold#cite_note-Proof4Dim-8) The descriptions of the fundamental groups as well as further information on the 4-manifolds that afford them can be found in Hillman's book[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-manifold#cite_note-HillmanBook-9)

### Spherical or compact type

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=4-manifold&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Spherical or compact type")]

Three geometries lie here, the [4-sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-sphere "4-sphere")![Image 13: {\displaystyle S^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bcd117989212318d27e78c5d7052a37e520172cf), the [complex projective plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_projective_plane "Complex projective plane")![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathbf {P} _{\mathbb {C} }^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1844e0b8b894df564093df17c48ad69e83165e6f), and a product of two 2-spheres ![Image 15: {\displaystyle S^{2}\times S^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5eaa22c32235aa8292a6ae67cac14a5293851bd2). The fundamental group of any such manifold is finite.

This is the [four dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four_dimensional_space "Four dimensional space")[Euclidean space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_space "Euclidean space")![Image 16: {\displaystyle \mathbb {E} ^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e02756196353582bfdbb6b37fec4fb23e6996d5). With isometry group ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{4}\rtimes \mathrm {O} (4)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/37498ea920cf28d354f75501a931cc8f3be4ae9e). The fundamental group of any such manifold is a [Bieberbach group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bieberbach_group "Bieberbach group"). There are 74 homeomorphism classes of manifolds with geometry ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mathbb {E} ^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e02756196353582bfdbb6b37fec4fb23e6996d5), 27 [orientable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientable "Orientable") manifolds and 47 non-orientable manifolds.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-manifold#cite_note-4dimspacegroups-11)

There are two geometries of Nilpotent type ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Nil} ^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad9b92cfe38acbfd17f5530eb08259c8c4a873c6) and the reducible geometry ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Nil} ^{3}\times \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43bdec64bb4b72a5ec9385c82edda01205fa440f).

The ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Nil} ^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad9b92cfe38acbfd17f5530eb08259c8c4a873c6) geometry is a 4-dimensional nilpotent Lie group given as the [semi-direct product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semi-direct_product "Semi-direct product")![Image 22: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}\rtimes _{\Theta }\mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/039a4c3ae8596ace05ab4c13c1244d8287f0a4dd), where ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \Theta (t)=\mathrm {diag} [t,t,{\frac {1}{2}}t^{2}]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/77b1d43e7680f57ed75f8e303c945435a48523b5). The fundamental group of a closed orientable ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Nil} ^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad9b92cfe38acbfd17f5530eb08259c8c4a873c6)-manifold is [nilpotent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nilpotent "Nilpotent") of class 3.

For a closed 4-manifold ![Image 25: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) admitting a ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Nil} ^{3}\times \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43bdec64bb4b72a5ec9385c82edda01205fa440f) geometry, there is a finite cover ![Image 27: {\displaystyle M'}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a3b2ef3304c46b5e7859eec0b1bc057c8eb3f75) of ![Image 28: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) such that ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \pi _{1}M\cong \Gamma \times \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88f5de711ee7094351cee1b026b78f8de24b88b6). Here ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cfde86a3f7ec967af9955d0988592f0693d2b19) is the fundamental group of a [3-dimensional nilmanifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometrization_conjecture#Nil_geometry "Geometrization conjecture"). Thus, every such fundamental group is [nilpotent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nilpotent "Nilpotent") of class 2.

Note that one can always take ![Image 31: {\displaystyle \Gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4cfde86a3f7ec967af9955d0988592f0693d2b19) above to be one of the following groups ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \Gamma _{q}:=\langle x,y,z\ |\ xz=zx,\ zy=yz,\ xy=z^{q}yx\rangle }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ce756bfd9a824199508a3efd379b8010f7e4dce1), where ![Image 33: {\displaystyle q\in \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a8e290ee12a8d7b11dd1644982af99fde01c2d9d) is non-zero. These are all fundamental groups of torus bundles over the circle.

There are two unique geometries ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Sol} _{0}^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/275e5c4c379f2edb010ba7cc06af3ba8a5e5e522), and ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Sol} _{1}^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a6562c374d1085e5f2c4e0416a4d2b338a03162a). As well as a countably infinite family ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Sol} _{m,n}^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51b384764b40b0fe2608d7b135b8d6702d1e8ede) where ![Image 37: {\displaystyle m,n\geq 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1c2afda4ea90cb16e2644b6e187e84e5188b4ac4) are integers.

The ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Sol} _{0}^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/275e5c4c379f2edb010ba7cc06af3ba8a5e5e522)-geometry is the Lie group described by the semi-direct product ![Image 39: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}\rtimes _{\xi }\mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3dcbfc10957b013694a2e9f9642758b1b06694a5), where ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \xi (t)=\mathrm {diag} [e^{t},e^{t},e^{-2t}]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b0690fc00807d859296ab1a18e415697f6160036). The fundamental group of a closed ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Sol} _{0}^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/275e5c4c379f2edb010ba7cc06af3ba8a5e5e522)-manifold is a semidirect product ![Image 42: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} ^{3}\rtimes _{A}\mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad42439e8c0e8b774a71f32e37e1843d03749c4f) where ![Image 43: {\displaystyle A\in \mathrm {GL} _{3}(\mathbb {Z} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/646f617ed5d1b7d3cd40e1f6ef18b1222dc3694b) has one real [eigenvalue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvalue "Eigenvalue") and two conjugate complex eigenvalues. The fundamental group has [Hirsh length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycyclic_group#Hirsch_length "Polycyclic group") equal to 4.

The ![Image 44: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Sol} _{1}^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a6562c374d1085e5f2c4e0416a4d2b338a03162a)-geometry is the Lie group described by set of matrices ![Image 45: {\displaystyle \left\{\left[{\begin{array}{ccc}1&x&z\\0&t&y\\0&0&1\end{array}}\right]\ |\ x,y,z,t\in \mathbb {R} ,\ t>0\right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/671d63578aebcc58abc3be2158a50c966f6e4e89).

A closed ![Image 46: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Sol} _{1}^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a6562c374d1085e5f2c4e0416a4d2b338a03162a)-manifold ![Image 47: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is a mapping torus of a ![Image 48: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Nil} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a555b4f58ef04c45d1208d09bed46267f87b854a)-manifold. Its fundamental group is a semidirect product ![Image 49: {\displaystyle \Gamma _{q}\rtimes \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eee815837d391fbd672624f368ccaef142933850). The fundamental group has [Hirsh length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycyclic_group#Hirsch_length "Polycyclic group") equal to 4.

Define ![Image 50: {\displaystyle f(x)=x^{3}-mx^{2}+nx-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c324f59604ca29b858bca1c8f974c0eb04c2e95f). If ![Image 51: {\displaystyle m,n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6568e95b6bf8f39b7fd2c9b52b7b00ee124c6250) are positive integers such that ![Image 52: {\displaystyle 0<2{\sqrt {n}}\leq m<n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad4f0dec9c39ac77f9cfaa797398ce5f02f2c0c6), then ![Image 53: {\displaystyle f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/202945cce41ecebb6f643f31d119c514bec7a074) has three distinct real roots ![Image 54: {\displaystyle a,b,c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f13f068df656c1b1911ae9f81628c49a6181194d).

The ![Image 55: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Sol} _{m,n}^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51b384764b40b0fe2608d7b135b8d6702d1e8ede)-geometry is the Lie group described by the semi-direct product ![Image 56: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}\rtimes _{\Phi _{m,n}}\mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/edd92571f6b435b6c14094a136d5ed932fcffd25), where ![Image 57: {\displaystyle \Theta _{m,n}(t)=\mathrm {diag} [e^{at},e^{bt},e^{ct}]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6c73622d253aa3d5db91fca4eb331afce0f13238). The fundamental group of a closed ![Image 58: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Sol} _{m,n}^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51b384764b40b0fe2608d7b135b8d6702d1e8ede)-manifold is a semidirect product ![Image 59: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} ^{3}\rtimes _{A}\mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad42439e8c0e8b774a71f32e37e1843d03749c4f) where ![Image 60: {\displaystyle A\in \mathrm {GL} _{3}(\mathbb {Z} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/646f617ed5d1b7d3cd40e1f6ef18b1222dc3694b) has three distinct real eigenvalues. The fundamental group has [Hirsh length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polycyclic_group#Hirsch_length "Polycyclic group") equal to 4.

#### Isomorphisms between solvable geometries

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=4-manifold&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Isomorphisms between solvable geometries")]

Note that when ![Image 61: {\displaystyle n=m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/480d6131c6cb07a90f4ec18a376a59fab884b860) that ![Image 62: {\displaystyle \Theta _{n,n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32bd0010fca9fa7b9dc85996d895204900008894) has exactly one eigenvalue.

So there is an identification ![Image 63: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Sol} _{n,n}^{4}=\mathbf {Sol^{3}} \times \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9fcce9e449466106efb30f0f5a2358b1600cbe8).

We have that ![Image 64: {\displaystyle \mathbf {Sol} _{m,n}^{4}=\mathbf {Sol} _{m',n'}^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d38e6d1a51e2daeab5aa76bf5192d7ebe45bee5e) if the roots ![Image 65: {\displaystyle (a,b,c)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae973a762a92b9cd3eafe7f283890ccfa9b887e8) and ![Image 66: {\displaystyle (a',b',c')}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/78abc32e48bcc1ab16120f669f878a4478c9df98) satisfy ![Image 67: {\displaystyle \lambda (a,b,c)=(a',b',c')}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92b7d09219a2f02cda193750c989f10b1a83ec6a) for some real number ![Image 68: {\displaystyle \lambda }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b43d0ea3c9c025af1be9128e62a18fa74bedda2a).

A proof of these facts appears in.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-manifold#cite_note-MaWang2022-12)

There are two geometries here [real-hyperbolic 4-space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_space "Hyperbolic space")![Image 69: {\displaystyle \mathbf {H} _{\mathbb {R} }^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89bd762e8aa6b4f5c8b6dbd34c741a280edac8b7) and the [complex hyperbolic plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_hyperbolic_space "Complex hyperbolic space")![Image 70: {\displaystyle \mathbf {H} _{\mathbb {C} }^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9b7b9960070b002941e89fd2f6331754201432e6). The fundamental groups of closed manifolds here are [word hyperbolic groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_group "Hyperbolic group").

### Product of hyperbolic planes

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=4-manifold&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Product of hyperbolic planes")]

This is the geometry ![Image 71: {\displaystyle \mathbf {H} _{\mathbb {R} }^{2}\times \mathbf {H} _{\mathbb {R} }^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27c66e4cf0b0f8a291e8fdda6192ad92b0a9a9d9). Closed manifolds come in two forms here. A ![Image 72: {\displaystyle \mathbf {H} _{\mathbb {R} }^{2}\times \mathbf {H} _{\mathbb {R} }^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27c66e4cf0b0f8a291e8fdda6192ad92b0a9a9d9)-manifold is _reducible_ if it is finitely covered by a direct product of hyperbolic Riemann surfaces. Otherwise it is _irreducible_. The irreducible manifolds fundamental groups are [arithmetic groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Arithmetic_groups&action=edit&redlink=1 "Arithmetic groups (page does not exist)") by Margulis' arithmeticity theorem.

### The tangent space of the hyperbolic plane

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=4-manifold&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: The tangent space of the hyperbolic plane")]

This geometry admits no closed manifolds.

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=4-manifold&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Remaining geometries")]

A product of two 2-dimensional geometries ![Image 73: {\displaystyle S^{2}\times \mathbb {E} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e12316edb5832e34951e3ddcc674cc6e417311f2) and ![Image 74: {\displaystyle S^{2}\times \mathbf {H} _{\mathbb {R} }^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f30c6d518f9983d3f9e693646ce73a89a0800a2f).

A product of a 3-dimensional geometry with ![Image 75: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc). These are ![Image 76: {\displaystyle S^{3}\times \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/13d53dd24afc73066988d5622044309ab5ec8350), ![Image 77: {\displaystyle \mathbf {H} _{\mathbb {R} }^{3}\times \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/319ddf9ee5e4b20ae513dbb4ba9cc12d9e1ac267), and ![Image 78: {\displaystyle {\widetilde {\mathrm {SL} _{2}(\mathbb {R} )}}\times \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6cbefa39e22196c6289776561ccf00556f3df158).
