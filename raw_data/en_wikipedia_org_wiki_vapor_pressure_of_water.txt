The **vapor pressure of water** is the pressure exerted by molecules of water vapor in gaseous form (whether pure or in a mixture with other gases such as air). The **saturation vapor pressure** is the pressure at which [water vapor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_vapor "Water vapor") is [in thermodynamic equilibrium with its condensed state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturated_vapor "Saturated vapor"). At pressures higher than saturation vapor pressure, [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Properties_of_water "Properties of water") will [condense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condense "Condense"), while at lower pressures it will [evaporate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evaporate "Evaporate") or [sublimate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition) "Sublimation (phase transition)"). The saturation vapor pressure of water increases with increasing [temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Temperature "Temperature") and can be determined with the [Clausius–Clapeyron relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clausius%E2%80%93Clapeyron_relation "Clausius–Clapeyron relation"). The [boiling point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boiling_point "Boiling point") of water is the temperature at which the saturated vapor pressure equals the ambient pressure. Water [supercooled](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supercooling "Supercooling") below its normal freezing point has a higher vapor pressure than that of ice at the same temperature and is, thus, unstable.

Calculations of the (saturation) vapor pressure of water are commonly used in [meteorology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meteorology "Meteorology"). The temperature-vapor pressure relation inversely describes the relation between the [boiling point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boiling_point "Boiling point") of water and the pressure. This is relevant to both [pressure cooking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure_cooking "Pressure cooking") and cooking at high altitudes. An understanding of vapor pressure is also relevant in explaining high altitude [breathing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breathing "Breathing") and [cavitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cavitation "Cavitation").

A more detailed discussion of accuracy and considerations of the inaccuracy in temperature measurements is presented in Alduchov and Eskridge (1996). The analysis here shows the simple unattributed formula and the Antoine equation are reasonably accurate at 100°C, but quite poor for lower temperatures above freezing. [Tetens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetens_equation "Tetens equation") is much more accurate over the range from 0 to 50°C and very competitive at 75°C, but Antoine's is superior at 75°C and above. The unattributed formula must have zero error at around 26°C, but is of very poor accuracy outside a narrow range. Tetens' equations are generally much more accurate and arguably more straightforward for use at everyday temperatures (e.g., in meteorology). As expected,[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_][Buck's equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arden_Buck_equation "Arden Buck equation") for T> 0°C is significantly more accurate than Tetens, and its superiority increases markedly above 50°C, though it is more complicated to use. The Buck equation is even superior to the more complex [Goff-Gratch equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goff%E2%80%93Gratch_equation "Goff–Gratch equation") over the range needed for practical meteorology.

Numerical approximations
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Vapour_pressure_of_water&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Numerical approximations")]

For serious computation, Lowe (1977)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vapor_pressure_of_water#cite_note-Lowe1977-4) developed two pairs of equations for temperatures above and below freezing, with different levels of accuracy. They are all very accurate (compared to [Clausius-Clapeyron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clausius%E2%80%93Clapeyron_relation "Clausius–Clapeyron relation") and the [Goff-Gratch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goff%E2%80%93Gratch_equation "Goff–Gratch equation")) but use nested polynomials for very efficient computation. However, there are more recent reviews of possibly superior formulations, notably Wexler (1976, 1977),[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vapor_pressure_of_water#cite_note-Wexler1976-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vapor_pressure_of_water#cite_note-Wexler1977-6) reported by Flatau et al. (1992).[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vapor_pressure_of_water#cite_note-Flatau1992-7)

Examples of modern use of these formulae can additionally be found in NASA's GISS Model-E and Seinfeld and Pandis (2006). The former is an extremely simple Antoine equation, while the latter is a polynomial.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vapor_pressure_of_water#cite_note-8)

In 2018 a new physics-inspired approximation formula was devised and tested by Huang [[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vapor_pressure_of_water#cite_note-9) who also reviews other recent attempts.
