From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

This article assumes an understanding of the [tensor product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product "Tensor product") of [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") without chosen [bases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)"). An introduction to the nature and significance of tensors in a broad context can be found in the main [Tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") article.

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the modern [component-free](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Component-free "Component-free") approach to the theory of a **tensor** views a tensor as an [abstract object](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_object "Abstract object"), expressing some definite type of [multilinear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilinear_map "Multilinear map") concept. Their properties can be derived from their definitions, as [linear maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map "Linear map") or more generally; and the rules for manipulations of tensors arise as an extension of [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra") to [multilinear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilinear_algebra "Multilinear algebra").

In [differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), an intrinsic[_[definition needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] geometric statement may be described by a [tensor field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field "Tensor field") on a [manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold"), and then doesn't need to make reference to coordinates at all. The same is true in [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), of tensor fields describing a [physical property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_property "Physical property"). The component-free approach is also used extensively in [abstract algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abstract_algebra "Abstract algebra") and [homological algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homological_algebra "Homological algebra"), where tensors arise naturally.

Definition via tensor products of vector spaces
-----------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tensor_(intrinsic_definition)&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Definition via tensor products of vector spaces")]

Given a finite set {_V_ 1, ..., _V_ _n_} of [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") over a common [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)")F, one may form their [tensor product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product#Tensor_product_of_vector_spaces "Tensor product")_V_ 1 ⊗ ... ⊗ _V_ _n_, an element of which is termed a **tensor**.

A **tensor on the vector space**V is then defined to be an element of (i.e., a vector in) a vector space of the form: ![Image 1: {\displaystyle V\otimes \cdots \otimes V\otimes V^{*}\otimes \cdots \otimes V^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b51cd6d35926dedc4a0bf248185a6dfed38aaacd) where V∗ is the [dual space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_space "Dual space") of V.

If there are m copies of V and n copies of V∗ in our product, the tensor is said to be of **type (m, n)** and [contravariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariance_and_contravariance_of_vectors "Covariance and contravariance of vectors") of order m and covariant of order n and of total [order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_order "Tensor order")_m_ + _n_. The tensors of order zero are just the scalars (elements of the field F), those of contravariant order 1 are the vectors in V, and those of covariant order 1 are the [one-forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_functional "Linear functional") in V∗ (for this reason, the elements of the last two spaces are often called the contravariant and covariant vectors). The space of all tensors of type (_m_, _n_) is denoted ![Image 2: {\displaystyle T_{n}^{m}(V)=\underbrace {V\otimes \dots \otimes V} _{m}\otimes \underbrace {V^{*}\otimes \dots \otimes V^{*}} _{n}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07553040cc4afb575a00bb92325783a829217e01)

**Example 1.** The space of type (1, 1) tensors, ![Image 3: {\displaystyle T_{1}^{1}(V)=V\otimes V^{*},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ab53da96edaf8a10caad213f58b320a5e8d1551) is [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphic "Isomorphic") in a natural way to the space of [linear transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_transformations "Linear transformations") from V to V.

**Example 2.** A [bilinear form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_form "Bilinear form") on a real vector space V, ![Image 4: {\displaystyle V\times V\to F,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0fcaa8add09fa55ce781b9ef93efba288e73fb1b) corresponds in a natural way to a type (0, 2) tensor in ![Image 5: {\displaystyle T_{2}^{0}(V)=V^{*}\otimes V^{*}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/235dfea7c3d698efd6e99eee3b362d292e71a83b) An example of such a bilinear form may be defined,[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] termed the associated _[metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric\_tensor "Metric tensor")_, and is usually denoted g.

A **simple tensor** (also called a tensor of rank one, elementary tensor or decomposable tensor[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_(intrinsic_definition)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHackbusch20124-1)) is a tensor that can be written as a product of tensors of the form ![Image 6: {\displaystyle T=a\otimes b\otimes \cdots \otimes d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/106eecbeda8f779ed6df3a0ca45ef758ed96d37b) where _a_, _b_, ..., _d_ are nonzero and in V or V∗ – that is, if the tensor is nonzero and completely [factorizable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Factorization "Factorization"). Every tensor can be expressed as a sum of simple tensors. The **rank of a tensor**T is the minimum number of simple tensors that sum to T.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_(intrinsic_definition)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBourbaki1989II,_%C2%A77,_no._8-2)

The [zero tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zero_tensor "Zero tensor") has rank zero. A nonzero order 0 or 1 tensor always has rank 1. The rank of a non-zero order 2 or higher tensor is less than or equal to the product of the dimensions of all but the highest-dimensioned vectors in (a sum of products of) which the tensor can be expressed, which is _d_ _n_−1 when each product is of n vectors from a finite-dimensional vector space of dimension d.

The term _rank of a tensor_ extends the notion of the [rank of a matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rank_of_a_matrix "Rank of a matrix") in linear algebra, although the term is also often used to mean the order (or degree) of a tensor. The rank of a matrix is the minimum number of column vectors needed to span the [range of the matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Row_and_column_spaces "Row and column spaces"). A matrix thus has rank one if it can be written as an [outer product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_product "Outer product") of two nonzero vectors: ![Image 7: {\displaystyle A=vw^{\mathrm {T} }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf81d9e6bb91e5103d74ca52db61d1140b1cc087)

The rank of a matrix A is the smallest number of such outer products that can be summed to produce it: ![Image 8: {\displaystyle A=v_{1}w_{1}^{\mathrm {T} }+\cdots +v_{k}w_{k}^{\mathrm {T} }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7eee41b46c699e682d371d4d31d49135a76a2179)

In indices, a tensor of rank 1 is a tensor of the form ![Image 9: {\displaystyle T_{ij\dots }^{k\ell \dots }=a_{i}b_{j}\cdots c^{k}d^{\ell }\cdots .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/366755f09114966c0e725e712911cb5f59e1eaa4)

The rank of a tensor of order 2 agrees with the rank when the tensor is regarded as a [matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)"),[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_(intrinsic_definition)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHalmos1974%C2%A751-3) and can be determined from [Gaussian elimination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_elimination "Gaussian elimination") for instance. The rank of an order 3 or higher tensor is however often _very difficult_ to determine, and low rank decompositions of tensors are sometimes of great practical interest.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_(intrinsic_definition)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEde_Groote1987-4) In fact, the problem of finding the rank of an order 3 tensor over any [finite field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_field "Finite field") is [NP-Complete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NP-completeness "NP-completeness"), and over the rationals, is [NP-Hard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NP-hardness "NP-hardness").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_(intrinsic_definition)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEH%C3%A5stad1989-5) Computational tasks such as the efficient multiplication of matrices and the efficient evaluation of [polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial") can be recast as the problem of simultaneously evaluating a set of [bilinear forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bilinear_form "Bilinear form")![Image 10: {\displaystyle z_{k}=\sum _{ij}T_{ijk}x_{i}y_{j}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a5d0dffc09cc9ce119421e756085a00412f910ce) for given inputs x i and y j. If a low-rank decomposition of the tensor T is known, then an efficient [evaluation strategy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evaluation_strategy "Evaluation strategy") is known.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_(intrinsic_definition)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKnuth1998506%E2%80%93508-6)

The space ![Image 11: {\displaystyle T_{n}^{m}(V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a3e73852557ba4e649afcc93926c277347ce65c) can be characterized by a [universal property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_property "Universal property") in terms of [multilinear mappings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multilinear_map "Multilinear map"). Amongst the advantages of this approach are that it gives a way to show that many linear mappings are "natural" or "geometric" (in other words are independent of any choice of basis). Explicit computational information can then be written down using bases, and this order of priorities can be more convenient than proving a formula gives rise to a natural mapping. Another aspect is that tensor products are not used only for [free modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_module "Free module"), and the "universal" approach carries over more easily to more general situations.

A scalar-valued function on a [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product") (or [direct sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_sum_of_modules "Direct sum of modules")) of vector spaces ![Image 12: {\displaystyle f:V_{1}\times \cdots \times V_{N}\to F}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7561b67ae472921a607b9650ea720c7035f47c48) is multilinear if it is linear in each argument. The space of all multilinear mappings from _V_ 1 × ... × _V N_ to W is denoted _L N_(_V_ 1, ..., _V N_;_W_). When _N_ = 1, a multilinear mapping is just an ordinary linear mapping, and the space of all linear mappings from V to W is denoted _L_(_V_; _W_).

The [universal characterization of the tensor product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product#Universal_property "Tensor product") implies that, for each multilinear function ![Image 13: {\displaystyle f\in L^{m+n}(\underbrace {V^{*},\ldots ,V^{*}} _{m},\underbrace {V,\ldots ,V} _{n};W)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4159965927ec9f6494ee4c31ef2dd31bdaa293cb) (where W can represent the field of scalars, a vector space, or a tensor space) there exists a unique linear function ![Image 14: {\displaystyle T_{f}\in L(\underbrace {V^{*}\otimes \cdots \otimes V^{*}} _{m}\otimes \underbrace {V\otimes \cdots \otimes V} _{n};W)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/685ba1d9958e3bf4fe9c130f3414d44aa60497b7) such that ![Image 15: {\displaystyle f(\alpha _{1},\ldots ,\alpha _{m},v_{1},\ldots ,v_{n})=T_{f}(\alpha _{1}\otimes \cdots \otimes \alpha _{m}\otimes v_{1}\otimes \cdots \otimes v_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f48ae9b9729d4b44bac7f4b1b0e04aefbe6a016b) for all v i in V and α i in V∗.

Using the universal property, it follows, when V is [finite dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(vector_space) "Dimension (vector space)"), that the space of (_m_, _n_)-tensors admits a [natural isomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_isomorphism "Natural isomorphism")![Image 16: {\displaystyle T_{n}^{m}(V)\cong L(\underbrace {V^{*}\otimes \cdots \otimes V^{*}} _{m}\otimes \underbrace {V\otimes \cdots \otimes V} _{n};F)\cong L^{m+n}(\underbrace {V^{*},\ldots ,V^{*}} _{m},\underbrace {V,\ldots ,V} _{n};F).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83ecf5e0f9fae3b8391b5fe7c0bea37ab4be6135)

Each V in the definition of the tensor corresponds to a V∗ inside the argument of the linear maps, and vice versa. (Note that in the former case, there are m copies of V and n copies of V∗, and in the latter case vice versa). In particular, one has ![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}T_{0}^{1}(V)&\cong L(V^{*};F)\cong V,\\T_{1}^{0}(V)&\cong L(V;F)=V^{*},\\T_{1}^{1}(V)&\cong L(V;V).\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/98b8d334976ded27ed1c7381a2af74b602388503)

[Differential geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_geometry "Differential geometry"), [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") and [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering") must often deal with [tensor fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field "Tensor field") on [smooth manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_manifold "Smooth manifold"). The term _tensor_ is sometimes used as a shorthand for _tensor field_. A tensor field expresses the concept of a tensor that varies from point to point on the manifold.
