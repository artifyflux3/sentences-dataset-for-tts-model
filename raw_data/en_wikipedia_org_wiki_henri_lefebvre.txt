**Henri Lefebvre** ( [_lə-FEV-rə_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key"); French:[[ɑ̃ʁi ləfɛvʁ]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/French "Help:IPA/French"); 16 June 1901 – 29 June 1991) was a French [Marxist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marxist "Marxist")[philosopher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosopher "Philosopher") and [sociologist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sociology "Sociology"), best known for furthering the critique of [everyday life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Everyday_life "Everyday life"), for introducing the concepts of the [right to the city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_to_the_city "Right to the city") and the production of [social space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_space "Social space"), and for his work on [dialectical materialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dialectical_materialism "Dialectical materialism"), [alienation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marx%27s_theory_of_alienation "Marx's theory of alienation"), and criticism of [Stalinism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stalinism "Stalinism"), [existentialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Existentialism "Existentialism"), and [structuralism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structuralism "Structuralism"). In his prolific career, Lefebvre wrote more than sixty books and three hundred articles.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre#cite_note-3) He founded or took part in the founding of several intellectual and academic journals such as _Philosophies_, _La Revue Marxiste_, _[Arguments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arguments\_Group "Arguments Group")_, _Socialisme ou Barbarie_, and _Espaces et Sociétés_.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre#cite_note-4)

Lefebvre was born in [Hagetmau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hagetmau "Hagetmau"), [Landes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landes_(department) "Landes (department)"), France. He studied philosophy at the [University of Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Paris "University of Paris") (the Sorbonne), graduating in 1920. By 1924 he was working with [Paul Nizan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Nizan "Paul Nizan"), [Norbert Guterman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Norbert_Guterman "Norbert Guterman"), [Georges Friedmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georges_Friedmann "Georges Friedmann"), [Georges Politzer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georges_Politzer "Georges Politzer"), and [Pierre Morhange](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pierre_Morhange&action=edit&redlink=1 "Pierre Morhange (page does not exist)") in the _Philosophies_ group seeking a "philosophical revolution".[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre#cite_note-5) This brought them into contact with the [Surrealists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surrealism "Surrealism"), [Dadaists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dada "Dada"), and other groups, before they moved towards the [French Communist Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Communist_Party "French Communist Party") (PCF).

Lefebvre joined the PCF in 1928 and became one of the most prominent French Marxist intellectuals during the second quarter of the 20th century, before joining the [French resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_resistance "French resistance").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre#cite_note-6) From 1944 to 1949, he was the director of _[Radiodiffusion Française](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiodiffusion\_Fran%C3%A7aise "Radiodiffusion Française")_, a French radio broadcaster in [Toulouse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toulouse "Toulouse"). Among his works was a highly influential, anti-Stalinist text on dialectics called _Dialectical Materialism_ (1940). Seven years later, Lefebvre published his first volume of _The Critique of Everyday Life_.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre#cite_note-7) His early work on method was applauded and borrowed centrally by the philosopher [Jean-Paul Sartre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Paul_Sartre "Jean-Paul Sartre") in _[Critique of Dialectical Reason](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critique\_of\_Dialectical\_Reason "Critique of Dialectical Reason")_ (1960). During Lefebvre's thirty-year stint with the PCF, he was chosen to publish critical attacks on opposed theorists, especially existentialists like Sartre and Lefebvre's former colleague Nizan,[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre#cite_note-8) only to intentionally get himself expelled from the party for his own heterodox theoretical and political opinions in the late 1950s. He then went from serving as a primary intellectual for the PCF to becoming one of France's most important critics of the PCF's politics (e.g. immediately, the lack of an opinion on Algeria, and more generally, the partial apologism for and continuation of [Stalinism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stalinism "Stalinism")) and intellectual thought (i.e. [structuralism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structuralism "Structuralism"), especially the work of [Louis Althusser](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis_Althusser "Louis Althusser")).[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre#cite_note-9)

In 1961, Lefebvre became professor of sociology at the [University of Strasbourg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Strasbourg "University of Strasbourg"), before joining the faculty at the new university at [Nanterre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Paris_X:_Nanterre "University of Paris X: Nanterre") in 1965.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre#cite_note-10) He was one of the most respected professors, and he had influenced and analysed the [May 1968 student revolt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/May_1968_events_in_France "May 1968 events in France").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre#cite_note-11) Lefebvre introduced the concept of the [right to the city](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_to_the_city "Right to the city") in his 1968 book _Le Droit à la ville_[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre#cite_note-13) (the publication of the book predates the May 1968 revolts which took place in many French cities). Following the publication of this book, Lefebvre wrote several influential works on cities, urbanism, and space, including _The Production of Space_ (1974), which became one of the most influential and heavily cited works of [urban theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urban_theory "Urban theory"). By the 1970s, Lefebvre had also published some of the first critical statements on the work of [post-structuralists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Post-structuralists "Post-structuralists"), especially [Michel Foucault](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michel_Foucault "Michel Foucault").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre#cite_note-14) During the following years he was involved in the editorial group of _Arguments_, a [New Left](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Left "New Left") magazine which largely served to enable the French public to familiarize themselves with Central European revisionism.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre#cite_note-15)

Lefebvre died in 1991. In his obituary, _[Radical Philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radical\_Philosophy "Radical Philosophy")_ magazine honored his long and complex career and influence:

> the most prolific of French Marxist intellectuals, died during the night of 28–29 June 1991, less than a fortnight after his ninetieth birthday. During his long career, his work has gone in and out of fashion several times, and has influenced the development not only of philosophy but also of sociology, geography, political science and literary criticism.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre#cite_note-16)

Critique of everyday life
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Henri_Lefebvre&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Critique of everyday life")]

One of Lefebvre's most important contributions to social thought is the idea of the "critique of everyday life", which he pioneered in the 1930s. Lefebvre defined everyday life dialectically as the intersection of "illusion and truth, power and helplessness; the intersection of the sector man controls and the sector he does not control",[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre#cite_note-17) and is where the perpetually transformative conflict occurs between diverse, specific rhythms: the body's polyrhythmic bundles of natural rhythms, physiological (natural) rhythms, and social rhythms (Lefebvre and Régulier, 1985: 73).[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre#cite_note-18) The everyday was, in short, the space in which all life occurred, and between which all fragmented activities took place. It was the residual.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre#cite_note-19) While the theme presented itself in many works, it was most notably outlined in his eponymous three-volume study, which came out in individual installments, decades apart, in 1947, 1961, and 1981.

Lefebvre argued that everyday life was an underdeveloped sector compared to technology and production, and moreover that in the mid 20th century, capitalism changed such that everyday life was to be colonized. In this zone of everydayness (boredom) shared by everyone in society regardless of class or specialty, autocritique of everyday realities of boredom vs. societal promises of free time and leisure, could lead to people understanding and then revolutionizing their everyday lives. This was essential to Lefebvre because everyday life was where he saw capitalism surviving and reproducing itself. Without revolutionizing everyday life, capitalism would continue to diminish the quality of everyday life, and inhibit real self-expression. The critique of everyday life was crucial because it was for him only through the development of the conditions of human life—rather than abstract control of productive forces—that humans could reach a concrete utopian existence.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre#cite_note-20)

Lefebvre's work on everyday life was heavily influential in French theory, particularly for the [Situationists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Situationists "Situationists"), as well as in politics (e.g. for the May 1968 student revolts).[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre#cite_note-21) The third volume has also recently influenced scholars writing about digital technology and information in the present day,[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre#cite_note-22) since it has a section dealing with this topic at length, including analysis of the [Nora-Minc Report](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Nora-Minc_Report&action=edit&redlink=1 "Nora-Minc Report (page does not exist)")[[fr](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rapport_Nora-Minc "fr:Rapport Nora-Minc")] (1977); key aspects of [information theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_theory "Information theory"); and other general discussion of the "colonisation" of everyday life through information communication technologies as "devices" or "services".

Lefebvre dedicated a great deal of his philosophical writings to understanding the importance of (the production of) space in what he called the reproduction of social relations of production. This idea is the central argument in the book _The Survival of Capitalism_, written as a sort of prelude to _La Production de l'espace_ (1974) (_The Production of Space_).

Lefebvre contends that there are different modes of production of space (i.e. [spatialization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spatialization "Spatialization")) from natural space ('absolute space') to more complex spaces and flows whose meaning is produced in a social way (i.e. social space).[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre#cite_note-23) Lefebvre analyzes each historical mode as a three-part dialectic between everyday practices and perceptions (_le perçu_), representations or theories of space (_le conçu_) and the spatial [imaginary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Imaginary_(psychoanalysis)#French_culture "The Imaginary (psychoanalysis)") of the time (_le vécu_).[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre#cite_note-24)

Lefebvre's argument in _The Production of Space_ is that space is a social product, or a complex social construction (based on values, and the social production of meanings) which affects spatial practices and perceptions. Lefebvre argued that every society—and, therefore, every mode of production—produces a certain space, its own space.

Lefebvre's concept has been criticised: e.g. in _The Urban Question_, [Manuel Castells](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manuel_Castells "Manuel Castells"). Many responses to Castells are provided in _The Survival of Capitalism_, and some such as [Andy Merrifield](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andy_Merrifield "Andy Merrifield")[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre#cite_note-25) argue that the acceptance of those critiques in the academic world would be a motive for Lefebvre's effort in writing the long and theoretically dense _The Production of Space_. In "Actually-Existing Success: Economics, Aesthetics, and the Specificity of (Still-)Socialist Urbanism," [Michal Murawski](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Michal_Murawski&action=edit&redlink=1 "Michal Murawski (page does not exist)") critiques Lefebvre's dismissal of actually existing socialism by showing how socialist states produced differential space.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henri_Lefebvre#cite_note-26)
