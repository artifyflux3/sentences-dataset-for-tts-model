Parabolic coordinates 3D

**Paraboloidal coordinates** are three-dimensional [orthogonal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates "Orthogonal coordinates")![Image 2: {\displaystyle (\mu ,\nu ,\lambda )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/890e98332421a2f5e7d3a51fe6e51e6fb8b5127e) that generalize two-dimensional [parabolic coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabolic_coordinates "Parabolic coordinates"). They possess elliptic [paraboloids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paraboloids "Paraboloids") as one-coordinate surfaces. As such, they should be distinguished from [parabolic cylindrical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabolic_cylindrical_coordinates "Parabolic cylindrical coordinates") and [parabolic rotational coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parabolic_coordinates#Three-dimensional_parabolic_coordinates "Parabolic coordinates"), both of which are also generalizations of two-dimensional parabolic coordinates. The coordinate surfaces of the former are parabolic cylinders, and the coordinate surfaces of the latter are _circular_ paraboloids.

Differently from cylindrical and rotational parabolic coordinates, but similarly to the related [ellipsoidal coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ellipsoidal_coordinates "Ellipsoidal coordinates"), the coordinate surfaces of the paraboloidal coordinate system are _not_ produced by rotating or projecting any two-dimensional orthogonal coordinate system.

Differential operators
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Paraboloidal_coordinates&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Differential operators")]

Common differential operators can be expressed in the coordinates ![Image 20: {\displaystyle (\mu ,\nu ,\lambda )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/890e98332421a2f5e7d3a51fe6e51e6fb8b5127e) by substituting the scale factors into the [general formulas for these operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal_coordinates#Differential_operators_in_three_dimensions "Orthogonal coordinates"), which are applicable to any three-dimensional orthogonal coordinates. For instance, the [gradient operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient_operator "Gradient operator") is

![Image 21: {\displaystyle \nabla =\left[{\frac {\left(\mu -b\right)\left(\mu -c\right)}{\left(\mu -\nu \right)\left(\mu -\lambda \right)}}\right]^{1/2}\mathbf {e} _{\mu }{\frac {\partial }{\partial \mu }}+\left[{\frac {\left(b-\nu \right)\left(c-\nu \right)}{\left(\mu -\nu \right)\left(\lambda -\nu \right)}}\right]^{1/2}\mathbf {e} _{\nu }{\frac {\partial }{\partial \nu }}+\left[{\frac {\left(b-\lambda \right)\left(\lambda -c\right)}{\left(\lambda -\nu \right)\left(\mu -\lambda \right)}}\right]^{1/2}\mathbf {e} _{\lambda }{\frac {\partial }{\partial \lambda }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec6410a2adddb953e04da4b3aff0ccfbbc1f13ff)
and the [Laplacian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplacian "Laplacian") is

![Image 22: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\nabla ^{2}=&\left[{\frac {\left(\mu -b\right)\left(\mu -c\right)}{\left(\mu -\nu \right)\left(\mu -\lambda \right)}}\right]^{1/2}{\frac {\partial }{\partial \mu }}\left[(\mu -b)^{1/2}(\mu -c)^{1/2}{\frac {\partial }{\partial \mu }}\right]\\&+\left[{\frac {\left(b-\nu \right)\left(c-\nu \right)}{\left(\mu -\nu \right)\left(\lambda -\nu \right)}}\right]^{1/2}{\frac {\partial }{\partial \nu }}\left[(b-\nu )^{1/2}(c-\nu )^{1/2}{\frac {\partial }{\partial \nu }}\right]\\&+\left[{\frac {\left(b-\lambda \right)\left(\lambda -c\right)}{\left(\lambda -\nu \right)\left(\mu -\lambda \right)}}\right]^{1/2}{\frac {\partial }{\partial \lambda }}\left[(b-\lambda )^{1/2}(\lambda -c)^{1/2}{\frac {\partial }{\partial \lambda }}\right]\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f95e090fef3dd36493eaa9d354a25b85a8159228)
Paraboloidal coordinates can be useful for solving certain [partial differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_differential_equations "Partial differential equations"). For instance, the [Laplace equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace_equation "Laplace equation") and [Helmholtz equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Helmholtz_equation "Helmholtz equation") are both [separable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separable_partial_differential_equation "Separable partial differential equation") in paraboloidal coordinates. Hence, the coordinates can be used to solve these equations in geometries with paraboloidal symmetry, i.e. with boundary conditions specified on sections of paraboloids.

The Helmholtz equation is ![Image 23: {\displaystyle (\nabla ^{2}+k^{2})\psi =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/774c7a5068d7e2108dc24eb9715189fa369c583b). Taking ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \psi =M(\mu )N(\nu )\Lambda (\lambda )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e10d5e0e07464ff7e3ff98ec9fffe9e0b36a3bd), the separated equations are[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paraboloidal_coordinates#cite_note-3)

![Image 25: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}&(\mu -b)(\mu -c){\frac {d^{2}M}{d\mu ^{2}}}+{\frac {1}{2}}\left[2\mu -(b+c)\right]{\frac {dM}{d\mu }}+\left[k^{2}\mu ^{2}+\alpha _{3}\mu -\alpha _{2}\right]M=0\\&(b-\nu )(c-\nu ){\frac {d^{2}N}{d\nu ^{2}}}+{\frac {1}{2}}\left[2\nu -(b+c)\right]{\frac {dN}{d\nu }}+\left[k^{2}\nu ^{2}+\alpha _{3}\nu -\alpha _{2}\right]N=0\\&(b-\lambda )(\lambda -c){\frac {d^{2}\Lambda }{d\lambda ^{2}}}-{\frac {1}{2}}\left[2\lambda -(b+c)\right]{\frac {d\Lambda }{d\lambda }}-\left[k^{2}\lambda ^{2}+\alpha _{3}\lambda -\alpha _{2}\right]\Lambda =0\\\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e3b8c185588e8144f23a48b138a5673c4ccbb85b)
where ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \alpha _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aef5f08a56f51deb324e5eed2fb9b2e3c279889d) and ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \alpha _{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1bd642e6f0898fc7483aae007fc14a61ea5f7f3b) are the two separation constants. Similarly, the separated equations for the Laplace equation can be obtained by setting ![Image 28: {\displaystyle k=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6307c8a99dad7d0bcb712352ae0a748bd99a038b) in the above.

Each of the separated equations can be cast in the form of the [Baer equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baer_equation "Baer equation"). Direct solution of the equations is difficult, however, in part because the separation constants ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \alpha _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aef5f08a56f51deb324e5eed2fb9b2e3c279889d) and ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \alpha _{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1bd642e6f0898fc7483aae007fc14a61ea5f7f3b) appear simultaneously in all three equations.

Following the above approach, paraboloidal coordinates have been used to solve for the [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field") surrounding a [conducting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_conductor "Electrical conductor") paraboloid.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paraboloidal_coordinates#cite_note-Duggen2012-4)
