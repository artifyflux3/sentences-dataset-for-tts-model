**Jean-Baptiste le Rond d'Alembert**[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-1) ( [_DAL-əm-BAIR_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key");[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-2)French:[[ʒɑ̃batist lə ʁɔ̃dalɑ̃bɛʁ]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/French "Help:IPA/French"); 16 November 1717 – 29 October 1783) was a French mathematician, [mechanician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanics "Mechanics"), [physicist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physicist "Physicist"), philosopher, and [music theorist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Music_theorist "Music theorist"). Until 1759 he was, together with [Denis Diderot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denis_Diderot "Denis Diderot"), a co-editor of the _[Encyclopédie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Encyclop%C3%A9die "Encyclopédie")_.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-3)[D'Alembert's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%27Alembert%27s_formula "D'Alembert's formula") for obtaining solutions to the [wave equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_equation "Wave equation") is named after him.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-4)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-5)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-6) The wave equation is sometimes referred to as [d'Alembert's equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%27Alembert%27s_equation "D'Alembert's equation"), and the [fundamental theorem of algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_algebra "Fundamental theorem of algebra") is named after d'Alembert in French.

Born in Paris, d'Alembert was the [natural son](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illegitimate_child "Illegitimate child") of the writer [Claudine Guérin de Tencin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claudine_Gu%C3%A9rin_de_Tencin "Claudine Guérin de Tencin") and the chevalier [Louis-Camus Destouches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louis-Camus_Destouches "Louis-Camus Destouches"), an [artillery](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artillery "Artillery") officer. Destouches was abroad at the time of d'Alembert's birth. Days after birth his mother left him on the steps of the [Saint-Jean-le-Rond de Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Church_of_Saint-Jean-le-Rond,_Paris "Church of Saint-Jean-le-Rond, Paris") church. According to custom, he was named after the [patron saint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patron_saint "Patron saint") of the church. D'Alembert was placed in an [orphanage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orphanage "Orphanage") for [foundling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Child_abandonment "Child abandonment") children, but his father found him and placed him with the wife of a [glazier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glazier "Glazier"), Madame Rousseau, with whom he lived for nearly 50 years.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-7) She gave him little encouragement. When he told her of some discovery he had made or something he had written she generally replied,

> You will never be anything but a philosopher—and what is that but an ass who plagues himself all his life, that he may be talked about after he is dead.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-8)

Destouches secretly paid for the education of Jean le Rond, but did not want his [paternity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paternity_(law) "Paternity (law)") officially recognised.

Studies and adult life
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Jean_le_Rond_d%27Alembert&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Studies and adult life")]

D'Alembert first attended a private school. The chevalier Destouches left d'Alembert an [annuity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annuity_(financial_contracts) "Annuity (financial contracts)") of 1,200 [livres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_livre "French livre") on his death in 1726. Under the influence of the Destouches family, at the age of 12 d'Alembert entered the [Jansenist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jansenism "Jansenism")[Collège des Quatre-Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coll%C3%A8ge_des_Quatre-Nations "Collège des Quatre-Nations") (the institution was also known under the name "Collège Mazarin"). Here he studied philosophy, law, and the arts, graduating as _[baccalauréat en arts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bachelor\_of\_Arts "Bachelor of Arts")_ in 1735.

In his later life, d'Alembert scorned the [Cartesian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes "René Descartes") principles he had been taught by the [Jansenists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jansenism "Jansenism"): "physical promotion, innate ideas and the vortices". The Jansenists steered d'Alembert toward an [ecclesiastical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecclesiastical "Ecclesiastical") career, attempting to deter him from pursuits such as poetry and [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"). Theology was, however, "rather unsubstantial fodder" for d'Alembert. He entered law school for two years, and was nominated _[avocat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lawyer "Lawyer")_ in 1738.

He was also interested in medicine and mathematics. Jean enrolled first as Jean-Baptiste Daremberg and subsequently changed his name, perhaps for reasons of euphony, to d’Alembert.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-9)

Later, in recognition of d'Alembert's achievements, [Frederick the Great of Prussia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frederick_the_Great "Frederick the Great") proposed the name "d'Alembert" for a suspected (but non-existent) moon of Venus, however d'Alembert refused the honor.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-10)

Front page of a 1758 copy of _Traité de dynamique_

In July 1739 he made his first contribution to the field of mathematics, pointing out the errors he had detected in _[Analyse démontrée](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Analyse\_d%C3%A9montr%C3%A9e&action=edit&redlink=1 "Analyse démontrée (page does not exist)")_ (published 1708 by [Charles-René Reynaud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles-Ren%C3%A9_Reynaud "Charles-René Reynaud")) in a communication addressed to the [Académie des Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Academy_of_Sciences "French Academy of Sciences"). At the time _L'analyse démontrée_ was a standard work, which d'Alembert himself had used to study the [foundations of mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Foundations_of_mathematics "Foundations of mathematics"). D'Alembert was also a [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin") scholar of some note and worked in the latter part of his life on a translation of [Tacitus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tacitus "Tacitus"), for which he received wide praise including that of [Denis Diderot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Denis_Diderot "Denis Diderot").

In 1740, he submitted his second [scientific](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science "Science") work from the field of [fluid mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_dynamics "Fluid dynamics")_Mémoire sur la réfraction des corps solides_, which was recognised by [Clairaut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexis_Clairault "Alexis Clairault"). In this work d'Alembert theoretically explained [refraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Refraction "Refraction").

In 1741, after several failed attempts, d'Alembert was elected into the Académie des Sciences. He was later elected to the [Berlin Academy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prussian_Academy_of_Sciences "Prussian Academy of Sciences") in 1746[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-11) and a [Fellow of the Royal Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fellow_of_the_Royal_Society "Fellow of the Royal Society") in 1748.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-12)

In 1743, he published his most famous work, _Traité de dynamique_, in which he developed his own [laws of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%27Alembert%27s_principle "D'Alembert's principle").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-13)

When the _Encyclopédie_ was organised in the late 1740s, d'Alembert was engaged as co-editor (for mathematics and science) with Diderot, and served until a series of crises temporarily interrupted the publication in 1757. He authored over a thousand articles for it, including the famous _[Preliminary Discourse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Preliminary\_Discourse "Preliminary Discourse")_. D'Alembert "abandoned the foundation of [Materialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Materialism "Materialism")"[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-lange-14) when he "doubted whether there exists outside us anything corresponding to what we suppose we see."[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-lange-14) In this way, d'Alembert agreed with the [Idealist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idealism "Idealism")[Berkeley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Berkeley "George Berkeley") and anticipated the [transcendental idealism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcendental_idealism "Transcendental idealism") of [Kant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immanuel_Kant "Immanuel Kant").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

In 1752, he wrote about what is now called [D'Alembert's paradox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%27Alembert%27s_paradox "D'Alembert's paradox"): that the [drag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drag_(physics) "Drag (physics)") on a body immersed in an [inviscid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscosity "Viscosity"), [incompressible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incompressible_flow "Incompressible flow")[fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid "Fluid") is zero.

In 1754, d'Alembert was elected a member of the [Académie des sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acad%C3%A9mie_des_sciences "Académie des sciences"), of which he became Permanent Secretary on 9 April 1772.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-15)

In 1757, an article by d'Alembert in the seventh volume of the _Encyclopedia_ suggested that the Geneva clergymen had moved from [Calvinism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calvinism "Calvinism") to pure [Socinianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socinianism "Socinianism"), basing this on information provided by [Voltaire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voltaire "Voltaire"). The Pastors of Geneva were indignant, and appointed a committee to answer these charges. Under pressure from [Jacob Vernes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacob_Vernes "Jacob Vernes"), [Jean-Jacques Rousseau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Rousseau "Jean-Jacques Rousseau") and others, d'Alembert eventually made the excuse that he considered anyone who did not accept the Church of Rome to be a Socinianist, and that was all he meant, and he abstained from further work on the encyclopaedia following his response to the critique.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-16)

He was elected a Foreign Honorary Member of the [American Academy of Arts and Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Academy_of_Arts_and_Sciences "American Academy of Arts and Sciences") in 1781.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-AAAS-17)

D'Alembert's first exposure to music theory was in 1749 when he was called upon to review a _Mémoire_ submitted to the Académie by [Jean-Philippe Rameau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Philippe_Rameau "Jean-Philippe Rameau"). This article, written in conjunction with [Diderot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diderot "Diderot"), would later form the basis of Rameau's 1750 treatise _Démonstration du principe de l'harmonie_. D'Alembert wrote a glowing review praising the author's deductive character as an ideal scientific model. He saw in Rameau's music theories support for his own scientific ideas, a fully systematic method with a strongly deductive synthetic structure.

Two years later, in 1752, d'Alembert attempted a fully comprehensive survey of Rameau's works in his _Eléments de musique théorique et pratique suivant les principes de M. Rameau_.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-CT-18) Emphasizing Rameau's main claim that music was a mathematical science that had a single principle from which could be deduced all the elements and rules of musical practice as well as the explicit Cartesian methodology employed, d'Alembert helped to popularise the work of the composer and advertise his own theories.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-CT-18) He claims to have "clarified, developed, and simplified" the principles of Rameau, arguing that the single idea of the _[corps sonore](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Corps\_sonore&action=edit&redlink=1 "Corps sonore (page does not exist)")[[fr](https://fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/corps\_sonore "fr:corps sonore")]_ was not sufficient to derive the entirety of music.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-19) D'Alembert instead claimed that three principles would be necessary to generate the major [musical mode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Musical_mode "Musical mode"), the minor mode, and the identity of [octaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octaves "Octaves"). Because he was not a musician, however, d'Alembert misconstrued the finer points of Rameau's thinking, changing and removing concepts that would not fit neatly into his understanding of music.

Although initially grateful, Rameau eventually turned on d'Alembert while voicing his increasing dissatisfaction with [J. J. Rousseau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J._J._Rousseau "J. J. Rousseau")'s _Encyclopédie_ articles on music.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-20) This led to a series of bitter exchanges between the men and contributed to the end of d'Alembert and Rameau's friendship. A long preliminary discourse d'Alembert wrote for the 1762 edition of his _Elémens_ attempted to summarise the dispute and act as a final rebuttal.

D'Alembert also discussed various aspects of the state of music in his celebrated _Discours préliminaire_ of [Diderot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diderot "Diderot")'s _Encyclopédie_. D'Alembert claims that, compared to the other arts, music, "which speaks simultaneously to the imagination and the senses," has not been able to represent or imitate as much of reality because of the "lack of sufficient inventiveness and resourcefulness of those who cultivate it."[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-21) He wanted musical expression to deal with all physical sensations rather than merely the passions alone. D'Alembert believed that modern ([Baroque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baroque "Baroque")) music had only achieved perfection in his age, as there existed no classical [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greeks "Greeks") models to study and imitate. He claimed that "time destroyed all models which the ancients may have left us in this genre."[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-22) He praises Rameau as "that manly, courageous, and fruitful genius" who picked up the slack left by [Jean-Baptiste Lully](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean-Baptiste_Lully "Jean-Baptiste Lully") in the French musical arts.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-23)

Portrait of Jean Le Rond d'Alembert, 1777, by [Catherine Lusurier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catherine_Lusurier "Catherine Lusurier").

D'Alembert was a participant in several Parisian [salons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salon_(gathering) "Salon (gathering)"), particularly those of [Marie Thérèse Rodet Geoffrin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marie_Th%C3%A9r%C3%A8se_Rodet_Geoffrin "Marie Thérèse Rodet Geoffrin"), of the [marquise du Deffand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Madame_du_Deffand "Madame du Deffand") and of [Julie de Lespinasse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julie_de_Lespinasse "Julie de Lespinasse"). D'Alembert became infatuated with Julie de Lespinasse, and eventually took up residence with her.

He suffered bad health for many years and his death was as the result of a [urinary bladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urinary_bladder "Urinary bladder") illness. As a known [unbeliever](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infidel "Infidel"),[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-24)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-25)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_le_Rond_d'Alembert#cite_note-26) D'Alembert was buried in a common [unmarked grave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unmarked_grave "Unmarked grave").

In France, the [fundamental theorem of algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fundamental_theorem_of_algebra "Fundamental theorem of algebra") is known as the d'Alembert/[Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss "Carl Friedrich Gauss") theorem, as an error in d'Alembert's proof was caught by Gauss.

He also created his [ratio test](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ratio_test "Ratio test"), a test to determine if a [series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Series_(mathematics) "Series (mathematics)") converges.

The [D'Alembert operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%27Alembert_operator "D'Alembert operator"), which first arose in D'Alembert's analysis of vibrating strings, plays an important role in modern theoretical physics.

While he made great strides in mathematics and physics, d'Alembert is also famously known for incorrectly arguing in _Croix ou Pile_ that the [probability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability "Probability") of a coin landing heads increased for every time that it came up tails. In gambling, the strategy of decreasing one's bet the more one wins and increasing one's bet the more one loses is therefore called _the [D'Alembert system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%27Alembert\_system "D'Alembert system")_, a type of [martingale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Martingale_(betting_system) "Martingale (betting system)").

In [South Australia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Australia "South Australia"), a small inshore island in south-western [Spencer Gulf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spencer_Gulf "Spencer Gulf") was named _Ile d'Alembert_ by the French explorer, [Nicolas Baudin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolas_Baudin "Nicolas Baudin") during his [expedition to New Holland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolas_Baudin%27s_expedition_to_Australia "Nicolas Baudin's expedition to Australia"). The island is better known by the alternative English name of [Lipson Island](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lipson_Island "Lipson Island"). The island is a conservation park and seabird rookery.

Fictional portrayal
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Jean_le_Rond_d%27Alembert&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Fictional portrayal")]

[Diderot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diderot "Diderot") portrayed d'Alembert in _Le rêve de D'Alembert_ (_[D'Alembert's Dream](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%27Alembert%27s\_Dream "D'Alembert's Dream")_), written after the two men had become estranged. It depicts d'Alembert ill in bed, conducting a debate on materialist philosophy in his sleep.

[_D'Alembert's Principle_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%27Alembert%27s_Principle_(novel) "D'Alembert's Principle (novel)"), a 1996 novel by [Andrew Crumey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrew_Crumey "Andrew Crumey"), takes its title from [D'Alembert's principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/D%27Alembert%27s_principle "D'Alembert's principle") in physics. Its first part describes d'Alembert's life and his infatuation with [Julie de Lespinasse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julie_de_Lespinasse "Julie de Lespinasse").
