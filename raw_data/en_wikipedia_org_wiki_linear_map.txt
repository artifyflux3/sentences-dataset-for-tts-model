In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), and more specifically in [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra"), a **linear map** (also called a **linear mapping**, **linear transformation**, **vector space homomorphism**, or in some contexts **linear function**) is a [mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Map_(mathematics) "Map (mathematics)")![Image 1: {\displaystyle V\to W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/98b0589697c495103fca82279cf0c84b944b62b6) between two [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") that preserves the operations of [vector addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_addition "Vector addition") and [scalar multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_multiplication "Scalar multiplication"). The same names and the same definition are also used for the more general case of [modules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_(mathematics) "Module (mathematics)") over a [ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)"); see [Module homomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Module_homomorphism "Module homomorphism").

If a linear map is a [bijection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijection "Bijection") then it is called a **linear isomorphism**. In the case where ![Image 2: {\displaystyle V=W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/740038d36bd79466d6938d73b83fe737161fa1c6), a linear map is called a **linear endomorphism**. Sometimes the term **linear operator** refers to this case,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map#cite_note-1) but the term "linear operator" can have different meanings for different conventions: for example, it can be used to emphasize that ![Image 3: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) and ![Image 4: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7) are [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") vector spaces (not necessarily with ![Image 5: {\displaystyle V=W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/740038d36bd79466d6938d73b83fe737161fa1c6)),[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] or it can be used to emphasize that ![Image 6: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) is a [function space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_space "Function space"), which is a common convention in [functional analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_analysis "Functional analysis").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map#cite_note-2) Sometimes the term _[linear function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear\_function "Linear function")_ has the same meaning as _linear map_, while in [analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_analysis "Mathematical analysis") it does not.

A linear map from ![Image 7: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) to ![Image 8: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7) always maps the origin of ![Image 9: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) to the origin of ![Image 10: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7). Moreover, it maps [linear subspaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_subspace "Linear subspace") in ![Image 11: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) onto linear subspaces in ![Image 12: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7) (possibly of a lower [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(vector_space) "Dimension (vector space)"));[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map#cite_note-3) for example, it maps a [plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plane_(geometry) "Plane (geometry)") through the [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(geometry) "Origin (geometry)") in ![Image 13: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) to either a plane through the origin in ![Image 14: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7), a [line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_(geometry) "Line (geometry)") through the origin in ![Image 15: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7), or just the origin in ![Image 16: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7). Linear maps can often be represented as [matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)"), and simple examples include [rotation and reflection linear transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotations_and_reflections_in_two_dimensions "Rotations and reflections in two dimensions").

In the language of [category theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_theory "Category theory"), linear maps are the [morphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphism "Morphism") of vector spaces, and they form a category [equivalent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_of_categories "Equivalence of categories") to [the one of matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_of_matrices "Category of matrices").

Definition and first consequences
---------------------------------

Thus, a linear map is said to be _operation preserving_. In other words, it does not matter whether the linear map is applied before (the right hand sides of the above examples) or after (the left hand sides of the examples) the operations of addition and scalar multiplication.

By [the associativity of the addition operation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition#Associativity "Addition") denoted as +, for any vectors ![Image 23: {\textstyle \mathbf {u} _{1},\ldots ,\mathbf {u} _{n}\in V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4ff56d3267c11c192954e9af7dff69901de29dd) and scalars ![Image 24: {\textstyle c_{1},\ldots ,c_{n}\in K,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c74cdae5558856a84f5e91fd0c9f19af18a785da) the following equality holds:[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map#cite_note-5)![Image 25: {\displaystyle f(c_{1}\mathbf {u} _{1}+\cdots +c_{n}\mathbf {u} _{n})=c_{1}f(\mathbf {u} _{1})+\cdots +c_{n}f(\mathbf {u} _{n}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afe5085ab9435a45815acb7ce014696e9c7de5ed) Thus a linear map is one which preserves [linear combinations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_combination "Linear combination").

Denoting the zero elements of the vector spaces ![Image 26: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) and ![Image 27: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7) by ![Image 28: {\textstyle \mathbf {0} _{V}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6dae4bf2e588270286c59735162368e5ee0cc6af) and ![Image 29: {\textstyle \mathbf {0} _{W}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92ec046704f3874e6e8aadcb54c8004ef81ced79) respectively, it follows that ![Image 30: {\textstyle f(\mathbf {0} _{V})=\mathbf {0} _{W}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8cfb881efeac08d176d7cb5909e700529465bb5b) Let ![Image 31: {\displaystyle c=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9ee918699d0cb4b8c633cc1f520a8a7a174f44a) and ![Image 32: {\textstyle \mathbf {v} \in V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/557f9cbf990da31ddc7aa25ce3a7be5f87ee04d4) in the equation for homogeneity of degree 1: ![Image 33: {\displaystyle f(\mathbf {0} _{V})=f(0\mathbf {v} )=0f(\mathbf {v} )=\mathbf {0} _{W}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c844bd0eacf51dc36c7c090eb2da4ee51ed9cf69)

A linear map ![Image 34: {\displaystyle V\to K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/14f40344b7f468c6d9e2eae03f37ee6286f19d3a) with ![Image 35: {\displaystyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76fce82a62ed5461908f0dc8f037de4e3686b0) viewed as a one-dimensional vector space over itself is called a [linear functional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_functional "Linear functional").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map#cite_note-6)

These statements generalize to any left-module ![Image 36: {\textstyle {}_{R}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0b8868607ccc60aefecc3f650b7466a8ae6c67a1) over a ring ![Image 37: {\displaystyle R}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b0bfb3769bf24d80e15374dc37b0441e2616e33) without modification, and to any right-module upon reversing of the scalar multiplication.

*   A prototypical example that gives linear maps their name is a function ![Image 38: {\displaystyle f:\mathbb {R} \to \mathbb {R} :x\mapsto cx}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f558ca3675215137a38ff8742a10eebb7e3034e), of which the [graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_function "Graph of a function") is a line through the origin.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map#cite_note-7)
*   More generally, any [homothety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homothety "Homothety")![Image 39: {\textstyle \mathbf {v} \mapsto c\mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18bcdb55dbdd5c5d2b60de7f21f00771396b1d81) centered in the origin of a vector space is a linear map (here c is a scalar).
*   The zero map ![Image 40: {\textstyle \mathbf {x} \mapsto \mathbf {0} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/812b31dd59a14f41d4178bb777317bd3bdd88f37) between two vector spaces (over the same [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)")) is linear.
*   The [identity map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_function "Identity function") on any module is a linear operator.
*   For real numbers, the map ![Image 41: {\textstyle x\mapsto x^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b0bcf676f15aa22ab2429aeb7935d84835df8a9a) is not linear.
*   For real numbers, the map ![Image 42: {\textstyle x\mapsto x+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a43c220f372775e932f4d93e7d374279e91bda7a) is not linear (but is an [affine transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affine_transformation "Affine transformation")).
*   If ![Image 43: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) is a ![Image 44: {\displaystyle m\times n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/12b23d207d23dd430b93320539abbb0bde84870d)[real matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_matrix "Real matrix"), then ![Image 45: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) defines a linear map from ![Image 46: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) to ![Image 47: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a87a024931038d1858dc22e8a194e5978c3412e) by sending a [column vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Column_vector "Column vector")![Image 48: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} \in \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f33c7feddfbe1e5c87647a55639651d9fb2f23de) to the column vector ![Image 49: {\displaystyle A\mathbf {x} \in \mathbb {R} ^{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff693d6fcdb81f26d714c46b86c4188b3fd37c79). Conversely, any linear map between [finite-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite-dimensional "Finite-dimensional") vector spaces can be represented in this manner; see the [§Matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map#Matrices), below.
*   If ![Image 50: {\textstyle f:V\to W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abdeb127675edefd619f13be240e53fea69292a0) is an [isometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isometry "Isometry") between real [normed spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normed_space "Normed space") such that ![Image 51: {\textstyle f(0)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27ccedb9b9dff623a16340a136e140eea39f32b1) then ![Image 52: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is a linear map. This result is not necessarily true for complex normed space.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWilansky201321%E2%80%9326-8)
*   [Differentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") defines a linear map from the space of all differentiable functions to the space of all functions. It also defines a [linear operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_operator "Linear operator") on the space of all [smooth functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_function "Smooth function") (a linear operator is a [linear endomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_endomorphism "Linear endomorphism"), that is, a linear map with the same [domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_of_a_function "Domain of a function") and [codomain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codomain "Codomain")). Indeed, ![Image 53: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dx}}\left(af(x)+bg(x)\right)=a{\frac {df(x)}{dx}}+b{\frac {dg(x)}{dx}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a65e2ec756efdf4b4872efddf41d80b385b570f3)
*   A definite [integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral") over some [interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)")I is a linear map from the space of all real-valued integrable functions on I to ![Image 54: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc). Indeed, ![Image 55: {\displaystyle \int _{u}^{v}\left(af(x)+bg(x)\right)dx=a\int _{u}^{v}f(x)dx+b\int _{u}^{v}g(x)dx.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a800d98cf15f21de93e04db30a8ef21c73644d90)
*   An indefinite [integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral") (or [antiderivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiderivative "Antiderivative")) with a fixed integration starting point defines a linear map from the space of all real-valued integrable functions on ![Image 56: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) to the space of all real-valued, differentiable functions on ![Image 57: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc). Without a fixed starting point, the antiderivative maps to the [quotient space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_space_(linear_algebra) "Quotient space (linear algebra)") of the differentiable functions by the linear space of constant functions.
*   If ![Image 58: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) and ![Image 59: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7) are finite-dimensional vector spaces over a field F, of respective dimensions m and n, then the function that maps linear maps ![Image 60: {\textstyle f:V\to W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abdeb127675edefd619f13be240e53fea69292a0) to _n_ × _m_ matrices in the way described in [§Matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map#Matrices) (below) is a linear map, and even a [linear isomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_isomorphism "Linear isomorphism").
*   The [expected value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expected_value "Expected value") of a [random variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Random_variable#Definition "Random variable") (which is in fact a function, and as such an element of a vector space) is linear, as for random variables ![Image 61: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and ![Image 62: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) we have ![Image 63: {\displaystyle E[X+Y]=E[X]+E[Y]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea2251900ec2b03db1d6f870336155a2a09ff7f1) and ![Image 64: {\displaystyle E[aX]=aE[X]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3e34453ad80cdf674f4d15fab3e8096be81af79a), but the [variance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variance "Variance") of a random variable is not linear.

If ![Image 131: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) and ![Image 132: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7) are [finite-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite-dimensional "Finite-dimensional") vector spaces and a [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_of_a_vector_space "Basis of a vector space") is defined for each vector space, then every linear map from ![Image 133: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) to ![Image 134: {\displaystyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54a9c4c547f4d6111f81946cad242b18298d70b7) can be represented by a [matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)").[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map#cite_note-12) This is useful because it allows concrete calculations. Vector space of linear maps
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Linear_map&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Vector space of linear maps")]

The composition of linear maps is linear: if ![Image 191: {\displaystyle f:V\to W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/574dffa1c85efaef6b6ef553ebd8ad9cf7f87fd6) and ![Image 192: {\textstyle g:W\to Z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aa6d336769cb8e2e7f01807361a750577a9de618) are linear, then so is their [composition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relation_composition "Relation composition")![Image 193: {\textstyle g\circ f:V\to Z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9c271c698ec5bf92d613adc6c108191b7255d3ee). It follows from this that the [class](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Class_(set_theory) "Class (set theory)") of all vector spaces over a given field _K_, together with _K_-linear maps as [morphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphism "Morphism"), forms a [category](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_(mathematics) "Category (mathematics)").

The [inverse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_function "Inverse function") of a linear map, when defined, is again a linear map.

If ![Image 194: {\textstyle f_{1}:V\to W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5580af80f2164ff883a70b212bf71f90a98687b2) and ![Image 195: {\textstyle f_{2}:V\to W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d2aa07ed1e99afb386d5800d822e5868e6ead396) are linear, then so is their [pointwise](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pointwise "Pointwise") sum ![Image 196: {\displaystyle f_{1}+f_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7875d43d94bfdb30c7a2a5d72fea4a6960bc8787), which is defined by ![Image 197: {\displaystyle (f_{1}+f_{2})(\mathbf {x} )=f_{1}(\mathbf {x} )+f_{2}(\mathbf {x} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/145744cb4dbc4550b4d8efe2c0367cab34aa6487).

If ![Image 198: {\textstyle f:V\to W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abdeb127675edefd619f13be240e53fea69292a0) is linear and ![Image 199: {\textstyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d86dbd6183264b2f8569da1751380b173c7b185) is an element of the ground field ![Image 200: {\textstyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/985dcc2532a2d4d91b9a9610139216c63cf832d0), then the map ![Image 201: {\textstyle \alpha f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92df229298e2efdd1b742042aa01ce30ca4a3c76), defined by ![Image 202: {\textstyle (\alpha f)(\mathbf {x} )=\alpha (f(\mathbf {x} ))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ac29e0d3be09de95b773567b3ebf4b93214b300e), is also linear.

Thus the set ![Image 203: {\textstyle {\mathcal {L}}(V,W)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1013af4653ccaa67039dcff4a9e2e0af7a780534) of linear maps from ![Image 204: {\textstyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d67b50b7ba03a56fea637093cf80e12807852d19) to ![Image 205: {\textstyle W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e95737ee2530885a10b104e9cd5331077e1c88d5) itself forms a vector space over ![Image 206: {\textstyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/985dcc2532a2d4d91b9a9610139216c63cf832d0),[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map#cite_note-13) sometimes denoted ![Image 207: {\textstyle \operatorname {Hom} (V,W)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6c02a7e07ec8b60afb76d96cdf915f88fc253d5c).[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map#cite_note-14) Furthermore, in the case that ![Image 208: {\textstyle V=W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e9fcec532c5ab11d7dd4f3a7ac83d951179c547), this vector space, denoted ![Image 209: {\textstyle \operatorname {End} (V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca806cf0f1bbcf831f80a9e76bb303d9ab60eea2), is an [associative algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_algebra "Associative algebra") under [composition of maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composition_of_maps "Composition of maps"), since the composition of two linear maps is again a linear map, and the composition of maps is always associative. This case is discussed in more detail below.

Given again the finite-dimensional case, if bases have been chosen, then the composition of linear maps corresponds to the [matrix multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_multiplication "Matrix multiplication"), the addition of linear maps corresponds to the [matrix addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_addition "Matrix addition"), and the multiplication of linear maps with scalars corresponds to the multiplication of matrices with scalars.

### Endomorphisms and automorphisms

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Linear_map&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Endomorphisms and automorphisms")]

A linear transformation ![Image 210: {\textstyle f:V\to V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d4f05c06fb794079c3b28a1aa91c3ff7e4090d7a) is an [endomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endomorphism "Endomorphism") of ![Image 211: {\textstyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d67b50b7ba03a56fea637093cf80e12807852d19); the set of all such endomorphisms ![Image 212: {\textstyle \operatorname {End} (V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca806cf0f1bbcf831f80a9e76bb303d9ab60eea2) together with addition, composition and scalar multiplication as defined above forms an [associative algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_algebra "Associative algebra") with identity element over the field ![Image 213: {\textstyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/985dcc2532a2d4d91b9a9610139216c63cf832d0) (and in particular a [ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(algebra) "Ring (algebra)")). The multiplicative identity element of this algebra is the [identity map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_function "Identity function")![Image 214: {\textstyle \operatorname {id} :V\to V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/574028fa7c4de459a79fddd72a281420f140ad92).

An endomorphism of ![Image 215: {\textstyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d67b50b7ba03a56fea637093cf80e12807852d19) that is also an [isomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism "Isomorphism") is called an [automorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automorphism "Automorphism") of ![Image 216: {\textstyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d67b50b7ba03a56fea637093cf80e12807852d19). The composition of two automorphisms is again an automorphism, and the set of all automorphisms of ![Image 217: {\textstyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d67b50b7ba03a56fea637093cf80e12807852d19) forms a [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(math) "Group (math)"), the [automorphism group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automorphism_group "Automorphism group") of ![Image 218: {\textstyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d67b50b7ba03a56fea637093cf80e12807852d19) which is denoted by ![Image 219: {\textstyle \operatorname {Aut} (V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d2d57ebb96657a5dfd20060f637baf09bf42b1c) or ![Image 220: {\textstyle \operatorname {GL} (V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/58f4d6d756bd1541eefd853c705db15ae2c22769). Since the automorphisms are precisely those [endomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endomorphisms "Endomorphisms") which possess inverses under composition, ![Image 221: {\textstyle \operatorname {Aut} (V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d2d57ebb96657a5dfd20060f637baf09bf42b1c) is the group of [units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_(ring_theory) "Unit (ring theory)") in the ring ![Image 222: {\textstyle \operatorname {End} (V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca806cf0f1bbcf831f80a9e76bb303d9ab60eea2).

If ![Image 223: {\textstyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d67b50b7ba03a56fea637093cf80e12807852d19) has finite dimension ![Image 224: {\textstyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc6e1f880981346a604257ebcacdef24c0aca2d6), then ![Image 225: {\textstyle \operatorname {End} (V)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca806cf0f1bbcf831f80a9e76bb303d9ab60eea2) is [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism "Isomorphism") to the [associative algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_algebra "Associative algebra") of all ![Image 226: {\textstyle n\times n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b6fbcac74eb679edde27a00a9f2a17a51f9ea354) matrices with entries in ![Image 227: {\textstyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/985dcc2532a2d4d91b9a9610139216c63cf832d0). The automorphism group of ![Image 228: {\textstyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d67b50b7ba03a56fea637093cf80e12807852d19) is [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_isomorphism "Group isomorphism") to the [general linear group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_linear_group "General linear group")![Image 229: {\textstyle \operatorname {GL} (n,K)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eacb8fb84b2808e33a6db91b4214648f3b218718) of all ![Image 230: {\textstyle n\times n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b6fbcac74eb679edde27a00a9f2a17a51f9ea354) invertible matrices with entries in ![Image 231: {\textstyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/985dcc2532a2d4d91b9a9610139216c63cf832d0).

A subtler invariant of a linear transformation ![Image 254: {\textstyle f:V\to W}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abdeb127675edefd619f13be240e53fea69292a0) is the [_co_ kernel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cokernel "Cokernel"), which is defined as ![Image 255: {\displaystyle \operatorname {coker} (f):=W/f(V)=W/\operatorname {im} (f).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cada952f507e82dc08be8ae4940c10265a957f20)

This is the _dual_ notion to the kernel: just as the kernel is a _sub_ space of the _domain,_ the co-kernel is a [_quotient_ space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_space_(linear_algebra) "Quotient space (linear algebra)") of the _target._ Formally, one has the [exact sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exact_sequence "Exact sequence")![Image 256: {\displaystyle 0\to \ker(f)\to V\to W\to \operatorname {coker} (f)\to 0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/491ada04c60e9cf68cfd91182536d7eb6fd89d70)

These can be interpreted thus: given a linear equation _f_(**v**) = **w** to solve,

*   the kernel is the space of _solutions_ to the _homogeneous_ equation _f_(**v**) = 0, and its dimension is the number of [degrees of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom "Degrees of freedom") in the space of solutions, if it is not empty;
*   the co-kernel is the space of [constraints](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/constraint "wikt:constraint") that the solutions must satisfy, and its dimension is the maximal number of independent constraints.

The dimension of the co-kernel and the dimension of the image (the rank) add up to the dimension of the target space. For finite dimensions, this means that the dimension of the quotient space _W_/_f_(_V_) is the dimension of the target space minus the dimension of the image.

As a simple example, consider the map _f_: **R**2 → **R**2, given by _f_(_x_, _y_) = (0, _y_). Then for an equation _f_(_x_, _y_) = (_a_, _b_) to have a solution, we must have _a_ = 0 (one constraint), and in that case the solution space is (_x_, _b_) or equivalently stated, (0, _b_) + (_x_, 0), (one degree of freedom). The kernel may be expressed as the subspace (_x_, 0) <_V_: the value of _x_ is the freedom in a solution – while the cokernel may be expressed via the map _W_ → **R**, ![Image 257: {\textstyle (a,b)\mapsto (a)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1f53be07dccb809fa9764339428f5796bbc27a89): given a vector (_a_, _b_), the value of _a_ is the _obstruction_ to there being a solution.

An example illustrating the infinite-dimensional case is afforded by the map _f_: **R**∞ → **R**∞, ![Image 258: {\textstyle \left\{a_{n}\right\}\mapsto \left\{b_{n}\right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/71a9ce4d48b1a2fa236c77c64071ba6095f8fab1) with _b_ 1 = 0 and _b_ _n_ + 1 = _a n_ for _n_> 0. Its image consists of all sequences with first element 0, and thus its cokernel consists of the classes of sequences with identical first element. Thus, whereas its kernel has dimension 0 (it maps only the zero sequence to the zero sequence), its co-kernel has dimension 1. Since the domain and the target space are the same, the rank and the dimension of the kernel add up to the same [sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinal_number#Cardinal_addition "Cardinal number") as the rank and the dimension of the co-kernel (![Image 259: {\textstyle \aleph _{0}+0=\aleph _{0}+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/75625b037575c0eb5dfceea93de905fe4f4d4e8f)), but in the infinite-dimensional case it cannot be inferred that the kernel and the co-kernel of an [endomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endomorphism "Endomorphism") have the same dimension (0 ≠ 1). The reverse situation obtains for the map _h_: **R**∞ → **R**∞, ![Image 260: {\textstyle \left\{a_{n}\right\}\mapsto \left\{c_{n}\right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1df94ee38b2273f0d4094821dd78b11426e44aea) with _c n_ = _a_ _n_ + 1. Its image is the entire target space, and hence its co-kernel has dimension 0, but since it maps all sequences in which only the first element is non-zero to the zero sequence, its kernel has dimension 1.

For a linear operator with finite-dimensional kernel and co-kernel, one may define _index_ as: ![Image 261: {\displaystyle \operatorname {ind} (f):=\dim(\ker(f))-\dim(\operatorname {coker} (f)),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5371bf691407109c78cc6a5a36a46fd136654bc) namely the degrees of freedom minus the number of constraints.

For a transformation between finite-dimensional vector spaces, this is just the difference dim(_V_) − dim(_W_), by rank–nullity. This gives an indication of how many solutions or how many constraints one has: if mapping from a larger space to a smaller one, the map may be onto, and thus will have degrees of freedom even without constraints. Conversely, if mapping from a smaller space to a larger one, the map cannot be onto, and thus one will have constraints even without degrees of freedom.

The index of an operator is precisely the [Euler characteristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler_characteristic "Euler characteristic") of the 2-term complex 0 → _V_ → _W_ → 0. In [operator theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operator_theory "Operator theory"), the index of [Fredholm operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fredholm_operator "Fredholm operator") is an object of study, with a major result being the [Atiyah–Singer index theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atiyah%E2%80%93Singer_index_theorem "Atiyah–Singer index theorem").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map#cite_note-18)

Algebraic classifications of linear transformations
---------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Linear_map&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Algebraic classifications of linear transformations")]

No classification of linear maps could be exhaustive. The following incomplete list enumerates some important classifications that do not require any additional structure on the vector space.

Let V and W denote vector spaces over a field F and let _T_: _V_ → _W_ be a linear map.

T is said to be _[injective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injective "Injective")_ or a _[monomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monomorphism "Monomorphism")_ if any of the following equivalent conditions are true:

1.   T is [one-to-one](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injective "Injective") as a map of [sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)").
2.   ker _T_ = {0 _V_}
3.   dim(ker _T_) = 0
4.   T is [monic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monic_morphism "Monic morphism") or left-cancellable, which is to say, for any vector space U and any pair of linear maps _R_: _U_ → _V_ and _S_: _U_ → _V_, the equation _TR_ = _TS_ implies _R_ = _S_.
5.   T is [left-invertible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_(ring_theory) "Inverse (ring theory)"), which is to say there exists a linear map _S_: _W_ → _V_ such that _ST_ is the [identity map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_function "Identity function") on V.

T is said to be _[surjective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surjective "Surjective")_ or an _[epimorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epimorphism "Epimorphism")_ if any of the following equivalent conditions are true:

1.   T is [onto](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Surjective "Surjective") as a map of sets.
2.   [coker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cokernel "Cokernel")_T_ = {0 _W_}
3.   T is [epic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epimorphism "Epimorphism") or right-cancellable, which is to say, for any vector space U and any pair of linear maps _R_: _W_ → _U_ and _S_: _W_ → _U_, the equation _RT_ = _ST_ implies _R_ = _S_.
4.   T is [right-invertible](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_(ring_theory) "Inverse (ring theory)"), which is to say there exists a linear map _S_: _W_ → _V_ such that _TS_ is the [identity map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_function "Identity function") on W.

T is said to be an _[isomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism "Isomorphism")_ if it is both left- and right-invertible. This is equivalent to T being both one-to-one and onto (a [bijection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bijection "Bijection") of sets) or also to T being both epic and monic, and so being a [bimorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bimorphism "Bimorphism").

If _T_: _V_ → _V_ is an endomorphism, then:

*   If, for some positive integer n, the n-th iterate of T, _T_ _n_, is identically zero, then T is said to be [nilpotent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nilpotent "Nilpotent").
*   If _T_ 2 = _T_, then T is said to be [idempotent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idempotent "Idempotent")
*   If _T_ = _kI_, where k is some scalar, then T is said to be a scaling transformation or scalar multiplication map; see [scalar matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_matrix "Scalar matrix").

Given a linear map which is an [endomorphism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endomorphism "Endomorphism") whose matrix is _A_, in the basis _B_ of the space it transforms vector coordinates [u] as [v] = _A_[u]. As vectors change with the inverse of _B_ (vectors coordinates are [contravariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariance_and_contravariance_of_vectors "Covariance and contravariance of vectors")) its inverse transformation is [v] = _B_[v'].

Substituting this in the first expression ![Image 262: {\displaystyle B\left[v'\right]=AB\left[u'\right]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2855717373418c2c2134151c0d0f4d5957292f8) hence ![Image 263: {\displaystyle \left[v'\right]=B^{-1}AB\left[u'\right]=A'\left[u'\right].}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65eb8aa4babed0054fcf025303d23fd58309bf7e)

Therefore, the matrix in the new basis is _A′_ = _B_−1 _AB_, being _B_ the matrix of the given basis.

Therefore, linear maps are said to be 1-co- 1-contra-[variant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariance_and_contravariance_of_vectors "Covariance and contravariance of vectors") objects, or type (1, 1) [tensors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor").

A _linear transformation_ between [topological vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_vector_space "Topological vector space"), for example [normed spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Normed_space "Normed space"), may be [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function_(topology) "Continuous function (topology)"). If its domain and codomain are the same, it will then be a [continuous linear operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_linear_operator "Continuous linear operator"). A linear operator on a normed linear space is continuous if and only if it is [bounded](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bounded_operator "Bounded operator"), for example, when the domain is finite-dimensional.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_map#cite_note-19) An infinite-dimensional domain may have [discontinuous linear operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discontinuous_linear_operator "Discontinuous linear operator").

An example of an unbounded, hence discontinuous, linear transformation is differentiation on the space of smooth functions equipped with the supremum norm (a function with small values can have a derivative with large values, while the derivative of 0 is 0). For a specific example, sin(_nx_)/_n_ converges to 0, but its derivative cos(_nx_) does not, so differentiation is not continuous at 0 (and by a variation of this argument, it is not continuous anywhere).

A specific application of linear maps is for [geometric transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_transformation "Geometric transformation"), such as those performed in [computer graphics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_graphics "Computer graphics"), where the translation, rotation and scaling of 2D or 3D objects is performed by the use of a [transformation matrix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformation_matrix "Transformation matrix"). Linear mappings also are used as a mechanism for describing change: for example in calculus correspond to derivatives; or in relativity, used as a device to keep track of the local transformations of reference frames.

Another application of these transformations is in [compiler optimizations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compiler_optimizations "Compiler optimizations") of nested-loop code, and in [parallelizing compiler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelizing_compiler "Parallelizing compiler") techniques.
