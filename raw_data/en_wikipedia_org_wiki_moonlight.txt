[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Earthshine_Moon.jpg/250px-Earthshine_Moon.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Earthshine_Moon.jpg)

Earthlight illuminates the dim side of the Moon, while direct sunlight illuminates the bright side.

**Moonlight** (or **Moonshine**) is light from the surface of the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon"), consisting mostly of reflected [sunlight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunlight "Sunlight"), and some [earthlight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earthlight "Earthlight").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moonlight#cite_note-Toomer-1)

The ancient Greek philosopher [Anaxagoras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaxagoras "Anaxagoras") noted that "_the sun provides the moon with its brightness_".[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moonlight#cite_note-2) Ancient Chinese polymath [Zhang Heng](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zhang_Heng "Zhang Heng") concluded that the light of the moon comes from the Sun. He writes in his treatise, _The Spiritual Constitution of the Universe_, that the Sun and Moon are "like fire and water", where the Sun "gives out light", and the Moon "reflects it".[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moonlight#cite_note-3)

[Nyctalopia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nyctalopia "Nyctalopia") was called "moonblink" and thought to be caused by sleeping in moonlight in the tropics as late as the 19th century,[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moonlight#cite_note-4) but is actually caused by a deficiency in [Vitamin A](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitamin_A "Vitamin A"). Moonlight was historically thought to cause [equine recurrent uveitis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equine_recurrent_uveitis "Equine recurrent uveitis"), which was called "moon blindness".[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_][Moonmilk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moonmilk "Moonmilk"), a soft white limestone precipitate found in caves, was thought to be caused by the rays of the Moon.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moonlight#cite_note-Gessner-5)[Selenoplexia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Selenoplexia "Selenoplexia") was a supposed medical condition caused by the rays of the moon.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moonlight#cite_note-Lippincott-6)

Moonlight illuminates a lake and surroundings.

The color of moonlight appears bluish or silvery to the [human eye](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_eye "Human eye") compared to other, brighter light sources, however this is an illusion, due to the [Purkinje effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purkinje_effect "Purkinje effect").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] The intensity of moonlight varies greatly depending on the [lunar phase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_phase "Lunar phase"), with the [full moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Full_moon "Full moon") typically providing about 0.05–0.1[lux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lux "Lux") illumination.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moonlight#cite_note-supermoon-7) When a full Moon at [perigee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apsis "Apsis") (a "[supermoon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supermoon "Supermoon")") is viewed around upper [culmination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Culmination "Culmination") from the [tropics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropics "Tropics"), the [illuminance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Illuminance "Illuminance") can reach up to 0.32 lux.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moonlight#cite_note-supermoon-7) From Earth, the [apparent magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_magnitude "Apparent magnitude") of the full Moon is only about 1⁄380,000 that of the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] The Moon's [Bond albedo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_albedo "Bond albedo") averages 0.136,[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moonlight#cite_note-Saari-8) meaning only 13.6% of incident sunlight is reflected from the lunar surface. Moonlight takes approximately 1.26 seconds to reach Earth's surface. Moonlight is [scattered by particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_scattering_by_particles "Light scattering by particles") in the [atmosphere of Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Earth "Atmosphere of Earth"), which increases the [brightness](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sky_brightness "Sky brightness") of the [night sky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Night_sky "Night sky"), and decreases [contrast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contrast_(vision) "Contrast (vision)") between dimmer stars and the background. For this reason, many [astronomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomer "Astronomer") usually avoid [observing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observational_astronomy "Observational astronomy") the sky around a full moon.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/46/Lunar_eclipse_04-15-2014_by_R_Jay_GaBany.jpg/250px-Lunar_eclipse_04-15-2014_by_R_Jay_GaBany.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Lunar_eclipse_04-15-2014_by_R_Jay_GaBany.jpg)

Composite image of the [April 2014 total lunar eclipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/April_2014_lunar_eclipse "April 2014 lunar eclipse") from [Charleston, West Virginia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charleston,_West_Virginia "Charleston, West Virginia"), United States.

A [lunar eclipse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_eclipse "Lunar eclipse") is an [astronomical event](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_event "Astronomical event") that occurs when the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon") moves into the [Earth's shadow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_shadow "Earth's shadow"), causing the moonlight to be darkened.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moonlight#cite_note-9) Such an alignment occurs during an [eclipse season](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eclipse_season "Eclipse season"), approximately every six months, during the [full moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Full_moon "Full moon") phase, when the Moon's orbital plane is closest to [the plane of the Earth's orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecliptic "Ecliptic").

When the Moon is totally eclipsed by the Earth (a "deep eclipse"),[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moonlight#cite_note-QZ-2023-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moonlight#cite_note-AT-20231107-11) it takes on a reddish color that is caused by the planet when it completely blocks direct [sunlight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunlight "Sunlight") from reaching the Moon's surface, as the only light that is [reflected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffuse_reflection "Diffuse reflection") from the lunar surface is what has been [refracted](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_refraction "Atmospheric refraction") by the [Earth's atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere "Earth's atmosphere"). This light appears reddish due to the [Rayleigh scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rayleigh_scattering "Rayleigh scattering") of blue light, the same reason sunrises and sunsets are more orange than during the day.

Moonlight sometimes thought to have a harmful influence in folklore. For example, sleeping in the light of a full moon was believed to transform a person into a [werewolf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Werewolf "Werewolf"). The light of the Moon was thought to worsen the symptoms of [lunatics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunatics "Lunatics"), and to sleep in moonlight could make one blind, or mad.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moonlight#cite_note-12)

[Katie Paterson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Katie_Paterson "Katie Paterson") produced a display at [The Guggenheim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Guggenheim "The Guggenheim") in 2008, entitled _Light bulb to Simulate Moonlight_, which consisted of 289 lightbulbs coated to produce a similar spectrum to the light of the full Moon.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moonlight#cite_note-guggenheimPaterson-13)
