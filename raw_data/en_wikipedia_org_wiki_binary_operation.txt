From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Mathematical operation with two operands

Not to be confused with [Bitwise operation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitwise_operation "Bitwise operation").

[![Image 76](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Binary_operations_as_black_box.svg/250px-Binary_operations_as_black_box.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Binary_operations_as_black_box.svg)

A binary operation ∘{\displaystyle \circ }![Image 77: {\displaystyle \circ }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99add39d2b681e2de7ff62422c32704a05c7ec31) is a rule for combining the arguments x{\displaystyle x}![Image 78: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) and y{\displaystyle y}![Image 79: {\displaystyle y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a6208ec717213d4317e666f1ae872e00620a0d) to produce x∘y{\displaystyle x\circ y}![Image 80: {\displaystyle x\circ y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2ba86902ee98c41deb1275ddb8693977f27e1da)

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **binary operation** or **dyadic operation** is a rule for combining two [elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Element_(mathematics) "Element (mathematics)") (called [operands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operands "Operands")) to produce another element. More formally, a binary operation is an [operation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_(mathematics) "Operation (mathematics)") of [arity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arity "Arity") two.

More specifically, a **binary operation** on a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") is a [binary function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_function "Binary function") that maps every [pair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair "Ordered pair") of elements of the set to an element of the set. Examples include the familiar [arithmetic operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic_operations "Arithmetic operations") like [addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition "Addition"), [subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction "Subtraction"), [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication "Multiplication"), set operations like union, complement, intersection. Other examples are readily found in different areas of mathematics, such as [vector addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_addition "Vector addition"), [matrix multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_multiplication "Matrix multiplication"), and [conjugation in groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjugation_(group_theory) "Conjugation (group theory)").

A binary function that involves several sets is sometimes also called a _binary operation_. For example, [scalar multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_multiplication "Scalar multiplication") of [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") takes a scalar and a vector to produce a vector, and [scalar product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_product "Scalar product") takes two vectors to produce a scalar.

Binary operations are the keystone of most [structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_structure "Algebraic structure") that are studied in [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra "Algebra"), in particular in [semigroups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semigroup "Semigroup"), [monoids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monoid "Monoid"), [groups](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)"), [rings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(algebra) "Ring (algebra)"), [fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)"), and [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space").

-----------

More precisely, a binary operation on a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)")S{\displaystyle S}![Image 81: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) is a [mapping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Map_(mathematics) "Map (mathematics)") of the elements of the [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product")S×S{\displaystyle S\times S}![Image 82: {\displaystyle S\times S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b0f88969df0adf75522ab2d85717167d1069d28) to S{\displaystyle S}![Image 83: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2):[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_operation#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_operation#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_operation#cite_note-3)

f:S×S→S.{\displaystyle \,f\colon S\times S\rightarrow S.}![Image 84: {\displaystyle \,f\colon S\times S\rightarrow S.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c628614666437ac185c12fafb3f40dd37af8f23a)
If f{\displaystyle f}![Image 85: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is not a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") but a [partial function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_function "Partial function"), then f{\displaystyle f}![Image 86: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is called a **partial binary operation**. For instance, division is a partial binary operation on the set of all [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_numbers "Real numbers"), because one cannot [divide by zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_by_zero "Division by zero"): a 0{\displaystyle {\frac {a}{0}}}![Image 87: {\displaystyle {\frac {a}{0}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88d47cc8c7b0e1498279b2a31aaeab2488ecf986) is undefined for every real number a{\displaystyle a}![Image 88: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc). In both [model theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Model_theory "Model theory") and classical [universal algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_algebra "Universal algebra"), binary operations are required to be defined on all elements of S×S{\displaystyle S\times S}![Image 89: {\displaystyle S\times S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b0f88969df0adf75522ab2d85717167d1069d28). However, [partial algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_algebra "Partial algebra")[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_operation#cite_note-Gratzer2008-4) generalize [universal algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_algebra "Universal algebra") to allow partial operations.

Sometimes, especially in [computer science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science "Computer science"), the term binary operation is used for any [binary function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_function "Binary function").

Properties and examples
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Binary_operation&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Properties and examples")]

Typical examples of binary operations are the [addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition "Addition") (+{\displaystyle +}![Image 90: {\displaystyle +}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fe6ef363cd19902d1a7a71fb1c8b21e8ede52406)) and [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication "Multiplication") (×{\displaystyle \times }![Image 91: {\displaystyle \times }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ffafff1ad26cbe49045f19a67ce532116a32703)) of [numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number "Number") and [matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)") as well as [composition of functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Composition_of_functions "Composition of functions") on a single set. For instance,

*   On the set of real numbers R{\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }![Image 92: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc), f(a,b)=a+b{\displaystyle f(a,b)=a+b}![Image 93: {\displaystyle f(a,b)=a+b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/573ee9d41945bcbae7b153f145e3608a650fd523) is a binary operation since the sum of two real numbers is a real number.
*   On the set of natural numbers N{\displaystyle \mathbb {N} }![Image 94: {\displaystyle \mathbb {N} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdf9a96b565ea202d0f4322e9195613fb26a9bed), f(a,b)=a+b{\displaystyle f(a,b)=a+b}![Image 95: {\displaystyle f(a,b)=a+b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/573ee9d41945bcbae7b153f145e3608a650fd523) is a binary operation since the sum of two natural numbers is a natural number. This is a different binary operation than the previous one since the sets are different.
*   On the set M(2,R){\displaystyle M(2,\mathbb {R} )}![Image 96: {\displaystyle M(2,\mathbb {R} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b4e0e643a78f079d3f4276db8b7697b951c47b7) of 2×2{\displaystyle 2\times 2}![Image 97: {\displaystyle 2\times 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f8a0e3400ffb97d67c00267ed50cddfe824cbe80) matrices with real entries, f(A,B)=A+B{\displaystyle f(A,B)=A+B}![Image 98: {\displaystyle f(A,B)=A+B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d986ad97d609ad8c8a99174f433ea09521979e12) is a binary operation since the sum of two such matrices is a 2×2{\displaystyle 2\times 2}![Image 99: {\displaystyle 2\times 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f8a0e3400ffb97d67c00267ed50cddfe824cbe80) matrix.
*   On the set M(2,R){\displaystyle M(2,\mathbb {R} )}![Image 100: {\displaystyle M(2,\mathbb {R} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b4e0e643a78f079d3f4276db8b7697b951c47b7) of 2×2{\displaystyle 2\times 2}![Image 101: {\displaystyle 2\times 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f8a0e3400ffb97d67c00267ed50cddfe824cbe80) matrices with real entries, f(A,B)=A B{\displaystyle f(A,B)=AB}![Image 102: {\displaystyle f(A,B)=AB}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de827f274c50d35236f84190fc5e95c3fab35508) is a binary operation since the product of two such matrices is a 2×2{\displaystyle 2\times 2}![Image 103: {\displaystyle 2\times 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f8a0e3400ffb97d67c00267ed50cddfe824cbe80) matrix.
*   For a given set C{\displaystyle C}![Image 104: {\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029), let S{\displaystyle S}![Image 105: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) be the set of all functions h:C→C{\displaystyle h\colon C\rightarrow C}![Image 106: {\displaystyle h\colon C\rightarrow C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e64083be1822f4ddf5a82cbed37a09105ba45038). Define f:S×S→S{\displaystyle f\colon S\times S\rightarrow S}![Image 107: {\displaystyle f\colon S\times S\rightarrow S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/814815abbff2e373dff7ab9497181093617f3c78) by f(h 1,h 2)(c)=(h 1∘h 2)(c)=h 1(h 2(c)){\displaystyle f(h_{1},h_{2})(c)=(h_{1}\circ h_{2})(c)=h_{1}(h_{2}(c))}![Image 108: {\displaystyle f(h_{1},h_{2})(c)=(h_{1}\circ h_{2})(c)=h_{1}(h_{2}(c))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8d8619c83e663504b9488f27fb15e8e3f538a2c7) for all c∈C{\displaystyle c\in C}![Image 109: {\displaystyle c\in C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/447d3982c94c23d6b6d01c90da81a6125aa26567), the composition of the two functions h 1{\displaystyle h_{1}}![Image 110: {\displaystyle h_{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/14e8880a2e4243a2fe5157e574a0547ef3d5d373) and h 2{\displaystyle h_{2}}![Image 111: {\displaystyle h_{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/56d2d1733d09f02f33694f72b6da94681021e0f0) in S{\displaystyle S}![Image 112: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2). Then f{\displaystyle f}![Image 113: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) is a binary operation since the composition of the two functions is again a function on the set C{\displaystyle C}![Image 114: {\displaystyle C}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fc55753007cd3c18576f7933f6f089196732029) (that is, a member of S{\displaystyle S}![Image 115: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2)).

Many binary operations of interest in both algebra and formal logic are [commutative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative "Commutative"), satisfying f(a,b)=f(b,a){\displaystyle f(a,b)=f(b,a)}![Image 116: {\displaystyle f(a,b)=f(b,a)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf13e075798a4813e81f54fa0b8661c7f6d826ee) for all elements a{\displaystyle a}![Image 117: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) and b{\displaystyle b}![Image 118: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3) in S{\displaystyle S}![Image 119: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2), or [associative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative "Associative"), satisfying f(f(a,b),c)=f(a,f(b,c)){\displaystyle f(f(a,b),c)=f(a,f(b,c))}![Image 120: {\displaystyle f(f(a,b),c)=f(a,f(b,c))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e1920afc59d9fd015bace49384bbce8ed531a1ee) for all a{\displaystyle a}![Image 121: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc), b{\displaystyle b}![Image 122: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3), and c{\displaystyle c}![Image 123: {\displaystyle c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a67b81c2de995bd608d5b2df50cd8cd7d92455) in S{\displaystyle S}![Image 124: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2). Many also have [identity elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_element "Identity element") and [inverse elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse_element "Inverse element").

The first three examples above are commutative and all of the above examples are associative.

On the set of real numbers R{\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }![Image 125: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc), [subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction "Subtraction"), that is, f(a,b)=a−b{\displaystyle f(a,b)=a-b}![Image 126: {\displaystyle f(a,b)=a-b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/718db466ad14493bdd2621a2721f803956b54b42), is a binary operation which is not commutative since, in general, a−b≠b−a{\displaystyle a-b\neq b-a}![Image 127: {\displaystyle a-b\neq b-a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86bc96bb738a24c3a743e76e6a7801e135f9ec2a). It is also not associative, since, in general, a−(b−c)≠(a−b)−c{\displaystyle a-(b-c)\neq (a-b)-c}![Image 128: {\displaystyle a-(b-c)\neq (a-b)-c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5236035f9fab1d869364a2d04a21dcf9fff22267); for instance, 1−(2−3)=2{\displaystyle 1-(2-3)=2}![Image 129: {\displaystyle 1-(2-3)=2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a746ab732681a206884bb4a1bfddb22c5867e528) but (1−2)−3=−4{\displaystyle (1-2)-3=-4}![Image 130: {\displaystyle (1-2)-3=-4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b23c37ecee7699de9e727fa73fa7e4dd41b070e5).

On the set of natural numbers N{\displaystyle \mathbb {N} }![Image 131: {\displaystyle \mathbb {N} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdf9a96b565ea202d0f4322e9195613fb26a9bed), the binary operation [exponentiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exponentiation "Exponentiation"), f(a,b)=a b{\displaystyle f(a,b)=a^{b}}![Image 132: {\displaystyle f(a,b)=a^{b}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/70b6f94e40229f97ea05ed941668495995f72be7), is not commutative since, a b≠b a{\displaystyle a^{b}\neq b^{a}}![Image 133: {\displaystyle a^{b}\neq b^{a}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e2eb3d303113ad449163583bc218a0b0b749ffa7) (cf. [Equation x y = y x](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equation_x%5Ey_%3D_y%5Ex "Equation x^y = y^x")), and is also not associative since f(f(a,b),c)≠f(a,f(b,c)){\displaystyle f(f(a,b),c)\neq f(a,f(b,c))}![Image 134: {\displaystyle f(f(a,b),c)\neq f(a,f(b,c))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a71966df2fcd79fd6c6ef81ddf1a895aee1b08de). For instance, with a=2{\displaystyle a=2}![Image 135: {\displaystyle a=2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4208bf5a67fc2ceb3a3bcd75aebb1d74fbb531bd), b=3{\displaystyle b=3}![Image 136: {\displaystyle b=3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5fd42532188ea1372b447a58bfebfc60c79484a), and c=2{\displaystyle c=2}![Image 137: {\displaystyle c=2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c128a061994fb9d137dc6ea4aa68c0958c550111), f(2 3,2)=f(8,2)=8 2=64{\displaystyle f(2^{3},2)=f(8,2)=8^{2}=64}![Image 138: {\displaystyle f(2^{3},2)=f(8,2)=8^{2}=64}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7d5310cdaa91c9c36438c26c85fb12eec67a6e5c), but f(2,3 2)=f(2,9)=2 9=512{\displaystyle f(2,3^{2})=f(2,9)=2^{9}=512}![Image 139: {\displaystyle f(2,3^{2})=f(2,9)=2^{9}=512}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/58c0f29e5af99ec7f7bf8cd2a218db36b3ed20c5). By changing the set N{\displaystyle \mathbb {N} }![Image 140: {\displaystyle \mathbb {N} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fdf9a96b565ea202d0f4322e9195613fb26a9bed) to the set of integers Z{\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }![Image 141: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449494a083e0a1fda2b61c62b2f09b6bee4633dc), this binary operation becomes a partial binary operation since it is now undefined when a=0{\displaystyle a=0}![Image 142: {\displaystyle a=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90d476e5e765a5d77bbcff32e4584579207ec7d8) and b{\displaystyle b}![Image 143: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3) is any negative integer. For either set, this operation has a _right identity_ (which is 1{\displaystyle 1}![Image 144: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf)) since f(a,1)=a{\displaystyle f(a,1)=a}![Image 145: {\displaystyle f(a,1)=a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e8891d87397eae3967dd552491e40cef55884526) for all a{\displaystyle a}![Image 146: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) in the set, which is not an _identity_ (two sided identity) since f(1,b)≠b{\displaystyle f(1,b)\neq b}![Image 147: {\displaystyle f(1,b)\neq b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ae6e71d14df49534f6b7dc6950e7bbaf641b298) in general.

[Division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_(mathematics) "Division (mathematics)") (÷{\displaystyle \div }![Image 148: {\displaystyle \div }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/837b35ee5d25b5ce7b07f292c27cc90533dd9fd4)), a partial binary operation on the set of real or rational numbers, is not commutative or associative. [Tetration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetration "Tetration") (↑↑{\displaystyle \uparrow \uparrow }![Image 149: {\displaystyle \uparrow \uparrow }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d4d9cfbd79129e084f89bfcfc791864d9781e4d)), as a binary operation on the natural numbers, is not commutative or associative and has no identity element.

Notation
--------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Binary_operation&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Notation")]

Binary operations are often written using [infix notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infix_notation "Infix notation") such as a∗b{\displaystyle a\ast b}![Image 150: {\displaystyle a\ast b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5311c1f292ef6f926ec2cca0de3fae75d25fca15), a+b{\displaystyle a+b}![Image 151: {\displaystyle a+b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a2391acf09244b9dba74eb940e871a6be7e7973a), a⋅b{\displaystyle a\cdot b}![Image 152: {\displaystyle a\cdot b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/620419d3ed53abc98659a5fc0f3a5eb6177830ae) or (by [juxtaposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Juxtaposition#Mathematics "Juxtaposition") with no symbol) a b{\displaystyle ab}![Image 153: {\displaystyle ab}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/49337c5cf256196e2292f7047cb5da68c24ca95d) rather than by functional notation of the form f(a,b){\displaystyle f(a,b)}![Image 154: {\displaystyle f(a,b)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/408a449654fb6a8e6141ee6bbcb637efe3f9e4c7). Powers are usually also written without operator, but with the second argument as [superscript](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superscript "Superscript").

Binary operations are sometimes written using prefix or (more frequently) postfix notation, both of which dispense with parentheses. They are also called, respectively, [Polish notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polish_notation "Polish notation")∗a b{\displaystyle \ast ab}![Image 155: {\displaystyle \ast ab}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb50e8b64dd85f6bbf573e6e3cd55b5e282b24f0) and [reverse Polish notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reverse_Polish_notation "Reverse Polish notation")a b∗{\displaystyle ab\ast }![Image 156: {\displaystyle ab\ast }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff28d5b025bc6834a2dfc2a2ab164346f306a0b0).

Binary operations as ternary relations
--------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Binary_operation&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Binary operations as ternary relations")]

A binary operation f{\displaystyle f}![Image 157: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) on a set S{\displaystyle S}![Image 158: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) may be viewed as a [ternary relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ternary_relation "Ternary relation") on S{\displaystyle S}![Image 159: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2), that is, the set of triples (a,b,f(a,b)){\displaystyle (a,b,f(a,b))}![Image 160: {\displaystyle (a,b,f(a,b))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/13a8de12483843538ac4bf429885b67b813f46fd) in S×S×S{\displaystyle S\times S\times S}![Image 161: {\displaystyle S\times S\times S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ed3b1dad02488be7c62875f85e6567be43794ea) for all a{\displaystyle a}![Image 162: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) and b{\displaystyle b}![Image 163: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3) in S{\displaystyle S}![Image 164: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2).

Other binary operations
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Binary_operation&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Other binary operations")]

For example, [scalar multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_multiplication "Scalar multiplication") in [linear algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_algebra "Linear algebra"). Here K{\displaystyle K}![Image 165: {\displaystyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76fce82a62ed5461908f0dc8f037de4e3686b0) is a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)") and S{\displaystyle S}![Image 166: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) is a [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") over that field.

Also the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product") of two vectors maps S×S{\displaystyle S\times S}![Image 167: {\displaystyle S\times S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b0f88969df0adf75522ab2d85717167d1069d28) to K{\displaystyle K}![Image 168: {\displaystyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76fce82a62ed5461908f0dc8f037de4e3686b0), where K{\displaystyle K}![Image 169: {\displaystyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76fce82a62ed5461908f0dc8f037de4e3686b0) is a field and S{\displaystyle S}![Image 170: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) is a vector space over K{\displaystyle K}![Image 171: {\displaystyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76fce82a62ed5461908f0dc8f037de4e3686b0). It depends on authors whether it is considered as a binary operation.
