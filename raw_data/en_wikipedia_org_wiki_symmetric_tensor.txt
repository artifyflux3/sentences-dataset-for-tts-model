In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **symmetric tensor** is an [unmixed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mixed_tensor "Mixed tensor")[tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") that is invariant under a [permutation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permutation "Permutation") of its vector arguments:

![Image 1: {\displaystyle T(v_{1},v_{2},\ldots ,v_{r})=T(v_{\sigma 1},v_{\sigma 2},\ldots ,v_{\sigma r})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/103d7912c013f8cd6df2b9c5848d29e5e501989a)
for every permutation _σ_ of the symbols {1, 2, ..., _r_}. Alternatively, a symmetric tensor of order _r_ represented in coordinates as a quantity with _r_ indices satisfies

![Image 2: {\displaystyle T_{i_{1}i_{2}\cdots i_{r}}=T_{i_{\sigma 1}i_{\sigma 2}\cdots i_{\sigma r}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/495e48164fb9dd9afedee585e5d3262baf5d47e4)
The space of symmetric tensors of order _r_ on a finite-dimensional [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space")_V_ is [naturally isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_isomorphism "Natural isomorphism") to the dual of the space of [homogeneous polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_polynomial "Homogeneous polynomial") of degree _r_ on _V_. Over [fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)") of [characteristic zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_zero "Characteristic zero"), the [graded vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graded_vector_space "Graded vector space") of all symmetric tensors can be naturally identified with the [symmetric algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_algebra "Symmetric algebra") on _V_. A related concept is that of the [antisymmetric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antisymmetric_tensor "Antisymmetric tensor") or [alternating form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternating_form "Alternating form"). Symmetric tensors occur widely in [engineering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engineering "Engineering"), [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics") and [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics").

Let _V_ be a vector space and

![Image 3: {\displaystyle T\in V^{\otimes k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/60b9890a50ba0c2fcb6dc122efc3c51f83fa1bfc)
a tensor of order _k_. Then _T_ is a symmetric tensor if

![Image 4: {\displaystyle \tau _{\sigma }T=T\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d030ba6df0edc1d2227c2d6933e2fe26d26a63f)
for the [braiding maps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_product#Tensor_powers_and_braiding "Tensor product") associated to every permutation _σ_ on the symbols {1,2,...,_k_} (or equivalently for every [transposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transposition_(mathematics) "Transposition (mathematics)") on these symbols).

Given a [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)") {_e_ _i_} of _V_, any symmetric tensor _T_ of rank _k_ can be written as

![Image 5: {\displaystyle T=\sum _{i_{1},\ldots ,i_{k}=1}^{N}T_{i_{1}i_{2}\cdots i_{k}}e^{i_{1}}\otimes e^{i_{2}}\otimes \cdots \otimes e^{i_{k}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/63e19e06557a24ff978c25f62fdf27331976d863)
for some unique list of coefficients ![Image 6: {\displaystyle T_{i_{1}i_{2}\cdots i_{k}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c5212a12eeb1982ba70da42b48e86cf96842609) (the _components_ of the tensor in the basis) that are symmetric on the indices. That is to say

![Image 7: {\displaystyle T_{i_{\sigma 1}i_{\sigma 2}\cdots i_{\sigma k}}=T_{i_{1}i_{2}\cdots i_{k}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/57f760028c4ea53286d37aa235d35d992a86dd0a)
for every [permutation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permutation "Permutation")_σ_.

The space of all symmetric tensors of order _k_ defined on _V_ is often denoted by _S_ _k_(_V_) or Sym _k_(_V_). It is itself a vector space, and if _V_ has dimension _N_ then the dimension of Sym _k_(_V_) is the [binomial coefficient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binomial_coefficient "Binomial coefficient")

![Image 8: {\displaystyle \dim \operatorname {Sym} ^{k}(V)={N+k-1 \choose k}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2958fb9327078a09e0c9b1efd89edb03e1bd8cf3)
We then construct Sym(_V_) as the [direct sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Direct_sum_of_vector_spaces "Direct sum of vector spaces") of Sym _k_(_V_) for _k_ = 0,1,2,...

![Image 9: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Sym} (V)=\bigoplus _{k=0}^{\infty }\operatorname {Sym} ^{k}(V).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cf287a5d5630464a4a1255bfc4cdf28653e20516)
There are many examples of symmetric tensors. Some include, the [metric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor "Metric tensor"), ![Image 10: {\displaystyle g_{\mu \nu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5bf4140993a891f5782167dc8a0c236dc7667b8), the [Einstein tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_tensor "Einstein tensor"), ![Image 11: {\displaystyle G_{\mu \nu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ce4a5b59de7eda449c1f08ed7a84ae5de88884a) and the [Ricci tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_tensor "Ricci tensor"), ![Image 12: {\displaystyle R_{\mu \nu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a5ee22d1a052bee0115efb8b5ffdaf10b04e42aa).

Many [material properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Material_properties "Material properties") and [fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(physics) "Field (physics)") used in physics and engineering can be represented as symmetric tensor fields; for example: [stress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stress_(physics) "Stress (physics)"), [strain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strain_tensor "Strain tensor"), and [anisotropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anisotropic "Anisotropic")[conductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_resistivity_and_conductivity "Electrical resistivity and conductivity"). Also, in [diffusion MRI](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffusion_MRI "Diffusion MRI") one often uses symmetric tensors to describe diffusion in the brain or other parts of the body.

Ellipsoids are examples of [algebraic varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_varieties "Algebraic varieties"); and so, for general rank, symmetric tensors, in the guise of [homogeneous polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_polynomial "Homogeneous polynomial"), are used to define [projective varieties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_varieties "Projective varieties"), and are often studied as such.

Given a [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold")![Image 13: {\displaystyle (M,g)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68e27d2e539fd0c3a9a7efab6257abd17de7fc57) equipped with its Levi-Civita connection ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \nabla }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3d0e93b78c50237f9ea83d027e4ebbdaef354b2), the [covariant curvature tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_curvature_tensor#Coordinate_expression "Riemann curvature tensor") is a symmetric order 2 tensor over the vector space ![Image 15: {\textstyle V=\Omega ^{2}(M)=\bigwedge ^{2}T^{*}M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a6149f49ba231632316c1c58ba8901508f6bc3ed) of differential 2-forms. This corresponds to the fact that, viewing ![Image 16: {\displaystyle R_{ijk\ell }\in (T^{*}M)^{\otimes 4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b185bdb936550189644d54608ac8d373e24dd9c0), we have the symmetry ![Image 17: {\displaystyle R_{ij\,k\ell }=R_{k\ell \,ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a9f2a2167e4250ccc3c6ea3ac1c493f4682bff09) between the first and second pairs of arguments in addition to antisymmetry within each pair: ![Image 18: {\displaystyle R_{jik\ell }=-R_{ijk\ell }=R_{ij\ell k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b02dde81e7ed5e9ed116b26e06384393d1bc7124).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_tensor#cite_note-1)

Symmetric part of a tensor
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Symmetric_tensor&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Symmetric part of a tensor")]

Suppose ![Image 19: {\displaystyle V}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af0f6064540e84211d0ffe4dac72098adfa52845) is a vector space over a field of [characteristic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Characteristic_(algebra) "Characteristic (algebra)") 0. If _T_ ∈ _V_⊗_k_ is a tensor of order ![Image 20: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40), then the symmetric part of ![Image 21: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0) is the symmetric tensor defined by

![Image 22: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Sym} \,T={\frac {1}{k!}}\sum _{\sigma \in {\mathfrak {S}}_{k}}\tau _{\sigma }T,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c2edb68f357dd628606c3a48dc2e8a3d06f554d)
the summation extending over the [symmetric group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_group "Symmetric group") on _k_ symbols. In terms of a basis, and employing the [Einstein summation convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_summation_convention "Einstein summation convention"), if

![Image 23: {\displaystyle T=T_{i_{1}i_{2}\cdots i_{k}}e^{i_{1}}\otimes e^{i_{2}}\otimes \cdots \otimes e^{i_{k}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e2eeacbb7d04586cd28b0d991f48cf5b58d2736)
then

![Image 24: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Sym} \,T={\frac {1}{k!}}\sum _{\sigma \in {\mathfrak {S}}_{k}}T_{i_{\sigma 1}i_{\sigma 2}\cdots i_{\sigma k}}e^{i_{1}}\otimes e^{i_{2}}\otimes \cdots \otimes e^{i_{k}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6fe887839cf6293b37dd842a67b7b41f1039c8df)
The components of the tensor appearing on the right are often denoted by

![Image 25: {\displaystyle T_{(i_{1}i_{2}\cdots i_{k})}={\frac {1}{k!}}\sum _{\sigma \in {\mathfrak {S}}_{k}}T_{i_{\sigma 1}i_{\sigma 2}\cdots i_{\sigma k}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/310b2bc8132713d48866c55b5d0a0cac1351f37a)
with parentheses () around the indices being symmetrized. Square brackets [] are used to indicate anti-symmetrization.

If _T_ is a simple tensor, given as a pure tensor product

![Image 26: {\displaystyle T=v_{1}\otimes v_{2}\otimes \cdots \otimes v_{r}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54b34c50f2583ffd68c5bb4b66bfc821c9c4b4e4)
then the symmetric part of _T_ is the symmetric product of the factors:

![Image 27: {\displaystyle v_{1}\odot v_{2}\odot \cdots \odot v_{r}:={\frac {1}{r!}}\sum _{\sigma \in {\mathfrak {S}}_{r}}v_{\sigma 1}\otimes v_{\sigma 2}\otimes \cdots \otimes v_{\sigma r}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bbb1aa0d3c2a0627f18effeed2644387e93834ea)
In general we can turn Sym(_V_) into an [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra "Algebra") by defining the commutative and associative product ⊙.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_tensor#cite_note-Kostrikin1997-2) Given two tensors _T_ 1 ∈ Sym _k_ 1(_V_) and _T_ 2 ∈ Sym _k_ 2(_V_), we use the symmetrization operator to define:

![Image 28: {\displaystyle T_{1}\odot T_{2}=\operatorname {Sym} (T_{1}\otimes T_{2})\quad \left(\in \operatorname {Sym} ^{k_{1}+k_{2}}(V)\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ec9ad5c1e17b33fdaf915d5400069cdd132365a)
It can be verified (as is done by Kostrikin and Manin[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_tensor#cite_note-Kostrikin1997-2)) that the resulting product is in fact commutative and associative. In some cases the operator is omitted: _T_ 1 _T_ 2 = _T_ 1 ⊙ _T_ 2.

In some cases an exponential notation is used:

![Image 29: {\displaystyle v^{\odot k}=\underbrace {v\odot v\odot \cdots \odot v} _{k{\text{ times}}}=\underbrace {v\otimes v\otimes \cdots \otimes v} _{k{\text{ times}}}=v^{\otimes k}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5bc1a5880fa8a123c3a46d309020867edcd73104)
Where _v_ is a vector. Again, in some cases the ⊙ is left out:

![Image 30: {\displaystyle v^{k}=\underbrace {v\,v\,\cdots \,v} _{k{\text{ times}}}=\underbrace {v\odot v\odot \cdots \odot v} _{k{\text{ times}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f85b297d28667ae374ac644ff6650fe1f477d988)
In analogy with the theory of [symmetric matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_matrix "Symmetric matrix"), a (real) symmetric tensor of order 2 can be "diagonalized". More precisely, for any tensor _T_∈Sym 2(_V_), there is an integer _r_, non-zero unit vectors _v_ 1,...,_v_ _r_∈_V_ and weights _λ_ 1,...,_λ_ _r_ such that

![Image 31: {\displaystyle T=\sum _{i=1}^{r}\lambda _{i}\,v_{i}\otimes v_{i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3cefd72fb0950d2c885002c0fca8b5a00d7ff410)
The minimum number _r_ for which such a decomposition is possible is the (symmetric) rank of _T_. The vectors appearing in this minimal expression are the _[principal axes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principal\_axis\_theorem "Principal axis theorem")_ of the tensor, and generally have an important physical meaning. For example, the principal axes of the [inertia tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertia_tensor "Inertia tensor") define the [Poinsot's ellipsoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poinsot%27s_ellipsoid "Poinsot's ellipsoid") representing the moment of inertia. Also see [Sylvester's law of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sylvester%27s_law_of_inertia "Sylvester's law of inertia").

For symmetric tensors of arbitrary order _k_, decompositions

![Image 32: {\displaystyle T=\sum _{i=1}^{r}\lambda _{i}\,v_{i}^{\otimes k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f0d3d530c63b5dd47cf38d6fb3f1c067d37cf09c)
are also possible. The minimum number _r_ for which such a decomposition is possible is the _symmetric_[rank](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_(intrinsic_definition)#Tensor_rank "Tensor (intrinsic definition)") of _T_.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_tensor#cite_note-Comon2008-3) This minimal decomposition is called a Waring decomposition; it is a symmetric form of the [tensor rank decomposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_rank_decomposition "Tensor rank decomposition"). For second-order tensors this corresponds to the rank of the matrix representing the tensor in any basis, and it is well known that the maximum rank is equal to the dimension of the underlying vector space. However, for higher orders this need not hold: the rank can be higher than the number of dimensions in the underlying vector space. Moreover, the rank and symmetric rank of a symmetric tensor may differ.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_tensor#cite_note-4)
