**Benzene** is an [organic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound")[chemical compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_compound "Chemical compound") with the [molecular formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula#Molecular_formula "Chemical formula") C 6 H 6. The benzene [molecule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule") is composed of six [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") atoms joined in a planar hexagonal [ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(chemistry) "Ring (chemistry)") with one [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") atom attached to each. Because it contains only [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") and hydrogen atoms, benzene is classed as a [hydrocarbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon").

Benzene is a natural constituent of [petroleum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petroleum "Petroleum") and is one of the elementary [petrochemicals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petrochemical "Petrochemical"). Due to the cyclic continuous [pi bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi_bond "Pi bond") between the carbon atoms and satisfying [Hückel's rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/H%C3%BCckel%27s_rule "Hückel's rule"), benzene is classed as an [aromatic hydrocarbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aromatic_hydrocarbon "Aromatic hydrocarbon"). Benzene is a colorless and highly [flammable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combustibility_and_flammability "Combustibility and flammability") liquid with a sweet smell, and is partially responsible for the aroma of [gasoline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gasoline "Gasoline"). It is used primarily as a [precursor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precursor_(chemistry) "Precursor (chemistry)") to the manufacture of chemicals with more complex structures, such as [ethylbenzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylbenzene "Ethylbenzene") and [cumene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cumene "Cumene"), of which billions of kilograms are produced annually. Although benzene is a major [industrial chemical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_industry "Chemical industry"), it finds limited use in consumer items because of its toxicity. Benzene is a [volatile organic compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volatile_organic_compound "Volatile organic compound").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-14)

Benzene is classified as a [carcinogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carcinogen "Carcinogen"). Its particular effects on [human health](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Human_health "Human health"), such as the long-term results of accidental exposure, have been reported on by news organizations such as _[The New York Times](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_New\_York\_Times "The New York Times")_. For instance, a 2022 article stated that benzene contamination in the [Boston metropolitan area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boston_metropolitan_area "Boston metropolitan area") caused hazardous conditions in multiple places, with the publication noting that the compound may eventually cause [leukemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leukemia "Leukemia") in some individuals.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-15)

The word "_benzene_" derives from "_gum benzoin_" ([benzoin resin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzoin_(resin) "Benzoin (resin)")), an aromatic resin known since ancient times in Southeast Asia, and later to European pharmacists and perfumers in the 16th century via trade routes.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-16) An [acidic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid "Acid") material was derived from benzoin by [sublimation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sublimation_(phase_transition) "Sublimation (phase transition)"), and named "flowers of benzoin", or benzoic acid. The hydrocarbon derived from benzoic acid thus acquired the name benzin, benzol, or benzene.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-rocke-17)[Michael Faraday](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Faraday "Michael Faraday") first isolated and identified benzene in 1825 from the oily residue derived from the production of illuminating gas, giving it the name _bicarburet of hydrogen_.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-18)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-19) In 1833, [Eilhard Mitscherlich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eilhard_Mitscherlich "Eilhard Mitscherlich") produced it by [distilling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Distillation "Distillation")[benzoic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzoic_acid "Benzoic acid") (from [gum benzoin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzoin_(resin) "Benzoin (resin)")) and [lime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_oxide "Calcium oxide"). He gave the compound the name _benzin_.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-20) In 1836, the French chemist [Auguste Laurent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auguste_Laurent "Auguste Laurent") named the substance "phène";[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-21) this word has become the root of the English word "[phenol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenol "Phenol")", which is [hydroxylated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxylation "Hydroxylation") benzene, and "[phenyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenyl_group "Phenyl group")", the radical formed by abstraction of a hydrogen atom from benzene.

In 1845, [Charles Blachford Mansfield](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Blachford_Mansfield "Charles Blachford Mansfield"), working under [August Wilhelm von Hofmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/August_Wilhelm_von_Hofmann "August Wilhelm von Hofmann"), isolated benzene from [coal tar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal_tar "Coal tar").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-22) Four years later, Mansfield began the first industrial-scale production of benzene, based on the coal-tar method.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-23)[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-24) Gradually, the sense developed among chemists that a number of substances were chemically related to benzene, comprising a diverse chemical family. In 1855, Hofmann was the first to apply the word "[aromatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aromaticity "Aromaticity")" to designate this family relationship, after a characteristic property of many of its members.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-25) In 1997, benzene was [detected in deep space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_interstellar_and_circumstellar_molecules "List of interstellar and circumstellar molecules").[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-Cernicharo_546-26)

The [empirical formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empirical_formula "Empirical formula") for benzene was long known, but its highly [polyunsaturated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturated_and_unsaturated_compounds "Saturated and unsaturated compounds") structure, with just one [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") atom for each [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") atom, was challenging to determine. [Archibald Scott Couper](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archibald_Scott_Couper "Archibald Scott Couper") in 1858 and [Johann Josef Loschmidt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Josef_Loschmidt "Johann Josef Loschmidt") in 1861[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-37) suggested possible structures that contained multiple double bonds or multiple rings, but in these years very little was known about aromatic chemistry, and so chemists were unable to adduce appropriate evidence to favor any particular formula.

But many chemists had begun to work on aromatic substances, especially in Germany, and relevant data was coming fast. In 1865, the German chemist [Friedrich August Kekulé](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_August_Kekul%C3%A9 "Friedrich August Kekulé") published a paper in French (for he was then teaching in Francophone Belgium) suggesting that the structure contained a ring of six carbon atoms with alternating single and double bonds. The next year he published a much longer paper in German on the same subject.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-Kekul%C3%A91865-31)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-38) Kekulé used evidence that had accumulated in the intervening years—namely, that there always appeared to be only one [isomer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomer "Isomer") of any [monoderivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative_(chemistry) "Derivative (chemistry)") of benzene, and that there always appeared to be exactly three isomers of every disubstituted derivative—now understood to correspond to the ortho, meta, and para patterns of [arene substitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arene_substitution_pattern "Arene substitution pattern")—to argue in support of his proposed structure.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-39) Kekulé's symmetrical ring could explain these curious facts, as well as benzene's 1:1 carbon-hydrogen ratio.

*   [![Image 31: Kekulé's 1872 modification of his 1865 theory, illustrating rapid alternation of double bonds[note 2]](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f8/Historic_Benzene_Formulae_Kekul%C3%A9_%28original%29.png/960px-Historic_Benzene_Formulae_Kekul%C3%A9_%28original%29.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Historic_Benzene_Formulae_Kekul%C3%A9_(original).png "Kekulé's 1872 modification of his 1865 theory, illustrating rapid alternation of double bonds[note 2]")

Kekulé's 1872 modification of his 1865 theory, illustrating rapid alternation of double bonds[[note 2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-40)

The new understanding of benzene, and hence of all aromatic compounds, proved to be so important for both pure and applied chemistry that in 1890 the [German Chemical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Chemical_Society "German Chemical Society") organized an elaborate appreciation in Kekulé's honor, celebrating the twenty-fifth anniversary of his first benzene paper. Here Kekulé spoke of the creation of the theory. He said that he had discovered the ring shape of the benzene molecule after having a reverie or day-dream of a snake biting its own tail (a symbol in ancient cultures known as the [ouroboros](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ouroboros "Ouroboros")).[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-41) This vision, he said, came to him after years of studying the nature of carbon-carbon bonds. This was seven years after he had solved the problem of how carbon atoms could bond to up to four other atoms at the same time. Curiously, a similar, humorous depiction of benzene had appeared in 1886 in a pamphlet entitled _Berichte der Durstigen Chemischen Gesellschaft_ (Journal of the Thirsty Chemical Society), a parody of the _Berichte der Deutschen Chemischen Gesellschaft_, only the parody had monkeys seizing each other in a circle, rather than snakes as in Kekulé's anecdote.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-42) Some historians have suggested that the parody was a lampoon of the snake anecdote, possibly already well known through oral transmission even if it had not yet appeared in print.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-rocke-17) Kekulé's 1890 speech[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-43) in which this anecdote appeared has been translated into English.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-44) If the anecdote is the memory of a real event, circumstances mentioned in the story suggest that it must have happened early in 1862.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-45)

In 1929, the cyclic nature of benzene was finally confirmed by the [crystallographer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Crystallography "Crystallography")[Kathleen Lonsdale](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kathleen_Lonsdale "Kathleen Lonsdale") using [X-ray diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_diffraction "X-ray diffraction") methods.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-46)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-47) Using large crystals of [hexamethylbenzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexamethylbenzene "Hexamethylbenzene"), a benzene derivative with the same core of six carbon atoms, Lonsdale obtained diffraction patterns. Through calculating more than thirty parameters, Lonsdale demonstrated that the benzene ring could not be anything but a flat hexagon, and provided accurate distances for all carbon-carbon bonds in the molecule.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-48)

The German chemist [Wilhelm Körner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_K%C3%B6rner "Wilhelm Körner") suggested the prefixes ortho-, meta-, para- to distinguish di-substituted benzene derivatives in 1867; however, he did not use the prefixes to distinguish the relative positions of the substituents on a benzene ring.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-49)[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-50) It was the German chemist [Carl Gräbe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Gr%C3%A4be "Carl Gräbe") who, in 1869, first used the prefixes ortho-, meta-, para- to denote specific relative locations of the substituents on a di-substituted aromatic ring (viz, naphthalene).[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-51) In 1870, the German chemist [Viktor Meyer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viktor_Meyer "Viktor Meyer") first applied Gräbe's nomenclature to benzene.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-52)

In 1903, [Ludwig Roselius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Roselius "Ludwig Roselius") popularized the use of benzene to [decaffeinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decaffeination "Decaffeination")[coffee](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coffee "Coffee"). This discovery led to the production of [Sanka](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanka "Sanka"). This process was later discontinued. Benzene was historically used as a significant component in many consumer products such as [liquid wrench](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Penetrating_oil "Penetrating oil"), several [paint strippers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paint_stripper "Paint stripper"), [rubber cements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubber_cement "Rubber cement"), spot removers, and other products. Manufacture of some of these benzene-containing formulations ceased in about 1950, although Liquid Wrench continued to contain significant amounts of benzene until the late 1970s.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-53)

Trace amounts of benzene are found in petroleum and coal. It is a byproduct of the incomplete combustion of many materials. For commercial use, until [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), much of benzene was obtained as a by-product of [coke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coke_(fuel) "Coke (fuel)") production (or "coke-oven light oil") for the [steel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steel "Steel") industry. However, in the 1950s, increased demand for benzene, especially from the growing [polymers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymer "Polymer") industry, necessitated the production of benzene from petroleum. Today, most benzene comes from the [petrochemical industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petrochemical_industry "Petrochemical industry"), with only a small fraction being produced from coal.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-Ullmann-54) Benzene has been detected on [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars").[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-NYT-20180607-55)[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-SCI-20180608a-56)[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-SCI-20180608c-57)

[![Image 32](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9a/Benzene_Representations.svg/500px-Benzene_Representations.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Benzene_Representations.svg)

The various representations of benzene.

[X-ray diffraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray_diffraction "X-ray diffraction") shows that all six carbon-carbon bonds in benzene are of the same length, at 140 [picometres](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Picometre "Picometre") (pm).[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-58) The C–C [bond lengths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bond_length "Bond length") are greater than a double bond (135 pm) but shorter than a single bond (147 pm). This intermediate distance is caused by electron [delocalization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delocalized_electron "Delocalized electron"): the electrons for C=C bonding are distributed equally between each of the six carbon atoms. Benzene has 6 hydrogen atoms, fewer than the corresponding parent [alkane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkane "Alkane"), [hexane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hexane "Hexane"), which has 14. Benzene and [cyclohexane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclohexane "Cyclohexane") have a similar structure; only the ring of delocalized electrons and the loss of one hydrogen per carbon distinguishes it from cyclohexane. The molecule is planar.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-59) The molecular orbital description involves the formation of three delocalized [π orbitals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pi_bond "Pi bond") spanning all six carbon atoms, while the valence bond description involves a superposition of [resonance structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resonance_(chemistry) "Resonance (chemistry)").[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-60)[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-61)[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-62)[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-63) It is likely that this stability contributes to the peculiar molecular and chemical properties known as [aromaticity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aromaticity "Aromaticity"). To reflect the delocalized nature of the bonding, benzene is often depicted with a circle inside a hexagonal arrangement of carbon atoms.

Derivatives of benzene occur sufficiently often as a component of organic molecules, so much so that the [Unicode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode "Unicode") Consortium has allocated a symbol in the [Miscellaneous Technical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Miscellaneous_Technical_(Unicode) "Miscellaneous Technical (Unicode)") block with the code U+232C (⌬) to represent it with three double bonds,[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-64) and U+23E3 (⏣) for a delocalized version.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-65)

Benzene derivatives
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Benzene&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Benzene derivatives")]

Many important chemical compounds are derived from benzene by replacing one or more of its hydrogen atoms with another [functional group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_group "Functional group"). Examples of simple benzene derivatives are [phenol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenol "Phenol"), [toluene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toluene "Toluene"), and [aniline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aniline "Aniline"), abbreviated PhOH, PhMe, and PhNH 2, respectively. Linking benzene rings gives [biphenyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biphenyl "Biphenyl"), C 6 H 5–C 6 H 5. Further loss of hydrogen gives "fused" aromatic hydrocarbons, such as [naphthalene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naphthalene "Naphthalene"), [anthracene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthracene "Anthracene"), [phenanthrene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenanthrene "Phenanthrene"), and [pyrene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrene "Pyrene"). The limit of the fusion process is the hydrogen-free allotrope of carbon, [graphite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graphite "Graphite").

In [heterocycles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heterocyclic_chemistry "Heterocyclic chemistry"), carbon atoms in the benzene ring are replaced with other elements. The most important variations contain [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen"). Replacing one CH with N gives the compound [pyridine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyridine "Pyridine"), C 5 H 5 N. Although benzene and pyridine are _structurally_ related, benzene cannot be converted into pyridine. Replacement of a second CH bond with N gives, depending on the location of the second N, [pyridazine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyridazine "Pyridazine"), [pyrimidine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrimidine "Pyrimidine"), or [pyrazine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrazine "Pyrazine").[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-66)

Four chemical processes contribute to industrial benzene production: [catalytic reforming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalytic_reforming "Catalytic reforming"), [toluene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toluene "Toluene") hydrodealkylation, toluene disproportionation, and [steam cracking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steam_cracking "Steam cracking") etc. According to the [ATSDR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ATSDR "ATSDR") Toxicological Profile for benzene, between 1978 and 1981, catalytic reformates accounted for approximately 44–50% of the total U.S. benzene production.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-Ullmann-54)

### Catalytic reforming

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Benzene&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Catalytic reforming")]

In catalytic reforming, a mixture of [hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbon "Hydrocarbon") with boiling points between 60 and 200°C is blended with [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") gas and then exposed to a [bifunctional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bifunctional "Bifunctional")[platinum chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platinum(II)_chloride "Platinum(II) chloride") or [rhenium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhenium "Rhenium") chloride [catalyst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalyst "Catalyst") at 500–525°C and pressures ranging from 8–50 atm. Under these conditions, [aliphatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aliphatic "Aliphatic") hydrocarbons form rings and lose hydrogen to become aromatic hydrocarbons. The aromatic products of the reaction are then separated from the reaction mixture (or reformate) by [extraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liquid-liquid_extraction "Liquid-liquid extraction") with any one of a number of [solvents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvent "Solvent"), including [diethylene glycol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diethylene_glycol "Diethylene glycol") or [sulfolane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfolane "Sulfolane"), and benzene is then separated from the other aromatics by distillation. The extraction step of aromatics from the reformate is designed to produce aromatics with lowest non-aromatic components. Recovery of the aromatics, commonly referred to as [BTX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BTX_(chemistry) "BTX (chemistry)") (benzene, toluene and xylene isomers), involves such extraction and distillation steps.

In similar fashion to this catalytic reforming, [UOP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UOP_LLC "UOP LLC") and [BP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BP "BP") commercialized a method from LPG (mainly propane and butane) to aromatics.

### Toluene hydrodealkylation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Benzene&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Toluene hydrodealkylation")]

Toluene [hydrodealkylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrodealkylation "Hydrodealkylation") converts [toluene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toluene "Toluene") to benzene. In this hydrogen-intensive process, toluene is mixed with hydrogen, then passed over a [chromium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromium "Chromium"), [molybdenum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molybdenum "Molybdenum"), or [platinum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platinum "Platinum")[oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxide "Oxide") catalyst at 500–650°C and 20–60 atm pressure. Sometimes, higher temperatures are used instead of a catalyst (at the similar reaction condition). Under these conditions, toluene undergoes dealkylation to benzene and [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane"):

C 6 H 5 CH 3 + H 2 → C 6 H 6 + CH 4
This irreversible reaction is accompanied by an equilibrium side reaction that produces [biphenyl](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biphenyl "Biphenyl") (diphenyl) at higher temperature:

If the raw material stream contains much non-aromatic components (paraffins or naphthenes), those are likely decomposed to lower hydrocarbons such as methane, which increases the consumption of hydrogen.

A typical reaction yield exceeds 95%. Sometimes, [xylenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xylene "Xylene") and heavier aromatics are used in place of toluene, with similar efficiency.

This is often called "on-purpose" methodology to produce benzene, compared to conventional BTX (benzene-toluene-xylene) extraction processes.

### Toluene disproportionation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Benzene&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Toluene disproportionation")]

Toluene [disproportionation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disproportionation "Disproportionation") (**TDP**) is the conversion of toluene to benzene and [xylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xylene "Xylene").[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-67)

Given that demand for _para_-xylene ([_p_-xylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P-Xylene "P-Xylene")) substantially exceeds demand for other xylene isomers, a refinement of the TDP process called **Selective TDP** (STDP) may be used. In this process, the xylene stream exiting the TDP unit is approximately 90% _p_-xylene. In some systems, even the benzene-to-xylenes ratio is modified to favor xylenes.

[Steam cracking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steam_cracking "Steam cracking") is the process for producing [ethylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylene "Ethylene") and other [alkenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkene "Alkene") from [aliphatic hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aliphatic_compound "Aliphatic compound"). Depending on the feedstock used to produce the olefins, steam cracking can produce a benzene-rich liquid by-product called _[pyrolysis gasoline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pyrolysis\_gasoline "Pyrolysis gasoline")_. Pyrolysis gasoline can be blended with other hydrocarbons as a gasoline additive, or routed through an extraction process to recover [BTX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BTX_(chemistry) "BTX (chemistry)") aromatics (benzene, toluene and xylenes).

Although of no commercial significance, many other routes to benzene exist. [Phenol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenol "Phenol") and [halobenzenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halobenzene "Halobenzene") can be reduced with metals. [Benzoic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzoic_acid "Benzoic acid") and its salts undergo [decarboxylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decarboxylation "Decarboxylation") to benzene. The reaction of the [diazonium compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diazonium_compound "Diazonium compound") derived from [aniline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aniline "Aniline") with [hypophosphorus acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypophosphorus_acid "Hypophosphorus acid") gives benzene. [Alkyne trimerisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkyne_trimerisation "Alkyne trimerisation") of [acetylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene "Acetylene") gives benzene. Complete [decarboxylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Decarboxylation "Decarboxylation") of [mellitic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mellitic_acid "Mellitic acid") gives benzene.

Benzene is used mainly as an intermediate to make other chemicals, above all [ethylbenzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylbenzene "Ethylbenzene") (and other [alkylbenzenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkylbenzenes "Alkylbenzenes")), [cumene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cumene "Cumene"), [cyclohexane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclohexane "Cyclohexane"), and [nitrobenzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrobenzene "Nitrobenzene"). In 1988, it was reported that two-thirds of all chemicals on the [American Chemical Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Chemical_Society "American Chemical Society")'s lists contained at least one benzene ring.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-nyt-68) More than half of the entire benzene production is processed into ethylbenzene, a precursor to [styrene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Styrene "Styrene"), which is used to make polymers and plastics like [polystyrene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polystyrene "Polystyrene"). Some 20% of the benzene production is used to manufacture cumene, which is needed to produce [phenol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenol "Phenol") and acetone for resins and adhesives. [Cyclohexane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclohexane "Cyclohexane") consumes around 10% of the world's benzene production; it is primarily used in the manufacture of nylon fibers, which are processed into textiles and engineering plastics. Smaller amounts of benzene are used to make some types of [rubbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubber "Rubber"), [lubricants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lubricant "Lubricant"), [dyes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dye "Dye"), [detergents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detergent "Detergent"), [drugs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drug "Drug"), [explosives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explosive "Explosive"), and [pesticides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pesticide "Pesticide"). In 2013, the biggest consumer country of benzene was China, followed by the USA. Benzene production is currently expanding in the Middle East and in Africa, whereas production capacities in Western Europe and North America are stagnating.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-Ceresana-69)

[Toluene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toluene "Toluene") is now often used as a substitute for benzene, for instance as a fuel additive. The solvent-properties of the two are similar, but toluene is less toxic and has a wider liquid range. Toluene is also processed into benzene.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-CeresanaB-70)

### Component of gasoline

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Benzene&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Component of gasoline")]

As a [gasoline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gasoline "Gasoline") (petrol) additive, benzene increases the [octane rating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octane_rating "Octane rating") and reduces [knocking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Engine_knocking "Engine knocking"). As a consequence, gasoline often contained several percent benzene before the 1950s, when [tetraethyl lead](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tetraethyl_lead "Tetraethyl lead") replaced it as the most widely used antiknock additive. With the global phaseout of leaded gasoline, benzene has made a comeback as a gasoline additive in some nations. In the [United States](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States "United States"), concern over its negative health effects and the possibility of benzene entering the [groundwater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Groundwater "Groundwater") has led to stringent regulation of gasoline's benzene content, with limits typically around 1%.[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-71) European petrol specifications now contain the same 1% limit on benzene content. The [United States Environmental Protection Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency "United States Environmental Protection Agency") introduced new regulations in 2011 that lowered the benzene content in gasoline to 0.62%.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-72)

In some European languages, the word for petroleum or gasoline is an exact cognate of "benzene". For instance all [Scandinavian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Germanic_languages "North Germanic languages") languages refer to gasoline as 'bensin'. In [Catalan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalan_language "Catalan language") the word 'benzina' can be used for gasoline, though now it is relatively rare. In [Polish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polish_language "Polish language") the word 'benzyna' is also used to mean gasoline or petrol.

The most common reactions of benzene involve substitution of a proton by other groups.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-StranksEtAl1970-73)[Electrophilic aromatic substitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrophilic_aromatic_substitution "Electrophilic aromatic substitution") is a general method of derivatizing benzene. Benzene is sufficiently [nucleophilic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleophile "Nucleophile") that it undergoes substitution by [acylium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acylium "Acylium") ions and alkyl [carbocations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbocation "Carbocation") to give substituted derivatives.

[![Image 34](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/57/OChem-Mech-ElectrophilicAromaticSubstitution-General.png/500px-OChem-Mech-ElectrophilicAromaticSubstitution-General.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:OChem-Mech-ElectrophilicAromaticSubstitution-General.png)

Electrophilic aromatic substitution of benzene

The most widely practiced example of this reaction is the [ethylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethylation "Ethylation") of benzene.

[![Image 35](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/EtC6H5route.png/330px-EtC6H5route.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:EtC6H5route.png)
Approximately 24,700,000 tons were produced in 1999.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-74) Highly instructive but of far less industrial significance is the [Friedel-Crafts alkylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedel-Crafts_alkylation "Friedel-Crafts alkylation") of benzene (and many other aromatic rings) using an [alkyl halide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkyl_halide "Alkyl halide") in the presence of a strong Lewis acid catalyst. Similarly, the [Friedel-Crafts acylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedel-Crafts_acylation "Friedel-Crafts acylation") is a related example of [electrophilic aromatic substitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrophilic_aromatic_substitution "Electrophilic aromatic substitution"). The reaction involves the [acylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acylation "Acylation") of benzene (or many other aromatic rings) with an [acyl chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acyl_chloride "Acyl chloride") using a strong [Lewis acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lewis_acid "Lewis acid")[catalyst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalyst "Catalyst") such as [aluminium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aluminium_chloride "Aluminium chloride") or [Iron(III) chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron(III)_chloride "Iron(III) chloride").

[![Image 36](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8b/Benzen_acylowany.svg/330px-Benzen_acylowany.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Benzen_acylowany.svg)

Friedel-Crafts acylation of benzene by acetyl chloride

### Sulfonation, chlorination, nitration

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Benzene&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Sulfonation, chlorination, nitration")]

Using electrophilic aromatic substitution, many functional groups are introduced onto the benzene framework. [Sulfonation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aromatic_sulfonation "Aromatic sulfonation") of benzene involves the use of [oleum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oleum "Oleum"), a mixture of sulfuric acid with [sulfur trioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_trioxide "Sulfur trioxide"). Sulfonated benzene derivatives are useful [detergents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Detergent "Detergent"). In [nitration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitration "Nitration"), benzene reacts with nitronium ions (NO 2+), which is a strong electrophile produced by combining sulfuric and nitric acids. [Nitrobenzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrobenzene "Nitrobenzene") is the precursor to [aniline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aniline "Aniline"). Chlorination is achieved with chlorine to give [chlorobenzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorobenzene "Chlorobenzene") in the presence of a Lewis acid catalyst such as aluminium tri-chloride.

Via [hydrogenation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogenation "Hydrogenation"), benzene and its derivatives convert to [cyclohexane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclohexane "Cyclohexane") and derivatives. This reaction is achieved by the use of high pressures of [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") in the presence of [heterogeneous catalysts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heterogeneous_catalyst "Heterogeneous catalyst"), such as finely divided [nickel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nickel "Nickel"). Whereas alkenes can be hydrogenated near room temperatures, benzene and related compounds are more reluctant substrates, requiring temperatures >100°C. This reaction is practiced on a large scale industrially. In the absence of the catalyst, benzene is impervious to hydrogen. Hydrogenation cannot be stopped to give cyclohexene or cyclohexadienes as these are superior substrates. [Birch reduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birch_reduction "Birch reduction"), a non catalytic process, however selectively hydrogenates benzene to the diene.

Benzene is an excellent [ligand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ligand "Ligand") in the [organometallic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_(chemistry) "Complex (chemistry)") chemistry of low-valent metals. Important examples include the sandwich and half-sandwich complexes, respectively, [Cr(C 6 H 6)2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bis(benzene)chromium "Bis(benzene)chromium") and [[RuCl 2(C 6 H 6)]2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/(Benzene)ruthenium_dichloride_dimer "(Benzene)ruthenium dichloride dimer").

[![Image 37](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Benzol.JPG/250px-Benzol.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Benzol.JPG)

A bottle of benzene. The warnings show benzene is a toxic and flammable liquid.

Benzene is classified as a [carcinogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carcinogen "Carcinogen"), which increases the risk of [cancer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cancer "Cancer") and other illnesses, and is also a notorious cause of [bone marrow failure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bone_marrow_failure "Bone marrow failure"). Substantial quantities of epidemiologic, clinical, and laboratory data link benzene to aplastic anemia, acute [leukemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leukemia "Leukemia"), bone marrow abnormalities and cardiovascular disease.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-75)[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-76)[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-Bard_2014_Jun_16;9(6)-77) The specific hematologic malignancies that benzene is associated with include: acute myeloid leukemia (AML), aplastic anemia, myelodysplastic syndrome (MDS), acute lymphoblastic leukemia (ALL), and chronic myeloid leukemia (CML).[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-Smith_2010_133%E2%80%9348-78)

Carcinogenic activity of benzene was discovered by Swedish pharmacologist [C. G. Santesson](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Carl_Gustaf_Santesson_(farmakolog)&action=edit&redlink=1 "Carl Gustaf Santesson (farmakolog) (page does not exist)")[[sv](https://sv.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Gustaf_Santesson_(farmakolog) "sv:Carl Gustaf Santesson (farmakolog)")] in 1897 on female workers of a tire-making factory.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-79)[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-80) The [American Petroleum Institute](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/American_Petroleum_Institute "American Petroleum Institute") (API) stated in 1948 that "it is generally considered that the only absolutely safe concentration for benzene is zero".[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-81) There is no safe exposure level; even tiny amounts can cause harm.[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-82) The [US Department of Health and Human Services](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/US_Department_of_Health_and_Human_Services "US Department of Health and Human Services") (DHHS) classifies benzene as a human carcinogen. Long-term exposure to excessive levels of benzene in the air causes leukemia, a potentially fatal cancer of the blood-forming organs. In particular, [acute myeloid leukemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acute_myeloid_leukemia "Acute myeloid leukemia") or [acute nonlymphocytic leukemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acute_nonlymphocytic_leukemia "Acute nonlymphocytic leukemia") (AML & ANLL) is caused by benzene.[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-83) IARC rated benzene as "known to be carcinogenic to humans" ([Group 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IARC_group_1_Carcinogens "IARC group 1 Carcinogens")).

As benzene is ubiquitous in gasoline and hydrocarbon fuels that are in use everywhere, human exposure to benzene is a global health problem. Benzene targets the liver, kidney, lung, heart and brain and can cause [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA "DNA") strand breaks and [chromosomal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromosomal "Chromosomal") damage, hence is [teratogenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teratology#Teratogenesis "Teratology") and [mutagenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutagen "Mutagen"). Benzene causes cancer in animals including humans. Benzene has been shown to cause cancer in both sexes of multiple species of laboratory animals exposed via various routes.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-84)[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-85)

Exposure to benzene
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Benzene&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Exposure to benzene")]

According to the [Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agency_for_Toxic_Substances_and_Disease_Registry "Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry") (ATSDR) (2007), benzene is both a synthetically made and naturally occurring chemical from processes that include: volcanic eruptions, wild fires, synthesis of chemicals such as [phenol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenol "Phenol"), production of [synthetic fibers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synthetic_fiber "Synthetic fiber"), and fabrication of [rubbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rubber "Rubber"), [lubricants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lubricant "Lubricant"), [pesticides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pesticide "Pesticide"), medications, and [dyes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dye "Dye"). The major sources of benzene exposure are [tobacco](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tobacco "Tobacco") smoke, automobile service stations, exhaust from motor vehicles, and industrial emissions; however, ingestion and dermal absorption of benzene can also occur through contact with contaminated water. Benzene is hepatically metabolized and excreted in the [urine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine "Urine"). Measurement of air and water levels of benzene is accomplished through collection via [activated charcoal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Activated_charcoal "Activated charcoal") tubes, which are then analyzed with a [gas chromatograph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_chromatograph "Gas chromatograph"). The measurement of benzene in humans can be accomplished via [urine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urine_test "Urine test"), [blood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_test "Blood test"), and [breath tests](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breath_test "Breath test"); however, all of these have their limitations because benzene is rapidly metabolized in the human body.[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-86)

Exposure to benzene may lead progressively to aplastic [anemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anemia "Anemia"), [leukaemia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leukaemia "Leukaemia"), and [multiple myeloma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiple_myeloma "Multiple myeloma").[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-87)

[OSHA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration "Occupational Safety and Health Administration") regulates levels of benzene in the workplace.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-88) The maximum allowable amount of benzene in workroom air during an 8-hour workday, 40-hour workweek is 1 ppm. As benzene can cause [cancer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cancer "Cancer"), [NIOSH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health "National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health") recommends that all workers wear special [breathing equipment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-contained_breathing_apparatus "Self-contained breathing apparatus") when they are likely to be exposed to benzene at levels exceeding the recommended (8-hour) exposure limit of 0.1 ppm.[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-89)

### Benzene exposure limits

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Benzene&action=edit&section=23 "Edit section: Benzene exposure limits")]

The [United States Environmental Protection Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Environmental_Protection_Agency "United States Environmental Protection Agency") has set a [maximum contaminant level](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maximum_contaminant_level "Maximum contaminant level") for benzene in [drinking water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drinking_water "Drinking water") at 0.005 mg/L (5 ppb), as promulgated via the U.S. National Primary Drinking Water Regulations.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-90) This regulation is based on preventing benzene [leukemogenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leukemogenesis "Leukemogenesis"). The maximum contaminant level goal ([MCLG](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=MCLG&action=edit&redlink=1 "MCLG (page does not exist)")), a nonenforceable health goal that would allow an adequate margin of safety for the prevention of adverse effects, is zero benzene concentration in drinking water. The EPA requires that spills or accidental releases into the environment of 10 pounds (4.5 kg) or more of benzene be reported.

The U.S. [Occupational Safety and Health Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration "Occupational Safety and Health Administration") (OSHA) has set a permissible exposure limit of 1 part of benzene per million parts of air (1 ppm) in the workplace during an 8-hour workday, 40-hour workweek. The short term exposure limit for airborne benzene is 5 ppm for 15 minutes.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-OSHASampling2015-91) These legal limits were based on studies demonstrating compelling evidence of health risk to workers exposed to benzene. The risk from exposure to 1 ppm for a working lifetime has been estimated as 5 excess leukemia deaths per 1,000 employees exposed. (This estimate assumes no threshold for benzene's carcinogenic effects.) OSHA has also established an action level of 0.5 ppm to encourage even lower exposures in the workplace.[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-92)

The U.S. [National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health "National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health") (NIOSH) revised the [Immediately Dangerous to Life and Health](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immediately_Dangerous_to_Life_and_Health "Immediately Dangerous to Life and Health") (IDLH) concentration for benzene to 500 ppm. The current NIOSH definition for an IDLH condition, as given in the NIOSH Respirator Selection Logic, is one that poses a threat of exposure to airborne contaminants when that exposure is likely to cause death or immediate or delayed permanent adverse health effects or prevent escape from such an environment.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-NIOSH2004-93) The purpose of establishing an IDLH value is (1) to ensure that the worker can escape from a given contaminated environment in the event of failure of the respiratory protection equipment and (2) is considered a maximum level above which only a highly reliable [breathing apparatus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-contained_breathing_apparatus "Self-contained breathing apparatus") providing maximum worker protection is permitted.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-NIOSH2004-93)[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-94) In September 1995, NIOSH issued a new policy for developing [recommended exposure limits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recommended_exposure_limit "Recommended exposure limit") (RELs) for substances, including carcinogens. As benzene can cause cancer, NIOSH recommends that all workers wear special breathing equipment when they are likely to be exposed to benzene at levels exceeding the REL (10-hour) of 0.1 ppm.[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-95) The NIOSH short-term exposure limit (STEL – 15 min) is 1 ppm.

American Conference of Governmental Industrial Hygienists (ACGIH) adopted Threshold Limit Values (TLVs) for benzene at 0.5 ppm TWA and 2.5 ppm STEL.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-OSHASampling2015-91)

#### Biomarkers of exposure

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Benzene&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: Biomarkers of exposure")]

Several tests can determine exposure to benzene. Benzene itself can be measured in breath, blood or urine, but such testing is usually limited to the first 24 hours post-exposure due to the relatively rapid removal of the chemical by exhalation or biotransformation. Most people in developed countries have measureable baseline levels of benzene and other aromatic petroleum hydrocarbons in their blood. In the body, benzene is enzymatically converted to a series of oxidation products including [muconic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muconic_acid "Muconic acid"), [phenylmercapturic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Phenylmercapturic_acid&action=edit&redlink=1 "Phenylmercapturic acid (page does not exist)"), [phenol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenol "Phenol"), [catechol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catechol "Catechol"), [hydroquinone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroquinone "Hydroquinone") and [1,2,4-trihydroxybenzene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroxyquinol "Hydroxyquinol"). Most of these metabolites have some value as biomarkers of human exposure, since they accumulate in the urine in proportion to the extent and duration of exposure, and they may still be present for some days after exposure has ceased. The current ACGIH biological exposure limits for occupational exposure are 500 μg/g creatinine for muconic acid and 25 μg/g creatinine for phenylmercapturic acid in an end-of-shift urine specimen.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-96)[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-97)[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-98)[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-99)

Even if it is not a common substrate for metabolism, benzene can be oxidized by both [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria "Bacteria") and [eukaryotes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryote "Eukaryote"). In bacteria, [dioxygenase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dioxygenase "Dioxygenase") enzyme can add an [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") to the ring, and the unstable product is immediately reduced (by [NADH](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NADH "NADH")) to a cyclic [diol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diol "Diol") with two double bonds, breaking the aromaticity. Next, the diol is newly reduced by NADH to [catechol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catechol "Catechol"). The catechol is then metabolized to [acetyl CoA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetyl_CoA "Acetyl CoA") and [succinyl CoA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Succinyl_CoA "Succinyl CoA"), used by organisms mainly in the [citric acid cycle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citric_acid_cycle "Citric acid cycle") for energy production.

Human metabolism of benzene is complex and begins in the liver. Several enzymes are involved. These include [cytochrome P450](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytochrome_P450 "Cytochrome P450") 2E1 (CYP2E1), quinine oxidoreductase (NQ01 or DT-diaphorase or [NAD(P)H dehydrogenase (quinone 1)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NAD(P)H_dehydrogenase_(quinone_1) "NAD(P)H dehydrogenase (quinone 1)")), GSH, and myeloperoxidase (MPO). CYP2E1 is involved at multiple steps: converting benzene to [oxepin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxepin "Oxepin") (benzene oxide), [phenol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenol "Phenol") to [hydroquinone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydroquinone "Hydroquinone"), and hydroquinone to both benzenetriol and [catechol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catechol "Catechol"). Hydroquinone, benzenetriol and catechol are converted to polyphenols. In the bone marrow, MPO converts these polyphenols to benzoquinones. These intermediates and metabolites induce genotoxicity by multiple mechanisms including inhibition of [topoisomerase II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_II_topoisomerase "Type II topoisomerase") (which maintains chromosome structure), disruption of [microtubules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microtubules "Microtubules") (which maintains cellular structure and organization), generation of oxygen free radicals (unstable species) that may lead to point mutations, increasing oxidative stress, inducing [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA "DNA") strand breaks, and altering DNA [methylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methylation "Methylation") (which can affect gene expression). NQ01 and GSH shift metabolism away from toxicity. NQ01 metabolizes [benzoquinone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzoquinone "Benzoquinone") toward [polyphenols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyphenols "Polyphenols") (counteracting the effect of MPO). GSH is involved with the formation of [phenylmercapturic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Phenylmercapturic_acid&action=edit&redlink=1 "Phenylmercapturic acid (page does not exist)").[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-Smith_2010_133%E2%80%9348-78)[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-100)

Genetic polymorphisms in these enzymes may induce loss of function or gain of function. For example, mutations in CYP2E1 increase activity and result in increased generation of toxic metabolites. NQ01 mutations result in loss of function and may result in decreased detoxification. Myeloperoxidase mutations result in loss of function and may result in decreased generation of toxic metabolites. GSH mutations or deletions result in loss of function and result in decreased detoxification. These genes may be targets for genetic screening for susceptibility to benzene toxicity.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-101)

#### Molecular toxicology

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Benzene&action=edit&section=27 "Edit section: Molecular toxicology")]

The paradigm of toxicological assessment of benzene is shifting towards the domain of molecular toxicology as it allows understanding of fundamental biological mechanisms in a better way. [Glutathione](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glutathione "Glutathione") seems to play an important role by protecting against benzene-induced DNA breaks and it is being identified as a new biomarker for exposure and effect.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-r3-102) Benzene causes chromosomal aberrations in the peripheral blood leukocytes and bone marrow explaining the higher incidence of leukemia and multiple myeloma caused by chronic exposure. These aberrations can be monitored using [fluorescent in situ hybridization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorescent_in_situ_hybridization "Fluorescent in situ hybridization") (FISH) with DNA probes to assess the effects of benzene along with the hematological tests as markers of hematotoxicity.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-r1-103) Benzene metabolism involves enzymes coded for by polymorphic genes. Studies have shown that genotype at these loci may influence susceptibility to the toxic effects of benzene exposure. Individuals carrying variant of NAD(P)H:quinone oxidoreductase 1 (NQO1), microsomal epoxide hydrolase (EPHX) and deletion of the glutathione S-transferase T1 (GSTT1) showed a greater frequency of DNA single-stranded breaks.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-r2-104)

#### Biological oxidation and carcinogenic activity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Benzene&action=edit&section=28 "Edit section: Biological oxidation and carcinogenic activity")]

One way of understanding the carcinogenic effects of benzene is by examining the products of biological oxidation. Pure benzene, for example, oxidizes in the body to produce an epoxide, [benzene oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene_oxide "Benzene oxide"), which is not excreted readily and can interact with DNA to produce harmful mutations.

Outdoor air may contain low levels of benzene from automobile service stations, wood smoke, tobacco smoke, the transfer of gasoline, exhaust from motor vehicles, and industrial emissions.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-105) About 50% of the entire nationwide (United States) exposure to benzene results from smoking tobacco or from exposure to tobacco smoke.[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-106) After smoking 32 cigarettes per day, the smoker would take in about 1.8 milligrams (mg) of benzene. This amount is about 10 times the average daily intake of benzene by nonsmokers.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-107)

Inhaled benzene is primarily expelled unchanged through exhalation. In a human study 16.4 to 41.6% of retained benzene was eliminated through the lungs within five to seven hours after a two- to three-hour exposure to 47 to 110 ppm and only 0.07 to 0.2% of the remaining benzene was excreted unchanged in the urine. After exposure to 63 to 405 mg/m 3 of benzene for 1 to 5 hours, 51 to 87% was excreted in the urine as phenol over a period of 23 to 50 hours. In another human study, 30% of absorbed dermally applied benzene, which is primarily metabolized in the liver, was excreted as phenol in the urine.[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-108)

#### Exposure from soft drinks

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Benzene&action=edit&section=31 "Edit section: Exposure from soft drinks")]

Under specific conditions and in the presence of other chemicals [benzoic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzoic_acid "Benzoic acid") (a preservative) and [ascorbic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ascorbic_acid "Ascorbic acid") (Vitamin C) may interact to produce benzene. In March 2006, the official [Food Standards Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Food_Standards_Agency "Food Standards Agency") in [United Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Kingdom "United Kingdom") conducted a survey of 150 brands of soft drinks. It found that four contained benzene levels above [World Health Organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Health_Organization "World Health Organization") limits. The affected batches were removed from sale. Similar problems were reported by the FDA in the United States.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-109)

#### Contamination of water supply

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Benzene&action=edit&section=32 "Edit section: Contamination of water supply")]

In 2005, the water supply to the city of [Harbin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harbin "Harbin") in China with a population of almost nine million people, was cut off because of a major benzene exposure.[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-Guardian-25Nov2005-110) Benzene leaked into the [Songhua River](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Songhua_River "Songhua River"), which supplies drinking water to the city, after [an explosion at a China National Petroleum Corporation (CNPC) factory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jilin_chemical_plant_explosions_2005 "Jilin chemical plant explosions 2005") in the city of Jilin on 13 November 2005.

When plastic water pipes are subject to high heat, the water may be contaminated with benzene.[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-111)

[Nazi Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_Germany "Nazi Germany") used benzene administered via [injection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Injection_(medicine) "Injection (medicine)") as one of their many methods for killing.[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-112)[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_note-113)

2.   **[^](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benzene#cite_ref-40)**Critics pointed out a problem with Kekulé's original (1865) structure for benzene: Whenever benzene underwent substitution at the ortho position, two distinguishable isomers should have resulted, depending on whether a double bond or a single bond existed between the carbon atoms to which the substituents were attached; however, no such isomers were observed. In 1872, Kekulé suggested that benzene had two complementary structures and that these forms rapidly interconverted, so that if there were a double bond between any pair of carbon atoms at one instant, that double bond would become a single bond at the next instant (and vice versa). To provide a mechanism for the conversion process, Kekulé proposed that the valency of an atom is determined by the frequency with which it collided with its neighbors in a molecule. As the carbon atoms in the benzene ring collided with each other, each carbon atom would collide twice with one neighbor during a given interval and then twice with its other neighbor during the next interval. Thus, a double bond would exist with one neighbor during the first interval and with the other neighbor during the next interval. Therefore, between the carbon atoms of benzene there were no fixed (i.e., constant) and distinct single or double bonds; instead, the bonds between the carbon atoms were identical. See [pages 86–89](https://de.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Datei:Kekule_-_Ueber_einige_Condensationsproducte_des_Aldehyds.pdf&page=10)[Archived](https://web.archive.org/web/20200320235654/https://de.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Datei%3AKekule_-_Ueber_einige_Condensationsproducte_des_Aldehyds.pdf&page=10) 2020-03-20 at the [Wayback Machine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wayback_Machine "Wayback Machine") of Auguste Kekulé (1872) "Ueber einige Condensationsprodukte des Aldehyds" (On some condensation products of aldehydes), _Liebig's Annalen der Chemie und Pharmacie_, **162**(1): 77–124, 309–320. From p. 89: _"Das einfachste Mittel aller Stöße eines Kohlenstoffatoms ergiebt sich aus der Summe der Stöße der beiden ersten Zeiteinheiten, die sich dann periodisch wiederholen. ... man sieht daher, daß jedes Kohlenstoffatom mit den beiden anderen, ... daß diese Verschiedenheit nur eine scheinbare, aber keine wirkliche ist."_ (The simplest average of all the collisions of a carbon atom [in benzene] comes from the sum of the collisions during the first two units of time, which then periodically repeat. ... thus one sees that each carbon atom collides equally often with the two others against which it bumps, [and] thus stands in exactly the same relation with its two neighbors. The usual structural formula for benzene expresses, of course, only the collisions that occur during _one_ unit of time, thus during one phase, and so one is led to the view [that] doubly substituted derivatives [of benzene] must be different at positions 1,2 and 1,6 [of the benzene ring]. If the idea [that was] just presented—or a similar one—can be regarded as correct, then [it] follows therefrom that this difference [between the bonds at positions 1,2 and 1,6] is only an apparent [one], not a real [one].)
