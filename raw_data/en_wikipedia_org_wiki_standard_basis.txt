From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Not to be confused with another name for a [Gröbner basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gr%C3%B6bner_basis "Gröbner basis").

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/3D_Vector.svg/330px-3D_Vector.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:3D_Vector.svg)

Every vector **a** in three dimensions is a [linear combination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_combination "Linear combination") of the standard basis vectors **i**, **j** and **k**.

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **standard basis** (also called **natural basis** or **[canonical basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_basis "Canonical basis")**) of a [coordinate vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_vector_space "Coordinate vector space") (such as ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d) or ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a53b4e76242764d1bca004168353c380fef25258)) is the set of vectors, each of whose components are all zero, except one that equals 1.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_basis#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoman200847ch._1-1) For example, in the case of the [Euclidean plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_plane "Euclidean plane")![Image 4: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd) formed by the pairs (_x_, _y_) of [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number"), the standard basis is formed by the vectors ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{x}=(1,0),\quad \mathbf {e} _{y}=(0,1).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7df22c2acda4a731663ab76ad947bca7bf74c8a4) Similarly, the standard basis for the [three-dimensional space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional_space "Three-dimensional space")![Image 6: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) is formed by vectors ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{x}=(1,0,0),\quad \mathbf {e} _{y}=(0,1,0),\quad \mathbf {e} _{z}=(0,0,1).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d8cd50a0dc81132865bfb30d4eb945e500a8a3d)

Here the vector **e**_x_ points in the _x_ direction, the vector **e**_y_ points in the _y_ direction, and the vector **e**_z_ points in the _z_ direction. There are several common [notations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_notation "Mathematical notation") for standard-basis vectors, including {**e**_x_,**e**_y_,**e**_z_}, {**e**1,**e**2,**e**3}, {**i**,**j**,**k**}, and {**x**,**y**,**z**}. These vectors are sometimes written with a [hat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumflex "Circumflex") to emphasize their status as [unit vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vector "Unit vector") (**standard unit vectors**).

These vectors are a [basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(linear_algebra) "Basis (linear algebra)") in the sense that any other vector can be expressed uniquely as a [linear combination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_combination "Linear combination") of these.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_basis#cite_note-2) For example, every vector **v** in three-dimensional space can be written uniquely as ![Image 8: {\displaystyle v_{x}\,\mathbf {e} _{x}+v_{y}\,\mathbf {e} _{y}+v_{z}\,\mathbf {e} _{z},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/929507d2da61aa2fab6296d3cb0742333c62c943) the [scalars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_(mathematics) "Scalar (mathematics)")![Image 9: {\displaystyle v_{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/704b7ad1ece77840fde455daa6d2e51e64282b5e),![Image 10: {\displaystyle v_{y}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/922aa64dad09633a401e14be9b4389795835cd8c),![Image 11: {\displaystyle v_{z}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b553b9ecda9fc7f5c9da6f14a870e219080984e0) being the [scalar components](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_component "Scalar component") of the vector **v**.

In the n-[dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension_(linear_algebra) "Dimension (linear algebra)") Euclidean space ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c510b63578322050121fe966f2e5770bea43308d), the standard basis consists of _n_ distinct vectors ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \{\mathbf {e} _{i}:1\leq i\leq n\},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bebecbd25289f85749da575e448954eb585b6c11) where **e**_i_ denotes the vector with a 1 in the i th [coordinate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate "Coordinate") and 0's elsewhere.

Standard bases can be defined for other [vector spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space"), whose definition involves [coefficients](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coefficient "Coefficient"), such as [polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial") and [matrices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_(mathematics) "Matrix (mathematics)"). In both cases, the standard basis consists of the elements of the space such that all coefficients but one are 0 and the non-zero one is 1. For polynomials, the standard basis thus consists of the [monomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monomial "Monomial") and is commonly called [monomial basis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monomial_basis "Monomial basis"). For matrices ![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {M}}_{m\times n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d7006535d3cc8cf4ea415d171f03d1cdda7e18d5), the standard basis consists of the _m_×_n_-matrices with exactly one non-zero entry, which is 1. For example, the standard basis for 2×2 matrices is formed by the 4 matrices ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbf {e} _{11}={\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&0\end{pmatrix}},\quad \mathbf {e} _{12}={\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\0&0\end{pmatrix}},\quad \mathbf {e} _{21}={\begin{pmatrix}0&0\\1&0\end{pmatrix}},\quad \mathbf {e} _{22}={\begin{pmatrix}0&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/400c256ad99ad5468486c8a2e0fa09c575d3e28c)

By definition, the standard basis is a [sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence "Sequence") of [orthogonal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthogonal "Orthogonal")[unit vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_vectors "Unit vectors"). In other words, it is an [ordered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_basis "Ordered basis") and [orthonormal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthonormal_basis "Orthonormal basis") basis.

However, an ordered orthonormal basis is not necessarily a standard basis. For instance the two vectors representing a 30° rotation of the 2D standard basis described above, i.e. , ![Image 16: {\displaystyle v_{1}=\left({{\sqrt {3}} \over 2},{1 \over 2}\right)\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3d7f8287ced855bc471f7519252967c9ce3d10d)![Image 17: {\displaystyle v_{2}=\left({1 \over 2},{-{\sqrt {3}} \over 2}\right)\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e25ceae36dfbfe86b87184658cea4af8c4862d0f) are also orthogonal unit vectors, but they are not aligned with the axes of the [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system"), so the basis with these vectors does not meet the definition of standard basis.

There is a _standard_ basis also for the ring of [polynomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial "Polynomial") in _n_ indeterminates over a [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)"), namely the [monomials](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monomial "Monomial").

All of the preceding are special cases of the [indexed family](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indexed_family "Indexed family")![Image 18: {\displaystyle {(e_{i})}_{i\in I}=((\delta _{ij})_{j\in I})_{i\in I}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9b712250db938fd9b1e970ebb527c88435d63b42) where ![Image 19: {\displaystyle I}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/535ea7fc4134a31cbe2251d9d3511374bc41be9f) is any set and ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \delta _{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa75d04c11480d976e1396951e02cbb3c4f71568) is the [Kronecker delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta "Kronecker delta"), equal to zero whenever _i_ ≠ _j_ and equal to 1 if _i_ = _j_. This family is the _canonical_ basis of the _R_-module ([free module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_module "Free module")) ![Image 21: {\displaystyle R^{(I)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/08c07dd98de2b46b8ccc59923f9012d98f255fcd) of all families ![Image 22: {\displaystyle f=(f_{i})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fed940d23990385c423f36dd04c244fb9f10ca42) from _I_ into a [ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)")_R_, which are [zero except for a finite number of indices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Finite_support "Finite support"), if we interpret 1 as 1 _R_, the [unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_(ring_theory) "Unit (ring theory)") in _R_.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_basis#cite_note-FOOTNOTERoman2008131ch._5-3)

The existence of other 'standard' bases has become a topic of interest in [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"), beginning with work of [Hodge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/W._V._D._Hodge "W. V. D. Hodge") from 1943 on [Grassmannians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grassmannian "Grassmannian"). It is now a part of [representation theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Representation_theory "Representation theory") called _standard monomial theory_. The idea of standard basis in the [universal enveloping algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_enveloping_algebra "Universal enveloping algebra") of a [Lie algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra "Lie algebra") is established by the [Poincaré–Birkhoff–Witt theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9%E2%80%93Birkhoff%E2%80%93Witt_theorem "Poincaré–Birkhoff–Witt theorem").

[Gröbner bases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gr%C3%B6bner_basis "Gröbner basis") are also sometimes called standard bases.

In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), the standard basis vectors for a given Euclidean space are sometimes referred to as the [versors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Versor_(physics) "Versor (physics)") of the axes of the corresponding Cartesian coordinate system.
