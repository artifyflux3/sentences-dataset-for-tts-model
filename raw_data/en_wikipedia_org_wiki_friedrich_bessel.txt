**Friedrich Wilhelm Bessel** (German:[[ˈbɛsl̩]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German "Help:IPA/Standard German"); 22 July 1784 – 17 March 1846) was a German [astronomer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomer "Astronomer"), [mathematician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician"), [physicist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physicist "Physicist"), and [geodesist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesy "Geodesy"). He was the first astronomer who determined reliable values for the distance from the Sun to another star by the method of [parallax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallax "Parallax"). Certain important mathematical functions were first studied systematically by Bessel and were named [Bessel functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bessel_function "Bessel function") in his honour.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-:0-1)

Bessel was born in [Minden](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minden "Minden"), [Westphalia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Westphalia "Westphalia"), then capital of the Prussian administrative region [Minden-Ravensberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minden-Ravensberg "Minden-Ravensberg"), as second son of a civil servant into a large family. At the age of 14 he left the school, because he did not like the education in Latin language, and apprenticed in the import-export concern Kulenkamp at [Bremen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bremen "Bremen"). The business's reliance on cargo ships led him to turn his [mathematical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") skills to problems in [navigation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Navigation "Navigation"). This in turn led to an interest in [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy") as a way of determining [longitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Longitude "Longitude").

Bessel came to the attention of [Heinrich Wilhelm Olbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinrich_Wilhelm_Olbers "Heinrich Wilhelm Olbers"), a practising physician of Bremen and well-known astronomer, by producing a refinement on the orbital calculations for [Halley's Comet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halley%27s_Comet "Halley's Comet") in 1804, using old observation data taken from [Thomas Harriot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_Harriot "Thomas Harriot") and [Nathaniel Torporley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nathaniel_Torporley "Nathaniel Torporley") in 1607.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-2)[Franz Xaver von Zach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franz_Xaver_von_Zach "Franz Xaver von Zach") edited the results in his journal _Monatliche Correspondenz_.

Having finished his commercial education, Bessel left Kulenkamp in 1806 and became assistant at [Johann Hieronymus Schröter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Hieronymus_Schr%C3%B6ter "Johann Hieronymus Schröter")'s private observatory in [Lilienthal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lilienthal,_Lower_Saxony "Lilienthal, Lower Saxony") near Bremen as successor of [Karl Ludwig Harding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Ludwig_Harding "Karl Ludwig Harding"). There he worked on [James Bradley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Bradley "James Bradley")'s stellar observation data to produce precise positions for some 3,222 stars.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChisholm1911-2)

Despite lacking any higher education, especially at university, Bessel was appointed director of the newly founded [Königsberg Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/K%C3%B6nigsberg_Observatory "Königsberg Observatory") by King [Frederick William III of Prussia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frederick_William_III_of_Prussia "Frederick William III of Prussia") in January 1810, at the age of 25, and remained in that position until his death. Some elder professors of the Philosophical Faculty disputed Bessel's right to teach mathematics without any academic degree. Therefore, he turned to his fellow [Carl Friedrich Gauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Friedrich_Gauss "Carl Friedrich Gauss"), who provided the award of an honorary doctor degree from the [University of Göttingen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_G%C3%B6ttingen "University of Göttingen") in March 1811. Both scientists were in correspondence from 1804 to 1843. In 1837 they got in quarrel about Gauss's habit of very slow publication.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-3)

In 1842 Bessel took part in the annual meeting of the [British Association for the Advancement of Science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_Association_for_the_Advancement_of_Science "British Association for the Advancement of Science") in Manchester, accompanied by the geophysicist [Georg Adolf Erman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Adolf_Erman "Georg Adolf Erman") and the mathematician [Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Gustav_Jacob_Jacobi "Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi"), where he gave a report on astronomical clocks.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-4)

Bessel married Johanna Hagen, the daughter of the chemist and pharmacist [Karl Gottfried Hagen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Gottfried_Hagen "Karl Gottfried Hagen") who was the uncle of the physician and biologist [Hermann August Hagen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_August_Hagen "Hermann August Hagen") and the hydraulic engineer [Gotthilf Hagen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gotthilf_Hagen "Gotthilf Hagen"), the latter also Bessel's student and assistant from 1816 to 1818. The physicist [Franz Ernst Neumann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franz_Ernst_Neumann "Franz Ernst Neumann"), Bessel's close companion and colleague, was married to Johanna Hagen's sister Florentine. Neumann introduced Bessel's exacting methods of measurement and data reduction into his mathematico-physical seminar, which he co-directed with [Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Gustav_Jacob_Jacobi "Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi") at Königsberg.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-5) These exacting methods had a lasting impact upon the work of Neumann's students and upon the Prussian conception of precision in measurement.

Bessel had two sons and three daughters. His elder son became an architect but died suddenly in 1840 aged 26; his younger son died shortly after birth. His eldest daughter, Marie, married the physicist [Georg Adolf Erman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Adolf_Erman "Georg Adolf Erman"), member of the scholar family [Erman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erman "Erman"). One of their sons in turn was the renowned [Egyptologist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptologist "Egyptologist")[Adolf Erman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adolf_Erman "Adolf Erman"). His third daughter Johanna married the politician [Adolf Hermann Hagen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adolf_Hermann_Hagen "Adolf Hermann Hagen"); one of their sons was the physicist [Ernst Bessel Hagen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernst_Bessel_Hagen "Ernst Bessel Hagen"), and the mathematician [Erich Bessel-Hagen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erich_Bessel-Hagen "Erich Bessel-Hagen") was a grandson of them. Bessel was [godfather](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Godparent "Godparent") of [Adolf von Baeyer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adolf_von_Baeyer "Adolf von Baeyer"), son of his collaborator [Johann Jacob Baeyer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Jacob_Baeyer "Johann Jacob Baeyer").

After several months of illness Bessel died in March 1846 at his observatory from [retroperitoneal fibrosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Retroperitoneal_fibrosis "Retroperitoneal fibrosis").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-7)

While the observatory was still in construction Bessel elaborated the _Fundamenta Astronomiae_ based on Bradley's observations. As a preliminary result he produced tables of [atmospheric refraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_refraction "Atmospheric refraction") that won him the [Lalande Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lalande_Prize "Lalande Prize") from the [French Academy of Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Academy_of_Sciences "French Academy of Sciences") in 1811. The Königsberg Observatory began operation in 1813.

Starting in 1819, Bessel determined the position of over 50,000 stars with a [meridian circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meridian_circle "Meridian circle") from [Reichenbach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_von_Reichenbach "Georg von Reichenbach"), assisted by some of his qualified students. The most prominent of them was [Friedrich Wilhelm Argelander](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Wilhelm_Argelander "Friedrich Wilhelm Argelander"), his successors were [Otto August Rosenberger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otto_August_Rosenberger "Otto August Rosenberger") and [August Ludwig Busch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/August_Ludwig_Busch "August Ludwig Busch").

Bessel determined the first reliable value for the [distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_distance_ladder "Cosmic distance ladder") between a star and the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") with a [heliometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliometer "Heliometer") from [Fraunhofer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_von_Fraunhofer "Joseph von Fraunhofer") using the method of [stellar parallax](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_parallax "Stellar parallax"). In 1838 he published a parallax of 0.314 [arcseconds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arcsecond "Arcsecond") for [61 Cygni](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/61_Cygni "61 Cygni"), which indicated that the star is 10.3 [ly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year "Light-year") away.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-10) Compared with the current measurement of 11.4 ly, Bessel's figure had an error of 9.6%. Thanks to these results astronomers had not only enlarged the vision of the universe well beyond the cosmic magnitude, but after the discovery in 1728 by [James Bradley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Bradley "James Bradley") of the [aberration of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aberration_(astronomy) "Aberration (astronomy)") a second empirical evidence of the Earth's relative movement was produced.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-11) A short time later [Friedrich Georg Wilhelm Struve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Georg_Wilhelm_Struve "Friedrich Georg Wilhelm Struve") and [Thomas Henderson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thomas_James_Henderson "Thomas James Henderson") reported the parallaxes of [Vega](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vega "Vega") and [Alpha Centauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri "Alpha Centauri").

Precise measurements with a new [meridian circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meridian_circle "Meridian circle") from Adolf Repsold allowed Bessel to notice deviations in the motions of [Sirius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirius "Sirius") and [Procyon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Procyon "Procyon"), which must be caused by the gravitational attraction of unseen companions.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-13) His announcement of Sirius's "dark companion" in 1844 was the first correct claim of a previously unobserved companion by positional measurement, and eventually led to the discovery of [Sirius B](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirius_B "Sirius B") by [Alvan Graham Clark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alvan_Graham_Clark "Alvan Graham Clark") in 1862, the first discovery of a [white dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf "White dwarf"). [John Martin Schaeberle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Martin_Schaeberle "John Martin Schaeberle") discovered Procyon B in 1896.

Bessel was the first scientist who realized the effect later called _[personal equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Personal\_equation "Personal equation")_, that several simultaneously observing persons determine slightly different values, especially recording the transit time of stars.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-14)

In 1824, Bessel developed a new method for calculating the circumstances of eclipses using the so-called [Besselian elements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Besselian_elements "Besselian elements"). His method simplified the calculation to such an extent, without sacrificing accuracy, that it is still in use.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-15)

On Bessel's proposal the [Prussian Academy of Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prussian_Academy_of_Sciences "Prussian Academy of Sciences") started the edition of the _Berliner Akademische Sternkarten_ (_Berlin Academic Star Charts_) in 1825 as an international project with [Johann Franz Encke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Franz_Encke "Johann Franz Encke") as executive editor. One unpublished new chart enabled [Johann Gottfried Galle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Gottfried_Galle "Johann Gottfried Galle") to find [Neptune](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discovery_of_Neptune "Discovery of Neptune") near the position calculated by [Le Verrier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Urbain_Le_Verrier "Urbain Le Verrier") in September 1846 at [Berlin Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Berlin_Observatory "Berlin Observatory").

In the 18th century a series of cylinder functions were introduced to solve [string vibration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_vibration "String vibration") problems by [Daniel Bernoulli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Daniel_Bernoulli "Daniel Bernoulli"), [Leonhard Euler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonhard_Euler "Leonhard Euler") and others. These functions were used by [Joseph-Louis Lagrange](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph-Louis_Lagrange "Joseph-Louis Lagrange") and Bessel to solve the [Kepler's equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%27s_equation "Kepler's equation"). Bessel later systematically studied the mathematical properties of these functions, now known as [Bessel functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bessel_function "Bessel function").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-:0-1) These functions are critical for the solution of certain [differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equations "Differential equations"), these functions are used throughout both classical and [quantum physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_physics "Quantum physics"). In his studies of this functions, Bessel published for the first time the [Fourier series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fourier_series "Fourier series"), before [Joseph Fourier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Fourier "Joseph Fourier") who had worked on it but his work was published decades later.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-:0-1)

A [correction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bessel%27s_correction "Bessel's correction") term in the formula for the [sample variance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sample_variance "Sample variance") estimator is named in his honour. This is the use of the factor _n_−1 in the denominator of the formula, rather than just _n_. This occurs when the _sample mean_ rather than the _population mean_ is used to centre the data and since the sample mean is a linear combination of the data the residual to the sample mean overcounts the number of degrees of freedom by the number of constraint equations — in this case one.

Bessel's geodetic apparatus, in the museum of Istituto Geografico Militare, Florence, Italy

Like numerous astronomers of his time Bessel dealt on the field of [geodesy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesy "Geodesy"), too,[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-16) first theoretically, when he published a method for solving the main [geodetic problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesics_on_an_ellipsoid "Geodesics on an ellipsoid").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-17) In 1830 he got the royal order for the survey of [East Prussia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Prussia "East Prussia") with the purpose to connect the yet existing Prussian and Russian [triangulation networks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triangulation_network "Triangulation network"). This work was carried out in cooperation with [Johann Jacob Baeyer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Jacob_Baeyer "Johann Jacob Baeyer"), then major of the Prussian army; the final report was published in 1838.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-18) He also obtained an estimate of increased accuracy for the [Earth's ellipsoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_ellipsoid "Earth's ellipsoid"), nowadays called the [Bessel ellipsoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bessel_ellipsoid "Bessel ellipsoid"), based on several [arc measurements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_measurements "Arc measurements").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-20)

The first cosmic object named after Bessel is [the largest crater](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bessel_(crater) "Bessel (crater)") in the Moon's [Mare Serenitatis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mare_Serenitatis "Mare Serenitatis").[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-31) The [main-belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Main-belt "Main-belt") asteroid [1552 Bessel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1552_Bessel "1552 Bessel") was named at the centenary of the parallax determination in 1938.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-1552-Bessel-32)

Geographical commemorations are two fjords in Greenland, [Bessel Fjord, NE Greenland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bessel_Fjord "Bessel Fjord") and [Bessel Fjord, NW Greenland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bessel_Fjord,_NW_Greenland "Bessel Fjord, NW Greenland").

_Xyletinus besseli_[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-33) a fossil [beetle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beetle "Beetle") from the [Eocene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eocene "Eocene") belonging to the family [Ptinidae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ptinidae "Ptinidae"), found in the [Baltic amber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baltic_amber "Baltic amber") in [Sambia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sambia_Peninsula "Sambia Peninsula"), was named in his honour.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Bessel#cite_note-Alekseev_Bukejs_2019_p.-34)

BeSSel, the [_Bar and Spiral Structure Legacy Survey_](https://www3.mpifr-bonn.mpg.de/staff/abrunthaler/BeSSeL/index.shtml), is named after him.
