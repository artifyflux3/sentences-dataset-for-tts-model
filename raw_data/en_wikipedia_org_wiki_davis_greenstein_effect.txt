From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

[Polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polarization_(waves) "Polarization (waves)") of [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation") is a useful tool for detecting various [astronomical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy") phenomenon. For example, energy can become polarized by passing through [interstellar dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_dust "Interstellar dust") or by [magnetic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field"). Microwave energy from the primordial universe can be used to study the physics of that environment.

The polarization of [starlight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star#Radiation "Star") was first observed by the [astronomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomer "Astronomer")[William Hiltner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Hiltner "William Hiltner") and John S. Hall in 1949. Subsequently, [Jesse Greenstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jesse_Greenstein "Jesse Greenstein") and [Leverett Davis, Jr.](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Leverett_Davis,_Jr.&action=edit&redlink=1 "Leverett Davis, Jr. (page does not exist)") developed theories allowing the use of polarization data to trace interstellar magnetic fields. Though the integrated [thermal radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermal_radiation "Thermal radiation") of [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star") is not usually appreciably polarized at source, scattering by [interstellar dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_dust "Interstellar dust") can impose polarization on starlight over long distances. Net polarization at the source can occur if the [photosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosphere "Photosphere") itself is asymmetric, due to [limb polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Limb_polarization&action=edit&redlink=1 "Limb polarization (page does not exist)"). Plane polarization of starlight generated at the star itself is observed for [Ap stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ap_star "Ap star") (peculiar A type stars).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Davis%E2%80%93Greenstein_effect#cite_note-1)

Both [circular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circular_polarization "Circular polarization") and [linear polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_polarization "Linear polarization") of [sunlight](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunlight "Sunlight") has been measured. Circular polarization is mainly due to transmission and absorption effects in strongly magnetic regions of the Sun's surface. Another mechanism that gives rise to circular polarization is the so-called "alignment-to-orientation mechanism". Continuum light is linearly polarized at different locations across the face of the Sun (limb polarization) though taken as a whole, this polarization cancels. Linear polarization in spectral lines is usually created by [anisotropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anisotropic "Anisotropic")[scattering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scattering "Scattering") of photons on atoms and ions which can themselves be polarized by this interaction. The linearly polarized spectrum of the Sun is often called the [second solar spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_solar_spectrum "Second solar spectrum"). [Atomic polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Atomic_polarization&action=edit&redlink=1 "Atomic polarization (page does not exist)") can be modified in weak magnetic fields by the [Hanle effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hanle_effect "Hanle effect"). As a result, polarization of the scattered photons is also modified providing a diagnostics tool for understanding [stellar magnetic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_magnetic_field "Stellar magnetic field").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Davis%E2%80%93Greenstein_effect#cite_note-Landi-2)

Polarization is also present in radiation from [coherent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coherent_radiation "Coherent radiation") astronomical sources due to the [Zeeman effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zeeman_effect "Zeeman effect") (e.g. hydroxyl or methanol [masers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_maser "Astrophysical maser")).

The large radio lobes in [active galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Active_galaxy "Active galaxy") and [pulsar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulsar "Pulsar") radio radiation (which may, it is speculated, sometimes be coherent) also show polarization.

Apart from providing information on sources of radiation and scattering, polarization also probes the interstellar [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field") in our galaxy as well as in radio galaxies via [Faraday rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Faraday_rotation "Faraday rotation").[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Davis%E2%80%93Greenstein_effect#cite_note-3): 119, 124 [[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Davis%E2%80%93Greenstein_effect#cite_note-KarttunenKr%C3%B6ger2007-4): 336–337  In some cases it can be difficult to determine how much of the Faraday rotation is in the external source and how much is local to our own galaxy, but in many cases it is possible to find another distant source nearby in the sky; thus by comparing the candidate source and the reference source, the results can be untangled.

Cosmic microwave background
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Polarization_in_astronomy&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Cosmic microwave background")]

The polarization of the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background#Polarization "Cosmic microwave background") (CMB) is also being used to study the physics of the very [early universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Early_universe "Early universe").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Davis%E2%80%93Greenstein_effect#cite_note-boyle-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Davis%E2%80%93Greenstein_effect#cite_note-tegmark-6) CMB exhibits 2 components of polarization: [B-mode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B-modes "B-modes") (divergence-free like magnetic field) and [E-mode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B-modes "B-modes") (curl-free gradient-only like electric field) polarization. The [BICEP2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BICEP2 "BICEP2") telescope located at the South Pole initially claimed the detection of B-mode polarization in the CMB, though the initially claimed result was later retracted. The polarization modes of the CMB may provide more information about the influence of [gravitational waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_wave "Gravitational wave") on the development of the early universe.

It has been suggested that astronomical sources of polarised light caused the [chirality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chirality "Chirality") found in biological molecules on Earth.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Davis%E2%80%93Greenstein_effect#cite_note-7)
