In [logic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logic "Logic") and [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **logical biconditional**, also known as **material biconditional** or **equivalence** or **bidirectional implication** or **biimplication** or **bientailment**, is the [logical connective](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_connective "Logical connective") used to conjoin two statements P{\displaystyle P}![Image 102: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) and Q{\displaystyle Q}![Image 103: {\displaystyle Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8752c7023b4b3286800fe3238271bbca681219ed) to form the statement "P{\displaystyle P}![Image 104: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a)[if and only if](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If_and_only_if "If and only if")Q{\displaystyle Q}![Image 105: {\displaystyle Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8752c7023b4b3286800fe3238271bbca681219ed)" (often abbreviated as "P{\displaystyle P}![Image 106: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) iff Q{\displaystyle Q}![Image 107: {\displaystyle Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8752c7023b4b3286800fe3238271bbca681219ed)"[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_biconditional#cite_note-:2-1)), where P{\displaystyle P}![Image 108: {\displaystyle P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4dc73bf40314945ff376bd363916a738548d40a) is known as the _[antecedent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antecedent\_(logic) "Antecedent (logic)")_, and Q{\displaystyle Q}![Image 109: {\displaystyle Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8752c7023b4b3286800fe3238271bbca681219ed) the _[consequent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Consequent "Consequent")_.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_biconditional#cite_note-:1-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_biconditional#cite_note-3)

Nowadays, notations to represent equivalence include ↔,⇔,≡{\displaystyle \leftrightarrow ,\Leftrightarrow ,\equiv }![Image 110: {\displaystyle \leftrightarrow ,\Leftrightarrow ,\equiv }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7e5a2012c9d789f3dd98a6adcba218e91ed3e60b).

P↔Q{\displaystyle P\leftrightarrow Q}![Image 111: {\displaystyle P\leftrightarrow Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43e9c3ed4d9717db81fc3794218f377bea4f6eb0) is logically equivalent to both (P→Q)∧(Q→P){\displaystyle (P\rightarrow Q)\land (Q\rightarrow P)}![Image 112: {\displaystyle (P\rightarrow Q)\land (Q\rightarrow P)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f32671a03a64d47feee37e84edf78d094ecce0be) and (P∧Q)∨(¬P∧¬Q){\displaystyle (P\land Q)\lor (\neg P\land \neg Q)}![Image 113: {\displaystyle (P\land Q)\lor (\neg P\land \neg Q)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/acba17910384f15b1f928005ea6311ad59028898), and the [XNOR](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/XNOR_gate "XNOR gate") (exclusive NOR) [Boolean operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_connective "Logical connective"), which means "both or neither".

Semantically, the only case where a logical biconditional is different from a [material conditional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Material_conditional "Material conditional") is the case where the hypothesis (antecedent) is false but the conclusion (consequent) is true. In this case, the result is true for the conditional, but false for the biconditional.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_biconditional#cite_note-:1-2)

In the conceptual interpretation, _P_ = _Q_ means "All P's are Q's and all Q's are P's". In other words, the sets P and Q coincide: they are identical. However, this does not mean that P and Q need to have the same meaning (e.g., P could be "equiangular trilateral" and Q could be "equilateral triangle"). When phrased as a sentence, the antecedent is the _subject_ and the consequent is the _predicate_ of a [universal affirmative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_affirmative "Universal affirmative") proposition (e.g., in the phrase "all men are mortal", "men" is the subject and "mortal" is the predicate).

In the propositional interpretation, P↔Q{\displaystyle P\leftrightarrow Q}![Image 114: {\displaystyle P\leftrightarrow Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43e9c3ed4d9717db81fc3794218f377bea4f6eb0) means that P implies Q and Q implies P; in other words, the propositions are [logically equivalent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logically_equivalent "Logically equivalent"), in the sense that both are either jointly true or jointly false. Again, this does not mean that they need to have the same meaning, as P could be "the triangle ABC has two equal sides" and Q could be "the triangle ABC has two equal angles". In general, the antecedent is the _premise_, or the _cause_, and the consequent is the _consequence_. When an implication is translated by a _hypothetical_ (or _conditional_) judgment, the antecedent is called the _hypothesis_ (or the _condition_) and the consequent is called the _thesis_.

A common way of demonstrating a biconditional of the form P↔Q{\displaystyle P\leftrightarrow Q}![Image 115: {\displaystyle P\leftrightarrow Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/43e9c3ed4d9717db81fc3794218f377bea4f6eb0) is to demonstrate that P→Q{\displaystyle P\rightarrow Q}![Image 116: {\displaystyle P\rightarrow Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86439ea857adc8eaec93c4d14270b8ba6bd2a6a9) and Q→P{\displaystyle Q\rightarrow P}![Image 117: {\displaystyle Q\rightarrow P}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a02fdef5455a1bfa49458c8d9821b1211b1e6e2b) separately (due to its equivalence to the conjunction of the two converse [conditionals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Material_conditional "Material conditional")[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_biconditional#cite_note-:1-2)). Yet another way of demonstrating the same biconditional is by demonstrating that P→Q{\displaystyle P\rightarrow Q}![Image 118: {\displaystyle P\rightarrow Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86439ea857adc8eaec93c4d14270b8ba6bd2a6a9) and ¬P→¬Q{\displaystyle \neg P\rightarrow \neg Q}![Image 119: {\displaystyle \neg P\rightarrow \neg Q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dddf975e2d6626e24fbb8f41e7a2b16c592adcca).

When both members of the biconditional are propositions, it can be separated into two conditionals, of which one is called a _theorem_ and the other its _reciprocal_.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] Thus whenever a theorem and its reciprocal are true, we have a biconditional. A simple theorem gives rise to an implication, whose antecedent is the _hypothesis_ and whose consequent is the _thesis_ of the theorem.

It is often said that the hypothesis is the _[sufficient condition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sufficient\_condition "Sufficient condition")_ of the thesis, and that the thesis is the _[necessary condition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Necessary\_condition "Necessary condition")_ of the hypothesis. That is, it is sufficient that the hypothesis be true for the thesis to be true, while it is necessary that the thesis be true if the hypothesis were true. When a theorem and its reciprocal are true, its hypothesis is said to be the [necessary and sufficient condition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Necessary_and_sufficient_condition "Necessary and sufficient condition") of the thesis. That is, the hypothesis is both the cause and the consequence of the thesis at the same time.

Definition
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Logical_biconditional&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Definition")]

[Logical equality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_equality "Logical equality") (also known as biconditional) is an [operation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_operation "Logical operation") on two [logical values](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_value "Logical value"), typically the values of two [propositions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proposition "Proposition"), that produces a value of _true_ if and only if both operands are false or both operands are true.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_biconditional#cite_note-:1-2)

Rules of inference
------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Logical_biconditional&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Rules of inference")]

Main article: [Rules of inference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rules_of_inference "Rules of inference")

Like all connectives in first-order logic, the biconditional has rules of inference that govern its use in formal proofs.

### Biconditional introduction

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Logical_biconditional&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Biconditional introduction")]

Main article: [Biconditional introduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biconditional_introduction "Biconditional introduction")

Biconditional introduction allows one to infer that if B follows from A and A follows from B, then A [if and only if](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If_and_only_if "If and only if") B.

For example, from the statements "if I'm breathing, then I'm alive" and "if I'm alive, then I'm breathing", it can be inferred that "I'm breathing if and only if I'm alive" or equivalently, "I'm alive if and only if I'm breathing." Or more schematically:

 B → A   
 A → B 
 ∴ A ↔ B
 B → A   
 A → B 
 ∴ B ↔ A

### Biconditional elimination

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Logical_biconditional&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Biconditional elimination")]

Biconditional elimination allows one to infer a [conditional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Material_conditional "Material conditional") from a biconditional: if A ↔ B is true, then one may infer either A → B, or B → A.

For example, if it is true that I'm breathing [if and only if](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/If_and_only_if "If and only if") I'm alive, then it's true that _if_ I'm breathing, then I'm alive; likewise, it's true that _if_ I'm alive, then I'm breathing. Or more schematically:

 A ↔ B
 ∴ A → B
 A ↔ B
 ∴ B → A

Colloquial usage
----------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Logical_biconditional&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Colloquial usage")]

One unambiguous way of stating a biconditional in plain English is to adopt the form "_b_ if _a_ and _a_ if _b_"—if the standard form "_a_ if and only if _b_" is not used. Slightly more formally, one could also say that "_b_ implies _a_ and _a_ implies _b_", or "_a_ is necessary and sufficient for _b_". The plain English "if'" may sometimes be used as a biconditional (especially in the context of a mathematical definition[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_biconditional#cite_note-15)). In which case, one must take into consideration the surrounding context when interpreting these words.

For example, the statement "I'll buy you a new wallet if you need one" may be interpreted as a biconditional, since the speaker doesn't intend a valid outcome to be buying the wallet whether or not the wallet is needed (as in a conditional). However, "it is cloudy if it is raining" is generally not meant as a biconditional, since it can still be cloudy even if it is not raining.
