The [compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_compound "Chemical compound")**hydrogen chloride** has the [chemical formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")HCl and as such is a [hydrogen halide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_halide "Hydrogen halide"). At [room temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Room_temperature "Room temperature"), it is a colorless [gas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas "Gas"), which forms white fumes of [hydrochloric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid "Hydrochloric acid") upon contact with atmospheric [water vapor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_vapor "Water vapor"). Hydrogen chloride gas and hydrochloric acid are important in technology and industry. Hydrochloric acid, the [aqueous solution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aqueous_solution "Aqueous solution") of hydrogen chloride, is also commonly given the formula HCl.

[![Image 19](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Hydrochloric_acid_01.jpg/150px-Hydrochloric_acid_01.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hydrochloric_acid_01.jpg)

Hydrochloric acid fumes turning pH paper red showing that the fumes are acidic

Hydrogen chloride is a [diatomic molecule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diatomic_molecule "Diatomic molecule"), consisting of a [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen")[atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom") H and a [chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine") atom Cl connected by a [polar covalent bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polar-covalent_bond "Polar-covalent bond"). The chlorine atom is much more [electronegative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronegativity "Electronegativity") than the hydrogen atom, which makes this bond polar. Consequently, the molecule has a large [dipole moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_dipole_moment "Molecular dipole moment") with a negative [partial charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_charge "Partial charge") (δ−) at the chlorine atom and a positive partial charge (δ+) at the hydrogen atom.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride#cite_note-9) In part because of its high polarity, HCl is very [soluble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soluble "Soluble") in [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water "Water") (and in other polar [solvents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvent "Solvent")).

Upon contact, H 2 O and HCl combine to form [hydronium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydronium "Hydronium")[cations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cation "Cation")[H 3 O]+ and [chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloride "Chloride")[anions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anion "Anion")Cl− through a reversible [chemical reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_reaction "Chemical reaction"):

HCl + H 2 O → [H 3 O]+ + Cl−
The resulting solution is called [hydrochloric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid "Hydrochloric acid") and is a [strong acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strong_acid "Strong acid"). The [acid dissociation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid_dissociation_constant "Acid dissociation constant") or ionization constant, _K_ a, is large, which means HCl dissociates or ionizes practically completely in water. Even in the absence of water, hydrogen chloride can still act as an acid. For example, hydrogen chloride can dissolve in certain other solvents such as [methanol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methanol "Methanol"):

HCl + CH 3 OH → [CH 3 OH 2]+ + Cl−
Hydrogen chloride can [protonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protonation "Protonation") molecules or ions and can also serve as an acid-[catalyst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalyst "Catalyst") for chemical reactions where [anhydrous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anhydrous "Anhydrous") (water-free) conditions are desired.

Because of its acidic nature, hydrogen chloride is a [corrosive substance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corrosive_substance "Corrosive substance"), particularly in the presence of moisture.

Frozen HCl undergoes a phase transition at 98.4 K (−174.8°C; −282.5°F). X-ray powder diffraction of the frozen material shows that the material changes from an [orthorhombic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthorhombic_crystal_system "Orthorhombic crystal system") structure to a [cubic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system "Cubic crystal system") one during this transition. In both structures the chlorine atoms are in a [face-centered array](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cubic_crystal_system#Bravais_lattices "Cubic crystal system"). However, the hydrogen atoms could not be located.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride#cite_note-10) Analysis of spectroscopic and dielectric data, and determination of the structure of DCl (deuterium chloride) indicates that HCl forms zigzag chains in the solid, as does [HF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_fluoride "Hydrogen fluoride") (see figure on right).[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride#cite_note-11)

Infrared (IR) absorption spectrum

[![Image 23](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/27/HCl37.JPG/330px-HCl37.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:HCl37.JPG)

One doublet in the IR spectrum resulting from the isotopic composition of chlorine

The [infrared spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared_spectrum "Infrared spectrum") of gaseous hydrogen chloride, shown on the left, consists of a number of sharp absorption lines grouped around 2886 cm−1 (wavelength ~3.47 μm). At room temperature, almost all molecules are in the ground vibrational state _v_=0. Including anharmonicity the vibrational energy can be written as:

![Image 24: {\displaystyle E_{\mathrm {v} }=h\nu _{e}\left(v+{\tfrac {1}{2}}\right)+hx_{e}\nu _{e}\left(v+{\tfrac {1}{2}}\right)^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68cc630472a776f5c5f86d596e521a162639a221)
To promote an HCl molecule from the _v_=0 to the _v_=1 state, we would expect to see an infrared absorption about _ν_ o=_ν_ e+2 _x_ e _ν_ e =2880 cm−1. However, this absorption corresponding to the Q-branch is not observed due to it being forbidden by symmetry. Instead, two sets of signals (P- and R-branches) are seen owing to a simultaneous change in the rotational state of the molecules. Because of quantum mechanical selection rules, only certain rotational transitions are permitted. The states are characterized by the rotational quantum number _J_= 0, 1, 2, 3, ... selection rules state that Δ _J_ is only able to take values of ±1.

![Image 25: {\displaystyle E(J)_{\mathrm {rot} }=h\cdot B\cdot J(J+1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/096a44217016d3796c21f5b68907685f39eac4ba)
The value of the rotational constant _B_ is much smaller than the vibrational one _ν_ o, such that a much smaller amount of energy is required to rotate the molecule; for a typical molecule, this lies within the microwave region. However, the vibrational energy of HCl molecule places its absorptions within the infrared region, allowing a spectrum showing the rovibrational transitions of this molecule to be easily collected using an [infrared spectrometer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared_spectrometer "Infrared spectrometer") with a gas cell. The latter can even be made of quartz as the HCl absorption lies in a window of transparency for this material.

Naturally abundant chlorine consists of two isotopes, 35 Cl and 37 Cl, in a ratio of approximately 3:1. While the spring constants are nearly identical, the disparate [reduced masses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reduced_mass "Reduced mass") of H 35 Cl and H 37 Cl cause measurable differences in the rotational energy, thus doublets are observed on close inspection of each absorption line, weighted in the same ratio of 3:1.

Most hydrogen chloride produced on an industrial scale is used for [hydrochloric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid "Hydrochloric acid") production.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride#cite_note-Ullmann-13)

In the 17th century, [Johann Rudolf Glauber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Rudolf_Glauber "Johann Rudolf Glauber") from [Karlstadt am Main, Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karlstadt_am_Main "Karlstadt am Main") used [sodium chloride salt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride "Sodium chloride") and [sulfuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid") for the preparation of [sodium sulfate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_sulfate "Sodium sulfate") in the [Mannheim process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mannheim_process "Mannheim process"), releasing hydrogen chloride. [Joseph Priestley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Priestley "Joseph Priestley") of [Leeds, England](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leeds,_England "Leeds, England") prepared pure hydrogen chloride in 1772,[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride#cite_note-14) and by 1808 [Humphry Davy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humphry_Davy "Humphry Davy") of [Penzance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Penzance "Penzance"), England had proved that the chemical composition included [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") and [chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride#cite_note-15)

Flame inside HCl oven

Hydrogen chloride is produced by combining [chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine") and [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen"):

Cl 2 + H 2 → 2 HCl
As the reaction is [exothermic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exothermic_reaction "Exothermic reaction"), the installation is called an HCl [oven](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oven "Oven") or HCl burner. The resulting hydrogen chloride gas is [absorbed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absorption_(chemistry) "Absorption (chemistry)") in [deionized water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deionized_water "Deionized water"), resulting in chemically pure hydrochloric acid. This reaction can give a very pure product, e.g. for use in the food industry.

The reaction can also be triggered by blue light.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride#cite_note-16)

The industrial production of hydrogen chloride is often integrated with the formation of [chlorinated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Halogenation "Halogenation") and [fluorinated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorine "Fluorine")[organic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_chemistry "Organic chemistry") compounds, e.g., [Teflon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Teflon "Teflon"), [Freon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freon "Freon"), and other [CFCs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorofluorocarbon "Chlorofluorocarbon"), as well as [chloroacetic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloroacetic_acid "Chloroacetic acid") and [PVC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride "Polyvinyl chloride"). Often this production of hydrochloric acid is integrated with captive use of it on-site. In the [chemical reactions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_reaction "Chemical reaction"), hydrogen [atoms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom") on the hydrocarbon are replaced by chlorine atoms, whereupon the released hydrogen atom recombines with the spare atom from the chlorine molecule, forming hydrogen chloride. Fluorination is a subsequent chlorine-replacement reaction, producing again hydrogen chloride:

RH + Cl 2 → RCl + HCl RCl + HF → RF + HCl
The resulting hydrogen chloride is either reused directly or absorbed in water, resulting in hydrochloric acid of technical or industrial grade.

Small amounts of hydrogen chloride for laboratory use can be generated in an _HCl [generator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas\_generator "Gas generator")_ by dehydrating hydrochloric acid with either [sulfuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid") or anhydrous [calcium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calcium_chloride "Calcium chloride"). Alternatively, HCl can be generated by the reaction of sulfuric acid with sodium chloride:[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride#cite_note-17)

NaCl + H 2 SO 4 → [NaHSO 4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_bisulfate "Sodium bisulfate") + HCl[↑](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gas_evolution_reaction "Gas evolution reaction")
This reaction occurs at room temperature. Provided there is NaCl remaining in the generator and it is heated above 200°C, the reaction proceeds further:

NaCl + NaHSO 4 → [Na 2 SO 4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_sulfate "Sodium sulfate") + HCl↑
For such generators to function, the reagents should be dry.

Hydrogen chloride can also be prepared by the [hydrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrolysis "Hydrolysis") of certain reactive chloride compounds such as [phosphorus chlorides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_chlorides "Phosphorus chlorides"), [thionyl chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thionyl_chloride "Thionyl chloride") (SOCl 2), and [acyl chlorides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acyl_chloride "Acyl chloride"). For example, cold water can be gradually dripped onto [phosphorus pentachloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphorus_pentachloride "Phosphorus pentachloride") (PCl 5) to give HCl:

PCl 5 + H 2 O → [POCl 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosphoryl_chloride "Phosphoryl chloride") + 2 HCl↑
Most hydrogen chloride is consumed in the production of hydrochloric acid. It is also used in the production of [vinyl chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinyl_chloride "Vinyl chloride") and many [alkyl chlorides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkyl_chloride "Alkyl chloride").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride#cite_note-Ullmann-13)[Trichlorosilane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trichlorosilane "Trichlorosilane"), a precursor to ultrapure silicon, is produced by the reaction of hydrogen chloride and silicon at around 300 °C.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride#cite_note-18)

Si + 3 HCl → HSiCl 3 + H 2
Around 900, the authors of the Arabic writings attributed to [Jabir ibn Hayyan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jabir_ibn_Hayyan "Jabir ibn Hayyan") (Latin: Geber) and the Persian physician and alchemist [Abu Bakr al-Razi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abu_Bakr_al-Razi "Abu Bakr al-Razi") (c. 865–925, Latin: Rhazes) were experimenting with [sal ammoniac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sal_ammoniac "Sal ammoniac") ([ammonium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_chloride "Ammonium chloride")), which when it was distilled together with [vitriol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vitriol "Vitriol") (hydrated [sulfates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfates "Sulfates") of various metals) produced hydrogen chloride.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride#cite_note-19) It is possible that in one of his experiments, al-Razi stumbled upon a primitive method to produce [hydrochloric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid "Hydrochloric acid").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride#cite_note-20) However, it appears that in most of these early experiments with [chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloride "Chloride")[salts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt_(chemistry) "Salt (chemistry)"), the gaseous products were discarded, and hydrogen chloride may have been produced many times before it was discovered that it can be put to chemical use.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride#cite_note-21)

One of the first such uses was the synthesis of [mercury(II) chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury(II)_chloride "Mercury(II) chloride") (corrosive sublimate), whose production from the heating of [mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercury_(element) "Mercury (element)") either with [alum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alum "Alum") and ammonium chloride or with vitriol and sodium chloride was first described in the _De aluminibus et salibus_ ("On Alums and Salts"), an eleventh- or twelfth century Arabic text falsely attributed to Abu Bakr al-Razi and [translated into Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_translations_of_the_12th_century "Latin translations of the 12th century") by [Gerard of Cremona](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gerard_of_Cremona "Gerard of Cremona") (1144–1187).[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride#cite_note-22)

Another important development was the discovery by [pseudo-Geber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Geber "Pseudo-Geber") (in the _De inventione veritatis_, "On the Discovery of Truth", after c. 1300) that by adding ammonium chloride to [nitric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitric_acid "Nitric acid"), a strong solvent capable of dissolving gold (i.e., _[aqua regia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aqua\_regia "Aqua regia")_) could be produced.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride#cite_note-23)

After the discovery in the late sixteenth century of the process by which unmixed [hydrochloric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid "Hydrochloric acid") can be prepared,[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride#cite_note-24) it was recognized that this new acid (then known as _spirit of salt_ or _acidum salis_) released vaporous hydrogen chloride, which was called _marine acid air_. In the 17th century, [Johann Rudolf Glauber](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Rudolf_Glauber "Johann Rudolf Glauber") used salt ([sodium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride "Sodium chloride")) and [sulfuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid") for the preparation of [sodium sulfate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_sulfate "Sodium sulfate"), releasing hydrogen chloride gas (see production, above). In 1772, [Carl Wilhelm Scheele](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Scheele "Carl Wilhelm Scheele") also reported this reaction and is sometimes credited with its discovery. [Joseph Priestley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Priestley "Joseph Priestley") prepared hydrogen chloride in 1772, and in 1810 [Humphry Davy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humphry_Davy "Humphry Davy") established that it is composed of [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen") and [chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine").[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride#cite_note-25)

During the [Industrial Revolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Industrial_Revolution "Industrial Revolution"), demand for [alkaline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkaline "Alkaline") substances such as [soda ash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soda_ash "Soda ash") increased, and [Nicolas Leblanc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicolas_Leblanc "Nicolas Leblanc") developed a new industrial-scale process for producing the soda ash. In the [Leblanc process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leblanc_process "Leblanc process"), salt was converted to soda ash, using sulfuric acid, limestone, and coal, giving hydrogen chloride as by-product. Initially, this gas was vented to air, but the [Alkali Act 1863](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkali_Act_1863 "Alkali Act 1863") prohibited such release, so then soda ash producers absorbed the HCl waste gas in water, producing hydrochloric acid on an industrial scale. Later, the [Hargreaves process](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hargreaves_process&action=edit&redlink=1 "Hargreaves process (page does not exist)") was developed, which is similar to the Leblanc process except [sulfur dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide "Sulfur dioxide"), water, and air are used instead of sulfuric acid in a reaction which is exothermic overall. In the early 20th century the Leblanc process was effectively replaced by the [Solvay process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solvay_process "Solvay process"), which did not produce HCl. However, hydrogen chloride production continued as a step in hydrochloric acid production.

Historical uses of hydrogen chloride in the 20th century include hydrochlorinations of [alkynes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkynes "Alkynes") in producing the chlorinated [monomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monomer "Monomer")[chloroprene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloroprene "Chloroprene") and [vinyl chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vinyl_chloride "Vinyl chloride"), which are subsequently [polymerized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymer "Polymer") to make polychloroprene ([Neoprene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neoprene "Neoprene")) and [polyvinyl chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyvinyl_chloride "Polyvinyl chloride") (PVC), respectively. In the production of vinyl chloride, [acetylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene "Acetylene") (C 2 H 2) is hydrochlorinated by adding the HCl across the [triple bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple_bond "Triple bond") of the C 2 H 2 molecule, turning the triple into a [double bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_bond "Double bond"), yielding vinyl chloride.

The "acetylene process", used until the 1960s for making [chloroprene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloroprene "Chloroprene"), starts out by joining two [acetylene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetylene "Acetylene") molecules, and then adds HCl to the joined intermediate across the [triple bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple_bond "Triple bond") to convert it to chloroprene as shown here:

[![Image 26](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/03/Chloroprene_synthesis.svg/500px-Chloroprene_synthesis.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Chloroprene_synthesis.svg)
This "acetylene process" has been replaced by a process which adds [Cl 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine") to the double bond of ethylene instead, and subsequent elimination produces HCl instead, as well as chloroprene.

Hydrogen chloride forms corrosive [hydrochloric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrochloric_acid "Hydrochloric acid") on contact with water found in body tissue. [Inhalation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inhalation "Inhalation") of the fumes can cause [coughing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cough "Cough"), [choking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Choking "Choking"), [inflammation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflammation "Inflammation") of the nose, throat, and upper [respiratory tract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Respiratory_tract "Respiratory tract"), and in severe cases, [pulmonary edema](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pulmonary_edema "Pulmonary edema"), [circulatory system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circulatory_system "Circulatory system") failure, and death.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride#cite_note-26) Skin contact can cause redness, [pain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pain "Pain"), and severe [chemical burns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_burn "Chemical burn"). Hydrogen chloride may cause severe burns to the eye and permanent eye damage.

The U.S. [Occupational Safety and Health Administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Occupational_Safety_and_Health_Administration "Occupational Safety and Health Administration") and the [National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Institute_for_Occupational_Safety_and_Health "National Institute for Occupational Safety and Health") have established occupational exposure limits for hydrogen chloride at a ceiling of 5 ppm (7 mg/m 3),[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride#cite_note-27) and compiled extensive information on hydrogen chloride workplace safety concerns.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_chloride#cite_note-28)
