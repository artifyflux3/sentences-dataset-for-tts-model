From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b6/Heliospheric-current-sheet.gif/330px-Heliospheric-current-sheet.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Heliospheric-current-sheet.gif)

The [heliospheric current sheet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliospheric_current_sheet "Heliospheric current sheet") results from the influence of the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun")'s [rotating magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotating_magnetic_field "Rotating magnetic field") on the [plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)") in the interplanetary medium.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-1)

The **interplanetary medium** (**IPM**) or **interplanetary space** consists of the mass and energy which fills the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System"), and through which all the larger Solar System bodies, such as [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet"), [dwarf planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dwarf_planet "Dwarf planet"), [asteroids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid "Asteroid"), and [comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet "Comet"), move. The IPM stops at the [heliopause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliopause_(astronomy) "Heliopause (astronomy)"), outside of which the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium") begins. Before 1950, interplanetary space was widely considered to either be an empty vacuum, or consisting of "[aether](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aether_theories "Aether theories")".

Composition and physical characteristics
----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_medium&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Composition and physical characteristics")]

The interplanetary medium includes [interplanetary dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud "Interplanetary dust cloud") and gas, [cosmic rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_ray "Cosmic ray"), and hot [plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)") from the [solar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind "Solar wind").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-Dick_2019-2) The density of the medium is very low, with the solar wind component decreasing in [inverse proportion to the square of the distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse-square_law "Inverse-square law") from the Sun.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-3) The solar wind is variable, and may be affected by [magnetic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field") and [solar activity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_activity "Solar activity") events such as [coronal mass ejections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coronal_mass_ejection "Coronal mass ejection"). Typical particle densities in the interplanetary medium are about 5-40 particles/cm 3, but exhibit substantial variation.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-:0-4): Figure 1  In the vicinity of the [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth"), it contains about 5 particles/cm 3,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-:2-5): 326  but values as high as 100 particles/cm 3 have been observed.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-:0-4): Figure 2

The temperature of the interplanetary medium varies through the solar system. [Joseph Fourier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Fourier "Joseph Fourier") estimated that interplanetary medium must have temperatures comparable to those observed at [Earth's poles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_poles "Earth's poles"), but [on faulty grounds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_for_the_wrong_reason "Right for the wrong reason"): lacking modern estimates of [atmospheric heat transport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmospheric_circulation "Atmospheric circulation"), he saw no other means to explain the relative consistency of [Earth's climate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_climate "Earth's climate").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-6) A very hot interplanetary medium remained a minor position among geophysicists as late as 1959, when Chapman proposed a temperature on the order of 10000 K,[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-:3-7) but observation in [Low Earth orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low_Earth_orbit "Low Earth orbit") of the [exosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exosphere "Exosphere") soon contradicted his position.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] In fact, both Fourier and Chapman's final predictions were correct: because the interplanetary medium is so [rarefied](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rarefied "Rarefied"), it does not exhibit [thermodynamic equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thermodynamic_equilibrium "Thermodynamic equilibrium"). Instead, different components have different temperatures.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-:0-4): 4 [[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-:2-5)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-:1-8) The solar wind exhibits temperatures consistent with Chapman's estimate in [cislunar space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cislunar_space "Cislunar space"),[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-:2-5): 326, 329 [[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-:1-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-9) and dust particles near Earth's orbit exhibit temperatures 257–298 K (3–77°F),[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-:4-10): 157  averaging about 283 K (50°F).[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-11) In general, the solar wind temperature decreases [proportional to the inverse-square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverse-square_law "Inverse-square law") of the distance to the Sun;[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-:3-7) the temperature of the dust decreases proportional to the inverse [cube root](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube_root "Cube root") of the distance.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-:4-10): 157  For dust particles within the [asteroid belt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asteroid_belt "Asteroid belt"), typical temperatures range from 200 K (−100°F) at 2.2 AU down to 165 K (−163°F) at 3.2 AU.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-12)

The solar wind component of the interplanetary medium is a [plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)"), or gas of [ions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ion "Ion"), and has the physical characteristics of a plasma, rather than a simple gas. For example, it carries the Sun's magnetic field with it, is highly electrically conductive[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-Gla%C3%9Fmeier_et_al_2008-13) (resulting in the [heliospheric current sheet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliospheric_current_sheet "Heliospheric current sheet")), forms plasma [double layers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_layer_(plasma) "Double layer (plasma)") where it comes into contact with a planetary magnetosphere or at the [heliopause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliopause_(astronomy) "Heliopause (astronomy)"),[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] and exhibits filamentation (such as in [aurorae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy) "Aurora (astronomy)")).

The plasma in the interplanetary medium is also responsible for the strength of the Sun's magnetic field at the orbit of the Earth being over 100 times greater than originally anticipated. If space were an ideal vacuum, then the Sun's 10−4 tesla magnetic dipole field would reduce with the cube of the distance to about 10−11 tesla. But satellite observations show that it is about 100 times greater at around 7[nT](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nanotesla "Nanotesla").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-Gla%C3%9Fmeier_et_al_2008-13)[Magnetohydrodynamic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetohydrodynamic "Magnetohydrodynamic") (MHD) theory predicts that the motion of a conducting fluid (e.g., the interplanetary medium) in a magnetic field induces electric currents which in turn generate magnetic fields, and in this respect it behaves like an [MHD dynamo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MHD_dynamo "MHD dynamo").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Extent of the interplanetary medium
-----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_medium&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Extent of the interplanetary medium")]

The outer edge of the [heliosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliosphere "Heliosphere") is the boundary between the flow of the solar wind and the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium"). This boundary is known as the [heliopause](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliopause_(astronomy) "Heliopause (astronomy)") and is believed to be a fairly sharp transition[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-14) that was measured at a distance of around 120[AU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit "Astronomical unit") from the Sun by the [Voyager missions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voyager_program "Voyager program").[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-15) The interplanetary medium thus fills the volume contained within the heliopause. The actual shape of this volume remains uncertain, extending only 100 AU toward the [solar apex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_apex "Solar apex") but up to 800 AU in the polar direction.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-16)

Interaction with planets
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_medium&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Interaction with planets")]

How the interplanetary medium interacts with planets depends on whether they have [magnetic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field") or not. Bodies such as the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon") have no magnetic field and the [solar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind "Solar wind") can impact directly on their surface. Over billions of years, the [lunar regolith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_regolith "Lunar regolith") has acted as a collector for solar wind particles, and so studies of rocks from the [lunar surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geology_of_the_Moon "Geology of the Moon") can be valuable in studies of the solar wind.

High-energy particles from the solar wind impacting on the lunar surface also cause it to emit faintly at [X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray") wavelengths.

Planets with their own magnetic field, such as the Earth and [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter"), are surrounded by a [magnetosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetosphere "Magnetosphere") within which their magnetic field is dominant over the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun")'s. This disrupts the flow of the solar wind, which is channelled around the magnetosphere. Material from the solar wind can "leak" into the magnetosphere, causing [aurorae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aurora_(astronomy) "Aurora (astronomy)") and also populating the [Van Allen radiation belts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Van_Allen_radiation_belts "Van Allen radiation belts") with ionised material.

Observable phenomena of the interplanetary medium
-------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Interplanetary_medium&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Observable phenomena of the interplanetary medium")]

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Night_Sky_from_Hawai%E2%80%98i_and_Chile_%28iotw2225c%29.jpg/500px-Night_Sky_from_Hawai%E2%80%98i_and_Chile_%28iotw2225c%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Night_Sky_from_Hawai%E2%80%98i_and_Chile_(iotw2225c).jpg)

The [interplanetary dust cloud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud "Interplanetary dust cloud") illuminated and visible as [zodiacal light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zodiacal_light "Zodiacal light"), with its parts the _false dawn_,[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-17)_[gegenschein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gegenschein "Gegenschein")_ and the rest of its band, which is visually crossed by the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way"), in this composite image of the night sky above the northern and southern hemisphere

The interplanetary medium is responsible for several optical phenomena visible from Earth. [Zodiacal light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zodiacal_light "Zodiacal light") is a broad band of faint light sometimes seen after sunset and before sunrise, stretched along the [ecliptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecliptic "Ecliptic") and appearing brightest near the horizon. This glow is caused by sunlight [scattered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_scattering_by_particles "Light scattering by particles") by [dust particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_dust_cloud "Interplanetary dust cloud") in the interplanetary medium between Earth and the Sun.

A similar phenomenon centered at the [antisolar point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antisolar_point "Antisolar point"), [gegenschein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gegenschein "Gegenschein") is visible in a naturally dark, moonless [night sky](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Night_sky "Night sky"). Much fainter than zodiacal light, this effect is caused by sunlight [backscattered](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Backscatter "Backscatter") by [dust particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_dust "Cosmic dust") beyond Earth's orbit.

The term "interplanetary" appears to have been first used in print in 1691 by the scientist [Robert Boyle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Boyle "Robert Boyle"): "The air is different from the æther (or vacuum) in the... interplanetary spaces" Boyle _Hist. Air_. In 1898, American astronomer [Charles Augustus Young](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_Augustus_Young "Charles Augustus Young") wrote: "Inter-planetary space is a vacuum, far more perfect than anything we can produce by artificial means..." (_The Elements of Astronomy_, Charles Augustus Young, 1898).

The notion that space is considered to be a [vacuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum "Vacuum") filled with an "[aether](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aether_(classical_element) "Aether (classical element)")", or just a cold, dark vacuum continued up until the 1950s. Tufts University Professor of astronomy, Kenneth R. Lang, writing in 2000 noted, "Half a century ago, most people visualized our planet as a solitary sphere traveling in a cold, dark vacuum of space around the Sun".[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interplanetary_medium#cite_note-18) In 2002, Akasofu stated "The view that interplanetary space is a vacuum into which the Sun intermittently emitted corpuscular streams was changed radically by [Ludwig Biermann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ludwig_Biermann "Ludwig Biermann") (1951, 1953) who proposed on the basis of comet tails, that the Sun continuously blows its atmosphere out in all directions at supersonic speed" ([Syun-Ichi Akasofu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syun-Ichi_Akasofu "Syun-Ichi Akasofu"), _Exploring the Secrets of the Aurora_, 2002)
