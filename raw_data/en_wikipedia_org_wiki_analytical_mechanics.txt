In [theoretical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoretical_physics "Theoretical physics") and [mathematical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_physics "Mathematical physics"), **analytical mechanics**, or **theoretical mechanics** is a collection of closely related formulations of [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics"). Analytical mechanics uses _[scalar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar\_(physics) "Scalar (physics)")_ properties of motion representing the system as a whole—usually its [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy") and [potential energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potential_energy "Potential energy"). The [equations of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equations_of_motion "Equations of motion") are derived from the scalar quantity by some underlying principle about the scalar's [variation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus_of_variations "Calculus of variations").

Analytical mechanics was developed by many scientists and mathematicians during the 18th century and onward, after [Newtonian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newtonian_mechanics "Newtonian mechanics"). Newtonian mechanics considers [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") quantities of motion, particularly [accelerations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration"), [momenta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum "Momentum"), [forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force"), of the constituents of the system; it can also be called _vectorial mechanics_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics#cite_note-Lanczos-1) A scalar is a quantity, whereas a vector is represented by quantity and direction. The results of these two different approaches are equivalent, but the analytical mechanics approach has many advantages for complex problems.

Analytical mechanics takes advantage of a system's _constraints_ to solve problems. The constraints limit the [degrees of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(physics_and_chemistry) "Degrees of freedom (physics and chemistry)") the system can have, and can be used to reduce the number of coordinates needed to solve for the motion. The formalism is well suited to arbitrary choices of coordinates, known in the context as [generalized coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_coordinates "Generalized coordinates"). The kinetic and potential energies of the system are expressed using these generalized coordinates or momenta, and the equations of motion can be readily set up, thus analytical mechanics allows numerous mechanical problems to be solved with greater efficiency than fully vectorial methods. It does not always work for non-[conservative forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservative_force "Conservative force") or dissipative forces like [friction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friction "Friction"), in which case one may revert to Newtonian mechanics.

Two dominant branches of analytical mechanics are [Lagrangian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics") (using generalized coordinates and corresponding generalized velocities in [configuration space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(physics) "Configuration space (physics)")) and [Hamiltonian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics "Hamiltonian mechanics") (using coordinates and corresponding momenta in [phase space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_space "Phase space")). Both formulations are equivalent by a [Legendre transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legendre_transformation#Hamilton%E2%80%93Lagrange_mechanics "Legendre transformation") on the generalized coordinates, velocities and momenta; therefore, both contain the same information for describing the dynamics of a system. There are other formulations such as [Hamilton–Jacobi theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamilton%E2%80%93Jacobi_theory "Hamilton–Jacobi theory"), [Routhian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Routhian_mechanics "Routhian mechanics"), and [Appell's equation of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Appell%27s_equation_of_motion "Appell's equation of motion"). All equations of motion for particles and fields, in any formalism, can be derived from the widely applicable result called the [principle of least action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principle_of_least_action "Principle of least action"). One result is [Noether's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noether%27s_theorem "Noether's theorem"), a statement which connects [conservation laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_law "Conservation law") to their associated [symmetries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_(physics) "Symmetry (physics)").

Analytical mechanics does not introduce new physics and is not more general than Newtonian mechanics. Rather it is a collection of equivalent formalisms which have broad application. In fact the same principles and formalisms can be used in [relativistic mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_mechanics "Relativistic mechanics") and [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), and with some modifications, [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") and [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory").

Analytical mechanics is used widely, from fundamental physics to [applied mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Applied_mathematics "Applied mathematics"), particularly [chaos theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory "Chaos theory").

The methods of analytical mechanics apply to discrete particles, each with a finite number of degrees of freedom. They can be modified to describe continuous fields or fluids, which have infinite degrees of freedom. The definitions and equations have a close analogy with those of mechanics.

The goal of mechanical theory is to solve mechanical problems, such as arise in physics and engineering. Starting from a physical system—such as a mechanism or a star system—a [mathematical model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_model "Mathematical model") is developed in the form of a differential equation. The model can be solved numerically or analytically to determine the motion of the system.

Newton's vectorial approach to mechanics describes motion with the help of [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(mathematics_and_physics) "Vector (mathematics and physics)") quantities such as [force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Force "Force"), [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity"), [acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration"). These quantities characterise the [motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Motion "Motion") of a body idealised as a ["mass point"](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_point_geometry "Mass point geometry") or a "[particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle "Particle")" understood as a single point to which a mass is attached. Newton's method has been successfully applied to a wide range of physical problems, including the motion of a particle in [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth")'s [gravitational field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_field "Gravitational field") and the motion of planets around the Sun. In this approach, Newton's laws describe the motion by a differential equation and then the problem is reduced to the solving of that equation.

When a mechanical system contains many particles, however (such as a complex mechanism or a [fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid "Fluid")), Newton's approach is difficult to apply. Using a Newtonian approach is possible, under proper precautions, namely isolating each single particle from the others, and determining all the forces acting on it. Such analysis is cumbersome even in relatively simple systems. Newton thought that [his third law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_third_law "Newton's third law") "action equals reaction" would take care of all complications.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] This is false even for such simple system as [rotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation") of a [solid body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_body "Solid body").[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] In more complicated systems, the vectorial approach cannot give an adequate description.

The analytical approach simplifies problems by treating [mechanical systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mechanical_system "Mechanical system") as ensembles of particles that interact with each other, rather considering each particle as an isolated unit. In the vectorial approach, forces must be determined individually for each particle, whereas in the analytical approach it is enough to know one single function which contains implicitly all the forces acting on and in the system. Such simplification is often done using certain kinematic conditions which are stated _a priori_. However, the analytical treatment does not require the knowledge of these forces and takes these kinematic conditions for granted.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Still, deriving the equations of motion of a complicated mechanical system requires a unifying basis from which they follow.[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] This is provided by various [variational principles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variational_principle "Variational principle"): behind each set of equations there is a principle that expresses the meaning of the entire set. Given a fundamental and universal quantity called [_action_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics) "Action (physics)"), the principle that this action be stationary under small variation of some other mechanical quantity generates the required set of differential equations. The statement of the principle does not require any special [coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system"), and all results are expressed in [generalized coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_coordinates "Generalized coordinates"). This means that the analytical equations of motion do not change upon a [coordinate transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_transformation "Coordinate transformation"), an [invariance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_(physics) "Invariant (physics)") property that is lacking in the vectorial equations of motion.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics#cite_note-Lanczos1-2)

It is not altogether clear what is meant by 'solving' a set of differential equations. A problem is regarded as solved when the particles coordinates at time _t_ are expressed as simple functions of _t_ and of parameters defining the initial positions and velocities. However, 'simple function' is not a [well-defined](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Well-defined "Well-defined") concept: nowadays, a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)")_f_(_t_) is not regarded as a formal expression in _t_ ([elementary function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_function "Elementary function")) as in the time of Newton but most generally as a quantity determined by _t_, and it is not possible to draw a sharp line between 'simple' and 'not simple' functions. If one speaks merely of 'functions', then every mechanical problem is solved as soon as it has been well stated in differential equations, because given the initial conditions and _t_ determine the coordinates at _t_. This is a fact especially at present with the modern methods of [computer modelling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_simulation "Computer simulation") which provide arithmetical solutions to mechanical problems to any desired degree of accuracy, the [differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equation "Differential equation") being replaced by [difference equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Difference_equation "Difference equation").

Still, though lacking precise definitions, it is obvious that the [two-body problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-body_problem "Two-body problem") has a simple solution, whereas the [three-body problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-body_problem "Three-body problem") has not. The two-body problem is solved by formulas involving parameters; their values can be changed to study the class of all solutions, that is, the [mathematical structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_structure "Mathematical structure") of the problem. Moreover, an accurate mental or drawn picture can be made for the motion of two bodies, and it can be as real and accurate as the real bodies moving and interacting. In the three-body problem, parameters can also be assigned specific values; however, the solution at these assigned values or a collection of such solutions does not reveal the mathematical structure of the problem. As in many other problems, the mathematical structure can be elucidated only by examining the differential equations themselves.

Analytical mechanics aims at even more: not at understanding the mathematical structure of a single mechanical problem, but that of a class of problems so wide that they encompass most of mechanics. It concentrates on systems to which Lagrangian or Hamiltonian equations of motion are applicable and that include a very wide range of problems indeed.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics#cite_note-3)

Development of analytical mechanics has two objectives: (i) increase the range of solvable problems by developing standard techniques with a wide range of applicability, and (ii) understand the mathematical structure of mechanics. In the long run, however, (ii) can help (i) more than a concentration on specific problems for which methods have already been designed.

### Generalized coordinates and constraints

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Analytical_mechanics&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Generalized coordinates and constraints")]

In [Newtonian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newtonian_mechanics "Newtonian mechanics"), one customarily uses all three [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates"), or other 3D [coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coordinate_system "Coordinate system"), to refer to a body's [position](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_(vector) "Position (vector)") during its motion. In physical systems, however, some structure or other system usually constrains the body's motion from taking certain directions and pathways. So a full set of Cartesian coordinates is often unneeded, as the constraints determine the evolving relations among the coordinates, which relations can be modeled by equations corresponding to the constraints. In the Lagrangian and Hamiltonian formalisms, the constraints are incorporated into the motion's geometry, reducing the number of coordinates to the minimum needed to model the motion. These are known as _generalized coordinates_, denoted _q i_ (_i_ = 1, 2, 3...).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics#cite_note-4): 231

Generalized coordinates incorporate constraints on the system. There is one generalized coordinate _q i_ for each [degree of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(physics_and_chemistry) "Degrees of freedom (physics and chemistry)") (for convenience labelled by an index _i_ = 1, 2..._N_), i.e. each way the system can change its [configuration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(physics) "Configuration space (physics)"); as curvilinear lengths or angles of rotation. Generalized coordinates are not the same as curvilinear coordinates. The number of _curvilinear_ coordinates equals the [dimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension") of the position space in question (usually 3 for 3d space), while the number of _generalized_ coordinates is not necessarily equal to this dimension; constraints can reduce the number of degrees of freedom (hence the number of generalized coordinates required to define the configuration of the system), following the general rule:[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics#cite_note-autogenerated1-5)[_[dubious](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy\_dispute#Disputed\_statement "Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute") – [discuss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Talk:Analytical\_mechanics#Dubious "Talk:Analytical mechanics")_]

_[**dimension of position space** (usually 3)] × [number of **constituents** of system ("particles")] − (number of **constraints**)_

_= (number of **degrees of freedom**) = (number of **generalized coordinates**)_

For a system with _N_ degrees of freedom, the generalized coordinates can be collected into an _N_-[tuple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple "Tuple"): ![Image 1: {\displaystyle \mathbf {q} =(q_{1},q_{2},\dots ,q_{N})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4108dbed78ac909e8d2bde0e7b21f6909523553d) and the [time derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_derivative "Time derivative") (here denoted by an overdot) of this tuple give the _generalized velocities_: ![Image 2: {\displaystyle {\frac {d\mathbf {q} }{dt}}=\left({\frac {dq_{1}}{dt}},{\frac {dq_{2}}{dt}},\dots ,{\frac {dq_{N}}{dt}}\right)\equiv \mathbf {\dot {q}} =({\dot {q}}_{1},{\dot {q}}_{2},\dots ,{\dot {q}}_{N}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3f97f77da9a4ea06a6cbbae9b478eab92870ae91)

### D'Alembert's principle of virtual work

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Analytical_mechanics&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: D'Alembert's principle of virtual work")]

D'Alembert's principle states that infinitesimal _[virtual work](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virtual\_work "Virtual work")_ done by a force across reversible displacements is zero, which is the work done by a force consistent with ideal constraints of the system. The idea of a constraint is useful – since this limits what the system can do, and can provide steps to solving for the motion of the system. The equation for D'Alembert's principle is:[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics#cite_note-Torby1984-6): 265 ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \delta W={\boldsymbol {\mathcal {Q}}}\cdot \delta \mathbf {q} =0\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2442fd1bacea312c1f30c517f4bac9b74f1466ed) where ![Image 4: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\mathcal {Q}}}=({\mathcal {Q}}_{1},{\mathcal {Q}}_{2},\dots ,{\mathcal {Q}}_{N})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a57262acaabe12f2ebee798021ec58477e9a9e82) are the [generalized forces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_forces "Generalized forces") (script Q instead of ordinary Q is used here to prevent conflict with canonical transformations below) and **q** are the generalized coordinates. This leads to the generalized form of [Newton's laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws "Newton's laws") in the language of analytical mechanics: ![Image 5: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\mathcal {Q}}}={\frac {d}{dt}}\left({\frac {\partial T}{\partial \mathbf {\dot {q}} }}\right)-{\frac {\partial T}{\partial \mathbf {q} }}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5402a765bbd6ce55e7f99be220bf885298bde289)

where _T_ is the total [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy") of the system, and the notation ![Image 6: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial }{\partial \mathbf {q} }}=\left({\frac {\partial }{\partial q_{1}}},{\frac {\partial }{\partial q_{2}}},\dots ,{\frac {\partial }{\partial q_{N}}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5bd0e482b2f8256c76e53f53f7602efafd9e5ea0) is a useful shorthand (see [matrix calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matrix_calculus#Scalar-by-vector "Matrix calculus") for this notation).

If the curvilinear coordinate system is defined by the standard [position vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_vector "Position vector")**r**, and if the position vector can be written in terms of the generalized coordinates **q** and time t in the form: ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} =\mathbf {r} (\mathbf {q} (t),t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/de0192baf63dcc15665c45cd1dbb56cefced4b72) and this relation holds for all times t, then **q** are called _holonomic constraints_.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics#cite_note-7) Vector **r** is explicitly dependent on _t_ in cases when the constraints vary with time, not just because of **q**(_t_). For time-independent situations, the constraints are also called **[scleronomic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scleronomous "Scleronomous")**, for time-dependent cases they are called **[rheonomic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rheonomous "Rheonomous")**.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics#cite_note-autogenerated1-5)

Lagrangian mechanics
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Analytical_mechanics&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Lagrangian mechanics")]

The introduction of generalized coordinates and the fundamental Lagrangian function:

![Image 8: {\displaystyle L(\mathbf {q} ,\mathbf {\dot {q}} ,t)=T(\mathbf {q} ,\mathbf {\dot {q}} ,t)-V(\mathbf {q} ,\mathbf {\dot {q}} ,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2d317dd317acac4e0434577bdde4ca25e7a2c663)
where _T_ is the total [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy") and _V_ is the total [potential energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potential_energy "Potential energy") of the entire system, then either following the [calculus of variations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calculus_of_variations "Calculus of variations") or using the above formula – lead to the [Euler–Lagrange equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%E2%80%93Lagrange_equations "Euler–Lagrange equations");

![Image 9: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}\left({\frac {\partial L}{\partial \mathbf {\dot {q}} }}\right)={\frac {\partial L}{\partial \mathbf {q} }}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba5db7c9f259426f691e498a7ead4a563d04de53)
which are a set of _N_ second-order [ordinary differential equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinary_differential_equation "Ordinary differential equation"), one for each _q i_(_t_).

This formulation identifies the actual path followed by the motion as a selection of the path over which the [time integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_integral "Time integral") of [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy") is least, assuming the total energy to be fixed, and imposing no conditions on the time of transit.

The Lagrangian formulation uses the **[configuration space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(physics) "Configuration space (physics)")** of the system, the [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)") of all possible generalized coordinates:

![Image 10: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {C}}=\{\mathbf {q} \in \mathbb {R} ^{N}\}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ee2b2a05c1bae592ba11710d75690722944a08c)
where ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{N}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/12d5be9beb2f7a56cdee3c6563c9453a913a0c92) is _N_-dimensional [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") space (see also [set-builder notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set-builder_notation "Set-builder notation")). The particular solution to the Euler–Lagrange equations is called a _(configuration) path or trajectory_, i.e. one particular **q**(_t_) subject to the required [initial conditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Initial_conditions "Initial conditions"). The general solutions form a set of possible configurations as functions of time:

![Image 12: {\displaystyle \{\mathbf {q} (t)\in \mathbb {R} ^{N}\,:\,t\geq 0,t\in \mathbb {R} \}\subseteq {\mathcal {C}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a744b222be9f89ba7d81d73a04c92de907157044)
The configuration space can be defined more generally, and indeed more deeply, in terms of [topological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology")[manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold") and the [tangent bundle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_bundle "Tangent bundle").

Hamiltonian mechanics
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Analytical_mechanics&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Hamiltonian mechanics")]

The [Legendre transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legendre_transformation "Legendre transformation") of the Lagrangian replaces the generalized coordinates and velocities (**q**, **q̇**) with (**q**, **p**); the generalized coordinates and the _[generalized momentums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical\_coordinates "Canonical coordinates")_ conjugate to the generalized coordinates:

![Image 13: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} ={\frac {\partial L}{\partial \mathbf {\dot {q}} }}=\left({\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{1}}},{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{2}}},\cdots {\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{N}}}\right)=(p_{1},p_{2}\cdots p_{N})\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/755cca92430fc160b80cff729d58b1d08ce04cf7)
and introduces the Hamiltonian (which is in terms of generalized coordinates and momentums):

![Image 14: {\displaystyle H(\mathbf {q} ,\mathbf {p} ,t)=\mathbf {p} \cdot \mathbf {\dot {q}} -L(\mathbf {q} ,\mathbf {\dot {q}} ,t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02d8ea095fb19629c2bb91179dbe7698f81084d5)
where ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \cdot }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ba2c023bad1bd39ed49080f729cbf26bc448c9ba) denotes the [dot product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dot_product "Dot product"), also leading to [Hamilton's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics "Hamiltonian mechanics"):

![Image 16: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\dot {p}} =-{\frac {\partial H}{\partial \mathbf {q} }}\,,\quad \mathbf {\dot {q}} =+{\frac {\partial H}{\partial \mathbf {p} }}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/978b69872d211bfa764866383772528b71d36401)
which are now a set of 2 _N_ first-order ordinary differential equations, one for each _q i_(_t_) and _p i_(_t_). Another result from the Legendre transformation relates the time derivatives of the Lagrangian and Hamiltonian:

![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\frac {dH}{dt}}=-{\frac {\partial L}{\partial t}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/36650720877459adba7a975bcd807085a5e02417)
which is often considered one of Hamilton's equations of motion additionally to the others. The generalized momentums can be written in terms of the generalized forces in the same way as Newton's second law:

![Image 18: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\dot {p}} ={\boldsymbol {\mathcal {Q}}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fd47b665c53a1e2f42dd3305a2d1b433e2a4453c)
Analogous to the configuration space, the set of all momentums is the **generalized [momentum space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum_space "Momentum space")**:

![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {M}}=\{\mathbf {p} \in \mathbb {R} ^{N}\}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fd6ceced43240e312f146f3d5f32c7728d0bc249)
("Momentum space" also refers to "**k**-space"; the set of all [wave vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_vector "Wave vector") (given by [De Broglie relations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Broglie_relation "De Broglie relation")) as used in quantum mechanics and theory of [waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave "Wave"))

The set of all positions and momentums form the **[phase space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_space "Phase space")**:

![Image 20: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {P}}={\mathcal {C}}\times {\mathcal {M}}=\{(\mathbf {q} ,\mathbf {p} )\in \mathbb {R} ^{2N}\}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/978a6c9242b2457d3ad4fd63f0e74aabeafb3a96)
that is, the [Cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product") of the configuration space and generalized momentum space.

A particular solution to Hamilton's equations is called a _[phase path](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase\_portrait "Phase portrait")_, a particular curve (**q**(_t_),**p**(_t_)) subject to the required initial conditions. The set of all phase paths, the general solution to the differential equations, is the _[phase portrait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase\_portrait "Phase portrait")_:

![Image 21: {\displaystyle \{(\mathbf {q} (t),\mathbf {p} (t))\in \mathbb {R} ^{2N}\,:\,t\geq 0,t\in \mathbb {R} \}\subseteq {\mathcal {P}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f52eacc73d9fdcb53e4d6f2a258bf7bb491f023c)

### The Poisson bracket

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Analytical_mechanics&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: The Poisson bracket")]

All dynamical variables can be derived from position **q**, momentum **p**, and time _t_, and written as a function of these: _A_ = _A_(**q**, **p**, _t_). If _A_(**q**, **p**, _t_) and _B_(**q**, **p**, _t_) are two scalar valued dynamical variables, the _Poisson bracket_ is defined by the generalized coordinates and momentums:

![Image 22: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\{A,B\}\equiv \{A,B\}_{\mathbf {q} ,\mathbf {p} }&={\frac {\partial A}{\partial \mathbf {q} }}\cdot {\frac {\partial B}{\partial \mathbf {p} }}-{\frac {\partial A}{\partial \mathbf {p} }}\cdot {\frac {\partial B}{\partial \mathbf {q} }}\\&\equiv \sum _{k}{\frac {\partial A}{\partial q_{k}}}{\frac {\partial B}{\partial p_{k}}}-{\frac {\partial A}{\partial p_{k}}}{\frac {\partial B}{\partial q_{k}}}\,,\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/690864d895a2dddc8a30367dd0241eb8463f6eec)
Calculating the [total derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_derivative "Total derivative") of one of these, say _A_, and substituting Hamilton's equations into the result leads to the time evolution of _A_:

![Image 23: {\displaystyle {\frac {dA}{dt}}=\{A,H\}+{\frac {\partial A}{\partial t}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/09ef487c6206253a741390cd07b5daec7dbe83f9)
This equation in _A_ is closely related to the equation of motion in the [Heisenberg picture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heisenberg_picture "Heisenberg picture") of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), in which classical dynamical variables become [quantum operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operator_(physics) "Operator (physics)") (indicated by hats (^)), and the Poisson bracket is replaced by the [commutator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutator "Commutator") of operators via Dirac's [canonical quantization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_quantization "Canonical quantization"):

![Image 24: {\displaystyle \{A,B\}\rightarrow {\frac {1}{i\hbar }}[{\hat {A}},{\hat {B}}]\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0990f0c0d756e9d159c393f67053ba88f848789e)

Properties of the Lagrangian and the Hamiltonian
------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Analytical_mechanics&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Properties of the Lagrangian and the Hamiltonian")]

Following are overlapping properties between the Lagrangian and Hamiltonian functions.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics#cite_note-autogenerated1-5)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics#cite_note-8)

*   All the individual generalized coordinates _q i_(_t_), velocities _q̇i_(_t_) and momenta _p i_(_t_) for every degree of freedom are mutually independent. Explicit time-dependence of a function means the function actually includes time _t_ as a variable in addition to the **q**(_t_), **p**(_t_), not simply as a parameter through **q**(_t_) and **p**(_t_), which would mean explicit time-independence.
*   The Lagrangian is invariant under addition of the _[total](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total\_derivative "Total derivative")_[time derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_derivative "Time derivative") of any function of **q'_and_ t _, that is: ![Image 25: {\displaystyle L'=L+{\frac {d}{dt}}F(\mathbf {q} ,t)\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a092aa22be96ff7905c6d63b7bf408fd788cc41) so each Lagrangian_ L _and_ L** describe _exactly the same motion_. In other words, the Lagrangian of a system is not unique.
*   Analogously, the Hamiltonian is invariant under addition of the _[partial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial\_derivative "Partial derivative")_ time derivative of any function of **q**, **p** and _t_, that is: ![Image 26: {\displaystyle K=H+{\frac {\partial }{\partial t}}G(\mathbf {q} ,\mathbf {p} ,t)\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/57b7349a45acbe5e07b559d974e658b08516d1fa) (_K_ is a frequently used letter in this case). This property is used in [canonical transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_transformations "Canonical transformations") (see below).
*   If the Lagrangian is independent of some generalized coordinates, then the generalized momenta conjugate to those coordinates are [constants of the motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_of_motion "Constant of motion"), i.e. are [conserved](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conserved_quantity "Conserved quantity"), this immediately follows from Lagrange's equations: ![Image 27: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial L}{\partial q_{j}}}=0\,\rightarrow \,{\frac {dp_{j}}{dt}}={\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial L}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{j}}}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d39e167cc6f8fb2bb3b6f0551035d3cde431fd88) Such coordinates are "[cyclic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics")" or "ignorable". It can be shown that the Hamiltonian is also cyclic in exactly the same generalized coordinates.
*   If the Lagrangian is time-independent the Hamiltonian is also time-independent (i.e. both are constant in time).
*   If the kinetic energy is a [homogeneous function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneous_function "Homogeneous function") of degree 2 of the generalized velocities, _and_ the Lagrangian is explicitly time-independent, then: ![Image 28: {\displaystyle T((\lambda {\dot {q}}_{i})^{2},(\lambda {\dot {q}}_{j}\lambda {\dot {q}}_{k}),\mathbf {q} )=\lambda ^{2}T(({\dot {q}}_{i})^{2},{\dot {q}}_{j}{\dot {q}}_{k},\mathbf {q} )\,,\quad L(\mathbf {q} ,\mathbf {\dot {q}} )\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b4c03b4f12b2db6b72ee01019dd809c39352d80) where _λ_ is a constant, then the Hamiltonian will be the _total conserved energy_, equal to the total kinetic and potential energies of the system: ![Image 29: {\displaystyle H=T+V=E\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2344137ee38b286034106e45619af86e3b180e01) This is the basis for the [Schrödinger equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Schr%C3%B6dinger_equation "Schrödinger equation"), inserting [quantum operators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operators_(physics) "Operators (physics)") directly obtains it.

Principle of least action
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Analytical_mechanics&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Principle of least action")]

[![Image 30](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Least_action_principle.svg/250px-Least_action_principle.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Least_action_principle.svg)

As the system evolves, **q** traces a path through [configuration space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Configuration_space_(physics) "Configuration space (physics)") (only some are shown). The path taken by the system (red) has a stationary action (δ _S_ = 0) under small changes in the configuration of the system (δ**q**).[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics#cite_note-9)

[Action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Action_(physics) "Action (physics)") is another quantity in analytical mechanics defined as a [functional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_(mathematics) "Functional (mathematics)") of the Lagrangian:

![Image 31: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {S}}=\int _{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}L(\mathbf {q} ,\mathbf {\dot {q}} ,t)dt\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a01c63276027d35eaac9fd3f5a1c38f6830b7198)
A general way to find the equations of motion from the action is the _[principle of least action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principle\_of\_least\_action "Principle of least action")_:[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics#cite_note-10)

![Image 32: {\displaystyle \delta {\mathcal {S}}=\delta \int _{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}L(\mathbf {q} ,\mathbf {\dot {q}} ,t)dt=0\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/502089db2515c18fbd0d324ec5920ba27694444a)
where the departure _t_ 1 and arrival _t_ 2 times are fixed.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics#cite_note-Lanczos-1) The term "path" or "trajectory" refers to the [time evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_evolution "Time evolution") of the system as a path through configuration space ![Image 33: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {C}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e7b3edab7022ca9e2976651bc59c489513ee9019), in other words **q**(_t_) tracing out a path in ![Image 34: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {C}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e7b3edab7022ca9e2976651bc59c489513ee9019). The path for which action is least is the path taken by the system.

From this principle, _all_[equations of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equations_of_motion "Equations of motion") in classical mechanics can be derived. This approach can be extended to fields rather than a system of particles (see below), and underlies the [path integral formulation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Path_integral_formulation "Path integral formulation") of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"),[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics#cite_note-autogenerated2004-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics#cite_note-autogenerated3-12) and is used for calculating [geodesic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geodesic "Geodesic") motion in [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics#cite_note-13)

Hamiltonian-Jacobi mechanics
----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Analytical_mechanics&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Hamiltonian-Jacobi mechanics")]

[Canonical transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_transformations "Canonical transformations")
The invariance of the Hamiltonian (under addition of the partial time derivative of an arbitrary function of **p**, **q**, and _t_) allows the Hamiltonian in one set of coordinates **q** and momenta **p** to be transformed into a new set **Q** = **Q**(**q**, **p**, _t_) and **P** = **P**(**q**, **p**, _t_), in four possible ways:

![Image 35: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}&K(\mathbf {Q} ,\mathbf {P} ,t)=H(\mathbf {q} ,\mathbf {p} ,t)+{\frac {\partial }{\partial t}}G_{1}(\mathbf {q} ,\mathbf {Q} ,t)\\&K(\mathbf {Q} ,\mathbf {P} ,t)=H(\mathbf {q} ,\mathbf {p} ,t)+{\frac {\partial }{\partial t}}G_{2}(\mathbf {q} ,\mathbf {P} ,t)\\&K(\mathbf {Q} ,\mathbf {P} ,t)=H(\mathbf {q} ,\mathbf {p} ,t)+{\frac {\partial }{\partial t}}G_{3}(\mathbf {p} ,\mathbf {Q} ,t)\\&K(\mathbf {Q} ,\mathbf {P} ,t)=H(\mathbf {q} ,\mathbf {p} ,t)+{\frac {\partial }{\partial t}}G_{4}(\mathbf {p} ,\mathbf {P} ,t)\\\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3be69e83f56deed7970cf13eb3bb31190b733e65)
With the restriction on **P** and **Q** such that the transformed Hamiltonian system is:

![Image 36: {\displaystyle \mathbf {\dot {P}} =-{\frac {\partial K}{\partial \mathbf {Q} }}\,,\quad \mathbf {\dot {Q}} =+{\frac {\partial K}{\partial \mathbf {P} }}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f61cd354d14cd40555e35cf1064896ddca18217e)
the above transformations are called _canonical transformations_, each function _G n_ is called a [generating function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generating_function_(physics) "Generating function (physics)") of the "_n_ th kind" or "type-_n_". The transformation of coordinates and momenta can allow simplification for solving Hamilton's equations for a given problem.

The choice of **Q** and **P** is completely arbitrary, but not every choice leads to a canonical transformation. One simple criterion for a transformation **q** → **Q** and **p** → **P** to be canonical is the Poisson bracket be unity,

![Image 37: {\displaystyle \{Q_{i},P_{i}\}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a6c138b4c61e267fa251fb3f1774e6a40dc668fe)
for all _i_ = 1, 2,..._N_. If this does not hold then the transformation is not canonical.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics#cite_note-autogenerated1-5)

The [Hamilton–Jacobi equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamilton%E2%80%93Jacobi_equation "Hamilton–Jacobi equation")
By setting the canonically transformed Hamiltonian _K_ = 0, and the type-2 generating function equal to **Hamilton's principal function** (also the action ![Image 38: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {S}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2302a18e269dbecc43c57c0c2aced3bfae15278d)) plus an arbitrary constant _C_:

![Image 39: {\displaystyle G_{2}(\mathbf {q} ,t)={\mathcal {S}}(\mathbf {q} ,t)+C\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68fd97362044478542c364338e9dfcfacb003679)
the generalized momenta become:

![Image 40: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} ={\frac {\partial {\mathcal {S}}}{\partial \mathbf {q} }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/daad0d26d780a9bc8221fccbeff7c2666b8bb8bf)
and **P** is constant, then the Hamiltonian-Jacobi equation (HJE) can be derived from the type-2 canonical transformation:

![Image 41: {\displaystyle H=-{\frac {\partial {\mathcal {S}}}{\partial t}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8412dffbbbc772774efa451cb1a170b8e4768732)
where _H_ is the Hamiltonian as before:

![Image 42: {\displaystyle H=H(\mathbf {q} ,\mathbf {p} ,t)=H\left(\mathbf {q} ,{\frac {\partial {\mathcal {S}}}{\partial \mathbf {q} }},t\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea9da5b705a640d0e8a7894c733c17594f49f1f8)
Another related function is **Hamilton's characteristic function**

![Image 43: {\displaystyle W(\mathbf {q} )={\mathcal {S}}(\mathbf {q} ,t)+Et}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52305aae24eeb6af2055f21bd779e0443e2b7788)
used to solve the HJE by [additive separation of variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Separation_of_variables "Separation of variables") for a time-independent Hamiltonian _H_.

The study of the solutions of the Hamilton–Jacobi equations leads naturally to the study of [symplectic manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symplectic_manifold "Symplectic manifold") and [symplectic topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symplectic_topology "Symplectic topology").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics#cite_note-Arnold-14)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics#cite_note-Doran-15) In this formulation, the solutions of the Hamilton–Jacobi equations are the [integral curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral_curve "Integral curve") of [Hamiltonian vector fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_vector_field "Hamiltonian vector field").

Routhian mechanics is a hybrid formulation of Lagrangian and Hamiltonian mechanics, not often used but especially useful for removing cyclic coordinates.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] If the Lagrangian of a system has _s_ cyclic coordinates **q** = _q_ 1, _q_ 2, ... _q s_ with conjugate momenta **p** = _p_ 1, _p_ 2, ... _p s_, with the rest of the coordinates non-cyclic and denoted **ζ** = _ζ_ 1, _ζ_ 1, ..., _ζ N − s_, they can be removed by introducing the _Routhian_:

![Image 44: {\displaystyle R=\mathbf {p} \cdot \mathbf {\dot {q}} -L(\mathbf {q} ,\mathbf {p} ,{\boldsymbol {\zeta }},{\dot {\boldsymbol {\zeta }}})\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e751774079bb0830d8b6ab5688917434bb64872)
which leads to a set of 2 _s_ Hamiltonian equations for the cyclic coordinates **q**,

![Image 45: {\displaystyle {\dot {\mathbf {q} }}=+{\frac {\partial R}{\partial \mathbf {p} }}\,,\quad {\dot {\mathbf {p} }}=-{\frac {\partial R}{\partial \mathbf {q} }}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a432a15e49c0bbbc37bdcf79d99e6fef4386c6bf)
and _N_ − _s_ Lagrangian equations in the non cyclic coordinates **ζ**.

![Image 46: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {\partial R}{\partial {\dot {\boldsymbol {\zeta }}}}}={\frac {\partial R}{\partial {\boldsymbol {\zeta }}}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2bc8e1794d171cf15d734a2e43880c22ef9ed12d)
Set up in this way, although the Routhian has the form of the Hamiltonian, it can be thought of a Lagrangian with _N_ − _s_ degrees of freedom.

The coordinates **q** do not have to be cyclic, the partition between which coordinates enter the Hamiltonian equations and those which enter the Lagrangian equations is arbitrary. It is simply convenient to let the Hamiltonian equations remove the cyclic coordinates, leaving the non cyclic coordinates to the Lagrangian equations of motion.

Appellian mechanics
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Analytical_mechanics&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Appellian mechanics")]

[Appell's equation of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Appell%27s_equation_of_motion "Appell's equation of motion") involve generalized accelerations, the second time derivatives of the generalized coordinates:

![Image 47: {\displaystyle \alpha _{r}={\ddot {q}}_{r}={\frac {d^{2}q_{r}}{dt^{2}}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e974dd09ba7c380dd894c71fd02a21744013374)
as well as generalized forces mentioned above in D'Alembert's principle. The equations are

![Image 48: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {Q}}_{r}={\frac {\partial S}{\partial \alpha _{r}}}\,,\quad S={\frac {1}{2}}\sum _{k=1}^{N}m_{k}\mathbf {a} _{k}^{2}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f76b3d4169f32f022a34b5f781a02439e713d24f)
where

![Image 49: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} _{k}={\ddot {\mathbf {r} }}_{k}={\frac {d^{2}\mathbf {r} _{k}}{dt^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/37d2859a614b0c2194776e7f9f9624389eaec986)
is the acceleration of the _k_ particle, the second time derivative of its position vector. Each acceleration **a**_k_ is expressed in terms of the generalized accelerations _α r_, likewise each **r**k are expressed in terms the generalized coordinates _q r_.

Classical field theory
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Analytical_mechanics&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Classical field theory")]

Generalized coordinates apply to discrete particles. For _N_[scalar fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_field "Scalar field")_φ i_(**r**, _t_) where _i_ = 1, 2, ... _N_, the **[Lagrangian density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_density "Lagrangian density")** is a function of these fields and their space and time derivatives, and possibly the space and time coordinates themselves: ![Image 50: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}={\mathcal {L}}(\phi _{1},\phi _{2},\dots ,\nabla \phi _{1},\nabla \phi _{2},\dots ,\partial _{t}\phi _{1},\partial _{t}\phi _{2},\ldots ,\mathbf {r} ,t)\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1a26f83aad67477dd25b2926584c1b105fedd3be) and the Euler–Lagrange equations have an analogue for fields: ![Image 51: {\displaystyle \partial _{\mu }\left({\frac {\partial {\mathcal {L}}}{\partial (\partial _{\mu }\phi _{i})}}\right)={\frac {\partial {\mathcal {L}}}{\partial \phi _{i}}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1bb130245e1443f8ae391e3626e20025e00aff50) where _∂μ_ denotes the [4-gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/4-gradient "4-gradient") and the [summation convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Summation_convention "Summation convention") has been used. For _N_ scalar fields, these Lagrangian field equations are a set of _N_ second order partial differential equations in the fields, which in general will be coupled and nonlinear.

This scalar field formulation can be extended to [vector fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_field "Vector field"), [tensor fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field "Tensor field"), and [spinor fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spinor_field "Spinor field").

The Lagrangian is the [volume integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_integral "Volume integral") of the Lagrangian density:[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics#cite_note-autogenerated3-12)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics#cite_note-16)![Image 52: {\displaystyle L=\int _{\mathcal {V}}{\mathcal {L}}\,dV\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6bcf5343ce61385888bc6b4a3097fdea69812ba5)

Originally developed for classical fields, the above formulation is applicable to all physical fields in classical, quantum, and relativistic situations: such as [Newtonian gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_law_of_universal_gravitation "Newton's law of universal gravitation"), [classical electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_electromagnetism "Classical electromagnetism"), [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"), and [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory"). It is a question of determining the correct Lagrangian density to generate the correct field equation.

The corresponding "momentum" field densities conjugate to the _N_ scalar fields _φ i_(**r**, _t_) are:[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics#cite_note-autogenerated3-12)![Image 53: {\displaystyle \pi _{i}(\mathbf {r} ,t)={\frac {\partial {\mathcal {L}}}{\partial {\dot {\phi }}_{i}}}\,\quad {\dot {\phi }}_{i}\equiv {\frac {\partial \phi _{i}}{\partial t}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/94a72824a50389fe3d01fecfe59b8ef7665e1b53) where in this context the overdot denotes a partial time derivative, not a total time derivative. The **Hamiltonian density**![Image 54: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {H}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/19ef4c7b923a5125ac91aa491838a95ee15b804f) is defined by analogy with mechanics: ![Image 55: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {H}}(\phi _{1},\phi _{2},\ldots ,\pi _{1},\pi _{2},\ldots ,\mathbf {r} ,t)=\sum _{i=1}^{N}{\dot {\phi }}_{i}(\mathbf {r} ,t)\pi _{i}(\mathbf {r} ,t)-{\mathcal {L}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/948dfa1984da8babf5ccc998aade5357217e6a3d)

The equations of motion are: ![Image 56: {\displaystyle {\dot {\phi }}_{i}=+{\frac {\delta {\mathcal {H}}}{\delta \pi _{i}}}\,,\quad {\dot {\pi }}_{i}=-{\frac {\delta {\mathcal {H}}}{\delta \phi _{i}}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/881ef54b4daf3194ab22b58ffceed770e49e8030) where the [variational derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variational_derivative "Variational derivative")![Image 57: {\displaystyle {\frac {\delta }{\delta \phi _{i}}}={\frac {\partial }{\partial \phi _{i}}}-\partial _{\mu }{\frac {\partial }{\partial (\partial _{\mu }\phi _{i})}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a85f51075670856427c7152fc9a199dc2dc8838d) must be used instead of merely partial derivatives. For _N_ fields, these Hamiltonian field equations are a set of 2 _N_ first order partial differential equations, which in general will be coupled and nonlinear.

Again, the volume integral of the Hamiltonian density is the Hamiltonian ![Image 58: {\displaystyle H=\int _{\mathcal {V}}{\mathcal {H}}\,dV\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a8494c89892fdb27c4f6ac5e740e65679ff3bc8)

Symmetry, conservation, and Noether's theorem
---------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Analytical_mechanics&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Symmetry, conservation, and Noether's theorem")]

[Symmetry transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_(physics) "Symmetry (physics)") in classical space and time
Each transformation can be described by an operator (i.e. function acting on the position **r** or momentum **p** variables to change them). The following are the cases when the operator does not change **r** or **p**, i.e. symmetries.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics#cite_note-autogenerated2004-11)

[Noether's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noether%27s_theorem "Noether's theorem")
Noether's theorem states that a [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_variable "Continuous variable") symmetry transformation of the action corresponds to a [conservation law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_law_(physics) "Conservation law (physics)"), i.e. the action (and hence the Lagrangian) does not change under a transformation parameterized by a [parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameter "Parameter")_s_: ![Image 77: {\displaystyle L[q(s,t),{\dot {q}}(s,t)]=L[q(t),{\dot {q}}(t)]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a306c98f32aae1bd3da6bef5e96de807c6343bb) the Lagrangian describes the same motion independent of _s_, which can be length, angle of rotation, or time. The corresponding momenta to _q_ will be conserved.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analytical_mechanics#cite_note-autogenerated1-5)
