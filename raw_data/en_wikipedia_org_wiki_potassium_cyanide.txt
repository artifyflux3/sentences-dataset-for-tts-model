**Potassium cyanide** is a compound with the [formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula")**KCN**. It is a colorless salt, similar in appearance to [sugar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sugar "Sugar"), that is highly [soluble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soluble "Soluble") in water. Most KCN is used in [gold mining](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold_mining "Gold mining"), [organic synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_synthesis "Organic synthesis"), and [electroplating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electroplating "Electroplating"). Smaller applications include [jewelry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jewelry "Jewelry") for chemical [gilding](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gilding "Gilding") and [buffing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buffing "Buffing").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide#cite_note-Ullmann-4) Potassium cyanide is highly toxic, and a dose of 200 to 300 milligrams will kill nearly any human.

The moist solid emits small amounts of [hydrogen cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide "Hydrogen cyanide") due to [hydrolysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrolysis "Hydrolysis") (reaction with water). Hydrogen cyanide is often described as having an odor resembling that of [bitter almonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Almond#Sweet_and_bitter_almonds "Almond").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide#cite_note-6)

The taste of potassium cyanide has been described as acrid and bitter, with a burning sensation similar to lye.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide#cite_note-7) However, potassium cyanide kills so rapidly its taste has not been reliably documented. In 2006, an Indian man named M.P. Prasad killed himself using potassium cyanide. He was a [goldsmith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goldsmith "Goldsmith") and was aware of the mystery behind its taste. In the suicide note Prasad left, the final words written were that potassium cyanide "burns the tongue and tastes acrid", but for obvious reasons this description has not been independently confirmed.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide#cite_note-Sydney_Herald-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide#cite_note-Labatut-9)

KCN is produced by treating [hydrogen cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide "Hydrogen cyanide") with an aqueous solution of [potassium hydroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_hydroxide "Potassium hydroxide"), followed by evaporation of the solution in a vacuum:[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide#cite_note-Ullmann-4)

HCN + KOH → KCN + H 2 O

About 50,000 tons of potassium cyanide are produced yearly.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide#cite_note-Ullmann-4)

### Historical production

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Potassium_cyanide&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Historical production")]

Before 1900 and the invention of the [Castner process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Castner_process "Castner process"), potassium cyanide was the most important source of [alkali metal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkali_metal "Alkali metal")[cyanides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_cyanide "Sodium cyanide").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide#cite_note-Ullmann-4) In this historical process, potassium cyanide was produced by decomposing [potassium ferrocyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_ferrocyanide "Potassium ferrocyanide"):[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide#cite_note-Wagner-10)

K 4[Fe(CN)6] → 4 KCN + FeC 2 + N 2

In aqueous solution, KCN is dissociated into hydrated potassium (K+) ions and [cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanide "Cyanide") (CN−) ions. As a solid, KCN has structure resembling [sodium chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_chloride "Sodium chloride"): with each potassium ion surrounded by six cyanide ions, and vice versa. Despite being diatomic, and thus less symmetric than chloride, the cyanide ions rotate so rapidly that their time-averaged shape is spherical. At low temperature and high pressure, this free rotation is hindered, resulting in a less symmetric crystal structure with the cyanide ions arranged in sheets.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide#cite_note-Stokes1993-12)

KCN and [sodium cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_cyanide "Sodium cyanide") (NaCN) are widely used in [organic synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_synthesis "Organic synthesis") for the preparation of [nitriles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrile "Nitrile") and [carboxylic acids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carboxylic_acid "Carboxylic acid"), particularly in the [von Richter reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Von_Richter_reaction "Von Richter reaction"). It also finds use for the synthesis of [hydantoins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydantoins "Hydantoins"), which can be useful synthetic intermediates, when reacted with a carbonyl compound such as an [aldehyde](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aldehyde "Aldehyde") or [ketone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ketone "Ketone") in the presence of [ammonium carbonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonium_carbonate "Ammonium carbonate").

KCN is used as a [photographic fixer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photographic_fixer "Photographic fixer") in the wet plate [collodion process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collodion_process "Collodion process").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide#cite_note-13) The KCN dissolves silver where it has not been made insoluble by the developer. This reveals and stabilizes the image, making it no longer sensitive to light. Modern wet plate photographers may prefer less toxic fixers, often opting for [sodium thiosulfate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_thiosulfate "Sodium thiosulfate"), but KCN is still used.

In the 19th century, cyanogen [soap](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soap "Soap"), a preparation containing potassium cyanide, was used by [photographers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photography "Photography") to remove [silver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silver "Silver") stains from their hands.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide#cite_note-14): 11 [[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide#cite_note-15): 73 [[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide#cite_note-16)

### Potassium gold cyanide

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Potassium_cyanide&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Potassium gold cyanide")]

In [gold mining](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold_mining "Gold mining"), KCN forms the water-soluble salt [potassium gold cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_gold_cyanide "Potassium gold cyanide") (or gold potassium cyanide) and potassium hydroxide from gold metal in the presence of oxygen (usually from the surrounding air) and water:

4 Au + 8 KCN + O 2 + 2 H 2 O → 4 K[Au(CN)2] + 4 KOH
A similar process uses NaCN to produce [sodium gold cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_gold_cyanide "Sodium gold cyanide") (NaAu(CN 2)).

### Analytical chemistry

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Potassium_cyanide&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Analytical chemistry")]

In analytical chemistry, potassium cyanide is used as complexing agent for chemical analysis of zinc in water and wastewater. The cyanide group complexes zinc and other heavy metals, which is later extracted and analyzed in a spectro-photometer.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide#cite_note-17)

Potassium cyanide is a potent inhibitor of [cellular respiration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellular_respiration "Cellular respiration"), acting on mitochondrial [cytochrome c oxidase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytochrome_c_oxidase "Cytochrome c oxidase"), hence blocking [oxidative phosphorylation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidative_phosphorylation "Oxidative phosphorylation"). [Lactic acidosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lactic_acidosis "Lactic acidosis") then occurs as a consequence of [anaerobic metabolism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anaerobic_metabolism "Anaerobic metabolism"). Initially, acute cyanide poisoning causes a red or ruddy complexion in the victim because the tissues are not able to use the [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") in the blood. The effects of potassium cyanide and sodium cyanide are identical, and symptoms of poisoning typically occur within a few minutes of ingesting the substance: the person loses consciousness, and [brain death](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brain_death "Brain death") eventually follows. During this period the victim may suffer [convulsions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convulsion "Convulsion"). Death is caused by [histotoxic hypoxia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Histotoxic_hypoxia#Cyanide "Histotoxic hypoxia")/[cerebral hypoxia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cerebral_hypoxia "Cerebral hypoxia"). The expected LD100 dose (human) for potassium cyanide is 200–300 mg while the median lethal dose LD50 is estimated at 140 mg.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide#cite_note-18)

Due to toxicity considerations, the disposal of cyanide is subject to stringent regulations. Industrial cyanide effluent is typically destroyed by oxidation using [peroxysulfuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peroxysulfuric_acid_(disambiguation) "Peroxysulfuric acid (disambiguation)"), [hydrogen peroxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_peroxide "Hydrogen peroxide"), [sulfur dioxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfur_dioxide "Sulfur dioxide")/copper salts ("Inco process") or all three ("Combiox Process"). Use of [sodium hypochlorite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_hypochlorite "Sodium hypochlorite"), traditional for laboratory-scale wastes, is impractical on a commercial scale. Hydrolysis at higher temperatures is highly effective, but requires specialized equipment. Lastly, cyanide wastes can be acidified for recovery of [hydrogen cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide "Hydrogen cyanide").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide#cite_note-Ullmann-4)
