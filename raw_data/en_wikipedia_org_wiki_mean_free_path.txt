[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Gamma_ray_mean_free_path.png/250px-Gamma_ray_mean_free_path.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Gamma_ray_mean_free_path.png)

Mean free path of [gamma rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_rays "Gamma rays") ([very high energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Very_high_energy "Very high energy") and [ultra high energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultra_high_energy "Ultra high energy")) based on photon energy (expressed in [electron-volts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron-volts "Electron-volts") on horizontal axis). Mean free path is expressed on a 10 log scale of [mega-parsecs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Megaparsecs "Megaparsecs") (i.e. "–1" indicates 0.1 Mpc, "3" equals 1,000 Mpc, etc.). The primary form of [attenuation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attenuation "Attenuation") is [pair production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pair_production "Pair production") by collision with [extragalactic background light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extragalactic_background_light "Extragalactic background light") (EBL) and [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background") (CMB).

In [physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics "Physics"), **mean free path** is the average distance over which a moving [particle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle "Particle") (such as an [atom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atom "Atom"), a [molecule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule"), or a [photon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon")) travels before substantially changing its direction or energy (or, in a specific context, other properties), typically as a result of one or more successive [collisions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collisions "Collisions") with other particles.

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/5/52/Mean_free_path.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Mean_free_path.png)

Slab of target

Imagine a beam of particles being shot through a target, and consider an infinitesimally thin slab of the target (see the figure).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_free_path#cite_note-1) The atoms (or particles) that might stop a beam particle are shown in red. The magnitude of the mean free path depends on the characteristics of the system. Assuming that all the target particles are at rest but only the beam particle is moving, that gives an expression for the mean free path:

![Image 3: {\displaystyle \ell =(\sigma n)^{-1},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/453f307180558c6f9a4db255e01997dc647e2a6c)

where ℓ is the mean free path, n is the number of target particles per unit volume, and σ is the effective [cross-sectional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross_section_(physics) "Cross section (physics)") area for collision.

The area of the slab is _L_ 2, and its volume is _L_ 2 _dx_. The typical number of stopping atoms in the slab is the concentration n times the volume, i.e., _n L_ 2 _dx_. The probability that a beam particle will be stopped in that slab is the net area of the stopping atoms divided by the total area of the slab:

![Image 4: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {P}}({\text{stopping within }}dx)={\frac {{\text{Area}}_{\text{atoms}}}{{\text{Area}}_{\text{slab}}}}={\frac {\sigma nL^{2}\,dx}{L^{2}}}=n\sigma \,dx,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e29e924092c824d4f27e04b356e77365bbd94ef)

where σ is the area (or, more formally, the "[scattering cross-section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scattering_cross-section "Scattering cross-section")") of one atom.

The drop in beam intensity equals the incoming beam intensity multiplied by the probability of the particle being stopped within the slab:

![Image 5: {\displaystyle dI=-In\sigma \,dx.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3942d655aaba65bbf14d0a3792d2bbbafbdc9cf9)

This is an [ordinary differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordinary_differential_equation "Ordinary differential equation"):

![Image 6: {\displaystyle {\frac {dI}{dx}}=-In\sigma {\overset {\text{def}}{=}}-{\frac {I}{\ell }},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e871d7ec2443d166c6cac605fb7fa77a38855d8d)

whose solution is known as [Beer–Lambert law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beer%E2%80%93Lambert_law "Beer–Lambert law") and has the form ![Image 7: {\displaystyle I=I_{0}e^{-x/\ell }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/12c11548d3281c105f529f00c502c3fcd99fce8c), where x is the distance traveled by the beam through the target, and _I_ 0 is the beam intensity before it entered the target; ℓ is called the mean free path because it equals the [mean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean "Mean") distance traveled by a beam particle before being stopped. To see this, note that the probability that a particle is absorbed between x and _x_ + _dx_ is given by

![Image 8: {\displaystyle d{\mathcal {P}}(x)={\frac {I(x)-I(x+dx)}{I_{0}}}={\frac {1}{\ell }}e^{-x/\ell }dx.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1844cc8e7c24509b6376c5df99639c84e06ebbfe)

Thus the [expectation value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expectation_value "Expectation value") (or average, or simply mean) of x is

![Image 9: {\displaystyle \langle x\rangle {\overset {\text{def}}{=}}\int _{0}^{\infty }xd{\mathcal {P}}(x)=\int _{0}^{\infty }{\frac {x}{\ell }}e^{-x/\ell }\,dx=\ell .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2216abdedb0405d8b6fb739aa3e8ef814321c550)

The fraction of particles that are not stopped ([attenuated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attenuation "Attenuation")) by the slab is called [transmission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transmittance "Transmittance")![Image 10: {\displaystyle T=I/I_{0}=e^{-x/\ell }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88b327453d4fb81a1d97c3c5c4bdb8cfa40d3a48), where x is equal to the thickness of the slab.

Kinetic theory of gases
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mean_free_path&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Kinetic theory of gases")]

In the [kinetic theory of gases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_theory_of_gases "Kinetic theory of gases"), the _mean free path_ of a particle, such as a [molecule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecule "Molecule"), is the average distance the particle travels between collisions with other moving particles. The derivation above assumed the target particles to be at rest; therefore, in reality, the formula ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \ell =(n\sigma )^{-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2e92faccfb35ab8f93e765bda60a3e77c7c5798c) holds for a beam particle with a high speed ![Image 12: {\displaystyle v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e07b00e7fc0847fbd16391c778d65bc25c452597) relative to the velocities of an ensemble of identical particles with random locations. In that case, the motions of target particles are comparatively negligible, hence the relative velocity ![Image 13: {\displaystyle v_{\text{rel}}\approx v}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53b1a1d636cb8628760c053e23497f4b36956276).

If, on the other hand, the beam particle is part of an established equilibrium with identical particles, then the square of relative velocity is:

![Image 14: {\displaystyle \left\langle \mathbf {v} _{\text{relative}}^{2}\right\rangle =\left\langle \left(\mathbf {v} _{1}-\mathbf {v} _{2}\right)^{2}\right\rangle =\left\langle \mathbf {v} _{1}^{2}+\mathbf {v} _{2}^{2}-2\mathbf {v} _{1}\cdot \mathbf {v} _{2}\right\rangle .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2c48f966347918b1e81e09aec5adefaf93c756e9)

In equilibrium, ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/282458bb19c231f94697405bddd93af04a34cabe) and ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/498720fbe6f897f2b86d2cf0f37498d682932aa0) are random and uncorrelated, therefore ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \langle \mathbf {v} _{1}\cdot \mathbf {v} _{2}\rangle =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b3e883c9b2307e0c4466b5d8d12dd9814334eb59), and the relative speed is

![Image 18: {\displaystyle v_{\text{rel}}={\sqrt {\langle \mathbf {v} _{\text{relative}}^{2}\rangle }}={\sqrt {\langle \mathbf {v} _{1}^{2}+\mathbf {v} _{2}^{2}\rangle }}={\sqrt {2}}v.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5b7980df0b35784aa4456760ccf6b3ce76ddd558)

This means that the number of collisions is ![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b4afc1e27d418021bf10898eb44a7f5f315735ff) times the number with stationary targets. Therefore, the following relationship applies:[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_free_path#cite_note-2)

![Image 20: {\displaystyle \ell =\left({\sqrt {2}}\,n\sigma \right)^{-1},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ee6d5939f1ba3b1abb5b687f06bace66d209377)

and using ![Image 21: {\displaystyle n=N/V=p/(k_{\text{B}}T)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/424a1797b1ce25ed39980cd7f290ca6019396970) ([ideal gas law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_gas_law "Ideal gas law")) and ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \sigma =\pi d^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d9b9f378a573e715907a68854ca5797a35a4af6a) (effective cross-sectional area for spherical particles with diameter ![Image 23: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab)), it may be shown that the mean free path is[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_free_path#cite_note-3)

![Image 24: {\displaystyle \ell ={\frac {k_{\text{B}}T}{{\sqrt {2}}\pi d^{2}p}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c466aeb86c8a8894fe6a78078faa9bfbd20ed775)

where _k_ B is the [Boltzmann constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boltzmann_constant "Boltzmann constant"), ![Image 25: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) is the pressure of the gas and ![Image 26: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0) is the absolute temperature.

In practice, the diameter of gas molecules is not well defined. In fact, the [kinetic diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_diameter "Kinetic diameter") of a molecule is defined in terms of the mean free path. Typically, gas molecules do not behave like hard spheres, but rather attract each other at larger distances and repel each other at shorter distances, as can be described with a [Lennard-Jones potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lennard-Jones_potential "Lennard-Jones potential"). One way to deal with such "soft" molecules is to use the Lennard-Jones σ parameter as the diameter.

Another way is to assume a hard-sphere gas that has the same [viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamic_viscosity "Dynamic viscosity") as the actual gas being considered. This leads to a mean free path [[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_free_path#cite_note-4)

![Image 27: {\displaystyle \ell ={\frac {\mu }{\rho }}{\sqrt {\frac {\pi m}{2k_{\text{B}}T}}}={\frac {\mu }{p}}{\sqrt {\frac {\pi k_{\text{B}}T}{2m}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9005fb407a26ea323a57c405f29bac077b43f52a)

where ![Image 28: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) is the [molecular mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_mass "Molecular mass"), ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \rho =mp/(k_{\text{B}}T)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc56d56daa32ec7a221ba2cadfe5a6ceeb7ff359) is the density of ideal gas, and _μ_ is the dynamic viscosity. This expression can be put into the following convenient form

![Image 30: {\displaystyle \ell ={\frac {\mu }{p}}{\sqrt {\frac {\pi R_{\text{specific}}T}{2}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ba4359c39e64ca29bd4334fb4c685895fab71b0)

with ![Image 31: {\displaystyle R_{\text{specific}}=k_{\text{B}}/m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aeb7539ef355ad0e344a21ec7e3bc1f3db11c4bd) being the [specific gas constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Specific_gas_constant "Specific gas constant"), equal to 287 J/(kg*K) for air. Viscosity _μ_ is low, 18.5 μPa·s at (25 °C, 1 bar), and p-dependent.

Mean free path for photons in energy range from 1 keV to 10 MeV for elements with [_Z_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_number "Atomic number") = 1 to 100.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_free_path#cite_note-7) The discontinuities are due to low density of gas elements. In [gamma-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma-ray "Gamma-ray")[radiography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiography "Radiography") the _mean free path_ of a [pencil beam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pencil_beam "Pencil beam") of mono-energetic [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon") is the average distance a photon travels between collisions with atoms of the target material. It depends on the material and the energy of the photons:

![Image 33: {\displaystyle \ell =\mu ^{-1}=((\mu /\rho )\rho )^{-1},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d0df61c323d64b2bd00b1066f925357c008c7e2f)

where _μ_ is the [linear attenuation coefficient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_attenuation_coefficient "Linear attenuation coefficient"), _μ/ρ_ is the [mass attenuation coefficient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_attenuation_coefficient "Mass attenuation coefficient") and _ρ_ is the [density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density "Density") of the material. The [mass attenuation coefficient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_attenuation_coefficient "Mass attenuation coefficient") can be looked up or calculated for any material and energy combination using the [National Institute of Standards and Technology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Institute_of_Standards_and_Technology "National Institute of Standards and Technology") (NIST) databases.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_free_path#cite_note-NIST1-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_free_path#cite_note-NIST2-9)

In [X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray")[radiography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiography "Radiography") the calculation of the _mean free path_ is more complicated, because photons are not mono-energetic, but have some [distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency_distribution "Frequency distribution") of energies called a [spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectrum "Spectrum"). As photons move through the target material, they are [attenuated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attenuation "Attenuation") with probabilities depending on their energy, as a result their distribution changes in process called spectrum hardening. Because of spectrum hardening, the _mean free path_ of the [X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray") spectrum changes with distance.

Sometimes one measures the thickness of a material in the _number of mean free paths_. Material with the thickness of one _mean free path_ will attenuate to 37% (1/[_e_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E_(mathematical_constant) "E (mathematical constant)")) of photons. This concept is closely related to [half-value layer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Half-value_layer "Half-value layer") (HVL): a material with a thickness of one HVL will attenuate 50% of photons. A standard x-ray image is a transmission image, an image with negative logarithm of its intensities is sometimes called a _number of mean free paths_ image.

In macroscopic charge transport, the mean free path of a [charge carrier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charge_carrier "Charge carrier") in a metal ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \ell }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f066e981e530bacc07efc6a10fa82deee985929e) is proportional to the [electrical mobility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_mobility "Electrical mobility")![Image 35: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161), a value directly related to [electrical conductivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrical_conductivity "Electrical conductivity"), that is: ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \mu ={\frac {q\tau }{m}}={\frac {q\ell }{m^{*}v_{\text{F}}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e536145348116e6c7014858e643d0bada4ab36de)

where _q_ is the [charge](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elementary_charge "Elementary charge"), ![Image 37: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) is the [mean free time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_free_time "Mean free time"), _m*_ is the [effective mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effective_mass_(solid-state_physics) "Effective mass (solid-state physics)"), and _v_ F is the [Fermi velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi_velocity "Fermi velocity") of the charge carrier. The Fermi velocity can easily be derived from the [Fermi energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fermi_energy "Fermi energy") via the non-relativistic kinetic energy equation. In [thin films](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thin_film "Thin film"), however, the film thickness can be smaller than the predicted mean free path, making surface scattering much more noticeable, effectively increasing the [resistivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resistivity "Resistivity").

[Electron mobility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electron_mobility "Electron mobility") through a medium with dimensions smaller than the mean free path of electrons occurs through [ballistic conduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ballistic_conduction "Ballistic conduction") or ballistic transport. In such scenarios electrons alter their motion only in collisions with conductor walls.

If one takes a suspension of non-light-absorbing particles of diameter _d_ with a [volume fraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume_fraction "Volume fraction")_Φ_, the mean free path of the photons is:[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_free_path#cite_note-10)

![Image 38: {\displaystyle \ell ={\frac {2d}{3\Phi Q_{\text{s}}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a0a9e79527ae72fde116dde28b31bd1887700a83)

where _Q_ s is the scattering efficiency factor. _Q_ s can be evaluated numerically for spherical particles using [Mie theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mie_theory "Mie theory").

In an otherwise empty cavity, the mean free path of a single particle bouncing off the walls is:

![Image 39: {\displaystyle \ell ={\frac {FV}{S}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/03b48ad72e1c9403fe914b76976bd2a6ac90940b)

where _V_ is the volume of the cavity, _S_ is the total inside surface area of the cavity, and _F_ is a constant related to the shape of the cavity. For most simple cavity shapes, _F_ is approximately 4.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_free_path#cite_note-YoungRW-11)

This relation is used in the derivation of the [Sabine equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reverberation "Reverberation") in acoustics, using a geometrical approximation of sound propagation.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_free_path#cite_note-12)

### Nuclear and particle physics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mean_free_path&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Nuclear and particle physics")]

In particle physics the concept of the mean free path is not commonly used, being replaced by the similar concept of [attenuation length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Attenuation_length "Attenuation length"). In particular, for high-energy photons, which mostly interact by electron–positron [pair production](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pair_production "Pair production"), the [radiation length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiation_length "Radiation length") is used much like the mean free path in radiography.

Independent-particle models in nuclear physics require the undisturbed orbiting of [nucleons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nucleon "Nucleon") within the [nucleus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atomic_nucleus "Atomic nucleus") before they interact with other nucleons.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_free_path#cite_note-13)

> The effective mean free path of a nucleon in nuclear matter must be somewhat larger than the nuclear dimensions in order to allow the use of the independent particle model. This requirement seems to be in contradiction to the assumptions made in the theory ... We are facing here one of the fundamental problems of nuclear structure physics which has yet to be solved.

— John Markus Blatt and [Victor Weisskopf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Victor_Weisskopf "Victor Weisskopf"), _Theoretical nuclear physics_ (1952)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_free_path#cite_note-14)
