A **space telescope** (also known as **space observatory**) is a [telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telescope "Telescope") in outer space used to observe astronomical objects. Suggested by [Lyman Spitzer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lyman_Spitzer "Lyman Spitzer") in 1946, the first operational telescopes were the American [Orbiting Astronomical Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbiting_Astronomical_Observatory "Orbiting Astronomical Observatory"), [OAO-2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OAO-2 "OAO-2") launched in 1968, and the Soviet [Orion 1 ultraviolet telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(space_telescope) "Orion (space telescope)") aboard space station [Salyut 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salyut_1 "Salyut 1") in 1971. Space telescopes avoid several problems caused by the atmosphere, including the absorption or scattering of certain wavelengths of light, obstruction by clouds, and distortions due to atmospheric refraction such as [twinkling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twinkling "Twinkling"). Space telescopes can also observe dim objects during the daytime, and they avoid [light pollution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_pollution "Light pollution") which [ground-based observatories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observatory#Ground-based_observatories "Observatory") encounter. They are divided into two types: Satellites which map the entire sky ([astronomical survey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_survey "Astronomical survey")), and satellites which focus on selected [astronomical objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_object "Astronomical object") or parts of the sky and beyond. Space telescopes are distinct from [Earth imaging satellites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth_imaging_satellite "Earth imaging satellite"), which point toward Earth for [satellite imaging](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Satellite_imaging "Satellite imaging"), applied for [weather analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weather_satellite "Weather satellite"), [espionage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reconnaissance_satellite "Reconnaissance satellite"), and [other types of information gathering](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remote_sensing "Remote sensing").

History
-------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Space_telescope&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: History")]

In 1946, American theoretical astrophysicist [Lyman Spitzer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lyman_Spitzer "Lyman Spitzer"), "father of Hubble" proposed to put a telescope in space.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_telescope#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_telescope#cite_note-2) Spitzer's proposal called for a large telescope that would not be hindered by Earth's atmosphere. After lobbying in the 1960s and 70s for such a system to be built, Spitzer's vision ultimately materialized into the [Hubble Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope "Hubble Space Telescope"), which was launched on April 24, 1990, by the [Space Shuttle _Discovery_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Shuttle_Discovery "Space Shuttle Discovery") (STS-31).[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_telescope#cite_note-3) This was launched due to many efforts by Nancy Grace Roman, "mother of Hubble", who was the first Chief of Astronomy and first female executive at NASA.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_telescope#cite_note-4) She was a program scientist that worked to convince NASA, Congress, and others that Hubble was "very well worth doing".[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_telescope#cite_note-5)

The first operational space telescopes were the American [Orbiting Astronomical Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbiting_Astronomical_Observatory "Orbiting Astronomical Observatory"), [OAO-2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OAO-2 "OAO-2") launched in 1968, and the Soviet [Orion 1 ultraviolet telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_(space_telescope) "Orion (space telescope)") aboard space station [Salyut 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salyut_1 "Salyut 1") in 1971.

Advantages
----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Space_telescope&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Advantages")]

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/ff/NASA%27s_Great_Observatories_Provide_a_Detailed_View_of_Kepler%27s_Supernova_Remnant.jpg/250px-NASA%27s_Great_Observatories_Provide_a_Detailed_View_of_Kepler%27s_Supernova_Remnant.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:NASA%27s_Great_Observatories_Provide_a_Detailed_View_of_Kepler%27s_Supernova_Remnant.jpg)

[Kepler's Supernova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%27s_Supernova "Kepler's Supernova") observed in visible light, infrared, and X-rays by NASA's three [Great Observatories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Observatories "Great Observatories")

Performing [astronomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomy "Astronomy") from [ground-based observatories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observatory#Ground-based_observatories "Observatory") on Earth is limited by the filtering and distortion of [electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation") ([scintillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scintillation_(astronomy) "Scintillation (astronomy)") or twinkling) due to the [atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth%27s_atmosphere "Earth's atmosphere"). A telescope orbiting Earth outside the atmosphere is subject neither to twinkling nor to [light pollution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_pollution "Light pollution") from artificial light sources on Earth. As a result, the [angular resolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_resolution "Angular resolution") of space telescopes is often much higher than a ground-based telescope with a similar [aperture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aperture "Aperture"). Many larger terrestrial telescopes, however, reduce atmospheric effects with [adaptive optics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adaptive_optics "Adaptive optics").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_telescope#cite_note-nasa-6)

Space-based astronomy is more important for frequency ranges that are outside the [optical window](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Optical_window "Optical window") and the [radio window](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_window "Radio window"), the only two wavelength ranges of the [electromagnetic spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_spectrum "Electromagnetic spectrum") that are not severely attenuated by the atmosphere.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_telescope#cite_note-nasa-6) Since the Earth's atmosphere blocks [X-rays](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-rays "X-rays"),[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_telescope#cite_note-7) and also largely blocks [infrared](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infrared "Infrared")[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_telescope#cite_note-8) and [ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet")[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_telescope#cite_note-9) radiation, telescopes and observatories such as the [Chandra X-ray Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chandra_X-ray_Observatory "Chandra X-ray Observatory"), the [James Webb Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope "James Webb Space Telescope"), the [XMM-Newton observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/XMM-Newton "XMM-Newton") and the (now deactivated) [International Ultraviolet Explorer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Ultraviolet_Explorer "International Ultraviolet Explorer") are stationed above the Earth's atmosphere.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_telescope#cite_note-10)

Disadvantages
-------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Space_telescope&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Disadvantages")]

Space telescopes are much more expensive to build than ground-based telescopes. Due to their location, space telescopes are also extremely difficult to maintain. The Hubble Space Telescope was serviced by the [Space Shuttle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Shuttle "Space Shuttle"), but most space telescopes cannot be serviced at all.

Future of space observatories
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Space_telescope&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Future of space observatories")]

Satellites have been launched and operated by [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA"), [ISRO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_Space_Research_Organisation "Indian Space Research Organisation"), [ESA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Space_Agency "European Space Agency"), [CNSA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_National_Space_Administration "China National Space Administration"), [JAXA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JAXA "JAXA") and the [Soviet space program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soviet_space_program "Soviet space program") (later succeeded by [Roscosmos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roscosmos "Roscosmos") of Russia). As of 2022, many space observatories have already completed their missions, while others continue operating on extended time. However, the future availability of space telescopes and observatories depends on timely and sufficient funding. While future space observatories are planned by NASA, JAXA and the [CNSA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/China_National_Space_Administration "China National Space Administration"), scientists fear that there would be gaps in coverage that would not be covered immediately by future projects and this would affect research in fundamental science.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_telescope#cite_note-NDTV-11)

On 16 January 2023, NASA announced preliminary considerations of several future space telescope programs, including the Great Observatory Technology Maturation Program, [Habitable Worlds Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habitable_Worlds_Observatory "Habitable Worlds Observatory"), and New Great Observatories.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_telescope#cite_note-SN-20230116-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_telescope#cite_note-SA-20230123-13)
