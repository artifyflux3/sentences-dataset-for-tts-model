A **star system** or **stellar system** is a small number of [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star") that orbit each other,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-1) bound by [gravitational attraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity"). It may sometimes be used to refer to a single star. [[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-2) A large group of stars bound by gravitation is generally called a _[star cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star\_cluster "Star cluster")_ or _[galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy")_, although, broadly speaking, they are also star systems. Star systems are not to be confused with [planetary systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_system "Planetary system"), which include planets and similar bodies (such as [comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet "Comet")).

A star system of two stars is known as a _[binary star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary\_star "Binary star")_, _binary star system_ or _physical [double star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double\_star "Double star")_.

Systems with four or more components are rare, and are much less commonly found than those with 2 or 3.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-toko-3) Multiple-star systems are called _triple_, _ternary_, or _trinary_ if they contain three stars; _quadruple_ or _quaternary_ if they contain four stars; _quintuple_ or _quintenary_ with five stars; _sextuple_ or _sextenary_ with six stars; _septuple_ or _septenary_ with seven stars; and _octuple_ or _octenary_ with eight stars.

These systems are smaller than [open star clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_star_cluster "Open star cluster"), which have more complex dynamics and typically have from 100 to 1,000 stars.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-Galactic_Dynamics_1987-4)

Optical doubles and multiples
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Star_system&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Optical doubles and multiples")]

Binary and multiple star systems are also known as a _physical_ multiple stars, to distinguish them from _optical_ multiple stars, which merely look close together when viewed from Earth. _Multiple stars_ may refer to either optical or physical,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-5)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-toko-3)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-7) but optical multiples do not form a star _system_.

Triple stars that are _not_ all gravitationally bound (and thus do not form a triple star system) might comprise a physical binary and an _optical_ companion (such as [Beta Cephei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Beta_Cephei "Beta Cephei")) or, in rare cases, a purely _optical_ triple star (such as [Gamma Serpentis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamma_Serpentis "Gamma Serpentis")).

[Research on binary and multiple stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_stars#Research_findings "Binary stars") estimates they make up about a third of the star systems in the [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") galaxy, with two-thirds of stars being single.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-8)

Binary stars are the most common non-single stars. With multiple star systems, the number of known systems decreases exponentially with multiplicity.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-tok1-9) For example, in the 1999 revision of Tokovinin's catalog[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-toko-3) of physical multiple stars, 551 out of the 728 systems described are triple. However, because of suspected [selection effects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Selection_effect "Selection effect"), the ability to interpret these statistics is very limited.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-10)

There are various [methods to detect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_star#Methods_of_observation "Binary star") star systems and distinguish them from optical binaries multiples. These include:

*   Make observations six months apart and look for differences caused by [parallaxes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_parallax "Stellar parallax"). (Not feasible for distant stars.)
*   Directly observe the stars orbiting each other or an apparently empty space (such as a dim star or [neutron star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_star "Neutron star")). (Not feasible for distant stars or those with long orbital periods.)
*   Observe a varying [Doppler shift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doppler_shift "Doppler shift").
*   Observe fluctuations in brightness that result from [eclipses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eclipse "Eclipse"). (Relies on the Earth being in the [orbital plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_plane "Orbital plane").)
*   Observe fluctuations in brightness that result from stars reflecting each others' light or gravitationally deforming each other.

Orbital characteristics
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Star_system&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Orbital characteristics")]

In systems that satisfy the assumptions of the [two-body problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-body_problem "Two-body problem") – including having negligible [tidal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tidal_force "Tidal force") effects, perturbations (from the gravity of other bodies), and [transfer of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_transfer#Astrophysics "Mass transfer") between stars – the two stars will trace out a stable [elliptical orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptical_orbit "Elliptical orbit") around the [barycenter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Barycenter "Barycenter") of the system. Examples of binary systems are [Sirius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirius "Sirius"), [Procyon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Procyon "Procyon") and [Cygnus X-1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cygnus_X-1 "Cygnus X-1"), the last of which probably consists of a star and a [black hole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole "Black hole").

Multiple-star systems can be divided into two main dynamical classes:

*   Hierarchical systems are stable and consist of nested orbits that do not interact much. Each level of the hierarchy can be treated as a [two-body problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-body_problem "Two-body problem").
*   Trapezia have unstable, strongly interacting orbits and are modelled as an [n-body problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-body_problem "N-body problem"), exhibiting [chaotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory "Chaos theory") behavior.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-11) They can have 2, 3, or 4 stars.

### Hierarchical systems

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Star_system&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Hierarchical systems")]

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Smoke_ring_for_a_halo.jpg/250px-Smoke_ring_for_a_halo.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Smoke_ring_for_a_halo.jpg)

Star system named [DI Cha](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DI_Cha "DI Cha"). While only two stars are apparent, it is actually a quadruple system containing two sets of binary stars.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-12)

Most multiple-star systems are organized in what is called a _hierarchical system_: the stars in the system can be divided into two smaller groups, each of which traverses a larger orbit around the system's [center of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_mass "Center of mass"). Each of these smaller groups must also be hierarchical, which means that they must be divided into smaller subgroups which themselves are hierarchical, and so on.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-evans1968-13) Each level of the hierarchy can be treated as a [two-body problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-body_problem "Two-body problem") by considering close pairs as if they were a single star. In these systems there is little interaction between the orbits and the stars' motion will continue to approximate stable[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-toko-3)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-heintz1-14)[Keplerian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kepler%27s_laws_of_planetary_motion "Kepler's laws of planetary motion") orbits around the system's center of mass.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-15)

For example, stable trinary systems consist of two stars in a close [binary system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_star "Binary star"), with a third orbiting this pair at a distance much larger than that of the binary orbit.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-16)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-evans1968-13) If the inner and outer orbits are comparable in size, the system may become dynamically unstable, leading to a star being ejected from the system.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-17)[EZ Aquarii](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EZ_Aquarii "EZ Aquarii") is an example of a physical hierarchical triple system, which has an outer star orbiting an inner binary composed of two more [red dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_dwarf "Red dwarf") stars.

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/Mobile-diagrams.png/250px-Mobile-diagrams.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Mobile-diagrams.png)

_Mobile diagrams_: 

1.   multiplex
2.   simplex, binary system
3.   simplex, triple system, hierarchy 2
4.   simplex, quadruple system, hierarchy 2
5.   simplex, quadruple system, hierarchy 3
6.   simplex, quintuple system, hierarchy 4.

Hierarchical arrangements can be organized by what Evans (1968) called _mobile diagrams_, which look similar to ornamental mobiles hung from the ceiling. Each level of the mobile illustrates the decomposition of the system into two or more systems with smaller size. Evans calls a diagram _multiplex_ if there is a node with more than two _children_, i.e. if the decomposition of some subsystem involves two or more orbits with comparable size. Because multiplexes may be unstable, multiple stars are expected to be _simplex_, meaning that at each level there are exactly two _children_. Evans calls the number of levels in the diagram its _hierarchy_.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-evans1968-13)

*   A simplex diagram of hierarchy 1, as in (b), describes a binary system.
*   A simplex diagram of hierarchy 2 may describe a triple system, as in (c), or a quadruple system, as in (d).
*   A simplex diagram of hierarchy 3 may describe a system with anywhere from four to eight components. The mobile diagram in (e) shows an example of a quadruple system with hierarchy 3, consisting of a single distant component orbiting a close binary system, with one of the components of the close binary being an even closer binary.
*   A real example of a system with hierarchy 3 is [Castor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Castor_(star) "Castor (star)"), also known as Alpha Geminorum or α Gem. It consists of what appears to be a [visual binary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Visual_binary "Visual binary")[star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star") which, upon closer inspection, can be seen to consist of two [spectroscopic binary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_binary "Spectroscopic binary") stars. By itself, this would be a quadruple hierarchy 2 system as in (d), but it is orbited by a fainter more distant component, which is also a close red dwarf binary. This forms a sextuple system of hierarchy 3.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-18)
*   The maximum hierarchy occurring in A. A. Tokovinin's Multiple Star Catalogue, as of 1999, is 4.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-toko-3) For example, the stars [Gliese 644A and Gliese 644B](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V1054_Ophiuchi "V1054 Ophiuchi") form what appears to be a close visual [binary star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_star "Binary star"); because Gliese 644B is a [spectroscopic binary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic_binary "Spectroscopic binary"), this is actually a triple system. The triple system has the more distant visual companion Gliese 643 and the still more distant visual companion Gliese 644C, which, because of their common motion with Gliese 644AB, are thought to be gravitationally bound to the triple system. This forms a quintuple system whose mobile diagram would be the diagram of level 4 appearing in (f).[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-19)

Higher hierarchies are also possible.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-evans1968-13)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-20) Most of these higher hierarchies either are stable or suffer from internal [perturbations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perturbation_(astronomy) "Perturbation (astronomy)").[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-21)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-22)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-23) Others consider complex multiple stars will in time theoretically disintegrate into less complex multiple stars, like more common observed triples or quadruples.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-24)[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-25)

Trapezia are usually very young, unstable systems. These are thought to form in stellar nurseries, and quickly fragment into stable multiple stars, which in the process may eject components as galactic [high-velocity stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Runaway_stars "Runaway stars").[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-heintztrapezia-26)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-27) They are named after the multiple star system known as the [Trapezium Cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezium_Cluster "Trapezium Cluster") in the heart of the [Orion Nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_Nebula "Orion Nebula").[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-heintztrapezia-26) Such systems are not rare, and commonly appear close to or within bright [nebulae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nebula "Nebula"). These stars have no standard hierarchical arrangements, but compete for stable orbits. This relationship is called _interplay_.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-Heintz_1978_68-28) Such stars eventually settle down to a close binary with a distant companion, with the other star(s) previously in the system ejected into interstellar space at high velocities.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-Heintz_1978_68-28) This dynamic may explain the [runaway stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Runaway_star "Runaway star") that might have been ejected during a collision of two binary star groups or a multiple system. This event is credited with ejecting [AE Aurigae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AE_Aurigae "AE Aurigae"), [Mu Columbae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mu_Columbae "Mu Columbae") and [53 Arietis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/53_Arietis "53 Arietis") at above 200 km·s−1 and has been traced to the [Trapezium cluster](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezium_cluster "Trapezium cluster") in the [Orion Nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orion_Nebula "Orion Nebula") some two million years ago.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-Blaauw-29)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-Hoogerwerf-30)

Designations and nomenclature
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Star_system&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Designations and nomenclature")]

### Multiple star designations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Star_system&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Multiple star designations")]

The components of multiple stars can be specified by appending the suffixes _A_, _B_, _C_, etc., to the system's designation. Suffixes such as _AB_ may be used to denote the pair consisting of _A_ and _B_. The sequence of letters _B_, _C_, etc. may be assigned in order of separation from the component _A_.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-Heintz19-31)[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-fmt-32) Components discovered close to an already known component may be assigned suffixes such as _Aa_, _Ba_, and so forth.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-fmt-32)

### Nomenclature in the Multiple Star Catalogue

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Star_system&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Nomenclature in the Multiple Star Catalogue")]

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/83/Tokovinin-multiple-star-notation.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Tokovinin-multiple-star-notation.png)

Subsystem notation in Tokovinin's Multiple Star Catalogue

A. A. Tokovinin's Multiple Star Catalogue uses a system in which each subsystem in a mobile diagram is encoded by a sequence of digits. In the mobile diagram (d) above, for example, the widest system would be given the number 1, while the subsystem containing its primary component would be numbered 11 and the subsystem containing its secondary component would be numbered 12. Subsystems which would appear below this in the mobile diagram will be given numbers with three, four, or more digits. When describing a non-hierarchical system by this method, the same subsystem number will be used more than once; for example, a system with three visual components, A, B, and C, no two of which can be grouped into a subsystem, would have two subsystems numbered 1 denoting the two binaries AB and AC. In this case, if B and C were subsequently resolved into binaries, they would be given the subsystem numbers 12 and 13.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-toko-3)

### Future multiple star system nomenclature

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Star_system&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Future multiple star system nomenclature")]

The current nomenclature for double and multiple stars can cause confusion as binary stars discovered in different ways are given different designations (for example, [discoverer designations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_star_designation "Double star designation") for visual binary stars and [variable star designations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_star_designations "Variable star designations") for eclipsing binary stars), and, worse, component letters may be assigned differently by different authors, so that, for example, one person's _A_ can be another's _C_.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-post191-33) Discussion starting in 1999 resulted in four proposed schemes to address this problem:[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-post191-33)

*   KoMa, a hierarchical scheme using upper- and lower-case letters and Arabic and Roman numerals;
*   The Urban/Corbin Designation Method, a hierarchical numeric scheme similar to the [Dewey Decimal Classification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dewey_Decimal_Classification "Dewey Decimal Classification") system;[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-34)
*   The Sequential Designation Method, a non-hierarchical scheme in which components and subsystems are assigned numbers in order of discovery;[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-35) and
*   WMC, the Washington Multiplicity Catalog, a hierarchical scheme in which the suffixes used in the [Washington Double Star Catalog](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Washington_Double_Star_Catalog "Washington Double Star Catalog") are extended with additional suffixed letters and numbers.

For a designation system, identifying the hierarchy within the system has the advantage that it makes identifying subsystems and computing their properties easier. However, it causes problems when new components are discovered at a level above or intermediate to the existing hierarchy. In this case, part of the hierarchy will shift inwards. Components which are found to be nonexistent, or are later reassigned to a different subsystem, also cause problems.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-36)[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-37)

During the 24th General Assembly of the [International Astronomical Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union "International Astronomical Union") in 2000, the WMC scheme was endorsed and it was resolved by Commissions 5, 8, 26, 42, and 45 that it should be expanded into a usable uniform designation scheme.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-post191-33) A sample of a catalog using the WMC scheme, covering half an hour of [right ascension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_ascension "Right ascension"), was later prepared.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-38) The issue was discussed again at the 25th General Assembly in 2003, and it was again resolved by commissions 5, 8, 26, 42, and 45, as well as the Working Group on Interferometry, that the WMC scheme should be expanded and further developed.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-39)

The sample WMC is hierarchically organized; the hierarchy used is based on observed orbital periods or separations. Since it contains many visual [double stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_star "Double star"), which may be optical rather than physical, this hierarchy may be only apparent. It uses upper-case letters (A, B, ...) for the first level of the hierarchy, lower-case letters (a, b, ...) for the second level, and numbers (1, 2, ...) for the third. Subsequent levels would use alternating lower-case letters and numbers, but no examples of this were found in the sample.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-post191-33)

*   [Sirius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirius "Sirius"), a binary consisting of a [main-sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Main-sequence "Main-sequence") type A star and a [white dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf "White dwarf")
*   [Procyon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Procyon "Procyon"), which is similar to Sirius
*   [Mira](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mira "Mira"), a variable consisting of a red giant and a [white dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/White_dwarf "White dwarf")
*   [Delta Cephei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delta_Cephei "Delta Cephei"), a Cepheid variable
*   [Almaaz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epsilon_Aurigae "Epsilon Aurigae"), an eclipsing binary

*   [Alpha Centauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri "Alpha Centauri") is a triple star composed of a main binary [yellow dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yellow_dwarf_star "Yellow dwarf star") and an [orange dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orange_dwarf_star "Orange dwarf star") pair ([Rigil Kentaurus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri_A "Alpha Centauri A") and [Toliman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alpha_Centauri_B "Alpha Centauri B")), and an outlying [red dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_dwarf "Red dwarf"), [Proxima Centauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proxima_Centauri "Proxima Centauri"). Together, Rigil Kentaurus and Toliman form a physical [binary star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_star "Binary star"), designated as Alpha Centauri AB, α Cen AB, or RHD 1 AB, where the AB denotes this is a [binary system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_star "Binary star").[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-wds2006-40) The moderately eccentric [orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit "Orbit") of the binary can make the components be as close as 11 [AU](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomical_unit "Astronomical unit") or as far away as 36 AU. Proxima Centauri, also (though less frequently) called Alpha Centauri C, is much farther away (between 4300 and 13,000 AU) from α Cen AB, and orbits the central pair with a period of 547,000 (+66,000/-40,000) years.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-41)
*   [Polaris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polaris "Polaris") or Alpha Ursae Minoris (α UMi), the north star, is a triple star system in which the closer companion star is extremely close to the main star—so close that it was only known from its gravitational tug on Polaris A (α UMi A) until it was imaged by the [Hubble Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope "Hubble Space Telescope") in 2006.
*   [Gliese 667](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gliese_667 "Gliese 667") is a triple star system with two K-type main sequence stars and a [red dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_dwarf "Red dwarf"). The red dwarf, C, hosts between two and seven planets, of which one, Cc, alongside the unconfirmed Cf and Ce, are potentially habitable.
*   [HD 188753](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HD_188753 "HD 188753") is a triple star system located approximately 149 [light-years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year "Light-year") away from [Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Earth "Earth") in the [constellation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constellation "Constellation")[Cygnus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cygnus_(constellation) "Cygnus (constellation)"). The system is composed of HD 188753A, a [yellow dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yellow_dwarf_star "Yellow dwarf star"); HD 188753B, an [orange dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orange_dwarf "Orange dwarf"); and HD 188753C, a [red dwarf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_dwarf "Red dwarf"). B and C orbit each other every 156 days, and, as a group, orbit A every 25.7 years.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-42)
*   [Fomalhaut](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fomalhaut "Fomalhaut") (α PsA, α Piscis Austrini) is a triple star system in the [constellation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constellation "Constellation")[Piscis Austrinus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piscis_Austrinus "Piscis Austrinus"). It was discovered to be a triple system in 2013, when the K type [flare star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flare_star "Flare star") TW Piscis Austrini and the red dwarf LP 876-10 were all confirmed to share [proper motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proper_motion "Proper motion") through space. The primary has a massive dust disk similar to that of the early [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System"), but much more massive. It also contains a gas giant, [Fomalhaut b](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fomalhaut_b "Fomalhaut b"). That same year, the tertiary star, LP 876-10 was also confirmed to house a dust disk.
*   [HD 181068](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HD_181068 "HD 181068") is a unique triple system, consisting of a [red giant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_giant "Red giant") and two main-sequence stars. The orbits of the stars are oriented in such a way that all three stars eclipse each other.

[HD 98800](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HD_98800 "HD 98800") is a quadruple star system located in the [TW Hydrae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TW_Hydrae "TW Hydrae") association.

*   [Capella](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capella "Capella"), a pair of [giant stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giant_star "Giant star") orbited by a pair of [red dwarfs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_dwarf "Red dwarf"), around 42 light years away from the Solar System. It has an [apparent magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apparent_magnitude "Apparent magnitude") of around 0.08, making Capella one of the brightest stars in the night sky.

*   [Mizar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mizar_(star) "Mizar (star)") is often said to have been the first [binary star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_star "Binary star") discovered when it was observed in 1650 by [Giovanni Battista Riccioli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Giovanni_Battista_Riccioli "Giovanni Battista Riccioli")[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-44),p.1[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-45) but it was probably observed earlier, by [Benedetto Castelli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benedetto_Castelli "Benedetto Castelli") and [Galileo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galileo "Galileo").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] Later, [spectroscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopy "Spectroscopy") of its components Mizar A and B revealed that they are both binary stars themselves.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-mizar-46)
*   [HD 98800](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HD_98800 "HD 98800")
*   The [PH1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PH1 "PH1") system has the planet [PH1 b](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=PH1_b&action=edit&redlink=1 "PH1 b (page does not exist)") (discovered in 2012 by the [Planet Hunters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet_Hunters "Planet Hunters") group, a part of the [Zooniverse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zooniverse "Zooniverse")) orbiting two of the four stars, making it the first known planet to be in a quadruple star system.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-PlanetHunters-47)
*   [KOI-2626](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=KOI-2626&action=edit&redlink=1 "KOI-2626 (page does not exist)") is the first quadruple star system with an Earth-sized planet.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-48)
*   [Xi Tauri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Xi_Tauri "Xi Tauri") (ξ Tau, ξ Tauri), located about 222 [light years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year "Light year") away, is a [spectroscopic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectroscopic "Spectroscopic") and eclipsing quadruple star consisting of three blue-white B-[type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_classification "Stellar classification")[main-sequence stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Main-sequence_star "Main-sequence star"), along with an [F-type star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/F-type_star "F-type star"). Two of the [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stars "Stars") are in a close [orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbit "Orbit") and revolve around each other once every 7.15 days. These in turn orbit the third star once every 145 days. The fourth star orbits the other three stars roughly every fifty years.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_system#cite_note-Nemravov%C3%A12013-49)
