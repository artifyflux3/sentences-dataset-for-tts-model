The composition is dedicated to Renaissance astronomer Nicolaus Copernicus .

Performances [ edit ] World premiere on January 23, 2013, at Salle Pleyel , Paris .

[ 1 ] Dutch premiere on March 1, 2014, at the Concertgebouw , Amsterdam , conducted by Marc Albrecht .

[ 2 ] Ukrainian premiere on September 17, 2015, at the S. Lyudkevych Concert Hall, Lviv , by the INSO-Lviv Youth Academic Symphony Orchestra, conducted by Anna Skryleva.

[ 3 ] A planned performance by the Residentie Orkest in April 2020, conducted by Andrea Battistoni [ it ] , was cancelled due to the COVID-19_pandemic .

[ 4 ] Swedish premiere on March 6–10, 2023, as part of Orchestra Project 4 , performed by the symphony orchestra of the Academy of Music and Drama at the University of Gothenburg , conducted by Henrik Schaefer .

German premiere on October 24, 2023, at the Sauerland Herbst Brass Festival at the Hagen Theatre , performed by The Hagen Philharmonic Orchestra [ de ] , conducted by Rodrigo Tomillo.

Swedish performance on March 21, 2024, as part of HSO – Storslagen romantik , performed at the Helsingborg Concert Hall [ se ] by the Helsingborg Symphony Orchester [ se ] , conducted by Joana Carneiro .

[ 5 ] Background [ edit ] The name of this composition was taken from the Nicolaus Copernicus Monument, Toruń which bears a Latin inscription drawn up by Alexander von Humboldt: Nicolaus Copernicus Thorunensis, terrae motor, solis caelique stator - Nicolaus Copernicus of Thorun, mover of the earth, stopper of the sun and heavens In the score Azarova graphically and musically shows Copernicus’s heliocentric cosmology which displaced the Earth from the center of the universe.
