In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a **tuple** is a finite [sequence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequence "Sequence") or _ordered list_ of [numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number "Number") or, more generally, [mathematical objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_object "Mathematical object"), which are called the _elements_ of the tuple. An **n-tuple** is a tuple of n elements, where n is a non-negative [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer"). There is only one 0-tuple, called the _empty tuple_. A 1-tuple and a 2-tuple are commonly called a [singleton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singleton_(mathematics) "Singleton (mathematics)") and an [ordered pair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair "Ordered pair"), respectively. The term _"infinite tuple"_ is occasionally used for _"infinite sequences"_.

Tuples are usually written by listing the elements within parentheses "( )" and separated by commas; for example, (2, 7, 4, 1, 7) denotes a 5-tuple. Other types of brackets are sometimes used, although they may have a different meaning.[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple#cite_note-1)

An n-tuple can be formally defined as the [image](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Image_(function) "Image (function)") of a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") that has the set of the n first [natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number") as its [domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Domain_of_a_function "Domain of a function"). Tuples may be also defined from ordered pairs by a [recurrence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recurrence_relation "Recurrence relation") starting from an [ordered pair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair "Ordered pair"); indeed, an n-tuple can be identified with the ordered pair of its (_n_ − 1) first elements and its n th element, for example, ![Image 1: {\displaystyle \left(\left(\left(1,2\right),3\right),4\right)=\left(1,2,3,4\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6f6d62a8b2f73f7b906f1d94fb72f68b4a4b76f6).

In [computer science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Computer_science "Computer science"), tuples come in many forms. Most typed [functional programming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Functional_programming "Functional programming") languages implement tuples directly as [product types](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_type "Product type"),[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple#cite_note-2) tightly associated with [algebraic data types](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_data_type "Algebraic data type"), [pattern matching](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pattern_matching "Pattern matching"), and [destructuring assignment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Assignment_(computer_science)#Parallel_assignment "Assignment (computer science)").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple#cite_note-3) Many programming languages offer an alternative to tuples, known as [record types](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Record_(computer_science) "Record (computer science)"), featuring unordered elements accessed by label.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple#cite_note-4) A few programming languages combine ordered tuple product types and unordered record types into a single construct, as in [C structs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Struct_(C_programming_language) "Struct (C programming language)") and Haskell records. [Relational databases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relational_database "Relational database") may formally identify their [rows](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Row_(database) "Row (database)") (records) as _tuples_.

Tuples also occur in [relational algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relational_algebra "Relational algebra"); when programming the [semantic web](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semantic_web "Semantic web") with the [Resource Description Framework](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Resource_Description_Framework "Resource Description Framework") (RDF); in [linguistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linguistics "Linguistics");[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple#cite_note-5) and in [philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy "Philosophy").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple#cite_note-6)

The term originated as an abstraction of the sequence: single, couple/double, triple, quadruple, quintuple, sextuple, septuple, octuple, ..., _n_‑tuple, ..., where the prefixes are taken from the [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin") names of the numerals. The unique 0-tuple is called the _null tuple_ or _empty tuple_. A 1‑tuple is called a _single_ (or _singleton_), a 2‑tuple is called an _ordered pair_ or _couple_, and a 3‑tuple is called a _triple_ (or _triplet_). The number _n_ can be any nonnegative [integer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer"). For example, a [complex number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") can be represented as a 2‑tuple of reals, a [quaternion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quaternion "Quaternion") can be represented as a 4‑tuple, an [octonion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octonion "Octonion") can be represented as an 8‑tuple, and a [sedenion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sedenion "Sedenion") can be represented as a 16‑tuple.

Although these uses treat _‑tuple_ as the suffix, the original suffix was _‑ple_ as in "triple" (three-fold) or "decuple" (ten‑fold). This originates from [medieval Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Medieval_Latin "Medieval Latin")_plus_ (meaning "more") related to [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_language "Greek language") ‑πλοῦς, which replaced the classical and late antique _‑plex_ (meaning "folded"), as in "duplex".[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple#cite_note-7)[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple#cite_note-8)

The general rule for the identity of two _n_-tuples is

Tuple elements are ordered: tuple ![Image 6: {\displaystyle (1,2,3)\neq (3,2,1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eb286d33ffedcfeda837cf023d49a2183f18f495), but set ![Image 7: {\displaystyle \{1,2,3\}=\{3,2,1\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/04feef21743d18c28161ceb0349fd4b9c2fac481).
3.   A tuple has a finite number of elements, while a set or a [multiset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiset "Multiset") may have an infinite number of elements.

There are several definitions of tuples that give them the properties described in the previous section.

### Tuples as nested ordered pairs

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tuple&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Tuples as nested ordered pairs")]

Another way of modeling tuples in set theory is as nested [ordered pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair "Ordered pair"). This approach assumes that the notion of ordered pair has already been defined.

1.   The 0-tuple (i.e. the empty tuple) is represented by the empty set ![Image 22: {\displaystyle \emptyset }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6af50205f42bb2ec3c666b7b847d2c7f96e464c7).
2.   An _n_-tuple, with _n_> 0, can be defined as an ordered pair of its first entry and an (_n_ − 1)-tuple (which contains the remaining entries when _n_> 1): ![Image 23: {\displaystyle (a_{1},a_{2},a_{3},\ldots ,a_{n})=(a_{1},(a_{2},a_{3},\ldots ,a_{n}))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2698402ce9489cc8059e92ff8e5dbf8a1589b301)

This definition can be applied recursively to the (_n_ − 1)-tuple:

![Image 24: {\displaystyle (a_{1},a_{2},a_{3},\ldots ,a_{n})=(a_{1},(a_{2},(a_{3},(\ldots ,(a_{n},\emptyset )\ldots ))))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86d7088e31e634c43425cfbdc23d19e20bd4b699)
Thus, for example:

![Image 25: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}(1,2,3)&=(1,(2,(3,\emptyset )))\\(1,2,3,4)&=(1,(2,(3,(4,\emptyset ))))\\\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3656e1d003f033bc8a46dad6198f35cf84dadb3a)
A variant of this definition starts "peeling off" elements from the other end:

1.   The 0-tuple is the empty set ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \emptyset }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6af50205f42bb2ec3c666b7b847d2c7f96e464c7).
2.   For _n_> 0: ![Image 27: {\displaystyle (a_{1},a_{2},a_{3},\ldots ,a_{n})=((a_{1},a_{2},a_{3},\ldots ,a_{n-1}),a_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e930380928a844376aa6bf8737dbb65b5a789ec)

This definition can be applied recursively:

![Image 28: {\displaystyle (a_{1},a_{2},a_{3},\ldots ,a_{n})=((\ldots (((\emptyset ,a_{1}),a_{2}),a_{3}),\ldots ),a_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b973e7b4db6ce38786bf2affa33fbdccfa66410)
Thus, for example:

![Image 29: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}(1,2,3)&=(((\emptyset ,1),2),3)\\(1,2,3,4)&=((((\emptyset ,1),2),3),4)\\\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/118f0960978983f2adab89a72c1b80d7774787ea)

### Tuples as nested sets

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Tuple&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Tuples as nested sets")]

Using [Kuratowski's representation for an ordered pair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ordered_pair#Kuratowski's_definition "Ordered pair"), the second definition above can be reformulated in terms of pure [set theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_theory "Set theory"):

1.   The 0-tuple (i.e. the empty tuple) is represented by the empty set ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \emptyset }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6af50205f42bb2ec3c666b7b847d2c7f96e464c7);
2.   Let ![Image 31: {\displaystyle x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87f9e315fd7e2ba406057a97300593c4802b53e4) be an _n_-tuple ![Image 32: {\displaystyle (a_{1},a_{2},\ldots ,a_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ce7edfb2f544f6e6158189ea1af9d07141fc996b), and let ![Image 33: {\displaystyle x\rightarrow b\equiv (a_{1},a_{2},\ldots ,a_{n},b)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a2c0aac90de0b68b17eefbb3a5d191d1b4fbf757). Then, ![Image 34: {\displaystyle x\rightarrow b\equiv \{\{x\},\{x,b\}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3762e24a28653c7e366fbed8be0937f4338bac3f). (The right arrow, ![Image 35: {\displaystyle \rightarrow }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53e574cc3aa5b4bf5f3f5906caf121a378eef08b), could be read as "adjoined with".)

In this formulation:

![Image 36: {\displaystyle {\begin{array}{lclcl}()&&&=&\emptyset \\&&&&\\(1)&=&()\rightarrow 1&=&\{\{()\},\{(),1\}\}\\&&&=&\{\{\emptyset \},\{\emptyset ,1\}\}\\&&&&\\(1,2)&=&(1)\rightarrow 2&=&\{\{(1)\},\{(1),2\}\}\\&&&=&\{\{\{\{\emptyset \},\{\emptyset ,1\}\}\},\\&&&&\{\{\{\emptyset \},\{\emptyset ,1\}\},2\}\}\\&&&&\\(1,2,3)&=&(1,2)\rightarrow 3&=&\{\{(1,2)\},\{(1,2),3\}\}\\&&&=&\{\{\{\{\{\{\emptyset \},\{\emptyset ,1\}\}\},\\&&&&\{\{\{\emptyset \},\{\emptyset ,1\}\},2\}\}\},\\&&&&\{\{\{\{\{\emptyset \},\{\emptyset ,1\}\}\},\\&&&&\{\{\{\emptyset \},\{\emptyset ,1\}\},2\}\},3\}\}\\\end{array}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5654421f5c61a66d3dd75921c42fb9de304b727b)
In [discrete mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_mathematics "Discrete mathematics"), especially [combinatorics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Combinatorics "Combinatorics") and finite [probability theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_theory "Probability theory"), _n_-tuples arise in the context of various counting problems and are treated more informally as ordered lists of length _n_.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple#cite_note-9)_n_-tuples whose entries come from a set of _m_ elements are also called _arrangements with repetition_, _[permutations of a multiset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permutation#Permutations\_of\_multisets "Permutation")_ and, in some non-English literature, _variations with repetition_. The number of _n_-tuples of an _m_-set is _m_ _n_. This follows from the combinatorial [rule of product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rule_of_product "Rule of product").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple#cite_note-10) If _S_ is a finite set of [cardinality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinality "Cardinality")_m_, this number is the cardinality of the _n_-fold [Cartesian power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product#n-ary_Cartesian_power "Cartesian product")_S_ × _S_ × ⋯ × _S_. Tuples are elements of this product set.

In [type theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_theory "Type theory"), commonly used in [programming languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Programming_language "Programming language"), a tuple has a [product type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_type "Product type"); this fixes not only the length, but also the underlying types of each component. Formally:

![Image 37: {\displaystyle (x_{1},x_{2},\ldots ,x_{n}):{\mathsf {T}}_{1}\times {\mathsf {T}}_{2}\times \ldots \times {\mathsf {T}}_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d1b990b0ddf99b248978055b4a78e7785063fc04)
and the [projections](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projection_(mathematics) "Projection (mathematics)") are term constructors:

![Image 38: {\displaystyle \pi _{1}(x):{\mathsf {T}}_{1},~\pi _{2}(x):{\mathsf {T}}_{2},~\ldots ,~\pi _{n}(x):{\mathsf {T}}_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3672cdb5ab9186a72830406d60c8c4ddc85ba40e)
The tuple with labeled elements used in the [relational model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relational_model "Relational model") has a [record type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Record_(computer_science) "Record (computer science)"). Both of these types can be defined as simple extensions of the [simply typed lambda calculus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simply_typed_lambda_calculus "Simply typed lambda calculus").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tuple#cite_note-pierce2002-11)

and the interpretation of the basic terms is:
