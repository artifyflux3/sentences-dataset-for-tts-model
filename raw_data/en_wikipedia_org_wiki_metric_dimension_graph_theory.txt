In [graph theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_theory "Graph theory"), the **metric dimension** of a graph _G_ is the minimum cardinality of a subset _S_ of vertices such that all other vertices are uniquely determined by their distances to the vertices in _S_. Finding the metric dimension of a graph is an [NP-hard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NP-hard "NP-hard") problem; the decision version, determining whether the metric dimension is less than a given value, is [NP-complete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NP-complete "NP-complete").

Detailed definition
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Detailed definition")]

For an ordered subset ![Image 1: {\displaystyle W=\{w_{1},w_{2},\dots ,w_{k}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2172444b671ef0d5a84aca42ef86d59de375fc54) of vertices and a vertex _v_ in a connected graph _G_, the representation of _v_ with respect to _W_ is the ordered _k_-tuple ![Image 2: {\displaystyle r(v|W)=(d(v,w_{1}),d(v,w_{2}),\dots ,d(v,w_{k}))}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f00291b822985e76f59b75f27ccdb83bc25a54c7), where _d_(_x_,_y_) represents the distance between the vertices _x_ and _y_. The set _W_ is a resolving set (or locating set) for _G_ if every two vertices of _G_ have distinct representations. The metric dimension of _G_ is the minimum cardinality of a resolving set for _G_. A resolving set containing a minimum number of vertices is called a basis (or reference set) for _G_. Resolving sets for graphs were introduced independently by [Slater (1975)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#CITEREFSlater1975) and [Harary & Melter (1976)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#CITEREFHararyMelter1976), while the concept of a resolving set and that of metric dimension were defined much earlier in the more general context of metric spaces by [Blumenthal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonard_Blumenthal "Leonard Blumenthal") in his monograph _Theory and Applications of Distance Geometry_. Graphs are special examples of metric spaces with their intrinsic path metric.

If a tree is a path, its metric dimension is one. Otherwise, let _L_ denote the set of leaves, degree-one vertices in the tree. Let _K_ be the set of vertices that have degree greater than two, and that are connected by paths of degree-two vertices to one or more leaves. Then the metric dimension is |_L_|−|_K_|. A basis of this cardinality may be formed by removing from _L_ one of the leaves associated with each vertex in _K_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-1) The same algorithm is valid for the line graph of the tree, and thus any tree and its line graph have the same metric dimension.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFengXuWang2013-2)

### Relations between the order, the metric dimension and the diameter

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Relations between the order, the metric dimension and the diameter")]

[Khuller, Raghavachari & Rosenfeld (1996)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#CITEREFKhullerRaghavachariRosenfeld1996) prove the inequality ![Image 3: {\displaystyle n\leq D^{\beta }+\beta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0f631d92fa7c4e1b8c602f06b54722fc7652f3b2) for any n-vertex graph with [diameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diameter_(graph_theory) "Diameter (graph theory)")![Image 4: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6) and metric dimension ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \beta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ed48a5e36207156fb792fa79d29925d2f7901e8). This bounds follows from the fact that each vertex that is not in the resolving set is uniquely determined by a distance vector of length ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \beta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ed48a5e36207156fb792fa79d29925d2f7901e8) with each entry being an integer between 1 and ![Image 7: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6) (there are precisely ![Image 8: {\displaystyle D^{\beta }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b1f9fa3cdc0050a6f8c99ad71656560fed67802d) such vectors). However, the bound is only achieved for ![Image 9: {\displaystyle D\leq 3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1814dbcace0789d61619151ec9bfd4246ef6e6aa) or ![Image 10: {\displaystyle \beta =1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73416922785589e358ae2bb10c7633667b4c24a2); the more precise bound ![Image 11: {\displaystyle n\leq \left(\lfloor 2D/3\rfloor +1\right)^{\beta }+\beta \sum _{i=1}^{\lceil D/3\rceil }(2i-1)^{\beta -1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9a4e8ec3f9a3c341c2ba90f2d4b19b99135b0bb) is proved by [Hernando et al. (2010)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#CITEREFHernandoMoraPelayoSeara2010).

For specific graph classes, smaller bounds can hold. For example, [Beaudou et al. (2018)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#CITEREFBeaudouDankelmannFoucaudHenning2018) proved that ![Image 12: {\displaystyle n\leq (\beta D+4)(D+2)/8}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e819ef0183ac6f325f222e081c4fa12ab3591d18) for trees (the bound being tight for even values of D), and a bound of the form ![Image 13: {\displaystyle n=O(D^{2}\beta )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b847914d485f15ea513abb7c9b85701957d98417) for [outerplanar graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outerplanar_graph "Outerplanar graph"). The same authors proved that ![Image 14: {\displaystyle n\leq (D\beta +1)^{t-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/13e164b5efd09c645f76f83b8f0553ad219e6e20) for graphs with no [complete graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complete_graph "Complete graph") of order t as a [minor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_minor "Graph minor") and also gave bounds for [chordal graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chordal_graph "Chordal graph") and graphs of bounded [treewidth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treewidth "Treewidth"). The authors [Foucaud et al. (2017a)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#CITEREFFoucaudMertziosNaserasrParreau2017a) proved bounds of the form ![Image 15: {\displaystyle n=O(D\beta ^{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0e31d8636497d35aefcfc51e5dd68d2c1b8fb70a) for [interval graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_graph "Interval graph") and [permutation graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permutation_graph "Permutation graph"), and bounds of the form ![Image 16: {\displaystyle n=O(D\beta )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d09d2df8bd648821f28c3f69f24f0352eb9889ae) for [unit interval graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_interval_graph "Unit interval graph"), bipartite permutation graphs and [cographs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cograph "Cograph").

Computational complexity
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Computational complexity")]

### Decision complexity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Decision complexity")]

Deciding whether the metric dimension of a graph is at most a given integer is NP-complete.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGareyJohnson1979-3) It remains NP-complete for bounded-degree [planar graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planar_graph "Planar graph"),[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-FOOTNOTED%C3%ADazPottonenSernavan_Leeuwen2012-4)[split graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Split_graph "Split graph"), [bipartite graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipartite_graph "Bipartite graph") and their [complements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complement_(graph_theory) "Complement (graph theory)"), [line graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_graph "Line graph") of bipartite graphs,[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEpsteinLevinWoeginger2012-5)[unit disk graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_disk_graph "Unit disk graph"),[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHoffmannWanke2013-6)[interval graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_graph "Interval graph") of diameter 2 and [permutation graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permutation_graph "Permutation graph") of diameter 2,[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFoucaudMertziosNaserasrParreau2017b-7) and graphs of bounded [treewidth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treewidth "Treewidth").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-FOOTNOTELiPilipczuk2022-8)

For any fixed constant _k_, the graphs of metric dimension at most _k_ can be recognized in [polynomial time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial_time "Polynomial time"), by testing all possible _k_-tuples of vertices, but this algorithm is not [fixed-parameter tractable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameterized_complexity "Parameterized complexity") (for the natural parameter _k_, the solution size). Answering a question posed by [Lokshtanov (2010)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#CITEREFLokshtanov2010), [Hartung & Nichterlein (2013)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#CITEREFHartungNichterlein2013) show that the metric dimension decision problem is complete for the parameterized complexity class W[2], implying that a time bound of the form _n_ O(_k_) as achieved by this naive algorithm is likely optimal and that a fixed-parameter tractable algorithm (for the parameterization by _k_) is unlikely to exist. Nevertheless, the problem becomes [fixed-parameter tractable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parameterized_complexity "Parameterized complexity") when restricted to [interval graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_graph "Interval graph"),[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFoucaudMertziosNaserasrParreau2017b-7) and more generally to graphs of bounded tree-length,[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBelmonteFominGolovachRamanujan2015-9) such as [chordal graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chordal_graph "Chordal graph"), [permutation graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Permutation_graph "Permutation graph") or asteroidal-triple-free graphs.

Deciding whether the metric dimension of a tree is at most a given integer can be done in linear time[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-10) Other linear-time algorithms exist for [cographs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cograph "Cograph"),[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEpsteinLevinWoeginger2012-5)[chain graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chain_graph "Chain graph"),[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEFernauHeggernesvan_'t_HofMeister2015-11) and cactus block graphs[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHoffmannEltermanWanke2016-12) (a class including both [cactus graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cactus_graph "Cactus graph") and [block graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Block_graph "Block graph")). The problem may be solved in polynomial time on [outerplanar graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outerplanar_graph "Outerplanar graph").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-FOOTNOTED%C3%ADazPottonenSernavan_Leeuwen2012-4) It may also be solved in polynomial time for graphs of bounded [cyclomatic number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclomatic_number "Cyclomatic number"),[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEpsteinLevinWoeginger2012-5) but this algorithm is again not fixed-parameter tractable (for the parameter "cyclomatic number") because the exponent in the polynomial depends on the cyclomatic number. There exist fixed-parameter tractable algorithms to solve the metric dimension problem for the parameters "[vertex cover](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_cover "Vertex cover")",[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHartungNichterlein2013-13) "max leaf number",[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEEppstein2015-14) and "modular width".[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBelmonteFominGolovachRamanujan2015-9) Graphs with bounded cyclomatic number, vertex cover number or max leaf number all have bounded [treewidth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treewidth "Treewidth"), however it is an open problem to determine the complexity of the metric dimension problem even on graphs of treewidth 2, that is, [series–parallel graphs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Series%E2%80%93parallel_graph "Series–parallel graph").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBelmonteFominGolovachRamanujan2015-9)

### Approximation complexity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Approximation complexity")]

The metric dimension of an arbitrary _n_-vertex graph may be approximated in polynomial time to within an [approximation ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Approximation_algorithm "Approximation algorithm") of ![Image 17: {\displaystyle 2\log n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7fb46158527909f2a8994c8d570c20691085cca4) by expressing it as a [set cover problem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_cover_problem "Set cover problem"), a problem of covering all of a given collection of elements by as few sets as possible in a given [family of sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Family_of_sets "Family of sets"). [[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEKhullerRaghavachariRosenfeld1996-15) In the set cover problem formed from a metric dimension problem, the elements to be covered are the ![Image 18: {\displaystyle {\tbinom {n}{2}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd5a9995ef7f68d5dd7d90d27c024615cf9fe4ec) pairs of vertices to be distinguished, and the sets that can cover them are the sets of pairs that can be distinguished by a single chosen vertex. The approximation bound then follows by applying standard approximation algorithms for set cover. An alternative [greedy algorithm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greedy_algorithm "Greedy algorithm") that chooses vertices according to the difference in [entropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Entropy "Entropy") between the equivalence classes of distance vectors before and after the choice achieves an even better approximation ratio, ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \log n+\log \log _{2}n+1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11a33adcaedda49cb63395b67531fbe5e4f92c88).[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHauptmannSchmiedViehmann2012-16) This approximation ratio is close to best possible, as under standard complexity-theoretic assumptions a ratio of ![Image 20: {\displaystyle (1-\epsilon )\log n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f437e97465cbf34c2f770944f9731fca347b5edb) cannot be achieved in polynomial time for any ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \epsilon >0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/568095ad3924314374a5ab68fae17343661f2a71).[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHauptmannSchmiedViehmann2012-16) The latter hardness of approximation still holds for instances restricted to subcubic graphs,[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHartungNichterlein2013-13) and even to [bipartite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bipartite_graph "Bipartite graph") subcubic graphs.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_dimension_(graph_theory)#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHartung2014-17)
