In [Newtonian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newtonian_mechanics "Newtonian mechanics"), **momentum** (pl.: **momenta** or **momentums**; more specifically **linear momentum** or **translational momentum**) is the [product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication "Multiplication") of the [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") and [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") of an object. It is a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") quantity, possessing a magnitude and a direction. If _m_ is an object's mass and **v** is its velocity (also a vector quantity), then the object's momentum **p** (from Latin _[pellere](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pello#Latin "wikt:pello")_ "push, drive") is: ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} =m\mathbf {v} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21233e5c3bb1a4db8ed4a3ceb873f166a495c7f9) In the [International System of Units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") (SI), the [unit of measurement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_of_measurement "Unit of measurement") of momentum is the [kilogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilogram "Kilogram")[metre per second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre_per_second "Metre per second") (kg⋅m/s), which is [dimensionally equivalent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensional_analysis "Dimensional analysis") to the [newton-second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton-second "Newton-second").

[Newton's second law of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion#Newton's_second_law "Newton's laws of motion") states that the rate of change of a body's momentum is equal to the net force acting on it. Momentum depends on the [frame of reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_of_reference "Frame of reference"), but in any [inertial frame of reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inertial_frame_of_reference "Inertial frame of reference"), it is a _conserved_ quantity, meaning that if a [closed system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_system "Closed system") is not affected by external forces, its total momentum does not change. Momentum is also conserved in [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity") (with a modified formula) and, in a modified form, in [electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electrodynamics "Electrodynamics"), [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory"), and [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"). It is an expression of one of the fundamental symmetries of space and time: [translational symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translational_symmetry "Translational symmetry").

Advanced formulations of classical mechanics, [Lagrangian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics") and [Hamiltonian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics "Hamiltonian mechanics"), allow one to choose coordinate systems that incorporate symmetries and constraints. In these systems the conserved quantity is **generalized momentum**, and in general this is different from the **kinetic** momentum defined above. The concept of generalized momentum is carried over into quantum mechanics, where it becomes an operator on a [wave function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_function "Wave function"). The momentum and position operators are related by the [Heisenberg uncertainty principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heisenberg_uncertainty_principle "Heisenberg uncertainty principle").

In continuous systems such as [electromagnetic fields](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_field "Electromagnetic field"), [fluid dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_dynamics "Fluid dynamics") and [deformable bodies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deformable_bodies "Deformable bodies"), a **momentum density** can be defined as momentum per volume (a [volume-specific quantity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volume-specific_quantity "Volume-specific quantity")). A [continuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_(physics) "Continuum (physics)") version of the conservation of momentum leads to equations such as the [Navier–Stokes equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Navier%E2%80%93Stokes_equations "Navier–Stokes equations") for fluids or the [Cauchy momentum equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy_momentum_equation "Cauchy momentum equation") for deformable solids or fluids.

Classical
---------

Momentum is a [vector quantity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_quantity "Vector quantity"): it has both magnitude and direction. Since momentum has a direction, it can be used to predict the resulting direction and speed of motion of objects after they collide. Below, the basic properties of momentum are described in one dimension. The vector equations are almost identical to the scalar equations (see [multiple dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#Multiple_dimensions)).

### Single particle

The momentum of a particle is conventionally represented by the letter _p_. It is the product of two quantities, the particle's [mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass") (represented by the letter _m_) and its [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") (_v_):[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-FeynmanCh9-1)[![Image 4: {\displaystyle p=mv.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd84eb5cc8d8ca0cefd6f93d8a00fbacb4da17f)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q41273)

The unit of momentum is the product of the units of mass and velocity. In [SI units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_units "SI units"), if the mass is in kilograms and the velocity is in meters per second then the momentum is in kilogram meters per second (kg⋅m/s). In [cgs units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Centimetre%E2%80%93gram%E2%80%93second_system_of_units "Centimetre–gram–second system of units"), if the mass is in grams and the velocity in centimeters per second, then the momentum is in gram centimeters per second (g⋅cm/s).

Being a vector, momentum has magnitude and direction. For example, a 1 kg model airplane, traveling due north at 1 m/s in straight and level flight, has a momentum of 1 kg⋅m/s due north measured with reference to the ground.

### Many particles

The momentum of a system of particles is the vector sum of their momenta. If two particles have respective masses m 1 and m 2, and velocities v 1 and v 2, the total momentum is ![Image 5: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}p&=p_{1}+p_{2}\\&=m_{1}v_{1}+m_{2}v_{2}\,.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4fe17d5389e60187d1d65004d9512046244c37be) The momenta of more than two particles can be added more generally with the following: ![Image 6: {\displaystyle p=\sum _{i}m_{i}v_{i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7bb4d4327b75bf0c95cec88e11852c64191d24da)

A system of particles has a [center of mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_mass "Center of mass"), a point determined by the weighted sum of their positions: ![Image 7: {\displaystyle r_{\text{cm}}={\frac {m_{1}r_{1}+m_{2}r_{2}+\cdots }{m_{1}+m_{2}+\cdots }}={\frac {\sum _{i}m_{i}r_{i}}{\sum _{i}m_{i}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e385b6c3a7d2d5318264932b6f2b9c7b2b5c215)

If one or more of the particles is moving, the center of mass of the system will generally be moving as well (unless the system is in pure rotation around it). If the total mass of the particles is ![Image 8: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc), and the center of mass is moving at velocity v cm, the momentum of the system is:

![Image 9: {\displaystyle p=mv_{\text{cm}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/820e8ac543167e213ad98d35a031075ca69004c7)

This is known as [Euler's first law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_laws_of_motion "Euler's laws of motion").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-BookRags-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-McGillKing-3)

### Relation to force

If the net force F applied to a particle is constant, and is applied for a time interval Δ t, the momentum of the particle changes by an amount [![Image 10: {\displaystyle \Delta p=F\Delta t\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/96ed84409d35164bb09b2e3d994d04d3eccd4e98)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q2397319)

In differential form, this is [Newton's second law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_second_law "Newton's second law"); the rate of change of the momentum of a particle is equal to the instantaneous force F acting on it,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-FeynmanCh9-1)![Image 11: {\displaystyle F={\frac {{\text{d}}p}{{\text{d}}t}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/29216db2c3868257343422b890cd3e53d29f526a)

If the net force experienced by a particle changes as a function of time, F(t), the change in momentum (or [impulse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impulse_(physics) "Impulse (physics)")J) between times t 1 and t 2 is ![Image 12: {\displaystyle \Delta p=J=\int _{t_{1}}^{t_{2}}F(t)\,{\text{d}}t\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07a6e49c02b9c9c03218ff822a7378f8ce79a54e)

Impulse is measured in the [derived units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SI_derived_unit "SI derived unit") of the [newton second](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_second "Newton second") (1 N⋅s = 1 kg⋅m/s) or [dyne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dyne "Dyne") second (1 dyne⋅s = 1 g⋅cm/s)

Under the assumption of constant mass m, it is equivalent to write

![Image 13: {\displaystyle F={\frac {{\text{d}}(mv)}{{\text{d}}t}}=m{\frac {{\text{d}}v}{{\text{d}}t}}=ma,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7265791c5837fec0dbe14d5184af30389ca79b01)

hence the net force is equal to the mass of the particle times its [acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-FeynmanCh9-1)

_Example_: A model airplane of mass 1 kg accelerates from rest to a velocity of 6 m/s due north in 2 s. The net force required to produce this acceleration is 3[newtons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton_(unit) "Newton (unit)") due north. The change in momentum is 6 kg⋅m/s due north. The rate of change of momentum is 3(kg⋅m/s)/s due north which is numerically equivalent to 3 newtons.

### Conservation

In a [closed system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_system "Closed system") (one that does not exchange any matter with its surroundings and is not acted on by external forces) the total momentum remains constant. This fact, known as the **law of conservation of momentum**, is implied by [Newton's laws of motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_laws_of_motion "Newton's laws of motion").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-FeynmanCh10-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-5) Suppose, for example, that two particles interact. As explained by the third law, the forces between them are equal in magnitude but opposite in direction. If the particles are numbered 1 and 2, the second law states that F 1 = ⁠d p 1/d t⁠ and F 2 = ⁠d p 2/d t⁠. Therefore,

![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\frac {{\text{d}}p_{1}}{{\text{d}}t}}=-{\frac {{\text{d}}p_{2}}{{\text{d}}t}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6b498a548d127e73e01cb37428f2a38c2c2b636) with the negative sign indicating that the forces oppose. Equivalently,

![Image 15: {\displaystyle {\frac {\text{d}}{{\text{d}}t}}\left(p_{1}+p_{2}\right)=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a867b9dc0465dca892fc5b85171326187f0851a2)

If the velocities of the particles are v A1 and v B1 before the interaction, and afterwards they are v A2 and v B2, then

[![Image 16: {\displaystyle m_{A}v_{A1}+m_{B}v_{B1}=m_{A}v_{A2}+m_{B}v_{B2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d6e18b5524cbf54c82a2f40cdfd9ab8a9af12bdc)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q2305665)

This law holds no matter how complicated the force is between particles. Similarly, if there are several particles, the momentum exchanged between each pair of particles adds to zero, so the total change in momentum is zero. The conservation of the total momentum of a number of interacting particles can be expressed as [[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-FeynmanCh10-4)![Image 17: {\displaystyle m_{A}v_{A}+m_{B}v_{B}+m_{C}v_{C}+\ldots ={\text{constant}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/94a127d647842eeb326373b627b49b33001cf6b0)

This conservation law applies to all interactions, including [collisions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Collision "Collision") (both [elastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elastic_collision "Elastic collision") and [inelastic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inelastic_collision "Inelastic collision")) and separations caused by explosive forces.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-FeynmanCh10-4) It can also be generalized to situations where Newton's laws do not hold, for example in the [theory of relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_relativity "Theory of relativity") and in [electrodynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_electromagnetism "Classical electromagnetism").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-Goldstein54-6)

### Dependence on reference frame

Momentum is a measurable quantity, and the measurement depends on the [frame of reference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frame_of_reference "Frame of reference"). For example: if an aircraft of mass 1000 kg is flying through the air at a speed of 50 m/s its momentum can be calculated to be 50,000 kg.m/s. If the aircraft is flying into a headwind of 5 m/s its speed relative to the surface of the Earth is only 45 m/s and its momentum can be calculated to be 45,000 kg.m/s. Both calculations are equally correct. In both frames of reference, any change in momentum will be found to be consistent with the relevant laws of physics.

Suppose x is a position in an inertial frame of reference. From the point of view of another frame of reference, moving at a constant speed u relative to the other, the position (represented by a primed coordinate) changes with time as

![Image 18: {\displaystyle x'=x-ut\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/05b7bb71b62bcd46e6e5e85be6c2624e0ad3bf27)

This is called a [Galilean transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilean_transformation "Galilean transformation").

If a particle is moving at speed ⁠d x/d t⁠ = v in the first frame of reference, in the second, it is moving at speed

![Image 19: {\displaystyle v'={\frac {{\text{d}}x'}{{\text{d}}t}}=v-u\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8ecbb23d542254786df69a4a380385a23ab7db0d)

Since u does not change, the second reference frame is also an inertial frame and the accelerations are the same:

![Image 20: {\displaystyle a'={\frac {{\text{d}}v'}{{\text{d}}t}}=a\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32794263e1cd078fca081a5e366c1a4ce9026128)

Thus, momentum is conserved in both reference frames. Moreover, as long as the force has the same form, in both frames, Newton's second law is unchanged. Forces such as Newtonian gravity, which depend only on the scalar distance between objects, satisfy this criterion. This independence of reference frame is called Newtonian relativity or [Galilean invariance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilean_invariance "Galilean invariance").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-7)

A change of reference frame can often simplify calculations of motion. For example, in a collision of two particles, a reference frame can be chosen where one particle begins at rest. Another commonly used reference frame is the [center of mass frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Center_of_mass_frame "Center of mass frame") – one that is moving with the center of mass. In this frame, the total momentum is zero.

### Application to collisions

If two particles, each of known momentum, collide and coalesce, the law of conservation of momentum can be used to determine the momentum of the coalesced body. If the outcome of the collision is that the two particles separate, the law is not sufficient to determine the momentum of each particle. If the momentum of one particle after the collision is known, the law can be used to determine the momentum of the other particle. Alternatively if the combined [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy") after the collision is known, the law can be used to determine the momentum of each particle after the collision.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-8) Kinetic energy is usually not conserved. If it is conserved, the collision is called an _[elastic collision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elastic\_collision "Elastic collision")_; if not, it is an _[inelastic collision](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inelastic\_collision "Inelastic collision")_.

#### Elastic collisions

[![Image 21](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c6/Elastischer_sto%C3%9F.gif/250px-Elastischer_sto%C3%9F.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Elastischer_sto%C3%9F.gif)

Elastic collision of equal masses

[![Image 22](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Elastischer_sto%C3%9F3.gif/250px-Elastischer_sto%C3%9F3.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Elastischer_sto%C3%9F3.gif)

Elastic collision of unequal masses

An elastic collision is one in which no [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy") is transformed into heat or some other form of energy. Perfectly elastic collisions can occur when the objects do not touch each other, as for example in atomic or nuclear scattering where electric repulsion keeps the objects apart. A [slingshot maneuver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity_assist "Gravity assist") of a satellite around a planet can also be viewed as a perfectly elastic collision. A collision between two [pool](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pool_billiards "Pool billiards") balls is a good example of an _almost_ totally elastic collision, due to their high [rigidity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stiffness "Stiffness"), but when bodies come in contact there is always some [dissipation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissipation "Dissipation").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-9)

A head-on elastic collision between two bodies can be represented by velocities in one dimension, along a line passing through the bodies. If the velocities are v A1 and v B1 before the collision and v A2 and v B2 after, the equations expressing conservation of momentum and kinetic energy are:

![Image 23: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}m_{A}v_{A1}+m_{B}v_{B1}&=m_{A}v_{A2}+m_{B}v_{B2}\\{\tfrac {1}{2}}m_{A}v_{A1}^{2}+{\tfrac {1}{2}}m_{B}v_{B1}^{2}&={\tfrac {1}{2}}m_{A}v_{A2}^{2}+{\tfrac {1}{2}}m_{B}v_{B2}^{2}\,.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d39be080c25e5354eeca0a90d15fb34897ef03e2)

A change of reference frame can simplify analysis of a collision. For example, suppose there are two bodies of equal mass m, one stationary and one approaching the other at a speed v (as in the figure). The center of mass is moving at speed ⁠v/2⁠ and both bodies are moving towards it at speed ⁠v/2⁠. Because of the symmetry, after the collision both must be moving away from the center of mass at the same speed. Adding the speed of the center of mass to both, we find that the body that was moving is now stopped and the other is moving away at speed v. The bodies have exchanged their velocities. Regardless of the velocities of the bodies, a switch to the center of mass frame leads us to the same conclusion. Therefore, the final velocities are given by[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-FeynmanCh10-4)

![Image 24: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}v_{A2}&=v_{B1}\\v_{B2}&=v_{A1}\,.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/242d3b584b063c7b9438da956be0e06a893df288)

In general, when the initial velocities are known, the final velocities are given by[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-10)

![Image 25: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}v_{A2}&=\left({\frac {m_{A}-m_{B}}{m_{A}+m_{B}}}\right)v_{A1}+\left({\frac {2m_{B}}{m_{A}+m_{B}}}\right)v_{B1}\\v_{B2}&=\left({\frac {m_{B}-m_{A}}{m_{A}+m_{B}}}\right)v_{B1}+\left({\frac {2m_{A}}{m_{A}+m_{B}}}\right)v_{A1}\,.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2da536fb0316fb5a6608a4d8fb6466a94593356b)

If one body has much greater mass than the other, its velocity will be little affected by a collision while the other body will experience a large change.

#### Inelastic collisions

[![Image 26](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/de/Inelastischer_sto%C3%9F.gif/250px-Inelastischer_sto%C3%9F.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Inelastischer_sto%C3%9F.gif)

a perfectly inelastic collision between equal masses

In an inelastic collision, some of the kinetic energy of the colliding bodies is converted into other forms of energy (such as [heat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heat "Heat") or [sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sound "Sound")). Examples include [traffic collisions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traffic_collisions "Traffic collisions"),[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-11) in which the effect of loss of kinetic energy can be seen in the damage to the vehicles; electrons losing some of their energy to atoms (as in the [Franck–Hertz experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Franck%E2%80%93Hertz_experiment "Franck–Hertz experiment"));[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-12) and [particle accelerators](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_accelerator "Particle accelerator") in which the kinetic energy is converted into mass in the form of new particles.

In a perfectly inelastic collision (such as a bug hitting a windshield), both bodies have the same motion afterwards. A head-on inelastic collision between two bodies can be represented by velocities in one dimension, along a line passing through the bodies. If the velocities are v A1 and v B1 before the collision then in a perfectly inelastic collision both bodies will be travelling with velocity v 2 after the collision. The equation expressing conservation of momentum is:

![Image 27: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}m_{A}v_{A1}+m_{B}v_{B1}&=\left(m_{A}+m_{B}\right)v_{2}\,.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6b061d520fd1927a26fdc0cfc236ae3775a202d)

If one body is motionless to begin with (e.g. ![Image 28: {\displaystyle v_{B1}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bcafefd2292bf2651403211eb3f19109807c4bdc)), the equation for conservation of momentum is

![Image 29: {\displaystyle m_{A}v_{A1}=\left(m_{A}+m_{B}\right)v_{2}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b227d560bbc813ebacb4ef0677540440b349fff)

so

![Image 30: {\displaystyle v_{2}={\frac {m_{A}}{m_{A}+m_{B}}}v_{A1}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f51ffab923d9a04db8d436ae9b0d8a7ac1fd2b21)

In a different situation, if the frame of reference is moving at the final velocity such that ![Image 31: {\displaystyle v_{2}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff598dc268fa4d52edf5eea2c524356cfb6472b6), the objects would be brought to rest by a perfectly inelastic collision and 100% of the kinetic energy is converted to other forms of energy. In this instance the initial velocities of the bodies would be non-zero, or the bodies would have to be massless.

One measure of the inelasticity of the collision is the [coefficient of restitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coefficient_of_restitution "Coefficient of restitution")C R, defined as the ratio of relative velocity of separation to relative velocity of approach. In applying this measure to a ball bouncing from a solid surface, this can be easily measured using the following formula:[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-13)

![Image 32: {\displaystyle C_{\text{R}}={\sqrt {\frac {\text{bounce height}}{\text{drop height}}}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/89c352c513f4323754caefd5d0c9f301ee18107a)

The momentum and energy equations also apply to the motions of objects that begin together and then move apart. For example, an [explosion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explosion "Explosion") is the result of a chain reaction that transforms potential energy stored in chemical, mechanical, or nuclear form into kinetic energy, acoustic energy, and electromagnetic radiation. [Rockets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket "Rocket") also make use of conservation of momentum: propellant is thrust outward, gaining momentum, and an equal and opposite momentum is imparted to the rocket.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-14)

### Multiple dimensions

[![Image 33](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Elastischer_sto%C3%9F_2D.gif/250px-Elastischer_sto%C3%9F_2D.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Elastischer_sto%C3%9F_2D.gif)

Two-dimensional elastic collision. There is no motion perpendicular to the image, so only two components are needed to represent the velocities and momenta. The two blue vectors represent velocities after the collision and add vectorially to get the initial (red) velocity.

Real motion has both direction and velocity and must be represented by a [vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(geometry) "Vector (geometry)"). In a coordinate system with x, y, z axes, velocity has components v x in the x-direction, v y in the y-direction, v z in the z-direction. The vector is represented by a boldface symbol:[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-FeynmanCh11-15)

![Image 34: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} =\left(v_{x},v_{y},v_{z}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87828c8eca817d5a29bbc10ed280a19589fab149)

Similarly, the momentum is a vector quantity and is represented by a boldface symbol:

![Image 35: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} =\left(p_{x},p_{y},p_{z}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f811d2e927c0b4fcc89d88be76237af3ac53748d)

The equations in the previous sections, work in vector form if the scalars p and v are replaced by vectors **p** and **v**. Each vector equation represents three scalar equations. For example,

![Image 36: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} =m\mathbf {v} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a271a96e7b925fd39686375167c76d406e87c813)

represents three equations:[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-FeynmanCh11-15)

![Image 37: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}p_{x}&=mv_{x}\\p_{y}&=mv_{y}\\p_{z}&=mv_{z}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/048790c67b2f03abfdefa3c80144a78fa965a15c)

The kinetic energy equations are exceptions to the above replacement rule. The equations are still one-dimensional, but each scalar represents the [magnitude of the vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitude_(mathematics)#Euclidean_vector_space "Magnitude (mathematics)"), for example,

![Image 38: {\displaystyle v^{2}=v_{x}^{2}+v_{y}^{2}+v_{z}^{2}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bdc305729a263c2bbcdab32f6f765118536af299)

Each vector equation represents three scalar equations. Often coordinates can be chosen so that only two components are needed, as in the figure. Each component can be obtained separately and the results combined to produce a vector result.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-FeynmanCh11-15)

A simple construction involving the center of mass frame can be used to show that if a stationary elastic sphere is struck by a moving sphere, the two will head off at right angles after the collision (as in the figure).[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-16)

### Objects of variable mass

The concept of momentum plays a fundamental role in explaining the behavior of variable-mass objects such as a [rocket](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rocket "Rocket") ejecting fuel or a [star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star")[accreting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accretion_(astrophysics) "Accretion (astrophysics)") gas. In analyzing such an object, one treats the object's mass as a function that varies with time: m(t). The momentum of the object at time t is therefore p(t) = m(t)v(t). One might then try to invoke Newton's second law of motion by saying that the external force F on the object is related to its momentum p(t) by F = ⁠d p/d t⁠, but this is incorrect, as is the related expression found by applying the product rule to ⁠d(m v)/d t⁠:[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-kleppner135-17)

![Image 39: {\displaystyle F=m(t){\frac {{\text{d}}v}{{\text{d}}t}}+v(t){\frac {{\text{d}}m}{{\text{d}}t}}.{\text{(incorrect)}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a345e1181b8dcd52d40ec0b3b1f4c2855c44330f)

This equation does not correctly describe the motion of variable-mass objects. The correct equation is

![Image 40: {\displaystyle F=m(t){\frac {{\text{d}}v}{{\text{d}}t}}-u{\frac {{\text{d}}m}{{\text{d}}t}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/95af30150307a77c42d87687fd27a91c9928ecec)

where u is the velocity of the ejected/accreted mass _as seen in the object's rest frame_.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-kleppner135-17) This is distinct from v, which is the velocity of the object itself as seen in an inertial frame.

This equation is derived by keeping track of both the momentum of the object as well as the momentum of the ejected/accreted mass (d m). When considered together, the object and the mass (d m) constitute a closed system in which total momentum is conserved.

![Image 41: {\displaystyle P(t+{\text{d}}t)=(m-{\text{d}}m)(v+{\text{d}}v)+{\text{d}}m(v-u)=mv+m{\text{d}}v-u{\text{d}}m=P(t)+m{\text{d}}v-u{\text{d}}m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/84a76792a96d7c1b01b2f5c86b87859e984bdcb7)

Generalized
-----------

Newton's laws can be difficult to apply to many kinds of motion because the motion is limited by _constraints_. For example, a bead on an abacus is constrained to move along its wire and a pendulum bob is constrained to swing at a fixed distance from the pivot. Many such constraints can be incorporated by changing the normal [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates") to a set of _[generalized coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized\_coordinates "Generalized coordinates")_ that may be fewer in number.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-18) Refined mathematical methods have been developed for solving mechanics problems in generalized coordinates. They introduce a _generalized momentum_, also known as the _canonical momentum_ or _conjugate momentum_, that extends the concepts of both linear momentum and [angular momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_momentum "Angular momentum"). To distinguish it from generalized momentum, the product of mass and velocity is also referred to as _mechanical momentum_, _kinetic momentum_ or _kinematic momentum_.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-Goldstein54-6)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-19)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-FeynmanQM-20) The two main methods are described below.

### Lagrangian mechanics

In [Lagrangian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics"), a Lagrangian is defined as the difference between the kinetic energy T and the [potential energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potential_energy "Potential energy")V:

![Image 42: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}=T-V\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb009b20edbf4244a71a1f8564ba40a89e57fd64)

If the generalized coordinates are represented as a vector **q** = (q 1, q 2, ... , q N)  and time differentiation is represented by a dot over the variable, then the equations of motion (known as the Lagrange or [Euler–Lagrange equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%E2%80%93Lagrange_equation "Euler–Lagrange equation")) are a set of N equations:[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-21)

![Image 43: {\displaystyle {\frac {\text{d}}{{\text{d}}t}}\left({\frac {\partial {\mathcal {L}}}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{j}}}\right)-{\frac {\partial {\mathcal {L}}}{\partial q_{j}}}=0\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e704f970e1677b0dfcbc5b1e3766c632a3e306c4)

If a coordinate q i is not a Cartesian coordinate, the associated generalized momentum component p i does not necessarily have the dimensions of linear momentum. Even if q i is a Cartesian coordinate, p i will not be the same as the mechanical momentum if the potential depends on velocity.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-Goldstein54-6) Some sources represent the kinematic momentum by the symbol **Π**.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-Lerner-22)

In this mathematical framework, a generalized momentum is associated with the generalized coordinates. Its components are defined as

![Image 44: {\displaystyle p_{j}={\frac {\partial {\mathcal {L}}}{\partial {\dot {q}}_{j}}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7a4539399149807e96a002329571bf09ab728257)

Each component p j is said to be the _conjugate momentum_ for the coordinate q j.

Now if a given coordinate q i does not appear in the Lagrangian (although its time derivative might appear), then p j is constant. This is the generalization of the conservation of momentum.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-Goldstein54-6)

Even if the generalized coordinates are just the ordinary spatial coordinates, the conjugate momenta are not necessarily the ordinary momentum coordinates. An example is found in the section on electromagnetism.

### Hamiltonian mechanics

In [Hamiltonian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics "Hamiltonian mechanics"), the Lagrangian (a function of generalized coordinates and their derivatives) is replaced by a Hamiltonian that is a function of generalized coordinates and momentum. The Hamiltonian is defined as

![Image 45: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {H}}\left(\mathbf {q} ,\mathbf {p} ,t\right)=\mathbf {p} \cdot {\dot {\mathbf {q} }}-{\mathcal {L}}\left(\mathbf {q} ,{\dot {\mathbf {q} }},t\right)\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f54145f5d85289262062b635cd5583d713c364c0)

where the momentum is obtained by differentiating the Lagrangian as above. The Hamiltonian equations of motion are[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-23)

![Image 46: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\dot {q}}_{i}&={\frac {\partial {\mathcal {H}}}{\partial p_{i}}}\\-{\dot {p}}_{i}&={\frac {\partial {\mathcal {H}}}{\partial q_{i}}}\\-{\frac {\partial {\mathcal {L}}}{\partial t}}&={\frac {{\text{d}}{\mathcal {H}}}{{\text{d}}t}}\,.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/83e21a7536d8c0de930c9a9333673946a18c52da)

As in Lagrangian mechanics, if a generalized coordinate does not appear in the Hamiltonian, its conjugate momentum component is conserved.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-24)

### Symmetry and conservation

Conservation of momentum is a mathematical consequence of the [homogeneity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homogeneity_(physics) "Homogeneity (physics)") (shift [symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry "Symmetry")) of space (position in space is the [canonical conjugate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Canonical_conjugate "Canonical conjugate") quantity to momentum). That is, conservation of momentum is a consequence of the fact that the laws of physics do not depend on position; this is a special case of [Noether's theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noether%27s_theorem "Noether's theorem").[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-25) For systems that do not have this symmetry, it may not be possible to define conservation of momentum. Examples where conservation of momentum does not apply include [curved spacetimes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curved_space "Curved space") in [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity")[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-26) or [time crystals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time_crystals "Time crystals") in [condensed matter physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Condensed_matter_physics "Condensed matter physics").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-Grossman_2012-27)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-Cowen_2012-28)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-Powell_2013-29)[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-Gibney_2017-30)

### In deformable bodies and fluids

#### Conservation in a continuum

[![Image 47](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f0/Equation_motion_body.svg/250px-Equation_motion_body.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Equation_motion_body.svg)

Motion of a material body

In fields such as [fluid dynamics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_dynamics "Fluid dynamics") and [solid mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solid_mechanics "Solid mechanics"), it is not feasible to follow the motion of individual atoms or molecules. Instead, the materials must be approximated by a [continuum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuum_mechanics "Continuum mechanics") in which, at each point, there is a particle or [fluid parcel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluid_parcel "Fluid parcel") that is assigned the average of the properties of atoms in a small region nearby. In particular, it has a density ρ and velocity **v** that depend on time t and position **r**. The momentum per unit volume is ρ**v**.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-31)

Consider a column of water in [hydrostatic equilibrium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrostatic_equilibrium "Hydrostatic equilibrium"). All the forces on the water are in balance and the water is motionless. On any given drop of water, two forces are balanced. The first is gravity, which acts directly on each atom and molecule inside. The gravitational force per unit volume is ρ**g**, where **g** is the [gravitational acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_acceleration "Gravitational acceleration"). The second force is the sum of all the forces exerted on its surface by the surrounding water. The force from below is greater than the force from above by just the amount needed to balance gravity. The normal force per unit area is the [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure")p. The average force per unit volume inside the droplet is the gradient of the pressure, so the force balance equation is[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-FeynmanCh40-32)

![Image 48: {\displaystyle -\nabla p+\rho \mathbf {g} =0\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5af9af8a07b2a2fbc21114360572dcd4d4583dc9)

If the forces are not balanced, the droplet accelerates. This acceleration is not simply the partial derivative ⁠_∂_**v**/_∂_ t⁠ because the fluid in a given volume changes with time. Instead, the [material derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Material_derivative "Material derivative") is needed:[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-33)

![Image 49: {\displaystyle {\frac {D}{Dt}}\equiv {\frac {\partial }{\partial t}}+\mathbf {v} \cdot {\boldsymbol {\nabla }}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06e6300772911a8adf746640cd17a3873349ee6e)

Applied to any physical quantity, the material derivative includes the rate of change at a point and the changes due to [advection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Advection "Advection") as fluid is carried past the point. Per unit volume, the rate of change in momentum is equal to ρ⁠_D_**v**/_D_ t⁠. This is equal to the net force on the droplet.

Forces that can change the momentum of a droplet include the gradient of the pressure and gravity, as above. In addition, surface forces can deform the droplet. In the simplest case, a [shear stress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shear_stress "Shear stress")τ, exerted by a force parallel to the surface of the droplet, is proportional to the rate of deformation or [strain rate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strain_rate "Strain rate"). Such a shear stress occurs if the fluid has a velocity gradient because the fluid is moving faster on one side than another. If the speed in the x direction varies with z, the tangential force in direction x per unit area normal to the z direction is

![Image 50: {\displaystyle \sigma _{zx}=-\mu {\frac {\partial v_{x}}{\partial z}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/63aa1a782152c0cdc4ed33f749c22eb19c3adad0)

where μ is the [viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscosity "Viscosity"). This is also a [flux](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flux "Flux"), or flow per unit area, of x-momentum through the surface.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-34)

Including the effect of viscosity, the momentum balance equations for the [incompressible flow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incompressible_flow "Incompressible flow") of a [Newtonian fluid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newtonian_fluid "Newtonian fluid") are

![Image 51: {\displaystyle \rho {\frac {D\mathbf {v} }{Dt}}=-{\boldsymbol {\nabla }}p+\mu \nabla ^{2}\mathbf {v} +\rho \mathbf {g} .\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8f63d8f029a0627ff37ef9c1f252acb31f9eca5b)

These are known as the [Navier–Stokes equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Navier%E2%80%93Stokes_equations "Navier–Stokes equations").[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-35)

The momentum balance equations can be extended to more general materials, including solids. For each surface with normal in direction i and force in direction j, there is a stress component σ i j. The nine components make up the [Cauchy stress tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy_stress_tensor "Cauchy stress tensor")**σ**, which includes both pressure and shear. The local conservation of momentum is expressed by the [Cauchy momentum equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy_momentum_equation "Cauchy momentum equation"):

![Image 52: {\displaystyle \rho {\frac {D\mathbf {v} }{Dt}}={\boldsymbol {\nabla }}\cdot {\boldsymbol {\sigma }}+\mathbf {f} \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2138aab218677da33d90206fdb945fda9e630f0)

where **f** is the [body force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Body_force "Body force").[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-36)

The Cauchy momentum equation is broadly applicable to [deformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deformation_(mechanics) "Deformation (mechanics)") of solids and liquids. The relationship between the stresses and the strain rate depends on the properties of the material (see [Types of viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Viscosity#Types_of_viscosity "Viscosity")).

#### Acoustic waves

A disturbance in a medium gives rise to oscillations, or [waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave "Wave"), that propagate away from their source. In a fluid, small changes in pressure p can often be described by the [acoustic wave equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acoustic_wave_equation "Acoustic wave equation"):

![Image 53: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial ^{2}p}{\partial t^{2}}}=c^{2}\nabla ^{2}p\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c8b5b90da3fdb0bbb9a7f465a9ccfb1c10a26932)

where c is the [speed of sound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_sound "Speed of sound"). In a solid, similar equations can be obtained for propagation of pressure ([P-waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/P-wave "P-wave")) and shear ([S-waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S-waves "S-waves")).[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-37)

The flux, or transport per unit area, of a momentum component ρ v j by a velocity v i is equal to ρ v j v j.[_[dubious](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Accuracy\_dispute#Disputed\_statement "Wikipedia:Accuracy dispute") – [discuss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Talk:Momentum#Dubious "Talk:Momentum")_] In the linear approximation that leads to the above acoustic equation, the time average of this flux is zero. However, nonlinear effects can give rise to a nonzero average.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-38) It is possible for momentum flux to occur even though the wave itself does not have a mean momentum.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-39)

### In electromagnetics

#### Particle in a field

In [Maxwell's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell%27s_equations "Maxwell's equations"), the forces between particles are mediated by electric and magnetic fields. The electromagnetic force (_[Lorentz force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz\_force "Lorentz force")_) on a particle with charge q due to a combination of [electric field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_field "Electric field")**E** and [magnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_field "Magnetic field")**B** is

[![Image 54: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} =q(\mathbf {E} +\mathbf {v} \times \mathbf {B} ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/98b985e6332ac21f2eb2b6aeefae9135fe6fe3c6)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q849919)

(in [SI units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units")).[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-40): 2  It has an [electric potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electric_potential "Electric potential")φ(**r**, t) and [magnetic vector potential](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetic_vector_potential "Magnetic vector potential")**A**(**r**, t).[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-Lerner-22) In the non-relativistic regime, its generalized momentum is

![Image 55: {\displaystyle \mathbf {P} =m\mathbf {\mathbf {v} } +q\mathbf {A} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6d598dfaaef9d1d457882afd31e9f7aa402a24b4)

while in relativistic mechanics this becomes

![Image 56: {\displaystyle \mathbf {P} =\gamma m\mathbf {\mathbf {v} } +q\mathbf {A} .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9adf040ba5faf7e2c8b4b2d9126842872c80bcd2)

The quantity V = q**A** is sometimes called the _potential momentum_.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-41)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-42)[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-43) It is the momentum due to the interaction of the particle with the electromagnetic fields. The name is an analogy with the potential energy U = q φ, which is the energy due to the interaction of the particle with the electromagnetic fields. These quantities form a four-vector, so the analogy is consistent; besides, the concept of potential momentum is important in explaining the so-called [hidden momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hidden_momentum "Hidden momentum") of the electromagnetic fields.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-44)

#### Conservation

In Newtonian mechanics, the law of conservation of momentum can be derived from the [law of action and reaction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Law_of_action_and_reaction "Law of action and reaction"), which states that every force has a reciprocating equal and opposite force. Under some circumstances, moving charged particles can exert forces on each other in non-opposite directions.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-Griffiths-45) Nevertheless, the combined momentum of the particles and the electromagnetic field is conserved.

##### Vacuum

The Lorentz force imparts a momentum to the particle, so by Newton's second law the particle must impart a momentum to the electromagnetic fields.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-Jackson238-46)

In a vacuum, the momentum per unit volume is

![Image 57: {\displaystyle \mathbf {g} ={\frac {1}{\mu _{0}c^{2}}}\mathbf {E} \times \mathbf {B} \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c371084d5c2924bf8320bad230b7a1fac7b1460)

where μ 0 is the [vacuum permeability](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_permeability "Vacuum permeability") and c is the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light"). The momentum density is proportional to the [Poynting vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poynting_vector "Poynting vector")**S** which gives the directional rate of energy transfer per unit area:[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-Jackson238-46)[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-FeynmanCh27-47)

![Image 58: {\displaystyle \mathbf {g} ={\frac {\mathbf {S} }{c^{2}}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c0ddc861f562f9087b493317dc68094b75683f84)

If momentum is to be conserved over the volume V over a region Q, changes in the momentum of matter through the Lorentz force must be balanced by changes in the momentum of the electromagnetic field and outflow of momentum. If **P**mech is the momentum of all the particles in Q, and the particles are treated as a continuum, then Newton's second law gives

![Image 59: {\displaystyle {\frac {{\text{d}}\mathbf {P} _{\text{mech}}}{{\text{d}}t}}=\iiint \limits _{Q}\left(\rho \mathbf {E} +\mathbf {J} \times \mathbf {B} \right){\text{d}}V\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fde8523263b682f31bc9d09789f5cd90c27dfedb)

The electromagnetic momentum is

![Image 60: {\displaystyle \mathbf {P} _{\text{field}}={\frac {1}{\mu _{0}c^{2}}}\iiint \limits _{Q}\mathbf {E} \times \mathbf {B} \,dV\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7419ebbb4464057c334f377e37d3780f6b5ef82f)

and the equation for conservation of each component i of the momentum is

![Image 61: {\displaystyle {\frac {\text{d}}{{\text{d}}t}}\left(\mathbf {P} _{\text{mech}}+\mathbf {P} _{\text{field}}\right)_{i}=\iint \limits _{\sigma }\left(\sum \limits _{j}T_{ij}n_{j}\right){\text{d}}\Sigma \,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/109082aa9621f1778eda695cd09e4ceb4184cf86)

The term on the right is an integral over the surface area Σ of the surface σ representing momentum flow into and out of the volume, and n j is a component of the surface normal of S. The quantity T i j is called the [Maxwell stress tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maxwell_stress_tensor "Maxwell stress tensor"), defined as[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-Jackson238-46)

![Image 62: {\displaystyle T_{ij}\equiv \epsilon _{0}\left(E_{i}E_{j}-{\frac {1}{2}}\delta _{ij}E^{2}\right)+{\frac {1}{\mu _{0}}}\left(B_{i}B_{j}-{\frac {1}{2}}\delta _{ij}B^{2}\right)\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8efd050fa2761fbc80dbb43034fe8a3b038aabf7)

##### Media

The above results are for the _microscopic_ Maxwell equations, applicable to electromagnetic forces in a vacuum (or on a very small scale in media). It is more difficult to define momentum density in media because the division into electromagnetic and mechanical is arbitrary. The definition of electromagnetic momentum density is modified to

![Image 63: {\displaystyle \mathbf {g} ={\frac {1}{c^{2}}}\mathbf {E} \times \mathbf {H} ={\frac {\mathbf {S} }{c^{2}}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cca3cb60e3dc5a35e1770d30eeb906a0bf3ffaf2)

where the H-field **H** is related to the B-field and the [magnetization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnetization "Magnetization")**M** by

![Image 64: {\displaystyle \mathbf {B} =\mu _{0}\left(\mathbf {H} +\mathbf {M} \right)\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b7fa74e0861419d936cab17363805189acd3179e)

The electromagnetic stress tensor depends on the properties of the media.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-Jackson238-46)

Non-classical
-------------

### Quantum mechanical

In [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), momentum is defined as a [self-adjoint operator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Self-adjoint_operator "Self-adjoint operator") on the [wave function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wave_function "Wave function"). The [Heisenberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Werner_Heisenberg "Werner Heisenberg")[uncertainty principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uncertainty_principle "Uncertainty principle") defines limits on how accurately the momentum and position of a single observable system can be known at once. In quantum mechanics, position and momentum are [conjugate variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conjugate_variables "Conjugate variables").

For a single particle described in the position basis the momentum operator can be written as

![Image 65: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} ={\hbar  \over i}\nabla =-i\hbar \nabla \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2ad37fde62d56189418758cf5dc239a5d1e5cb59)

where ∇ is the [gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gradient "Gradient") operator, ħ is the [reduced Planck constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reduced_Planck_constant "Reduced Planck constant"), and i is the [imaginary unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit "Imaginary unit"). This is a commonly encountered form of the momentum operator, though the momentum operator in other bases can take other forms. For example, in [momentum space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum_space "Momentum space") the momentum operator is represented by the [eigenvalue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eigenvalue "Eigenvalue") equation

![Image 66: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} \psi (p)=p\psi (p)\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b928c9445f9939bd3c0ba212c0b619750e259699)

where the operator **p** acting on a wave eigenfunction ψ(p) yields that wave function multiplied by the eigenvalue p, in an analogous fashion to the way that the position operator acting on a wave function ψ(x) yields that wave function multiplied by the eigenvalue x.

For both massive and massless objects, relativistic momentum is related to the [phase constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phase_constant "Phase constant")β by[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-48)

![Image 67: {\displaystyle p=\hbar \beta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/808e88a516cd633249ab865b1c22a2d377d5ca00)

[Electromagnetic radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_radiation "Electromagnetic radiation") (including [visible light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light "Light"), [ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet") light, and [radio waves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_waves "Radio waves")) is carried by [photons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photons "Photons"). Even though photons (the particle aspect of light) have no mass, they still carry momentum. This leads to applications such as the [solar sail](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_sail "Solar sail"). The calculation of the momentum of light within [dielectric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dielectric "Dielectric") media is somewhat controversial (see [Abraham–Minkowski controversy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abraham%E2%80%93Minkowski_controversy "Abraham–Minkowski controversy")).[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-49)[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-50)

### Relativistic

#### Lorentz invariance

Newtonian physics assumes that [absolute time and space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_time_and_space "Absolute time and space") exist outside of any observer; this gives rise to [Galilean invariance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilean_invariance "Galilean invariance"). It also results in a prediction that the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light") can vary from one reference frame to another. This is contrary to what has been observed. In the [special theory of relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_theory_of_relativity "Special theory of relativity"), Einstein keeps the postulate that the equations of motion do not depend on the reference frame, but assumes that the speed of light c is invariant. As a result, position and time in two reference frames are related by the [Lorentz transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_transformation "Lorentz transformation") instead of the [Galilean transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilean_transformation "Galilean transformation").[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-RindlerCh2-51)

Consider, for example, one reference frame moving relative to another at velocity v in the x direction. The Galilean transformation gives the coordinates of the moving frame as

![Image 68: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}t'&=t\\x'&=x-vt\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c037154888facbaaea129b6cb93719cc067fa800)

while the Lorentz transformation gives[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-FeynmanCh15-52)

![Image 69: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}t'&=\gamma \left(t-{\frac {vx}{c^{2}}}\right)\\x'&=\gamma \left(x-vt\right)\,\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bd5d38963e4c0c8c9b0aec9aca30e6c49ab68c34)

where γ is the [Lorentz factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_factor "Lorentz factor"):

[![Image 70: {\displaystyle \gamma ={\frac {1}{\sqrt {1-v^{2}/c^{2}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cfe4e10cca53d8ca26a83eaf41c74c23b3ce4219)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q599404)

Newton's second law, with mass fixed, is not invariant under a Lorentz transformation. However, it can be made invariant by making the _inertial mass_ m of an object a function of velocity:

![Image 71: {\displaystyle m=\gamma m_{0}\,;}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e966a59aaab70d4dd8855d02c0da45afc9c97e2c)

m 0 is the object's [invariant mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_mass "Invariant mass").[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-Rindler-53)

The modified momentum,

![Image 72: {\displaystyle \mathbf {p} =\gamma m_{0}\mathbf {v} \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d2c196a3d5f67016106e1b305fc89cf06e914b8a)

obeys Newton's second law:

![Image 73: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} ={\frac {d\mathbf {p} }{dt}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99ecbac6df650ec669de4173a064ab3824920644)

Within the domain of classical mechanics, relativistic momentum closely approximates Newtonian momentum: at low velocity, γ m 0**v** is approximately equal to m 0**v**, the Newtonian expression for momentum.

#### Four-vector formulation

In the theory of special relativity, physical quantities are expressed in terms of [four-vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-vector "Four-vector") that include time as a fourth coordinate along with the three space coordinates. These vectors are generally represented by capital letters, for example **R** for position. The expression for the _four-momentum_ depends on how the coordinates are expressed. Time may be given in its normal units or multiplied by the speed of light so that all the components of the four-vector have dimensions of length. If the latter scaling is used, an interval of [proper time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proper_time "Proper time"), τ, defined by[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-54)

![Image 74: {\displaystyle c^{2}{\text{d}}\tau ^{2}=c^{2}{\text{d}}t^{2}-{\text{d}}x^{2}-{\text{d}}y^{2}-{\text{d}}z^{2}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0095543d611a232a4ba8f049fddec7b8264e00ba)

is [invariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Invariant_(physics) "Invariant (physics)") under Lorentz transformations (in this expression and in what follows the (+ − − −)[metric signature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_signature "Metric signature") has been used, different authors use different conventions). Mathematically this invariance can be ensured in one of two ways: by treating the four-vectors as [Euclidean vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") and multiplying time by [√−1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_unit "Imaginary unit"); or by keeping time a real quantity and embedding the vectors in a [Minkowski space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_space "Minkowski space").[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-55) In a Minkowski space, the [scalar product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scalar_product "Scalar product") of two four-vectors **U** = (U 0, U 1, U 2, U 3) and **V** = (V 0, V 1, V 2, V 3) is defined as

![Image 75: {\displaystyle \mathbf {U} \cdot \mathbf {V} =U_{0}V_{0}-U_{1}V_{1}-U_{2}V_{2}-U_{3}V_{3}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7c3dfb58b4e97dd14acfa16692a0a639a3588979)

In all the coordinate systems, the ([contravariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariance_and_contravariance_of_vectors "Covariance and contravariance of vectors")) relativistic four-velocity is defined by

![Image 76: {\displaystyle \mathbf {U} \equiv {\frac {{\text{d}}\mathbf {R} }{{\text{d}}\tau }}=\gamma {\frac {{\text{d}}\mathbf {R} }{{\text{d}}t}}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0c9b6a92fa2321a83a2113c34322182ff6c2dbb5)

and the (contravariant) [four-momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-momentum "Four-momentum") is

[![Image 77: {\displaystyle \mathbf {P} =m_{0}\mathbf {U} \,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40cec5840d1b8724ce04e95c8b428575e3060fb8)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q1068463)

where m 0 is the invariant mass. If **R** = (c t, x, y, z) (in Minkowski space), then

![Image 78: {\displaystyle \mathbf {P} =\gamma m_{0}\left(c,\mathbf {v} \right)=(mc,\mathbf {p} )\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2bae79ae206d7d8da13d9d9110efc7403608a6ef)

Using Einstein's [mass–energy equivalence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass%E2%80%93energy_equivalence "Mass–energy equivalence"), E = m c 2, this can be rewritten as

![Image 79: {\displaystyle \mathbf {P} =\left({\frac {E}{c}},\mathbf {p} \right)\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8675fb30bbff8e7d4152dd5c16e348ba79f9c976)

Thus, conservation of four-momentum is Lorentz-invariant and implies conservation of both mass and energy.

The magnitude of the momentum four-vector is equal to m 0 c:

![Image 80: {\displaystyle \|\mathbf {P} \|^{2}=\mathbf {P} \cdot \mathbf {P} =\gamma ^{2}m_{0}^{2}\left(c^{2}-v^{2}\right)=(m_{0}c)^{2}\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a77a8fea2237b1c59ee6bf950a1f0e89775fc402)

and is invariant across all reference frames.

The relativistic energy–momentum relationship holds even for massless particles such as photons; by setting m 0 = 0 it follows that

![Image 81: {\displaystyle E=pc\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2e55dc867ed9d89166a2c7318f609e0cd5ff911)

In a game of relativistic "billiards", if a stationary particle is hit by a moving particle in an elastic collision, the paths formed by the two afterwards will form an acute angle. This is unlike the non-relativistic case where they travel at right angles.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-56)

The four-momentum of a planar wave can be related to a wave four-vector[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-57)

![Image 82: {\displaystyle \mathbf {P} =\left({\frac {E}{c}},{\vec {\mathbf {p} }}\right)=\hbar \mathbf {K} =\hbar \left({\frac {\omega }{c}},{\vec {\mathbf {k} }}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aa7e3358ef32788e1fe4d339c6b47a90b379bddc)

For a particle, the relationship between temporal components, E = ħ ω, is the [Planck–Einstein relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck%E2%80%93Einstein_relation "Planck–Einstein relation"), and the relation between spatial components, **p** = ħ**k**, describes a [de Broglie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Broglie "De Broglie")[matter wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter_wave "Matter wave").

History of the concept
----------------------

### Impetus

#### John Philoponus

In about 530 AD, [John Philoponus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Philoponus "John Philoponus") developed a concept of momentum in _On Physics_, a commentary to [Aristotle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aristotle "Aristotle")'s _[Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physics\_(Aristotle) "Physics (Aristotle)")_. Aristotle claimed that everything that is moving must be kept moving by something. For example, a thrown ball must be kept moving by motions of the air. Philoponus pointed out the absurdity in Aristotle's claim that motion of an object is promoted by the same air that is resisting its passage. He proposed instead that an impetus was imparted to the object in the act of throwing it.[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-58)

#### Ibn Sīnā

[![Image 83: Engraving of Ibn Sīnā](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/1950_%22Avicenna%22_stamp_of_Iran_%28cropped%29.jpg/250px-1950_%22Avicenna%22_stamp_of_Iran_%28cropped%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:1950_%22Avicenna%22_stamp_of_Iran_(cropped).jpg)

Ibn Sīnā

(980–1037)

In 1020, [Ibn Sīnā](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avicenna "Avicenna") (also known by his [Latinized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latinisation_of_names "Latinisation of names") name Avicenna) read Philoponus and published his own theory of motion in _[The Book of Healing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The\_Book\_of\_Healing "The Book of Healing")_. He agreed that an impetus is imparted to a projectile by the thrower; but unlike Philoponus, who believed that it was a temporary virtue that would decline even in a vacuum, he viewed it as a persistent, requiring external forces such as [air resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_resistance "Air resistance") to dissipate it.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-Espinoza-59)[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-Nasr-60)[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-Sayili-61)

#### Peter Olivi, Jean Buridan

In the 13th and 14th century, [Peter Olivi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peter_Olivi "Peter Olivi") and [Jean Buridan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jean_Buridan "Jean Buridan") read and refined the work of Philoponus, and possibly that of Ibn Sīnā.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-Sayili-61) Buridan, who in about 1350 was made rector of the University of Paris, referred to [impetus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theory_of_impetus "Theory of impetus") being proportional to the weight times the speed. Moreover, Buridan's theory was different from his predecessor's in that he did not consider impetus to be self-dissipating, asserting that a body would be arrested by the forces of air resistance and gravity which might be opposing its impetus.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-62)[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-Park-63)

### Quantity of motion

#### René Descartes

In _[Principles of Philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Principles\_of\_Philosophy "Principles of Philosophy")_ (_Principia Philosophiae_) from 1644, the French philosopher [René Descartes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Descartes "René Descartes") defined "quantity of motion" (_[Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin\_language "Latin language"): quantitas motus_) as the product of size and speed,[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-:0-64) and claimed that the total quantity of motion in the universe is conserved.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-:0-64)[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-65)

[![Image 84: Portrait of René Descartes](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b1/Frans_Hals_-_Portret_van_Ren%C3%A9_Descartes_%28cropped%292.jpg/250px-Frans_Hals_-_Portret_van_Ren%C3%A9_Descartes_%28cropped%292.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Frans_Hals_-_Portret_van_Ren%C3%A9_Descartes_(cropped)2.jpg)

René Descartes

(1596–1650)

> If x is twice the size of y, and is moving half as fast, then there's the same amount of motion in each.

> [God] created matter, along with its motion ... merely by letting things run their course, he preserves the same amount of motion ... as he put there in the beginning.

This should not be read as a statement of the modern law of [conservation of momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_momentum "Conservation of momentum"), since Descartes had no concept of mass as distinct from weight and size. (The concept of mass, as distinct from weight, was introduced by Newton in 1686.)[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-66) More important, he believed that it is speed rather than velocity that is conserved. So for Descartes, if a moving object were to bounce off a surface, changing its direction but not its speed, there would be no change in its quantity of motion.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-67)[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-68)[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-69)[Galileo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galileo "Galileo"), in his _[Two New Sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two\_New\_Sciences "Two New Sciences")_ (published in 1638), used the [Italian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Italian_language "Italian language") word _impeto_ to similarly describe Descartes's quantity of motion.

#### Christiaan Huygens

[![Image 85: Portrait of Christiaan Huygens](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Christiaan_Huygens-painting_%28cropped%29.jpeg/250px-Christiaan_Huygens-painting_%28cropped%29.jpeg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Christiaan_Huygens-painting_(cropped).jpeg)

Christiaan Huygens

(1629–1695)

In the 1600s, [Christiaan Huygens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens "Christiaan Huygens") concluded quite early that [Descartes's laws](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_laws_of_motion "Cartesian laws of motion") for the elastic collision of two bodies must be wrong, and he formulated the correct laws.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-70) An important step was his recognition of the [Galilean invariance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galilean_invariance "Galilean invariance") of the problems.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-71) His views then took many years to be circulated. He passed them on in person to [William Brouncker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/William_Brouncker,_2nd_Viscount_Brouncker "William Brouncker, 2nd Viscount Brouncker") and [Christopher Wren](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christopher_Wren "Christopher Wren") in London, in 1661.[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-72) What Spinoza wrote to [Henry Oldenburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henry_Oldenburg "Henry Oldenburg") about them, in 1666 during the [Second Anglo-Dutch War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Anglo-Dutch_War "Second Anglo-Dutch War"), was guarded.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-Israel2001-73) Huygens had actually worked them out in a manuscript _De motu corporum ex percussione_ in the period 1652–1656. The war ended in 1667, and Huygens announced his results to the Royal Society in 1668. He published them in the _[Journal des sçavans](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Journal\_des\_s%C3%A7avans "Journal des sçavans")_ in 1669.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-74)

### Momentum

#### John Wallis

In 1670, [John Wallis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Wallis "John Wallis"), in _Mechanica sive De Motu, Tractatus Geometricus_, stated the law of conservation of momentum: "the initial state of the body, either of rest or of motion, will persist" and "If the force is greater than the resistance, motion will result".[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-75) Wallis used _momentum_ for quantity of motion, and _vis_ for force.

#### Gottfried Leibniz

In 1686, [Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gottfried_Wilhelm_Leibniz "Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz"), in _[Discourse on Metaphysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discourse\_on\_Metaphysics "Discourse on Metaphysics")_, gave an argument against Descartes' construction of the conservation of the "quantity of motion" using an example of dropping blocks of different sizes different distances. He points out that force is conserved but quantity of motion, construed as the product of size and speed of an object, is not conserved.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-76)

#### Isaac Newton

[![Image 86: Portrait of Isaac Newton by James Thronill, after Sir Godfrey Kneller](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Isaac_Newton_by_James_Thronill%2C_after_Sir_Godfrey_Kneller.jpg/250px-Isaac_Newton_by_James_Thronill%2C_after_Sir_Godfrey_Kneller.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Isaac_Newton_by_James_Thronill,_after_Sir_Godfrey_Kneller.jpg)

Isaac Newton

(1642–1727)

In 1687, [Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton"), in _[Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophi%C3%A6\_Naturalis\_Principia\_Mathematica "Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica")_, just like Wallis, showed a similar casting around for words to use for the mathematical momentum. His Definition II defines _quantitas motus_, "quantity of motion", as "arising from the velocity and quantity of matter conjointly", which identifies it as momentum.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-77) Thus when in Law II he refers to _mutatio motus_, "change of motion", being proportional to the force impressed, he is generally taken to mean momentum and not motion.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-78)

#### John Jennings

In 1721, [John Jennings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Jennings_(tutor) "John Jennings (tutor)") published _Miscellanea_, where the momentum in its current mathematical sense is attested, five years before the final edition of Newton's _Principia Mathematica_. _Momentum_ M or "quantity of motion" was being defined for students as "a rectangle", the product of Q and V, where Q is "quantity of material" and V is "velocity", ⁠s/t⁠.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Momentum#cite_note-79)

In 1728, the [Cyclopedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclop%C3%A6dia,_or_an_Universal_Dictionary_of_Arts_and_Sciences "Cyclopædia, or an Universal Dictionary of Arts and Sciences") states:

> The _Momentum_, _Impetus_, or Quantity of Motion of any Body, is the _Factum_ [i.e., product] of its Velocity, (or the Space it moves in a given Time, see Motion) multiplied into its Mass.
