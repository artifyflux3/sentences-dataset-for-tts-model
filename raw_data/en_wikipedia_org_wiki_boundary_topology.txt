A set (in light blue) and its boundary (in dark blue).

In [topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") and [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") in general, the **boundary** of a subset S of a [topological space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space")X is the set of points in the [closure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closure_(topology) "Closure (topology)") of S not belonging to the [interior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interior_(topology) "Interior (topology)") of S. An element of the boundary of S is called a **boundary point** of S. The term **boundary operation** refers to finding or taking the boundary of a set. Notations used for boundary of a set S include ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \operatorname {bd} (S),\operatorname {fr} (S),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/76f1bb953501c11dfda19f46552eb4d7866c4278) and ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \partial S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c609f4d3c5692ea4495479ef47594dc67f9fa464).

Some authors (for example Willard, in _General Topology_) use the term **frontier** instead of boundary in an attempt to avoid confusion with a [different definition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold#Manifold_with_boundary "Manifold") used in [algebraic topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_topology "Algebraic topology") and the theory of [manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold"). Despite widespread acceptance of the meaning of the terms boundary and frontier, they have sometimes been used to refer to other sets. For example, _Metric Spaces_ by E. T. Copson uses the term boundary to refer to [Hausdorff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Felix_Hausdorff "Felix Hausdorff")'s **border**, which is defined as the intersection of a set with its boundary.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boundary_(topology)#cite_note-1) Hausdorff also introduced the term **residue**, which is defined as the intersection of a set with the closure of the border of its complement.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boundary_(topology)#cite_note-2)

There are several equivalent definitions for the **boundary** of a subset ![Image 4: {\displaystyle S\subseteq X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/44aba72977e43f863dd873b095d1dc0bd3f17608) of a topological space ![Image 5: {\displaystyle X,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/09ba32eeb405f7f5f2bac1eb12987c47d2fd42df) which will be denoted by ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \partial _{X}S,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/aa2db3e8cdf3a648cc99f73342e5ae01470cd57a)![Image 7: {\displaystyle \operatorname {Bd} _{X}S,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e5eccd0554a4d31a863bce2649ed130eb09b0db6) or simply ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \partial S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c609f4d3c5692ea4495479ef47594dc67f9fa464) if ![Image 9: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is understood:

1.   It is the [closure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closure_(topology) "Closure (topology)") of ![Image 10: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2)[minus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_subtraction "Set subtraction") the [interior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interior_(topology) "Interior (topology)") of ![Image 11: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) in ![Image 12: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab): ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \partial S~:=~{\overline {S}}\setminus \operatorname {int} _{X}S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e04975f01f6af8ba517d1fa3717234b22fb73bf7) where ![Image 14: {\displaystyle {\overline {S}}=\operatorname {cl} _{X}S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f4fb6eede8f3b0bac4104dc88a46db2b6561b2bc) denotes the [closure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closure_(topology) "Closure (topology)") of ![Image 15: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) in ![Image 16: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \operatorname {int} _{X}S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9cb5e3f386aa669431efe627876ebbeac4f8868d) denotes the [topological interior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interior_(topology) "Interior (topology)") of ![Image 18: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) in ![Image 19: {\displaystyle X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ba76c5a460c4a0bb1639a193bc1830f0a773e03)
2.   It is the intersection of the closure of ![Image 20: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) with the closure of its [complement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complement_(set_theory) "Complement (set theory)"): ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \partial S~:=~{\overline {S}}\cap {\overline {(X\setminus S)}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dfea1420454ae5c6dc7bde30f1e518083e9ce853)
3.   It is the set of points ![Image 22: {\displaystyle p\in X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fae5d7ae3f7710fc989d0bd6dca038d74107f16d) such that every [neighborhood](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neighborhood_(topology) "Neighborhood (topology)") of ![Image 23: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) contains at least one point of ![Image 24: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) and at least one point not of ![Image 25: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2): ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \partial S~:=~\{p\in X:{\text{ for every neighborhood }}O{\text{ of }}p,\ O\cap S\neq \varnothing \,{\text{ and }}\,O\cap (X\setminus S)\neq \varnothing \}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/680b2f9fbfdc66f4fb012d7b92a9787df51aace6)
4.   It is all points in ![Image 27: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) which are not in either the interior or [exterior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interior_(topology)#Exterior_of_a_set "Interior (topology)") of ![Image 28: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2): ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \partial S~:=~X\setminus \left(\operatorname {int} _{X}S\cup \operatorname {ext} _{X}S\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4372c6a41201b25de29be13a28c76d5f88e64987) where ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \operatorname {int} _{X}S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9cb5e3f386aa669431efe627876ebbeac4f8868d) denotes the interior of ![Image 31: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) in ![Image 32: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \operatorname {ext} _{X}S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed7f7012b2c1b3f89537eb7dadd9b8de69034fc7) denotes the exterior of ![Image 34: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) in ![Image 35: {\displaystyle X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ba76c5a460c4a0bb1639a193bc1830f0a773e03)

A **boundary point** of a set is any element of that set's boundary. The boundary ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \partial _{X}S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ed60827917abec164aa44fe529d368421a106a9) defined above is sometimes called the set's **topological boundary** to distinguish it from other similarly named notions such as [the boundary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boundary_of_a_manifold "Boundary of a manifold") of a [manifold with boundary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold_with_boundary "Manifold with boundary") or the boundary of a [manifold with corners](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold_with_corners "Manifold with corners"), to name just a few examples.

A [connected component](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Connected_space#Formal_definition "Connected space") of the boundary of S is called a **boundary component** of S.

The closure of a set ![Image 37: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) equals the union of the set with its boundary: ![Image 38: {\displaystyle {\overline {S}}=S\cup \partial _{X}S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6b71dc6f05f59e77a4f89f7c962c68f02098a85a) where ![Image 39: {\displaystyle {\overline {S}}=\operatorname {cl} _{X}S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f4fb6eede8f3b0bac4104dc88a46db2b6561b2bc) denotes the [closure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closure_(topology) "Closure (topology)") of ![Image 40: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) in ![Image 41: {\displaystyle X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ba76c5a460c4a0bb1639a193bc1830f0a773e03) A set is closed if and only if it contains its boundary, and open if and only if it is disjoint from its boundary. The boundary of a set is [closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_set "Closed set");[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boundary_(topology)#cite_note-3) this follows from the formula ![Image 42: {\displaystyle \partial _{X}S~:=~{\overline {S}}\cap {\overline {(X\setminus S)}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02e4b40d5c9184e5e5666d9f7c2873a033f57df5) which expresses ![Image 43: {\displaystyle \partial _{X}S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ed60827917abec164aa44fe529d368421a106a9) as the intersection of two closed subsets of ![Image 44: {\displaystyle X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ba76c5a460c4a0bb1639a193bc1830f0a773e03)

("Trichotomy") Given any subset ![Image 45: {\displaystyle S\subseteq X,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bcee64dbb80f9474630b849a15567dc2acd95608) each point of ![Image 46: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) lies in exactly one of the three sets ![Image 47: {\displaystyle \operatorname {int} _{X}S,\partial _{X}S,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3ac46d75e8ea7da6c79d28aa5d1e4f482351da2c) and ![Image 48: {\displaystyle \operatorname {int} _{X}(X\setminus S).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/49ef010cb0f7d5729dc7916af6f07298b18cc072) Said differently, ![Image 49: {\displaystyle X~=~\left(\operatorname {int} _{X}S\right)\;\cup \;\left(\partial _{X}S\right)\;\cup \;\left(\operatorname {int} _{X}(X\setminus S)\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/564da4fe9a0be08abbfe282ff13ff633eb3adbcf) and these three sets are [pairwise disjoint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pairwise_disjoint "Pairwise disjoint"). Consequently, if these set are not empty[[note 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boundary_(topology)#cite_note-4) then they form a [partition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partition_of_a_set "Partition of a set") of ![Image 50: {\displaystyle X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ba76c5a460c4a0bb1639a193bc1830f0a773e03)

A point ![Image 51: {\displaystyle p\in X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fae5d7ae3f7710fc989d0bd6dca038d74107f16d) is a boundary point of a set if and only if every neighborhood of ![Image 52: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) contains at least one point in the set and at least one point not in the set. The boundary of the interior of a set as well as the boundary of the closure of a set are both contained in the boundary of the set.

Every point of ![Image 62: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) is either an interior point or a boundary point. Also, every point of ![Image 63: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) is either an accumulation point or an isolated point. Likewise, every boundary point of ![Image 64: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest\_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) is either an accumulation point or an isolated point. Isolated points are always boundary points._

### Characterizations and general examples

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Boundary_(topology)&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Characterizations and general examples")]

A set and its complement have the same boundary: ![Image 65: {\displaystyle \partial _{X}S=\partial _{X}(X\setminus S).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/628741399fe902ed9da0cfb3cb4f8d9f14c2474c)

A set ![Image 66: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) is a [dense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dense_subset "Dense subset")[open](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_set "Open set") subset of ![Image 67: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) if and only if ![Image 68: {\displaystyle \partial _{X}U=X\setminus U.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a12bc16ed0b96f4c968314ef5e94e716c00df4c3)

The interior of the boundary of a closed set is empty.[[proof 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boundary_(topology)#cite_note-5) Consequently, the interior of the boundary of the closure of a set is empty. The interior of the boundary of an open set is also empty.[[proof 2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boundary_(topology)#cite_note-6) Consequently, the interior of the boundary of the interior of a set is empty. In particular, if ![Image 69: {\displaystyle S\subseteq X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/44aba72977e43f863dd873b095d1dc0bd3f17608) is a closed or open subset of ![Image 70: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) then there does not exist any nonempty subset ![Image 71: {\displaystyle U\subseteq \partial _{X}S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dab37911fe6a305fd7566e679e9ffd4f7ab0f2d7) such that ![Image 72: {\displaystyle U}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/458a728f53b9a0274f059cd695e067c430956025) is open in ![Image 73: {\displaystyle X.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5ba76c5a460c4a0bb1639a193bc1830f0a773e03) This fact is important for the definition and use of [nowhere dense subsets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nowhere_dense_set "Nowhere dense set"), [meager subsets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meager_set "Meager set"), and [Baire spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baire_space "Baire space").

A set is the boundary of some open set if and only if it is closed and [nowhere dense](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nowhere_dense_set "Nowhere dense set"). The boundary of a set is empty if and only if the set is both closed and open (that is, a [clopen set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clopen_set "Clopen set")).

[![Image 74](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3c/Mandelbrot_Components.svg/250px-Mandelbrot_Components.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Mandelbrot_Components.svg)

Boundary of hyperbolic components of [Mandelbrot set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandelbrot_set "Mandelbrot set")

Consider the real line ![Image 75: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) with the usual topology (that is, the topology whose [basis sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Basis_(topology) "Basis (topology)") are [open intervals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Open_interval "Open interval")) and ![Image 76: {\displaystyle \mathbb {Q} ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91185244fbdded6ea99a5e9e6603299128b10928) the subset of rational numbers (whose [topological interior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interior_(topology) "Interior (topology)") in ![Image 77: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc) is empty). Then

*   ![Image 78: {\displaystyle \partial (0,5)=\partial [0,5)=\partial (0,5]=\partial [0,5]=\{0,5\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/84d5dd08b8ce4a5959c14f806036b74dda17723a)
*   ![Image 79: {\displaystyle \partial \varnothing =\varnothing }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/18ccccfb5e40982b150f06c236bdc9de97bfe0c3)
*   ![Image 80: {\displaystyle \partial \mathbb {Q} =\mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52fd1f017f66356f1ce9924ca6c6b8d5501c568b)
*   ![Image 81: {\displaystyle \partial (\mathbb {Q} \cap [0,1])=[0,1]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/992baab3b186c92f1b758497f0437ae600af6c5c)

These last two examples illustrate the fact that the boundary of a [dense set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dense_set "Dense set") with empty interior is its closure. They also show that it is possible for the boundary ![Image 82: {\displaystyle \partial S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c609f4d3c5692ea4495479ef47594dc67f9fa464) of a subset ![Image 83: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) to contain a non-empty open subset of ![Image 84: {\displaystyle X:=\mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/90d9c278ab99543cfeec30ab45f03df268799dd1); that is, for the interior of ![Image 85: {\displaystyle \partial S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c609f4d3c5692ea4495479ef47594dc67f9fa464) in ![Image 86: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) to be non-empty. However, a _closed_ subset's boundary always has an empty interior.

In the space of rational numbers with the usual topology (the [subspace topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subspace_topology "Subspace topology") of ![Image 87: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc)), the boundary of ![Image 88: {\displaystyle (-\infty ,a),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b780a595a8ef307e01b813a44d8c57bfbc82d98) where ![Image 89: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) is irrational, is empty.

The boundary of a set is a [topological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") notion and may change if one changes the topology. For example, given the usual topology on ![Image 90: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d349b099a2e00103b347c5f640a30e0af2a6ee18) the boundary of a closed disk ![Image 91: {\displaystyle \Omega =\left\{(x,y):x^{2}+y^{2}\leq 1\right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23efe11a99614155c12c66839b123734644792de) is the disk's surrounding circle: ![Image 92: {\displaystyle \partial \Omega =\left\{(x,y):x^{2}+y^{2}=1\right\}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/79baac61b24149150345317362971b37f00fa4b7) If the disk is viewed as a set in ![Image 93: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f936ddf584f8f3dd2a0ed08917001b7a404c10b5) with its own usual topology, that is, ![Image 94: {\displaystyle \Omega =\left\{(x,y,0):x^{2}+y^{2}\leq 1\right\},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d641bda2627f6be1fed8ba05b9ee59a5b696ee6b) then the boundary of the disk is the disk itself: ![Image 95: {\displaystyle \partial \Omega =\Omega .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/678b979e8d8e57b31fcffd177ea77eff2c5f6a42) If the disk is viewed as its own topological space (with the subspace topology of ![Image 96: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e150115ab9f63023215109595b76686a1ff890fd)), then the boundary of the disk is empty.

Boundary of a boundary
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Boundary_(topology)&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Boundary of a boundary")]

For any set ![Image 166: {\displaystyle S,\partial S\supseteq \partial \partial S,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5e881f0f0ab7405497b69d67898f05fa1728ef0a) where [![Image 167: {\displaystyle \,\supseteq \,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d6b0a683c276de4f94c4cc36590ecdefd9d56e2b)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_mathematical_symbols_by_subject#Set_relations "List of mathematical symbols by subject") denotes the [superset](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Superset "Superset") with equality holding if and only if the boundary of ![Image 168: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) has no interior points, which will be the case for example if ![Image 169: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) is either closed or open. Since the boundary of a set is closed, ![Image 170: {\displaystyle \partial \partial S=\partial \partial \partial S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c0bdfddcefd8a00692ddaef33fcecc80b04d3e00) for any set ![Image 171: {\displaystyle S.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/23bbb1f0f6ebdfa78b4fed06049640f7386bb44b) The boundary operator thus satisfies a weakened kind of [idempotence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Idempotence "Idempotence").

In discussing boundaries of [manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold "Manifold") or [simplexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex "Simplex") and their [simplicial complexes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplicial_complex "Simplicial complex"), one often meets the assertion that the boundary of the boundary is always empty. Indeed, the construction of the [singular homology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Singular_homology "Singular homology") rests critically on this fact. The explanation for the apparent incongruity is that the topological boundary (the subject of this article) is a slightly different concept from the boundary of a manifold or of a simplicial complex. For example, the boundary of an open disk viewed as a manifold is empty, as is its topological boundary viewed as a subset of itself, while its topological boundary viewed as a subset of the real plane is the circle surrounding the disk. Conversely, the boundary of a closed disk viewed as a manifold is the bounding circle, as is its topological boundary viewed as a subset of the real plane, while its topological boundary viewed as a subset of itself is empty. In particular, the topological boundary depends on the ambient space, while the boundary of a manifold is invariant.
