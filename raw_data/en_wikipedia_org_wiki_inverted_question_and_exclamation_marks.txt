The **upside-down** (also **inverted**, **turned** or **rotated**) **question mark**¿ and **exclamation mark**¡ are [punctuation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punctuation "Punctuation") marks used to begin interrogative and exclamatory sentences or clauses in [Spanish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_language "Spanish language") and some languages that have cultural ties with Spain, such as [Asturian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asturian_language "Asturian language") and [Waray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waray_language "Waray language").[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverted_question_and_exclamation_marks#cite_note-1) The initial marks are mirrored at the end of the sentence or clause by the ordinary [question mark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Question_mark "Question mark"), ?, or [exclamation mark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exclamation_mark "Exclamation mark"), !.

Upside-down marks are supported by various standards, including [Unicode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode "Unicode"), and [HTML](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SGML_entity "SGML entity"). They can be entered directly on keyboards designed for Spanish-speaking countries.

Punctuation marks in Spanish, showing their positions relative to the [baseline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baseline_(typography) "Baseline (typography)")

The upside-down question mark ¿ is written before the first letter of an interrogative sentence or clause to indicate that a question follows. It is a rotated form of the standard symbol "?" recognized by speakers of other languages written with the [Latin script](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_script "Latin script"). A regular question mark is written at the end of the sentence or clause.

Upside-down punctuation is important in [Spanish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_language "Spanish language") since the syntax of the language means that both statements and questions or exclamations could have the same wording.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverted_question_and_exclamation_marks#cite_note-Rosetta-2) "Do you like summer?" and "You like summer." are translated respectively as _"¿Te gusta el verano?"_ and _"Te gusta el verano."_ (There is not always a difference between the wording of a [yes–no question](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yes%E2%80%93no_question "Yes–no question") and the corresponding statement in Spanish.)

In sentences that are both declarative and interrogative, the clause that asks a question is isolated with the starting-symbol upside-down question mark, for example: _"Si no puedes ir con ellos, ¿quieres ir con nosotros?"_ ("If you cannot go with them, would you like to go with us?"), not *_"¿Si no puedes ir con ellos, quieres ir con nosotros?"_ This helps to recognize questions and exclamations in long sentences.

Unlike the ending marks, which are printed along the [baseline](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baseline_(typography) "Baseline (typography)") of the text, the upside-down marks (¿ and ¡) [descend below the line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Descender "Descender").

Upside-down marks, simple in the era of hand typesetting, were originally recommended by the _[Real Academia Española](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal\_Spanish\_Academy "Royal Spanish Academy")_ (Royal Spanish Academy), in the second edition of the _Ortografía de la lengua castellana_ (Orthography of the Castilian language) in 1754[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverted_question_and_exclamation_marks#cite_note-3) recommending it as the symbol indicating the beginning of a question in written Spanish—e.g. _"¿Cuántos años tienes?"_ ("How old are you?"; lit.'How many years do you have?'). The Real Academia also ordered the same upside-down-symbol system for statements of exclamation, using the symbols "¡" and "!".

These new rules were slow to be adopted: there are 19th-century books in which the printer uses neither "¡" nor "¿".[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverted_question_and_exclamation_marks#cite_note-4)

Outside of the Spanish-speaking world, [John Wilkins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Wilkins "John Wilkins") proposed using the upside-down exclamation mark "¡" as a symbol at the end of a sentence to [denote irony](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Irony_punctuation "Irony punctuation") in 1668. He was one of many, including [Desiderius Erasmus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desiderius_Erasmus "Desiderius Erasmus"), who felt there was a need for such a punctuation mark, but Wilkins' proposal, like the other attempts, failed to take hold.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverted_question_and_exclamation_marks#cite_note-Houston2013-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverted_question_and_exclamation_marks#cite_note-6)

Some writers omit the upside-down question mark in the case of a short unambiguous question such as: _"Quién viene?"_ ("Who comes?"). This is the criterion in [Galician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galician_language "Galician language")[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverted_question_and_exclamation_marks#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverted_question_and_exclamation_marks#cite_note-8) and formerly in [Catalan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Catalan_language "Catalan language").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverted_question_and_exclamation_marks#cite_note-9) Certain Catalan-language authorities, such as [Joan Solà i Cortassa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joan_Sol%C3%A0_i_Cortassa "Joan Solà i Cortassa"), insist that both the opening and closing question marks be used for clarity.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverted_question_and_exclamation_marks#cite_note-Houston2013-5) The current [Institute for Catalan Studies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Institute_for_Catalan_Studies "Institute for Catalan Studies") prescription is never to use the upside-down marks for Catalan.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverted_question_and_exclamation_marks#cite_note-10)

Some Spanish-language writers, among them Nobel laureate [Pablo Neruda](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pablo_Neruda "Pablo Neruda") (1904–1973), refuse to use the upside-down question mark.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverted_question_and_exclamation_marks#cite_note-11)

Upside-down marks are often omitted when [texting](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Texting "Texting").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

It is acceptable in Spanish to begin a sentence with an opening upside-down [exclamation mark](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exclamation_mark "Exclamation mark") ("¡") and end it with a question mark ("?"), or vice versa, for statements that are questions but also have a clear sense of exclamation or surprise such as: _¡Y tú quién te crees?_ ("And who do you think you are?!"). Normally, four signs are used, always with one type in the outer side and the other in the inner side (nested) (_\_¿¡Y tú quién te crees!?\__, _\_¡¿Y tú quién te crees?!\__)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inverted_question_and_exclamation_marks#cite_note-12)

[Unicode 5.1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unicode#Versions "Unicode") also includes U+2E18⸘INVERTED INTERROBANG, which is an [upside-down version of the interrobang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interrobang#Inverted_interrobang "Interrobang"), a nonstandard punctuation mark used to denote both excitement and a question in one glyph. It is also known as a "gnaborretni" () ([interrobang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interrobang "Interrobang") spelled backwards).
