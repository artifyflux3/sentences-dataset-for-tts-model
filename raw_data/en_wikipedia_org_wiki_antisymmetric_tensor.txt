From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Tensor equal to the negative of any of its transpositions

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") and [theoretical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoretical_physics "Theoretical physics"), a [tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor") is **antisymmetric** or **alternating on** (or **with respect to**) **an index subset** if it alternates [sign](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sign_(mathematics) "Sign (mathematics)") (+/−) when any two indices of the subset are interchanged.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antisymmetric_tensor#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antisymmetric_tensor#cite_note-2) The index subset must generally either be all _covariant_ or all _contravariant_.

For example, T i j k…=−T j i k…=T j k i…=−T k j i…=T k i j…=−T i k j…{\displaystyle T_{ijk\dots }=-T_{jik\dots }=T_{jki\dots }=-T_{kji\dots }=T_{kij\dots }=-T_{ikj\dots }}![Image 24: {\displaystyle T_{ijk\dots }=-T_{jik\dots }=T_{jki\dots }=-T_{kji\dots }=T_{kij\dots }=-T_{ikj\dots }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a81f2ef2945250357e7b5d0745776ffe51c9e6b4) holds when the tensor is antisymmetric with respect to its first three indices.

If a tensor changes sign under exchange of _each_ pair of its indices, then the tensor is **completely** (or **totally**) **antisymmetric**. A completely antisymmetric covariant [tensor field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_field "Tensor field") of [order](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_order "Tensor order")k{\displaystyle k}![Image 25: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) may be referred to as a [differential k{\displaystyle k}![Image 26: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form"), and a completely antisymmetric contravariant tensor field may be referred to as a [k{\displaystyle k}![Image 27: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multivector "Multivector") field.

Antisymmetric and symmetric tensors
-----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Antisymmetric_tensor&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Antisymmetric and symmetric tensors")]

A tensor **A** that is antisymmetric on indices i{\displaystyle i}![Image 28: {\displaystyle i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/add78d8608ad86e54951b8c8bd6c8d8416533d20) and j{\displaystyle j}![Image 29: {\displaystyle j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f461e54f5c093e92a55547b9764291390f0b5d0) has the property that the [contraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_contraction "Tensor contraction") with a tensor **B** that is symmetric on indices i{\displaystyle i}![Image 30: {\displaystyle i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/add78d8608ad86e54951b8c8bd6c8d8416533d20) and j{\displaystyle j}![Image 31: {\displaystyle j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f461e54f5c093e92a55547b9764291390f0b5d0) is identically 0.

For a general tensor **U** with components U i j k…{\displaystyle U_{ijk\dots }}![Image 32: {\displaystyle U_{ijk\dots }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dd646d85b85791c9a0e297989cc10799b9d838da) and a pair of indices i{\displaystyle i}![Image 33: {\displaystyle i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/add78d8608ad86e54951b8c8bd6c8d8416533d20) and j,{\displaystyle j,}![Image 34: {\displaystyle j,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bcff3a7481f4597bfd961687562f495e266d2b32)**U** has symmetric and antisymmetric parts defined as:

U(i j)k…=1 2(U i j k…+U j i k…){\displaystyle U_{(ij)k\dots }={\frac {1}{2}}(U_{ijk\dots }+U_{jik\dots })}![Image 35: {\displaystyle U_{(ij)k\dots }={\frac {1}{2}}(U_{ijk\dots }+U_{jik\dots })}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4379276f7d89121b2870ab6faffbaabf901c37b9)(symmetric part)
U[i j]k…=1 2(U i j k…−U j i k…){\displaystyle U_{[ij]k\dots }={\frac {1}{2}}(U_{ijk\dots }-U_{jik\dots })}![Image 36: {\displaystyle U_{[ij]k\dots }={\frac {1}{2}}(U_{ijk\dots }-U_{jik\dots })}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e914bcff0ea5c2b16463d4b9ed87b781389e2d09)(antisymmetric part).

Similar definitions can be given for other pairs of indices. As the term "part" suggests, a tensor is the sum of its symmetric part and antisymmetric part for a given pair of indices, as in U i j k…=U(i j)k…+U[i j]k….{\displaystyle U_{ijk\dots }=U_{(ij)k\dots }+U_{[ij]k\dots }.}![Image 37: {\displaystyle U_{ijk\dots }=U_{(ij)k\dots }+U_{[ij]k\dots }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/532f585a7761c33f6c9975c29e75d3b1ac9259c5)

Notation
--------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Antisymmetric_tensor&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Notation")]

A shorthand notation for anti-symmetrization is denoted by a pair of square brackets. For example, in arbitrary dimensions, for an order 2 covariant tensor **M**, M[a b]=1 2!(M a b−M b a),{\displaystyle M_{[ab]}={\frac {1}{2!}}(M_{ab}-M_{ba}),}![Image 38: {\displaystyle M_{[ab]}={\frac {1}{2!}}(M_{ab}-M_{ba}),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22c0ae6c35816412ce4f3d29478b6a49022ce281) and for an order 3 covariant tensor **T**, T[a b c]=1 3!(T a b c−T a c b+T b c a−T b a c+T c a b−T c b a).{\displaystyle T_{[abc]}={\frac {1}{3!}}(T_{abc}-T_{acb}+T_{bca}-T_{bac}+T_{cab}-T_{cba}).}![Image 39: {\displaystyle T_{[abc]}={\frac {1}{3!}}(T_{abc}-T_{acb}+T_{bca}-T_{bac}+T_{cab}-T_{cba}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/391d99b9fe448b8556303a32b0a9fa0bb17a2068)

In any 2 and 3 dimensions, these can be written as M[a b]=1 2!δ a b c d M c d,T[a b c]=1 3!δ a b c d e f T d e f.{\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}M_{[ab]}&={\frac {1}{2!}}\,\delta _{ab}^{cd}M_{cd},\\[2pt]T_{[abc]}&={\frac {1}{3!}}\,\delta _{abc}^{def}T_{def}.\end{aligned}}}![Image 40: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}M_{[ab]}&={\frac {1}{2!}}\,\delta _{ab}^{cd}M_{cd},\\[2pt]T_{[abc]}&={\frac {1}{3!}}\,\delta _{abc}^{def}T_{def}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1c7fbe1a24bd64c4f5674df95e0098e654d9a63d) where δ a b…c d…{\displaystyle \delta _{ab\dots }^{cd\dots }}![Image 41: {\displaystyle \delta _{ab\dots }^{cd\dots }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/08d249a06c02f2dcca9bee834442a49b340b5173) is the [generalized Kronecker delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_Kronecker_delta "Generalized Kronecker delta"), and the [Einstein summation convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_summation_convention "Einstein summation convention") is in use.

More generally, irrespective of the number of dimensions, antisymmetrization over p{\displaystyle p}![Image 42: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) indices may be expressed as T[a 1…a p]=1 p!δ a 1…a p b 1…b p T b 1…b p.{\displaystyle T_{[a_{1}\dots a_{p}]}={\frac {1}{p!}}\delta _{a_{1}\dots a_{p}}^{b_{1}\dots b_{p}}T_{b_{1}\dots b_{p}}.}![Image 43: {\displaystyle T_{[a_{1}\dots a_{p}]}={\frac {1}{p!}}\delta _{a_{1}\dots a_{p}}^{b_{1}\dots b_{p}}T_{b_{1}\dots b_{p}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3fa91d7d51fa72cf48c44b05a5af10098e037b44)

In general, every tensor of rank 2 can be decomposed into a symmetric and anti-symmetric pair as: T i j=1 2(T i j+T j i)+1 2(T i j−T j i).{\displaystyle T_{ij}={\frac {1}{2}}(T_{ij}+T_{ji})+{\frac {1}{2}}(T_{ij}-T_{ji}).}![Image 44: {\displaystyle T_{ij}={\frac {1}{2}}(T_{ij}+T_{ji})+{\frac {1}{2}}(T_{ij}-T_{ji}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed403bbed871173babfd5c42599ddddc0c0836de)

This decomposition is not in general true for tensors of rank 3 or more, which have more complex symmetries.

Examples
--------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Antisymmetric_tensor&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Examples")]

Totally antisymmetric tensors include:

*   Trivially, all scalars and vectors (tensors of order 0 and 1) are totally antisymmetric (as well as being totally symmetric).
*   The [electromagnetic tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_tensor "Electromagnetic tensor"), F μ ν{\displaystyle F_{\mu \nu }}![Image 45: {\displaystyle F_{\mu \nu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f24e4d8457e87f45e16137c04eae9d3343843caa) in [electromagnetism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetism "Electromagnetism").
*   The [Riemannian volume form](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_volume_form "Riemannian volume form") on a [pseudo-Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudo-Riemannian_manifold "Pseudo-Riemannian manifold").
