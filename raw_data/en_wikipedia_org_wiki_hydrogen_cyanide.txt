**Hydrogen cyanide** (formerly known as **prussic acid**) is a [chemical compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_compound "Chemical compound") with the [formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_formula "Chemical formula") HCN and [structural formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structural_formula "Structural formula")H−C≡N. It is a highly [toxic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Toxic "Toxic") and [flammable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flammable "Flammable") liquid that [boils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Boiling "Boiling") slightly above [room temperature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Room_temperature "Room temperature"), at 25.6°C (78.1°F). HCN is produced on an industrial scale and is a highly valued [precursor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precursor_(chemistry) "Precursor (chemistry)") to many chemical compounds ranging from [polymers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polymers "Polymers") to pharmaceuticals. Large-scale applications are for the production of [potassium cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide "Potassium cyanide") and [adiponitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adiponitrile "Adiponitrile"), used in mining and plastics, respectively.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-Ullmann-10) It is more toxic than solid cyanide compounds due to its [volatile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volatility_(chemistry) "Volatility (chemistry)") nature. A solution of hydrogen cyanide in [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Water_(molecule) "Water (molecule)"), represented as HCN([aq](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aqueous "Aqueous")), is called _hydrocyanic acid_. The [salts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt_(chemistry) "Salt (chemistry)") of the cyanide anion are known as [cyanides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanide "Cyanide").

Whether hydrogen cyanide is an [organic compound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_compound "Organic compound") or not is a topic of debate among chemists. It is traditionally considered [inorganic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inorganic_compound "Inorganic compound"), but can also be considered a [nitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrile "Nitrile"),[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-11) giving rise to its alternative names of methanenitrile and formonitrile.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-pubchem-2)

Structure and general properties
--------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_cyanide&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Structure and general properties")]

Hydrogen cyanide is a [linear molecule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linear_molecular_geometry "Linear molecular geometry"), with a [triple bond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Triple_bond "Triple bond") between [carbon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon "Carbon") and [nitrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrogen "Nitrogen").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-12) The [isomer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomer "Isomer") of HCN is HNC, [hydrogen isocyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_isocyanide "Hydrogen isocyanide").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-13)

About half of people are unable to [detect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olfactory_system "Olfactory system") the odor of hydrogen cyanide owing to a recessive [genetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene "Gene")[trait](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trait_(biological) "Trait (biological)").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-14) For those that can detect it, the odor has been described as almond-like.

Chemical properties
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_cyanide&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Chemical properties")]

Hydrogen cyanide is weakly [acidic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acidic "Acidic") with a [p _K_ a](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acid_dissociation_constant "Acid dissociation constant") of 9.2. It partially [ionizes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ionization "Ionization") in [water](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aqueous_solution "Aqueous solution") to give the [cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanide "Cyanide") anion, CN−. HCN forms hydrogen bonds with its conjugate base, species such as (CN−)(HCN)n.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-15)

Hydrogen cyanide reacts with [alkenes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkene "Alkene") to give nitriles. The conversion, which is called [hydrocyanation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocyanation "Hydrocyanation"), employs nickel complexes as catalysts.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-16)

RCH=CH 2 + HCN → RCH 2−CH 2 CN
Four molecules of HCN will tetramerize into [diaminomaleonitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diaminomaleonitrile "Diaminomaleonitrile").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-17)

[Metal cyanides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metal_cyanide "Metal cyanide") are typically prepared by [salt metathesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salt_metathesis "Salt metathesis") from alkali metal cyanide salts, but [mercuric cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mercuric_cyanide "Mercuric cyanide") is formed from aqueous hydrogen cyanide:[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-18)

HgO + 2 HCN → Hg(CN)2 + H 2 O

History of discovery and naming
-------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_cyanide&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: History of discovery and naming")]

Hydrogen cyanide was first isolated in 1752 by French chemist [Pierre Macquer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pierre_Macquer "Pierre Macquer") who converted [Prussian blue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prussian_blue "Prussian blue") to an [iron oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_oxide "Iron oxide") plus a volatile component and found that these could be used to reconstitute it.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-19) The new component was what is now known as hydrogen cyanide. It was subsequently prepared from Prussian blue by the Swedish chemist [Carl Wilhelm Scheele](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Scheele "Carl Wilhelm Scheele") in 1782,[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-20) and was eventually given the German name _Blausäure_ (_lit_. "Blue acid") because of its acidic nature in water and its derivation from Prussian blue. In English, it became known popularly as _prussic acid._

In 1787, the French chemist [Claude Louis Berthollet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Claude_Louis_Berthollet "Claude Louis Berthollet") showed that prussic acid did not contain oxygen,[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-21) an important contribution to acid theory, which had hitherto postulated that acids must contain oxygen[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-22) (hence the name of [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") itself, which is derived from Greek elements that mean "acid-former" and are likewise [calqued](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calque "Calque") into German as _Sauerstoff_).

In 1811, [Joseph Louis Gay-Lussac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Louis_Gay-Lussac "Joseph Louis Gay-Lussac") prepared pure, liquified hydrogen cyanide,[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-23) and in 1815 he deduced Prussic acid's chemical formula.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-24)

The word _cyanide_ for the radical in hydrogen cyanide was derived from its French equivalent, _cyanure_, which Gay-Lussac constructed from the Ancient Greek word _κύανος_ for dark blue enamel or [lapis lazuli](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lapis_lazuli "Lapis lazuli"), again owing to the chemical’s derivation from Prussian blue. Incidentally, the Greek word is also the root of the English color name _[cyan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyan "Cyan")_.

Production and synthesis
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_cyanide&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Production and synthesis")]

The most important process is the [Andrussow oxidation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Andrussow_oxidation "Andrussow oxidation") invented by [Leonid Andrussow](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leonid_Andrussow "Leonid Andrussow") at [IG Farben](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/IG_Farben "IG Farben") in which [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane") and [ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia") react in the presence of [oxygen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxygen "Oxygen") at about 1,200°C (2,190°F) over a [platinum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platinum "Platinum") catalyst:[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-25)

2 CH 4 + 2 NH 3 + 3 O 2 → 2 HCN + 6 H 2 O
In 2006, between 500 million and 1 billion pounds (between 230,000 and 450,000 t) were produced in the US.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-26) Hydrogen cyanide is produced in large quantities by several processes and is a recovered waste product from the manufacture of [acrylonitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acrylonitrile "Acrylonitrile").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-Ullmann-10)

Of lesser importance is the [Degussa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degussa "Degussa") process ([BMA process](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BMA_process "BMA process")) in which no oxygen is added and the energy must be transferred indirectly through the reactor wall:[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-27)

CH 4 + NH 3 → HCN + 3H 2
This reaction is akin to [steam reforming](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steam_reforming "Steam reforming"), the reaction of [methane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methane "Methane") and water to give [carbon monoxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_monoxide "Carbon monoxide") and [hydrogen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen "Hydrogen").

In the Shawinigan Process, [hydrocarbons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocarbons "Hydrocarbons"), e.g. [propane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Propane "Propane"), are reacted with ammonia.

In the laboratory, small amounts of HCN are produced by the addition of acids to cyanide salts of [alkali metals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alkali_metals "Alkali metals"):

H+ + CN− → HCN
This reaction is sometimes the basis of accidental poisonings because the acid converts a nonvolatile cyanide salt into the gaseous HCN.

Hydrogen cyanide could be obtained from [potassium ferricyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_ferricyanide "Potassium ferricyanide") and acid:

6 H+ + [Fe(CN)6]−3 → 6 HCN + Fe+3[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-28)[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-29)

### Historical methods of production

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_cyanide&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Historical methods of production")]

The large demand for cyanides for mining operations in the 1890s was met by [George Thomas Beilby](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_Thomas_Beilby "George Thomas Beilby"), who patented a method to produce hydrogen cyanide by passing [ammonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ammonia "Ammonia") over glowing [coal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coal "Coal") in 1892. This method was used until [Hamilton Castner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamilton_Castner "Hamilton Castner") in 1894 developed a synthesis starting from coal, ammonia, and [sodium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium "Sodium") yielding [sodium cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_cyanide "Sodium cyanide"), which reacts with acid to form gaseous HCN.

HCN is the precursor to [sodium cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_cyanide "Sodium cyanide") and [potassium cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide "Potassium cyanide"), which are used mainly in [gold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gold "Gold") and [silver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silver "Silver") mining and for the [electroplating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electroplating "Electroplating") of those metals. Via the intermediacy of [cyanohydrins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanohydrin "Cyanohydrin"), a variety of useful organic compounds are prepared from HCN including the [monomer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monomer "Monomer")[methyl methacrylate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_methacrylate "Methyl methacrylate"), from [acetone](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acetone "Acetone"), the [amino acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amino_acid "Amino acid")[methionine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methionine "Methionine"), via the [Strecker synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strecker_synthesis "Strecker synthesis"), and the chelating agents [EDTA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EDTA "EDTA") and [NTA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitrilotriacetic_acid "Nitrilotriacetic acid"). Via the [hydrocyanation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrocyanation "Hydrocyanation") process, HCN is added to [butadiene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butadiene "Butadiene") to give [adiponitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adiponitrile "Adiponitrile"), a precursor to [Nylon-6,6](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nylon "Nylon").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-Ullmann-10)

HCN is used globally as a [fumigant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fumigant "Fumigant") against many species of pest insects that infest food production facilities. Both its efficacy and method of application lead to very small amounts of the fumigant being used compared to other toxic substances used for the same purpose.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-30) Using HCN as a fumigant also has less environmental impact, compared to some other fumigants such as [sulfuryl fluoride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuryl_fluoride "Sulfuryl fluoride"),[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-31) and [methyl bromide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methyl_bromide "Methyl bromide").[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-32)

HCN is obtainable from [fruits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fruit "Fruit") that have a [pit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Endocarp "Endocarp"), such as [cherries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cherry "Cherry"), [apricots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apricot "Apricot"), [apples](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apple "Apple"), and nuts such as [bitter almonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bitter_almonds "Bitter almonds"), from which almond oil and extract is made. Many of these pits contain small amounts of [cyanohydrins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanohydrin "Cyanohydrin") such as [mandelonitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandelonitrile "Mandelonitrile") and [amygdalin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amygdalin "Amygdalin"), which slowly release hydrogen cyanide.[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-33)[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-34) One hundred grams of crushed apple seeds can yield about 70 mg of HCN.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-35) The roots of [cassava](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassava "Cassava") plants contain [cyanogenic glycosides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanogenic_glycosides "Cyanogenic glycosides") such as [linamarin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Linamarin "Linamarin"), which decompose into HCN in yields of up to 370 mg per kilogram of fresh root.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-36) Some [millipedes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Millipede "Millipede"), such as _[Harpaphe haydeniana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harpaphe\_haydeniana "Harpaphe haydeniana")_, _[Desmoxytes purpurosea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Desmoxytes\_purpurosea "Desmoxytes purpurosea")_, and _[Apheloria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apheloria "Apheloria")_ release hydrogen cyanide as a defense mechanism,[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-37) as do certain insects, such as [burnet moths](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zygaenidae "Zygaenidae") and the larvae of _[Paropsisterna eucalyptus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paropsisterna "Paropsisterna")_.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-38) Hydrogen cyanide is contained in the exhaust of vehicles, and in smoke from burning nitrogen-containing [plastics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plastic "Plastic").

[![Image 20](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c1/PIA18431-SaturnMoon-Titan-SouthPoleVortex-Cloud-20121129.jpg/250px-PIA18431-SaturnMoon-Titan-SouthPoleVortex-Cloud-20121129.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:PIA18431-SaturnMoon-Titan-SouthPoleVortex-Cloud-20121129.jpg)

The South Pole Vortex of Saturn's moon [Titan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Titan_(moon) "Titan (moon)") is a giant swirling cloud of HCN (November 29, 2012)

HCN has been measured in [Titan's atmosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere_of_Titan "Atmosphere of Titan") by four instruments on the [Cassini space probe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cassini%E2%80%93Huygens "Cassini–Huygens"), one instrument on [Voyager](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Voyager_1 "Voyager 1"), and one instrument on Earth.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-39) One of these measurements was _in situ_, where the Cassini spacecraft dipped between 1,000 and 1,100 km (620 and 680 mi) above Titan's surface to collect atmospheric gas for [mass spectrometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass_spectrometry "Mass spectrometry") analysis.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-40) HCN initially forms in Titan's atmosphere through the reaction of photochemically produced methane and nitrogen radicals which proceed through the H 2 CN intermediate, e.g., (CH 3 + N → H 2 CN + H → HCN + H 2).[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-pearce2020-41)[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-42) Ultraviolet radiation breaks HCN up into CN + H; however, CN is efficiently recycled back into HCN via the reaction CN + CH 4 → HCN + CH 3.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-pearce2020-41)

It has been postulated that carbon from a cascade of asteroids (known as the [Late Heavy Bombardment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Late_Heavy_Bombardment "Late Heavy Bombardment")), resulting from interaction of Jupiter and Saturn, blasted the surface of young Earth and reacted with nitrogen in Earth's atmosphere to form HCN.[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-43)

Some authors[_[who?](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Manual\_of\_Style/Words\_to\_watch#Unsupported\_attributions "Wikipedia:Manual of Style/Words to watch")_] have shown that [neurons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neuron "Neuron") can produce hydrogen cyanide upon activation of their [opioid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Opioid "Opioid")[receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Receptor_(biochemistry) "Receptor (biochemistry)") by endogenous or exogenous opioids. They have also shown that neuronal production of HCN activates [NMDA receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NMDA_receptor "NMDA receptor") and plays a role in [signal transduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signal_transduction "Signal transduction") between neuronal cells ([neurotransmission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neurotransmission "Neurotransmission")). Moreover, increased endogenous neuronal HCN production under opioids was seemingly needed for adequate opioid [analgesia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Analgesia "Analgesia"), as analgesic action of opioids was attenuated by HCN scavengers. They considered endogenous HCN to be a [neuromodulator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neuromodulator "Neuromodulator").[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-pmid9369328-44)

It has also been shown that, while stimulating [muscarinic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Muscarinic "Muscarinic")[cholinergic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cholinergic "Cholinergic") receptors in cultured [pheochromocytoma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pheochromocytoma "Pheochromocytoma") cells _increases_ HCN production, in a living organism (_in vivo_) muscarinic cholinergic stimulation actually _decreases_ HCN production.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-45)

[Leukocytes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leukocyte "Leukocyte") generate HCN during [phagocytosis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phagocytosis "Phagocytosis"), and can kill [bacteria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacteria "Bacteria"), [fungi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungi "Fungi"), and other pathogens by generating several different toxic chemicals, one of which is hydrogen cyanide.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-pmid9369328-44)

The [vasodilatation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vasodilatation "Vasodilatation") caused by [sodium nitroprusside](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_nitroprusside "Sodium nitroprusside") has been shown to be mediated not only by NO generation, but also by endogenous cyanide generation, which adds not only toxicity, but also some additional antihypertensive efficacy compared to [nitroglycerine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nitroglycerine "Nitroglycerine") and other non-cyanogenic nitrates which do not cause blood cyanide levels to rise.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-46)

HCN is a constituent of [tobacco smoke](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tobacco_smoke "Tobacco smoke").[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-47)

### HCN and the origin of life

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_cyanide&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: HCN and the origin of life")]

As a precursor to amino acids and nucleic acids, hydrogen cyanide has been proposed to have played a part in the [origin of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abiogenesis "Abiogenesis"). Compounds of special interest are [oligomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oligomer "Oligomer") of HCN including its trimer [aminomalononitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aminomalononitrile "Aminomalononitrile") and tetramer [diaminomaleonitrile](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diaminomaleonitrile "Diaminomaleonitrile"), which can be described as (HCN)3 and (HCN)4, respectively.[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-48) Although the relationship of these chemical reactions to the origin of life theory remains speculative, studies in this area uncovered new pathways to organic compounds derived from the condensation of HCN (e.g. [Adenine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Adenine "Adenine")).[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-49)

Because hydrogen cyanide is a precursor to nucleic acids, which are critical for terrestrial life, [astronomers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astronomers "Astronomers") are incentivized to search for derivatives of HCN. [[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-50)

HCN has been detected in the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium")[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-Snyder,_Lewis_E.;_Buhl,_David_1971_L47-51) and in the atmospheres of [carbon stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_star "Carbon star").[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-52) Since then, extensive studies have probed formation and destruction pathways of HCN in various environments and examined its use as a tracer for a variety of astronomical species and processes. HCN can be [observed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observation "Observation") from ground-based [telescopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Telescope "Telescope") through a number of [atmospheric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atmosphere "Atmosphere") windows.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-53) The J=1→0, J=3→2, J= 4→3, and J=10→9 pure [rotational transitions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_transition "Rotational transition") have all been observed.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-Snyder,_Lewis_E.;_Buhl,_David_1971_L47-51)[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-54)[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-55)

HCN is formed in [interstellar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium") clouds through one of two major pathways:[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-Boger,_G._I._and_Sternberg,_A._2005_302-56) via a neutral-neutral reaction (CH 2 + N → HCN + H) and via [dissociative recombination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissociative_recombination "Dissociative recombination") (HCNH+ + e− → HCN + H). The dissociative recombination pathway is dominant by 30%; however, the [HCNH+](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HCNH%2B "HCNH+") must be in its linear form. Dissociative recombination with its structural isomer, H 2 NC+, exclusively produces [hydrogen isocyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_isocyanide "Hydrogen isocyanide") (HNC).

HCN is destroyed in interstellar clouds through a number of mechanisms depending on the location in the cloud.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-Boger,_G._I._and_Sternberg,_A._2005_302-56) In [photon-dominated regions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon-dominated_region "Photon-dominated region") (PDRs), photodissociation dominates, producing [CN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanide "Cyanide") (HCN + ν → CN + H). At further depths, photodissociation by cosmic rays dominate, producing CN (HCN + cr → CN + H). In the dark core, two competing mechanisms destroy it, forming HCN+ and HCNH+ (HCN + H+ → HCN+ + H; HCN + HCO+ → HCNH+ + CO). The reaction with HCO+ dominates by a factor of ~3.5. HCN has been used to analyze a variety of species and processes in the interstellar medium. It has been suggested as a tracer for dense molecular gas[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-57)[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-58) and as a tracer of stellar inflow in high-mass star-forming regions.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-59) Further, the HNC/HCN ratio has been shown to be an excellent method for distinguishing between PDRs and X-ray-dominated regions (XDRs).[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-60)

On 11 August 2014, astronomers released studies, using the [Atacama Large Millimeter/Submillimeter Array (ALMA)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atacama_Large_Millimeter_Array "Atacama Large Millimeter Array") for the first time, that detailed the distribution of HCN, [HNC](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_isocyanide "Hydrogen isocyanide"), [H 2 CO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Formaldehyde "Formaldehyde"), and [dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dust "Dust") inside the [comae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coma_(cometary) "Coma (cometary)") of [comets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet "Comet")[C/2012 F6 (Lemmon)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C/2012_F6_(Lemmon) "C/2012 F6 (Lemmon)") and [C/2012 S1 (ISON)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comet_ISON "Comet ISON").[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-61)[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-62)

In February 2016, it was announced that traces of hydrogen cyanide were found in the atmosphere of the hot [Super-Earth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Super-Earth "Super-Earth")[55 Cancri e](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/55_Cancri_e "55 Cancri e") with NASA's [Hubble Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope "Hubble Space Telescope").[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-63)

On 14 December 2023, astronomers reported the first time discovery, in the [plumes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plume_(fluid_dynamics) "Plume (fluid dynamics)") of [Enceladus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enceladus "Enceladus"), moon of the planet [Saturn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Saturn "Saturn"), of hydrogen cyanide, a possible chemical essential for [life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life "Life")[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-64) as we know it, as well as other [organic molecules](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_molecule "Organic molecule"), some of which are yet to be better identified and understood. According to the researchers, "these [newly discovered] compounds could potentially support extant [microbial communities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microorganism "Microorganism") or drive complex [organic synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organic_synthesis "Organic synthesis") leading to the [origin of life](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_of_life "Origin of life")."[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-65)[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-66)

As a poison and chemical weapon
-------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hydrogen_cyanide&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: As a poison and chemical weapon")]

In [World War I](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_I "World War I"), hydrogen cyanide was used by the French from 1916 as a chemical weapon against the [Central Powers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Central_Powers "Central Powers"), and by the United States and [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy "Kingdom of Italy") in 1918. It was not found to be effective enough due to weather conditions.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-67)[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-68) The gas is lighter than air and rapidly disperses up into the atmosphere. Rapid dilution made its use in the field impractical. In contrast, denser agents such as [phosgene](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phosgene "Phosgene") or [chlorine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chlorine "Chlorine") tended to remain at ground level and sank into the [trenches](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trench_warfare "Trench warfare") of the Western Front's battlefields. Compared to such agents, hydrogen cyanide had to be present in higher concentrations in order to be fatal. To increase gas persistence, it was mixed with smoke producing compounds. For example, French composition called _Vincennite_ combined 50% hydrogen cyanide with 30% [arsenic trichloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arsenic_trichloride "Arsenic trichloride") and 15% [stannic chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stannic_chloride "Stannic chloride") for smoke production, plus 5% [chloroform](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloroform "Chloroform").[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-69)

A hydrogen cyanide concentration of 100–200 [ppm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parts_per_million "Parts per million") in breathing air will kill a human within 10 to 60 minutes.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-cyanidecode1-70) A hydrogen cyanide concentration of 2000 ppm (about 2380 mg/m 3) will kill a human in about one minute.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-cyanidecode1-70) The toxic effect is caused by the action of the cyanide ion, which halts [cellular respiration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cellular_respiration "Cellular respiration"). It acts as a [non-competitive inhibitor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Non-competitive_inhibitor "Non-competitive inhibitor") for an enzyme in mitochondria called [cytochrome c oxidase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cytochrome_c_oxidase "Cytochrome c oxidase"). As such, hydrogen cyanide is commonly listed among [chemical weapons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_weapons "Chemical weapons") as a [blood agent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blood_agent "Blood agent").[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-71)

The [Chemical Weapons Convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chemical_Weapons_Convention "Chemical Weapons Convention") lists it under [Schedule 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_Schedule_3_substances_(CWC) "List of Schedule 3 substances (CWC)") as a potential weapon which has large-scale industrial uses. Signatory countries must declare manufacturing plants that produce more than 30 metric tons per year, and allow inspection by the [Organisation for the Prohibition of Chemical Weapons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organisation_for_the_Prohibition_of_Chemical_Weapons "Organisation for the Prohibition of Chemical Weapons").

Perhaps its most infamous use is _[Zyklon B](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zyklon\_B "Zyklon B")_ (German: _Cyclone B_, with the **B** standing for _Blausäure_ – prussic acid; also, to distinguish it from an earlier product later known as Zyklon A),[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-72) used in the [Nazi German](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_Germany "Nazi Germany")[extermination camps](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Extermination_camps "Extermination camps") of [Majdanek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Majdanek "Majdanek") and [Auschwitz-Birkenau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Auschwitz-Birkenau "Auschwitz-Birkenau") during [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II") to kill Jews and other persecuted minorities _en masse_ as part of their [Final Solution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Final_Solution "Final Solution") genocide program. Hydrogen cyanide was also used in the camps for delousing clothing in attempts to eradicate diseases carried by lice and other parasites. One of the original Czech producers continued making Zyklon B under the trademark "Uragan D2"[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-73) until around 2015.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-74)

During [World War II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_War_II "World War II"), the US considered using it, along with [cyanogen chloride](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyanogen_chloride "Cyanogen chloride"), as part of [Operation Downfall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operation_Downfall "Operation Downfall"), the planned invasion of Japan, but President [Harry Truman](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harry_Truman "Harry Truman") decided against it, instead using the atomic bombs developed by the secret [Manhattan Project](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manhattan_Project "Manhattan Project").[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-75)

Hydrogen cyanide was also the agent employed in judicial [execution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capital_punishment_in_the_United_States "Capital punishment in the United States") in some [U.S. states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._state "U.S. state"), where it was produced during the execution by the action of [sulfuric acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sulfuric_acid "Sulfuric acid") on [sodium cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sodium_cyanide "Sodium cyanide") or [potassium cyanide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Potassium_cyanide "Potassium cyanide").[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-76)

Under the name _prussic acid_, HCN has been used as a killing agent in [whaling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Whaling "Whaling") harpoons, though it was quickly abandoned for being dangerous to the crew.[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-irons-77) From the middle of the 18th century it was used in a number of poisoning murders and suicides.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-78)

Hydrogen cyanide gas in air is explosive at concentrations above 5.6%.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrogen_cyanide#cite_note-79)
