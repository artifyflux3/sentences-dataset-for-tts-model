From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), [mathematical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_physics "Mathematical physics"), and [theoretical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theoretical_physics "Theoretical physics"), the **spin tensor** is a quantity used to describe the [rotational motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_motion "Rotational motion") of particles in [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime"). The spin tensor has application in [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") and [special relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_relativity "Special relativity"), as well as [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics"), [relativistic quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_quantum_mechanics "Relativistic quantum mechanics"), and [quantum field theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_field_theory "Quantum field theory").

The [special Euclidean group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special_Euclidean_group "Special Euclidean group") SE(_d_) of [direct isometries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_group#Direct_and_indirect_isometries "Euclidean group") is generated by [translations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry) "Translation (geometry)") and [rotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation"). Its [Lie algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra "Lie algebra") is written ![Image 1: {\displaystyle {\mathfrak {se}}(d)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/076aae503da5383065e31bdc923136a90bae5169).

This article uses [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates") and [tensor index notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor_index_notation "Tensor index notation").

Background on Noether currents
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Spin_tensor&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Background on Noether currents")]

The [Noether current](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Noether_current "Noether current") for translations in space is momentum, while the current for increments in time is energy. These two statements combine into one in spacetime: translations in spacetime, i.e. a displacement between two events, is generated by the four-momentum _P_. Conservation of four-momentum is given by the [continuity equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuity_equation "Continuity equation"):

![Image 2: {\displaystyle \partial _{\nu }T^{\mu \nu }=0\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5a3d4362e71e055b2667c152c173dc81a2cde874)

where ![Image 3: {\displaystyle T^{\mu \nu }\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/be91df8980932a641a0d4565248424b357704465) is the [stress–energy tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stress%E2%80%93energy_tensor "Stress–energy tensor"), and ∂ are [partial derivatives](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Partial_derivative "Partial derivative") that make up the [four-gradient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-gradient "Four-gradient") (in non-Cartesian coordinates this must be replaced by the [covariant derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariant_derivative "Covariant derivative")). Integrating over space:

![Image 4: {\displaystyle \int d^{3}xT^{\mu 0}\left({\vec {x}},t\right)=P^{\mu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d783dfcdc5efd3201b303a76b72a89131e9ecb1)

gives the [four-momentum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-momentum "Four-momentum") vector at time _t_.

The Noether current for a rotation about the point _y_ is given by a tensor of 3rd order, denoted ![Image 5: {\displaystyle M_{y}^{\alpha \beta \mu }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ece4023b5c3b2ebb9eba5a961356b65428d526e5). Because of the [Lie algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lie_algebra "Lie algebra") relations

![Image 6: {\displaystyle M_{y}^{\alpha \beta \mu }(x)=M_{0}^{\alpha \beta \mu }(x)+y^{\alpha }T^{\beta \mu }(x)-y^{\beta }T^{\alpha \mu }(x)\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54c0a75df351c08a118611886a4834f49e006d49)

where the 0 subscript indicates the [origin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Origin_(mathematics) "Origin (mathematics)") (unlike momentum, angular momentum depends on the origin), the integral:

![Image 7: {\displaystyle \int d^{3}xM_{0}^{\mu \nu }({\vec {x}},t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/545b1218e409790036d40db75ec8ac9b6d820a67)

gives the [angular momentum tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_angular_momentum "Relativistic angular momentum")![Image 8: {\displaystyle M^{\mu \nu }\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/07e3fb897776b217d8fae9ad5e33efd949f369c3) at time _t_.

The **spin tensor** is defined at a point **x** to be the value of the Noether current at **x** of a rotation about _x_,

![Image 9: {\displaystyle S^{\alpha \beta \mu }(\mathbf {x} )\mathrel {\stackrel {\mathrm {def} }{=}} M_{x}^{\alpha \beta \mu }(\mathbf {x} )=M_{0}^{\alpha \beta \mu }(\mathbf {x} )+x^{\alpha }T^{\beta \mu }(\mathbf {x} )-x^{\beta }T^{\alpha \mu }(\mathbf {x} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ce4c56ca383169130ae487ee5c76e1a84372901b)

The continuity equation

![Image 10: {\displaystyle \partial _{\mu }M_{0}^{\alpha \beta \mu }=0\,,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1104119e8ddfa80be6fd52adf7b9efbf44d5e569)

implies:

![Image 11: {\displaystyle \partial _{\mu }S^{\alpha \beta \mu }=T^{\beta \alpha }-T^{\alpha \beta }\neq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0f84bab7b57ef7f1c0a2c44c956b037d6d0ebfa9)

and therefore, the [stress–energy tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stress%E2%80%93energy_tensor "Stress–energy tensor") is not a [symmetric tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_tensor "Symmetric tensor").

The quantity _S_ is the density of [spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)") angular momentum (spin in this case is not only for a point-like particle, but also for an extended body), and _M_ is the density of orbital angular momentum. The total angular momentum is always the sum of spin and orbital contributions.

The relation:

![Image 12: {\displaystyle T_{ij}-T_{ji}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4ad06c4edea36a12cffb7e5e77e74f729f324242)

gives the [torque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque") density showing the rate of conversion between the orbital angular momentum and spin.

Examples of materials with a nonzero spin density are [molecular fluids](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Molecular_fluid&action=edit&redlink=1 "Molecular fluid (page does not exist)"), the [electromagnetic field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electromagnetic_field "Electromagnetic field") and [turbulent fluids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Turbulent_fluid "Turbulent fluid"). For molecular fluids, the individual molecules may be spinning. The electromagnetic field can have [circularly polarized light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circularly_polarized_light "Circularly polarized light"). For turbulent fluids, we may arbitrarily make a distinction between long wavelength phenomena and short wavelength phenomena. A long wavelength [vorticity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vorticity "Vorticity") may be converted via turbulence into tinier and tinier vortices transporting the angular momentum into smaller and smaller wavelengths while simultaneously reducing the [vorticity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vorticity "Vorticity"). This can be approximated by the [eddy viscosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eddy_viscosity "Eddy viscosity").
