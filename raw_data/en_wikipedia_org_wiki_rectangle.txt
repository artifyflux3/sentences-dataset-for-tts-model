In [Euclidean plane geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_geometry "Euclidean geometry"), a **rectangle** is a [rectilinear](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectilinear_polygon "Rectilinear polygon")[convex polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polygon "Convex polygon") or a [quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral "Quadrilateral") with four [right angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_angle "Right angle"). It can also be defined as: an equiangular quadrilateral, since equiangular means that all of its angles are equal (360°/4 = 90°); or a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") containing a right angle. A rectangle with four sides of equal length is a _[square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square "Square")_. The term "[oblong](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/oblong "wikt:oblong")" is used to refer to a non-[square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square "Square") rectangle.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle#cite_note-:0-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle#cite_note-3) A rectangle with [vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)")_ABCD_ would be denoted as [![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/37/Rectanglen.PNG/20px-Rectanglen.PNG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Rectanglen.PNG)_ABCD_.

The word rectangle comes from the [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin "Latin")_rectangulus_, which is a combination of _rectus_ (as an adjective, right, proper) and _angulus_ ([angle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle")).

A **[crossed rectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle#Crossed_rectangles)** is a crossed (self-intersecting) quadrilateral which consists of two opposite sides of a rectangle along with the two diagonals[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle#cite_note-4) (therefore only two sides are parallel). It is a special case of an [antiparallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antiparallelogram "Antiparallelogram"), and its angles are not right angles and not all equal, though opposite angles are equal. Other geometries, such as [spherical](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_geometry "Spherical geometry"), [elliptic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_geometry "Elliptic geometry"), and [hyperbolic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry "Hyperbolic geometry"), have so-called rectangles with opposite sides equal in length and equal angles that are not right angles.

Rectangles are involved in many [tiling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle#Tessellations) problems, such as tiling the plane by rectangles or tiling a rectangle by [polygons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon "Polygon").

Classification
--------------

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/14/Symmetries_of_square.svg/330px-Symmetries_of_square.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Symmetries_of_square.svg)

A rectangle is a special case of both [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") and [trapezoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid "Trapezoid"). A [square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square "Square") is a special case of a rectangle.

### Traditional hierarchy

A rectangle is a special case of a [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") in which each pair of adjacent [sides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edge_(geometry) "Edge (geometry)") is [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular").

A parallelogram is a special case of a trapezium (known as a [trapezoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trapezoid "Trapezoid") in North America) in which _both_ pairs of opposite sides are [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_(geometry) "Parallel (geometry)") and [equal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equality_(mathematics) "Equality (mathematics)") in [length](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Length "Length").

A trapezium is a [convex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polygon "Convex polygon")[quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral "Quadrilateral") which has at least one pair of [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_(geometry) "Parallel (geometry)") opposite sides.

A convex quadrilateral is

*   **[Simple](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_polygon "Simple polygon")**: The boundary does not cross itself.
*   **[Star-shaped](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star-shaped_polygon "Star-shaped polygon")**: The whole interior is visible from a single point, without crossing any edge.

### Alternative hierarchy

De Villiers defines a rectangle more generally as any quadrilateral with [axes of symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflection_symmetry "Reflection symmetry") through each pair of opposite sides.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle#cite_note-9) This definition includes both right-angled rectangles and crossed rectangles. Each has an axis of symmetry parallel to and equidistant from a pair of opposite sides, and another which is the [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") bisector of those sides, but, in the case of the crossed rectangle, the first [axis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Axis_of_symmetry "Axis of symmetry") is not an axis of [symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry "Symmetry") for either side that it bisects.

Quadrilaterals with two axes of symmetry, each through a pair of opposite sides, belong to the larger class of quadrilaterals with at least one axis of symmetry through a pair of opposite sides. These quadrilaterals comprise [isosceles trapezia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isosceles_trapezia "Isosceles trapezia") and crossed isosceles trapezia (crossed quadrilaterals with the same [vertex arrangement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_arrangement "Vertex arrangement") as isosceles trapezia).

Properties
----------

### Symmetry

A rectangle is [cyclic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyclic_polygon "Cyclic polygon"): all [corners](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corner_angle "Corner angle") lie on a single [circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circle "Circle").

It is [equiangular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equiangular_polygon "Equiangular polygon"): all its corner [angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angle "Angle") are equal (each of 90 [degrees](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degree_(angle) "Degree (angle)")).

It is isogonal or [vertex-transitive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex-transitive "Vertex-transitive"): all corners lie within the same [symmetry orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetry_orbit "Symmetry orbit").

It has two [lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_(geometry) "Line (geometry)") of [reflectional symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reflectional_symmetry "Reflectional symmetry") and [rotational symmetry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotational_symmetry "Rotational symmetry") of order 2 (through 180°).

### Rectangle-rhombus duality

The [dual polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dual_polygon "Dual polygon") of a rectangle is a [rhombus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombus "Rhombus"), as shown in the table below.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle#cite_note-10)

| Rectangle | Rhombus |
| --- | --- |
| All _angles_ are equal. | All _sides_ are equal. |
| Alternate _sides_ are equal. | Alternate _angles_ are equal. |
| Its centre is equidistant from its _[vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex\_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)")_, hence it has a _[circumcircle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circumcircle "Circumcircle")_. | Its centre is equidistant from its _sides_, hence it has an _incircle_. |
| Two axes of symmetry bisect opposite _sides_. | Two axes of symmetry bisect opposite _angles_. |
| Diagonals are equal in _length_. | Diagonals intersect at equal _angles_. |

*   The figure formed by joining, in order, the midpoints of the sides of a rectangle is a [rhombus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhombus "Rhombus") and vice versa.

### Miscellaneous

A rectangle is a [rectilinear polygon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectilinear_polygon "Rectilinear polygon"): its sides meet at right angles.

A rectangle in the plane can be defined by five independent [degrees of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(mechanics) "Degrees of freedom (mechanics)") consisting, for example, of three for position (comprising two of [translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry) "Translation (geometry)") and one of [rotation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation")), one for shape ([aspect ratio](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aspect_ratio#Rectangles "Aspect ratio")), and one for overall size (area).

Two rectangles, neither of which will fit inside the other, are said to be [incomparable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comparability "Comparability").

Theorems
--------

The [isoperimetric theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isoperimetric_theorem "Isoperimetric theorem") for rectangles states that among all rectangles of a given [perimeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perimeter "Perimeter"), the square has the largest [area](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Area "Area").

The midpoints of the sides of any [quadrilateral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrilateral "Quadrilateral") with [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular")[diagonals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diagonals "Diagonals") form a rectangle.

A [parallelogram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallelogram "Parallelogram") with equal [diagonals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diagonals "Diagonals") is a rectangle.

The [Japanese theorem for cyclic quadrilaterals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Japanese_theorem_for_cyclic_quadrilaterals "Japanese theorem for cyclic quadrilaterals")[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle#cite_note-12) states that the incentres of the four triangles determined by the vertices of a cyclic quadrilateral taken three at a time form a rectangle.

The [British flag theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/British_flag_theorem "British flag theorem") states that with vertices denoted _A_, _B_, _C_, and _D_, for any point _P_ on the same plane of a rectangle:[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle#cite_note-13)

For every convex body _C_ in the plane, we can [inscribe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inscribed_figure "Inscribed figure") a rectangle _r_ in _C_ such that a [homothetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homothetic_transformation "Homothetic transformation") copy _R_ of _r_ is circumscribed about _C_ and the positive homothety ratio is at most 2 and ![Image 13: {\displaystyle 0.5{\text{ × Area}}(R)\leq {\text{Area}}(C)\leq 2{\text{ × Area}}(r)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6ef89315793c9e39db06d7e49d165e6921c821c2).[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle#cite_note-14)

Crossed rectangles
------------------

A [_crossed_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_self-intersecting_polygons "List of self-intersecting polygons")_quadrilateral_ (self-intersecting) consists of two opposite sides of a non-self-intersecting quadrilateral along with the two diagonals. Similarly, a crossed rectangle is a _crossed quadrilateral_ which consists of two opposite sides of a rectangle along with the two diagonals. It has the same [vertex arrangement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_arrangement "Vertex arrangement") as the rectangle. It appears as two identical triangles with a common vertex, but the geometric intersection is not considered a vertex.

A _crossed quadrilateral_ is sometimes likened to a [bow tie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bow_tie "Bow tie") or [butterfly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Butterfly "Butterfly"), sometimes called an "angular eight". A [three-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional "Three-dimensional") rectangular [wire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wire "Wire")[frame](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_frame "Space frame") that is twisted can take the shape of a bow tie.

The interior of a _crossed rectangle_ can have a [polygon density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polygon_density "Polygon density") of ±1 in each triangle, dependent upon the winding orientation as clockwise or counterclockwise.

A _crossed rectangle_ may be considered [equiangular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equiangular_polygon "Equiangular polygon") if right and left turns are allowed. As with any _crossed quadrilateral_, the sum of its [interior angles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interior_angle "Interior angle") is 720°, allowing for internal angles to appear on the outside and exceed 180°.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle#cite_note-16)

A rectangle and a crossed rectangle are quadrilaterals with the following properties in common:

*   Opposite sides are equal in length.
*   The two diagonals are equal in length.
*   It has two lines of reflectional symmetry and rotational symmetry of order 2 (through 180°).

Other rectangles
----------------

[![Image 20](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0f/Saddle_rectangle_example.png/250px-Saddle_rectangle_example.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Saddle_rectangle_example.png)

A **saddle rectangle** has 4 nonplanar vertices, [alternated](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alternation_(geometry) "Alternation (geometry)") from vertices of a [rectangular cuboid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangular_cuboid "Rectangular cuboid"), with a unique [minimal surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minimal_surface "Minimal surface") interior defined as a linear combination of the four vertices, creating a saddle surface. This example shows 4 blue edges of the rectangle, and two [green](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green "Green") diagonals, all being diagonal of the cuboid rectangular faces.

In [spherical geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_geometry "Spherical geometry"), a **spherical rectangle** is a figure whose four edges are [great circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_circle "Great circle") arcs which meet at equal angles greater than 90°. Opposite arcs are equal in length. The surface of a sphere in Euclidean solid geometry is a non-Euclidean surface in the sense of elliptic geometry. Spherical geometry is the simplest form of elliptic geometry.

In [elliptic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_geometry "Elliptic geometry"), an **elliptic rectangle** is a figure in the elliptic plane whose four edges are elliptic arcs which meet at equal angles greater than 90°. Opposite arcs are equal in length.

In [hyperbolic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_geometry "Hyperbolic geometry"), a **hyperbolic rectangle** is a figure in the hyperbolic plane whose four edges are hyperbolic arcs which meet at equal angles less than 90°. Opposite arcs are equal in length.

Squared, perfect, and other tiled rectangles
--------------------------------------------

[![Image 21](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/5/59/Perfektes_Rechteck.svg/250px-Perfektes_Rechteck.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Perfektes_Rechteck.svg)

A perfect rectangle of order 9

[![Image 22](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9f/Smallest_perfect_squared_squares.svg/250px-Smallest_perfect_squared_squares.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Smallest_perfect_squared_squares.svg)

Lowest-order perfect squared square (1) and the three smallest perfect squared squares (2–4) – all are simple squared squares

A rectangle tiled by squares, rectangles, or triangles is said to be a "squared", "rectangled", or "triangulated" (or "triangled") rectangle respectively. The tiled rectangle is _perfect_[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle#cite_note-BSST-17)[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle#cite_note-18) if the tiles are [similar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Similarity_(geometry) "Similarity (geometry)") and finite in number and no two tiles are the same size. If two such tiles are the same size, the tiling is _imperfect_. In a perfect (or imperfect) triangled rectangle the triangles must be [right triangles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Right_triangle "Right triangle"). A database of all known perfect rectangles, perfect squares and related shapes can be found at [squaring.net](http://www.squaring.net/). The lowest number of squares need for a perfect tiling of a rectangle is 9[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle#cite_note-19) and the lowest number needed for a [perfect tilling a square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Squaring_the_square "Squaring the square") is 21, found in 1978 by computer search.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle#cite_note-20)

A rectangle has [commensurable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commensurability_(mathematics) "Commensurability (mathematics)") sides if and only if it is tileable by a finite number of unequal squares.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle#cite_note-BSST-17)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rectangle#cite_note-21) The same is true if the tiles are unequal isosceles [right triangles](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/right_triangle "wikt:right triangle").

The tilings of rectangles by other tiles which have attracted the most attention are those by congruent non-rectangular [polyominoes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyomino "Polyomino"), allowing all rotations and reflections. There are also tilings by congruent [polyaboloes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polyabolo "Polyabolo").
