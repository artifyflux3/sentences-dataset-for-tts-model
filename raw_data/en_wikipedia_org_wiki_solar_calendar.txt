A **solar calendar** is a [calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calendar "Calendar") whose dates indicates the [season](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Season "Season") or almost equivalently the apparent [position of the Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_of_the_Sun "Position of the Sun") relative to the stars. The [Gregorian calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorian_calendar "Gregorian calendar"), widely accepted as a standard in the world, is an example of a solar calendar. The main other types of calendar are [lunar calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_calendar "Lunar calendar") and [lunisolar calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar "Lunisolar calendar"), whose months correspond to cycles of [Moon phases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon_phase "Moon phase"). The months of the Gregorian calendar do not correspond to cycles of the Moon phase.

The Egyptians appear to have been the first to develop a solar calendar, using as a fixed point the annual sunrise reappearance of the Dog Star—[Sirius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sirius "Sirius"), or Sothis—in the eastern sky, which coincided with the annual flooding of the Nile River. They constructed a calendar of 365 days, consisting of 12 months of 30 days each, with 5 days added at the year’s end. The Egyptians’ failure to account for the extra fraction of a day, however, caused their calendar to drift gradually into error.

Examples
--------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Solar_calendar&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Examples")]

The oldest solar calendars include the [Julian calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Julian_calendar "Julian calendar") and the [Coptic calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coptic_calendar "Coptic calendar"). They both have a year of 365 days, which is extended to 366 once every four years, without exception, so have a mean year of 365.25 days. As solar calendars became more accurate, they evolved into two types.

### Tropical solar calendars

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Solar_calendar&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Tropical solar calendars")]

[![Image 12: [icon]](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg)This section **needs expansion**. You can help by [adding to it](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Solar_calendar&action=edit&section=). _(January 2017)_

If the position of the Earth in its orbit around the Sun is reckoned with respect to the [Equinox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equinox "Equinox"), the point at which the orbit crosses the celestial equator, then its dates accurately indicate the [seasons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Season "Season"), that is, they are synchronized with the [declination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Declination "Declination") of the Sun. Such a calendar is called a **tropical solar calendar**[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]..

The duration of the _mean_ calendar year of such a calendar approximates some form of the [tropical year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tropical_year "Tropical year"), usually either the [mean tropical year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mean_tropical_year "Mean tropical year") or the [vernal equinox year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vernal_equinox_year "Vernal equinox year").

The following are tropical solar calendars:

*   Ancient Armenian calendar
*   [Bengali calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bengali_calendar "Bengali calendar") (National and official calendar in Bangladesh)
*   [Gregorian calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorian_calendar "Gregorian calendar")
*   [Iranian calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iranian_calendar "Iranian calendar") (Jalāli calendar) 
    *   [Tabarian calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tabarian_calendar "Tabarian calendar") (Tabarian calendar)

*   [Indian national calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indian_national_calendar "Indian national calendar") (Saka calendar)
*   [French Republican calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/French_Republican_calendar "French Republican calendar")

Every one of these calendars has a year of 365 days, which is occasionally extended by adding an extra day to form a [leap year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leap_year "Leap year"), a method called "[intercalation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intercalation_(timekeeping) "Intercalation (timekeeping)")", the inserted day being "intercalary".

The [Baháʼí calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bah%C3%A1%CA%BC%C3%AD_calendar "Baháʼí calendar"), another example of a solar calendar, always begins the year on the [vernal equinox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/March_equinox "March equinox") and sets its intercalary days so that the following year also begins on the vernal equinox. The moment of the vernal equinox in the northern hemisphere is determined using the location of [Tehran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tehran "Tehran") "by means of astronomical computations from reliable sources".[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_calendar#cite_note-UHJ1-1)

### Sidereal solar calendars

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Solar_calendar&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Sidereal solar calendars")]

Further information: [Sidereal time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sidereal_time "Sidereal time")

[![Image 13: [icon]](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg)This section **needs expansion**. You can help by [adding to it](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Solar_calendar&action=edit&section=). _(January 2017)_

If the position of the Earth (see above) is reckoned with respect to the fixed stars, then the dates indicate the [zodiacal constellation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zodiacal_constellation "Zodiacal constellation") near which the Sun can be found. A calendar of this type is called a **sidereal solar calendar**.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_calendar#cite_note-2)

The mean calendar year of such a calendar approximates the [sidereal year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sidereal_year "Sidereal year").

Leaping from one lunation to another, but one Sidereal year is the period between two occurrences of the sun, as measured by the stars' solar calendar, which is derived from the Earth's orbit around the sun every 28 years.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_calendar#cite_note-3)

Indian calendars like the [Hindu calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindu_calendar "Hindu calendar"), [Tamil calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tamil_calendar "Tamil calendar"), [Bengali calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bengali_calendar "Bengali calendar") (revised) and [Malayalam calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Malayalam_calendar "Malayalam calendar") are sidereal solar calendars. The [Thai solar calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thai_solar_calendar "Thai solar calendar") when based on the [Hindu solar calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hindu_calendar "Hindu calendar") was also a sidereal calendar. They are calculated on the basis of the apparent motion of the Sun through the twelve [zodiacal signs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zodiac#Constellations "Zodiac") rather than the tropical movement of the Earth.

Non-solar calendars
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Solar_calendar&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Non-solar calendars")]

[![Image 14: [icon]](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg)This section **needs expansion**. You can help by [adding to it](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Solar_calendar&action=edit&section=). _(January 2017)_

The [Islamic calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islamic_calendar "Islamic calendar") is a purely [lunar calendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_calendar "Lunar calendar") and has a year, whose start drifts through the seasons and so is not a solar calendar. The Maya [Tzolkin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tzolkin "Tzolkin") calendar, which follows a 260-day cycle, has no year, therefore it is not a solar calendar. Also, any calendar synchronized only to the [synodic period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synodic_period "Synodic period") of [Venus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Venus "Venus") would not be solar.

Lunisolar calendars
-------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Solar_calendar&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Lunisolar calendars")]

[![Image 15: [icon]](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg/20px-Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Wiki_letter_w_cropped.svg)This section **needs expansion**. You can help by [adding to it](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Solar_calendar&action=edit&section=). _(January 2017)_

[Lunisolar calendars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunisolar_calendar "Lunisolar calendar") may be regarded as solar calendars, although their dates additionally indicate the moon phase. Typical lunisolar calendars have years marked with a whole number of lunar months, so they can not indicate the position of Earth relative to the Sun with the same accuracy as a purely solar calendar.
