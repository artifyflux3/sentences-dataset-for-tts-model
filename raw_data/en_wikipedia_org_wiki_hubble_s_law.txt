[![Image 1](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/ac/Raisinbread.gif/330px-Raisinbread.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Raisinbread.gif)

An analogy for explaining Hubble's law, using [raisins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raisin "Raisin") in a rising loaf of bread in place of galaxies. If a raisin is twice as far away from a place as another raisin, then the farther raisin would move away from that place twice as quickly.

**Hubble's law**, also known as the **Hubble–Lemaître law**,[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-1) is the observation in [physical cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_cosmology "Physical cosmology") that [galaxies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxies "Galaxies") are moving away from Earth at speeds proportional to their distance. In other words, the farther a galaxy is from the Earth, the faster it moves away. A galaxy's [recessional velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recessional_velocity "Recessional velocity") is typically determined by measuring its [redshift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift"), a shift in the frequency of [light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light "Light") emitted by the galaxy.

The discovery of Hubble's law is attributed to work published by [Edwin Hubble](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edwin_Hubble "Edwin Hubble") in 1929,[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-4) but the notion of the universe expanding at a calculable rate was first derived from [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") equations in 1922 by [Alexander Friedmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Friedmann "Alexander Friedmann"). The [Friedmann equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedmann_equations "Friedmann equations") showed the universe might be expanding, and presented the expansion speed if that were the case.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-5) Before Hubble, astronomer [Carl Wilhelm Wirtz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Wilhelm_Wirtz "Carl Wilhelm Wirtz") had, in 1922[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Wirtz-1922-6) and 1924,[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Wirtz-1924-7) deduced with his own data that galaxies that appeared smaller and dimmer had larger redshifts and thus that more distant galaxies recede faster from the observer. In 1927, [Georges Lemaître](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georges_Lema%C3%AEtre "Georges Lemaître") concluded that the universe might be expanding by noting the proportionality of the recessional velocity of distant bodies to their respective distances. He estimated a value for this ratio, which—after Hubble confirmed cosmic expansion and determined a more precise value for it two years later—became known as the Hubble constant.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-NYT-20170220-8)[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Livio-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-:1-12) Hubble inferred the recession velocity of the objects from their [redshifts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift "Redshift"), many of which were earlier measured and related to velocity by [Vesto Slipher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vesto_Slipher "Vesto Slipher") in 1917.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-13)[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-MS_Longair2-14)[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-15) Combining Slipher's velocities with [Henrietta Swan Leavitt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Henrietta_Swan_Leavitt "Henrietta Swan Leavitt")'s intergalactic distance calculations and methodology allowed Hubble to better calculate an expansion rate for the universe.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-16)

Hubble's law is considered the first observational basis for the [expansion of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expansion_of_the_universe "Expansion of the universe"), and is one of the pieces of evidence most often cited in support of the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang") model.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-NYT-20170220-8)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Coles-17) The motion of astronomical objects due solely to this expansion is known as the **Hubble flow**.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-18) It is described by the equation _v_ = _H_ 0 _D_, with _H_ 0 the constant of proportionality—the **Hubble constant**—between the "proper distance" D to a galaxy (which can change over time, unlike the [comoving distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comoving_and_proper_distances "Comoving and proper distances")) and its speed of separation v, i.e. the [derivative](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derivative "Derivative") of proper distance with respect to the [cosmic time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_time "Cosmic time") coordinate.[[a]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-19) Though the Hubble constant _H_ 0 is constant at any given moment in time, the **Hubble parameter**H, of which the Hubble constant is the current value, varies with time, so the term _constant_ is sometimes thought of as somewhat of a misnomer.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-NYT-20190225-20)[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-21)

The Hubble constant is most frequently quoted in [km](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilometre "Kilometre")/[s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second "Second")/[Mpc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec#Megaparsecs_and_gigaparsecs "Parsec"), which gives the speed of a galaxy 1 megaparsec (3.09×10 19 km) away as 70 km/s. Simplifying the units of the generalized form reveals that _H_ 0 specifies a [frequency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frequency "Frequency") (SI unit: [s−1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hertz "Hertz")), leading the reciprocal of _H_ 0 to be known as the [Hubble time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#Hubble_time) (14.4 billion years). The Hubble constant can also be stated as a relative rate of expansion. In this form _H_ 0= 7%/[Gyr](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Year#Abbreviations_for_%22years_ago%22 "Year"), meaning that, at the current rate of expansion, it takes one billion years for an unbound structure to grow by 7%.

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bc/Three_steps_to_the_Hubble_constant.jpg/960px-Three_steps_to_the_Hubble_constant.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Three_steps_to_the_Hubble_constant.jpg)

Three steps to the Hubble constant[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-22)

A decade before Hubble made his observations, a number of [physicists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physicists "Physicists") and [mathematicians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematician "Mathematician") had established a consistent theory of an expanding universe by using [Einstein field equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_field_equations "Einstein field equations") of [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity"). Applying the most [general principles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_principle "Cosmological principle") to the nature of the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe") yielded a [dynamic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dynamics_(mechanics) "Dynamics (mechanics)") solution that conflicted with the then-prevalent notion of a [static universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Static_universe "Static universe").

### Slipher's observations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hubble%27s_law&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Slipher's observations")]

In 1912, [Vesto M. Slipher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vesto_M._Slipher "Vesto M. Slipher") measured the first [Doppler shift](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doppler_shift "Doppler shift") of a "[spiral nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spiral_nebula "Spiral nebula")" (the obsolete term for spiral galaxies) and soon discovered that almost all such objects were receding from Earth. He did not grasp the cosmological implications of this fact, and indeed at the time it was [highly controversial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shapley%E2%80%93Curtis_debate "Shapley–Curtis debate") whether or not these nebulae were "island universes" outside the Milky Way galaxy.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-23)[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-24)

In 1922, [Alexander Friedmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Friedmann "Alexander Friedmann") derived his Friedmann equations from [Einstein field equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_field_equations "Einstein field equations"), showing that the universe might expand at a rate calculable by the equations.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-25) The parameter used by Friedmann is known today as the [scale factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scale_factor_(cosmology) "Scale factor (cosmology)") and can be considered as a [scale invariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scale_invariance "Scale invariance") form of the [proportionality constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Proportionality_(mathematics) "Proportionality (mathematics)") of Hubble's law. Georges Lemaître independently found a similar solution in his 1927 paper discussed in the following section. The Friedmann equations are derived by inserting the [metric for a homogeneous and isotropic universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedmann%E2%80%93Lema%C3%AEtre%E2%80%93Robertson%E2%80%93Walker_metric "Friedmann–Lemaître–Robertson–Walker metric") into Einstein's field equations for a fluid with a given [density](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Density "Density") and [pressure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pressure "Pressure"). This idea of an expanding spacetime would eventually lead to the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang") and [Steady State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Steady_State_Theory "Steady State Theory") theories of [cosmology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmology "Cosmology").

### Lemaître's equation

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hubble%27s_law&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Lemaître's equation")]

In 1927, two years before Hubble published his own article, the Belgian priest and astronomer Georges Lemaître was the first to publish research deriving what is now known as Hubble's law. According to the Canadian astronomer [Sidney van den Bergh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sidney_van_den_Bergh "Sidney van den Bergh"), "the 1927 discovery of the expansion of the universe by Lemaître was published in French in a low-impact journal. In the 1931 high-impact English translation of this article, a critical equation was changed by omitting reference to what is now known as the Hubble constant."[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-26) It is now known that the alterations in the translated paper were carried out by Lemaître himself.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Livio-10)[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-27)

### Shape of the universe

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hubble%27s_law&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Shape of the universe")]

Before the advent of modern cosmology, there was considerable talk about the size and [shape of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape_of_the_universe "Shape of the universe"). In 1920, the [Shapley–Curtis debate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_Debate_(astronomy) "Great Debate (astronomy)") took place between [Harlow Shapley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harlow_Shapley "Harlow Shapley") and [Heber D. Curtis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heber_Doust_Curtis "Heber Doust Curtis") over this issue. Shapley argued for a small universe the size of the Milky Way galaxy, and Curtis argued that the universe was much larger. The issue was resolved in the coming decade with Hubble's improved observations.

### Cepheid variable stars outside the Milky Way

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hubble%27s_law&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Cepheid variable stars outside the Milky Way")]

Edwin Hubble did most of his professional astronomical observing work at [Mount Wilson Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mount_Wilson_Observatory "Mount Wilson Observatory"),[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-28) home to the world's most powerful telescope at the time. His observations of [Cepheid variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cepheid_variable "Cepheid variable") stars in "spiral nebulae" enabled him to calculate the distances to these objects. Surprisingly, these objects were discovered to be at distances which placed them well outside the Milky Way. They continued to be called _nebulae_, and it was only gradually that the term _galaxies_ replaced it.

### Combining redshifts with distance measurements

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hubble%27s_law&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Combining redshifts with distance measurements")]

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2c/Hubble_constant.JPG/500px-Hubble_constant.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hubble_constant.JPG)

Fit of [redshift velocities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#Redshift_velocity) to Hubble's law.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Keel-29) Various estimates for the [Hubble constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#Determining_the_Hubble_constant) exist.

The velocities and distances that appear in Hubble's law are not directly measured. The velocities are inferred from the redshift _z_ = ∆_λ_/_λ_ of radiation and distance is inferred from brightness. Hubble sought to correlate brightness with parameter z.

Combining his measurements of galaxy distances with Vesto Slipher and [Milton Humason](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milton_Humason "Milton Humason")'s measurements of the redshifts associated with the galaxies, Hubble discovered a rough proportionality between redshift of an object and its distance. Though there was considerable [scatter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variance "Variance") (now known to be caused by [peculiar velocities](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peculiar_velocity "Peculiar velocity")—the 'Hubble flow' is used to refer to the region of space far enough out that the recession velocity is larger than local peculiar velocities), Hubble was able to plot a trend line from the 46 galaxies he studied and obtain a value for the Hubble constant of 500(km/s)/Mpc (much higher than the currently accepted value due to errors in his distance calibrations; see [cosmic distance ladder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_distance_ladder "Cosmic distance ladder") for details).[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-damnh-30)

Hubble's law can be easily depicted in a "Hubble diagram" in which the velocity (assumed approximately proportional to the redshift) of an object is plotted with respect to its distance from the observer.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-31) A straight line of positive slope on this diagram is the visual depiction of Hubble's law.

### Cosmological constant abandoned

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hubble%27s_law&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Cosmological constant abandoned")]

After Hubble's discovery was published, [Albert Einstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Albert_Einstein "Albert Einstein") abandoned his work on the [cosmological constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_constant "Cosmological constant"), a [term](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Term_(logic) "Term (logic)") he had inserted into his equations of general relativity to coerce them into producing the static solution he previously considered the correct state of the universe. The Einstein equations in their simplest form model either an expanding or contracting universe, so Einstein introduced the constant to counter expansion or contraction and lead to a static and flat universe.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-mapcc-32) After Hubble's discovery that the universe was, in fact, expanding, Einstein called his faulty assumption that the universe is static his "greatest mistake".[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-mapcc-32) On its own, general relativity could predict the expansion of the universe, which (through [observations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tests_of_general_relativity "Tests of general relativity") such as the [bending of light by large masses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_lens "Gravitational lens"), or the [precession of the orbit of Mercury](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perihelion_precession_of_Mercury "Perihelion precession of Mercury")) could be experimentally observed and compared to his theoretical calculations using particular solutions of the equations he had originally formulated.

In 1931, Einstein went to Mount Wilson Observatory to thank Hubble for providing the observational basis for modern cosmology.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-33)

The cosmological constant has regained attention in recent decades as a hypothetical explanation for [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy").[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-34)

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bd/Velocity-redshift.JPG/500px-Velocity-redshift.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Velocity-redshift.JPG)

A variety of possible recessional velocity vs. redshift functions including the simple linear relation _v_ = _cz_; a variety of possible shapes from theories related to general relativity; and a curve that does not permit speeds faster than light in accordance with special relativity. All curves are linear at low redshifts.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-D&L-35)

The discovery of the linear relationship between redshift and distance, coupled with a supposed linear relation between [recessional velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recessional_velocity "Recessional velocity") and redshift, yields a straightforward mathematical expression for Hubble's law as follows:

![Image 5: {\displaystyle v=H_{0}\,D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d7c164df8a15901f4d545126ca77df93ba65e765)

where

*   v is the recessional velocity, typically expressed in km/s.
*   _H_ 0 is Hubble's constant and corresponds to the value of H (often termed the **Hubble parameter** which is a value that is [time dependent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time-variant_system "Time-variant system") and which can be expressed in terms of the [scale factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scale_factor_(cosmology) "Scale factor (cosmology)")) in the Friedmann equations taken at the time of observation denoted by the subscript 0. This value is the same throughout the universe for a given [comoving time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comoving_time#Comoving_coordinates "Comoving time").
*   D is the proper distance (which can change over time, unlike the [comoving distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comoving_distance "Comoving distance"), which is constant) from the [galaxy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy "Galaxy") to the observer, measured in [mega](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mega- "Mega-")[parsecs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec") (Mpc), in the 3-space defined by given [cosmological time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_time "Cosmological time"). (Recession velocity is just _v_ = _dD/dt_).

Hubble's law is considered a fundamental relation between recessional velocity and distance. However, the relation between recessional velocity and redshift depends on the cosmological model adopted and is not established except for small redshifts.

For distances D larger than the radius of the [Hubble sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_sphere "Hubble sphere")_r_ HS, objects recede at a rate faster than the [speed of light](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Speed_of_light "Speed of light") (_See_[Uses of the proper distance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comoving_distance#Uses_of_the_proper_distance "Comoving distance") for a discussion of the significance of this):

![Image 6: {\displaystyle r_{\text{HS}}={\frac {c}{H_{0}}}\ .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7307fee2a99fbdb1475267734c80abc1b5009fbf)

Since the Hubble "constant" is a constant only in space, not in time, the radius of the Hubble sphere may increase or decrease over various time intervals. The subscript '0' indicates the value of the Hubble constant today.[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Keel-29) Current evidence suggests that the expansion of the universe is accelerating (_see_[Accelerating universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_universe "Accelerating universe")), meaning that for any given galaxy, the recession velocity dD/dt is increasing over time as the galaxy moves to greater and greater distances; however, the Hubble parameter is actually thought to be decreasing with time, meaning that if we were to look at some _fixed_ distance D and watch a series of different galaxies pass that distance, later galaxies would pass that distance at a smaller velocity than earlier ones.[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-36)

### Redshift velocity and recessional velocity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hubble%27s_law&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Redshift velocity and recessional velocity")]

Redshift can be measured by determining the wavelength of a known transition, such as hydrogen α-lines for distant quasars, and finding the fractional shift compared to a stationary reference. Thus, redshift is a quantity unambiguously acquired from observation. Care is required, however, in translating these to recessional velocities: for small redshift values, a linear relation of redshift to recessional velocity applies, but more generally the redshift-distance law is nonlinear, meaning the co-relation must be derived specifically for each given model and epoch.[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Harrison-37)

The redshift z is often described as a _redshift velocity_, which is the recessional velocity that would produce the same redshift _if_ it were caused by a linear [Doppler effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doppler_effect "Doppler effect") (which, however, is not the case, as the velocities involved are too large to use a non-relativistic formula for Doppler shift). This redshift velocity can easily exceed the speed of light.[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Madsen-38) In other words, to determine the redshift velocity _v_ rs, the relation:

![Image 7: {\displaystyle v_{\text{rs}}\equiv cz,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e3598bd7a50865db7c3569b7384147323f343af6)

is used.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Dekel-39)[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Padmanabhan-40) That is, there is _no fundamental difference_ between redshift velocity and redshift: they are rigidly proportional, and not related by any theoretical reasoning. The motivation behind the "redshift velocity" terminology is that the redshift velocity agrees with the velocity from a low-velocity simplification of the so-called [Fizeau–Doppler formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Relativistic_Doppler_effect "Relativistic Doppler effect")[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Sartori-41)

![Image 8: {\displaystyle z={\frac {\lambda _{\text{o}}}{\lambda _{\text{e}}}}-1={\sqrt {\frac {1+{\frac {v}{c}}}{1-{\frac {v}{c}}}}}-1\approx {\frac {v}{c}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86aa6b104da32d20b3f0c29f22b24abf0ec086e6)

Here, _λ_ o, _λ_ e are the observed and emitted wavelengths respectively. The "redshift velocity" _v_ rs is not so simply related to real velocity at larger velocities, however, and this terminology leads to confusion if interpreted as a real velocity. Next, the connection between redshift or redshift velocity and recessional velocity is discussed.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-L_Sartori-42)

#### Recessional velocity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hubble%27s_law&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Recessional velocity")]

Suppose _R_(_t_) is called the _scale factor_ of the universe, and increases as the universe expands in a manner that depends upon the [cosmological model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Physical_cosmology "Physical cosmology") selected. Its meaning is that all measured proper distances _D_(_t_) between co-moving points increase proportionally to R. (The co-moving points are not moving relative to their local environments.) In other words:

![Image 9: {\displaystyle {\frac {D(t)}{D(t_{0})}}={\frac {R(t)}{R(t_{0})}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d79ece78d57b7a6a14e3ff94133754b93a6a51f0)

where _t_ 0 is some reference time.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-43) If light is emitted from a galaxy at time _t_ e and received by us at _t_ 0, it is redshifted due to the expansion of the universe, and this redshift z is simply:

![Image 10: {\displaystyle z={\frac {R(t_{0})}{R(t_{\text{e}})}}-1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c64898933a256c02aa9dd44ae382073893073316)

Suppose a galaxy is at distance D, and this distance changes with time at a rate d t D. We call this rate of recession the "recession velocity" _v_ r:

![Image 11: {\displaystyle v_{\text{r}}=d_{t}D={\frac {d_{t}R}{R}}D.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb861b6582f7c9efb0ca24426a5cd998c5a3ae34)

We now define the Hubble constant as

![Image 12: {\displaystyle H\equiv {\frac {d_{t}R}{R}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0cb13fb57afa8286601828ec7c734aa8c92bdcce)

and discover the Hubble law:

![Image 13: {\displaystyle v_{\text{r}}=HD.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f39aa7443eb682cde63935199f26cc3d5d33fca0)

From this perspective, Hubble's law is a fundamental relation between (i) the recessional velocity associated with the expansion of the universe and (ii) the distance to an object; the connection between redshift and distance is a crutch used to connect Hubble's law with observations. This law can be related to redshift z approximately by making a [Taylor series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor_series "Taylor series") expansion:

![Image 14: {\displaystyle z={\frac {R(t_{0})}{R(t_{e})}}-1\approx {\frac {R(t_{0})}{R(t_{0})\left(1+(t_{e}-t_{0})H(t_{0})\right)}}-1\approx (t_{0}-t_{e})H(t_{0}),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bef15b8714a9350d497da09d548292fa25ddea92)

If the distance is not too large, all other complications of the model become small corrections, and the time interval is simply the distance divided by the speed of light:

![Image 15: {\displaystyle z\approx (t_{0}-t_{\text{e}})H(t_{0})\approx {\frac {D}{c}}H(t_{0}),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3fd1c87d122768de828a99e2088d80b453b0406e)

or

![Image 16: {\displaystyle cz\approx DH(t_{0})=v_{r}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/99770c6896da9817c8672e34069587c91b8923a8)

According to this approach, the relation _cz_ = _v_ r is an approximation valid at low redshifts, to be replaced by a relation at large redshifts that is model-dependent. See [velocity-redshift figure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#redshift).

### Observability of parameters

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hubble%27s_law&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Observability of parameters")]

Strictly speaking, neither v nor D in the formula are directly observable, because they are properties _now_ of a galaxy, whereas our observations refer to the galaxy in the past, at the time that the light we currently see left it.

For relatively nearby galaxies (redshift z much less than one), v and D will not have changed much, and v can be estimated using the formula v = zc where c is the speed of light. This gives the empirical relation found by Hubble.

For distant galaxies, v (or D) cannot be calculated from z without specifying a detailed model for how H changes with time. The redshift is not even directly related to the recession velocity at the time the light set out, but it does have a simple interpretation: (1 + _z_) is the factor by which the universe has expanded while the photon was traveling towards the observer.

### Expansion velocity vs. peculiar velocity

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hubble%27s_law&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Expansion velocity vs. peculiar velocity")]

In using Hubble's law to determine distances, only the velocity due to the expansion of the universe can be used. Since gravitationally interacting galaxies move relative to each other independent of the expansion of the universe,[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-AM-20170818-44) these relative velocities, called peculiar velocities, need to be accounted for in the application of Hubble's law. Such peculiar velocities give rise to [redshift-space distortions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redshift-space_distortions "Redshift-space distortions").

### Time-dependence of Hubble parameter

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hubble%27s_law&action=edit&section=16 "Edit section: Time-dependence of Hubble parameter")]

The parameter H is commonly called the "Hubble constant", but that is a misnomer since it is constant in space only at a fixed time; it varies with time in nearly all cosmological models, and all observations of far distant objects are also observations into the distant past, when the "constant" had a different value. "Hubble parameter" is a more correct term, with _H_ 0 denoting the present-day value.

Another common source of confusion is that the accelerating universe does _not_ imply that the Hubble parameter is actually increasing with time; since ![Image 17: {\displaystyle H(t)\equiv {\dot {a}}(t)/a(t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d249ba1a079d982bf9459dcd81a006b1087ff858), in most accelerating models ![Image 18: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) increases relatively faster than ![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\dot {a}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c9167337d4bf7b72b9099d7d5714497cda8643ca), so H decreases with time. (The recession velocity of one chosen galaxy does increase, but different galaxies passing a sphere of fixed radius cross the sphere more slowly at later times.)

On defining the dimensionless [deceleration parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deceleration_parameter "Deceleration parameter")![Image 20: {\textstyle q\equiv -{\frac {{\ddot {a}}\,a}{{\dot {a}}^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a6aa47fc03e661044153b8c2d85c1a5dd0793d6), it follows that

![Image 21: {\displaystyle {\frac {dH}{dt}}=-H^{2}(1+q)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6074f6135894d0c22f4a25365aae3a755f9624ba)

From this it is seen that the Hubble parameter is decreasing with time, unless _q_< -1; the latter can only occur if the universe contains [phantom energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phantom_energy "Phantom energy"), regarded as theoretically somewhat improbable.

However, in the standard [Lambda cold dark matter model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model "Lambda-CDM model") (Lambda-CDM or ΛCDM model), q will tend to −1 from above in the distant future as the cosmological constant becomes increasingly dominant over matter; this implies that H will approach from above to a constant value of ≈57(km/s)/Mpc, and the scale factor of the universe will then grow exponentially in time.

### Idealized Hubble's law

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hubble%27s_law&action=edit&section=17 "Edit section: Idealized Hubble's law")]

The mathematical derivation of an idealized Hubble's law for a uniformly expanding universe is a fairly elementary theorem of geometry in 3-dimensional [Cartesian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system")/Newtonian coordinate space, which, considered as a [metric space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_space "Metric space"), is entirely [homogeneous and isotropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_principle "Cosmological principle") (properties do not vary with location or direction). Simply stated, the theorem is this:

> Any two points which are moving away from the origin, each along straight lines and with speed proportional to distance from the origin, will be moving away from each other with a speed proportional to their distance apart.

In fact, this applies to non-Cartesian spaces as long as they are locally homogeneous and isotropic, specifically to the negatively and positively curved spaces frequently considered as cosmological models (see [shape of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape_of_the_universe "Shape of the universe")).

An observation stemming from this theorem is that seeing objects recede from us on Earth is not an indication that Earth is near to a center from which the expansion is occurring, but rather that _every_ observer in an expanding universe will see objects receding from them.

### Ultimate fate and age of the universe

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hubble%27s_law&action=edit&section=18 "Edit section: Ultimate fate and age of the universe")]

[![Image 22](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/dc/Friedmann_universes.svg/500px-Friedmann_universes.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Friedmann_universes.svg)

The [age](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_the_universe "Age of the universe") and [ultimate fate of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultimate_fate_of_the_universe "Ultimate fate of the universe") can be determined by measuring the Hubble constant today and extrapolating with the observed value of the deceleration parameter, uniquely characterized by values of density parameters (Ω M for [matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter") and Ω Λ for dark energy).

A **closed universe** with Ω M> 1 and Ω Λ = 0 comes to an end in a [Big Crunch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Crunch "Big Crunch") and is considerably younger than its Hubble age.

An **open universe** with Ω M ≤ 1 and Ω Λ = 0 expands forever and has an age that is closer to its Hubble age. For the accelerating universe with nonzero Ω Λ that we inhabit, the age of the universe is coincidentally very close to the Hubble age.

The value of the Hubble parameter changes over time, either increasing or decreasing depending on the value of the so-called [deceleration parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deceleration_parameter "Deceleration parameter")q, which is defined by

![Image 23: {\displaystyle q=-\left(1+{\frac {\dot {H}}{H^{2}}}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ddaa2e820b7e67291868f468e5adb91f9f529439)

In a universe with a deceleration parameter equal to zero, it follows that _H_ = 1/_t_, where t is the time since the Big Bang. A non-zero, time-dependent value of q simply requires [integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integral "Integral") of the Friedmann equations backwards from the present time to the time when the [comoving horizon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Particle_horizon "Particle horizon") size was zero.

It was long thought that q was positive, indicating that the expansion is slowing down due to gravitational attraction. This would imply an age of the universe less than 1/_H_ (which is about 14 billion years). For instance, a value for q of 1/2 (once favoured by most theorists) would give the age of the universe as 2/(3 _H_). The discovery in 1998 that q is apparently negative means that the universe could actually be older than 1/_H_. However, estimates of the [age of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_the_universe "Age of the universe") are very close to 1/_H_.

The expansion of space summarized by the Big Bang interpretation of Hubble's law is relevant to the old conundrum known as [Olbers' paradox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Olbers%27_paradox "Olbers' paradox"): If the universe were [infinite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinity "Infinity") in size, [static](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Static_universe "Static universe"), and filled with a uniform distribution of [stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star"), then every line of sight in the sky would end on a star, and the sky would be as [bright](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brightness "Brightness") as the surface of a star. However, the night sky is largely dark.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Chase_etal_2004-45)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Asimov1974-46)

Since the 17th century, astronomers and other thinkers have proposed many possible ways to resolve this paradox, but the currently accepted resolution depends in part on the Big Bang theory, and in part on the Hubble expansion: in a universe that existed for a finite amount of time, only the light of a finite number of stars has had enough time to reach us, and the paradox is resolved. Additionally, in an expanding universe, distant objects recede from us, which causes the light emanated from them to be redshifted and diminished in brightness by the time we see it.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Chase_etal_2004-45)[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Asimov1974-46)

### Dimensionless Hubble constant

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hubble%27s_law&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Dimensionless Hubble constant")]

Instead of working with Hubble's constant, a common practice is to introduce the **dimensionless Hubble constant**, usually denoted by h and commonly referred to as "little h",[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-damnh-30) then to write Hubble's constant _H_ 0 as _h_ × 100 km⋅[s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second "Second")−1⋅[Mpc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec "Parsec")−1, all the relative uncertainty of the true value of _H_ 0 being then relegated to h.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-47) The dimensionless Hubble constant is often used when giving distances that are calculated from redshift z using the formula _d_ ≈ ⁠_c_/_H_ 0⁠ × _z_. Since _H_ 0 is not precisely known, the distance is expressed as:

![Image 24: {\displaystyle cz/H_{0}\approx (2998\times z){\text{ Mpc }}h^{-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/56b742f2a03decd6981bb635dfdf33ae8657ff1c)

In other words, one calculates 2998 × z and one gives the units as Mpc _h_-1 or _h_-1 Mpc.

Occasionally a reference value other than 100 may be chosen, in which case a subscript is presented after h to avoid confusion; e.g. _h_ 70 denotes _H_ 0 = 70 _h_ 70([km/s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metre_per_second "Metre per second"))/[Mpc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parsec#Megaparsecs_and_gigaparsecs "Parsec"), which implies _h_ 70 = _h_ / 0.7.

This should not be confused with the [dimensionless value](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimensionless_physical_constant "Dimensionless physical constant") of Hubble's constant, usually expressed in terms of [Planck units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_units "Planck units"), obtained by multiplying _H_ 0 by 1.75×10−63 (from definitions of parsec and [_t_ P](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_time "Planck time")), for example for _H_ 0 = 70, a Planck unit version of 1.2×10−61 is obtained.

### Acceleration of the expansion

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hubble%27s_law&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Acceleration of the expansion")]

A value for q measured from [standard candle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_candle "Standard candle") observations of [Type Ia supernovae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_Ia_supernova "Type Ia supernova"), which was determined in 1998 to be negative, surprised many astronomers with the implication that the expansion of the universe is currently "accelerating"[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-48) (although the Hubble factor is still decreasing with time, as mentioned above in the [Interpretation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#Interpretation) section; see the articles on [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy") and the ΛCDM model).

Derivation of the Hubble parameter
----------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hubble%27s_law&action=edit&section=22 "Edit section: Derivation of the Hubble parameter")]

Start with the [Friedmann equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedmann_equations "Friedmann equations"):

![Image 25: {\displaystyle H^{2}\equiv \left({\frac {\dot {a}}{a}}\right)^{2}={\frac {8\pi G}{3}}\rho -{\frac {kc^{2}}{a^{2}}}+{\frac {\Lambda c^{2}}{3}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d899f1472a9c11883159831aeb1d02b2bf941d51)

where H is the Hubble parameter, a is the [scale factor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scale_factor_(universe) "Scale factor (universe)"), G is the [gravitational constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_constant "Gravitational constant"), k is the normalised spatial curvature of the universe and equal to −1, 0, or 1, and Λ is the cosmological constant.

where _ρ_ _m_ 0 is the density of matter today. From the Friedmann equation and thermodynamic principles we know for non-relativistic particles that their mass density decreases proportional to the inverse volume of the universe, so the equation above must be true. Units derived from the Hubble constant
--------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hubble%27s_law&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: Units derived from the Hubble constant")]

The Hubble constant _H_ 0 has units of inverse time; the **Hubble time**t H is simply defined as the inverse of the Hubble constant,[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-51) i.e.

![Image 40: {\displaystyle t_{H}\equiv {\frac {1}{H_{0}}}={\frac {1}{67.8\mathrm {~(km/s)/Mpc} }}=4.55\times 10^{17}\mathrm {~s} =14.4{\text{ billion years}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3b33e46e5e9df707814f3a7b751e70abe478d392)

This is slightly different from the [age of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_the_universe "Age of the universe"), which is approximately 13.8 billion years. The Hubble time is the age it would have had if the expansion had been linear,[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-52) and it is different from the real age of the universe because the expansion is not linear; it depends on the energy content of the universe (see [§Derivation of the Hubble parameter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#Derivation_of_the_Hubble_parameter)).

We currently appear to be approaching a period where the expansion of the universe is exponential due to the increasing dominance of [vacuum energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuum_energy "Vacuum energy"). In this regime, the Hubble parameter is constant, and the universe grows by a factor [e](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E_(mathematical_constant) "E (mathematical constant)") each Hubble time:

![Image 41: {\displaystyle H\equiv {\frac {\dot {a}}{a}}={\textrm {constant}}\quad \Longrightarrow \quad a\propto e^{Ht}=e^{\frac {t}{t_{H}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d99e523759010972346c125777b6e84e739881f4)

Likewise, the generally accepted value of 2.27[Es](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exa- "Exa-")−1 means that (at the current rate) the universe would grow by a factor of e 2.27 in one [exasecond](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Exasecond "Exasecond").

Over long periods of time, the dynamics are complicated by general relativity, dark energy, [inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflation_(cosmology) "Inflation (cosmology)"), etc., as explained above.

The Hubble length or Hubble distance is a unit of distance in cosmology, defined as _cH_−1 — the speed of light multiplied by the Hubble time. It is equivalent to 4,420 million parsecs or 14.4 billion light years. (The numerical value of the Hubble length in light years is, by definition, equal to that of the Hubble time in years.) Substituting _D_ = _cH_−1 into the equation for Hubble's law, _v_ = _H_ 0 _D_ reveals that the Hubble distance specifies the distance from our location to those galaxies which are _currently_ receding from us at the speed of light.

The Hubble volume is sometimes defined as a volume of the universe with a [comoving](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comoving_and_proper_distances "Comoving and proper distances") size of _cH_−1. The exact definition varies: it is sometimes defined as the volume of a sphere with radius _cH_−1, or alternatively, a cube of side _cH_−1. Some cosmologists even use the term Hubble volume to refer to the volume of the [observable universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Observable_universe "Observable universe"), although this has a radius approximately three times larger.

Determining the Hubble constant
-------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hubble%27s_law&action=edit&section=29 "Edit section: Determining the Hubble constant")]

[![Image 42](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/b7/Recent_Hubble%27s_Constant_Values.png/250px-Recent_Hubble%27s_Constant_Values.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Recent_Hubble%27s_Constant_Values.png)

The value of the Hubble constant in (km/s)/Mpc, including measurement uncertainty, for recent surveys[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-planck_overview-53)

The value of the Hubble constant, _H_ 0, cannot be measured directly, but is derived from a combination of astronomical observations and model-dependent assumptions. Increasingly accurate observations and new models over many decades have led to two sets of highly precise values which do not agree. This difference is known as the "Hubble tension".[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-NYT-20170220-8)[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-VerdeReview2024-54)

### Earlier measurements

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hubble%27s_law&action=edit&section=30 "Edit section: Earlier measurements")]

For the original 1929 estimate of the constant now bearing his name, Hubble used observations of [Cepheid variable](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cepheid_variable "Cepheid variable") stars as "[standard candles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_distance_ladder#Standard_candles "Cosmic distance ladder")" to measure distance.[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Allen-55) The result he obtained was 500(km/s)/Mpc, much larger than the value astronomers currently calculate. Later observations by astronomer [Walter Baade](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Walter_Baade "Walter Baade") led him to realize that there were distinct "[populations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_population "Stellar population")" for stars (Population I and Population II) in a galaxy. The same observations led him to discover that there are two types of Cepheid variable stars with different luminosities. Using this discovery, he recalculated Hubble constant and the size of the known universe, doubling the previous calculation made by Hubble in 1929.[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-56)[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-57)[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Allen-55) He announced this finding to considerable astonishment at the 1952 meeting of the [International Astronomical Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Astronomical_Union "International Astronomical Union") in Rome.

For most of the second half of the 20th century, the value of _H_ 0 was estimated to be between 50 and 90(km/s)/Mpc.

The value of the Hubble constant was the topic of a long and rather bitter controversy between [Gérard de Vaucouleurs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_de_Vaucouleurs "Gérard de Vaucouleurs"), who claimed the value was around 100, and [Allan Sandage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allan_Sandage "Allan Sandage"), who claimed the value was near 50.[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Overbye-58) In one demonstration of vitriol between the parties, when Sandage and his colleague [Gustav Andreas Tammann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustav_Andreas_Tammann "Gustav Andreas Tammann") formally acknowledged the shortcomings of confirming the systematic error of their method in 1975, Vaucouleurs responded: "It is unfortunate that this sober warning was so soon forgotten and ignored by most astronomers and textbook writers".[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-:0-59) In 1996, a debate moderated by [John Bahcall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_N._Bahcall "John N. Bahcall") between Sidney van den Bergh and Gustav Tammann was held in similar fashion to the earlier Shapley–Curtis debate over these two competing values.

This previously wide variance in estimates was partially resolved with the introduction of the [ΛCDM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model "Lambda-CDM model") model of the universe in the late 1990s. Incorporating the ΛCDM model, observations of high-redshift clusters at X-ray and microwave wavelengths using the [Sunyaev–Zel'dovich effect](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sunyaev%E2%80%93Zel%27dovich_effect "Sunyaev–Zel'dovich effect"), measurements of anisotropies in the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background") radiation, and optical surveys all gave a value of around 50–70 km/s/Mpc for the constant.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Myers1999-60)

### Precision cosmology and the Hubble tension

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hubble%27s_law&action=edit&section=31 "Edit section: Precision cosmology and the Hubble tension")]

By the late 1990s, advances in ideas and technology allowed higher precision measurements.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Turner-2022-61) However, two major categories of methods, each with high precision, fail to agree. "Late universe" measurements using calibrated distance ladder techniques have converged on a value of approximately 73(km/s)/Mpc. Since 2000, "early universe" techniques based on measurements of the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background") have become available, and these agree on a value near 67.7(km/s)/Mpc.[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-62) (This accounts for the change in the expansion rate since the early universe, so is comparable to the first number.) Initially, this discrepancy was within the estimated [measurement uncertainties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measurement_uncertainty "Measurement uncertainty") and thus no cause for concern. However, as techniques have improved, the estimated measurement uncertainties have shrunk, but the discrepancies have _not_, to the point that the disagreement is now highly [statistically significant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistically_significant "Statistically significant"). This discrepancy is called the **Hubble tension**.[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-LS-20190826-63)[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-di_Valentino_2021_153001-64)

An example of an "early" measurement, the [Planck mission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft) "Planck (spacecraft)") published in 2018 gives a value for _H_ 0 = of 67.4±0.5(km/s)/Mpc.[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-2018planckcosmos-65) In the "late" camp is the higher value of 74.03±1.42(km/s)/Mpc determined by the [Hubble Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope "Hubble Space Telescope")[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-SA2019-66) and confirmed by the [James Webb Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/James_Webb_Space_Telescope "James Webb Space Telescope") in 2023.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-67)[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-68) The "early" and "late" measurements disagree at the >5 [_σ_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Standard_deviation "Standard deviation") level, beyond a plausible level of chance.[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Riess2019-69)[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-70) The resolution to this disagreement is an ongoing area of active research.[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-71)

### Reducing systematic errors

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hubble%27s_law&action=edit&section=32 "Edit section: Reducing systematic errors")]

Since 2013, extensive checks for possible systematic errors and improvements in reproducibility have been undertaken.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-VerdeReview2024-54)

The "late universe" or distance ladder measurements typically employ three stages or "rungs". In the first rung, distances to [Cepheids](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cepheids "Cepheids") are determined while trying to reduce luminosity errors from dust and correlations of [metallicity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metallicity "Metallicity") with luminosity. The second rung uses [Type Ia supernova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_Ia_supernova "Type Ia supernova"), explosions of almost constant amounts of mass. Thusly, these produce very similar amounts of light; the primary systematic error in this case is the limited number of objects that can be observed. The third rung of the distance ladder measures the red-shift of supernovae to extract the Hubble flow, and from that the constant. At this rung, corrections due to [motion other than expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Peculiar_velocity "Peculiar velocity") are applied.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-VerdeReview2024-54): 2.1  As an example of the kind of work needed to reduce systematic errors, photometry on observations from the James Webb Space Telescope of extra-galactic Cepheids confirm the findings from the HST. The higher resolution avoided confusion from crowding of stars in the field of view but came to the same value for H 0.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-72)[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-VerdeReview2024-54)

The "early universe" or inverse distance ladder measures the observable consequences of spherical sound waves on primordial plasma density. These pressure waves – called [baryon acoustic oscillations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon_acoustic_oscillations "Baryon acoustic oscillations") (BAO) – ceased once the universe cooled enough for electrons to stay bound to nuclei, ending the plasma and allowing the photons trapped by interaction with the plasma to escape. The subsequent pressure waves are evident in very small perturbations in the density imprinted on the cosmic microwave background, and on the large-scale density of galaxies across the sky. Detailed structure in high-precision measurements of the CMB can be matched to physics models of the oscillations. These models depend upon the Hubble constant such that a match reveals a value for the constant. Similarly, the BAO affects the statistical distribution of matter, observed as distant galaxies across the sky.

These two independent measurements produce similar values for the constant from the current models, giving strong evidence that systematic errors in the measurements themselves do not affect the result.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-VerdeReview2024-54): Sup. B

### Other kinds of measurements

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hubble%27s_law&action=edit&section=33 "Edit section: Other kinds of measurements")]

In addition to measurements based on calibrated distance ladder techniques or measurements of the CMB, other methods have been used to determine the Hubble constant.

One alternative method for constraining the Hubble constant involves transient events seen in multiple images of a [strongly lensed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Strong_gravitational_lensing "Strong gravitational lensing") object. A transient event, such as a supernova, is seen at different times in each of the lensed images, and if this _time delay_ between each image can be measured, it can be used to constrain the Hubble constant. This method is commonly known as "time-delay cosmography", and was first proposed by [Refsdal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sjur_Refsdal "Sjur Refsdal") in 1964,[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-73) years before the first strongly lensed object was observed. The first strongly lensed supernova to be discovered was named [SN Refsdal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SN_Refsdal "SN Refsdal") in his honor. While Refsdal suggested this could be done with supernovae, he also noted that extremely luminous and distant star-like objects could also be used. These objects were later named [quasars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quasar "Quasar"), and to date (April 2025) the majority of time-delay cosmography measurements have been done with strongly lensed quasars. This is because current samples of lensed quasars vastly outnumber known lensed supernovae, of which <10 are known. This is expected to change dramatically in the next few years, with surveys such as [LSST](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vera_C._Rubin_Observatory "Vera C. Rubin Observatory") expected to discover ~10 lensed SNe in the first three years of observation.[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-74) For example time-delay constraints on H0, see the results from STRIDES and H0LiCOW in the table below.

In October 2018, scientists used information from [gravitational wave](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_wave "Gravitational wave") events (especially those involving the [merger of neutron stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_star_merger "Neutron star merger"), like [GW170817](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/GW170817 "GW170817")), of determining the Hubble constant.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-PHYS-20181022-75)[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-NAT-20181017-76)

In July 2019, astronomers reported that a new method to determine the Hubble constant, and resolve the discrepancy of earlier methods, has been proposed based on the mergers of pairs of [neutron stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_star "Neutron star"), following the detection of the neutron star merger of GW170817, an event known as a [dark siren](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_siren "Dark siren").[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-EA-20190708-77)[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-NRAO-20190708-78) Their measurement of the Hubble constant is 73.3+5.3

−5.0 (km/s)/Mpc.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-NAT-20190708-79)

Also in July 2019, astronomers reported another new method, using data from the [Hubble Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope "Hubble Space Telescope") and based on distances to [red giant stars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Red_giant "Red giant") calculated using the [tip of the red-giant branch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tip_of_the_red-giant_branch "Tip of the red-giant branch") (TRGB) distance indicator. Their measurement of the Hubble constant is 69.8+1.9

−1.9(km/s)/Mpc.[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-EA-20190716-80)[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-SCI-20190719-81)[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-The_Carnegie-Chicago_Hubble_Program-82)

In February 2020, the Megamaser Cosmology Project published independent results based on [astrophysical masers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_masers "Astrophysical masers") visible at cosmological distances and which do not require multi-step calibration. That work confirmed the distance ladder results and differed from the early-universe results at a statistical significance level of 95%.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-megamaser-83)

In July 2020, measurements of the cosmic background radiation by the [Atacama Cosmology Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Atacama_Cosmology_Telescope "Atacama Cosmology Telescope") predict that the Universe should be expanding more slowly than is currently observed.[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-84)

In July 2023, an independent estimate of the Hubble constant was derived from a [kilonova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kilonova "Kilonova"), the optical afterglow of a [neutron star merger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neutron_star_merger "Neutron star merger"), using the [expanding photosphere method](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expanding_photosphere_method "Expanding photosphere method").[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-aanda.org-85) Due to the blackbody nature of early kilonova spectra,[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-86) such systems provide strongly constraining estimators of cosmic distance. Using the kilonova [AT2017gfo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AT2017gfo "AT2017gfo") (the aftermath of, once again, GW170817), these measurements indicate a local-estimate of the Hubble constant of 67.0±3.6(km/s)/Mpc.[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-nature.com-87)[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-aanda.org-85)

[![Image 44](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/06/Hubbleconstants_color.png/960px-Hubbleconstants_color.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hubbleconstants_color.png)

Estimates in black represent calibrated distance ladder measurements which tend to cluster around 73(km/s)/Mpc; red represents early universe CMB/BAO measurements with ΛCDM parameters which show good agreement on a figure near 67(km/s)/Mpc, while blue are other techniques, whose uncertainties are not yet small enough to decide between the two.

### Possible resolutions of the Hubble tension

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hubble%27s_law&action=edit&section=34 "Edit section: Possible resolutions of the Hubble tension")]

The cause of the Hubble tension is unknown,[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-88) and there are many possible proposed solutions. The most conservative is that there is an unknown systematic error affecting either early-universe or late-universe observations. Although intuitively appealing, this explanation requires multiple unrelated effects regardless of whether early-universe or late-universe observations are incorrect, and there are no obvious candidates. Furthermore, any such systematic error would need to affect multiple different instruments, since both the early-universe and late-universe observations come from several different telescopes.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-VerdeReview2024-54)

Alternatively, it could be that the observations are correct, but some unaccounted-for effect is causing the discrepancy. If the [cosmological principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_principle "Cosmological principle") fails (see [Lambda-CDM model §Violations of the cosmological principle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model#Violations_of_the_cosmological_principle "Lambda-CDM model")), then the existing interpretations of the Hubble constant and the Hubble tension have to be revised, which might resolve the Hubble tension.[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Snowmass21-89) In particular, we would need to be located within a very large void, up to about a redshift of 0.5, for such an explanation to conflate with supernovae and [baryon acoustic oscillation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon_acoustic_oscillation "Baryon acoustic oscillation") observations.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-di_Valentino_2021_153001-64) Yet another possibility is that the uncertainties in the measurements could have been underestimated, but given the internal agreements this is neither likely, nor resolves the overall tension.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-VerdeReview2024-54)

Finally, another possibility is new physics beyond the currently accepted cosmological model of the universe, the [ΛCDM model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model "Lambda-CDM model").[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-di_Valentino_2021_153001-64)[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-90) There are very many theories in this category, for example, replacing general relativity with [a modified theory of gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modified_Newtonian_dynamics "Modified Newtonian dynamics") could potentially resolve the tension,[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Haslbauer-91)[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-Mazurenko-92) as can a dark energy component in the early universe,[[b]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-93)[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-94) dark energy with a time-varying [equation of state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equation_of_state_(cosmology) "Equation of state (cosmology)"),[[c]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-95)[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-96) or [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter") that decays into dark radiation.[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-97) A problem faced by all these theories is that both early-universe and late-universe measurements rely on multiple independent lines of physics, and it is difficult to modify any of those lines while preserving their successes elsewhere. The scale of the challenge can be seen from how some authors have argued that new early-universe physics alone is not sufficient;[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-98)[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-99) while other authors argue that new late-universe physics alone is also not sufficient.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-100) Nonetheless, astronomers are trying, with interest in the Hubble tension growing strongly since the mid 2010s.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble's_law#cite_note-di_Valentino_2021_153001-64)
