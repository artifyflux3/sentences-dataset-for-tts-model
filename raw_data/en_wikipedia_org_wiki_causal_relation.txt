In [mathematical physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_physics "Mathematical physics"), the **causal structure** of a [Lorentzian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentzian_manifold "Lorentzian manifold") describes the possible [causal relationships](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causality_(physics) "Causality (physics)") between points in the manifold. Lorentzian manifolds can be classified according to the types of causal structures they admit (_[causality conditions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causality\_conditions "Causality conditions")_).

In [modern physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modern_physics "Modern physics") (especially [general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity")) [spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spacetime "Spacetime") is represented by a [Lorentzian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentzian_manifold "Lorentzian manifold"). The causal relations between points in the manifold are interpreted as describing which events in spacetime can influence which other events.

The causal structure of an arbitrary (possibly curved) Lorentzian manifold is made more complicated by the presence of [curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature "Curvature"). Discussions of the causal structure for such manifolds must be phrased in terms of [smooth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_function "Smooth function")[curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curve "Curve") joining pairs of points. Conditions on the [tangent vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_vectors "Tangent vectors") of the curves then define the causal relationships.

A tangent vector ![Image 5: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is:

Here we use the ![Image 6: {\displaystyle (-,+,+,+,\cdots )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/05f2be14b3c312489e85a71ab1c2afe9a1bb5ebe)[metric signature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_signature "Metric signature"). We say that a tangent vector is **non-spacelike** if it is null or timelike.

The canonical Lorentzian manifold is [Minkowski spacetime](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_spacetime "Minkowski spacetime"), where ![Image 7: {\displaystyle M=\mathbb {R} ^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5cdbbb611f6440d3f1da47525bcf154c9c8bd304) and ![Image 8: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) is the [flat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Curvature_of_space "Curvature of space")[Minkowski metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_metric "Minkowski metric"). The names for the tangent vectors come from the physics of this model. The causal relationships between points in Minkowski spacetime take a particularly simple form because the tangent space is also ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{4}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4abb9b9dab94f7b25a4210364f0f9032704bfb9) and hence the tangent vectors may be identified with points in the space. The four-dimensional vector ![Image 10: {\displaystyle X=(t,r)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/20674e6ed582b59319df38949b8880751150d86a) is classified according to the sign of ![Image 11: {\displaystyle g(X,X)=-c^{2}t^{2}+\|r\|^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b886a565d15f0132564e0cb9d17c4d5495d428be), where ![Image 12: {\displaystyle r\in \mathbb {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ea34913a6608d4a8746de837c91e6854cda360a0) is a [Cartesian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") coordinate in 3-dimensional space, ![Image 13: {\displaystyle c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a67b81c2de995bd608d5b2df50cd8cd7d92455) is the constant representing the universal speed limit, and ![Image 14: {\displaystyle t}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/65658b7b223af9e1acc877d848888ecdb4466560) is time. The classification of any vector in the space will be the same in all frames of reference that are related by a [Lorentz transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentz_transformation "Lorentz transformation") (but not by a general [Poincaré transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Poincar%C3%A9_transformation "Poincaré transformation") because the origin may then be displaced) because of the invariance of the metric.

At each point in ![Image 15: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) the timelike tangent vectors in the point's [tangent space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tangent_space "Tangent space") can be divided into two classes. To do this we first define an [equivalence relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_relation "Equivalence relation") on pairs of timelike tangent vectors.

If ![Image 16: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and ![Image 17: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) are two timelike tangent vectors at a point we say that ![Image 18: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) and ![Image 19: {\displaystyle Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/961d67d6b454b4df2301ac571808a3538b3a6d3f) are equivalent (written ![Image 20: {\displaystyle X\sim Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a5d68baf65b1398a52e9a21732e8e2a2fc39244b)) if ![Image 21: {\displaystyle \,g(X,Y)<0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8837b9c6186585e350bcf9f3de8ae6c7513a00e1).

There are then two [equivalence classes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_class "Equivalence class") which between them contain all timelike tangent vectors at the point. We can (arbitrarily) call one of these equivalence classes **future-directed** and call the other **past-directed**. Physically this designation of the two classes of future- and past-directed timelike vectors corresponds to a choice of an [arrow of time](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arrow_of_time "Arrow of time") at the point. The future- and past-directed designations can be extended to null vectors at a point by continuity.

A [Lorentzian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lorentzian_manifold "Lorentzian manifold") is **time-orientable**[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causal_relation#cite_note-1) if a continuous designation of future-directed and past-directed for non-spacelike vectors can be made over the entire manifold.

A **path** in ![Image 22: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is a [continuous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_function#Parametric_continuity "Smooth function") map ![Image 23: {\displaystyle \mu :\Sigma \to M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/76fb79282afacb82d9895c96bdf73a9ef1d5adcd) where ![Image 24: {\displaystyle \Sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e1f558f53cda207614abdf90162266c70bc5c1e) is a nondegenerate interval (i.e., a connected set containing more than one point) in ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/786849c765da7a84dbc3cce43e96aad58a5868dc). A **smooth** path has ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \mu }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9fd47b2a39f7a7856952afec1f1db72c67af6161) differentiable an appropriate number of times (typically ![Image 27: {\displaystyle C^{\infty }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/971ed05871d69309df32efdfd2020128c9cf69d8)), and a **regular** path has nonvanishing derivative.

A **curve** in ![Image 28: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is the image of a path or, more properly, an equivalence class of path-images related by re-parametrisation, i.e. [homeomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeomorphism "Homeomorphism") or [diffeomorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffeomorphism "Diffeomorphism") of ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \Sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e1f558f53cda207614abdf90162266c70bc5c1e). When ![Image 30: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is time-orientable, the curve is **oriented** if the parameter change is required to be [monotonic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monotonic_function "Monotonic function").

Smooth regular curves (or paths) in ![Image 31: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) can be classified depending on their tangent vectors. Such a curve is

*   **chronological** (or **timelike**) if the tangent vector is timelike at all points in the curve. Also called a [world line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_line "World line").[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causal_relation#cite_note-2)
*   **null** if the tangent vector is null at all points in the curve.
*   **spacelike** if the tangent vector is spacelike at all points in the curve.
*   **causal** (or **non-spacelike**) if the tangent vector is timelike _or_ null at all points in the curve.

The requirements of regularity and nondegeneracy of ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \Sigma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e1f558f53cda207614abdf90162266c70bc5c1e) ensure that closed causal curves (such as those consisting of a single point) are not automatically admitted by all spacetimes.

If the manifold is time-orientable then the non-spacelike curves can further be classified depending on their orientation with respect to time.

A chronological, null or causal curve in ![Image 33: {\displaystyle M}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f82cade9898ced02fdd08712e5f0c0151758a0dd) is

*   **future-directed** if, for every point in the curve, the tangent vector is future-directed.
*   **past-directed** if, for every point in the curve, the tangent vector is past-directed.

These definitions only apply to causal (chronological or null) curves because only timelike or null tangent vectors can be assigned an orientation with respect to time.

*   A [closed timelike curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_timelike_curve "Closed timelike curve") is a closed curve which is everywhere future-directed timelike (or everywhere past-directed timelike).
*   A **closed null curve** is a closed curve which is everywhere future-directed null (or everywhere past-directed null).
*   The [holonomy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holonomy "Holonomy") of the ratio of the rate of change of the affine parameter around a closed null geodesic is the **redshift factor**.

[Topological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology") properties:

Two metrics ![Image 62: {\displaystyle \,g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/98bd980ef3755427fdcb153b4b37eb38522a7429) and ![Image 63: {\displaystyle {\hat {g}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9351259e90225fb86e6d884862bd7ccc579db8ee) are **conformally related**[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causal_relation#cite_note-8) if ![Image 64: {\displaystyle {\hat {g}}=\Omega ^{2}g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b37b9c916239b689dd1b3816a6b836463c107ace) for some real function ![Image 65: {\displaystyle \Omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/24b0d5ca6f381068d756f6337c08e0af9d1eeb6f) called the **conformal factor**. (See [conformal map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformal_map#Riemannian_geometry "Conformal map")).

Looking at the definitions of which tangent vectors are timelike, null and spacelike we see they remain unchanged if we use ![Image 66: {\displaystyle \,g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/98bd980ef3755427fdcb153b4b37eb38522a7429) or ![Image 67: {\displaystyle {\hat {g}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9351259e90225fb86e6d884862bd7ccc579db8ee). As an example suppose ![Image 68: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is a timelike tangent vector with respect to the ![Image 69: {\displaystyle \,g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/98bd980ef3755427fdcb153b4b37eb38522a7429) metric. This means that ![Image 70: {\displaystyle \,g(X,X)<0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf2530170ccfa707b210e39534f0936b8e55f937). We then have that ![Image 71: {\displaystyle {\hat {g}}(X,X)=\Omega ^{2}g(X,X)<0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51103da00719e8c468a4814fdb831dd53f0e8e91) so ![Image 72: {\displaystyle X}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/68baa052181f707c662844a465bfeeb135e82bab) is a timelike tangent vector with respect to the ![Image 73: {\displaystyle {\hat {g}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9351259e90225fb86e6d884862bd7ccc579db8ee) too.

It follows from this that the causal structure of a Lorentzian manifold is unaffected by a [conformal transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformal_transformation "Conformal transformation").

A null geodesic remains a null geodesic under a conformal rescaling.

An infinite metric admits geodesics of infinite length/proper time. However, we can sometimes make a conformal rescaling of the metric with a conformal factor which falls off sufficiently fast to 0 as we approach infinity to get the **conformal boundary** of the manifold. The topological structure of the conformal boundary depends upon the causal structure.

In various spaces:

*   [Minkowski space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_space "Minkowski space"): ![Image 74: {\displaystyle i^{\pm }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cc056ef8d7fac81156b87b11e956fe08f697780d) are points, ℐ± are null sheets, and spacelike infinity has [codimension](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codimension "Codimension") 2.
*   [Anti-de Sitter space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-de_Sitter_space "Anti-de Sitter space"): there's no timelike or null infinity, and spacelike infinity has codimension 1.
*   [de Sitter space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De_Sitter_space "De Sitter space"): the future and past timelike infinity has codimension 1.

Gravitational singularity
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Causal_structure&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Gravitational singularity")]

A geodesic is called _extendible_ if there exists a point ![Image 75: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) such that, for every neighborhood ![Image 76: {\displaystyle O}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d70e1d0d87e2ef1092ea1ffe2923d9933ff18fc) of ![Image 77: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36), there exists a value ![Image 78: {\displaystyle t_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/02d3006c4190b1939b04d9b9bb21006fb4e6fa4a) such that ![Image 79: {\displaystyle \gamma (t)\in O}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c090c142463fba18f1e4f98d861db731e202b050) for all ![Image 80: {\displaystyle t>t_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/41e02e9ea5fe42219433e4140c99ee5235a956fd). Otherwise, the geodesic is _inextendible_. A geodesic is said to be _complete_ if its affine parameter can be extended to both ![Image 81: {\displaystyle +\infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bddbb0e4420a7e744cf71bd71216e11b0bf88831) and ![Image 82: {\displaystyle -\infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca2608c4b5fd3bffc73585f8c67e379b4e99b6f1).[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causal_relation#cite_note-Reall-9)

A spacetime manifold is geodesically complete if every inextendible causal geodesic is complete. If at least one inextendible causal geodesic is incomplete, then the spacetime is said to be geodesically incomplete. If the spacetime manifold itself can be extended (i.e., it is extendible as a differentiable manifold), it must also be geodesically incomplete. The manifold is said to have a _singularity_ if the spacetime is both geodesically incomplete and inextendible as a manifold.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causal_relation#cite_note-Reall-9)[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Causal_relation#cite_note-10)

*   For [black holes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Black_hole "Black hole"), the future timelike boundary ends on a [gravitational singularity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_singularity "Gravitational singularity") in some places.
*   For the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang"), the past timelike boundary is also a singularity.

The [absolute event horizon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Absolute_horizon "Absolute horizon") is the past null cone of the future timelike infinity. It is generated by null geodesics which obey the [Raychaudhuri optical equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raychaudhuri_equation "Raychaudhuri equation").
