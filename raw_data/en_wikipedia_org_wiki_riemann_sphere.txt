The Riemann sphere can be visualized as the complex number plane wrapped around a sphere (by some form of [stereographic projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stereographic_projection "Stereographic projection") – details are given below).

[![Image 2](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/32/Riemann_sphere1.svg/250px-Riemann_sphere1.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Riemann_sphere1.svg)

[Stereographic projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stereographic_projection "Stereographic projection") of [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number")![Image 3: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3) and ![Image 4: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a) onto the points ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) and ![Image 6: {\displaystyle \beta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ed48a5e36207156fb792fa79d29925d2f7901e8) of the **Riemann sphere** - connect ![Image 7: {\displaystyle P(\infty )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/445968f47626247cb80a2201dc239c891db83781) with A or B by a line and determine the intersection with the sphere.

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **Riemann sphere**, named after [Bernhard Riemann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bernhard_Riemann "Bernhard Riemann"), [[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_sphere#cite_note-FOOTNOTERiemann1857-1) is a [model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_model "Mathematical model") of the **extended complex plane** (also called the **closed complex plane**): the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane") plus one [point at infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Point_at_infinity "Point at infinity"). This extended plane represents the **extended complex numbers**, that is, the [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number") plus a value ![Image 8: {\displaystyle \infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c26c105004f30c27aa7c2a9c601550a4183b1f21) for [infinity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Infinity "Infinity"). With the Riemann model, the point ![Image 9: {\displaystyle \infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c26c105004f30c27aa7c2a9c601550a4183b1f21) is near to very large numbers, just as the point ![Image 10: {\displaystyle 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2aae8864a3c1fec9585261791a809ddec1489950) is near to very small numbers.

The extended complex numbers are useful in [complex analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_analysis "Complex analysis") because they allow for [division by zero](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_by_zero "Division by zero") in some circumstances, in a way that makes expressions such as ![Image 11: {\displaystyle 1/0=\infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/93f961b4cd18e46d2b2c55706698397151ae7bd7)[well-behaved](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Well-behaved "Well-behaved"). For example, any [rational function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_function "Rational function") on the complex plane can be extended to a [holomorphic function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holomorphic_function "Holomorphic function") on the Riemann sphere, with the [poles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pole_(complex_analysis) "Pole (complex analysis)") of the rational function mapping to infinity. More generally, any [meromorphic function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meromorphic_function "Meromorphic function") can be thought of as a holomorphic function whose [codomain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Codomain "Codomain") is the Riemann sphere.

In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), the Riemann sphere is the prototypical example of a [Riemann surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_surface "Riemann surface"), and is one of the simplest [complex manifolds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_manifold "Complex manifold"). In [projective geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_geometry "Projective geometry"), the sphere is an example of a [complex projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_projective_space "Complex projective space") and can be thought of as the **complex [projective line](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_line "Projective line")**![Image 12: {\displaystyle \mathbf {P} ^{1}(\mathbf {C} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8ad36aa52f587ebbcbdd22ef95ca901faca2fa6), the [projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_space "Projective space") of all [complex lines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_line "Complex line") in ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \mathbf {C} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b977492b76f197efebf8bceb399814966be79c35). As with any [compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_space "Compact space") Riemann surface, the sphere may also be viewed as a projective [algebraic curve](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_curve "Algebraic curve"), making it a fundamental example in [algebraic geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_geometry "Algebraic geometry"). It also finds utility in other disciplines that depend on analysis and geometry, such as the [Bloch sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bloch_sphere "Bloch sphere") of [quantum mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_mechanics "Quantum mechanics") and in other [branches of physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Branches_of_physics "Branches of physics").

Extended complex numbers
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Riemann_sphere&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Extended complex numbers")]

The **extended complex numbers** consist of the complex numbers ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7) together with ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c26c105004f30c27aa7c2a9c601550a4183b1f21). The set of extended complex numbers may be written as ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} \cup \{\infty \}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eb61866ff76f7d364846c91f6840957a946d6525), and is often denoted by adding some decoration to the letter ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9add4085095b9b6d28d045fd9c92c2c09f549a7), such as

![Image 18: {\displaystyle {\widehat {\mathbb {C} }},\quad {\overline {\mathbb {C} }},\quad {\text{or}}\quad \mathbb {C} _{\infty }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/701dfc37f83bf36521960c011acc4d7e15d3c9fd)
The notation ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} ^{*}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/12e4a3fc8042b1c73d31cfe579c92d34f1255137) has also seen use, but as this notation is also used for the punctured plane ![Image 20: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} \setminus \{0\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/91caa89c0163b2f76279b22233ea55460f82a63b), it can lead to ambiguity.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_sphere#cite_note-2)

Geometrically, the set of extended complex numbers is referred to as the **Riemann sphere** (or **extended complex plane**).

Any [rational function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_function "Rational function")![Image 37: {\displaystyle f(z)=g(z)/h(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/85111b5263ed0b27c792ebdaf2b42fdf299405af) (in other words, ![Image 38: {\displaystyle f(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d8dd568d570b390c337c0a911f0a1c5c214e8240) is the ratio of polynomial functions ![Image 39: {\displaystyle g(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8eb6fb9c0f0d402f5ebbebfd5e34bedd39a4a52b) and ![Image 40: {\displaystyle h(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eb5239f31e57a3120e928486857a5ea8fb8eee80) of ![Image 41: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98) with complex coefficients, such that ![Image 42: {\displaystyle g(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8eb6fb9c0f0d402f5ebbebfd5e34bedd39a4a52b) and ![Image 43: {\displaystyle h(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eb5239f31e57a3120e928486857a5ea8fb8eee80) have no common factor) can be extended to a [continuous function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continuous_function "Continuous function") on the Riemann sphere. Specifically, if ![Image 44: {\displaystyle z_{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9e72d1d86e86355892b39b8eb32b964834e113bf) is a complex number such that the denominator ![Image 45: {\displaystyle h(z_{0})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/949384006f08505cae17a7aae7cd97bfbb174978) is zero but the numerator ![Image 46: {\displaystyle g(z_{0})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c6add98f5a34471c0f35af5a13925e08be5c47c9) is nonzero, then ![Image 47: {\displaystyle f(z_{0})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/73cfc1c8984281badb19cb819c8f14b6bb69010b) can be defined as ![Image 48: {\displaystyle \infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c26c105004f30c27aa7c2a9c601550a4183b1f21). Moreover, ![Image 49: {\displaystyle f(\infty )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/98ce6c8773ad320f77fa2b0867bb3900ec7b7153) can be defined as the [limit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Limit_of_a_function "Limit of a function") of ![Image 50: {\displaystyle f(z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d8dd568d570b390c337c0a911f0a1c5c214e8240) as ![Image 51: {\displaystyle z\to \infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/380ee74715fed4cd83f76bacac1add6aad2d58c3), which may be finite or infinite.

The set of complex rational functions—whose mathematical symbol is ![Image 52: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} (z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5917f17c3d7c66273521f830ef30a8950703188c)—form all possible [holomorphic functions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holomorphic_function "Holomorphic function") from the Riemann sphere to itself, when it is viewed as a [Riemann surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_surface "Riemann surface"), except for the constant function taking the value ![Image 53: {\displaystyle \infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c26c105004f30c27aa7c2a9c601550a4183b1f21) everywhere. The functions of ![Image 54: {\displaystyle \mathbb {C} (z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5917f17c3d7c66273521f830ef30a8950703188c) form an algebraic field, known as _the field of rational functions on the sphere_.

As a complex manifold
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Riemann_sphere&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: As a complex manifold")]

As a one-dimensional [complex manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_manifold "Complex manifold"), the Riemann sphere can be described by two [charts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chart_(topology) "Chart (topology)"), both with domain equal to the complex number plane ![Image 62: {\displaystyle \mathbf {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11de80478fce9090e43eed19100b37cc841661e8). Let ![Image 63: {\displaystyle \zeta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5c3916703cae7938143d38865f78f27faadd4ae) be a complex number in one copy of ![Image 64: {\displaystyle \mathbf {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11de80478fce9090e43eed19100b37cc841661e8), and let ![Image 65: {\displaystyle \xi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0b461aaf61091abd5d2c808931c48b8ff9647db) be a complex number in another copy of ![Image 66: {\displaystyle \mathbf {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11de80478fce9090e43eed19100b37cc841661e8). Identify each nonzero complex number ![Image 67: {\displaystyle \zeta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5c3916703cae7938143d38865f78f27faadd4ae) of the first ![Image 68: {\displaystyle \mathbf {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11de80478fce9090e43eed19100b37cc841661e8) with the nonzero complex number ![Image 69: {\displaystyle 1/\xi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/174ddef5beab4ed15d9fad4774a6691932869fb9) of the second ![Image 70: {\displaystyle \mathbf {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11de80478fce9090e43eed19100b37cc841661e8). Then the map

![Image 71: {\displaystyle f(z)={\frac {1}{z}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/77cdcc0bfc31f43fededf1e77226f87ae35abb34)
is called the [transition map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transition_map "Transition map") between the two copies of ![Image 72: {\displaystyle \mathbf {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11de80478fce9090e43eed19100b37cc841661e8)—the so-called charts—glueing them together. Since the transition maps are [holomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Holomorphic_function "Holomorphic function"), they define a complex manifold, called the **Riemann sphere**. As a complex manifold of 1 complex dimension (i.e. 2 real dimensions), this is also called a **Riemann surface**.

Intuitively, the transition maps indicate how to glue two planes together to form the Riemann sphere. The planes are glued in an "inside-out" manner, so that they overlap almost everywhere, with each plane contributing just one point (its origin) missing from the other plane. In other words, (almost) every point in the Riemann sphere has both a ![Image 73: {\displaystyle \zeta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5c3916703cae7938143d38865f78f27faadd4ae) value and a ![Image 74: {\displaystyle \xi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0b461aaf61091abd5d2c808931c48b8ff9647db) value, and the two values are related by ![Image 75: {\displaystyle \zeta =1/\xi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afc1ca5220d2ba6b7b12d75f44b994621931aa51). The point where ![Image 76: {\displaystyle \xi =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/da5354e193004a0e2f16e7d4a76ea499ffcca225) should then have ![Image 77: {\displaystyle \zeta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5c3916703cae7938143d38865f78f27faadd4ae)-value "![Image 78: {\displaystyle 1/0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5d6c2a431df105fb423ded3679bfd7104af53fa5)"; in this sense, the origin of the ![Image 79: {\displaystyle \xi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0b461aaf61091abd5d2c808931c48b8ff9647db)-chart plays the role of ![Image 80: {\displaystyle \infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c26c105004f30c27aa7c2a9c601550a4183b1f21) in the ![Image 81: {\displaystyle \zeta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5c3916703cae7938143d38865f78f27faadd4ae)-chart. Symmetrically, the origin of the ![Image 82: {\displaystyle \zeta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5c3916703cae7938143d38865f78f27faadd4ae)-chart plays the role of ![Image 83: {\displaystyle \infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c26c105004f30c27aa7c2a9c601550a4183b1f21) in the ![Image 84: {\displaystyle \xi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0b461aaf61091abd5d2c808931c48b8ff9647db)-chart.

[Topologically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topology "Topology"), the resulting space is the [one-point compactification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compactification_(mathematics) "Compactification (mathematics)") of a plane into the sphere. However, the Riemann sphere is not merely a topological sphere. It is a sphere with a well-defined [complex structure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_manifold "Complex manifold"), so that around every point on the sphere there is a neighborhood that can be [biholomorphically](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biholomorphism "Biholomorphism") identified with ![Image 85: {\displaystyle \mathbf {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11de80478fce9090e43eed19100b37cc841661e8).

On the other hand, the [uniformization theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniformization_theorem "Uniformization theorem"), a central result in the classification of Riemann surfaces, states that every [simply-connected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simply-connected "Simply-connected") Riemann surface is biholomorphic to the complex plane, the [hyperbolic plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperbolic_space "Hyperbolic space"), or the Riemann sphere. Of these, the Riemann sphere is the only one that is a [closed surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_manifold "Closed manifold") (a [compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_space "Compact space") surface without [boundary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Manifold_with_boundary "Manifold with boundary")). Hence the two-dimensional sphere admits a unique complex structure turning it into a one-dimensional complex manifold.

As the complex projective line
------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Riemann_sphere&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: As the complex projective line")]

The Riemann sphere can also be defined as the **complex projective line**. The points of the complex projective line can be defined as [equivalence classes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equivalence_class "Equivalence class") of _non-null vectors_ in the complex vector space ![Image 86: {\displaystyle \mathbf {C} ^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b977492b76f197efebf8bceb399814966be79c35): two non-null vectors ![Image 87: {\displaystyle (w,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ae409ea20331911bfd7cc7ecded2b6734d6d2d6e) and ![Image 88: {\displaystyle (u,v)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eadf12294edccd7a29c99cfc1765e4a14bf47e58) are equivalent iff ![Image 89: {\displaystyle (w,z)=(\lambda u,\lambda v)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a88f53ecb79254a743ab4f5ec1c57d1d92aabb66) for some non-zero coefficient ![Image 90: {\displaystyle \lambda \in \mathbf {C} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a19b37ac629b822b8fb24a05e9daef7794f38e4b).

In this case, the equivalence class is written ![Image 91: {\displaystyle [w,z]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35df723ea782005592eef7065e0c2e15cda4b8dc) using [projective coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_coordinates "Projective coordinates"). Given any point ![Image 92: {\displaystyle [w,z]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35df723ea782005592eef7065e0c2e15cda4b8dc) in the complex projective line, one of ![Image 93: {\displaystyle w}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/88b1e0c8e1be5ebe69d18a8010676fa42d7961e6) and ![Image 94: {\displaystyle z}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bf368e72c009decd9b6686ee84a375632e11de98) must be non-zero, say ![Image 95: {\displaystyle w\neq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d46cad72cecacc5589e98dd8f487e7723a112ceb). Then by the notion of equivalence, ![Image 96: {\displaystyle [w,z]=\left[1,z/w\right]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/269372fe3c508319f5fd7d5532706c59aac30ecc), which is in a chart for the Riemann sphere manifold.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_sphere#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGoldman19991-3)

This treatment of the Riemann sphere connects most readily to projective geometry. For example, any line (or smooth conic) in the [complex projective plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_projective_plane "Complex projective plane") is biholomorphic to the complex projective line. It is also convenient for studying the sphere's [automorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automorphism "Automorphism"), later in this article.

The Riemann sphere can be visualized as the unit sphere ![Image 98: {\displaystyle x^{2}+y^{2}+z^{2}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a5b5c3e3525a1604f9425edb80835d236fe7af82) in the three-dimensional real space ![Image 99: {\displaystyle \mathbf {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/42ee047387e551a89e8481e1a9e974dcc5fd5acc). To this end, consider the [stereographic projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stereographic_projection "Stereographic projection") from the unit sphere minus the point ![Image 100: {\displaystyle (0,0,1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/25b35543ef5f96df2e9aea6cced9bdd57dab7e1a) onto the plane ![Image 101: {\displaystyle z=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/463359fa7c7563dc29f2079e63195b0035f1ab5a) which we identify with the complex plane by ![Image 102: {\displaystyle \zeta =x+iy}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e77114c652ec142e37d857e889b078680bc106a0). In [Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinates "Cartesian coordinates")![Image 103: {\displaystyle (x,y,z)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/22a8c93372e8f8b6e24d523bd5545aed3430baf4) and [spherical coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spherical_coordinates "Spherical coordinates")![Image 104: {\displaystyle (\theta ,\varphi )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a4a5ec1ea493f289750fb4d685da225bac7a62f7) on the sphere (with ![Image 105: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) the [zenith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zenith "Zenith") and ![Image 106: {\displaystyle \varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/33ee699558d09cf9d653f6351f9fda0b2f4aaa3e) the [azimuth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azimuth "Azimuth")), the projection is

![Image 107: {\displaystyle \zeta ={\frac {x+iy}{1-z}}={\cot }{\bigl (}{\tfrac {1}{2}}\theta {\bigr )}\,e^{i\varphi }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a6e2673c4cdeec31760a19ffa566591ace6fee80)
Similarly, stereographic projection from ![Image 108: {\displaystyle (0,0,-1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2fe7ded50a977a81c8f0f03bee443a0530133932) onto the plane ![Image 109: {\displaystyle z=0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/463359fa7c7563dc29f2079e63195b0035f1ab5a) identified with another copy of the complex plane by ![Image 110: {\displaystyle \xi =x-iy,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8158c27fff5c6ef50f28d1975327920f4b2bb882) is written

![Image 111: {\displaystyle \xi ={\frac {x-iy}{1+z}}={\tan }{\bigl (}{\tfrac {1}{2}}\theta {\bigr )}\,e^{-i\varphi }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/31b791ad6d008b74899950b13af8a57588c91e56)
The inverses of these two stereographic projections are maps from the complex plane to the sphere. The first inverse covers the sphere except the point ![Image 112: {\displaystyle (0,0,1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/25b35543ef5f96df2e9aea6cced9bdd57dab7e1a), and the second covers the sphere except the point ![Image 113: {\displaystyle (0,0,-1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2fe7ded50a977a81c8f0f03bee443a0530133932). The two complex planes, that are the domains of these maps, are identified differently with the plane ![Image 114: {\displaystyle z=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b92bfc06485cc90286474b14a516a68d8bfdd7b3), because an [orientation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orientation_(mathematics) "Orientation (mathematics)")-reversal is necessary to maintain consistent orientation on the sphere.

The transition maps between ![Image 115: {\displaystyle \zeta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5c3916703cae7938143d38865f78f27faadd4ae)-coordinates and ![Image 116: {\displaystyle \xi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0b461aaf61091abd5d2c808931c48b8ff9647db)-coordinates are obtained by composing one projection with the inverse of the other. They turn out to be ![Image 117: {\displaystyle \zeta =1/\xi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afc1ca5220d2ba6b7b12d75f44b994621931aa51) and ![Image 118: {\displaystyle \xi =1/\zeta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b668ef445ece2f7e16e41eba26e109835647e949), as described above. Thus the unit sphere is [diffeomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diffeomorphism "Diffeomorphism") to the Riemann sphere.

Under this diffeomorphism, the unit circle in the ![Image 119: {\displaystyle \zeta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5c3916703cae7938143d38865f78f27faadd4ae)-chart, the unit circle in the ![Image 120: {\displaystyle \xi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0b461aaf61091abd5d2c808931c48b8ff9647db)-chart, and the equator of the unit sphere are all identified. The unit disk ![Image 121: {\displaystyle |\zeta |<1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a2d084285316b8de127fe8ff32247533e5fbf9ba) is identified with the southern hemisphere ![Image 122: {\displaystyle z<0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a2d2a26ddecf19ed4b470af505b06859d13b2738), while the unit disk ![Image 123: {\displaystyle |\xi |<1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/acc1e2d659b997b9d975ae66b7ea7099ad9464b5) is identified with the northern hemisphere ![Image 124: {\displaystyle z>0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0f44149d6ef295968e2c1d391c2f98c1da9fca30).

A Riemann surface does not come equipped with any particular [Riemannian metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_metric "Riemannian metric"). The Riemann surface's conformal structure does, however, determine a class of metrics: all those whose subordinate conformal structure is the given one. In more detail: The complex structure of the Riemann surface does uniquely determine a metric up to [conformal equivalence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformal_equivalence "Conformal equivalence"). (Two metrics are said to be conformally equivalent if they differ by multiplication by a positive [smooth function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_function "Smooth function").) Conversely, any metric on an [oriented surface](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oriented_surface "Oriented surface") uniquely determines a complex structure, which depends on the metric only up to conformal equivalence. Complex structures on an oriented surface are therefore in one-to-one correspondence with conformal classes of metrics on that surface.

Within a given conformal class, one can use conformal symmetry to find a representative metric with convenient properties. In particular, there is always a complete metric with [constant curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_curvature "Constant curvature") in any given conformal class.

In the case of the Riemann sphere, the [Gauss–Bonnet theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss%E2%80%93Bonnet_theorem "Gauss–Bonnet theorem") implies that a constant-curvature metric ![Image 125: {\displaystyle \gamma }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a223c880b0ce3da8f64ee33c4f0010beee400b1a) must have positive [curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gaussian_curvature "Gaussian curvature")![Image 126: {\displaystyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76fce82a62ed5461908f0dc8f037de4e3686b0). It follows that the metric must be [isometric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isometry_(Riemannian_geometry) "Isometry (Riemannian geometry)") to the sphere of radius ![Image 127: {\displaystyle 1/{\sqrt {K}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9db2127933d4d789c370f780ff6c92bd88fa1ea2) in ![Image 128: {\displaystyle \mathbf {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/42ee047387e551a89e8481e1a9e974dcc5fd5acc) via stereographic projection. In the ![Image 129: {\displaystyle \zeta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d5c3916703cae7938143d38865f78f27faadd4ae)-chart on the Riemann sphere, the metric with ![Image 130: {\displaystyle K=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/664e812426e011891ed84ff62421ec49e21f7159) is given by

![Image 131: {\displaystyle ds^{2}=2\gamma _{\zeta {\overline {\zeta }}}\,d\zeta \,d{\overline {\zeta }}={\frac {4}{\left(1+\zeta {\overline {\zeta }}\right)^{2}}}\,d\zeta \,d{\overline {\zeta }}=\left({\frac {2}{1+|\zeta |^{2}}}\right)^{2}\,|d\zeta |^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c676e276356b9c9f2454db4f07afb00baf5b8431)
In real coordinates ![Image 132: {\displaystyle \zeta =u+iv}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5a97021df2b95f7647cb50c1aef5fb22edcd453), the formula is

![Image 133: {\displaystyle ds^{2}={\frac {4}{\left(1+u^{2}+v^{2}\right)^{2}}}\left(du^{2}+dv^{2}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b10b05de6d2e9e8086febab8c1273fe687397a7)
Up to a constant factor, this metric agrees with the standard [Fubini–Study metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fubini%E2%80%93Study_metric "Fubini–Study metric") on complex projective space (of which the Riemann sphere is an example). The two non-vanishing [Christoffel symbols](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christoffel_symbols "Christoffel symbols") of its [Levi-Civita connection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_connection "Levi-Civita connection") are ![Image 134: {\displaystyle \Gamma _{\zeta \zeta }^{\zeta }=-2{\overline {\zeta }}/(1+|\zeta |^{2})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/57811015fc85cddfe253b1c6fa06b2f29aa2757d) and its conjugate. This metric is therefore equal to its own [Ricci curvature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ricci_curvature_tensor "Ricci curvature tensor"), ![Image 135: {\displaystyle \gamma =\mathrm {Ric} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3bc03fa1f5315df91cfa73a7dc3bf8225fb843b).

Up to scaling, this is the _only_ metric on the sphere whose group of orientation-preserving isometries is 3-dimensional (and none is more than 3-dimensional); that group is called [![Image 136: {\displaystyle {\mbox{SO}}(3)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52df4b86c67164ce61e9520bb794456463d14e5a)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SO(3) "SO(3)"). In this sense, this is by far the most symmetric metric on the sphere. (The group of all isometries, known as [![Image 137: {\displaystyle {\mbox{O}}(3)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/316327a60328ab5d833626e1138b4a3dd9fcc642)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/O(3) "O(3)"), is also 3-dimensional, but unlike ![Image 138: {\displaystyle {\mbox{SO}}(3)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52df4b86c67164ce61e9520bb794456463d14e5a) is not a connected space.)

Conversely, let ![Image 139: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) denote the sphere (as an abstract [smooth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_manifold "Differentiable manifold") or [topological manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_manifold "Topological manifold")). By the uniformization theorem there exists a unique complex structure on ![Image 140: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) up to conformal equivalence. It follows that any metric on ![Image 141: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) is conformally equivalent to the [round metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metric_tensor#The_round_metric_on_a_sphere "Metric tensor"). All such metrics determine the same conformal geometry. The round metric is therefore not intrinsic to the Riemann sphere, since "roundness" is not an invariant of conformal geometry. The Riemann sphere is only a [conformal manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformal_manifold "Conformal manifold"), not a [Riemannian manifold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemannian_manifold "Riemannian manifold"). However, if one needs to do Riemannian geometry on the Riemann sphere, the round metric is a natural choice (with any fixed radius, though radius ![Image 142: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf) is the simplest and most common choice). That is because only a round metric on the Riemann sphere has its isometry group be a 3-dimensional group. (Namely, the group known as [![Image 143: {\displaystyle {\mbox{SO}}(3)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52df4b86c67164ce61e9520bb794456463d14e5a)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_group_SO(3) "Rotation group SO(3)"), a continuous ("Lie") group that is topologically the 3-dimensional [projective space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_space "Projective space")![Image 144: {\displaystyle \mathbf {P} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3fdce62db73d7e8a2509d3bdfb13fa793c5ffa7c).)

[![Image 145](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/a/a2/Mob3d-elip-opp-200.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Mob3d-elip-opp-200.png)

A [Möbius transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6bius_transformation "Möbius transformation") acting on the sphere, and on the plane by [stereographic projection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stereographic_projection "Stereographic projection").

The study of any [mathematical object](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_object "Mathematical object") is aided by an understanding of its [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") of automorphisms, meaning the maps from the object to itself that preserve the essential structure of the object. In the case of the Riemann sphere, an automorphism is an invertible [conformal map](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conformal_map "Conformal map") (i.e. biholomorphic map) from the Riemann sphere to itself. It turns out that the only such maps are the [Möbius transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6bius_transformation "Möbius transformation"). These are functions of the form

![Image 146: {\displaystyle f(\zeta )={\frac {a\zeta +b}{c\zeta +d}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11e460def870d436918342949cb50239f8a899d1)
where ![Image 147: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc), ![Image 148: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3), ![Image 149: {\displaystyle c}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86a67b81c2de995bd608d5b2df50cd8cd7d92455), and ![Image 150: {\displaystyle d}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e85ff03cbe0c7341af6b982e47e9f90d235c66ab) are complex numbers such that ![Image 151: {\displaystyle ad-bc\neq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d88bcc30262e583f4511b6da0d10ecfc1c94fbad). Examples of Möbius transformations include [dilations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scaling_(geometry) "Scaling (geometry)"), [rotations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation"), [translations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry) "Translation (geometry)"), and complex inversion. In fact, any Möbius transformation can be written as a composition of these.

The Möbius transformations are [homographies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homography "Homography") on the complex projective line. In [projective coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_coordinates "Projective coordinates"), the transformation _f_ can be written

![Image 152: {\displaystyle [\zeta ,\ 1]{\begin{pmatrix}a&c\\b&d\end{pmatrix}}\ =\ [a\zeta +b,\ c\zeta +d]\ =\ {\begin{pmatrix}{\tfrac {a\zeta +b}{c\zeta +d}}&1\end{pmatrix}}\ =\ [f(\zeta ),\ 1].}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a646e37fae38b7019f24cf225eb27b6d0cf4b920)
Thus the Möbius transformations can be described as two-by-two complex matrices with nonzero [determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant"). Since they act on projective coordinates, two matrices yield the same Möbius transformation if and only if they differ by a nonzero factor. The [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") of Möbius transformations is the [projective linear group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Projective_linear_group "Projective linear group")![Image 153: {\displaystyle {\mbox{PGL}}(2,\mathbf {C} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f46f2896cbfcfed78fda1190f45aba8e9c9d715).

If one endows the Riemann sphere with the [Fubini–Study metric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fubini%E2%80%93Study_metric "Fubini–Study metric"), then not all Möbius transformations are isometries; for example, the dilations and translations are not. The isometries form a proper subgroup of ![Image 154: {\displaystyle {\mbox{PGL}}(2,\mathbf {C} )}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f46f2896cbfcfed78fda1190f45aba8e9c9d715), namely ![Image 155: {\displaystyle {\mbox{PSU}}(2)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/516ede4a4590bbb2ea0039ea16dea03be5a6935e). This subgroup is isomorphic to the [rotation group ![Image 156: {\displaystyle {\mbox{SO}}(3)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52df4b86c67164ce61e9520bb794456463d14e5a)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation_group_SO(3) "Rotation group SO(3)"), which is the group of symmetries of the unit sphere in ![Image 157: {\displaystyle \mathbf {R} ^{3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/42ee047387e551a89e8481e1a9e974dcc5fd5acc) (which, when restricted to the sphere, become the isometries of the sphere).

In complex analysis, a meromorphic function on the complex plane (or on any Riemann surface, for that matter) is a ratio ![Image 158: {\displaystyle f/g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5c6b7962d3532e248f07cd42b1bdc9e007b137d) of two holomorphic functions ![Image 159: {\displaystyle f}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/132e57acb643253e7810ee9702d9581f159a1c61) and ![Image 160: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77). As a map to the complex numbers, it is undefined wherever ![Image 161: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) is zero. However, it induces a holomorphic map ![Image 162: {\displaystyle (f,g)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a230d05990654b0fdcc68c1987f0cc6a27ead28c) to the complex projective line that is well-defined even where ![Image 163: {\displaystyle g=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0607b4f563220901d455691e4a1705d598fdbaeb). This construction is helpful in the study of holomorphic and meromorphic functions. For example, on a compact Riemann surface there are no non-constant holomorphic maps to the complex numbers, but holomorphic maps to the complex projective line are abundant.

The Riemann sphere is often cited as a construction on which one can easily visualise [generalised circles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalised_circle "Generalised circle"), [Möbius transformations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M%C3%B6bius_transformation "Möbius transformation") and conformal maps between connected open subsets of the extended complex plane.

The Riemann sphere has many uses in physics. In quantum mechanics, points on the complex projective line are natural values for [photon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon "Photon")[polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photon_polarization "Photon polarization") states, [spin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spin_(physics) "Spin (physics)") states of [massive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mass "Mass")[particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subatomic_particle "Subatomic particle") of spin ![Image 164: {\displaystyle 1/2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e308a3a46b7fdce07cc09dcab9e8d8f73e37d935), and 2-state particles in general (see also [Quantum bit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_bit "Quantum bit") and [Bloch sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bloch_sphere "Bloch sphere")). The Riemann sphere has been suggested as a [relativistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_relativity "General relativity") model for the [celestial sphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_sphere "Celestial sphere").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riemann_sphere#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPenrose2007428%E2%80%93430-4) In [string theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/String_theory "String theory"), the [worldsheets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Worldsheet "Worldsheet") of strings are Riemann surfaces, and the Riemann sphere, being the simplest Riemann surface, plays a significant role. It is also important in [twistor theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Twistor_theory "Twistor theory").
