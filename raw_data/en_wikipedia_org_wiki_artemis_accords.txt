The **Artemis Accords** are a series of non-binding multilateral arrangements[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-1) between the United States government and other world governments that elaborates on the norms expected to be followed in outer space.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-2) The Accords are related to the [Artemis program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_program "Artemis program"), an American-led effort to return humans to the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon") by 2027, with the ultimate goal of expanding space exploration to Mars and beyond.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-3)

As of 24 July 2025, with the accession of [Senegal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Senegal "Senegal"), 56 countries have signed the Accords, including twenty-eight in [Europe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Europe "Europe"), ten in [Asia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Asia "Asia"), seven in [South America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_America "South America"), five in [North America](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_America "North America"), four in [Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Africa "Africa"), and two in [Oceania](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oceania "Oceania").

Drafted by [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA") and the [U.S. Department of State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State "United States Department of State"), the Accords establish a framework for cooperation in the civil exploration and peaceful use of the [Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon "Moon"), [Mars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mars "Mars"), and other [astronomical objects](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Celestial_objects "Celestial objects").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-4) They are explicitly grounded in the [United Nations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_Nations "United Nations")[Outer Space Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty "Outer Space Treaty") of 1967, which signatories are obliged to uphold, and cite most major U.N.-brokered conventions constituting [space law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_law "Space law").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-auto-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-Newman_2020-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-ASIL-8)[[note 1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-9)

The Accords were originally signed on 13 October 2020 by representatives of the [national space agencies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_government_space_agencies "List of government space agencies") of eight countries: Australia, Canada, Italy, Japan, [Luxembourg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Luxembourg "Luxembourg"), the United Arab Emirates, the United Kingdom, and the United States.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-auto-5) The Accords remain open for signature indefinitely, as NASA anticipates more nations joining.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-10) Additional signatories can choose to directly participate in Artemis program activities, or may agree simply to commit to the principles for responsible exploration of the Moon as set out in the Accords.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-11)

On 5 May 2020, _[Reuters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reuters "Reuters")_ published an exclusive report that the [Donald Trump administration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_presidency_of_Donald_Trump "First presidency of Donald Trump") was drafting a new international agreement for [mining on the Moon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_resources "Lunar resources"), which would draw from the 1967 [Outer Space Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty "Outer Space Treaty").[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-nasa-artemis20200516-65)[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-66) Ten days later, then-[NASA Administrator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_administrators_and_deputy_administrators_of_NASA "List of administrators and deputy administrators of NASA")[Jim Bridenstine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jim_Bridenstine "Jim Bridenstine") officially announced the Artemis Accords, a series of agreements with partner nations aimed at establishing a governing framework for exploring and mining the Moon.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-sdc20200515-67)

The Accords originated from the eponymous [Artemis Program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Program "Artemis Program"), an American plan launched in 2017 to send the first woman and the next man to the Moon by 2024.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-:0-68) Bridenstine stated that the agreements were intended to create a uniform set of guidelines for countries to avoid potential conflict or misunderstanding in future space endeavors; governments that sign the Accords may formally take part in the Artemis Program.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-:0-68) The Accords were drafted by NASA, the [U.S. Department of State](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States_Department_of_State "United States Department of State"), and the newly re-established [National Space Council](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Space_Council "National Space Council"); a draft was released to several governments for consultation before the final document was announced in May 2020.[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-sdc20200515-67)[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-nasa-artemis20200516-65)

On 13 October 2020, in a recorded and [livestreamed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Livestreaming "Livestreaming") ceremony, the Accords were signed by the directors of the national space agencies of the United States, Australia, Canada, Japan, Luxembourg, Italy, the United Kingdom, and the United Arab Emirates.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-auto-5)[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-Verge-69) The head of the Ukrainian national space agency signed the Accords exactly one month later.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-Ukraine_Atremis-20)[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-70)

In 2021, South Korea became the tenth country to sign the Accords,[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-South_Korea_Atremis-21)[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-71) with New Zealand joining a week later.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-New_Zealand_Atremis-22) The following June, Brazil became the first country in Latin America to join the Artemis Accords,[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-72) after previously indicating its intent to sign in 2020.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-Brazil_Artemis-23)[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-73) Poland signed the Accords at the 72nd [International Astronautical Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Astronautical_Congress "International Astronautical Congress") (IAC) in Dubai, with the head of the Polish Space Agency expressing a desire to develop indigenous Polish space technology.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-Poland_Artemis-24) Mexico joined the Accords in December 2021.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-Artemis_Mexico-25)

In 2022, the number of signatories of the Accords more than doubled from the previous year: Israel signed,[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-74) followed by Romania,[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-75) Bahrain,[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-76) and Singapore[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-77) in March; Colombia in May,[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-Colombia_Artemis-30) and France on 7 June 2022, the 60th anniversary of the founding of [its space program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CNES "CNES")[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-France_Artemis-31) (pursuant to meetings in November 2021 between U.S. Vice President [Kamala Harris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kamala_Harris "Kamala Harris") and French President [Emmanuel Macron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Emmanuel_Macron "Emmanuel Macron") in which he expressed France's intent to join).[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-78)[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-79)[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-80)[[80]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-81) Saudi Arabia signed the Accords on 14 July 2022, becoming the second Middle Eastern and Arab country to join.[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-Saudi_Artemis-32) On 13 December 2022, at the [United States‚ÄìAfrica Leaders Summit 2022](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/United_States%E2%80%93Africa_Leaders_Summit_2022 "United States‚ÄìAfrica Leaders Summit 2022"), Rwanda and Nigeria became the first African nations to sign the Artemis Accords.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-Nigeria_Artemis-33)[[81]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-auto3-82)

Representatives from signatory nations held their first meeting on 19 September 2022 at the IAC to discuss the Accords and cooperation in space more broadly.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-83)[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-84)

Although a prerequisite for taking part in the Artemis Program, the Accords have been interpreted as codifying key principles and guidelines for exploring space generally.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-Newman_2020-7) Their stated purpose is to "provide for operational implementation of important obligations contained in the [Outer Space Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Outer_Space_Treaty "Outer Space Treaty") and other instruments." The Accords are a single document, signed by each country that commits to the Accords' principles. Bilateral agreements between space agencies for specific operations on the Moon and beyond are expected to reference the Accords and implement them in particular projects.

The provisions:[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-100)

*   Affirm that cooperative activities under these Accords should be exclusively for peaceful purposes and in accordance with relevant international law.
*   Confirm a commitment to transparency and to share scientific information, consistent with Article XI of the Outer Space Treaty.
*   Call for a commitment to use reasonable efforts to utilize current interoperability standards for space-based infrastructure, and to establish standards when they do not exist or are inadequate.
*   Call for a commitment to take all reasonable efforts to render necessary assistance to personnel in outer space who are in distress and according to their obligations under the [Rescue and Return Agreement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rescue_Agreement "Rescue Agreement").
*   Specify responsibility for the registration of objects in space, as required by the [Registration Convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Registration_Convention "Registration Convention")
*   Call for a commitment to publicly share information on their activities and to the open sharing of scientific data. While doing so, signatories agree to coordinate with each other to provide appropriate protection for any proprietary and/or export-controlled information, and this provision does not extend to private sector operations unless conducted on behalf of a signatory.
*   Include an agreement to preserve outer space heritage, which they consider to comprise historically significant human or robotic landing sites, artifacts, spacecraft, and other evidence of activity, and to contribute to multinational efforts to develop practices and rules to do so.
*   Include an agreement that extraction and utilization of space resources should be conducted in a manner that complies with the Outer Space Treaty and in support of safe and sustainable activities. The signatories affirm that this does not inherently constitute national appropriation, which is prohibited by the Outer Space Treaty. They also express an intent to contribute to multilateral efforts to further develop international practices and rules on this subject.
*   Reaffirm the signatories commitment to the Outer Space Treaty's provisions relating to due regard and harmful interference with other nations' activities, and to provide information regarding the location and nature of space-based activities. Signatories express an intention to contribute to multilateral efforts to further develop international practices, criteria, and rules to assure this. To implement this, the Accords provide for the announcement of "safety zones", where other operations or an anomalous event could reasonably cause harmful interference. The size and scope of these safe zones should be based on the nature and environment of the operations involved and determined in a reasonable manner leveraging commonly accepted scientific and engineering principles. Within their safety zones, the signatories commit to respect the principle of free access to all areas of celestial bodies by others and all other provisions of the Outer Space Treaty.
*   Include a commitment to mitigate space debris and to limit the generation of new, harmful space debris in the normal operations, break-up in operational or post-mission phases, and accidents.

The Artemis Accords have generally been welcomed for advancing international law and cooperation in space.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-Newman_2020-7) Observers note that the substance of the Accords is "uncontentious" and represent a "significant political attempt to codify key principles of space law" for governing nations' space activities.[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-Newman_2020-7) International legal scholars also credit the agreement with helping influence space exploration in the direction of uniform standards of cooperation and peaceful use.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-101) The Accords have also been lauded for being the first time several nations have agreed to recognize the presence of human cultural heritage in outer space and the need to protect it.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-102)

With Australia signing and ratifying both the [Moon Treaty](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moon_Treaty "Moon Treaty") as well as the Artemis Accords, there has been a discussion if they can be harmonized.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-Australian_Institute_of_International_Affairs_2021-103) In this light an _Implementation Agreement_ for the Moon Treaty has been advocated for, as a way to compensate for the shortcomings of the Moon Treaty and to harmonize it with other laws, allowing it to be more widely accepted.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-The_Space_Review_2020-104)[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-105)

The Accords have also been criticized for allegedly being "too centered on American and commercial interests." Russia has condemned them as a "blatant attempt to create international space law that favors the United States."[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-pmid33033208-106) Beside possibly being an opportunity for China[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] in light of the [Wolf Amendment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolf_Amendment "Wolf Amendment"), Chinese government affiliated media has called the Accords "akin to European colonial [enclosure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enclosure "Enclosure") land-taking methods."[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-The_Diplomat_2020-107) Russia and China have since reached an understanding to work together on the Chinese _[International Lunar Research Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International\_Lunar\_Research\_Station "International Lunar Research Station")_ concept, to serve as a potential competing option for third parties such as Pakistan.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-The_Diplomat_2021-108)

Two researchers writing in _[Science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science\_(journal) "Science (journal)")_ magazine's Policy Forum have called on countries to speak up about their objections, and argued that the United States should go through the United Nations treaty process in order to negotiate on space mining. They were concerned NASA's Accords, if accepted by many nations, would enable the Accords' interpretation of the Outer Space Treaty to prevail.[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-pmid33033208-106) Acceptance of the Artemis Accords is a prerequisite for participation in NASA's [Artemis lunar program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_program "Artemis program").[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-pmid33033208-106)

Critics also contend that since the Outer Space Treaty expressly forbids nations from staking claim to another planetary body, the Accords violate space law by allowing signatories to lay claim to any resources extracted from celestial objects.[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-109) Frans von der Dunk of the [University of Nebraska-Lincoln](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Nebraska%E2%80%93Lincoln "University of Nebraska‚ÄìLincoln") claims the Accords strengthen "the US interpretation of the Outer Space Treaty", namely "the basic right for individual States to allow the private sector to become engaged" in commercial activities. The weakened alternative interpretation is that "unilateral approval of commercial exploitation is _not_ in compliance with the Outer Space Treaty, and that only an international regime, notably‚Äîpresumably‚Äîincluding an international licensing system, could legitimise such commercial exploitation."[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-110)[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Artemis_Accords#cite_note-111)
