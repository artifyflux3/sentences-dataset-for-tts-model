Thought experiment about gravity

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/73/Newton_Cannon.svg/250px-Newton_Cannon.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Newton_Cannon.svg)

A cannon on top of a very high mountain shoots a cannonball horizontally. If the speed is low, the cannonball quickly falls back to Earth (A,B). At [intermediate speeds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_speed "Orbital speed"), it will revolve around Earth along an elliptical orbit (C,D). Beyond the [escape velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Escape_velocity "Escape velocity"), it will leave the Earth without returning (E).

**Newton's cannonball** was a [thought experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thought_experiment "Thought experiment")[Isaac Newton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isaac_Newton "Isaac Newton") used to hypothesize that the force of [gravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity "Gravity") was universal, and it was the key force for [planetary motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planetary_orbit "Planetary orbit"). It appeared in his posthumously published 1728 work _De mundi systemate_ (also published in English as _[A Treatise of the System of the World](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophi%C3%A6\_Naturalis\_Principia\_Mathematica#Book\_3,\_De\_mundi\_systemate "Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica")_).[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_cannonball#cite_note-treatiselat-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_cannonball#cite_note-treatiseeng-2)

Theory
------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Newton%27s_cannonball&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Theory")]

In this experiment from his book (pp.5–8),[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_cannonball#cite_note-treatiseeng-2) Newton visualizes a stone being projected from the top of a high mountain, and "that there is no air about the earth, or at least that it is endowed with little or no power of resisting".

As a gravitational force acts on the projectile, it will follow a different path depending on its initial velocity.

If the speed is low, it will simply fall back on Earth.

If the speed is the [orbital speed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orbital_speed "Orbital speed") at that altitude, it will go on circling around the Earth along a fixed circular orbit "and return to the mountain from which it was projected".

If the speed is higher than the orbital velocity, but not high enough to leave Earth altogether (lower than the [escape velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Escape_velocity "Escape velocity")), it will continue revolving around Earth along an elliptical orbit.

If the speed is very high, it will leave Earth in a parabolic (at exactly escape velocity) or hyperbolic trajectory.

Source
------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Newton%27s_cannonball&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Source")]

[Newton's original plan](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophi%C3%A6_Naturalis_Principia_Mathematica#Preliminary_version "Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica") for _[Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophi%C3%A6\_Naturalis\_Principia\_Mathematica "Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica")_ was that it should consist of two books, the first analyzing basic laws of motion, and the second applying them to the Solar System. In order to include more material on motion in resisting media, the first book was split into two; the succeeding (now third) book, originally written in a more popular style, was rewritten to be more mathematical.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_cannonball#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_cannonball#cite_note-cohen2004-4) However, manuscripts of an earlier draft of this last book survived, and a version of it was published in 1728 as _De mundi systemate_; an English translation was also published earlier in 1728 under the name _A Treatise of the System of the World_.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_cannonball#cite_note-treatiselat-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_cannonball#cite_note-treatiseeng-2)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton's_cannonball#cite_note-cohen2004-4) The thought experiment occurs near the start of this work.
