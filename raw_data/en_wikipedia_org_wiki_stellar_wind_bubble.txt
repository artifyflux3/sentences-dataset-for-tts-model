From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

Cavity of hot gas blown from a star

"Astrosphere" redirects here. For the astrosphere of the Sun, see [Heliosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliosphere "Heliosphere").

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/29/Hubble_Sees_a_Star_%E2%80%98Inflating%E2%80%99_a_Giant_Bubble_%2826534662246%29.jpg/250px-Hubble_Sees_a_Star_%E2%80%98Inflating%E2%80%99_a_Giant_Bubble_%2826534662246%29.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Hubble_Sees_a_Star_%E2%80%98Inflating%E2%80%99_a_Giant_Bubble_(26534662246).jpg)

The [Bubble Nebula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bubble_Nebula "Bubble Nebula") (NGC 7635), imaged by the [Hubble Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope "Hubble Space Telescope"), is seven [light years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_year "Light year") across

A **stellar-wind bubble** is a cavity [light-years](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light-year "Light-year") across filled with hot gas blown into the [interstellar medium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interstellar_medium "Interstellar medium") by the high-velocity (several thousand km/s) [stellar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_wind "Stellar wind") from a single massive [star](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star "Star") of [type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar_classification "Stellar classification")[O](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/O-type_star "O-type star") or [B](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/B-type_main-sequence_star "B-type main-sequence star"). Weaker stellar winds also blow bubble structures, which are also called **astrospheres**. The [heliosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliosphere "Heliosphere") blown by the [solar wind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_wind "Solar wind"), within which all the major [planets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planet "Planet") of the [Solar System](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_System "Solar System") are embedded, is a small example of a stellar-wind bubble.

Stellar-wind bubbles have a two-shock structure.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stellar-wind_bubble#cite_note-1) The freely-expanding stellar wind hits an inner termination shock, where its kinetic energy is thermalized, producing 10 6[K](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kelvin "Kelvin"), [X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray")-emitting [plasma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plasma_(physics) "Plasma (physics)"). The hot, high-pressure, shocked wind expands, driving a shock into the surrounding interstellar gas. If the surrounding gas is dense enough (number densities [n>0.1 cm−3{\displaystyle n>0.1{\mbox{ cm}}^{-3}}![Image 7: {\displaystyle n>0.1{\mbox{ cm}}^{-3}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8805944b658e91d11108368533e7cbc2496cb66b)](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Special:MathWikibase&qid=Q120634820) or so), the swept-up gas radiatively cools far faster than the hot interior, forming a thin, relatively dense shell around the hot, shocked wind.
