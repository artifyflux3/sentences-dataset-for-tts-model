Letters to indicate vowels in some Semitic languages

Not to be confused with [Plene scriptum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plene_scriptum "Plene scriptum").

A _**mater lectionis**_ ([/ˌ m eɪ t ər ˌ l ɛ k t i ˈ oʊ n ɪ s/](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English "Help:IPA/English")[](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/transcoded/7/7d/LL-Q1860_%28eng%29-Persent101-mater_lectionis.wav/LL-Q1860_%28eng%29-Persent101-mater_lectionis.wav.mp3 "Play audio")[ⓘ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:LL-Q1860_(eng)-Persent101-mater_lectionis.wav "File:LL-Q1860 (eng)-Persent101-mater lectionis.wav")[_MAY-tər LEK-tee-OH-niss_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key"), [/ˌ m ɑː t ər-/](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English "Help:IPA/English")[_MAH-tər -⁠_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key");[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mater_lectionis#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mater_lectionis#cite_note-MW-2)[Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_language "Latin language") for 'mother of reading', pl._**matres lectionis**_[/ˌ m ɑː t r eɪ s-/](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English "Help:IPA/English")[_MAH-trayss -⁠_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Pronunciation_respelling_key "Help:Pronunciation respelling key");[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mater_lectionis#cite_note-MW-2) original [Hebrew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebrew_language "Hebrew language"): אֵם קְרִיאָה, [romanized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanization_of_Hebrew "Romanization of Hebrew"):_ʾēm qərîʾāh_) is any consonant letter that is used to indicate a vowel, primarily in the writing of [Semitic languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semitic_languages "Semitic languages") such as [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic "Arabic"), [Hebrew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebrew_language "Hebrew language") and [Syriac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syriac_language "Syriac language"). The letters that do this in Hebrew are _[aleph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aleph "Aleph")_ א, _[he](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/He\_(letter) "He (letter)")_ ה, _[vav](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waw\_(letter) "Waw (letter)")_ ו and _[yud](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yodh "Yodh")_ י, with the latter two in particular being more often vowels than they are consonants. In Arabic, the _matres lectionis_ (though they are much less often referred to thus) are [_ʾalif_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aleph#Arabic "Aleph")ا, [_wāw_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waw_(letter)#Arabic_w%C4%81w "Waw (letter)")و and [_yāʾ_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yodh#Arabic_y%C4%81%CA%BC "Yodh")ي.

The original value of the _matres lectionis_ corresponds closely to what are called in modern linguistics _[glides](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glide\_(linguistics) "Glide (linguistics)")_ or _semivowels_.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mater_lectionis#cite_note-3)

Overview
--------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mater_lectionis&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Overview")]

Because the scripts used to write some Semitic languages lack vowel letters, unambiguous reading of a text might be difficult. Therefore, to indicate vowels (mostly long), consonant letters are used. For example, in the Hebrew [construct-state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Construct_state "Construct state") form _bēt_, meaning "the house of", the middle letter [י](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%D7%99 "י") in the spelling בית acts as a vowel, but in the corresponding absolute-state form _bayit_ ("house"), which is spelled the same, the same letter represents a genuine consonant. _Matres lectionis_ are extensively employed only in Hebrew, [Aramaic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aramaic_language "Aramaic language"), [Syriac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Syriac_language "Syriac language") and [Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabic "Arabic"), but the phenomenon is also found in the [Ugaritic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ugaritic_alphabet "Ugaritic alphabet"), [Moabite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moabite_language "Moabite language"), [South Arabian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Arabian_alphabet "South Arabian alphabet") and [Phoenician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenician_alphabet "Phoenician alphabet") alphabets.

Origins and development
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mater_lectionis&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Origins and development")]

Historically, the practice of using _matres lectionis_ seems to have originated when /aj/ and /aw/ diphthongs, written with the _[yod](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yodh "Yodh")_ י and the _[waw](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Waw\_(letter) "Waw (letter)")_ ו consonant letters respectively, [monophthongized](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monophthongization "Monophthongization") to simple long vowels /eː/ and /oː/. This epiphenomenal association between consonant letters and vowel sounds was then seized upon and used in words without historic diphthongs.

In general terms, it is observable that early [Phoenician](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phoenician_alphabet "Phoenician alphabet") texts have very few _matres lectionis_, and that during most of the 1st millennium BCE, Hebrew and Aramaic were quicker to develop _matres lectionis_ than Phoenician. However, in its latest period of development in [North Africa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Africa "North Africa") (referred to as "[Punic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Punic_language "Punic language")"), Phoenician developed a very full use of _matres lectionis_, including the use of the letter _[ayin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ayin "Ayin")_ ע‎, also used for this purpose much later in [Yiddish orthography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yiddish_orthography "Yiddish orthography").

In pre-exilic Hebrew, there was a significant development of the use of the letter _[he](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/He\_(letter) "He (letter)")_ ה to indicate word final vowels other than _ī_ and _ū_. This was probably inspired by the phonological change of the third-person singular possessive suffix from /ahuː/>/aw/>/oː/ in most environments. However, in later periods of Hebrew, the orthography was changed so word-final _ō_ was no longer written with ה, except in a few archaically-spelled proper names, such as [Solomon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solomon "Solomon")שלמה and [Shiloh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shiloh_(biblical_figure) "Shiloh (biblical figure)")שלה. The difference between the spelling of the third-person singular possessive suffix (as attached to singular nouns) with ה in early Hebrew versus with ו in later Hebrew has become an issue in the authentication of the [Jehoash Inscription](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jehoash_Inscription "Jehoash Inscription").

According to Sass (5), already in the Middle Kingdom there were some cases of _matres lectionis_, i.e. consonant graphemes which were used to transcribe vowels in foreign words, namely in Punic (Jensen 290, Naveh 62), Aramaic, and Hebrew (ה, ו, י; sometimes even _[aleph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aleph "Aleph")_ א; Naveh 62). Naveh (ibid.) notes that the earliest Aramaic and Hebrew documents already used _matres lectionis_. Some scholars argue that the Greeks must therefore have borrowed their alphabet from the Arameans. However, the practice has older roots, as the [Semitic cuneiform alphabet of Ugarit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ugaritic_alphabet "Ugaritic alphabet") (13th century BC) already had _matres lectionis_ (Naveh 138).

Usage in different languages
----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mater_lectionis&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Usage in different languages")]

### Hebrew

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mater_lectionis&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Hebrew")]

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/Question_book-new.svg/60px-Question_book-new.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Question_book-new.svg)This section **does not [cite](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources "Wikipedia:Citing sources") any [sources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability "Wikipedia:Verifiability")**. Please help [improve this section](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Special:EditPage/Mater_lectionis "Special:EditPage/Mater lectionis") by [adding citations to reliable sources](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Referencing_for_beginners "Help:Referencing for beginners"). Unsourced material may be challenged and [removed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability#Burden_of_evidence "Wikipedia:Verifiability")._(January 2025)_ _([Learn how and when to remove this message](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:Maintenance\_template\_removal "Help:Maintenance template removal"))_

Further information: [Hebrew spelling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hebrew_spelling "Hebrew spelling")

The earliest method of indicating some vowels in Hebrew writing was to use the consonant letters _yod_ י, _waw_ ו, _he_ ה, and _aleph_ א of the Hebrew alphabet to also write long [vowels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vowels "Vowels") in some cases. Originally, א and ה were only used as matres lectiones at the end of words, and י and ו were used mainly to write the original [diphthongs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diphthongs "Diphthongs")/aw/ and /aj/ as well as /VjV/ sequences (/j/ surrounded by two vowels, which sometimes simplified to plain long vowels). Gradually, as it was found to be insufficient for differentiating between similar nouns, י and ו were also inserted to mark some long vowels of non-diphthongal origin.

If words can be written with or without _matres lectionis_, spellings that include the letters are called _malē_ (Hebrew) or _plene_ (Latin), meaning "full", and spellings without them are called _ḥaser_ or _defective_. In some verb forms, _matres lectionis_ are almost always used. Around the 9th century CE it was decided that the system of _matres lectionis_ did not suffice to indicate the vowels precisely enough for purposes of liturgical recitation of Biblical texts so a supplemental [vowel pointing system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Niqqud "Niqqud") (_niqqud_, diacritic symbols indicating vowel pronunciation and other important phonological features not written by the traditional basic consonantal orthography) joined _matres lectionis_ as part of the Hebrew writing system.

In some words in Hebrew, there is a choice of whether to use a _mater lectionis_ or not, and in modern printed texts _matres lectionis_ are sometimes used even for short vowels, which is considered to be grammatically incorrect according to traditional norms, though instances are found as far back as [Talmudic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Talmud "Talmud") times. Such texts from Judaea and Galilee were noticeably more inclined to _malē_ spellings than texts from [Babylonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Babylonia "Babylonia"). Similarly, in the [Middle Ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Ages "Middle Ages"), [Ashkenazi Jews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ashkenazi_Jews "Ashkenazi Jews") tended to use _malē_ spellings under the influence of [European languages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Languages_of_Europe "Languages of Europe"), but [Sephardi Jews](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sephardi_Jews "Sephardi Jews") tended to use _ḥaser_ spellings under the influence of Arabic.

Most commonly, _yod_ י indicates _i_ or _e_, while _waw_ ו indicates _o_ or _u_. _Aleph_ א was not systematically developed as a _mater lectionis_ in Hebrew (unlike in Aramaic and Arabic), but it is occasionally used to indicate an _a_ vowel. (However, a silent א, indicating an original [glottal stop](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glottal_stop "Glottal stop") consonant sound that has become silent in some contexts in Hebrew pronunciation, can occur after almost any vowel.) At the end of a word, _he_ ה can also be used to indicate that the vowel _a_ or _e_ should be pronounced.

### Arabic

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mater_lectionis&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Arabic")]

In Arabic, there is no choice, and the almost invariable rule is that a long vowel is written with a _mater lectionis_ and a short vowel with a diacritic symbol, but the [Uthmanic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uthman "Uthman") orthography, the one in which the [Quran](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quran "Quran") is traditionally written and printed, has some differences, which are not always consistent. Also, under influence from orthography of European languages, transliterating of vowels in borrowed words into Arabic is usually done using _matres lectionis_ in place of diacritics, even when the vowel transliterated is short or when words from another Semitic language, such as Hebrew, are transliterated. That phenomenon is augmented by the neglect of diacritics in most printed forms since the beginning of mechanical printing.

The name given to the three _matres lectionis_ by traditional Arabic grammar is _ḥurūf al-līn wa-l-madd_ (حروف اللين والمدّ, 'consonants of softness and lengthening'), or _ḥurūf al-ʿilla_ (حروف العلّة, 'causal consonants' or 'consonants of infirmity', because as in Greek grammar, words with 'accidents' were deemed to be afflicted, ill, in opposition to 'healthy' words without accidents).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mater_lectionis#cite_note-4)

Informal orthographies of spoken varieties of Arabic also use _[hāʾ](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/He\_(letter)#Arabic\_h%C4%81%CA%BE "He (letter)")_ ه to indicate a shorter version of _[alif](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aleph#Arabic "Aleph")_ ا in final position, a usage augmented by the ambiguity of the use of ه and _[tāʾ marbūṭah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/T%C4%81%CA%BE\_marb%C5%AB%E1%B9%ADah "Tāʾ marbūṭah")_ ة in formal Arabic orthography. It is a formal orthography in other languages that use Arabic script, such as [Kurdish alphabets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurdish_alphabets "Kurdish alphabets").

### Syriac

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mater_lectionis&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Syriac")]

Syriac-Aramaic vowels are classified into three groups: the _alap_ (ܐ), the _waw_ (ܘ), and the _yod_ (ܝ). The _mater lectionis_ was developed as early as the 6th century to represent long vowels, which were earlier denoted by a dot under the line. The most frequent ones are the _yod_ and the _waw_, while the _alap_ is mostly restricted to some transliterated words.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mater_lectionis#cite_note-5)

### Mandaic

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mater_lectionis&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Mandaic")]

In the [Mandaic alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mandaic_alphabet "Mandaic alphabet"), vowels are usually written out in full. The first letter, _a_ (corresponding to _alaph_), is used to represent a range of open vowels. The sixth letter, _wa_, is used for close back vowels (_u_ and _o_), and the tenth letter, _ya_ is used for close front vowels (_i_ and _e_). These last two can also serve as the consonants _w/v_ and _y_. The eighth letter corresponds to the Semitic _[heth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heth "Heth")_, and is called _eh_; it is pronounced as a long _i_-vowel but is used only as a suffix for the [third person singular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grammatical_person "Grammatical person"). The sixteenth letter, _e_ (Aramaic _[ayn](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ayin "Ayin")_), usually represents _e_ at the beginning of a word or, when followed by _wa_ or _ya_, represents initial _u_ or _i_ respectively.

Influence on other languages
----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Mater_lectionis&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Influence on other languages")]

Later, in some adaptations of the Arabic alphabet (such those sometimes used for [Kurdish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurdish_alphabets "Kurdish alphabets") and [Uyghur](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uyghur_Arabic_alphabet "Uyghur Arabic alphabet")) and of the Hebrew alphabet (such as those used for [Judeo-Arabic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judeo-Arabic "Judeo-Arabic"), [Yiddish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yiddish "Yiddish") and [Judaeo-Spanish](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Judaeo-Spanish "Judaeo-Spanish")), _matres lectionis_ were generally used for all or most vowels, thus in effect becoming vowel letters: see [Yiddish orthography](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yiddish_orthography "Yiddish orthography"). This tendency was taken to its logical conclusion in fully alphabetic scripts such as [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_alphabet "Greek alphabet"), [Latin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Latin_alphabet "Latin alphabet"), and [Cyrillic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cyrillic_script "Cyrillic script"). Many of the vowel letters in such languages historically go back to _matres lectionis_ in the Phoenician script. For example, the letter ⟨i⟩ was originally derived from the consonant letter _yod_. Similarly the vowel letters in the [Avestan alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avestan_alphabet "Avestan alphabet") were adapted from _matres lectionis_ in the version of the [Aramaic alphabet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Aramaic_alphabet "Aramaic alphabet") adapted as the [Pahlavi scripts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pahlavi_scripts "Pahlavi scripts").
