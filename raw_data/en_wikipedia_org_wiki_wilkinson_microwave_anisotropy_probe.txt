The **Wilkinson Microwave Anisotropy Probe** (**WMAP**), originally known as the **Microwave Anisotropy Probe** (**MAP** and **Explorer 80**), was a [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA") spacecraft operating from 2001 to 2010 which measured temperature differences across the sky in the [cosmic microwave background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background "Cosmic microwave background") (CMB) – the radiant heat remaining from the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-6) Headed by Professor [Charles L. Bennett](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charles_L._Bennett "Charles L. Bennett") of [Johns Hopkins University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johns_Hopkins_University "Johns Hopkins University"), the mission was developed in a joint partnership between the NASA [Goddard Space Flight Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Goddard_Space_Flight_Center "Goddard Space Flight Center") and [Princeton University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Princeton_University "Princeton University").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2003PressRelease-7) The WMAP spacecraft was launched on 30 June 2001 from [Florida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Florida "Florida"). The WMAP mission succeeded the [COBE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Background_Explorer "Cosmic Background Explorer") space mission and was the second medium-class (MIDEX) spacecraft in the NASA [Explorer program](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Explorer_program "Explorer program"). In 2003, MAP was renamed WMAP in honor of cosmologist [David Todd Wilkinson](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/David_Todd_Wilkinson "David Todd Wilkinson") (1935–2002),[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2003PressRelease-7) who had been a member of the mission's science team. After nine years of operations, WMAP was switched off in 2010, following the launch of the more advanced [Planck spacecraft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft) "Planck (spacecraft)") by [European Space Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Space_Agency "European Space Agency") (ESA) in 2009.

WMAP's measurements played a key role in establishing the current Standard Model of Cosmology: the [Lambda-CDM model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lambda-CDM_model "Lambda-CDM model"). The WMAP data are very well fit by a universe that is dominated by [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy") in the form of a [cosmological constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmological_constant "Cosmological constant"). Other cosmological data are also consistent, and together tightly constrain the Model. In the Lambda-CDM model of the universe, the [age of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Age_of_the_universe "Age of the universe") is 13.772±0.059 billion years. The WMAP mission's determination of the age of the universe is to better than 1% precision.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-8) The current expansion rate of the universe is (see [Hubble constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble%27s_law "Hubble's law")) 69.32±0.80 km·s−1·Mpc−1. The content of the universe currently consists of 4.628%±0.093% ordinary [baryonic matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryonic#Baryonic_matter "Baryonic"); 24.02%+0.88%

−0.87%[cold dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_dark_matter "Cold dark matter") (CDM) that neither emits nor absorbs light; and 71.35%+0.95%

−0.96% of [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy") in the form of a cosmological constant that [accelerates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Accelerating_expansion_of_the_universe "Accelerating expansion of the universe") the [expansion of the universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Expansion_of_the_universe "Expansion of the universe").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-9) Less than 1% of the current content of the universe is in neutrinos, but WMAP's measurements have found, for the first time in 2008, that the data prefer the existence of a [cosmic neutrino background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background "Cosmic neutrino background")[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2009Hinshaw-10) with an effective number of neutrino species of 3.26±0.35. The contents point to a Euclidean [flat geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shape_of_the_universe "Shape of the universe"), with curvature (![Image 4: {\displaystyle \Omega _{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b18029871270775ecb905d24b7fc1a0016930965)) of −0.0027+0.0039

−0.0038. The WMAP measurements also support the [cosmic inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflation_(cosmology) "Inflation (cosmology)") paradigm in several ways, including the flatness measurement.

The mission has won various awards: according to _Science_ magazine, the WMAP was the _[Breakthrough of the Year](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breakthrough\_of\_the\_Year "Breakthrough of the Year") for 2003_.[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2003Seife-11) This mission's results papers were first and second in the "Super Hot Papers in Science Since 2003" list.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-incites-12) Of the all-time most referenced papers in physics and astronomy in the [INSPIRE-HEP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/INSPIRE-HEP "INSPIRE-HEP") database, only three have been published since 2000, and all three are WMAP publications. Bennett, [Lyman A. Page Jr.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lyman_Page "Lyman Page"), and David N. Spergel, the latter both of Princeton University, shared the 2010 [Shaw Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shaw_Prize "Shaw Prize") in astronomy for their work on WMAP.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-13) Bennett and the WMAP science team were awarded the 2012 [Gruber Prize](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gruber_Prize "Gruber Prize") in cosmology. The 2018 [Breakthrough Prize in Fundamental Physics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Breakthrough_Prize_in_Fundamental_Physics "Breakthrough Prize in Fundamental Physics") was awarded to Bennett, Gary Hinshaw, Norman Jarosik, Page, Spergel, and the WMAP science team.

In October 2010, the WMAP spacecraft was [derelict](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category:Derelict_satellites_in_heliocentric_orbit "Category:Derelict satellites in heliocentric orbit") in a [heliocentric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit "Heliocentric orbit")[graveyard orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graveyard_orbit "Graveyard orbit") after completing nine years of operations.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-dn20101007-14) All WMAP data are released to the public and have been subject to careful scrutiny. The final official data release was the [nine-year release](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#Nine-year_data_release) in 2012.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-Space-20121221-15)[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-arXiv-20121220-16)

Some aspects of the data are statistically unusual for the Standard Model of Cosmology. For example, the largest angular-scale measurement, the [quadrupole moment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quadrupole_moment "Quadrupole moment"), is somewhat smaller than the Model would predict, but this discrepancy is not highly significant.[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-17) A large [cold spot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WMAP_cold_spot "WMAP cold spot") and other features of the data are more statistically significant, and research continues into these.

The WMAP objective was to measure the temperature differences in the [Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_microwave_background_radiation "Cosmic microwave background radiation"). The anisotropies then were used to measure the universe's geometry, content, and [evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution_of_the_universe "Evolution of the universe"); and to test the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang") model, and the [cosmic inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_inflation "Cosmic inflation") theory.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2003Bennett-18) For that, the mission created a full-sky map of the CMB, with a 13 [arcminutes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minute_and_second_of_arc "Minute and second of arc") resolution via multi-frequency observation. The map required the fewest [systematic errors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systematic_error "Systematic error"), no correlated pixel noise, and accurate calibration, to ensure angular-scale accuracy greater than its resolution.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2003Bennett-18) The map contains 3,145,728 pixels, and uses the [HEALPix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HEALPix "HEALPix") scheme to pixelize the sphere.[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2003Bennettb-19) The telescope also measured the CMB's E-mode polarization,[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2003Bennett-18) and foreground polarization.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2009Hinshaw-10) Its service life was 27 months; 3 to reach the [L 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_point#L2 "Lagrange point") position, and 2 years of observation.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2003Bennett-18)

The MAP mission was proposed to NASA in 1995, selected for definition study in 1996, and approved for development in 1997.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-news_facts-20)[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-news_events-21)

The WMAP was preceded by two missions to observe the CMB; (i) the Soviet [RELIKT-1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/RELIKT-1 "RELIKT-1") that reported the upper-limit measurements of CMB anisotropies, and (ii) the U.S. [COBE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Background_Explorer "Cosmic Background Explorer") satellite that first reported large-scale CMB fluctuations. The WMAP was 45 times more sensitive, with 33 times the angular resolution of its COBE satellite predecessor.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2008Limon-22) The successor European Planck mission (operational 2009–2013) had a higher resolution and higher sensitivity than WMAP and observed in 9 frequency bands rather than WMAP's 5, allowing improved astrophysical foreground models.

The telescope's primary reflecting mirrors are a pair of [Gregorian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gregorian_telescope "Gregorian telescope") 1.4×1.6 m (4 ft 7 in×5 ft 3 in) dishes (facing opposite directions), that focus the signal onto a pair of 0.9×1.0 m (2 ft 11 in×3 ft 3 in) secondary reflecting mirrors. They are shaped for optimal performance: a [carbon fibre](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carbon_fibre "Carbon fibre") shell upon a Korex core, thinly-coated with aluminium and [silicon oxide](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silicon_oxide "Silicon oxide"). The secondary reflectors transmit the signals to the corrugated feedhorns that sit on a [focal plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cardinal_point_(optics) "Cardinal point (optics)") array box beneath the primary reflectors.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2003Bennett-18)

The receivers are [polarization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polarization_(waves) "Polarization (waves)")-sensitive differential [radiometers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radiometer "Radiometer") measuring the difference between two telescope beams. The signal is amplified with [High-electron-mobility transistor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/High-electron-mobility_transistor "High-electron-mobility transistor") (HEMT) [low-noise amplifiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low-noise_amplifier "Low-noise amplifier"), built by the [National Radio Astronomy Observatory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Radio_Astronomy_Observatory "National Radio Astronomy Observatory") (NRAO). There are 20 feeds, 10 in each direction, from which a radiometer collects a signal; the measure is the difference in the sky signal from opposite directions. The directional separation [azimuth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Azimuth "Azimuth") is 180°; the total angle is 141°. To improve subtraction of foreground signals from our [Milky Way](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Milky_Way "Milky Way") galaxy, the WMAP used five discrete radio frequency bands, from 23 GHz to 94 GHz.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2003Bennett-18)

The WMAP's base is a 5.0 m (16.4 ft)-diameter [solar panel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solar_panel "Solar panel") array that keeps the instruments in shadow during CMB observations, (by keeping the craft constantly angled at 22°, relative to the [Sun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun "Sun")). Upon the array sit a bottom deck (supporting the warm components) and a top deck. The telescope's cold components: the focal-plane array and the mirrors, are separated from the warm components with a cylindrical, 33 cm (13 in)-long thermal isolation shell atop the deck.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2003Bennett-18)

Passive thermal radiators cool the WMAP to approximately 90 K (−183.2°C; −297.7°F); they are connected to the [low-noise amplifiers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Low-noise_amplifier "Low-noise amplifier"). The telescope consumes 419 W of power. The available telescope heaters are emergency-survival heaters, and there is a transmitter heater, used to warm them when off. The WMAP spacecraft's temperature is monitored with [platinum resistance thermometers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Platinum_resistance_thermometer "Platinum resistance thermometer").[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2003Bennett-18)

The WMAP's calibration is effected with the CMB dipole and measurements of [Jupiter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jupiter "Jupiter"); the beam patterns are measured against Jupiter. The telescope's data are relayed daily via a 2-GHz [transponder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transponder_(satellite_communications) "Transponder (satellite communications)") providing a 667 [kbit/s](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kbit/s "Kbit/s") downlink to a 70 m (230 ft) [Deep Space Network](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA_Deep_Space_Network "NASA Deep Space Network") station. The spacecraft has two transponders, one a redundant backup; they are minimally active – about 40 minutes daily – to minimize [radio frequency interference](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radio_frequency_interference "Radio frequency interference"). The telescope's position is maintained, in its three axes, with three [reaction wheels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reaction_wheel "Reaction wheel"), [gyroscopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gyroscope "Gyroscope"), two [star trackers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Star_tracker "Star tracker") and [Sun sensors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sun_sensor "Sun sensor"), and is steered with eight [hydrazine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hydrazine "Hydrazine") thrusters.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2003Bennett-18)

WMAP

The WMAP spacecraft arrived at the [Kennedy Space Center](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kennedy_Space_Center "Kennedy Space Center") on 20 April 2001. After being tested for two months, it was launched via [Delta II](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Delta_II "Delta II") 7425 launch vehicle from the [Cape Canaveral Space Force Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cape_Canaveral_Space_Force_Station "Cape Canaveral Space Force Station") on 30 June 2001.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-news_facts-20)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2008Limon-22) It began operating on its internal power five minutes before its launching, and continued so operating until the solar panel array deployed. The WMAP was activated and monitored while it cooled. On 2 July 2001, it began working, first with in-flight testing (from launching until 17 August 2001), then began constant, formal work.[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2008Limon-22) Afterwards, it effected three Earth-Moon phase loops, measuring its [sidelobes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sidelobe "Sidelobe"), then flew by the Moon on 30 July 2001, en route to the Sun-Earth [L 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_point#L2 "Lagrange point")[Lagrange point](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_point "Lagrange point"), arriving there on 1 October 2001, becoming the first CMB observation mission posted there.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-news_facts-20)

Locating the spacecraft at [Lagrange 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_point "Lagrange point"), (1,500,000 km (930,000 mi) from Earth) thermally stabilizes it and minimizes the contaminating solar, terrestrial, and lunar emissions registered. To view the entire sky, without looking to the Sun, the WMAP traces a path around [L 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrange_point#L2 "Lagrange point") in a [Lissajous orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lissajous_orbit "Lissajous orbit") ca. 1.0° to 10°,[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2003Bennett-18) with a 6-month period.[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-news_facts-20) The telescope rotates once every 2 minutes 9 seconds (0.464 [rpm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Revolutions_per_minute "Revolutions per minute")) and [precesses](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Precession "Precession") at the rate of 1 revolution per hour.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2003Bennett-18) WMAP measured the entire sky every six months, and completed its first, full-sky observation in April 2002.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-news_events-21)

### Pseudo-Correlation Radiometer

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Pseudo-Correlation Radiometer")]

The WMAP instrument consists of pseudo-correlation differential radiometers fed by two back-to-back 1.5 m (4 ft 11 in) primary Gregorian reflectors. This instrument uses five frequency bands from 22 GHz to 90 GHz to facilitate rejection of foreground signals from our own Galaxy. The WMAP instrument has a 3.5° x 3.5° [field of view](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_of_view "Field of view") (FoV).[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-Experiment1-23)

Foreground radiation subtraction
--------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Foreground radiation subtraction")]

The WMAP observed in five frequencies, permitting the measurement and subtraction of foreground contamination (from the Milky Way and extra-galactic sources) of the CMB. The main emission mechanisms are [synchrotron radiation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Synchrotron_radiation "Synchrotron radiation") and [free-free emission](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bremsstrahlung "Bremsstrahlung") (dominating the lower frequencies), and [astrophysical dust](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Astrophysical_dust "Astrophysical dust") emissions (dominating the higher frequencies). The spectral properties of these emissions contribute different amounts to the five frequencies, thus permitting their identification and subtraction.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2003Bennett-18)

Foreground contamination is removed in several ways. First, subtract extant emission maps from the WMAP's measurements; second, use the components' known spectral values to identify them; third, simultaneously fit the position and spectra data of the foreground emission, using extra data sets. Foreground contamination was reduced by using only the full-sky map portions with the least foreground contamination, while masking the remaining map portions.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2003Bennett-18)

The five-year models of foreground emission, at different frequencies. Red = Synchrotron; Green = free-free; Blue = thermal dust. On 11 February 2003, NASA published the first-year's worth of WMAP data. The latest calculated age and composition of the early universe were presented. In addition, an image of the early universe, that "contains such stunning detail, that it may be one of the most important scientific results of recent years" was presented. ### Three-year data release

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Three-year data release")]

[![Image 32](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/Microwave_Sky_polarization.png/250px-Microwave_Sky_polarization.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Microwave_Sky_polarization.png)

Three-year WMAP image of background cosmic radiation (2006)

The three-year WMAP data were released on 17 March 2006. The data included temperature and polarization measurements of the CMB, which provided further confirmation of the standard flat Lambda-CDM model and new evidence in support of [inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflation_(cosmology) "Inflation (cosmology)").

The 3-year WMAP data alone shows that the universe must have [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter"). Results were computed both only using WMAP data, and also with a mix of parameter constraints from other instruments, including other CMB experiments ([Arcminute Cosmology Bolometer Array Receiver](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arcminute_Cosmology_Bolometer_Array_Receiver "Arcminute Cosmology Bolometer Array Receiver") (ACBAR), [Cosmic Background Imager](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Background_Imager "Cosmic Background Imager") (CBI) and [BOOMERANG](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BOOMERanG_experiment "BOOMERanG experiment")), [Sloan Digital Sky Survey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sloan_Digital_Sky_Survey "Sloan Digital Sky Survey") (SDSS), the [2dF Galaxy Redshift Survey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/2dF_Galaxy_Redshift_Survey "2dF Galaxy Redshift Survey"), the [Supernova Legacy Survey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Supernova_Legacy_Survey "Supernova Legacy Survey") and constraints on the [Hubble constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble%27s_law "Hubble's law") from the [Hubble Space Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hubble_Space_Telescope "Hubble Space Telescope").[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2007Spergel-25)

### Five-year data release

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Five-year data release")]

[![Image 40](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/28/WMAP_2008.png/250px-WMAP_2008.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:WMAP_2008.png)

Five-year WMAP image of background cosmic radiation (2008)

The five-year WMAP data were released on 28 February 2008. The data included new evidence for the [cosmic neutrino background](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_neutrino_background "Cosmic neutrino background"), evidence that it took over half billion years for the first stars to reionize the universe, and new constraints on [cosmic inflation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_inflation "Cosmic inflation").[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2008PressRelease-27)

[![Image 41](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ea/WMAP_2008_TT_and_TE_spectra.png/250px-WMAP_2008_TT_and_TE_spectra.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:WMAP_2008_TT_and_TE_spectra.png)

The five-year total-intensity and polarization spectra from WMAP

[![Image 42](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/21/WMAP_2008_universe_content.png/250px-WMAP_2008_universe_content.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:WMAP_2008_universe_content.png)

Matter/energy content in the current universe (top) and at the time of photon decoupling in the [recombination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recombination_(cosmology) "Recombination (cosmology)")[epoch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epoch_(astronomy) "Epoch (astronomy)") 380,000 years after the Big Bang (bottom)

The improvement in the results came from both having an extra two years of measurements (the data set runs between midnight on 10 August 2001 to midnight of 9 August 2006), as well as using improved data processing techniques and a better characterization of the instrument, most notably of the beam shapes. They also make use of the 33-GHz observations for estimating cosmological parameters; previously only the 41-GHz and 61-GHz channels had been used.

Improved masks were used to remove foregrounds.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2009Hinshaw-10) Improvements to the spectra were in the 3rd acoustic peak, and the polarization spectra.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2009Hinshaw-10)

The measurements put constraints on the content of the universe at the time that the CMB was emitted; at the time 10% of the universe was made up of neutrinos, 12% of atoms, 15% of photons and 63% dark matter. The contribution of [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy") at the time was negligible.[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2008PressRelease-27) It also constrained the content of the present-day universe; 4.6% atoms, 23% dark matter and 72% dark energy.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2009Hinshaw-10)

The WMAP five-year data was combined with measurements from [Type Ia supernova](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_Ia_supernova "Type Ia supernova") (SNe) and [Baryon acoustic oscillations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryon_acoustic_oscillations "Baryon acoustic oscillations") (BAO).[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-2009Hinshaw-10)

### Seven-year data release

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Seven-year data release")]

[![Image 58](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/2/2d/WMAP_2010.png/250px-WMAP_2010.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:WMAP_2010.png)

7-year WMAP image of background cosmic radiation (2010)

The seven-year WMAP data were released on 26 January 2010. As part of this release, claims for inconsistencies with the standard model were investigated.[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-WMAP_CMB-29) Most were shown not to be statistically significant, and likely due to _a posteriori_ selection (where one sees a weird deviation, but fails to consider properly how hard one has been looking; a deviation with 1:1000 likelihood will typically be found if one tries one thousand times). For the deviations that do remain, there are no alternative cosmological ideas (for instance, there seem to be correlations with the ecliptic pole). It seems most likely these are due to other effects, with the report mentioning uncertainties in the precise beam shape and other possible small remaining instrumental and analysis issues.

The other confirmation of major significance is of the total amount of matter/energy in the universe in the form of dark energy – 72.8% (within 1.6%) as non 'particle' background, and dark matter – 22.7% (within 1.4%) of non baryonic (sub-atomic) 'particle' energy. This leaves matter, or [baryonic particles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Baryonic_particles "Baryonic particles") (atoms) at only 4.56% (within 0.16%).

Interviews with Charles Bennett and Lyman Page about WMAP

The main result of the mission is contained in the various oval maps of the CMB temperature differences. These oval images present the temperature distribution derived by the WMAP team from the observations by the telescope during the mission. Measured is the temperature obtained from a [Planck's law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck%27s_law "Planck's law") interpretation of the microwave background. The oval map covers the whole sky. The results are a snapshot of the universe around 375,000 years after the [Big Bang](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Big_Bang "Big Bang"), which happened about 13.8 billion years ago. The microwave background is very homogeneous in temperature (the relative variations from the mean, which presently is still 2.7 kelvins, are only of the order of 5×10−5). The temperature variations corresponding to the local directions are presented through different colors (the "red" directions are hotter, the "blue" directions cooler than the average).[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

Follow-on missions and future measurements
------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Follow-on missions and future measurements")]

[![Image 90](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/64/PIA16874-CobeWmapPlanckComparison-20130321.jpg/250px-PIA16874-CobeWmapPlanckComparison-20130321.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:PIA16874-CobeWmapPlanckComparison-20130321.jpg)

Comparison of [CMB](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CMB "CMB") results from [COBE](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Background_Explorer "Cosmic Background Explorer"), [WMAP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WMAP "WMAP") and [Planck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft) "Planck (spacecraft)") – 21 March 2013

The original timeline for WMAP gave it two years of observations; these were completed by September 2003. Mission extensions were granted in 2002, 2004, 2006, and 2008 giving the spacecraft a total of 9 observing years, which ended August 2010[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-news_facts-20) and in October 2010 the spacecraft was moved to a [heliocentric "graveyard" orbit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heliocentric_orbit "Heliocentric orbit").[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-dn20101007-14)

The [Planck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft) "Planck (spacecraft)") spacecraft also measured the CMB from 2009 to 2013 and aims to refine the measurements made by WMAP, both in total intensity and polarization. Various ground- and balloon-based instruments have also made CMB contributions, and others are being constructed to do so. Many are aimed at searching for the B-mode polarization expected from the simplest models of inflation, including [The E and B Experiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_E_and_B_Experiment "The E and B Experiment") (EBEX), [Spider](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spider_(polarimeter) "Spider (polarimeter)"), [BICEP and Keck Array](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/BICEP_and_Keck_Array "BICEP and Keck Array") (BICEP2), [Keck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Keck_Array "Keck Array"), [QUIET](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/QUIET "QUIET"), [Cosmology Large Angular Scale Surveyor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmology_Large_Angular_Scale_Surveyor "Cosmology Large Angular Scale Surveyor") (CLASS), [South Pole Telescope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/South_Pole_Telescope "South Pole Telescope") (SPTpol) and others.

On 21 March 2013, the European-led research team behind the Planck spacecraft released the mission's all-sky map of the cosmic microwave background.[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-NASA-20130321-34)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-NYT-20130321g-35) The map suggests the [universe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universe "Universe") is slightly older than previously thought. According to the map, subtle fluctuations in temperature were imprinted on the deep sky when the cosmos was about 370,000 years old. The imprint reflects ripples that arose as early, in the existence of the universe, as the first nonillionth (10−30) of a second. Apparently, these ripples gave rise to the present vast [cosmic web](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cosmic_Web#Large-scale_structure "Cosmic Web") of [galaxy clusters](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galaxy_cluster "Galaxy cluster") and [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter"). Based on the 2013 data, the universe contains 4.9% [ordinary matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Matter "Matter"), 26.8% [dark matter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_matter "Dark matter") and 68.3% [dark energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dark_energy "Dark energy"). On 5 February 2015, new data was released by the Planck mission, according to which the age of the universe is [13.799 ± 0.021](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft)#2015_data_release "Planck (spacecraft)")[billion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1,000,000,000_(number) "1,000,000,000 (number)") years and the Hubble constant is [67.74 ± 0.46 (km/s)/Mpc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Planck_(spacecraft)#2015_data_release "Planck (spacecraft)").[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilkinson_Microwave_Anisotropy_Probe#cite_note-Planck_2015-36)
