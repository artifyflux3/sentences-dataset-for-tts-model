In [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics"), **Appell's equation of motion** (aka the **Gibbs–Appell equation of motion**) is an alternative general formulation of [classical mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_mechanics "Classical mechanics") described by [Josiah Willard Gibbs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Josiah_Willard_Gibbs "Josiah Willard Gibbs") in 1879[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Appell's_equation_of_motion#cite_note-gibbs1879-1) and [Paul Émile Appell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_%C3%89mile_Appell "Paul Émile Appell") in 1900.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Appell's_equation_of_motion#cite_note-appell_1900a-2)

The Gibbs-Appell equation reads

where ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \alpha _{r}={\ddot {q}}_{r}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/05a17b8a3f2167d5c2a61c63d1623f9c8c556ad1) is an arbitrary generalized acceleration, or the second time derivative of the [generalized coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_coordinate "Generalized coordinate")![Image 3: {\displaystyle q_{r}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb9264e78953a531893ab79e8b6a0305c46ae43f), and ![Image 4: {\displaystyle Q_{r}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d499b1ea4f782a76aef1e44edceba5ba326f731d) is its corresponding [generalized force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_force "Generalized force"). The generalized force gives the work done

where the index ![Image 6: {\displaystyle r}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1ecb613aa2984f0576f70f86650b7c2a132538) runs over the ![Image 7: {\displaystyle D}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f34a0c600395e5d4345287e21fb26efd386990e6) generalized coordinates ![Image 8: {\displaystyle q_{r}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb9264e78953a531893ab79e8b6a0305c46ae43f), which usually correspond to the [degrees of freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Degrees_of_freedom_(physics_and_chemistry) "Degrees of freedom (physics and chemistry)") of the system. The function ![Image 9: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) is defined as the mass-weighted sum of the particle [accelerations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Acceleration "Acceleration") squared,

where the index ![Image 11: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) runs over the ![Image 12: {\displaystyle K}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2b76fce82a62ed5461908f0dc8f037de4e3686b0) particles, and

![Image 13: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} _{k}={\ddot {\mathbf {r} }}_{k}={\frac {d^{2}\mathbf {r} _{k}}{dt^{2}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/37d2859a614b0c2194776e7f9f9624389eaec986)
is the acceleration of the ![Image 14: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-th particle, the second time derivative of its [position vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Position_vector "Position vector")![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} _{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01da6ca6fcfe0ac44e859a2bad84c3d90846f195). Each ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} _{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01da6ca6fcfe0ac44e859a2bad84c3d90846f195) is expressed in terms of [generalized coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Generalized_coordinates "Generalized coordinates"), and ![Image 17: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} _{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2fef3d225da4a6f0148aff6dc7abe7ecdf7c8d3a) is expressed in terms of the generalized accelerations.

Relations to other formulations of classical mechanics
------------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Appell%27s_equation_of_motion&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Relations to other formulations of classical mechanics")]

Appell's formulation does not introduce any new physics to classical mechanics and as such is equivalent to other reformulations of classical mechanics, such as [Lagrangian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics"), and [Hamiltonian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hamiltonian_mechanics "Hamiltonian mechanics"). All classical mechanics is contained within Newton's laws of motion. In some cases, Appell's equation of motion may be more convenient than the commonly used Lagrangian mechanics, particularly when [nonholonomic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nonholonomic_system "Nonholonomic system") constraints are involved. In fact, Appell's equation leads directly to Lagrange's equations of motion.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Appell's_equation_of_motion#cite_note-3) Moreover, it can be used to derive Kane's equations, which are particularly suited for describing the motion of complex spacecraft.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Appell's_equation_of_motion#cite_note-4) Appell's formulation is an application of [Gauss' principle of least constraint](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gauss%27_principle_of_least_constraint "Gauss' principle of least constraint").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Appell's_equation_of_motion#cite_note-5)

The change in the particle positions **r**_k_ for an infinitesimal change in the _D_ generalized coordinates is

![Image 18: {\displaystyle d\mathbf {r} _{k}=\sum _{r=1}^{D}dq_{r}{\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} _{k}}{\partial q_{r}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/63d74068468c8b2b91df7cbad52aa3baa1a85a08)
Taking two derivatives with respect to time yields an equivalent equation for the accelerations

![Image 19: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial \mathbf {a} _{k}}{\partial \alpha _{r}}}={\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} _{k}}{\partial q_{r}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f06a8795835f91b9efb591cceb1861ce82c486ee)
The work done by an infinitesimal change _dq r_ in the generalized coordinates is

![Image 20: {\displaystyle dW=\sum _{r=1}^{D}Q_{r}dq_{r}=\sum _{k=1}^{N}\mathbf {F} _{k}\cdot d\mathbf {r} _{k}=\sum _{k=1}^{N}m_{k}\mathbf {a} _{k}\cdot d\mathbf {r} _{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f9d0596f22f1bdc959097ea010bb96bb1ec63a66)
where Newton's second law for the _k_ th particle

![Image 21: {\displaystyle \mathbf {F} _{k}=m_{k}\mathbf {a} _{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4b602926ab6ee4b2a16e93787b21109e59c2367b)
has been used. Substituting the formula for _d_**r**_k_ and swapping the order of the two summations yields the formulae

![Image 22: {\displaystyle dW=\sum _{r=1}^{D}Q_{r}dq_{r}=\sum _{k=1}^{N}m_{k}\mathbf {a} _{k}\cdot \sum _{r=1}^{D}dq_{r}\left({\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} _{k}}{\partial q_{r}}}\right)=\sum _{r=1}^{D}dq_{r}\sum _{k=1}^{N}m_{k}\mathbf {a} _{k}\cdot \left({\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} _{k}}{\partial q_{r}}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5598418729ec6b1dd36ab86e73125e0c7ec22da7)
Therefore, the generalized forces are

![Image 23: {\displaystyle Q_{r}=\sum _{k=1}^{N}m_{k}\mathbf {a} _{k}\cdot \left({\frac {\partial \mathbf {r} _{k}}{\partial q_{r}}}\right)=\sum _{k=1}^{N}m_{k}\mathbf {a} _{k}\cdot \left({\frac {\partial \mathbf {a} _{k}}{\partial \alpha _{r}}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/52c9a8e878da568d134f3f12d99f9368fd3c9d7d)
This equals the derivative of _S_ with respect to the generalized accelerations

![Image 24: {\displaystyle {\frac {\partial S}{\partial \alpha _{r}}}={\frac {\partial }{\partial \alpha _{r}}}{\frac {1}{2}}\sum _{k=1}^{N}m_{k}\left|\mathbf {a} _{k}\right|^{2}=\sum _{k=1}^{N}m_{k}\mathbf {a} _{k}\cdot \left({\frac {\partial \mathbf {a} _{k}}{\partial \alpha _{r}}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f6f9e8b4d0d97e79b8464d2d48129f14d398b30a)
yielding Appell's equation of motion

### Euler's equations of rigid body dynamics

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Appell%27s_equation_of_motion&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Euler's equations of rigid body dynamics")]

[Euler's equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%27s_equations_(rigid_body_dynamics) "Euler's equations (rigid body dynamics)") provide an excellent illustration of Appell's formulation.

Consider a rigid body of _N_ particles joined by rigid rods. The rotation of the body may be described by an [angular velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity")[vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_(geometric) "Vector (geometric)")![Image 26: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\omega }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7cb8af7a2f64af348e559652b6b1f0d2415ba444), and the corresponding angular acceleration vector

![Image 27: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\alpha }}={\frac {d{\boldsymbol {\omega }}}{dt}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/daf74c1daee683b8d989345bd673fc2364f0fefe)
The generalized force for a rotation is the torque ![Image 28: {\displaystyle {\textbf {N}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7a8a4a5c0c845ddce8e7c6fb66f9f5ef16472a54), since the work done for an infinitesimal rotation ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \delta {\boldsymbol {\phi }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0abd47c7d229fa82d6dc80ba763399489d4a369b) is ![Image 30: {\displaystyle dW=\mathbf {N} \cdot \delta {\boldsymbol {\phi }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/598ddb939c6bfd05ebe0a4664775fed6bf561329). The velocity of the ![Image 31: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40)-th particle is given by

![Image 32: {\displaystyle \mathbf {v} _{k}={\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times \mathbf {r} _{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/888ab808cc310381cfc369628dfa3cdca9d0157a)
where ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \mathbf {r} _{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01da6ca6fcfe0ac44e859a2bad84c3d90846f195) is the particle's position in Cartesian coordinates; its corresponding acceleration is

![Image 34: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} _{k}={\frac {d\mathbf {v} _{k}}{dt}}={\boldsymbol {\alpha }}\times \mathbf {r} _{k}+{\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times \mathbf {v} _{k}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/633fee4b8b41d111de6374e12c76339b55bbbe9e)
Therefore, the function ![Image 35: {\displaystyle S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4611d85173cd3b508e67077d4a1252c9c05abca2) may be written as

![Image 36: {\displaystyle S={\frac {1}{2}}\sum _{k=1}^{N}m_{k}\left(\mathbf {a} _{k}\cdot \mathbf {a} _{k}\right)={\frac {1}{2}}\sum _{k=1}^{N}m_{k}\left\{\left({\boldsymbol {\alpha }}\times \mathbf {r} _{k}\right)^{2}+\left({\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times \mathbf {v} _{k}\right)^{2}+2\left({\boldsymbol {\alpha }}\times \mathbf {r} _{k}\right)\cdot \left({\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times \mathbf {v} _{k}\right)\right\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eadfcca0d9fcd457fa1657c70e7ad028c3b6dd3d)
Setting the derivative of _S_ with respect to ![Image 37: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\alpha }}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a585d2bb19071162720ea56a7b087dab3ec17156) equal to the torque yields Euler's equations

![Image 38: {\displaystyle I_{xx}\alpha _{x}-\left(I_{yy}-I_{zz}\right)\omega _{y}\omega _{z}=N_{x}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ca902b328cbfeb05002a0033307b0266b8c1ce80)![Image 39: {\displaystyle I_{yy}\alpha _{y}-\left(I_{zz}-I_{xx}\right)\omega _{z}\omega _{x}=N_{y}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e99caf8bc40222bfbc6fb20e114381a5ecddd566)![Image 40: {\displaystyle I_{zz}\alpha _{z}-\left(I_{xx}-I_{yy}\right)\omega _{x}\omega _{y}=N_{z}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e6e209ae41b37baa875c397dac1a3dfe66e8e75)
