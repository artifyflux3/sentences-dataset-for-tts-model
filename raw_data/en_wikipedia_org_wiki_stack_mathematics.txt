In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics") a **stack** or **2-sheaf** is, roughly speaking, a [sheaf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheaf_(mathematics) "Sheaf (mathematics)") that takes values in [categories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Category_(mathematics) "Category (mathematics)") rather than sets. Stacks are used to formalise some of the main constructions of [descent theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Descent_theory "Descent theory"), and to construct fine moduli stacks when [fine moduli spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fine_moduli_space "Fine moduli space") do not exist.

Descent theory is concerned with generalisations of situations where [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism "Isomorphism"), compatible geometrical objects (such as [vector bundles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_bundle "Vector bundle") on [topological spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Topological_space "Topological space")) can be "glued together" within a restriction of the topological basis. In a more general set-up the restrictions are replaced with [pullbacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pullback_(category_theory) "Pullback (category theory)"); [fibred categories](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fibred_category "Fibred category") then make a good framework to discuss the possibility of such gluing. The intuitive meaning of a stack is that it is a fibred category such that "all possible gluings work". The specification of gluings requires a definition of coverings with regard to which the gluings can be considered. It turns out that the general language for describing these coverings is that of a [Grothendieck topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grothendieck_topology "Grothendieck topology"). Thus a stack is formally given as a fibred category over another _base_ category, where the base has a Grothendieck topology and where the fibred category satisfies a few axioms that ensure existence and uniqueness of certain gluings with respect to the Grothendieck topology.

Stacks are the underlying structure of algebraic stacks (also called Artin stacks) and Deligne–Mumford stacks, which generalize [schemes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scheme_(mathematics) "Scheme (mathematics)") and [algebraic spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_space "Algebraic space") and which are particularly useful in studying [moduli spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moduli_space "Moduli space"). There are inclusions:

> schemes ⊆ algebraic spaces ⊆ Deligne–Mumford stacks ⊆ algebraic stacks (Artin stacks) ⊆ stacks.

[Edidin (2003)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stack_(mathematics)#CITEREFEdidin2003) and [Fantechi (2001)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stack_(mathematics)#CITEREFFantechi2001) give a brief introductory accounts of stacks, [Gómez (2001)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stack_(mathematics)#CITEREFG%C3%B3mez2001), [Olsson (2007)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stack_(mathematics)#CITEREFOlsson2007) and [Vistoli (2005)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stack_(mathematics)#CITEREFVistoli2005) give more detailed introductions, and [Laumon & Moret-Bailly (2000)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stack_(mathematics)#CITEREFLaumonMoret-Bailly2000) describes the more advanced theory.

Motivation and history
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Stack_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Motivation and history")]

> La conclusion pratique à laquelle je suis arrivé dès maintenant, c'est que chaque fois que en vertu de mes critères, une variété de modules (ou plutôt, un schéma de modules) pour la classification des variations (globales, ou infinitésimales) de certaines structures (variétés complètes non singulières, fibrés vectoriels, etc.) ne peut exister, malgré de bonnes hypothèses de platitude, propreté, et non singularité éventuellement, la raison en est seulement l'existence d'automorphismes de la structure qui empêche la technique de descente de marcher.

Grothendieck's letter to Serre, 1959 Nov 5.

The concept of stacks has its origin in the definition of effective descent data in [Grothendieck (1959)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stack_(mathematics)#CITEREFGrothendieck1959). In a 1959 letter to Serre, Grothendieck observed that a fundamental obstruction to constructing good moduli spaces is the existence of [automorphisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Automorphism "Automorphism"). A major motivation for stacks is that if a moduli _space_ for some problem does not exist because of the existence of automorphisms, it may still be possible to construct a moduli _stack_.

[Mumford (1965)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stack_(mathematics)#CITEREFMumford1965) studied the [Picard group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Picard_group "Picard group") of the [moduli stack of elliptic curves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moduli_stack_of_elliptic_curves "Moduli stack of elliptic curves"), before stacks had been defined. Stacks were first defined by Giraud([1966](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stack_(mathematics)#CITEREFGiraud1966), [1971](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stack_(mathematics)#CITEREFGiraud1971)), and the term "stack" was introduced by [Deligne & Mumford (1969)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stack_(mathematics)#CITEREFDeligneMumford1969) for the original French term "champ" meaning "field". In this paper they also introduced **Deligne–Mumford stacks**, which they called algebraic stacks, though the term "algebraic stack" now usually refers to the more general **Artin stacks** introduced by [Artin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Michael_Artin "Michael Artin")([1974](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stack_(mathematics)#CITEREFArtin1974)).

When defining quotients of schemes by group actions, it is often impossible for the quotient to be a scheme and still satisfy desirable properties for a quotient. For example, if a few points have non-trivial stabilisers, then the [categorical quotient](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Categorical_quotient "Categorical quotient") will not exist among schemes, but it will exist as a stack.

In the same way, [moduli spaces](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moduli_space "Moduli space") of curves, vector bundles, or other geometric objects are often best defined as stacks instead of schemes. Constructions of moduli spaces often proceed by first constructing a larger space parametrizing the objects in question, and then [quotienting by group action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quotient_stack "Quotient stack") to account for objects with automorphisms which have been overcounted.

The category _c_ is called a **[prestack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prestack "Prestack")** over a category _C_ with a [Grothendieck topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Grothendieck_topology "Grothendieck topology") if it is fibered over _C_ and for any object _U_ of _C_ and objects _x_, _y_ of _c_ with image _U_, the functor from the over category C/U to sets taking _F_:_V_→_U_ to Hom(_F_*_x_,_F_*_y_) is a sheaf. This terminology is not consistent with the terminology for sheaves: prestacks are the analogues of separated presheaves rather than presheaves. Some authors require this as a property of stacks, rather than of prestacks.

The category _c_ is called a **stack** over the category _C_ with a Grothendieck topology if it is a prestack over _C_ and every descent datum is effective. A **descent datum** consists roughly of a covering of an object _V_ of _C_ by a family _V i_, elements _x i_ in the fiber over _V i_, and morphisms _f ji_ between the restrictions of _x i_ and _x j_ to _V ij_=_V i_×_V_ _V j_ satisfying the compatibility condition _f ki_ = _f kj f ji_. The descent datum is called **effective** if the elements _x i_ are essentially the pullbacks of an element _x_ with image _V_.

A stack is called a **stack in groupoids** or a **(2,1)-sheaf** if it is also fibered in groupoids, meaning that its fibers (the inverse images of objects of _C_) are groupoids. Some authors use the word "stack" to refer to the more restrictive notion of a stack in groupoids.

An **algebraic stack** or **Artin stack** is a stack in groupoids _X_ over the fppf site such that the diagonal map of _X_ is representable and there exists a smooth surjection from (the stack associated to) a scheme to X. A morphism _Y_![Image 23: {\displaystyle \rightarrow }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53e574cc3aa5b4bf5f3f5906caf121a378eef08b)_X_ of stacks is **representable** if, for every morphism _S_![Image 24: {\displaystyle \rightarrow }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53e574cc3aa5b4bf5f3f5906caf121a378eef08b)_X_ from (the stack associated to) a scheme to X, the [fiber product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fiber_product "Fiber product")_Y_×_X_ _S_ is isomorphic to (the stack associated to) an [algebraic space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_space "Algebraic space"). The **fiber product** of stacks is defined using the usual [universal property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_property "Universal property"), and changing the requirement that diagrams commute to the requirement that they [2-commute](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=2-commute&action=edit&redlink=1 "2-commute (page does not exist)"). See also [morphism of algebraic stacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Morphism_of_algebraic_stacks "Morphism of algebraic stacks") for further information.

The motivation behind the representability of the diagonal is the following: the diagonal morphism ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \Delta :{\mathfrak {X}}\to {\mathfrak {X}}\times {\mathfrak {X}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eee6ac1e1d348d5f83c9d540e16712c3f8d89274) is representable if and only if for any pair of morphisms of algebraic spaces ![Image 26: {\displaystyle X,Y\to {\mathfrak {X}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4354c41cfa7c3d1f6b980ef9e4b4cb03d50ad1f5), their fiber product ![Image 27: {\displaystyle X\times _{\mathfrak {X}}Y}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8cb620c5b9c892a48acce4144fceb43e44688b74) is representable.

A **[Deligne–Mumford stack](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deligne%E2%80%93Mumford_stack "Deligne–Mumford stack")** is an algebraic stack _X_ such that there is an étale surjection from a scheme to _X_. Roughly speaking, Deligne–Mumford stacks can be thought of as algebraic stacks whose objects have no infinitesimal automorphisms.

#### Relative spec and proj

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Stack_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=15 "Edit section: Relative spec and proj")]

If _A_ is a quasi-coherent [sheaf of algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheaf_of_algebras "Sheaf of algebras") in an algebraic stack _X_ over a scheme _S_, then there is a stack Spec(_A_) generalizing the construction of the spectrum Spec(_A_) of a commutative ring _A_. An object of Spec(_A_) is given by an _S_-scheme _T_, an object _x_ of _X_(_T_), and a morphism of sheaves of algebras from _x_*(_A_) to the coordinate ring _O_(_T_) of _T_.

If _A_ is a quasi-coherent sheaf of graded algebras in an algebraic stack _X_ over a scheme _S_, then there is a stack Proj(_A_) generalizing the construction of the projective scheme Proj(_A_) of a graded ring _A_.

Quasi-coherent sheaves on algebraic stacks
------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Stack_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Quasi-coherent sheaves on algebraic stacks")]

On an algebraic stack one can construct a category of quasi-coherent sheaves similar to the category of quasi-coherent sheaves over a scheme.

A quasi-coherent sheaf is roughly one that looks locally like the [sheaf of a module](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sheaf_of_a_module "Sheaf of a module") over a ring. The first problem is to decide what one means by "locally": this involves the choice of a Grothendieck topology, and there are many possible choices for this, all of which have some problems and none of which seem completely satisfactory. The Grothendieck topology should be strong enough so that the stack is locally affine in this topology: schemes are locally affine in the Zariski topology so this is a good choice for schemes as Serre discovered, algebraic spaces and Deligne–Mumford stacks are locally affine in the etale topology so one usually uses the etale topology for these, while algebraic stacks are locally affine in the smooth topology so one can use the smooth topology in this case. For general algebraic stacks the etale topology does not have enough open sets: for example, if G is a smooth connected group then the only etale covers of the classifying stack BG are unions of copies of BG, which are not enough to give the right theory of quasicoherent sheaves.

Instead of using the smooth topology for algebraic stacks one often uses a modification of it called the [Lis-Et topology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Smooth_topology "Smooth topology") (short for Lisse-Etale: lisse is the French term for smooth), which has the same open sets as the smooth topology but the open covers are given by etale rather than smooth maps. This usually seems to lead to an equivalent category of quasi-coherent sheaves, but is easier to use: for example it is easier to compare with the etale topology on algebraic spaces. The Lis-Et topology has a subtle technical problem: a morphism between stacks does not in general give a morphism between the corresponding topoi. (The problem is that while one can construct a pair of adjoint functors _f_*, _f_*, as needed for a geometric morphism of topoi, the functor _f_* is not left exact in general. This problem is notorious for having caused some errors in published papers and books.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stack_(mathematics)#cite_note-5)) This means that constructing the pullback of a quasicoherent sheaf under a morphism of stacks requires some extra effort.

It is also possible to use finer topologies. Most reasonable "sufficiently large" Grothendieck topologies seem to lead to equivalent categories of quasi-coherent sheaves, but the larger a topology is the harder it is to handle, so one generally prefers to use smaller topologies as long as they have enough open sets. For example, the big fppf topology leads to essentially the same category of quasi-coherent sheaves as the Lis-Et topology, but has a subtle problem: the natural embedding of quasi-coherent sheaves into O _X_ modules in this topology is not exact (it does not preserve kernels in general).

Other types of stack
--------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Stack_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: Other types of stack")]

**[Differentiable stacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differentiable_stack "Differentiable stack")** and **topological stacks** are defined in a way similar to algebraic stacks, except that the underlying category of affine schemes is replaced by the category of smooth manifolds or topological spaces.

More generally one can define the notion of an _n_-sheaf or _n_–1 stack, which is roughly a sort of sheaf taking values in _n_–1 categories. There are several inequivalent ways of doing this. 1-sheaves are the same as sheaves, and 2-sheaves are the same as stacks. They are called [higher stacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Higher_stack "Higher stack").

A very similar and analogous extension is to develop the stack theory on non-discrete objects (i.e., a space is really a [spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spectrum_(topology) "Spectrum (topology)") in algebraic topology). The resulting stacky objects are called [derived stacks](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derived_stack "Derived stack") (or spectral stacks). [Jacob Lurie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacob_Lurie "Jacob Lurie")'s under-construction book _Spectral Algebraic Geometry_ studies a generalization that he calls a [spectral Deligne–Mumford stack](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Spectral_Deligne%E2%80%93Mumford_stack&action=edit&redlink=1 "Spectral Deligne–Mumford stack (page does not exist)"). By definition, it is a ringed [∞-topos](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%E2%88%9E-topos "∞-topos") that is étale-locally the [étale spectrum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89tale_spectrum "Étale spectrum") of an [**E**∞-ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/E-infinity_ring "E-infinity ring") (this notion subsumes that of a [derived scheme](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Derived_scheme "Derived scheme"), at least in characteristic zero.)

Set-theoretical problems
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Stack_(mathematics)&action=edit&section=26 "Edit section: Set-theoretical problems")]

There are some minor set theoretical problems with the usual foundation of the theory of stacks, because stacks are often defined as certain functors to the category of sets and are therefore not sets. There are several ways to deal with this problem:

*   One can work with Grothendieck universes: a stack is then a functor between classes of some fixed Grothendieck universe, so these classes and the stacks are sets in a larger Grothendieck universe. The drawback of this approach is that one has to assume the existence of enough Grothendieck universes, which is essentially a [large cardinal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Large_cardinal "Large cardinal") axiom.
*   One can define stacks as functors to the set of sets of sufficiently large rank, and keep careful track of the ranks of the various sets one uses. The problem with this is that it involves some additional rather tiresome bookkeeping.
*   One can use reflection principles from set theory stating that one can find set models of any finite fragment of the axioms of ZFC to show that one can automatically find sets that are sufficiently close approximations to the universe of all sets.
*   One can simply ignore the problem. This is the approach taken by many authors.
