An **International Standard Serial Number** (**ISSN**) is an eight-digit code to [uniquely identify](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unique_identifier "Unique identifier") a [periodical publication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serial_(publishing) "Serial (publishing)") (periodical), such as a magazine.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISSN_(identifier)#cite_note-Whatis-1) The ISSN is especially helpful in distinguishing between serials with the same title. ISSNs are used in ordering, cataloging, interlibrary loans, and other practices in connection with serial literature.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISSN_(identifier)#cite_note-2)

The ISSN system was first drafted as an [International Organization for Standardization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Organization_for_Standardization "International Organization for Standardization") (ISO) [international standard](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_standard "International standard") in 1971 and published as ISO 3297 in 1975.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISSN_(identifier)#cite_note-3) ISO subcommittee [TC 46/SC 9](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/TC_46/SC_9 "TC 46/SC 9") is responsible for maintaining the standard.

When a serial with the same [content](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Content_(media) "Content (media)") is published in more than one [media type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Media_(communication) "Media (communication)"), a different ISSN is assigned to each media type. For example, many serials are published both in [print](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Printing "Printing") and [electronic media](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_media "Electronic media"). The ISSN system refers to these types as **print ISSN** (**p-ISSN**) and **electronic ISSN** (**e-ISSN**).[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISSN_(identifier)#cite_note-4) Consequently, as defined in ISO 3297:2007, every serial in the ISSN system is also assigned a **linking ISSN** (**ISSN-L**), typically the same as the ISSN assigned to the serial in its first published medium, which links together all ISSNs assigned to the serial in every medium.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISSN_(identifier)#cite_note-5)

Code format
-----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ISSN&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Code format")]

An ISSN is an eight-digit code, divided by a hyphen into two four-digit numbers.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISSN_(identifier)#cite_note-Whatis-1) The last digit, which may be zero through nine or an X, is a [check digit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Check_digit "Check digit"), so the ISSN is uniquely represented by its first seven digits. Formally, the general form of the ISSN (also named "ISSN structure" or "ISSN syntax") can be expressed as follows:[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISSN_(identifier)#cite_note-rfc3044-6)

`NNNN-NNNC`

where `N` is in the set {_0,1,2,...,9_}, a decimal digit character, and `C` is in {_0,1,2,...,9,X_}; or by a [Perl Compatible Regular Expressions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perl_Compatible_Regular_Expressions "Perl Compatible Regular Expressions") (PCRE) [regular expression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_expression "Regular expression"):

`^[0-9]{4}-[0-9]{3}[0-9X]$`.

For example, the ISSN of the journal _[Hearing Research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hearing\_Research "Hearing Research")_, is 0378-5955, where the final 5 is the check digit, that is `C`=5. To calculate the check digit, the following algorithm may be used:

Each of the first seven digits of the ISSN is multiplied by its position in the number, counting from the right, that is, 8, 7, 6, 5, 4, 3, and 2, respectively, and the resulting products are added: 
0⋅8+3⋅7+7⋅6+8⋅5+5⋅4+9⋅3+5⋅2=0+21+42+40+20+27+10=160.{\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}&0\cdot 8+3\cdot 7+7\cdot 6+8\cdot 5+5\cdot 4+9\cdot 3+5\cdot 2\\&=0+21+42+40+20+27+10\\&=160\;.\end{aligned}}}![Image 15: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}&0\cdot 8+3\cdot 7+7\cdot 6+8\cdot 5+5\cdot 4+9\cdot 3+5\cdot 2\\&=0+21+42+40+20+27+10\\&=160\;.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a413195e95f6e5787852022cac196f5594b2a9e7)

The [remainder](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Remainder "Remainder") of this sum [modulo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modulo "Modulo") 11 is then calculated: 160 11=14 remainder 6=14+6 11{\displaystyle {\frac {160}{11}}=14{\mbox{ remainder }}6=14+{\frac {6}{11}}}![Image 16: {\displaystyle {\frac {160}{11}}=14{\mbox{ remainder }}6=14+{\frac {6}{11}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/10546bc3ccf2e421660938a3b52242c1e2485b42)

If there is no remainder, the check digit is 0; otherwise the remainder is subtracted from 11. If the result is less than 10, it yields the check digit: 11−6=5.{\displaystyle 11-6=5\;.}![Image 17: {\displaystyle 11-6=5\;.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a300e4a478f863a5c8b04707ffa2af63ec82ab8d) Thus, in this example, the check digit `C` is 5.

 If the result is 10 (that is, if the remainder is 1), the check digit is an uppercase X (like a [Roman ten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_numerals "Roman numerals")).

To confirm the check digit, calculate the sum of all eight digits of the ISSN multiplied by their position in the number, counting from the right. (If the check digit is X, add 10 to the sum.) The remainder of the sum modulo 11 must be 0. There is an online ISSN checker that can validate an ISSN, based on the above algorithm.[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]

### In EANs

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ISSN&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: In EANs")]

ISSNs can be encoded in [EAN-13 bar codes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Article_Number_(EAN) "International Article Number (EAN)") with a 977 "country code" (compare the 978 "[bookland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bookland "Bookland")" country code for [ISBNs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISBN "ISBN")), followed by the 7 main digits of the ISSN (the check digit is not included), followed by 2 publisher-defined digits, followed by the EAN check digit (in most cases, this will not match the ISSN check digit, since they are each calculated in a different way).[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISSN_(identifier)#cite_note-7)

Code assignment, maintenance and look-up
----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ISSN&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Code assignment, maintenance and look-up")]

ISSN codes are assigned by a network of ISSN National Centres, usually located at [national libraries](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_library "National library") and coordinated by the **ISSN International Centre** (CIEPS[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISSN_(identifier)#cite_note-8)) based in [Paris](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paris "Paris"). The International Centre is an [intergovernmental organization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intergovernmental_organization "Intergovernmental organization") created in 1974 through an agreement between [UNESCO](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UNESCO "UNESCO") and the French government.

### Linking ISSN

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ISSN&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Linking ISSN")]

_ISSN-L_ is a [unique identifier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unique_identifier "Unique identifier") for all versions of the serial containing the same [content](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Content_(media) "Content (media)") across different media. As defined by _ISO 3297:2007_, the "linking ISSN (ISSN-L)" provides a mechanism for collocation or linking among the different media versions of the same continuing resource. The _ISSN-L_ is one of a serial's existing ISSNs, so does not change the use or assignment of "ordinary" ISSNs;[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISSN_(identifier)#cite_note-9) it is based on the ISSN of the first published medium version of the publication. If the print and online versions of the publication are published at the same time, the ISSN of the print version is chosen as the basis of the _ISSN-L_.

With _ISSN-L_ is possible to designate one single ISSN for all those media versions of the title. The use of _ISSN-L_ facilitates search, retrieval and delivery across all media versions for services like [OpenURL](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/OpenURL "OpenURL"), [library catalogues](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Library_catalogue "Library catalogue"), [search engines](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Search_engine "Search engine") or [knowledge bases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_base "Knowledge base").

### Register

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ISSN&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Register")]

The International Centre maintains a database of all ISSNs assigned worldwide, the _ISDS Register_ (International Serials Data System), otherwise known as the _ISSN Register_. At the end of 2016,[[update]](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ISSN&action=edit) the ISSN Register contained records for 1,943,572 items.[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISSN_(identifier)#cite_note-10) The Register is not freely available for interrogation on the web, but is available by subscription.

*   The print version of a serial typically will include the ISSN code as part of the publication information.
*   Most serial websites contain ISSN code information.
*   Derivative lists of publications will often contain ISSN codes; these can be found through on-line searches with the ISSN code itself or serial title.
*   [WorldCat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WorldCat "WorldCat") permits searching its catalog by ISSN, by entering "issn:" before the code in the query field. One can also go directly to an ISSN's record by appending it to "`https://www.worldcat.org/ISSN/`", e.g. [n2:1021-9749 – Search Results](https://www.worldcat.org/ISSN/1021-9749). This does not query the ISSN Register itself, but rather shows whether any WorldCat library holds an item with the given ISSN.

Comparison with other identifiers
---------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ISSN&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: Comparison with other identifiers")]

ISSN and [ISBN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISBN "ISBN") codes are similar in concept, where ISBNs are assigned to individual [books](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Book "Book"). An ISBN might be assigned for particular issues of a serial, in addition to the ISSN code for the serial as a whole. An ISSN, unlike the ISBN code, is an anonymous identifier associated with a serial title, containing no [information](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information "Information") as to the [publisher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Publisher "Publisher") or its [location](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Location_(geography) "Location (geography)"). For this reason a new ISSN is assigned to a serial each time it undergoes a major title change.

### Extensions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ISSN&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Extensions")]

Since the ISSN applies to an entire serial, other identifiers have been built on top of it to allow references to specific volumes, articles, or other identifiable components (like the [table of contents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Table_of_contents "Table of contents")): the [Publisher Item Identifier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Publisher_Item_Identifier "Publisher Item Identifier") (PII) and the [Serial Item and Contribution Identifier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Serial_Item_and_Contribution_Identifier "Serial Item and Contribution Identifier") (SICI).

### Media versus content

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ISSN&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Media versus content")]

Separate ISSNs are needed for serials in different media (except reproduction [microforms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microform "Microform")). Thus, the [print](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Print_media "Print media") and [electronic media](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_media "Electronic media") versions of a serial need separate ISSNs,[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISSN_(identifier)#cite_note-11) and [CD-ROM](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CD-ROM "CD-ROM") versions and [web](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_Wide_Web "World Wide Web") versions require different ISSNs. However, the same ISSN can be used for different file formats (e.g. [PDF](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PDF "PDF") and [HTML](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HTML "HTML")) of the same online serial.

This "media-oriented identification" of serials made sense in the 1970s. In the 1990s and onward, with personal computers, better screens, and the Web, it makes sense to consider only _content_, independent of media. This "content-oriented identification" of serials was a [repressed demand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Excess_demand "Excess demand") during a decade, but no ISSN update or initiative occurred. A natural extension for ISSN, the unique-identification of the articles in the serials, was the main demand application. An alternative serials' contents model arrived with the [indecs Content Model](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Indecs_Content_Model "Indecs Content Model") and its application, the [digital object identifier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_object_identifier "Digital object identifier") (DOI), an ISSN-independent initiative, consolidated in the 2000s.

Only later, in 2007, ISSN-L was defined in the new ISSN standard (ISO 3297:2007) as an "ISSN designated by the ISSN Network to enable collocation or versions of a continuing resource linking among the different media".[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISSN_(identifier)#cite_note-issnL-12)

Use in URNs
-----------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ISSN&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Use in URNs")]

An ISSN can be encoded as a [uniform resource name](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_resource_name "Uniform resource name") (URN) by prefixing it with "`urn:ISSN:`".[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISSN_(identifier)#cite_note-rfc3044-6) For example, [_Rail_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rail_(magazine) "Rail (magazine)") could be referred to as "`urn:ISSN:0953-4563`". URN namespaces are case-sensitive, and the ISSN namespace is all caps.[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISSN_(identifier)#cite_note-13) If the checksum digit is "X" then it is always encoded in uppercase in a URN.

### Problems

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ISSN&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Problems")]

The URNs are [content-oriented](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISSN_(identifier)#Media_versus_content), but ISSN is media-oriented:

*   **ISSN is not unique** when the concept is "a journal is a set of contents, generally copyrighted content": the same journal (same contents and same copyrights) may have two or more ISSN codes. A URN needs to point to "unique content" (a "unique journal" as a "set of contents" reference).

Example: [Nature](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature_(journal) "Nature (journal)") has an ISSN for print, 0028-0836, and another for the same content on the Web, 1476-4687; only the oldest (0028-0836) is used as a [unique identifier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unique_identifier "Unique identifier"). As the ISSN is not unique, the [U.S. National Library of Medicine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/U.S._National_Library_of_Medicine "U.S. National Library of Medicine") needed to create, prior to 2007, the NLM Unique ID (JID).[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISSN_(identifier)#cite_note-14)
*   **ISSN** does not offer [resolution mechanisms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_resource_name#Resolvers "Uniform resource name") like a [digital object identifier](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_object_identifier "Digital object identifier") (DOI) or a URN does, so the DOI is used as a URN for articles, with (for historical reasons) no need for an ISSN's existence.

Example: the DOI name "10.1038/nature13777" can be represented as an HTTP string by `https://doi.org/10.1038/nature13777`, and is [redirected](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/URL_redirection "URL redirection") (resolved) to the current article's page; but there is no ISSN online service, like `http://dx.issn.org/`, to resolve the ISSN of the journal (in this sample [1476-4687](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nature_(journal) "Nature (journal)")).
A unique URN for serials simplifies the search, recovery and delivery of data for various services including, in particular, [search systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_retrieval "Information retrieval") and [knowledge databases](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Knowledge_base "Knowledge base").[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISSN_(identifier)#cite_note-issnL-12)**ISSN-L** (see [Linking ISSN](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISSN_(identifier)#Linking_ISSN) above) was created to fill this gap.

Media category labels
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ISSN&action=edit&section=11 "Edit section: Media category labels")]

The two standard categories of media in which serials are most available are _print_ and _electronic_. In [metadata](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Metadata "Metadata") contexts (e.g., [JATS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/JATS "JATS")), these may have standard labels.

### Print ISSN

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ISSN&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Print ISSN")]

_p-ISSN_ is a standard label for "Print ISSN", the ISSN for the [print media](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Print_media "Print media") (paper) version of a serial. Usually it is the "default media" and so the "default ISSN".

### Electronic ISSN

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=ISSN&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Electronic ISSN")]

_e-ISSN_ (or _eISSN_) is a standard label for "Electronic ISSN", the ISSN for the [electronic media](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Electronic_media "Electronic media") (online) version of a serial.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ISSN_(identifier)#cite_note-15)
