Animation of a pendulum showing the [velocity and acceleration vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Equations_of_motion "Equations of motion").

A **pendulum** is a body suspended from a fixed support such that it freely swings back and forth under the influence of gravity. When a pendulum is displaced sideways from its resting, equilibrium position, it is subject to a restoring force due to gravity that will accelerate it back towards the equilibrium position. When released, the restoring force acting on the pendulum's mass causes it to oscillate about the equilibrium position, swinging it back and forth. The mathematics of [pendulums](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum "Pendulum") are in general quite complicated. Simplifying assumptions can be made, which in the case of a [simple pendulum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#Simple_gravity_pendulum) allow the equations of motion to be solved analytically for small-angle oscillations.

Simple gravity pendulum
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pendulum_(mechanics)&action=edit&section=1 "Edit section: Simple gravity pendulum")]

A _simple gravity pendulum_[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#cite_note-1) is an idealized mathematical model of a real pendulum.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#cite_note-Hyperphysics-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#cite_note-4) It is a weight (or [bob](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bob_(physics) "Bob (physics)")) on the end of a massless cord suspended from a [pivot](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/pivot "wikt:pivot"), without [friction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friction "Friction"). Since in the model there is no frictional energy loss, when given an initial displacement it swings back and forth with a constant [amplitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amplitude "Amplitude"). The model is based on the assumptions:

*   The rod or cord is massless, inextensible and always remains under tension.
*   The bob is a point mass.
*   The motion occurs in [two dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two_dimensions "Two dimensions").
*   The motion does not lose energy to external [friction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friction "Friction") or [air resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Air_resistance "Air resistance").
*   The gravitational field is uniform.
*   The support is immobile.

The [differential equation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_equation "Differential equation") which governs the motion of a simple pendulum is

![Image 2: {\displaystyle {\frac {d^{2}\theta }{dt^{2}}}+{\frac {g}{\ell }}\sin \theta =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/36e0d601a33a7562dfb162abd7e58859a40ccff1)Eq. 1

where g is the [magnitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Magnitude_(mathematics) "Magnitude (mathematics)") of the [gravitational field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravity_of_Earth "Gravity of Earth"), ℓ is the length of the rod or cord, and θ is the angle from the vertical to the pendulum.

**"Force" derivation of (**[Eq. 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#math_Eq._1)**)**

[![Image 3](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Pendulum_gravity.svg/330px-Pendulum_gravity.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Pendulum_gravity.svg)

**Figure 1.** Force diagram of a simple gravity pendulum.

Consider Figure 1 on the right, which shows the forces acting on a simple pendulum. Note that the path of the pendulum sweeps out an [arc](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arc_(geometry) "Arc (geometry)") of a circle. The angle θ is measured in [radians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian"), and this is crucial for this formula. The blue arrow is the [gravitational force](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_force "Gravitational force") acting on the bob, and the violet arrows are that same force resolved into components parallel and perpendicular to the bob's instantaneous motion. The direction of the bob's instantaneous [velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Velocity "Velocity") always points along the red axis, which is considered the tangential axis because its direction is always tangent to the circle. Consider [Newton's second law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_second_law "Newton's second law"), ![Image 4: {\displaystyle F=ma}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ca4e42b7d6d66f52294364928cb5f7c590f514c) where F is the sum of forces on the object, m is mass, and a is the acceleration. Newton's equation can be applied to the tangential axis only. This is because only changes in speed are of concern and the bob is forced to stay in a circular path. The short violet arrow represents the component of the gravitational force in the tangential axis, and trigonometry can be used to determine its magnitude. Thus, ![Image 5: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}F&=-mg\sin \theta =ma,\qquad {\text{so}}\\a&=-g\sin \theta ,\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92976e8a93fcd21f6d52f1dbb572a2b1253ed037) where g is the acceleration due to gravity near the surface of the earth. The negative sign on the right hand side implies that θ and a always point in opposite directions. This makes sense because when a pendulum swings further to the left, it is expected to accelerate back toward the right.

This linear acceleration a along the red axis can be related to the change in angle θ by the arc length formulas; s is arc length: ![Image 6: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}s&=\ell \theta ,\\v&={\frac {ds}{dt}}=\ell {\frac {d\theta }{dt}},\\a&={\frac {d^{2}s}{dt^{2}}}=\ell {\frac {d^{2}\theta }{dt^{2}}},\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/415f1942e08f8bf22360bad98425e6caa7e763e0) thus: ![Image 7: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\ell {\frac {d^{2}\theta }{dt^{2}}}&=-g\sin \theta ,\\{\frac {d^{2}\theta }{dt^{2}}}+{\frac {g}{\ell }}\sin \theta &=0.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb8d291cb1342997278eb83fb46190ac1f045774)

**"Torque" derivation of (**[Eq. 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#math_Eq._1)**)**

Equation (1) can be obtained using two definitions for torque. ![Image 8: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\tau }}=\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {F} ={\frac {d\mathbf {L} }{dt}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/84abc521d09760470a0e3a56515518e4dd866ae1)

First start by defining the torque on the pendulum bob using the force due to gravity. ![Image 9: {\displaystyle {\boldsymbol {\tau }}=\mathbf {l} \times \mathbf {F} _{\mathrm {g} },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/386c2ab7a35e9b572493bcaeb60105372bd36ca2) where **l** is the length vector of the pendulum and **F**g is the force due to gravity.

For now just consider the magnitude of the torque on the pendulum. ![Image 10: {\displaystyle |{\boldsymbol {\tau }}|=-mg\ell \sin \theta ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5cd8f009c3bc5563a9083de86b4b7e67e07f85d3) where m is the mass of the pendulum, g is the acceleration due to gravity, l is the length of the pendulum, and θ is the angle between the length vector and the force due to gravity.

Next rewrite the angular momentum. ![Image 11: {\displaystyle \mathbf {L} =\mathbf {r} \times \mathbf {p} =m\mathbf {r} \times ({\boldsymbol {\omega }}\times \mathbf {r} ).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d7b773c9e85c7d000110525d07f65e2facc93434) Again just consider the magnitude of the angular momentum. ![Image 12: {\displaystyle |\mathbf {L} |=mr^{2}\omega =m\ell ^{2}{\frac {d\theta }{dt}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a25bed0979e91009db5d95ee6b15dbf99ad60288) and its time derivative ![Image 13: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}|\mathbf {L} |=m\ell ^{2}{\frac {d^{2}\theta }{dt^{2}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7608da015d8fdd4946cd06c5d5717ab4d6ca488e)

The magnitudes can then be compared using **τ** = ⁠_d_**L**/_dt_⁠

![Image 14: {\displaystyle -mg\ell \sin \theta =m\ell ^{2}{\frac {d^{2}\theta }{dt^{2}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c0a726d6d4b31baf69c52debede8caf75fb2da85) thus: ![Image 15: {\displaystyle {\frac {d^{2}\theta }{dt^{2}}}+{\frac {g}{\ell }}\sin \theta =0,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/21fcc2029408d6d674b75431c3eb7ce216e316fb) which is the same result as obtained through force analysis.

**"Energy" derivation of (**[Eq. 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#math_Eq._1)**)**

[![Image 16](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/11/Simple_pendulum_height.svg/330px-Simple_pendulum_height.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Simple_pendulum_height.svg)

**Figure 2.** Trigonometry of a simple gravity pendulum.

It can also be obtained via the [conservation of mechanical energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conservation_of_mechanical_energy "Conservation of mechanical energy") principle: any object falling a vertical distance ![Image 17: {\displaystyle h}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b26be3e694314bc90c3215047e4a2010c6ee184a) would acquire [kinetic energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kinetic_energy "Kinetic energy") equal to that which it lost to the fall. In other words, [gravitational potential energy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_energy "Gravitational energy") is converted into kinetic energy. Change in potential energy is given by ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \Delta U=mgh.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a0f1349a0859d905641977226d4de256c0c5126)

The change in kinetic energy (body started from rest) is given by ![Image 19: {\displaystyle \Delta K={\tfrac {1}{2}}mv^{2}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cb280e9c9a47442f72f260cc49c14595b7feb16d)

Since no energy is lost, the gain in one must be equal to the loss in the other ![Image 20: {\displaystyle {\tfrac {1}{2}}mv^{2}=mgh.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f18a04650c151f69e7da4b4bc9d0bb3d44883b0)

The change in velocity for a given change in height can be expressed as ![Image 21: {\displaystyle v={\sqrt {2gh}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ebfc57513a63c0303701461b82773e9b19b57ac5)

Using the arc length formula above, this equation can be rewritten in terms of ⁠_dθ_/_dt_⁠: ![Image 22: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}v=\ell {\frac {d\theta }{dt}}&={\sqrt {2gh}},\quad {\text{so}}\\{\frac {d\theta }{dt}}&={\frac {\sqrt {2gh}}{\ell }},\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad0f71525138357f29187ea291322ca18d8286f0) where h is the vertical distance the pendulum fell. Look at Figure 2, which presents the trigonometry of a simple pendulum. If the pendulum starts its swing from some initial angle _θ_ 0, then _y_ 0, the vertical distance from the screw, is given by ![Image 23: {\displaystyle y_{0}=\ell \cos \theta _{0}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/35ea11788db5d70fe29063ae14106e22f0d81f9a)

Similarly, when _y_ 1, then ![Image 24: {\displaystyle y_{1}=\ell \cos \theta .}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/460f9380fab7c78962cc033c52be355ae2950b75)

Then h is the difference of the two ![Image 25: {\displaystyle h=\ell \left(\cos \theta -\cos \theta _{0}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5b7a3f249387bfab75fe88848a7854e199f0fd02)

In terms of ⁠_dθ_/_dt_⁠ gives

![Image 26: {\displaystyle {\frac {d\theta }{dt}}={\sqrt {{\frac {2g}{\ell }}(\cos \theta -\cos \theta _{0})}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0f9b419f7b1c7c05a6dd93a7e3642cd2245f2687)Eq. 2

This equation is known as the _first integral of motion_, it gives the velocity in terms of the location and includes an integration constant related to the initial displacement (_θ_ 0). Next, differentiate by applying the [chain rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chain_rule "Chain rule"), with respect to time to get the acceleration ![Image 27: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {d}{dt}}{\frac {d\theta }{dt}}&={\frac {d}{dt}}{\sqrt {{\frac {2g}{\ell }}\left(\cos \theta -\cos \theta _{0}\right)}},\\{\frac {d^{2}\theta }{dt^{2}}}&={\frac {1}{2}}{\frac {-{\frac {2g}{\ell }}\sin \theta }{\sqrt {{\frac {2g}{\ell }}(\cos \theta -\cos \theta _{0})}}}{\frac {d\theta }{dt}}\\&={\frac {1}{2}}{\frac {-{\frac {2g}{\ell }}\sin \theta }{\sqrt {{\frac {2g}{\ell }}(\cos \theta -\cos \theta _{0})}}}{\sqrt {{\frac {2g}{\ell }}(\cos \theta -\cos \theta _{0})}}=-{\frac {g}{\ell }}\sin \theta ,\\{\frac {d^{2}\theta }{dt^{2}}}&+{\frac {g}{\ell }}\sin \theta =0,\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1daa81e0971b74b985ac6b1a05ef4d455068ee3e)

which is the same result as obtained through force analysis.

**"Lagrange" derivation of (**[Eq. 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#math_Eq._1)**)**

[![Image 28: Coordinates of a simple gravity pendulum.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/df/Coordinates_of_a_simple_gravity_pendulum.png/330px-Coordinates_of_a_simple_gravity_pendulum.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Coordinates_of_a_simple_gravity_pendulum.png)

Coordinates of a simple gravity pendulum.

**[Equation 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#math_Equation_1)** can additionally be obtained through [Lagrangian Mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_Mechanics "Lagrangian Mechanics"). More specifically, using the [Euler–Lagrange equations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euler%E2%80%93Lagrange_equations "Euler–Lagrange equations") (or Lagrange's equations of the second kind) by identifying the Lagrangian of the system (![Image 29: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9027196ecb178d598958555ea01c43157d83597c)), the constraints (![Image 30: {\displaystyle q}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/06809d64fa7c817ffc7e323f85997f783dbdf71d)) and solving the following system of equations

![Image 31: {\displaystyle {\frac {d}{dt}}\left({\frac {\partial {\mathcal {L}}}{\partial {\dot {q_{j}}}}}\right)={\frac {\partial {\mathcal {L}}}{\partial q_{j}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8de837a73c06f33975f390a16eb06727e83db7ec)

If the origin of the [Cartesian coordinate system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") is defined as the point of suspension (or simply pivot), then the bob is at

![Image 32: {\displaystyle x=\ell \sin {\theta },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/827f14c591ef8fd5fea6130a643ff8726a55b03f)![Image 33: {\displaystyle y=-\ell \cos {\theta },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01392f24b804b09f7766dd4d865a9c72c3d0bc0d)

and the velocity of the bob, calculated via differentiating the coordinates with respect to time (using [dot notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Newton%27s_notation "Newton's notation") to indicate the time derivatives)

![Image 34: {\displaystyle {\dot {x}}=\ell {\dot {\theta }}\cos {\theta },}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7b4e66499b8c245fe204a6bfff2f35d2ece72a67)![Image 35: {\displaystyle {\dot {y}}=\ell {\dot {\theta }}\sin {\theta }.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/54b35f3c942ec474a74e7beef9932800c7b1af63)

Thus, the Lagrangian is

![Image 36: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\mathcal {L}}&=E_{k}-E_{p}\\&={\frac {1}{2}}mv^{2}-mgh\\&={\frac {1}{2}}m({\dot {x}}^{2}+{\dot {y}}^{2})-mg\ell (1-\cos {\theta })\\&={\frac {1}{2}}m\ell ^{2}{\dot {\theta }}^{2}-mg\ell +mg\ell \cos {\theta }.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/17f06d04f50cdc8149b2a6e27993004666578838)

The **Euler-Lagrange equation** (singular as there is only one constraint, ![Image 37: {\displaystyle q=\theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2c9dca29cb2a7de6c0994469f4bd9c7e64d6cd95)) is thus

![Image 38: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {d}{dt}}\left({\frac {\partial {\mathcal {L}}}{\partial {\dot {\theta }}}}\right)&={\frac {\partial {\mathcal {L}}}{\partial \theta }}\\{\frac {d}{dt}}(m\ell ^{2}{\dot {\theta }})&=-mg\ell \sin {\theta }\\m\ell ^{2}{\ddot {\theta }}&=-mg\ell \sin {\theta }\\{\ddot {\theta }}&=-{\frac {g}{\ell }}\sin {\theta }.\\\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3d6c7515d9cb0db6977946a30e838b53d3f76355)

Which can then be rearranged to match **[Equation 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#math_Equation_1)**, obtained through force analysis.

![Image 39: {\displaystyle {\frac {d^{2}\theta }{dt^{2}}}+{\frac {g}{\ell }}\sin {\theta }=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8330668c9921aa9ded433354534aae046c5d52bf)

Deriving via Lagrangian Mechanics, while excessive with a single pendulum, is useful for more complicated, [chaotic systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory "Chaos theory"), such as a [double pendulum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_pendulum "Double pendulum").

Small-angle approximation
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pendulum_(mechanics)&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: Small-angle approximation")]

[![Image 40](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d0/Small-angle_approximation_for_sine_function.svg/330px-Small-angle_approximation_for_sine_function.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Small-angle_approximation_for_sine_function.svg)

Small-angle approximation for the sine function: For _θ_ ≈ 0, the approximation sin _θ_ ≈ _θ_ can be made.

The differential equation given above is not easily solved, and there is no solution that can be written in terms of elementary functions. However, adding a restriction to the size of the oscillation's amplitude gives a form whose solution can be easily obtained. If it is assumed that the angle is much less than 1[radian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Radian "Radian") (often cited as less than 0.1 radians, about 6°), or ![Image 41: {\displaystyle \theta \ll 1,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86e7990dea584bc140379fe7514dd21f6fd37c73) then substituting for sin _θ_ into **[Eq. 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#math_Eq._1)** using the [small-angle approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small-angle_approximation "Small-angle approximation"), ![Image 42: {\displaystyle \sin \theta \approx \theta ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/551799e02d1311417aec2d24e483cc69f6e95a72) yields the equation for a [harmonic oscillator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_oscillator "Harmonic oscillator"), ![Image 43: {\displaystyle {\frac {d^{2}\theta }{dt^{2}}}+{\frac {g}{\ell }}\theta =0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a4a67eb92d55d29ed4df138689010456418a3b15)

The error due to the approximation is of order _θ_ 3 (from the [Taylor expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taylor_expansion "Taylor expansion") for sin _θ_).

Let the starting angle be _θ_ 0. If it is assumed that the pendulum is released with zero [angular velocity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Angular_velocity "Angular velocity"), the solution becomes

![Image 44: {\displaystyle \theta (t)=\theta _{0}\cos \left({\sqrt {\frac {g}{\ell }}}\,t\right)\quad \quad \quad \quad \theta _{0}\ll 1.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c87ee425bd0a91e0e2b11316a3fa755c474e6e77)

The motion is [simple harmonic motion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simple_harmonic_motion "Simple harmonic motion") where _θ_ 0 is the [amplitude](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Amplitude_(wave_motion) "Amplitude (wave motion)") of the oscillation (that is, the maximum angle between the rod of the pendulum and the vertical). The corresponding approximate [period](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Period_(physics) "Period (physics)") of the motion is then

![Image 45: {\displaystyle T_{0}=2\pi {\sqrt {\frac {\ell }{g}}}\quad \quad \quad \quad \quad \theta _{0}\ll 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/93d0e7e6fb1df1c2541d6fabbfba15924e35cde4)

which is known as [Christiaan Huygens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christiaan_Huygens "Christiaan Huygens")'s law for the period. Note that under the small-angle approximation, the period is independent of the amplitude _θ_ 0; this is the property of [isochronism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isochronous "Isochronous") that [Galileo](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Galileo "Galileo") discovered.

### Rule of thumb for pendulum length

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pendulum_(mechanics)&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Rule of thumb for pendulum length")]

![Image 46: {\displaystyle T_{0}=2\pi {\sqrt {\frac {\ell }{g}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/45513d0af67aac50e49d0d27c23441a819f68d67) gives ![Image 47: {\displaystyle \ell ={\frac {g}{\pi ^{2}}}{\frac {T_{0}^{2}}{4}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a44448e45af5f992f64fbc827d49cd8105fbdd0)

If [SI units](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_System_of_Units "International System of Units") are used (i.e. measure in metres and seconds), and assuming the measurement is taking place on the Earth's surface, then _g_ ≈ 9.81 m/s 2, and ⁠_g_/π 2⁠ ≈ 1 m/s 2 (0.994 is the approximation to 3 decimal places).

Therefore, relatively reasonable approximations for the length and period are: ![Image 48: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\ell &\approx {\frac {T_{0}^{2}}{4}},\\T_{0}&\approx 2{\sqrt {\ell }}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/506b1abb3f4baf0d13ce61234781f54a69feb788) where _T_ 0 is the number of seconds between _two_ beats (one beat for each side of the swing), and l is measured in metres.

For amplitudes beyond the [small angle approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small_angle_approximation "Small angle approximation"), one can compute the exact period by first inverting the equation for the angular velocity obtained from the energy method (**[Eq. 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#math_Eq._2)**), ![Image 52: {\displaystyle {\frac {dt}{d\theta }}={\sqrt {\frac {\ell }{2g}}}{\frac {1}{\sqrt {\cos \theta -\cos \theta _{0}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/51bccd2043205318f569ce35651ae641bdeea7ff) and then integrating over one complete cycle, ![Image 53: {\displaystyle T=t(\theta _{0}\rightarrow 0\rightarrow -\theta _{0}\rightarrow 0\rightarrow \theta _{0}),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9eeceb5e7a153347a445c096ebdce117da81c685) or twice the half-cycle ![Image 54: {\displaystyle T=2t(\theta _{0}\rightarrow 0\rightarrow -\theta _{0}),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a9fca80fced804f78f3804a2574f07133d5fd1b) or four times the quarter-cycle ![Image 55: {\displaystyle T=4t(\theta _{0}\rightarrow 0),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53341a4ec9b8c855fa514e9d5495f0dcf1117242) which leads to ![Image 56: {\displaystyle T=4{\sqrt {\frac {\ell }{2g}}}\int _{0}^{\theta _{0}}{\frac {d\theta }{\sqrt {\cos \theta -\cos \theta _{0}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd1126ba6fbb89d12d2d0d0f35b5e8fd2405897b)

Note that this integral diverges as _θ_ 0 approaches the vertical ![Image 57: {\displaystyle \lim _{\theta _{0}\to \pi }T=\infty ,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3a438efb1396a0a514b9ecf81605282cb68a445a) so that a pendulum with just the right energy to go vertical will never actually get there. (Conversely, a pendulum close to its maximum can take an arbitrarily long time to fall down.)

This integral can be rewritten in terms of [elliptic integrals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_integral "Elliptic integral") as ![Image 58: {\displaystyle T=4{\sqrt {\frac {\ell }{g}}}F\left({\frac {\pi }{2}},\sin {\frac {\theta _{0}}{2}}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9b33d77af48c6b672509da8394e0e7387675db8e) where F is the [incomplete elliptic integral of the first kind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_integral#Incomplete_elliptic_integral_of_the_first_kind "Elliptic integral") defined by ![Image 59: {\displaystyle F(\varphi ,k)=\int _{0}^{\varphi }{\frac {du}{\sqrt {1-k^{2}\sin ^{2}u}}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/53c4cc41b7466ec5e32287b19ec1b9ef4406e3d2)

Or more concisely by the [substitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integration_by_substitution "Integration by substitution")![Image 60: {\displaystyle \sin {u}={\frac {\sin {\frac {\theta }{2}}}{\sin {\frac {\theta _{0}}{2}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0001a204b21be8b6c02a5c2300ed6d91139a2a27) expressing θ in terms of u,

![Image 61: {\displaystyle T={\frac {2T_{0}}{\pi }}K(k),\qquad {\text{where}}\quad k=\sin {\frac {\theta _{0}}{2}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e6f280725bddea39058c92419cd125bd08f6fe25)Eq. 3

Here K is the [complete elliptic integral of the first kind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_integral#Complete_elliptic_integral_of_the_first_kind "Elliptic integral") defined by

![Image 62: {\displaystyle K(k)=F\left({\frac {\pi }{2}},k\right)=\int _{0}^{\frac {\pi }{2}}{\frac {du}{\sqrt {1-k^{2}\sin ^{2}u}}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8fcddb80e7edd88f5dc9d94339e0a14282eb6957)

For comparison of the approximation to the full solution, consider the period of a pendulum of length 1 m on Earth (g = 9.806 65 m/s 2) at an initial angle of 10 degrees is ![Image 63: {\displaystyle 4{\sqrt {\frac {1{\text{ m}}}{g}}}\ K\left(\sin {\frac {10^{\circ }}{2}}\right)\approx 2.0102{\text{ s}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1eeb0e58f60a6dade969a567f67ad2fece5bd61c) The linear approximation gives

![Image 64: {\displaystyle 2\pi {\sqrt {\frac {1{\text{ m}}}{g}}}\approx 2.0064{\text{ s}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b7d15004ca21cd398e2cb0a982406adc183795d)

The difference between the two values, less than 0.2%, is much less than that caused by the variation of g with geographical location.

From here there are many ways to proceed to calculate the elliptic integral.

### Legendre polynomial solution for the elliptic integral

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pendulum_(mechanics)&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Legendre polynomial solution for the elliptic integral")]

Given **[Eq. 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#math_Eq._3)** and the [Legendre polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legendre_polynomial "Legendre polynomial") solution for the elliptic integral: ![Image 65: {\displaystyle K(k)={\frac {\pi }{2}}\sum _{n=0}^{\infty }\left({\frac {(2n-1)!!}{(2n)!!}}k^{n}\right)^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af4bbf8d8de5f4e232e55d1f2f1949a49de0b84f) where _n_!! denotes the [double factorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_factorial "Double factorial"), an exact solution to the period of a simple pendulum is: ![Image 66: {\displaystyle {\begin{alignedat}{2}T&=2\pi {\sqrt {\frac {\ell }{g}}}\left(1+\left({\frac {1}{2}}\right)^{2}\sin ^{2}{\frac {\theta _{0}}{2}}+\left({\frac {1\cdot 3}{2\cdot 4}}\right)^{2}\sin ^{4}{\frac {\theta _{0}}{2}}+\left({\frac {1\cdot 3\cdot 5}{2\cdot 4\cdot 6}}\right)^{2}\sin ^{6}{\frac {\theta _{0}}{2}}+\cdots \right)\\&=2\pi {\sqrt {\frac {\ell }{g}}}\cdot \sum _{n=0}^{\infty }\left(\left({\frac {(2n)!}{(2^{n}\cdot n!)^{2}}}\right)^{2}\cdot \sin ^{2n}{\frac {\theta _{0}}{2}}\right).\end{alignedat}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/846ffad8ff8950fef5a7d2dffc8a02084b80efc4)

Figure 4 shows the relative errors using the power series. _T_ 0 is the linear approximation, and _T_ 2 to _T_ 10 include respectively the terms up to the 2nd to the 10th powers.

The resulting power series is:[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#cite_note-5)

![Image 68: {\displaystyle T=2\pi {\sqrt {\frac {\ell }{g}}}\left(1+{\frac {1}{16}}\theta _{0}^{2}+{\frac {11}{3\,072}}\theta _{0}^{4}+{\frac {173}{737\,280}}\theta _{0}^{6}+{\frac {22\,931}{1\,321\,205\,760}}\theta _{0}^{8}+{\frac {1\,319\,183}{951\,268\,147\,200}}\theta _{0}^{10}+{\frac {233\,526\,463}{2\,009\,078\,326\,886\,400}}\theta _{0}^{12}+\cdots \right),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/abb8b89ce384f561e5ccf875399b682fbc7014bb) more fractions available in the [On-Line Encyclopedia of Integer Sequences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On-Line_Encyclopedia_of_Integer_Sequences "On-Line Encyclopedia of Integer Sequences") with [OEIS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On-Line_Encyclopedia_of_Integer_Sequences "On-Line Encyclopedia of Integer Sequences"):[A223067](https://oeis.org/A223067 "oeis:A223067") having the numerators and [OEIS](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/On-Line_Encyclopedia_of_Integer_Sequences "On-Line Encyclopedia of Integer Sequences"):[A223068](https://oeis.org/A223068 "oeis:A223068") having the denominators.

### Arithmetic-geometric mean solution for elliptic integral

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pendulum_(mechanics)&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Arithmetic-geometric mean solution for elliptic integral")]

Given **[Eq. 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#math_Eq._3)** and the [arithmetic–geometric mean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arithmetic%E2%80%93geometric_mean "Arithmetic–geometric mean") solution of the elliptic integral: ![Image 69: {\displaystyle K(k)={\frac {\pi }{2M(1-k,1+k)}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6cf5ea3c77a718ddaa44c5f14aef2c3d8b2ec395) where _M_(_x_,_y_) is the arithmetic-geometric mean of x and y.

This yields an alternative and faster-converging formula for the period:[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#cite_note-Carvalhaes-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#cite_note-7)[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#cite_note-8)![Image 70: {\displaystyle T={\frac {2\pi }{M\left(1,\cos {\frac {\theta _{0}}{2}}\right)}}{\sqrt {\frac {\ell }{g}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/883bbc71bfe5f71a3f2bc2730b835b98f0641888)

The first iteration of this algorithm gives ![Image 71: {\displaystyle T_{1}={\frac {2T_{0}}{1+\cos {\frac {\theta _{0}}{2}}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/362318afdd0d44bef2e2bb1b10b7e0ff411e4878)

This approximation has the relative error of less than 1% for angles up to 96.11 degrees.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#cite_note-Carvalhaes-6) Since ![Image 72: {\textstyle {\frac {1}{2}}\left(1+\cos \left({\frac {\theta _{0}}{2}}\right)\right)=\cos ^{2}{\frac {\theta _{0}}{4}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/87905869855a43aa07003feb6c3a18f851a48c71) the expression can be written more concisely as ![Image 73: {\displaystyle T_{1}=T_{0}\sec ^{2}{\frac {\theta _{0}}{4}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e2407d18082a684c8656b1e06dd27bfe12e4184c)

The second order expansion of ![Image 74: {\displaystyle \sec ^{2}(\theta _{0}/4)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f251dae46a0ff0b62f63f4f22d648c1d1a1ab256) reduces to ![Image 75: {\textstyle T\approx T_{0}\left(1+{\frac {\theta _{0}^{2}}{16}}\right).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d032bbfabfedd133567b64f28972b1d85e159777)

A second iteration of this algorithm gives ![Image 76: {\displaystyle T_{2}={\frac {4T_{0}}{1+\cos {\frac {\theta _{0}}{2}}+2{\sqrt {\cos {\frac {\theta _{0}}{2}}}}}}={\frac {4T_{0}}{\left(1+{\sqrt {\cos {\frac {\theta _{0}}{2}}}}\right)^{2}}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8a571c5945d6201f9e5dc5dcab34df302650a96)

This second approximation has a relative error of less than 1% for angles up to 163.10 degrees.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#cite_note-Carvalhaes-6)

Approximate formulae for the nonlinear pendulum period
------------------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pendulum_(mechanics)&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Approximate formulae for the nonlinear pendulum period")]

Though the exact period ![Image 77: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0) can be determined, for any finite amplitude ![Image 78: {\displaystyle \theta _{0}<\pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6744041278768081059c2cf057015b98a8e3d701) rad, by evaluating the corresponding complete elliptic integral ![Image 79: {\displaystyle K(k)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38499eed5b7d12f7789d9e2547145f71ec15c5bb), where ![Image 80: {\displaystyle k\equiv \sin(\theta _{0}/2)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/64ee53115b055ca66e9886c2f21f4acc43a5d006), this is often avoided in applications because it is not possible to express this integral in a closed form in terms of elementary functions. This has made way for research on simple approximate formulae for the increase of the pendulum period with amplitude (useful in introductory physics labs, classical mechanics, electromagnetism, acoustics, electronics, superconductivity, etc.[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#cite_note-:0-9) The approximate formulae found by different authors can be classified as follows:

Of course, the increase of ![Image 81: {\displaystyle T}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ec7200acd984a1d3a3d7dc455e262fbe54f7f6e0) with amplitude is more apparent when ![Image 82: {\displaystyle \pi /2<\theta _{0}<\pi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0d1b7b003ec507acae8e15689553b81528f13087), as has been observed in many experiments using either a rigid rod or a disc.[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#cite_note-12) As accurate timers and sensors are currently available even in introductory physics labs, the experimental errors found in ‘very large-angle’ experiments are already small enough for a comparison with the exact period, and a very good agreement between theory and experiments in which friction is negligible has been found. Since this activity has been encouraged by many instructors, a simple approximate formula for the pendulum period valid for all possible amplitudes, to which experimental data could be compared, was sought. In 2008, Lima derived a weighted-average formula with this characteristic:[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#cite_note-:0-9)![Image 83: {\displaystyle T\approx {\frac {r\,a^{2}\,T_{\text{Lima}}+k^{2}\,T_{\text{Cromer}}}{r\,a^{2}+k^{2}}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ac542ea91db968fa472cb7e3c2f6f248e6d286e7) where ![Image 84: {\displaystyle r=7.17}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/46390fddb3d2afccadb7aaa8e05d977c7b261621), which presents a maximum error of only 0.6% (at ![Image 85: {\displaystyle \theta _{0}=95^{\circ }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1c3bddddd241cef9fe82fc535d787ffd7ac8d1c2)).

A **compound pendulum** (or **physical pendulum**) is one where the rod is not massless, and may have extended size; that is, an arbitrarily shaped [rigid body](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rigid_body "Rigid body") swinging by a pivot ![Image 112: {\displaystyle O}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d70e1d0d87e2ef1092ea1ffe2923d9933ff18fc). In this case the pendulum's period depends on its [moment of inertia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moment_of_inertia "Moment of inertia")![Image 113: {\displaystyle I_{O}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/706b66e292d9ff08c129c502cc75524b0e1c757a) around the pivot point.

The equation of [torque](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torque "Torque") gives: ![Image 114: {\displaystyle \tau =I\alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01a662a7ad75feca822305e985c0d19c277b0d55) where: ![Image 115: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) is the angular acceleration. ![Image 116: {\displaystyle \tau }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/38a7dcde9730ef0853809fefc18d88771f95206c) is the torque

The torque is generated by gravity so: ![Image 117: {\displaystyle \tau =-mgr_{\oplus }\sin \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a05e3aa97c9fe060451fb565ed3ab1a62f6c6e58) where:

*   ![Image 118: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) is the total mass of the rigid body (rod and bob)
*   ![Image 119: {\displaystyle r_{\oplus }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/819be26a40b50ed72e38fa52c662bf74e1ea1921) is the distance from the pivot point to the system's centre-of-mass
*   ![Image 120: {\displaystyle \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6e5ab2664b422d53eb0c7df3b87e1360d75ad9af) is the angle from the vertical

Hence, under the small-angle approximation, ![Image 121: {\displaystyle \sin \theta \approx \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a604432d08fc1bd289e40ba08eed0c7ec697ea3) (or equivalently when ![Image 122: {\displaystyle \theta _{\mathrm {max} }\ll 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/25dc40cd3b49033603efccab5208a2cbd5f677f1)), ![Image 123: {\displaystyle \alpha ={\ddot {\theta }}={\frac {mgr_{\oplus }}{I_{O}}}\sin \theta \approx -{\frac {mgr_{\oplus }}{I_{O}}}\theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/449323b21918140b3ef8c4500124d644c91f35db) where ![Image 124: {\displaystyle I_{O}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/706b66e292d9ff08c129c502cc75524b0e1c757a) is the moment of inertia of the body about the pivot point ![Image 125: {\displaystyle O}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9d70e1d0d87e2ef1092ea1ffe2923d9933ff18fc).

The expression for ![Image 126: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) is of the same form as the conventional simple pendulum and gives a period of[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#cite_note-Hyperphysics-2)![Image 127: {\displaystyle T=2\pi {\sqrt {\frac {I_{O}}{mgr_{\oplus }}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0112d59221f490b3c521f9a69aaa764a8386d1c1)

And a frequency of ![Image 128: {\displaystyle f={\frac {1}{T}}={\frac {1}{2\pi }}{\sqrt {\frac {mgr_{\oplus }}{I_{O}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/14c68e8c04411f6eff549cf7ffbb2b934ae549ac)

If the initial angle is taken into consideration (for large amplitudes), then the expression for ![Image 129: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3) becomes: ![Image 130: {\displaystyle \alpha ={\ddot {\theta }}=-{\frac {mgr_{\oplus }}{I_{O}}}\sin \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/cd592c586dfc9de2b56206cad1ec44c9aa49e884) and gives a period of: ![Image 131: {\displaystyle T=4\operatorname {K} \left(\sin ^{2}{\frac {\theta _{\mathrm {max} }}{2}}\right){\sqrt {\frac {I_{O}}{mgr_{\oplus }}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/420158f88ad56660699d840d1af1d9f52c847c6b) where ![Image 132: {\displaystyle \theta _{\mathrm {max} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/86c6468efc13b54397e5c21460c10ab05b1b7220) is the maximum angle of oscillation (with respect to the vertical) and ![Image 133: {\displaystyle \operatorname {K} (k)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/eb8e3cd058c5eb653fed893af92e81a35e76e4de) is the [complete elliptic integral of the first kind](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliptic_integral#Complete_elliptic_integral_of_the_first_kind "Elliptic integral").

An important concept is the **equivalent length**, ![Image 134: {\displaystyle \ell ^{\mathrm {eq} }}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4562c381095c325df0b56438c368f149621fd710), the length of a simple pendulums that has the same angular frequency ![Image 135: {\displaystyle \omega _{0}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a713d16c489051d4f515e12b1f86061c6be799b) as the compound pendulum: ![Image 136: {\displaystyle {\omega _{0}}^{2}={\frac {g}{\ell ^{\mathrm {eq} }}}:={\frac {mgr_{\oplus }}{I_{O}}}\implies \ell ^{\mathrm {eq} }={\frac {I_{O}}{mr_{\oplus }}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/11bcf3f49bfff2df991a46f1f2ca9fa719979125)

Consider the following cases:

![Image 137: {\displaystyle {\omega _{0}}^{2}={\frac {mgr_{\oplus }}{I_{O}}}={\frac {\left(m_{\mathrm {bob} }\ell +m_{\mathrm {rod} }{\frac {\ell }{2}}\right)g}{m_{\mathrm {bob} }\ell ^{2}+{\frac {1}{3}}m_{\mathrm {rod} }\ell ^{2}}}={\frac {g}{\ell }}{\frac {m_{\mathrm {bob} }+{\frac {m_{\mathrm {rod} }}{2}}}{m_{\mathrm {bob} }+{\frac {m_{\mathrm {rod} }}{3}}}}={\frac {g}{\ell }}{\frac {1+{\frac {m_{\mathrm {rod} }}{2m_{\mathrm {bob} }}}}{1+{\frac {m_{\mathrm {rod} }}{3m_{\mathrm {bob} }}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c2cdebe4ea9af1346172a036b721da86da87cdc) Where ![Image 138: {\displaystyle \ell ^{\mathrm {eq} }=\ell {\frac {1+{\frac {m_{\mathrm {rod} }}{3m_{\mathrm {bob} }}}}{1+{\frac {m_{\mathrm {rod} }}{2m_{\mathrm {bob} }}}}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0de186ce0f51276d5df4f8444f71fde278d47254). Notice these formulae can be particularized into the two previous cases studied before just by considering the mass of the rod or the bob to be zero respectively. Also notice that the formula does not depend on both the mass of the bob and the rod, but actually on their ratio, ![Image 139: {\displaystyle {\frac {m_{\mathrm {rod} }}{m_{\mathrm {bob} }}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f036beef51fd9d7a117345c3a236a90d5944f515). An approximation can be made for ![Image 140: {\displaystyle {\frac {m_{\mathrm {rod} }}{m_{\mathrm {bob} }}}\ll 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4a0ad74c1e0e5dcbc9d64778a47092d1fb7be426):

![Image 141: {\displaystyle {\omega _{0}}^{2}\approx {\frac {g}{\ell }}\left(1+{\frac {1}{6}}{\frac {m_{\mathrm {rod} }}{m_{\mathrm {bob} }}}+\cdots \right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e4b52235f95c842f6dfc5438eb7499018d84dcd7)

Notice how similar it is to the angular frequency in a [spring-mass system with effective mass](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effective_mass_(spring%E2%80%93mass_system) "Effective mass (spring–mass system)").

Damped, driven pendulum
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pendulum_(mechanics)&action=edit&section=12 "Edit section: Damped, driven pendulum")]

The above discussion focuses on a pendulum bob only acted upon by the force of gravity. Suppose a damping force, e.g. air resistance, as well as a sinusoidal driving force acts on the body. This system is a damped, driven [oscillator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_oscillator "Harmonic oscillator"), and is [chaotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory "Chaos theory").

Equation (1) can be written as

![Image 142: {\displaystyle ml^{2}{\frac {d^{2}\theta }{dt^{2}}}=-mgl\sin \theta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c189cbd1ad97781c36f1b5c84ae49c70709e79ff)

(see the [Torque derivation of Equation (1)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#Simple_gravity_pendulum) above).

A damping term and forcing term can be added to the right hand side to get

![Image 143: {\displaystyle ml^{2}{\frac {d^{2}\theta }{dt^{2}}}=-mgl\sin \theta -b{\frac {d\theta }{dt}}+a\cos(\Omega t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a64bb655baae53b05ad200f034393f6004a1373e)

where the damping is assumed to be directly proportional to the angular velocity (this is true for low-speed air resistance, see also [Drag (physics)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Drag_(physics) "Drag (physics)")). ![Image 144: {\displaystyle a}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ffd2487510aa438433a2579450ab2b3d557e5edc) and ![Image 145: {\displaystyle b}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f11423fbb2e967f986e36804a8ae4271734917c3) are constants defining the amplitude of forcing and the degree of damping respectively. ![Image 146: {\textstyle \Omega }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f0f531159ee31e33b4f75988d5abba38abadb3e2) is the angular frequency of the driving oscillations.

Dividing through by ![Image 147: {\textstyle ml^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fffef2ee3d7711682b0a2359e1342e9304f40e17):

![Image 148: {\displaystyle {\frac {d^{2}\theta }{dt^{2}}}+{\frac {b}{ml^{2}}}{\frac {d\theta }{dt}}+{\frac {g}{l}}{\sin \theta }-{\frac {a}{ml^{2}}}\cos(\Omega t)=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3519b0e1da53c9192f77cca85dc0a2f9adea4b55)

For a physical pendulum:

![Image 149: {\displaystyle {\frac {d^{2}\theta }{dt^{2}}}+{\frac {b}{I}}{\frac {d\theta }{dt}}+{\frac {mgr_{\oplus }}{I}}{\sin \theta }-{\frac {a}{I}}\cos(\Omega t)=0.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b914a59c493bff6eb32b38ee34976942322d103f)

This equation exhibits [chaotic behaviour](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chaos_theory "Chaos theory"). The exact motion of this pendulum can only be found numerically and is highly dependent on initial conditions, e.g. the initial velocity and the starting amplitude. However, the small angle approximation outlined above can still be used under the required conditions to give an approximate analytical solution.

Physical interpretation of the imaginary period
-----------------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Pendulum_(mechanics)&action=edit&section=13 "Edit section: Physical interpretation of the imaginary period")]

The [Jacobian elliptic function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jacobi%27s_elliptic_functions "Jacobi's elliptic functions") that expresses the position of a pendulum as a function of time is a [doubly periodic function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Doubly_periodic_function "Doubly periodic function") with a [real](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") period and an [imaginary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Imaginary_number "Imaginary number") period. The real period is, of course, the time it takes the pendulum to go through one full cycle. [Paul Appell](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Appell "Paul Appell") pointed out a physical interpretation of the imaginary period:[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pendulum_(mechanics)#cite_note-16) if _θ_ 0 is the maximum angle of one pendulum and 180° − _θ_ 0 is the maximum angle of another, then the real period of each is the magnitude of the imaginary period of the other.

[![Image 150](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ef/Coupled_pendulums.svg/500px-Coupled_pendulums.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Coupled_pendulums.svg)

Two identical simple pendulums coupled via a spring connecting the bobs.

**Coupled pendulums** can affect each other's motion, either through a direction connection (such as a spring connecting the bobs) or through motions in a supporting structure (such as a tabletop). The equations of motion for two identical simple pendulums coupled by a spring connecting the bobs can be obtained using [Lagrangian mechanics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lagrangian_mechanics "Lagrangian mechanics").

The kinetic energy of the system is: ![Image 151: {\displaystyle E_{\text{K}}={\frac {1}{2}}mL^{2}\left({\dot {\theta }}_{1}^{2}+{\dot {\theta }}_{2}^{2}\right)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/afd60e4ba1302860670077824230acbf8ed33ad6) where ![Image 152: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) is the mass of the bobs, ![Image 153: {\displaystyle L}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/103168b86f781fe6e9a4a87b8ea1cebe0ad4ede8) is the length of the strings, and ![Image 154: {\displaystyle \theta _{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f84b9443d095623e02fd287cd095123d70b0278), ![Image 155: {\displaystyle \theta _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ed6ea624b20b153403979ffaf5434fc36de2990) are the angular displacements of the two bobs from equilibrium.

The potential energy of the system is: ![Image 156: {\displaystyle E_{\text{p}}=mgL(2-\cos \theta _{1}-\cos \theta _{2})+{\frac {1}{2}}kL^{2}(\theta _{2}-\theta _{1})^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8335552e613d2da947fc84d3c79ea8b28f603df6)

where ![Image 157: {\displaystyle g}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d3556280e66fe2c0d0140df20935a6f057381d77) is the [gravitational acceleration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitational_acceleration "Gravitational acceleration"), and ![Image 158: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) is the [spring constant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hooke%27s_law "Hooke's law"). The displacement ![Image 159: {\displaystyle L(\theta _{2}-\theta _{1})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef908cfa96a8e20bc47452cb64a3468df4c849c1) of the spring from its equilibrium position assumes the [small angle approximation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Small-angle_approximation "Small-angle approximation").

The Lagrangian is then ![Image 160: {\displaystyle {\mathcal {L}}={\frac {1}{2}}mL^{2}\left({\dot {\theta }}_{1}^{2}+{\dot {\theta }}_{2}^{2}\right)-mgL(2-\cos \theta _{1}-\cos \theta _{2})-{\frac {1}{2}}kL^{2}(\theta _{2}-\theta _{1})^{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1d6073fa63adc3a522b111125ba9d6e75f85f433) which leads to the following set of coupled differential equations: ![Image 161: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\ddot {\theta }}_{1}+{\frac {g}{L}}\sin \theta _{1}+{\frac {k}{m}}(\theta _{1}-\theta _{2})&=0\\{\ddot {\theta }}_{2}+{\frac {g}{L}}\sin \theta _{2}-{\frac {k}{m}}(\theta _{1}-\theta _{2})&=0\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e812f67998b0577c4c898c28be9b935fafe998ec)

Adding and subtracting these two equations in turn, and applying the small angle approximation, gives two [harmonic oscillator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harmonic_oscillator#Simple_harmonic_oscillator "Harmonic oscillator") equations in the variables ![Image 162: {\displaystyle \theta _{1}+\theta _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ada26ece540b171c6bcb2948bfe94a74ab43d23c) and ![Image 163: {\displaystyle \theta _{1}-\theta _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/af63b4ff393ee3e4f309c44609ec5d8d26d254fb): ![Image 164: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\ddot {\theta }}_{1}+{\ddot {\theta }}_{2}+{\frac {g}{L}}(\theta _{1}+\theta _{2})&=0\\{\ddot {\theta }}_{1}-{\ddot {\theta }}_{2}+\left({\frac {g}{L}}+2{\frac {k}{m}}\right)(\theta _{1}-\theta _{2})&=0\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/621b9b4de785458b0a5d3572a091c6c864c404b0) with the corresponding solutions ![Image 165: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\theta _{1}+\theta _{2}&=A\cos(\omega _{1}t+\alpha )\\\theta _{1}-\theta _{2}&=B\cos(\omega _{2}t+\beta )\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/48197f25486e33c240b5c9d510f54c6e9e44ac36) where ![Image 166: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\omega _{1}&={\sqrt {\frac {g}{L}}}\\\omega _{2}&={\sqrt {{\frac {g}{L}}+2{\frac {k}{m}}}}\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/091afe0acdf8d8d728a5d4e856e98ca4cc4565b1)

and ![Image 167: {\displaystyle A}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7daff47fa58cdfd29dc333def748ff5fa4c923e3), ![Image 168: {\displaystyle B}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/47136aad860d145f75f3eed3022df827cee94d7a), ![Image 169: {\displaystyle \alpha }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b79333175c8b3f0840bfb4ec41b8072c83ea88d3), ![Image 170: {\displaystyle \beta }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7ed48a5e36207156fb792fa79d29925d2f7901e8) are [constants of integration](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Constant_of_integration "Constant of integration").

Expressing the solutions in terms of ![Image 171: {\displaystyle \theta _{1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7f84b9443d095623e02fd287cd095123d70b0278) and ![Image 172: {\displaystyle \theta _{2}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0ed6ea624b20b153403979ffaf5434fc36de2990) alone: ![Image 173: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\theta _{1}&={\frac {1}{2}}A\cos(\omega _{1}t+\alpha )+{\frac {1}{2}}B\cos(\omega _{2}t+\beta )\\\theta _{2}&={\frac {1}{2}}A\cos(\omega _{1}t+\alpha )-{\frac {1}{2}}B\cos(\omega _{2}t+\beta )\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/770d32cce179bc0482b44b0a587116544eb80f36)

If the bobs are not given an initial push, then the condition ![Image 174: {\displaystyle {\dot {\theta }}_{1}(0)={\dot {\theta }}_{2}(0)=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2c730582023e32923e756b91d5e88974a13414d4) requires ![Image 175: {\displaystyle \alpha =\beta =0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/7434b2c28160128e0a0c5a11c97635c26423c7c2), which gives (after some rearranging): ![Image 176: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}A&=\theta _{1}(0)+\theta _{2}(0)\\B&=\theta _{1}(0)-\theta _{2}(0)\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/66ade9f00698175b8231fa6ff7d8371ee15715e9)
