_**Arabidopsis thaliana**_, the **thale cress**, **mouse-ear cress** or **arabidopsis**, is a small plant from the mustard family ([Brassicaceae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brassicaceae "Brassicaceae")), native to Eurasia and Africa.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-grin-2)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-3)[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-4)[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-5)[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-kramer-natural-history-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-pmid28473417-7) Commonly found along the shoulders of roads and in disturbed land, it is generally considered a weed.

A [winter annual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Winter_annual "Winter annual") with a relatively short lifecycle, _A. thaliana_ is a popular [model organism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Model_organism "Model organism") in [plant biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant_biology "Plant biology") and genetics. For a complex multicellular [eukaryote](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eukaryote "Eukaryote"), _A. thaliana_ has a relatively small [genome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genome "Genome") of around 135 [megabase pairs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Base_pair#Length_measurements "Base pair").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Genome_Assembly-8) It was the first plant to have its genome sequenced, and is an important tool for understanding the [molecular biology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Molecular_biology "Molecular biology") of many plant traits, including flower development and [light sensing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phototropism "Phototropism").[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-9)

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/6d/194_Arabidopsis_thaliana%2C_Turritis_glabra.jpg/250px-194_Arabidopsis_thaliana%2C_Turritis_glabra.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:194_Arabidopsis_thaliana,_Turritis_glabra.jpg)

Botanical illustration

_Arabidopsis thaliana_ is an [annual](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Annual_plant "Annual plant") (rarely [biennial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biennial_plant "Biennial plant")) plant, usually growing to 20–25 cm tall.[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-kramer-natural-history-6) The [leaves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leaf "Leaf") form a rosette at the base of the plant, with a few leaves also on the flowering [stem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant_stem "Plant stem"). The basal leaves are green to slightly purplish in color, 1.5–5 cm long, and 2–10 mm broad, with an entire to coarsely serrated margin; the stem leaves are smaller and unstalked, usually with an entire margin. Leaves are covered with small, unicellular hairs called [trichomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trichome "Trichome"). The [flowers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flower "Flower") are 3 mm in diameter, arranged in a [corymb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inflorescence#Simple_inflorescences "Inflorescence"); their structure is that of the typical [Brassicaceae](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brassicaceae "Brassicaceae"). The fruit is a [silique](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Silique "Silique") 5–20 mm long, containing 20–30 [seeds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seed "Seed").[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-fnwe-10)[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-blamey-11)[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-fop-12)[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-foc-13) Roots are simple in structure, with a single primary root that grows vertically downward, later producing smaller [lateral roots](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lateral_root "Lateral root"). These roots form interactions with [rhizosphere](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rhizosphere_(ecology) "Rhizosphere (ecology)") bacteria such as _[Bacillus megaterium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacillus\_megaterium "Bacillus megaterium")_.[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-14)

[![Image 5](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/b/bf/M%C3%BC%C3%BCrlooga_%28Arabidopsis_thaliana%29_lehekarv_%28trihhoom%29_311_0804.JPG/250px-M%C3%BC%C3%BCrlooga_%28Arabidopsis_thaliana%29_lehekarv_%28trihhoom%29_311_0804.JPG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:M%C3%BC%C3%BCrlooga_(Arabidopsis_thaliana)_lehekarv_(trihhoom)_311_0804.JPG)

[Scanning electron micrograph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scanning_electron_microscope "Scanning electron microscope") of a [trichome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Trichome "Trichome"), a leaf hair of _A. thaliana_, a unique structure made of a single cell

_A. thaliana_ can complete its entire lifecycle in six weeks. The central stem that produces flowers grows after about 3 weeks, and the flowers naturally self-pollinate. In the lab, _A. thaliana_ may be grown in Petri plates, pots, or hydroponics, under fluorescent lights or in a greenhouse.[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-15)

The plant was first described in 1577 in the [Harz Mountains](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harz_Mountains "Harz Mountains") by [Johannes Thal](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Johannes_Thal&action=edit&redlink=1 "Johannes Thal (page does not exist)")[[de](https://de.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johannes_Thal "de:Johannes Thal")] (1542–1583), a physician from [Nordhausen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nordhausen,_Thuringia "Nordhausen, Thuringia"), [Thüringen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Th%C3%BCringen "Thüringen"), Germany, who called it _Pilosella siliquosa_. In 1753, [Carl Linnaeus](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Linnaeus "Carl Linnaeus") renamed the plant _Arabis thaliana_ in honor of Thal. In 1842, German botanist [Gustav Heynhold](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustav_Heynhold "Gustav Heynhold") erected the new genus _Arabidopsis_ and placed the plant in that genus. The [generic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genus "Genus") name, _[Arabidopsis](https://en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Arabidopsis "wikt:Arabidopsis")_, comes from [Greek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greek_(language) "Greek (language)"), meaning "resembling _[Arabis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabis "Arabis")_" (the genus in which Linnaeus had initially placed it).

Thousands of natural inbred accessions of _A. thaliana_ have been collected from throughout its natural and introduced range.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-GenomesConsortium2016-16) These accessions exhibit considerable genetic and phenotypic variation, which can be used to study the adaptation of this species to different environments.[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-GenomesConsortium2016-16)

Distribution and habitat
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Arabidopsis_thaliana&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Distribution and habitat")]

_A. thaliana_ is native to Europe, Asia, and Africa, and its geographic distribution is rather continuous from the [Mediterranean](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mediterranean_Sea "Mediterranean Sea") to [Scandinavia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scandinavia "Scandinavia") and Spain to [Greece](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Greece "Greece").[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-17) It also appears to be native in tropical alpine ecosystems in Africa and perhaps South Africa.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-18)[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-19) It has been introduced and naturalized worldwide,[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-eol-20) including in North America around the 17th century.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-21)

_A. thaliana_ readily grows and often pioneers rocky, sandy, and calcareous soils. It is generally considered a weed, due to its widespread distribution in agricultural fields, roadsides, railway lines, waste ground, and other disturbed habitats,[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-eol-20)[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-22) but due to its limited competitive ability and small size, it is not categorized as a noxious weed.[[23]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-23) Like most Brassicaceae species, _A. thaliana_ is edible by humans in a salad or cooked, but it does not enjoy widespread use as a spring vegetable.[[24]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-EOL-24)

Use as a model organism
-----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Arabidopsis_thaliana&action=edit&section=4 "Edit section: Use as a model organism")]

Botanists and biologists began to research _A. thaliana_ in the early 1900s, and the first systematic description of mutants was done around 1945.[[25]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-25)_A. thaliana_ is now widely used for studying [plant sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant_sciences "Plant sciences"), including [genetics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetics "Genetics"), [evolution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolution "Evolution"), population genetics, and plant development.[[26]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-26)[[27]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-27)[[28]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-28) Although _A. thaliana_ the plant has little direct significance for agriculture, _A. thaliana_ the model organism has revolutionized our understanding of the genetic, cellular, and molecular biology of flowering plants.

[![Image 6](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0a/Arabidopsis_mutants.jpg/250px-Arabidopsis_mutants.jpg)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Arabidopsis_mutants.jpg)

A double-flower mutant, first documented in 1873

The first mutant in _A. thaliana_ was documented in 1873 by [Alexander Braun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alexander_Braun "Alexander Braun"), describing a [double flower](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double_flower "Double flower") phenotype (the mutated gene was likely _[Agamous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agamous "Agamous")_, cloned and characterized in 1990).[[29]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-29)[Friedrich Laibach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Laibach "Friedrich Laibach") (who had published the chromosome number in 1907) did not propose _A. thaliana_ as a model organism, though, until 1943.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-meyerowitz2001-30) His student, Erna Reinholz, published her thesis on _A. thaliana_ in 1945, describing the first collection of _A. thaliana_ mutants that they generated using [X-ray](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/X-ray "X-ray")[mutagenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutagenesis "Mutagenesis"). Laibach continued his important contributions to _A. thaliana_ research by collecting a large number of accessions (often questionably referred to as "[ecotypes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ecotype "Ecotype")"). With the help of Albert Kranz, these were organised into a large collection of 750 natural accessions of _A. thaliana_ from around the world.

In the 1950s and 1960s, John Langridge and [George Rédei](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/George_R%C3%A9dei "George Rédei") played an important role in establishing _A. thaliana_ as a useful organism for biological laboratory experiments. Rédei wrote several scholarly reviews instrumental in introducing the model to the scientific community. The start of the _A. thaliana_ research community dates to a newsletter called _Arabidopsis_ Information Service,[[31]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Arabidopsis-Information-Service-31) established in 1964. The first International _Arabidopsis_ Conference was held in 1965, in [Göttingen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%B6ttingen "Göttingen"), Germany.

In the 1980s, _A. thaliana_ started to become widely used in plant research laboratories around the world. It was one of several candidates that included maize, [petunia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petunia "Petunia"), and tobacco.[[30]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-meyerowitz2001-30) The latter two were attractive, since they were easily transformable with the then-current technologies, while maize was a well-established genetic model for plant biology. The breakthrough year for _A. thaliana_ as a model plant was 1986, in which [T-DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transfer_DNA "Transfer DNA")-mediated [transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformation_(genetics)#Plants "Transformation (genetics)") and the first [cloned](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Clone_(genetics) "Clone (genetics)")_A. thaliana_ gene were described.[[32]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-32)[[33]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-33)

[![Image 7](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fa/Plastomap_of_Arabidopsis_thaliana.svg/250px-Plastomap_of_Arabidopsis_thaliana.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Plastomap_of_Arabidopsis_thaliana.svg)

Chloroplast genome map of _A. thaliana_:[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-NCBI_NC_000932-34)[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Sato1999-35) Introns are in grey. Some genes consist of 5′ and 3′ portions. Strand 1 and 2 genes are transcribed clockwise and counterclockwise, respectively. The innermost circle provides the boundaries of the large and small single-copy regions (LSC and SSC, violet) separated by a pair of inverted repeats (IRa and IRB, black).

Due to the small size of its [genome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genome "Genome"), and because it is [diploid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Diploid "Diploid"), _Arabidopsis thaliana_ is useful for genetic mapping and [sequencing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sequencing "Sequencing") — with about 157 megabase pairs[[36]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-36) and five [chromosomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chromosome "Chromosome"), _A. thaliana_ has one of the smallest genomes among plants.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Genome_Assembly-8) It was long thought to have the smallest genome of all flowering plants,[[37]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-37) but that title is now considered to belong to plants in the genus _[Genlisea](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genlisea "Genlisea")_, order [Lamiales](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lamiales "Lamiales"), with _[Genlisea tuberosa](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genlisea\_tuberosa "Genlisea tuberosa")_, a carnivorous plant, showing a genome size of approximately 61 Mbp.[[38]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Fleischmann_2014-38) It was the first plant genome to be sequenced, completed in 2000 by the _Arabidopsis_ Genome Initiative.[[39]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-39) The most up-to-date version of the _A. thaliana_ genome is maintained by the Arabidopsis Information Resource.[[40]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-40)

The genome encodes ~27,600 [protein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein "Protein")-coding [genes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene "Gene") and about 6,500 non-coding genes.[[41]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-41) However, the Uniprot database lists 39,342 proteins in their _Arabidopsis_ reference proteome.[[42]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-42) Among the 27,600 protein-coding genes 25,402 (91.8%) are now annotated with "meaningful" product names,[[43]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-43) although a large fraction of these proteins is likely only poorly understood and only known in general terms (e.g. as "DNA-binding protein without known specificity"). Uniprot lists more than 3,000 proteins as "uncharacterized" as part of the reference proteome.

The plastome of _A. thaliana_ is a 154,478 base-pair-long DNA molecule,[[34]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-NCBI_NC_000932-34) a size typically encountered in most flowering plants (see the [list of sequenced plastomes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_sequenced_plastomes#Flowering_plants "List of sequenced plastomes")). It comprises 136 genes coding for small subunit ribosomal proteins (_rps_, in yellow: see figure), large subunit ribosomal proteins (_rpl_, orange), hypothetical chloroplast open reading frame proteins (_ycf_, lemon), proteins involved in photosynthetic reactions (green) or in other functions (red), ribosomal RNAs (_rrn_, blue), and transfer RNAs (_trn_, black).[[35]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Sato1999-35)

#### Mitochondrial genome

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Arabidopsis_thaliana&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Mitochondrial genome")]

The mitochondrial genome of _A. thaliana_ is 367,808 base pairs long and contains 57 genes.[[44]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-NCBI_BK010421-44) There are many repeated regions in the arabidopsis is mitochondrial genome. The largest repeats [recombine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genetic_recombination "Genetic recombination") regularly and isomerize the genome.[[45]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-45) Like most plant mitochondrial genomes, the arabidopsis mitochondrial genome exists as a complex arrangement of overlapping branched and linear molecules _in vivo_.[[46]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-46)

[Genetic transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformation_(genetics) "Transformation (genetics)") of _A. thaliana_ is routine, using _[Agrobacterium tumefaciens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agrobacterium\_tumefaciens "Agrobacterium tumefaciens")_ to transfer [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Deoxyribonucleic_acid "Deoxyribonucleic acid") into the plant genome. The current protocol, termed "floral dip", involves simply dipping flowers into a solution containing _Agrobacterium_ carrying a plasmid of interest and a detergent.[[47]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-47)[[48]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-48) This method avoids the need for [tissue culture](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tissue_culture "Tissue culture") or plant regeneration.

The _A. thaliana_ gene knockout collections are a unique resource for plant biology made possible by the availability of high-throughput transformation and funding for genomics resources. The site of T-DNA insertions has been determined for over 300,000 independent transgenic lines, with the information and seeds accessible through online T-DNA databases.[[49]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-49) Through these collections, insertional mutants are available for most genes in _A. thaliana_.

Characterized accessions and mutant lines of _A. thaliana_ serve as experimental material in laboratory studies. The most commonly used background lines are L _er_ (Landsberg _erecta_), and Col, or Columbia.[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-arabidopsis.info-50) Other background lines less-often cited in the scientific literature are Ws, or Wassilewskija, C24, Cvi, or Cape Verde Islands, Nossen, etc. (see for ex.[[51]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-51)) Sets of closely related accessions named Col-0, Col-1, etc., have been obtained and characterized; in general, mutant lines are available through stock centers, of which best-known are the Nottingham Arabidopsis Stock Center-NASC[[50]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-arabidopsis.info-50) and the Arabidopsis Biological Resource Center-ABRC in Ohio, USA.[[52]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-52) The Col-0 accession was selected by Rédei from within a (nonirradiated) population of seeds designated 'Landsberg' which he received from Laibach.[[53]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-53) Columbia (named for the location of Rédei's former institution, [University of Missouri](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Missouri "University of Missouri")-[Columbia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Columbia,_Missouri "Columbia, Missouri")) was the reference accession sequenced in the Arabidopsis Genome Initiative. The Later (Landsberg erecta) line was selected by Rédei (because of its short stature) from a Landsberg population he had mutagenized with X-rays. As the L _er_ collection of mutants is derived from this initial line, L _er_-0 does not correspond to the Landsberg accessions, which designated La-0, La-1, etc.

Trichome formation is initiated by the GLABROUS1 protein. [Knockouts](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene_knockout "Gene knockout") of the corresponding gene lead to [glabrous](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glossary_of_botanical_terms#glabrous "Glossary of botanical terms") plants. This [phenotype](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenotype "Phenotype") has already been used in [gene editing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Genome_editing "Genome editing") experiments and might be of interest as visual marker for plant research to improve gene editing methods such as [CRISPR/Cas9.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/CRISPR#Genome_engineering "CRISPR")[[54]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-54)[[55]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-55)

#### Non-Mendelian inheritance controversy

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Arabidopsis_thaliana&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Non-Mendelian inheritance controversy")]

In 2005, scientists at [Purdue University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Purdue_University "Purdue University") proposed that _A. thaliana_ possessed an alternative to previously known mechanisms of [DNA repair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA_repair "DNA repair"), producing an unusual pattern of [inheritance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mendelian_inheritance "Mendelian inheritance"), but the phenomenon observed (reversion of mutant copies of the _[HOTHEAD](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HOTHEAD\_(gene) "HOTHEAD (gene)")_ gene to a wild-type state) was later suggested to be an artifact because the mutants show increased outcrossing due to organ fusion.[[56]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-56)[[57]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-57)[[58]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-58)

The plant's small size and rapid lifecycle are also advantageous for research. Having specialized as a [spring ephemeral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spring_ephemeral "Spring ephemeral"), it has been used to found several laboratory strains that take about 6 weeks from germination to mature seed. The small size of the plant is convenient for cultivation in a small space, and it produces many seeds. Further, the selfing nature of this plant assists genetic experiments. Also, as an individual plant can produce several thousand seeds, each of the above criteria leads to _A. thaliana_ being valued as a genetic model organism.

Arabidopsis is often the model for study of [SNAREs in plants](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/SNARE_(protein)#In_plants "SNARE (protein)"). This has shown SNAREs to be heavily involved in [vesicle trafficking](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vesicle_trafficking "Vesicle trafficking"). Zheng et al. 1999 found an arabidopsis SNARE called AtVTI1a is probably essential to [Golgi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Golgi_apparatus "Golgi apparatus")-[vacuole](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vacuole "Vacuole") trafficking. This is still a wide open field and plant SNAREs' role in trafficking remains understudied.[[59]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Raikhel-2017-59)

The [DNA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA "DNA") of plants is vulnerable to [ultraviolet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ultraviolet "Ultraviolet") light, and [DNA repair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/DNA_repair "DNA repair") mechanisms have evolved to avoid or repair genome damage caused by UV. Kaiser et al.[[60]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-60) showed that in _A. thaliana_ cyclobutane pyrimidine dimers (CPDs) induced by UV light can be repaired by expression of CPD [photolyase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photolyase "Photolyase").

### Germination in lunar regolith

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Arabidopsis_thaliana&action=edit&section=14 "Edit section: Germination in lunar regolith")]

On May 12, 2022, [NASA](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NASA "NASA") announced that specimens of _Arabidopsis thaliana_ had been successfully germinated and grown in samples of [lunar regolith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_regolith "Lunar regolith"). While the plants successfully germinated and grew into seedlings, they were not as robust as specimens that had been grown in [volcanic ash](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Volcanic_ash "Volcanic ash") as a control group, although the experiments also found some variation in the plants grown in regolith based on the location the samples were taken from, as _A. thaliana_ grown in regolith gathered during [Apollo 12](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo_12 "Apollo 12")&[Apollo 17](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo_17 "Apollo 17") were more robust than those grown in samples taken during [Apollo 11](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo_11 "Apollo 11").[[61]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-61)

_A. thaliana_ has been extensively studied as a model for flower development. The developing flower has four basic organs - [sepals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sepal "Sepal"), [petals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Petal "Petal"), [stamens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stamen "Stamen"), and [carpels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gynoecium "Gynoecium") (which go on to form [pistils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gynoecium "Gynoecium")). These organs are arranged in a series of whorls, four sepals on the outer whorl, followed by four petals inside this, six stamens, and a central carpel region. [Homeotic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeotic "Homeotic") mutations in _A. thaliana_ result in the change of one organ to another—in the case of the _agamous_ mutation, for example, stamens become petals and carpels are replaced with a new flower, resulting in a recursively repeated sepal-petal-petal pattern.

[![Image 8](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ee/ABC_flower_development.svg/250px-ABC_flower_development.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:ABC_flower_development.svg)

The ABC model of flower development was developed through studying _A. thaliana_.

Observations of homeotic mutations led to the formulation of the [ABC model of flower development](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/ABC_model "ABC model") by [E. Coen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Enrico_Coen "Enrico Coen") and [E. Meyerowitz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elliot_Meyerowitz "Elliot Meyerowitz").[[62]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-62) According to this model, floral organ identity genes are divided into three classes - class A genes (which affect sepals and petals), class B genes (which affect petals and stamens), and class C genes (which affect stamens and carpels). These genes code for [transcription factors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcription_factor "Transcription factor") that combine to cause tissue specification in their respective regions during development. Although developed through study of _A. thaliana_ flowers, this model is generally applicable to other flowering plants.

Studies of _A. thaliana_ have provided considerable insights with regards to the genetics of leaf morphogenesis, particularly in [dicotyledon-type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dicotyledon "Dicotyledon") plants.[[63]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-63)[[64]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-64) Much of the understanding has come from analyzing mutants in leaf development, some of which were identified in the 1960s, but were not analysed with genetic and molecular techniques until the mid-1990s. _A. thaliana_ leaves are well suited to studies of leaf development because they are relatively simple and stable.

Using _A. thaliana_, the genetics behind leaf shape development have become more clear and have been broken down into three stages: The initiation of the [leaf primordium](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Leaf_primordium&action=edit&redlink=1 "Leaf primordium (page does not exist)"), the establishment of [dorsiventrality](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dorsiventrality&action=edit&redlink=1 "Dorsiventrality (page does not exist)"), and the development of a marginal [meristem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meristem "Meristem"). Leaf primordia are initiated by the suppression of the genes and proteins of class I _[KNOX](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evolutionary\_history\_of\_plants "Evolutionary history of plants")_ family (such as _SHOOT APICAL MERISTEMLESS_). These class I KNOX proteins directly suppress [gibberellin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gibberellin "Gibberellin") biosynthesis in the leaf primordium. Many genetic factors were found to be involved in the suppression of these class I _KNOX_ genes in leaf primordia (such as _ASYMMETRIC LEAVES1,_ _BLADE-ON-PETIOLE1_, _SAWTOOTH1_, etc.). Thus, with this suppression, the levels of gibberellin increase and leaf primordium initiate growth.

The establishment of leaf dorsiventrality is important since the [dorsal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anatomical_terms_of_location "Anatomical terms of location") (adaxial) surface of the leaf is different from the ventral (abaxial) surface.[[65]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-65)

_A. thaliana_ is well suited for [light microscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Light_microscopy "Light microscopy") analysis. Young [seedlings](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Seedlings "Seedlings") on the whole, and their roots in particular, are relatively translucent. This, together with their small size, facilitates live cell imaging using both [fluorescence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fluorescence_microscopy "Fluorescence microscopy") and [confocal laser scanning microscopy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confocal_laser_scanning_microscopy "Confocal laser scanning microscopy").[[66]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-66) By wet-mounting seedlings in water or in culture media, plants may be imaged uninvasively, obviating the need for [fixation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fixation_(histology) "Fixation (histology)") and [sectioning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dissection "Dissection") and allowing [time-lapse](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Time-lapse "Time-lapse") measurements.[[67]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-67) Fluorescent protein constructs can be introduced through [transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transformation_(genetics) "Transformation (genetics)"). The [developmental](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant_morphology "Plant morphology") stage of each cell can be inferred from its location in the plant or by using [fluorescent protein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Green_fluorescent_protein "Green fluorescent protein")[markers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Biomarker "Biomarker"), allowing detailed [developmental analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Developmental_biology "Developmental biology").

### Light sensing, light emission, and circadian biology

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Arabidopsis_thaliana&action=edit&section=20 "Edit section: Light sensing, light emission, and circadian biology")]

The photoreceptors [phytochromes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phytochrome "Phytochrome") A, B, C, D, and E mediate red light-based [phototropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phototropism "Phototropism") response. Understanding the function of these receptors has helped plant biologists understand the signaling cascades that regulate [photoperiodism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoperiodism "Photoperiodism"), [germination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germination "Germination"), [de-etiolation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/De-etiolation "De-etiolation"), and [shade avoidance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Shade_avoidance "Shade avoidance") in plants. The genes _[FCA](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=FCA\_(plant\_gene)&action=edit&redlink=1 "FCA (plant gene) (page does not exist)")_,[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Simpson-Dean-2002-68)_[fy](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fy\_(plant\_gene)&action=edit&redlink=1 "Fy (plant gene) (page does not exist)")_,[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Simpson-Dean-2002-68)_[fpa](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fpa\_(plant\_gene)&action=edit&redlink=1 "Fpa (plant gene) (page does not exist)")_,[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Simpson-Dean-2002-68)_[LUMINIDEPENDENS](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=LUMINIDEPENDENS&action=edit&redlink=1 "LUMINIDEPENDENS (page does not exist)")_ (_ld_),[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Simpson-Dean-2002-68)_[fly](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fly\_(plant\_gene)&action=edit&redlink=1 "Fly (plant gene) (page does not exist)")_,[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Simpson-Dean-2002-68)_[fve](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Fve\_(plant\_gene)&action=edit&redlink=1 "Fve (plant gene) (page does not exist)")_[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Simpson-Dean-2002-68) and _[FLOWERING LOCUS C](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=FLOWERING\_LOCUS\_C&action=edit&redlink=1 "FLOWERING LOCUS C (page does not exist)")_ (_FLC_)[[69]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Friedman-2020-69)[[70]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Whittaker-Dean-2017-70) are involved in [photoperiod](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photoperiod "Photoperiod") triggering of flowering and [vernalization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vernalization "Vernalization"). Specifically Lee et al 1994 find _ld_ produces a [homeodomain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homeodomain "Homeodomain") and Blazquez et al 2001 that _fve_ produces a [WD40 repeat](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/WD40_repeat "WD40 repeat").[[68]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Simpson-Dean-2002-68)

The [UVR8](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UVR8 "UVR8") protein detects [UV-B](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UV-B "UV-B") light and mediates the response to this DNA-damaging wavelength.

_A. thaliana_ was used extensively in the study of the genetic basis of [phototropism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phototropism "Phototropism"), [chloroplast](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chloroplast "Chloroplast") alignment, and [stomal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Stoma "Stoma") aperture and other blue light-influenced processes.[[71]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-71) These traits respond to blue light, which is perceived by the [phototropin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phototropin "Phototropin") light receptors. Arabidopsis has also been important in understanding the functions of another blue light receptor, [cryptochrome](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cryptochrome "Cryptochrome"), which is especially important for light entrainment to control the plants' [circadian rhythms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Circadian_rhythm "Circadian rhythm").[[72]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-72) When the onset of darkness is unusually early, _A. thaliana_ reduces its metabolism of starch by an amount that effectively requires [division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant_arithmetic "Plant arithmetic").[[73]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-73)

Light responses were even found in roots, previously thought to be largely insensitive to light. While the [gravitropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gravitropism "Gravitropism") response of _A. thaliana_ root organs is their predominant tropic response, specimens treated with [mutagens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutagen "Mutagen") and selected for the absence of gravitropic action showed negative phototropic response to blue or white light, and positive response to red light, indicating that the roots also show positive phototropism.[[74]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-74)

In 2000, Dr. [Janet Braam](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Janet_Braam&action=edit&redlink=1 "Janet Braam (page does not exist)") of [Rice University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rice_University "Rice University") genetically engineered _A. thaliana_ to glow in the dark when touched. The effect was visible to ultrasensitive cameras.[[75]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-75)[_[better source needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:NOTRS "Wikipedia:NOTRS")_]

Multiple efforts, including the [Glowing Plant project](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Glowing_Plant_project "Glowing Plant project"), have sought to use _A. thaliana_ to increase plant luminescence intensity towards commercially viable levels.

### Thigmomorphogenesis (Touch response)

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Arabidopsis_thaliana&action=edit&section=21 "Edit section: Thigmomorphogenesis (Touch response)")]

In 1990, Janet Braam and [Ronald W. Davis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ronald_W._Davis "Ronald W. Davis") determined that _A. thaliana_ exhibits [thigmomorphogenesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thigmomorphogenesis "Thigmomorphogenesis") in response to wind, rain and touch.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Braam-1990-76) Four or more touch induced genes in _A. thaliana_ were found to be regulated by such stimuli.[[76]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Braam-1990-76) In 2002, [Massimo Pigliucci](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Massimo_Pigliucci "Massimo Pigliucci") found that _A. thaliana_ developed different patterns of branching in response to sustained exposure to wind, a display of [phenotypic plasticity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Phenotypic_plasticity "Phenotypic plasticity").[[77]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-77)

On January 2, 2019, China's [Chang'e-4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chang%27e-4 "Chang'e-4") lander brought _A. thaliana_ to the moon.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Letzter2019-78) A small [microcosm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microcosm_(experimental_ecosystem) "Microcosm (experimental ecosystem)") 'tin' in the lander contained _A. thaliana_, seeds of potatoes, and [silkworm](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bombyx_mori "Bombyx mori") eggs. As plants would support the silkworms with oxygen, and the silkworms would in turn provide the plants with necessary carbon dioxide and nutrients through their waste,[[79]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-79) researchers will evaluate whether plants successfully perform [photosynthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Photosynthesis "Photosynthesis"), and grow and bloom in the lunar environment.[[78]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Letzter2019-78)

Plant–pathogen interactions
---------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Arabidopsis_thaliana&action=edit&section=24 "Edit section: Plant–pathogen interactions")]

Understanding how plants achieve resistance is important to protect the world's food production, and the agriculture industry. Many model systems have been developed to better understand interactions between plants and [bacterial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bacterial "Bacterial"), [fungal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fungi "Fungi"), [oomycete](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oomycete "Oomycete"), [viral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Virus "Virus"), and [nematode](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nematode "Nematode") pathogens. _A. thaliana_ has been a powerful tool for the study of the subdiscipline of [plant pathology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant_pathology "Plant pathology"), that is, the interaction between plants and disease-causing [pathogens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathogens "Pathogens").

The use of _A. thaliana_ has led to many breakthroughs in the advancement of knowledge of how plants manifest [plant disease resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant_disease_resistance "Plant disease resistance"). The reason most plants are resistant to most pathogens is through nonhost resistance - not all pathogens will infect all plants. An example where _A. thaliana_ was used to determine the genes responsible for nonhost resistance is _[Blumeria graminis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Blumeria\_graminis "Blumeria graminis")_, the causal agent of powdery mildew of grasses. _A. thaliana_ mutants were developed using the [mutagen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mutagenic "Mutagenic")[ethyl methanesulfonate](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethyl_methanesulfonate "Ethyl methanesulfonate") and screened to identify mutants with increased infection by _B. graminis_.[[82]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-82)[[83]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-83)[[84]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-84) The mutants with higher infection rates are referred to as _PEN_ mutants due to the ability of _B. graminis_ to penetrate _A. thaliana_ to begin the disease process. The _PEN_ genes were later mapped to identify the genes responsible for nonhost resistance to _B. graminis_.

In general, when a plant is exposed to a pathogen, or [nonpathogenic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commensalism "Commensalism") microbe, an initial response, known as PAMP-triggered immunity (PTI), occurs because the plant detects conserved motifs known as [pathogen-associated molecular patterns](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathogen-associated_molecular_pattern "Pathogen-associated molecular pattern") (PAMPs).[[85]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-85) These PAMPs are detected by specialized [receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Receptor_(biochemistry) "Receptor (biochemistry)") in the host known as [pattern recognition receptors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pattern_recognition_receptors "Pattern recognition receptors") (PRRs) on the plant cell surface.

The best-characterized PRR in _A. thaliana_ is FLS2 (Flagellin-Sensing2), which recognizes bacterial [flagellin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Flagellin "Flagellin"),[[86]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-86)[[87]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-87) a specialized organelle used by microorganisms for the purpose of motility, as well as the [ligand](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ligand_(biochemistry) "Ligand (biochemistry)") flg22, which comprises the 22 amino acids recognized by FLS2. Discovery of FLS2 was facilitated by the identification of an _A. thaliana_ ecotype, Ws-0, that was unable to detect flg22, leading to the identification of the gene encoding FLS2. [FLS2 shows striking similarity to rice XA21, the first PRR isolated in 1995](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pamela_Ronald#Xa21:_Pattern_recognition_receptor-mediated_immunity "Pamela Ronald").[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_] Both flagellin and [UV-C](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/UV-C "UV-C") act similarly to increase [homologous recombination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Homologous_recombination "Homologous recombination") in _A. thaliana_, as demonstrated by Molinier et al. 2006. Beyond this [somatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Somatic_(biology) "Somatic (biology)") effect, they found this to [extend to subsequent generations of the plant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Epigenetic_trait "Epigenetic trait").[[88]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Urban-et-al-2018-88)

A second PRR, EF-Tu receptor (EFR), identified in _A. thaliana_, recognizes the bacterial [EF-Tu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/EF-Tu "EF-Tu") protein, the prokaryotic elongation factor used in [protein synthesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_synthesis "Protein synthesis"), as well as the laboratory-used ligand elf18.[[89]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-89) Using [_Agrobacterium_-mediated transformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agrobacterium#Uses_in_biotechnology "Agrobacterium"), a technique that takes advantage of the natural process by which _Agrobacterium_ transfers genes into host plants, the EFR gene was transformed into _[Nicotiana benthamiana](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nicotiana\_benthamiana "Nicotiana benthamiana")_, tobacco plant that does not recognize EF-Tu, thereby permitting recognition of bacterial EF-Tu[[90]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-90) thereby confirming EFR as the receptor of EF-Tu.

Both FLS2 and EFR use similar [signal transduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Signal_transduction "Signal transduction") pathways to initiate PTI. _A. thaliana_ has been instrumental in dissecting these pathways to better understand the regulation of immune responses, the most notable one being the [mitogen-activated protein kinase](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mitogen-activated_protein_kinase "Mitogen-activated protein kinase") (MAP kinase) cascade. Downstream responses of PTI include [callose](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Callose "Callose") deposition, the [oxidative burst](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oxidative_burst "Oxidative burst"), and transcription of defense-related genes.[[91]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-91)

PTI is able to combat pathogens in a nonspecific manner. A stronger and more specific response in plants is that of effector-triggered immunity (ETI), which is dependent upon the recognition of pathogen effectors, proteins secreted by the pathogen that alter functions in the host, by plant [resistance genes (R-genes)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/R_gene "R gene"), often described as [a gene-for-gene relationship](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene-for-gene_relationship "Gene-for-gene relationship"). This recognition may occur directly or indirectly via a guardee protein in a hypothesis known as [the guard hypothesis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gene-for-gene_relationship#The_guard_hypothesis "Gene-for-gene relationship"). The first R-gene cloned in _A. thaliana_ was _RPS2_ (resistance to _Pseudomonas syringae_ 2), which is responsible for recognition of the effector avrRpt2.[[92]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-92) The bacterial effector avrRpt2 is delivered into _A. thaliana_ via the [Type III secretion system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Type_III_secretion_system "Type III secretion system") of [_P. syringae_ pv. _tomato_ strain DC3000](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudomonas_syringae#Pseudomonas_syringae_pv._tomato_strain_DC3000_and_Arabidopsis_thaliana "Pseudomonas syringae"). Recognition of avrRpt2 by RPS2 occurs via the guardee protein RIN4, which is cleaved.[_[clarification needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Please\_clarify "Wikipedia:Please clarify")_] Recognition of a pathogen effector leads to a dramatic immune response known as the [hypersensitive response](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypersensitive_response "Hypersensitive response"), in which the infected plant cells undergo cell death to prevent the spread of the pathogen.[[93]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-93)

[Systemic acquired resistance](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Systemic_acquired_resistance "Systemic acquired resistance") (SAR) is another example of resistance that is better understood in plants because of research done in _A. thaliana_. Benzothiadiazol (BTH), a [salicylic acid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salicylic_acid "Salicylic acid") (SA) analog, has been used historically as an antifungal compound in crop plants. BTH, as well as SA, has been shown to induce SAR in plants. The initiation of the SAR pathway was first demonstrated in _A. thaliana_ in which increased SA levels are recognized by nonexpresser of PR genes 1 (_NPR1_)[[94]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-94) due to redox change in the cytosol, resulting in the [reduction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Redox "Redox") of _NPR1. NPR1_, which usually exists in a multiplex (oligomeric) state, becomes monomeric (a single unit) upon reduction.[[95]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-95) When NPR1 becomes monomeric, it [translocates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_targeting#Protein_translocation "Protein targeting") to the nucleus, where it interacts with many TGA [transcription factors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Transcription_factor "Transcription factor"), and is able to induce pathogen-related genes such as _PR1_.[[96]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-96) Another example of SAR would be the research done with transgenic tobacco plants, which express bacterial salicylate hydroxylase, nahG gene, requires the accumulation of SA for its expression[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Delaney,_T._1994-97)

Although not directly immunological, [intracellular transport](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intracellular_transport "Intracellular transport") affects [susceptibility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Susceptible_individual "Susceptible individual") by incorporating - or being tricked into incorporating - pathogen particles. For example, the _[Dynamin-related protein 2b](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Dynamin-related\_protein\_2b&action=edit&redlink=1 "Dynamin-related protein 2b (page does not exist)")/[drp2b](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Drp2b&action=edit&redlink=1 "Drp2b (page does not exist)")_ gene helps to move invaginated material into cells, with some mutants increasing _[PstDC3000](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PstDC3000 "PstDC3000")_ virulence even further.[[98]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Khaled-et-al-2015-98)

### Evolutionary aspect of plant-pathogen resistance

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Arabidopsis_thaliana&action=edit&section=25 "Edit section: Evolutionary aspect of plant-pathogen resistance")]

Plants are affected by multiple [pathogens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathogens "Pathogens") throughout their lifetimes. In response to the presence of pathogens, plants have evolved receptors on their cell surfaces to detect and respond to pathogens.[[99]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Bent1994-99)_Arabidopsis thaliana_ is a model organism used to determine specific defense mechanisms of plant-pathogen resistance.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Zipfel,_C._2004-100) These plants have special receptors on their cell surfaces that allow for detection of pathogens and initiate mechanisms to inhibit pathogen growth.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Zipfel,_C._2004-100) They contain two receptors, FLS2 (bacterial flagellin receptor) and EF-Tu (bacterial EF-Tu protein), which use signal transduction pathways to initiate the disease response pathway.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Zipfel,_C._2004-100) The pathway leads to the recognition of the pathogen causing the infected cells to undergo cell death to stop the spread of the pathogen.[[100]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Zipfel,_C._2004-100) Plants with FLS2 and EF-Tu receptors have shown to have increased fitness in the population.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Delaney,_T._1994-97) This has led to the belief that plant-pathogen resistance is an evolutionary mechanism that has built up over generations to respond to dynamic environments, such as increased predation and extreme temperatures.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Delaney,_T._1994-97)

_A. thaliana_ has also been used to study SAR.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Lawton,_K._1996-101) This pathway uses benzothiadiazol, a chemical inducer, to induce transcription factors, mRNA, of SAR genes. This accumulation of transcription factors leads to inhibition of pathogen-related genes.[[101]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Lawton,_K._1996-101)

Plant-pathogen interactions are important for an understanding of how plants have evolved to combat different types of pathogens that may affect them.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Delaney,_T._1994-97) Variation in resistance of plants across populations is due to variation in environmental factors. Plants that have evolved resistance, whether it be the general variation or the SAR variation, have been able to live longer and hold off necrosis of their tissue (premature death of cells), which leads to better adaptation and fitness for populations that are in rapidly changing environments.[[97]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Delaney,_T._1994-97) In the future, comparisons of the [pathosystems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pathosystem "Pathosystem") of wild populations + their [coevolved](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coevolution "Coevolution") pathogens with wild-wild hybrids of known parentage may reveal new mechanisms of [balancing selection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Balancing_selection "Balancing selection"). In [life history theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life_history_theory "Life history theory") we may find that _A. thaliana_ maintains certain alleles due to [pleitropy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pleitropy "Pleitropy") between plant-pathogen effects and other traits, as in livestock.[[102]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Fridman-2015-102)

Research in _A. thaliana_ suggests that the [immunity regulator protein family EDS1](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=EDS1_family&action=edit&redlink=1 "EDS1 family (page does not exist)") in general co-evolved with the [CC HELO](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=CCHELO&action=edit&redlink=1 "CCHELO (page does not exist)") family of [nucleotide-binding–leucine-rich-repeat-receptors (NLRs)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/NOD-like_receptor "NOD-like receptor"). Xiao et al. 2005 have shown that the [powdery mildew](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Powdery_mildew "Powdery mildew") immunity mediated by _A. thaliana_'s [RPW8](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=RPW8&action=edit&redlink=1 "RPW8 (page does not exist)") (which has a CC HELO[domain](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protein_domain "Protein domain")) is dependent on two members of this family: _[EDS1](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=EDS1&action=edit&redlink=1 "EDS1 (page does not exist)")_ itself and _[PAD4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/PAD4 "PAD4")_.[[103]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Lapin-et-al-2020-103)

_[RESISTANCE TO PSEUDOMONAS SYRINGAE 5](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=RESISTANCE\_TO\_PSEUDOMONAS\_SYRINGAE\_5&action=edit&redlink=1 "RESISTANCE TO PSEUDOMONAS SYRINGAE 5 (page does not exist)")/RPS5_ is a [disease resistance protein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Plant_disease_resistance_protein "Plant disease resistance protein") which guards _[AvrPphB SUSCEPTIBLE 1](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/AvrPphB\_SUSCEPTIBLE\_1 "AvrPphB SUSCEPTIBLE 1")/PBS1_. _PBS1_, as the name would suggest, is the target of _[AvrPphB](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=AvrPphB&action=edit&redlink=1 "AvrPphB (page does not exist)")_, an [effector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Effector_(biology) "Effector (biology)") produced by [_Pseudomonas syringae_ pv. _phaseolicola_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pseudomonas_syringae_pv._phaseolicola "Pseudomonas syringae pv. phaseolicola").[[104]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-Pottinger-Innes-2020-104)

Ongoing research on _A. thaliana_ is being performed on the [International Space Station](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Space_Station "International Space Station") by the [European Space Agency](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/European_Space_Agency "European Space Agency"). The goals are to study the growth and reproduction of plants from seed to seed in [microgravity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Microgravity "Microgravity").[[105]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-105)[[106]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-106)

Plant-on-a-chip devices in which _A. thaliana_ tissues can be cultured in semi-_in vitro_ conditions have been described.[[107]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-107) Use of these devices may aid understanding of pollen-tube guidance and the mechanism of sexual reproduction in _A. thaliana._

Researchers at the [University of Florida](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Florida "University of Florida") were able to grow the plant in [lunar soil](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lunar_soil "Lunar soil") originating from the [Sea of Tranquillity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mare_Tranquillitatis "Mare Tranquillitatis").[[108]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-108)

_A. thaliana_ is a predominantly self-pollinating plant with an outcrossing rate estimated at less than 0.3%.[[109]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-109) An analysis of the genome-wide pattern of linkage disequilibrium suggested that self-pollination evolved roughly a million years ago or more.[[110]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-pmid17656687-110) Meioses that lead to self-pollination are unlikely to produce significant beneficial genetic variability. However, these meioses can provide the adaptive benefit of recombinational repair of DNA damages during formation of germ cells at each generation.[[111]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Arabidopsis_thaliana#cite_note-111) Such a benefit may have been sufficient to allow the long-term persistence of meioses even when followed by self-fertilization. A physical mechanism for self-pollination in _A. thaliana_ is through pre-anthesis autogamy, such that fertilisation takes place largely before flower opening.
