In [geometry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometry "Geometry"), a **hypercube** is an [_n_-dimensional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-dimensional_space "N-dimensional space") analogue of a [square](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(geometry) "Square (geometry)") ([_n_ = 2](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Two-dimensional "Two-dimensional")) and a [cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube "Cube") ([_n_ = 3](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Three-dimensional "Three-dimensional")); the special case for [_n_ = 4](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Four-dimensional_space "Four-dimensional space") is known as a _[tesseract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract "Tesseract")_. It is a [closed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Closed_set "Closed set"), [compact](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compact_space "Compact space"), [convex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polytope "Convex polytope") figure whose 1-[skeleton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/N-skeleton "N-skeleton") consists of groups of opposite [parallel](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Parallel_(geometry) "Parallel (geometry)")[line segments](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Line_segment "Line segment") aligned in each of the space's [dimensions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dimension "Dimension"), [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") to each other and of the same length. A unit hypercube's longest diagonal in _n_ dimensions is equal to ![Image 3: {\displaystyle {\sqrt {n}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2a2994734eae382ce30100fb17b9447fd8e99f81).

An _n_-dimensional hypercube is more commonly referred to as an **_n_-cube** or sometimes as an **_n_-dimensional cube**.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube#cite_note-1)[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube#cite_note-2) The term **measure polytope** (originally from Elte, 1912)[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube#cite_note-3) is also used, notably in the work of [H. S. M. Coxeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Harold_Scott_MacDonald_Coxeter "Harold Scott MacDonald Coxeter") who also labels the hypercubes the γ n polytopes.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoxeter1973122%E2%80%93123%C2%A77.2_see_illustration_Fig_7.2%3Csmall%3EC%3C/small%3E-4)

The hypercube is the special case of a [hyperrectangle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hyperrectangle "Hyperrectangle") (also called an _n-orthotope_).

A _unit hypercube_ is a hypercube whose side has length one [unit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_(number) "Unit (number)"). Often, the hypercube whose corners (or _vertices_) are the 2 _n_ points in **R**_n_ with each coordinate equal to 0 or 1 is called _the_ unit hypercube.

### By the number of dimensions

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hypercube&action=edit&section=2 "Edit section: By the number of dimensions")]

[![Image 4](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d9/From_Point_to_Tesseract_%28Looped_Version%29.gif/250px-From_Point_to_Tesseract_%28Looped_Version%29.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:From_Point_to_Tesseract_(Looped_Version).gif)

An animation showing how to create a tesseract from a point.

A hypercube can be defined by increasing the numbers of dimensions of a shape:

**0** – A point is a hypercube of dimension zero.**1** – If one moves this point one unit length, it will sweep out a line segment, which is a unit hypercube of dimension one.**2** – If one moves this line segment its length in a [perpendicular](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Perpendicular "Perpendicular") direction from itself; it sweeps out a 2-dimensional square.**3** – If one moves the square one unit length in the direction perpendicular to the plane it lies on, it will generate a 3-dimensional cube.**4** – If one moves the cube one unit length into the fourth dimension, it generates a 4-dimensional unit hypercube (a unit [tesseract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract "Tesseract")).
This can be generalized to any number of dimensions. This process of sweeping out volumes can be formalized mathematically as a [Minkowski sum](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Minkowski_sum "Minkowski sum"): the _d_-dimensional hypercube is the Minkowski sum of _d_ mutually perpendicular unit-length line segments, and is therefore an example of a [zonotope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Zonotope "Zonotope").

The [1-skeleton](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Graph_of_a_polytope "Graph of a polytope") of a hypercube is a [hypercube graph](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube_graph "Hypercube graph").

Projection of a [rotating](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rotation "Rotation")[tesseract](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tesseract "Tesseract").

A unit hypercube of dimension ![Image 6: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) is the [convex hull](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_hull "Convex hull") of all the ![Image 7: {\displaystyle 2^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8226f30650ee4fe4e640c6d2798127e80e9c160d) points whose ![Image 8: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)[Cartesian coordinates](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_coordinate_system "Cartesian coordinate system") are each equal to either ![Image 9: {\displaystyle 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2aae8864a3c1fec9585261791a809ddec1489950) or ![Image 10: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf). These points are its [vertices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vertex_(geometry) "Vertex (geometry)"). The hypercube with these coordinates is also the [cartesian product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cartesian_product "Cartesian product")![Image 11: {\displaystyle [0,1]^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/40160923273b7109968df994dca832b91d957bf2) of ![Image 12: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) copies of the unit [interval](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Interval_(mathematics) "Interval (mathematics)")![Image 13: {\displaystyle [0,1]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/738f7d23bb2d9642bab520020873cccbef49768d). Another unit hypercube, centered at the origin of the ambient space, can be obtained from this one by a [translation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Translation_(geometry) "Translation (geometry)"). It is the convex hull of the ![Image 14: {\displaystyle 2^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8226f30650ee4fe4e640c6d2798127e80e9c160d) points whose vectors of Cartesian coordinates are

![Image 15: {\displaystyle \left(\pm {\frac {1}{2}},\pm {\frac {1}{2}},\cdots ,\pm {\frac {1}{2}}\right)\!.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8b0316b3b03fedc0d82b3f26d3dd2e42b49e57a5)
Here the symbol ![Image 16: {\displaystyle \pm }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/869e366caf596564de4de06cb0ba124056d4064b) means that each coordinate is either equal to ![Image 17: {\displaystyle 1/2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e308a3a46b7fdce07cc09dcab9e8d8f73e37d935) or to ![Image 18: {\displaystyle -1/2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6fb9deb994fbfacb465f9a9d34b0f85d3cfc3985). This unit hypercube is also the cartesian product ![Image 19: {\displaystyle [-1/2,1/2]^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/98cea6b9a196dae533439e1146656e8c29dd732d). Any unit hypercube has an edge length of ![Image 20: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf) and an ![Image 21: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-dimensional volume of ![Image 22: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf).

The ![Image 23: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-dimensional hypercube obtained as the convex hull of the points with coordinates ![Image 24: {\displaystyle (\pm 1,\pm 1,\cdots ,\pm 1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/08b276ad4375558fa5ab6fa9139b7c63a3309b5b) or, equivalently as the Cartesian product ![Image 25: {\displaystyle [-1,1]^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6a008254b1bf6d63ac3b13548c4c31180bcd43de) is also often considered due to the simpler form of its vertex coordinates. Its edge length is ![Image 26: {\displaystyle 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/901fc910c19990d0dbaaefe4726ceb1a4e217a0f), and its ![Image 27: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-dimensional volume is ![Image 28: {\displaystyle 2^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8226f30650ee4fe4e640c6d2798127e80e9c160d).

Every hypercube admits, as its faces, hypercubes of a lower dimension contained in its boundary. A hypercube of dimension ![Image 29: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) admits ![Image 30: {\displaystyle 2n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/134afa8ff09fdddd24b06f289e92e3a045092bd1) facets, or faces of dimension ![Image 31: {\displaystyle n-1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fbd0b0f32b28f51962943ee9ede4fb34198a2521): a (![Image 32: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf)-dimensional) line segment has ![Image 33: {\displaystyle 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/901fc910c19990d0dbaaefe4726ceb1a4e217a0f) endpoints; a (![Image 34: {\displaystyle 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/901fc910c19990d0dbaaefe4726ceb1a4e217a0f)-dimensional) square has ![Image 35: {\displaystyle 4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/295b4bf1de7cd3500e740e0f4f0635db22d87b42) sides or edges; a ![Image 36: {\displaystyle 3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/991e33c6e207b12546f15bdfee8b5726eafbbb2f)-dimensional cube has ![Image 37: {\displaystyle 6}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/39d81124420a058a7474dfeda48228fb6ee1e253) square faces; a (![Image 38: {\displaystyle 4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/295b4bf1de7cd3500e740e0f4f0635db22d87b42)-dimensional) tesseract has ![Image 39: {\displaystyle 8}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1aaa997e6ad67716cfaa9a02c4df860bf60a95b5) three-dimensional cubes as its facets. The number of vertices of a hypercube of dimension ![Image 40: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) is ![Image 41: {\displaystyle 2^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8226f30650ee4fe4e640c6d2798127e80e9c160d) (a usual, ![Image 42: {\displaystyle 3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/991e33c6e207b12546f15bdfee8b5726eafbbb2f)-dimensional cube has ![Image 43: {\displaystyle 2^{3}=8}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bb2dded8eba905e4a019b70abad935422b198db4) vertices, for instance).[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube#cite_note-5)

The number of the ![Image 44: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc)-dimensional hypercubes (just referred to as ![Image 45: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc)-cubes from here on) contained in the boundary of an ![Image 46: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b)-cube is

![Image 47: {\displaystyle E_{m,n}=2^{n-m}{n \choose m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8c3640d85e3b0cc57e1277e50f19381c5c407f1a),[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube#cite_note-FOOTNOTECoxeter1973122%C2%A77%C2%B725-6) where ![Image 48: {\displaystyle {n \choose m}={\frac {n!}{m!\,(n-m)!}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c1fb12e246daadda80da1a673ccef19208b8b453) and ![Image 49: {\displaystyle n!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/bae971720be3cc9b8d82f4cdac89cb89877514a6) denotes the [factorial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Factorial "Factorial") of ![Image 50: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b).
For example, the boundary of a ![Image 51: {\displaystyle 4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/295b4bf1de7cd3500e740e0f4f0635db22d87b42)-cube (![Image 52: {\displaystyle n=4}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/d928ec15aeef83aade867992ee473933adb6139d)) contains ![Image 53: {\displaystyle 8}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1aaa997e6ad67716cfaa9a02c4df860bf60a95b5) cubes (![Image 54: {\displaystyle 3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/991e33c6e207b12546f15bdfee8b5726eafbbb2f)-cubes), ![Image 55: {\displaystyle 24}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0be92101c8b0277e66fdefeef1ccdd7788e88ef5) squares (![Image 56: {\displaystyle 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/901fc910c19990d0dbaaefe4726ceb1a4e217a0f)-cubes), ![Image 57: {\displaystyle 32}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a28cd1505cccfe738e9c90499f48ef42bf2ed5c7) line segments (![Image 58: {\displaystyle 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/92d98b82a3778f043108d4e20960a9193df57cbf)-cubes) and ![Image 59: {\displaystyle 16}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/960615e346e1c003a911f45b1225113ea01b4ff7) vertices (![Image 60: {\displaystyle 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2aae8864a3c1fec9585261791a809ddec1489950)-cubes). This identity can be proven by a simple combinatorial argument: for each of the ![Image 61: {\displaystyle 2^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8226f30650ee4fe4e640c6d2798127e80e9c160d) vertices of the hypercube, there are ![Image 62: {\displaystyle {\tbinom {n}{m}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6f5fc3d476fe471ad644bcda5a5681e67cbf8511) ways to choose a collection of ![Image 63: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc) edges incident to that vertex. Each of these collections defines one of the ![Image 64: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc)-dimensional faces incident to the considered vertex. Doing this for all the vertices of the hypercube, each of the ![Image 65: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc)-dimensional faces of the hypercube is counted ![Image 66: {\displaystyle 2^{m}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/667d0154f26e56e3f7979803f08afac16b4dcb16) times since it has that many vertices, and we need to divide ![Image 67: {\displaystyle 2^{n}{\tbinom {n}{m}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fcfe8d9c36505aecab19a417c92758f2b78d9d42) by this number.

The number of facets of the hypercube can be used to compute the ![Image 68: {\displaystyle (n-1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df88c6333caaf6471cf277f24b802ff9931b133e)-dimensional volume of its boundary: that volume is ![Image 69: {\displaystyle 2n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/134afa8ff09fdddd24b06f289e92e3a045092bd1) times the volume of a ![Image 70: {\displaystyle (n-1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df88c6333caaf6471cf277f24b802ff9931b133e)-dimensional hypercube; that is, ![Image 71: {\displaystyle 2ns^{n-1}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/58728f35cf60e3d94563246d6ea78593c9da2275) where ![Image 72: {\displaystyle s}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/01d131dfd7673938b947072a13a9744fe997e632) is the length of the edges of the hypercube.

These numbers can also be generated by the linear [recurrence relation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recurrence_relation "Recurrence relation").

![Image 73: {\displaystyle E_{m,n}=2E_{m,n-1}+E_{m-1,n-1}\!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/498de618fb14e7124942c927ac9b88058aa38f11),  with ![Image 74: {\displaystyle E_{0,0}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ef40c8464e6877bc77e0d0dce27e72388d088cd7), and ![Image 75: {\displaystyle E_{m,n}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/684c3a89f8e663f91665c272dd2a8a2c09819313) when ![Image 76: {\displaystyle n<m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5cff096773597d7223f9d90162eb2d780dfc18dc), ![Image 77: {\displaystyle n<0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ad9f68fb8426c4411972241aac6c359e7812eaba), or ![Image 78: {\displaystyle m<0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6ba378205efd9dc579591efdd5baa301f4f6e2fa).
For example, extending a square via its 4 vertices adds one extra line segment (edge) per vertex. Adding the opposite square to form a cube provides ![Image 79: {\displaystyle E_{1,3}=12}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/086caa1f3c60e5d2e35664b64135f69ce190615e) line segments.

The extended [f-vector](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/F-vector "F-vector") for an _n_-cube can also be computed by expanding ![Image 80: {\displaystyle (2x+1)^{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/19dabd9d43c4ad5513e600bd687147bfc08a2b15) (concisely, (2,1)_n_), and reading off the coefficients of the resulting [polynomial](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polynomial#Multiplication "Polynomial"). For example, the elements of a tesseract is (2,1)4 = (4,4,1)2 = (16,32,24,8,1).

The hypercubes are one of the few families of [regular polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polytope "Regular polytope") that are represented in any number of dimensions.[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercube#cite_note-8)

The **hypercube** family is one of three [regular polytope](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Regular_polytope "Regular polytope") families, labeled by [Coxeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Coxeter "Coxeter") as _γ n_. The other two are the hypercube dual family, the **[cross-polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-polytope "Cross-polytope")**, labeled as _β n,_ and the **[simplices](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex "Simplex")**, labeled as _α n_. A fourth family, the [infinite tessellations of hypercubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hypercubic_honeycomb "Hypercubic honeycomb"), is labeled as _δ n_.

Another related family of semiregular and [uniform polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Uniform_polytope "Uniform polytope") is the **[demihypercubes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Demihypercube "Demihypercube")**, which are constructed from hypercubes with alternate vertices deleted and [simplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex "Simplex") facets added in the gaps, labeled as _hγ n_.

_n_-cubes can be combined with their duals (the [cross-polytopes](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cross-polytope "Cross-polytope")) to form compound polytopes:

*   In two dimensions, we obtain the [octagrammic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Octagram "Octagram") star figure {8/2},
*   In three dimensions we obtain the [compound of cube and octahedron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Compound_of_cube_and_octahedron "Compound of cube and octahedron"),
*   In four dimensions we obtain the compound of tesseract and 16-cell.

Relation to (_n_−1)-simplices
-----------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hypercube&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Relation to (n−1)-simplices")]

The graph of the _n_-hypercube's edges is [isomorphic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Isomorphism "Isomorphism") to the [Hasse diagram](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hasse_diagram "Hasse diagram") of the (_n_−1)-[simplex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Simplex "Simplex")'s [face lattice](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Convex_polytope#The_face_lattice "Convex polytope"). This can be seen by orienting the _n_-hypercube so that two opposite vertices lie vertically, corresponding to the (_n_−1)-simplex itself and the null polytope, respectively. Each vertex connected to the top vertex then uniquely maps to one of the (_n_−1)-simplex's facets (_n_−2 faces), and each vertex connected to those vertices maps to one of the simplex's _n_−3 faces, and so forth, and the vertices connected to the bottom vertex map to the simplex's vertices.

This relation may be used to generate the face lattice of an (_n_−1)-simplex efficiently, since face lattice enumeration algorithms applicable to general polytopes are more computationally expensive.

Relation to exponentiation
--------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Hypercube&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Relation to exponentiation")]

Any positive integer raised to another positive integer power will yield a third integer, with this third integer being a specific type of [figurate number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Figurate_number "Figurate number") corresponding to an _n_-cube with a number of dimensions corresponding to the exponential. For example, the exponent 2 will yield a [square number](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_number "Square number") or "perfect square", which can be arranged into a square shape with a side length corresponding to that of the base. Similarly, the exponent 3 will yield [a perfect cube](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cube_(algebra)#In_integers "Cube (algebra)"), an integer which can be arranged into a cube shape with a side length of the base. As a result, the act of raising a number to 2 or 3 is more commonly referred to as "[squaring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Square_(algebra) "Square (algebra)")" and "cubing", respectively. However, the names of higher-order hypercubes do not appear to be in common use for higher powers.
