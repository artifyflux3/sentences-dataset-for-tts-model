Symbolic statement![Image 3: {\displaystyle x*y=y*x\quad \forall x,y\in S.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f0b4ac067a9814abdfa618c913586a9ee9f56c7e)

In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), a [binary operation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_operation "Binary operation") is **commutative** if changing the order of the [operands](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Operand "Operand") does not change the result. It is a fundamental property of many binary operations, and many [mathematical proofs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematical_proof "Mathematical proof") depend on it. Perhaps most familiar as a property of arithmetic, e.g. "3 + 4 = 4 + 3" or "2 × 5 = 5 × 2", the property can also be used in more advanced settings. The name is needed because there are operations, such as [division](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Division_(mathematics) "Division (mathematics)") and [subtraction](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Subtraction "Subtraction"), that do not have it (for example, "3 − 5 ≠ 5 − 3"); such operations are _not_ commutative, and so are referred to as **noncommutative operations**.

The idea that simple operations, such as the [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication_(mathematics) "Multiplication (mathematics)") and [addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition "Addition") of numbers, are commutative was for many centuries implicitly assumed. Thus, this property was not named until the 19th century, when new [algebraic structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_structure "Algebraic structure") started to be studied.[[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative#cite_note-FOOTNOTERice2011[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidYruifIx88AQCpgPA4_4]-1)

A [binary operation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Binary_operation "Binary operation")![Image 4: {\displaystyle *}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e9972f426d9e07855984f73ee195a21dbc21755) on a [set](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)")_S_ is _commutative_ if ![Image 5: {\displaystyle x*y=y*x}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b8b24dba3d0ce7a781c3b05150ff4296209c3afd) for all ![Image 6: {\displaystyle x,y\in S}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1b918edf804af5d8be1bfe7c904398f80d2c2152).[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaracino2008[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidGW4fAAAAQBAJpgPA11_11]-2) An operation that is not commutative is said to be _noncommutative_.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHall1966[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidqqs8AAAAIAAJpgPA262_262%E2%80%93263]-3)

One says that x _commutes_ with _y_ or that x and y _commute_ under ![Image 7: {\displaystyle *}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8e9972f426d9e07855984f73ee195a21dbc21755) if[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative#cite_note-FOOTNOTELovett2022[httpbooksgooglecombooksidvp0IEQAAQBAJpgPA12_12]-4)![Image 8: {\displaystyle x*y=y*x.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/848e86c4b6f3f24d6cce3a5fb50665c08466cce6)

So, an operation is commutative if every two elements commute.[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative#cite_note-FOOTNOTELovett2022[httpbooksgooglecombooksidvp0IEQAAQBAJpgPA12_12]-4) An operation is noncommutative if there are two elements such that ![Image 9: {\displaystyle x*y\neq y*x.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b506fe18508461597bc8f5e5f0fcb9ed59a3ba17) This does not exclude the possibility that some pairs of elements commute.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEHall1966[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidqqs8AAAAIAAJpgPA262_262%E2%80%93263]-3)

[![Image 10](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/36/Commutative_Addition.svg/250px-Commutative_Addition.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Commutative_Addition.svg)

The cumulation of apples, which can be seen as an addition of natural numbers, is commutative.

### Commutative operations

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Commutative_property&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Commutative operations")]

[![Image 11](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3d/Vector_Addition.svg/250px-Vector_Addition.svg.png)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Vector_Addition.svg)

The addition of vectors is commutative, because ![Image 12: {\displaystyle {\vec {a}}+{\vec {b}}={\vec {b}}+{\vec {a}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/dcaa01e9959d82da83b5b2ddea6cd9f33e64878d)

*   [Addition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Addition "Addition") and [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication "Multiplication") are commutative in most [number systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Number_system "Number system"), and, in particular, between [natural numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_number "Natural number"), [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer"), [rational numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational_number "Rational number"), [real numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Real_number "Real number") and [complex numbers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_number "Complex number"). This is also true in every [field](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Field_(mathematics) "Field (mathematics)").[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative#cite_note-FOOTNOTERosen2013See_the_[httpsbooksgooglecombooksid-oVvEAAAQBAJpgSL1-PA1_Appendix_I]-5)
*   Addition is commutative in every [vector space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vector_space "Vector space") and in every [algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra_over_a_field "Algebra over a field").[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative#cite_note-FOOTNOTESterling2009[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidPsNJ1alC-bsCpgPA248_248]-6)
*   [Union](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Union_(set_theory) "Union (set theory)") and [intersection](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Intersection_(set_theory) "Intersection (set theory)") are commutative operations on [sets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Set_(mathematics) "Set (mathematics)").[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEJohnson2003[httpbooksgooglecombooksid-pZX2KS2KqMCpgPA642_642]-7)
*   "[And](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/And_(logic) "And (logic)")" and "[or](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Or_(logic) "Or (logic)")" are commutative [logical operations](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Logical_operation "Logical operation").[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEO'Regan2008[httpsbooksgooglecombooksid081H96F1enMCpgPA33_33]-8)

Commutative structures
----------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Commutative_property&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Commutative structures")]

Some types of [algebraic structures](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebraic_structure "Algebraic structure") involve an operation that does not require commutativity. If this operation is commutative for a specific structure, the structure is often said to be _commutative_. So,

*   a [commutative semigroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_semigroup "Commutative semigroup") is a [semigroup](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Semigroup "Semigroup") whose operation is commutative;[[14]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrillet20011%E2%80%932-14)
*   a [commutative monoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_monoid "Commutative monoid") is a [monoid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monoid "Monoid") whose operation is commutative;[[15]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGrillet20013-15)
*   a _commutative group_ or [abelian group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Abelian_group "Abelian group") is a [group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Group_(mathematics) "Group (mathematics)") whose operation is commutative;[[16]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGallian200634-16)
*   a [commutative ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_ring "Commutative ring") is a [ring](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ring_(mathematics) "Ring (mathematics)") whose [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication "Multiplication") is commutative. (Addition in a ring is always commutative.)[[17]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGallian2006236-17)

However, in the case of [algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Algebra_over_a_field "Algebra over a field"), the phrase "[commutative algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative_algebra_(structure) "Commutative algebra (structure)")" refers only to [associative algebras](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Associative_algebra "Associative algebra") that have a commutative multiplication.[[18]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative#cite_note-FOOTNOTETuset2025[httpsbooksgooglecombooksid1RE1EQAAQBAJpgPA99_99]-18)

History and etymology
---------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Commutative_property&action=edit&section=6 "Edit section: History and etymology")]

Records of the implicit use of the commutative property go back to ancient times. The [Egyptians](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egypt "Egypt") used the commutative property of [multiplication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Multiplication "Multiplication") to simplify computing [products](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Product_(mathematics) "Product (mathematics)").[[19]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEGayShute1987[httpsarchiveorgdetailsrhindmathematica0000robi_h8l4page16_16&dash;17]-19)[Euclid](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid "Euclid") is known to have assumed the commutative property of multiplication in his book [_Elements_](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclid%27s_Elements "Euclid's Elements").[[20]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative#cite_note-20) Formal uses of the commutative property arose in the late 18th and early 19th centuries when mathematicians began to work on a theory of functions. Nowadays, the commutative property is a well-known and basic property used in most branches of mathematics.[[2]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaracino2008[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidGW4fAAAAQBAJpgPA11_11]-2)

[![Image 13](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/da/Commutative_Word_Origin.PNG/250px-Commutative_Word_Origin.PNG)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Commutative_Word_Origin.PNG)

The first known use of the term was in a French Journal published in 1814

The first recorded use of the term _commutative_ was in a memoir by [François Servois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois-Joseph_Servois "François-Joseph Servois") in 1814, which used the word _commutatives_ when describing functions that have what is now called the commutative property.[[21]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAllaireBradley2002-21)_Commutative_ is the feminine form of the French adjective _commutatif_, which is derived from the French noun _commutation_ and the French verb _commuter_, meaning "to exchange" or "to switch", a cognate of _to commute_. The term then appeared in English in 1838. in [Duncan Gregory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Duncan_Gregory "Duncan Gregory")'s article entitled "On the real nature of symbolical algebra" published in 1840 in the [Transactions of the Royal Society of Edinburgh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Edinburgh "Royal Society of Edinburgh").[[22]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Commutative#cite_note-FOOTNOTERice2011[httpsbooksgooglecombooksidYruifIx88AQCpgPA4_4]Gregory1840-22)
