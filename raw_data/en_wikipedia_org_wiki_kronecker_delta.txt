In [mathematics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics "Mathematics"), the **Kronecker delta** (named after [Leopold Kronecker](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leopold_Kronecker "Leopold Kronecker")) is a [function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_(mathematics) "Function (mathematics)") of two [variables](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Variable_(mathematics) "Variable (mathematics)"), usually just non-negative [integers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Integer "Integer"). The function is 1 if the variables are equal, and 0 otherwise: ![Image 1: {\displaystyle \delta _{ij}={\begin{cases}0&{\text{if }}i\neq j,\\1&{\text{if }}i=j.\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/904297d67d13919327bab9243517aef32e96c550) or with use of [Iverson brackets](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iverson_bracket "Iverson bracket"): ![Image 2: {\displaystyle \delta _{ij}=[i=j]\,}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1419db152cf3420e549896141fbf42846adb3141) For example, ![Image 3: {\displaystyle \delta _{12}=0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed97c1190d8b39cec23b78dee88c97d8bf8dd21e) because ![Image 4: {\displaystyle 1\neq 2}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3658c313d1dd843bfbed053e2a99797db9c5d90c), whereas ![Image 5: {\displaystyle \delta _{33}=1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b5789cd478c64550600e22bca1a50b6fce6851a9) because ![Image 6: {\displaystyle 3=3}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/daa33142e4b6e144d2cd0b63f09ba12a92611c2f).

The Kronecker delta appears naturally in many areas of mathematics, physics, engineering and computer science, as a means of compactly expressing its definition above. Generalized versions of the Kronecker delta have found applications in differential geometry and modern tensor calculus, particularly in formulations of gauge theory and topological field models. [[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta#cite_note-1)

{\displaystyle \mathbf {I} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8a458c8aeb096ce732abf346ae8edf3e4f53a126) has entries equal to the Kronecker delta: ![Image 9: {\displaystyle I_{ij}=\delta _{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/93fee24ad3e992d7186b9db8dd39aac2b42001f8) where ![Image 10: {\displaystyle ,n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/084268c7751cf630173a19eb80a24873f4eb9c9c), and the [inner product](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner_product "Inner product") of [vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") can be written as ![Image 13: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} \cdot \mathbf {b} =\sum _{i,j=1}^{n}a_{i}\delta _{ij}b_{j}=\sum _{i=1}^{n}a_{i}b_{i}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6fe44103b66c8d161b80e128783b53676cca8add) Here the [Euclidean vectors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Euclidean_vector "Euclidean vector") are defined as n-tuples: ![Image 14: {\displaystyle \mathbf {a} =(a_{1},a_{2},\dots ,a_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/003ebf2334646f14c8eee0c6ade2944917768ac9) and ![Image 15: {\displaystyle \mathbf {b} =(b_{1},b_{2},...,b_{n})}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1ad5a7c3b2d110dde94be82d069bdaf06ba3c730) and the last step is obtained by using the values of the Kronecker delta to reduce the summation over ![Image 16: {\displaystyle j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f461e54f5c093e92a55547b9764291390f0b5d0).

It is common for i and j to be restricted to a set of the form {1, 2, ..., _n_} or {0, 1, ..., _n_ − 1}, but the Kronecker delta can be defined on an arbitrary set.

The following equations are satisfied: ![Image 17: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\sum _{j}\delta _{ij}a_{j}&=a_{i},\\\sum _{i}a_{i}\delta _{ij}&=a_{j},\\\sum _{k}\delta _{ik}\delta _{kj}&=\delta _{ij}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3b47ddd4e688377099b7b9debfeebe4054463ee) Therefore, the matrix **δ** can be considered as an identity matrix.

Another useful representation is the following form: ![Image 18: {\displaystyle \delta _{nm}=\lim _{N\to \infty }{\frac {1}{N}}\sum _{k=1}^{N}e^{2\pi i{\frac {k}{N}}(n-m)}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e0aff2cf369e241b63ceccab34ba3c7b18bbe25f) This can be derived using the formula for the [geometric series](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Geometric_series "Geometric series").

Digital signal processing
-------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kronecker_delta&action=edit&section=3 "Edit section: Digital signal processing")]

[![Image 24](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/8/88/Unit_impulse.gif)](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/File:Unit_impulse.gif)

Unit sample function

In the study of [digital signal processing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_signal_processing "Digital signal processing") (DSP), the Kronecker delta function sometimes means the unit sample function ![Image 25: {\displaystyle \delta [n]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f2a6caf535cb44fa3526b2f320330a805edfdfaa) , which represents a special case of the 2-dimensional Kronecker delta function ![Image 26: {\displaystyle \delta _{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa75d04c11480d976e1396951e02cbb3c4f71568) where the Kronecker indices include the number zero, and where one of the indices is zero: ![Image 27: {\displaystyle \delta [n]\equiv \delta _{n0}\equiv \delta _{0n}~~~{\text{where}}-\infty <n<\infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c28c4e2e66c8fdf2b58e0d331da4abb7b6561bdc)

Or more generally where: ![Image 28: {\displaystyle \delta [n-k]\equiv \delta [k-n]\equiv \delta _{nk}\equiv \delta _{kn}{\text{where}}-\infty <n<\infty ,-\infty <k<\infty }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5329246b55c0de893ba827476b2e1d48560a902a)

For discrete-time signals, it is conventional to place a single integer index in square braces; in contrast the Kronecker delta, ![Image 29: {\displaystyle \delta _{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa75d04c11480d976e1396951e02cbb3c4f71568), can have any number of indexes. In [LTI system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LTI_system "LTI system") theory, the discrete unit sample function is typically used as an input to a discrete-time system for determining the [impulse response](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Impulse_response "Impulse response") function of the system which characterizes the system for any general imput. In contrast, the typical purpose of the Kronecker delta function is for filtering terms from an [Einstein summation convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_summation_convention "Einstein summation convention").

The discrete unit sample function is more simply defined as: ![Image 30: {\displaystyle \delta [n]={\begin{cases}1&n=0\\0&n{\text{ is another integer}}\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/5467b63f6d785f214de98094c013e07e62f18abd)

In comparison, in [continuous-time systems](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_time_and_continuous_time "Discrete time and continuous time") the [Dirac delta function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_delta_function "Dirac delta function") is often confused for both the Kronecker delta function and the unit sample function. The Dirac delta is defined as: ![Image 31: {\displaystyle {\begin{cases}\int _{-\varepsilon }^{+\varepsilon }\delta (t)dt=1&\forall \varepsilon >0\\\delta (t)=0&\forall t\neq 0\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/218498fc1ab83f0858e2c255ee70b003e83a384c)

Unlike the Kronecker delta function ![Image 32: {\displaystyle \delta _{ij}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa75d04c11480d976e1396951e02cbb3c4f71568) and the unit sample function ![Image 33: {\displaystyle \delta [n]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f2a6caf535cb44fa3526b2f320330a805edfdfaa), the Dirac delta function ![Image 34: {\displaystyle \delta (t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c050147a97868286252447ef73515c8108edd398) does not have an integer index, it has a single continuous non-integer value t.

In continuous-time systems, the term "[unit impulse function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_impulse_function "Unit impulse function")" is used to refer to the [Dirac delta function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_delta_function "Dirac delta function")![Image 35: {\displaystyle \delta (t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c050147a97868286252447ef73515c8108edd398) or, in discrete-time systems, the Kronecker delta function ![Image 36: {\displaystyle \delta [n]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f2a6caf535cb44fa3526b2f320330a805edfdfaa).

The Kronecker delta has the so-called _sifting_ property that for ![Image 37: {\displaystyle j\in \mathbb {Z} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0db65729fda638316e85c4af930f94cac08ec9bb): ![Image 38: {\displaystyle \sum _{i=-\infty }^{\infty }a_{i}\delta _{ij}=a_{j}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c20a5426e752fdfd5ba593705eda5bdb47216938) and if the integers are viewed as a [measure space](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Measure_space "Measure space"), endowed with the [counting measure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Counting_measure "Counting measure"), then this property coincides with the defining property of the [Dirac delta function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_delta_function "Dirac delta function")![Image 39: {\displaystyle \int _{-\infty }^{\infty }\delta (x-y)f(x)\,dx=f(y),}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/383bd7f84c9715722bc302957acecc40f6c426f5) and in fact Dirac's delta was named after the Kronecker delta because of this analogous property.[[3]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta#cite_note-3) In signal processing it is usually the context (discrete or continuous time) that distinguishes the Kronecker and Dirac "functions". And by convention, ![Image 40: {\displaystyle \delta (t)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c050147a97868286252447ef73515c8108edd398) generally indicates continuous time (Dirac), whereas arguments like ![Image 41: {\displaystyle i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/add78d8608ad86e54951b8c8bd6c8d8416533d20), ![Image 42: {\displaystyle j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f461e54f5c093e92a55547b9764291390f0b5d0), ![Image 43: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40), ![Image 44: {\displaystyle l}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/829091f745070b9eb97a80244129025440a1cfac), ![Image 45: {\displaystyle m}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0a07d98bb302f3856cbabc47b2b9016692e3f7bc), and ![Image 46: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) are usually reserved for discrete time (Kronecker). Another common practice is to represent discrete sequences with square brackets; thus: ![Image 47: {\displaystyle \delta [n]}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f2a6caf535cb44fa3526b2f320330a805edfdfaa). The Kronecker delta is not the result of directly sampling the Dirac delta function.

The Kronecker delta forms the multiplicative [identity element](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Identity_element "Identity element") of an [incidence algebra](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Incidence_algebra "Incidence algebra").[[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta#cite_note-4)

Relationship to the Dirac delta function
----------------------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kronecker_delta&action=edit&section=5 "Edit section: Relationship to the Dirac delta function")]

In [probability theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_theory "Probability theory") and [statistics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Statistics "Statistics"), the Kronecker delta and [Dirac delta function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_delta_function "Dirac delta function") can both be used to represent a [discrete distribution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Discrete_distribution "Discrete distribution"). If the [support](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Support_(mathematics) "Support (mathematics)") of a distribution consists of points ![Image 48: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} =\{x_{1},\cdots ,x_{n}\}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/27126bd0be8471ec31e576adb58022b4394290c5), with corresponding probabilities ![Image 49: {\displaystyle p_{1},\cdots ,p_{n}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/3711f3a8dc0f30eca8b99ff7fb660d911007ab7a), then the [probability mass function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_mass_function "Probability mass function")![Image 50: {\displaystyle p(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8cb7afced134ef75572e5314a5d278c2d644f438) of the distribution over ![Image 51: {\displaystyle \mathbf {x} }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/32adf004df5eb0a8c7fd8c0b6b7405183c5a5ef2) can be written, using the Kronecker delta, as ![Image 52: {\displaystyle p(x)=\sum _{i=1}^{n}p_{i}\delta _{xx_{i}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/25349d5ec458a9e29bc079281df9226e55284e1b)

Equivalently, the [probability density function](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_density_function "Probability density function")![Image 53: {\displaystyle f(x)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/202945cce41ecebb6f643f31d119c514bec7a074) of the distribution can be written using the Dirac delta function as ![Image 54: {\displaystyle f(x)=\sum _{i=1}^{n}p_{i}\delta (x-x_{i}).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/df030dbfcfdbc915326e856b29fc21edd2ca9a94)

Under certain conditions, the Kronecker delta can arise from sampling a Dirac delta function. For example, if a Dirac delta impulse occurs exactly at a sampling point and is ideally lowpass-filtered (with cutoff at the critical frequency) per the [Nyquist–Shannon sampling theorem](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nyquist%E2%80%93Shannon_sampling_theorem "Nyquist–Shannon sampling theorem"), the resulting discrete-time signal will be a Kronecker delta function.

If it is considered as a type ![Image 55: {\displaystyle (1,1)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2c2a42feb07f4139bf871ae6856b11d4567bea23)[tensor](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tensor "Tensor"), the Kronecker tensor can be written ![Image 56: {\displaystyle \delta _{j}^{i}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c2c2996dd3d4c7152c99cf5491fd3de9f01b4f09) with a [covariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariance_and_contravariance_of_vectors "Covariance and contravariance of vectors") index ![Image 57: {\displaystyle j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f461e54f5c093e92a55547b9764291390f0b5d0) and [contravariant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covariance_and_contravariance_of_vectors "Covariance and contravariance of vectors") index ![Image 58: {\displaystyle i}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/add78d8608ad86e54951b8c8bd6c8d8416533d20): ![Image 59: {\displaystyle \delta _{j}^{i}={\begin{cases}0&(i\neq j),\\1&(i=j).\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/20ccd646b8a9d7b6b473c25eeefe7c37f0f5768b)

This tensor represents:

The **generalized Kronecker delta** or **multi-index Kronecker delta** of order ![Image 60: {\displaystyle 2p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ff0d3e38652941fc7a2844611f6c7fa8a23104a7) is a type ![Image 61: {\displaystyle (p,p)}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9cf14627a018c2fadb33c3892d496499d5f10aef) tensor that is completely [antisymmetric](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antisymmetric_tensor "Antisymmetric tensor") in its ![Image 62: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) upper indices, and also in its ![Image 63: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36) lower indices.

Two definitions that differ by a factor of ![Image 64: {\displaystyle p!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa1841c79d55f18352b0cbc9e1248be496ddb5b0) are in use. Below, the version is presented has nonzero components scaled to be ![Image 65: {\displaystyle \pm 1}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/0bfeaa85da53ad1947d8000926cfea33827ef1e0). The second version has nonzero components that are ![Image 66: {\displaystyle \pm 1/p!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b0bb327c488bff9516a13bcf777b3d8220cabd09), with consequent changes scaling factors in formulae, such as the scaling factors of ![Image 67: {\displaystyle 1/p!}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/4dd4bb43083622a0ebd95ae326083ab986476d00) in _[§Properties of the generalized Kronecker delta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker\_delta#Properties\_of\_the\_generalized\_Kronecker\_delta)_ below disappearing.[[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta#cite_note-5)

### Definitions of the generalized Kronecker delta

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kronecker_delta&action=edit&section=7 "Edit section: Definitions of the generalized Kronecker delta")]

In terms of the indices, the generalized Kronecker delta is defined as:[[6]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta#cite_note-6)[[7]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta#cite_note-7)![Image 68: {\displaystyle \delta _{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{p}}^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p}}={\begin{cases}{\phantom {-}}1&\quad {\text{if }}\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{p}{\text{ are distinct integers and are an even permutation of }}\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p}\\-1&\quad {\text{if }}\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{p}{\text{ are distinct integers and are an odd permutation of }}\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p}\\{\phantom {-}}0&\quad {\text{in all other cases}}.\end{cases}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fb0369f4fc873dd1700f9d7ecb5e776f637e8a99)

Let ![Image 69: {\displaystyle \mathrm {S} _{p}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ab14a78fe85361f76740e596753f7d14dc9abe15) be the [symmetric group](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Symmetric_group "Symmetric group") of degree ![Image 70: {\displaystyle p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/81eac1e205430d1f40810df36a0edffdc367af36), then: ![Image 71: {\displaystyle \delta _{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{p}}^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p}}=\sum _{\sigma \in \mathrm {S} _{p}}\operatorname {sgn}(\sigma )\,\delta _{\nu _{\sigma (1)}}^{\mu _{1}}\cdots \delta _{\nu _{\sigma (p)}}^{\mu _{p}}=\sum _{\sigma \in \mathrm {S} _{p}}\operatorname {sgn}(\sigma )\,\delta _{\nu _{1}}^{\mu _{\sigma (1)}}\cdots \delta _{\nu _{p}}^{\mu _{\sigma (p)}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/1e07d616063b80f5b068f850ef24fd192758d622)

Using [anti-symmetrization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antisymmetric_tensor#Notation "Antisymmetric tensor"): ![Image 72: {\displaystyle \delta _{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{p}}^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p}}=p!\delta _{[\nu _{1}}^{\mu _{1}}\dots \delta _{\nu _{p}]}^{\mu _{p}}=p!\delta _{\nu _{1}}^{[\mu _{1}}\dots \delta _{\nu _{p}}^{\mu _{p}]}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/710a3273176c3744ca733a604c2bafd0693fa380)

In terms of a ![Image 73: {\displaystyle p\times p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/67e6de741a3aa8176f5a487de8e8f602aa75c5e4)[determinant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant "Determinant"):[[8]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta#cite_note-8)![Image 74: {\displaystyle \delta _{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{p}}^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p}}={\begin{vmatrix}\delta _{\nu _{1}}^{\mu _{1}}&\cdots &\delta _{\nu _{p}}^{\mu _{1}}\\\vdots &\ddots &\vdots \\\delta _{\nu _{1}}^{\mu _{p}}&\cdots &\delta _{\nu _{p}}^{\mu _{p}}\end{vmatrix}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/059231786f82986288d9ab2ac1454cb7ebc4ed70)

Using the [Laplace expansion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Laplace_expansion "Laplace expansion") ([Laplace's formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Determinant#Laplace's_expansion_and_the_adjugate_matrix "Determinant")) of determinant, it may be defined [recursively](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Recursion "Recursion"):[[9]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta#cite_note-9)![Image 75: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}\delta _{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{p}}^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p}}&=\sum _{k=1}^{p}(-1)^{p+k}\delta _{\nu _{k}}^{\mu _{p}}\delta _{\nu _{1}\dots {\check {\nu }}_{k}\dots \nu _{p}}^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{k}\dots {\check {\mu }}_{p}}\\&=\delta _{\nu _{p}}^{\mu _{p}}\delta _{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{p-1}}^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p-1}}-\sum _{k=1}^{p-1}\delta _{\nu _{k}}^{\mu _{p}}\delta _{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{k-1}\,\nu _{p}\,\nu _{k+1}\dots \nu _{p-1}}^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{k-1}\,\mu _{k}\,\mu _{k+1}\dots \mu _{p-1}},\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/392b766c1589137c69df457c335ee44bfa890455) where the caron, ![Image 76: {\displaystyle {\check {}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f08407a335c55f00e818659141077bd781d2c66e), indicates an index that is omitted from the sequence.

When ![Image 77: {\displaystyle p=n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/95b1f8b7d3b5ff50fe52c091d067e7980dc1f94c) (the dimension of the vector space), in terms of the [Levi-Civita symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol "Levi-Civita symbol"): ![Image 78: {\displaystyle \delta _{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{n}}^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{n}}=\varepsilon ^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{n}}\varepsilon _{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{n}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f18a58ffee9d37a47e3d1f693e73ee747ac4f0c7) More generally, for ![Image 79: {\displaystyle m=n-p}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/146d19d6e1db5532a314f930bd969e11a85fbb38), using the [Einstein summation convention](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Einstein_summation_convention "Einstein summation convention"): ![Image 80: {\displaystyle \delta _{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{p}}^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p}}={\tfrac {1}{m!}}\varepsilon ^{\kappa _{1}\dots \kappa _{m}\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p}}\varepsilon _{\kappa _{1}\dots \kappa _{m}\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{p}}\,.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a3cc8e86936d10bd53d1e8c7456b2045fc617622)

### Contractions of the generalized Kronecker delta

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kronecker_delta&action=edit&section=8 "Edit section: Contractions of the generalized Kronecker delta")]

Kronecker Delta contractions depend on the dimension of the space. For example, ![Image 81: {\displaystyle \delta _{\mu _{1}}^{\nu _{1}}\delta _{\nu _{1}\nu _{2}}^{\mu _{1}\mu _{2}}=(d-1)\delta _{\nu _{2}}^{\mu _{2}},}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/08c1a1e01bfccc0ad4c7b4779f03cfe46c777946) where d is the dimension of the space. From this relation the full contracted delta is obtained as ![Image 82: {\displaystyle \delta _{\mu _{1}\mu _{2}}^{\nu _{1}\nu _{2}}\delta _{\nu _{1}\nu _{2}}^{\mu _{1}\mu _{2}}=2d(d-1).}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/8d995f5d68e08ee1071fe38a96b506cfcda9f6ec) The generalization of the preceding formulas is[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]![Image 83: {\displaystyle \delta _{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{n}}^{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{n}}\delta _{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{p}}^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p}}=n!{\frac {(d-p+n)!}{(d-p)!}}\delta _{\nu _{n+1}\dots \nu _{p}}^{\mu _{n+1}\dots \mu _{p}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/fa2dac8d7efcaeee7608ad50ed10f7109c3c7271)

### Properties of the generalized Kronecker delta

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kronecker_delta&action=edit&section=9 "Edit section: Properties of the generalized Kronecker delta")]

The generalized Kronecker delta may be used for [anti-symmetrization](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antisymmetric_tensor#Notation "Antisymmetric tensor"): ![Image 84: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {1}{p!}}\delta _{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{p}}^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p}}a^{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{p}}&=a^{[\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p}]},\\{\frac {1}{p!}}\delta _{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{p}}^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p}}a_{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p}}&=a_{[\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{p}]}.\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/e1ccc981b481f326109b888e413ea2a63fd290df)

From the above equations and the properties of [anti-symmetric tensors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anti-symmetric_tensor "Anti-symmetric tensor"), we can derive the properties of the generalized Kronecker delta: ![Image 85: {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}{\frac {1}{p!}}\delta _{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{p}}^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p}}a^{[\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{p}]}&=a^{[\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p}]},\\{\frac {1}{p!}}\delta _{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{p}}^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p}}a_{[\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p}]}&=a_{[\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{p}]},\\{\frac {1}{p!}}\delta _{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{p}}^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p}}\delta _{\kappa _{1}\dots \kappa _{p}}^{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{p}}&=\delta _{\kappa _{1}\dots \kappa _{p}}^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p}},\end{aligned}}}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/6c07422f76690597f31d078eee300632fedc8f66) which are the generalized version of formulae written in _[§Properties](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker\_delta#Properties)_. The last formula is equivalent to the [Cauchy–Binet formula](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy%E2%80%93Binet_formula "Cauchy–Binet formula").

Reducing the order via summation of the indices may be expressed by the identity[[10]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta#cite_note-10)![Image 86: {\displaystyle \delta _{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{s}\,\mu _{s+1}\dots \mu _{p}}^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{s}\,\mu _{s+1}\dots \mu _{p}}={\frac {(n-s)!}{(n-p)!}}\delta _{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{s}}^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{s}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9a22e7984ad21c9027f26001f2d5b02955487e54)

Using both the summation rule for the case ![Image 87: {\displaystyle p=n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/95b1f8b7d3b5ff50fe52c091d067e7980dc1f94c) and the relation with the Levi-Civita symbol, [the summation rule of the Levi-Civita symbol](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levi-Civita_symbol#n_dimensions "Levi-Civita symbol") is derived: ![Image 88: {\displaystyle \delta _{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{p}}^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p}}={\frac {1}{(n-p)!}}\varepsilon ^{\mu _{1}\dots \mu _{p}\,\kappa _{p+1}\dots \kappa _{n}}\varepsilon _{\nu _{1}\dots \nu _{p}\,\kappa _{p+1}\dots \kappa _{n}}.}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/404ada17c11edfab3da86795dc858ebc35025b0c) The 4D version of the last relation appears in Penrose's [spinor approach to general relativity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mathematics_of_general_relativity#Spinor_formalism "Mathematics of general relativity")[[11]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta#cite_note-11) that he later generalized, while he was developing Aitken's diagrams,[[12]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta#cite_note-12) to become part of the technique of [Penrose graphical notation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Penrose_graphical_notation "Penrose graphical notation").[[13]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kronecker_delta#cite_note-13) Also, this relation is extensively used in [S-duality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/S-duality "S-duality") theories, especially when written in the language of [differential forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Differential_form "Differential form") and [Hodge duals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hodge_star_operator#Duality "Hodge star operator").

Integral representations
------------------------

[[edit](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Kronecker_delta&action=edit&section=10 "Edit section: Integral representations")]

For any integers ![Image 89: {\displaystyle j}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/2f461e54f5c093e92a55547b9764291390f0b5d0) and ![Image 90: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40), the Kronecker delta can be written as a complex [contour integral](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Contour_integral "Contour integral") using a standard [residue](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Residue_(complex_analysis) "Residue (complex analysis)") calculation. The integral is taken over the [unit circle](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unit_circle "Unit circle") in the [complex plane](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Complex_plane "Complex plane"), oriented counterclockwise. An equivalent representation of the integral arises by parameterizing the contour by an angle around the origin. ![Image 91: {\displaystyle \delta _{jk}={\frac {1}{2\pi i}}\oint _{|z|=1}z^{j-k-1}\,dz={\frac {1}{2\pi }}\int _{0}^{2\pi }e^{i(j-k)\varphi }\,d\varphi }](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/ed1f87e0d7085a4cdf3c79c2c24e050797ec4057)

The Kronecker comb function with period ![Image 92: {\displaystyle N}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/f5e3890c981ae85503089652feb48b191b57aae3) is defined (using [DSP](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Digital_signal_processing "Digital signal processing") notation) as:[_[citation needed](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation\_needed "Wikipedia:Citation needed")_]![Image 93: {\displaystyle \Delta _{N}[n]=\sum _{k=-\infty }^{\infty }\delta [n-kN],}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/b56c378c8b3f5b7dc81c97ec44c41b57dabb3d8d) where ![Image 94: {\displaystyle N\neq 0}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/9176ba0573deaebe5dbe9a80523f9bc99af9f61e), ![Image 95: {\displaystyle k}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/c3c9a2c7b599b37105512c5d570edc034056dd40) and ![Image 96: {\displaystyle n}](https://wikimedia.org/api/rest_v1/media/math/render/svg/a601995d55609f2d9f5e233e36fbe9ea26011b3b) are integers. The Kronecker comb thus consists of an infinite series of unit impulses that are N units apart, aligned so one of the impulses occurs at zero. It may be considered to be the discrete analog of the [Dirac comb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dirac_comb "Dirac comb").
