Sir Isaac Newton was an English polymath active as a mathematician, physicist, astronomer, alchemist, theologian, and author.  
Newton was a key figure in the Scientific Revolution and the Enlightenment that followed.  
His book Philosophiæ Naturalis Principia Mathematica, first published in 1687, achieved the first great unification in physics and established classical mechanics.  
Newton also made seminal contributions to optics and shares credit with German mathematician Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz for formulating infinitesimal calculus.  
He contributed to and refined the scientific method, and his work is considered the most influential in bringing forth modern science.  
In the Principia, Newton formulated the laws of motion and universal gravitation that formed the dominant scientific viewpoint for centuries.  
He used his mathematical description of gravity to derive Kepler's laws of planetary motion and account for tides, the trajectories of comets, and other phenomena.  
Newton solved the two-body problem and introduced the three-body problem.  
He demonstrated that the motion of objects on Earth and celestial bodies could be accounted for by the same principles.  
Newton's inference that the Earth is an oblate spheroid was later confirmed by geodetic measurements.  
He built the first reflecting telescope and developed a sophisticated theory of colour based on the observation that a prism separates white light into the colours of the visible spectrum.  
His work on light was collected in his book Opticks, published in 1704.  
He originated prisms as beam expanders and multiple-prism arrays, which would later become integral to the development of tunable lasers.  
Newton also anticipated wave-particle duality and was the first to theorize the Goos–Hänchen effect.  
He formulated an empirical law of cooling, which was the first heat transfer formulation and serves as the formal basis of convective heat transfer.  
Newton made the first theoretical calculation of the speed of sound and introduced the notions of a Newtonian fluid and a black body.  
He was also the first to explain the Magnus effect.  
Newton was a fellow of Trinity College and the second Lucasian Professor of Mathematics at the University of Cambridge.  
He was a devout but unorthodox Christian who privately rejected the doctrine of the Trinity.  
Newton served two brief terms as Member of Parliament for the University of Cambridge.  
He was knighted by Queen Anne in 1705 and spent the last three decades of his life in London.  
Newton served as Warden and Master of the Royal Mint and as president of the Royal Society.  
Isaac Newton was born on Christmas Day, 25 December 1642 at Woolsthorpe Manor in Lincolnshire.  
Newton was educated at The King's School in Grantham, which taught Latin and Ancient Greek.  
He was admitted to Trinity College at the University of Cambridge in June 1661.  
At Cambridge, Newton started as a subsizar, paying his way by performing valet duties until he was awarded a scholarship in 1664.  
In 1665, he discovered the generalised binomial theorem and began to develop calculus.  
Newton was elected as a fellow of Trinity in October 1667.  
He was appointed Lucasian Professor of Mathematics at Cambridge at the age of 26.  
Newton was elected a Fellow of the Royal Society in 1672.  
In 1672 and again in 1681, Newton published revised editions of the Geographia Generalis.  
Newton's work on calculus began in 1664 and by 20 May 1665 he had already developed the calculus to compute tangents and curvature.  
Newton later became involved in a dispute with Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz over priority in the development of calculus.  
He generalized the binomial theorem to any real number and introduced the Puiseux series.  
Newton was the first to use fractional indices and to employ coordinate geometry to derive solutions to Diophantine equations.  
He approximated partial sums of the harmonic series by logarithms and was the first to use power series with confidence.  
Newton originated the Newton-Cotes formulas for numerical integration and initiated the field of calculus of variations.  
In 1666, Newton observed that the spectrum of colours exiting a prism is oblong, leading him to conclude that colour is intrinsic to light.  
From 1670 to 1672, Newton lectured on optics and investigated the refraction of light.  
He showed that the multicoloured image produced by a prism could be recomposed into white light by a lens and a second prism.  
Newton wrote down the first of the two normal equations known from ordinary least squares and devised an early form of regression analysis.  
He showed that coloured light does not change its properties by separating out a coloured beam and shining it on various objects.  
Newton constructed the first known functional reflecting telescope, today known as a Newtonian telescope.  
In late 1668, he was able to produce his first reflecting telescope, about eight inches long.  
Newton argued that light is composed of particles or corpuscles, which were refracted by accelerating into a denser medium.  
In his Hypothesis of Light of 1675, Newton posited the existence of the ether to transmit forces between particles.  
In 1704, Newton published Opticks, in which he expounded his corpuscular theory of light.  
Newton investigated electricity by constructing a primitive form of a frictional electrostatic generator using a glass globe.  
Newton had been developing his theory of gravitation as far back as 1665.  
In 1679, he returned to his work on celestial mechanics by considering gravitation and its effect on planetary orbits.  
After exchanges with Hooke, Newton worked out a proof that elliptical planetary orbits result from a centripetal force.  
The Principia was published on 5 July 1687 with encouragement and financial help from Edmond Halley.  
In this work, Newton stated the three universal laws of motion that describe the relationship between objects, forces, and motion.  
Newton used the Latin word gravitas for the effect that would become known as gravity and defined the law of universal gravitation.  
His work achieved the first great unification in physics and initiated the gravitational study of irregularities in the Moon's motion.  
Newton provided a theory for determining the orbits of comets and much more in the Principia.  
Newton made clear his heliocentric view of the Solar System, recognizing the deviation of the Sun from the center of gravity.  
Newton was criticised for introducing occult agencies into science because of his postulate of an invisible gravitational force.  
With the Principia, Newton became internationally recognised and acquired a circle of admirers.  
Newton studied heat and energy flow, formulating an empirical law of cooling in 1701.  
Newton introduced the notion of a Newtonian fluid with his formulation of the law of viscosity in Principia.  
He also discussed the circular motion of fluids and was the first to discuss Couette flow.  
Newton was the first to observe and qualitatively describe what would later be formalized as the Magnus effect.  
Newton rejected hypotheses in science, emphasizing explanations grounded strictly in observable data.  
Newton outlined four rules for the scientific method in his 1687 Principia.  
In the 1690s, Newton wrote religious tracts dealing with the literal and symbolic interpretation of the Bible.  
Newton was also a member of the Parliament of England for Cambridge University in 1689 and 1701.  
Newton moved to London in 1696 to take up the post of Warden of the Royal Mint.  
He took charge of England's great recoining and secured the job of deputy comptroller for Edmond Halley.  
Newton became Master of the Mint in 1699, a position he held for the last 30 years of his life.  
He estimated that 20 percent of coins taken in during the Great Recoinage of 1696 were counterfeit.  
Newton successfully prosecuted 28 coiners and improved minting technology during his tenure at the Mint.  
Newton was made president of the Royal Society in 1703 and an associate of the French Académie des Sciences.  
In April 1705, Queen Anne knighted Newton during a royal visit to Trinity College, Cambridge.  
Newton died in his sleep in London on 20 March 1727 and was buried in Westminster Abbey.  
Newton's papers went to John Conduitt and Catherine Barton after his death.  
Newton never married and was noted to have been reserved about his personal life.  
Newton appeared relatively modest about his achievements despite his fierce competitiveness with intellectual rivals.  
Despite his rivalry with Leibniz, Leibniz praised Newton's work as representing much the better half of mathematics up to that time.  
Newton's conception of the universe based upon natural laws became one of the seeds for Enlightenment ideology.  
Newton has been called the most influential figure in the history of Western science.  
He is regarded as the central figure in the history of science and the source of confidence in the power of science.  
The SI derived unit of force is named the newton in his honor.  
Newton often told the story that he was inspired to formulate his theory of gravitation by watching an apple fall from a tree.  
Various trees are claimed to be the apple tree described by Newton, including one at Woolsthorpe Manor.  
Newton's monument can be seen in Westminster Abbey, featuring a figure of Newton reclining on top of a sarcophagus.  
From 1978 until 1988, an image of Newton appeared on Series D £1 banknotes issued by the Bank of England.  
A statue of Isaac Newton can be seen at the Oxford University Museum of Natural History.  
A large bronze statue of Newton dominates the piazza of the British Library in London.

Toward the end of his life, Newton spent some time at Cranbury Park, near Winchester.  
Newton's hair was posthumously examined and found to contain mercury, probably resulting from his alchemical pursuits.
